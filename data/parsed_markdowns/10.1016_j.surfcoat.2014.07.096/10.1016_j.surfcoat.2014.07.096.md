# a b s t r a c t  

The paper presents the results of high temperature oxidation of EB-PVD (Electron Beam Physical Vapor Deposi- tion) TBCs (Thermal Barrier Coatings) deposited on a Pt-diffused second generation single crystal Ni-based su- peralloy. The Pt-diffused bond coating was obtained using a PVD (Physical Vapor Deposition) method applying a Closed Hollow Cathode (CHC) for the deposition of 5  μ m of Pt layer and subsequent annealing at 1140 °C for 2 h in vacuum. The TBC system was thermally cycled in 1.25 h cycles at 1100 °C for 730 h (584 cycles) until de- lamination of 10% of Yttria Stabilized Zirconia (YSZ) top coating. A non-destructive 3D optical scanning method was applied for a macroscopic examination of the YSZ delamination which allowed predicting and revealing of the regions of YSZ buckling prior to its spallation. The microstructure of the TBC system in the as-deposited and thermally cycled conditions was studied using FEG-SEM. The growth and evolution of the Thermally Grown Oxide (TGO) scale was performed using high resolution FEG-S/TEM and Focused Ion Beam (FIB) methods. The TGO in the as-deposited condition was found to consist of porous  α -Al 2 O 3  which grew to form distinctive dense and columnar grains with ionic diffusion of reactive elements (Hf and Y) through grain boundaries during high temperature exposure. Additionally the formation of nanometric NiAl 2 O 4  islands as well as high volume oxides containing Ni was found to accelerate the spallation of the YSZ top coat.

# 1. Introduction  

Thermal Barrier Coatings (TBCs) applied on turbine blades and vanes of modern jet engines provide signi ﬁ cant temperature reduction of the superalloys these components are made of which in turn results in lon- ger lifetimes and increased ef ﬁ ciency. Typical TBC system consists of a ceramic top coat made of 7 wt.% Yttria Stabilized Zirconia (7YSZ) depos- ited using Electron Beam Physical Vapor Deposition (EB-PVD) or plasma spraying and characterized by a low thermal conductivity and phase stability at the operating temperature. Between the substrate Ni-based superalloy and the YSZ top coating a bond coating is applied providing high temperature oxidation resistance due to its ability to form a protec- tive and slow growing  α -alumina TGO (Thermally Grown Oxide) which is considered as the most crucial component of the whole TBC system [1 – 3] .  

Recently a new type of Pt-diffused  γ / γ ′  bond coatings has been stud- ied as an alternative for commonly applied  β -(Ni,Pt)Al coatings  [15 – 17] . Their main advantage is the fact that these coatings exhibit the same crystallographic structure as the substrate and do not suffer from  β  → γ ′  and martensitic transformations which can promote rum- pling, leading to accelerated YSZ spallation  [15 – 19] . Additionally, it has been shown that Pt-diffused  γ / γ ′  bond coatings are not susceptible to degradation caused by the presence of water vapor in contrast to β -(Ni,Pt)Al coatings  [19] . However, due to elimination of the aluminiz- ing process the Pt-diffused  γ / γ ′  bond coatings exhibit relatively low Al concentration (around 18 at.%) thus other oxides can form during high temperature exposure, such as NiO or NiAl 2 O 4 , and compromise the whole TBC system  [17,20] .  

Most commonly applied bond coatings for TBC systems obtained using EB-PVD method are platinum modi ﬁ ed aluminide coatings which provide excellent high temperature resistance  [4 – 7] . However, under service conditions these bond coatings undergo a number of commonly reported degradation mechanisms that limit their durability  

While the Pt-diffused bond coatings studied so far in the literature were obtained by electroplating  [15]  or Spark Plasma Sintering (SPS) [16,21]  and subsequent annealing a novel method for deposition of Pt was applied in this study using CHC-PVD (Closed Hollow Cathode Phys- ical Vapor Deposition). # 2. Experimental procedure  

The investigation concerned EB-PVD TBC coated Ni-based single crystal superalloy with Pt-diffused  γ / γ ′  bond coating. The alloy's composition was [wt.%]: Al — 6.14, C — 0.06, Co — 7.3, Cr — 7.3, Hf — 0.15, Mo — 1.5, Re — 2.88, Ta — 6.5, W — 4.91, Zr — 0.01, Y — b 0.02, S — 0.2 ppm and Ni — balance.

The sample of the Ni superalloy (cylinder shaped, 10 mm diameter, 80 mm height) was put under heat treatment that consisted of solution and aging in order to obtain  γ – γ ′  microstructure. The bond coating was obtained by depositing 5  μ m of Pt using a CHC-PVD method, followed by vacuum annealing at 1140 °C for 2 h. The CHC-PVD is a clean process characterized by high deposition rates (5 nm/s for Pt and 1 nm/s for Pd). A closed hollow cathode assembly provides high plasma densities and ef ﬁ ciency as the coating material is deposited either on the coated component or is recombined on the chamber walls. Prior to YSZ deposi- tion the as-deposited bond coating was pre-oxidized at 1140 °C for 2 h in laboratory air atmosphere. The CHC-PVD deposition of Pt was performed at Fraunhofer IST and 7 wt%. YSZ TBC was produced using EB-PVD at DLR, Germany.  

The cyclic oxidation test was performed at 1100 °C in 1.25 h long cy- cles in laboratory air environment for 730 h (584 cycles). A 3D optical scanning method was applied for a nondestructive evaluation (NDE) of coating's degradation during thermal cycling. This method consists in capturing 3D images of the sample using a 3D optical scanner with +/ − 7  μ m accuracy before and after the cyclic oxidation test. The

# 3. Results and discussion  

3.1. Microstructural analysis of the as-deposited coating  

The cross-sectional microstructure of the as-deposited TBC system on a Pt-diffused  γ \ γ ′  bond coating on a single crystal Ni superalloy is presented in  Fig. 1 a and b along with marked areas where EDS analysis was performed. The results of EDS chemical composition analysis per- formed in the mid-gray (marked as 1) and light gray (marked as 2) areas are presented in  Table 1 . The elemental mapping from the region between the substrate and the Pt-enriched  γ / γ ′  bond coating (marked in  Fig. 1 b) is presented in  Fig. 2 a and b. The microstructure of the Ther- mally Grown Oxide (TGO) formed prior to YSZ deposition is presented in STEM-HAADF and BF images in  Fig. 3 a and b. The RE-doped grain boundary of  α -alumina grains along with corresponding elemental mapping of Y is presented in  Fig. 3 c and d, respectively.  

The microstructure of the as-deposited TBC system consists of a 150  μ m thick 7 wt.% YSZ ceramic top coat on a 42  μ m thick Pt-diffused γ \ γ ′  bond coating ( Fig. 1 a). The cross-sectional microstructure of the bond coating consists of two phases, marked as 1 and 2 in  Fig. 1 b (visible as mid-gray and light gray, respectively), described in the literature  [10, 22]  as a Pt-enriched  γ – γ ′  microstructure. The  γ  phase which is the ma- trix of the bond coating contains a higher amount of Cr, Co, W and Re in comparison to the  γ ′  phase which is mostly enriched in Pt, Al and Ta. On the basis of the EDS results ( Table 1 ) it can be concluded that both the  γ and  γ ′  phases are signi ﬁ cantly enriched with Pt (29 and 35.7 at.%, respectively) and that there is a distinct Al uptake since its level in the latter phase (16.9 at.%) is higher than in the substrate alloy. This indi- cates an outward diffusion of Al during the annealing of the Pt-layer that has also been observed by Zhang et al.  [10] .  

Between the outer Pt-rich zone of the bond coating and the substrate alloy a relatively thin zone (7  μ m thick) can be observed in the etched cross-sectional microstructure in  Fig. 1 b. It contains 600 – 700 nm large γ ′  precipitates enriched in Pt that also contain Al, Ni and Ta as evidenced by the elemental mapping in  Figs. 2 a and  2 b. The  γ  matrix in that region contains Mo, Re, Cr, Co and W.  

The Thermally Grown Oxide (TGO) formed on the Pt-diffused  γ / γ ′ bond coating prior to the deposition of 7 wt.% YSZ top coating consists of a porous 500 – 800 nm thick  α -alumina ( Fig. 3 a). The porous micro- structure indicates that the TGO initially formed as a transient alumina ( ɣ  or  Θ ) that grew by outward Al 3+   diffusion and transformed to the stable  α  polymorph during the short pre-oxidation treatment  [23] , as was also observed for pre-oxidized Pt-aluminide bond coating  [24] . The porosity of the  α -alumina grains derives from the volumetric changes that accompany the  Θ  to  α  transformation  [25,29] . The inter- face between the outer YSZ top coating and the TGO is distinct and no mixed zone formation was observed ( Fig. 3 b) which is in agreement with previous studies on YSZ coatings deposited on  α -alumina  [24, 26 – 28] . Interestingly, the STEM-HAADF analysis of the TGO microstruc- ture revealed the presence of Ni-rich bright-gray islands up to 1  μ m in width that were identi ﬁ ed using electron diffraction as NiAl 2 O 4  spinel ( Fig. 3 a). The occasional formation of Ni-rich oxides, such as NiO and/ or NiAl 2 O 4  can be explained by low Al level in the bond coating (8 at.% in  γ  and 17 at.% in  γ ′  phase)  [14,17,20]  which was unable to maintain an oxide scale consisting exclusively of alumina. Furthermore, the NiAl 2- O 4  may have formed by a solid state reaction with alumina or by an oxidation reaction  [30]  and its formation on Pt-diffused bond coatings has also been reported by Izumi et al.  [20]  according to whom the thick- ness of the Ni-rich oxide scale decreases with increasing Pt content in the coating. Underneath the outer porous alumina and NiAl 2 O 4  a thin layer (around 250 nm thick) of columnar  α -alumina grains that nucle- ated at the interface with the substrate was found ( Fig. 3 b). The micro- structure presented in STEM-BF image in  Fig. 3 b indicates that the columnar grains are somewhat larger underneath the NiAl 2 O 4  proving that it can serve as a nucleation site for stable  α -alumina polymorph and that the pre-oxidation time was long enough for new  α  grains to nucleate by oxidation reaction. The grain boundaries of these dense and columnar  α -alumina grains were found to be doped with REs (Reactive Elements) as can be seen on the STEM-HAADF image in Fig. 3 c — the grain boundary appears as a bright region between two α -Al 2 O 3  grains. Elemental distribution analysis revealed the segregation of Y to the grain boundaries ( Fig. 3 d). In addition, the substrate's surface underneath the  α  grains has a distinctive impinged morphology (oxide imprints) characteristic for oxide scales that grow by inward O 2 − diffu- sion ( Fig. 3 c). It is noteworthy that at the interface between the TGO and the bond coating no voids were found. Since interfacial voids can form during the transient oxidation stage  [13]  it can be concluded that they were healed by columnar  α  grains growing inward the substrate.  

3.2. Nondestructive evaluation of the TBC system after 584 1.25 h cycles of oxidation at 1100 °C  

The results of 3D nondestructive evaluation (NDE) of the investigat- ed TBC system are presented in  Fig. 4 a – c. The 3D model in  Fig. 4 a shows a deviation map representing changes that occurred on the surface of the sample in the period between the YSZ top coating deposition and failure during thermal cycling. The map is provided with a label where particular colors represent positive and negative deviations. The 3D map presented in  Fig. 4 b shows the point buckles that formed during thermal cycling while in  Fig. 4 c the cross-section of one of the buckles is presented. The results of SEM investigation that was performed to con ﬁ rm the NDE evaluation are presented in  Fig. 5 a – c.  

The results of 3D nondestructive evaluation of the studied TBC system after 584 1.25 h cycles of oxidation at 1100 °C revealed surface features that were not visible with unaided eyes. Besides the obvious re- gions of YSZ spallation ( Fig. 4 a) additional regions where YSZ buckling occurred could be identi ﬁ ed and quanti ﬁ ed ( Fig. 4 b) as to their extent and shape. After spallation of the top part of the YSZ coating (negative deviation represented by blue color) its remaining part underwent buckling (positive deviation represented by yellow to dark red colors) which reached the bottom of the sample. The green part of the 3D map marks the region where the YSZ top coating remained adherent to the substrate in spite of the spallation and buckling occurring in its vicinity. The most interesting features of the analyzed 3D maps are the point buckles ( Fig. 4 b,c) that formed during the cyclic oxidation test. Point measurements in these circular regions revealed buckling of the YSZ coating to the distance from 40  μ m to 70  μ m (0.04 – 0.07 mm) in the direction perpendicular to the surface. The cross-sectional analysis of one such region ( Fig. 4 c) revealed that it consists of a highly concen- trated buckle (70  μ m in height in that case) which did not propagate any farther. Nonetheless, buckling of the YSZ in such small regions on the whole surface of the sample could lead to stress generation and its ac- celerated spallation. The microstructure analysis of the buckled area ( Fig. 5 a) revealed that it contains a cone-shaped conglomerate of YSZ columns with radial cracks on the surface around it. The large size of the columns in that region indicates that they nucleated on a convoluted surface thus the presence of high volume oxides in that region prior to YSZ deposition could be assumed. In addition, there are several regions of adherent YSZ also with larger column diameters where such oxides might have been present but did not grow during high temperature oxidation to the extent that would cause buckling. This hypothesis is supported by the microstructural analysis of a similar region where YSZ spallation occurred, presented in  Fig. 5 b, and high volume oxides

However, as evidenced in  Fig. 5 c, it can be concluded that in some cases the alumina scale did not become rate controlling and the oxidation of nickel continued leading to a rapid volume increase and stress formation in the YSZ top coat which in turn underwent buckling and spallation.  

# 3.3. Microstructure evolution during the cyclic oxidation test at 1100 °C  

The cross-sectional microstructure of the investigated Pt-diffused  ɣ / ɣ ′ bond coating after 584 1.25 h cycles of oxidation at 1100 °C is presented in  Fig. 6  and the results of chemical composition analysis in microareas 1 – 3 are presented in  Table 2 .  

During the cyclic oxidation test at 1100 °C a continuous layer of ɣ  phase formed at the interface with the TGO where the content of Pt decreased from 29 at.% to 7 at.% while the Al content remained constant at around 8 at.% ( Table 2 ). Additionally the large grains of  ɣ ′  phase maintained Al level very close to that found in the as deposited state — around 18 at.% while Pt content decreased to 12 at.% from 35 at.%. These results are in agreement with those reported by Zhang et al. [15]  who suggested that the presence of Pt results in an uphill diffusion of Al so that its content is retained until a decrease of Pt concentration

During thermal cycling void formation was observed in the region at the boundary be- tween the superalloy and the Pt-diffused bond coating ( Fig. 6 ). Such a phenomenon was observed by Zhang et al. The voids observed in this study were round with about 10  μ m in diameter and could originate from unbalanced diffusion  ﬂ uxes between the substrate alloy and the coating  [15] . # 3.4. Evolution of the Thermally Grown Oxide (TGO)  

The high resolution STEM analysis of the TGO that formed during 584 1.25 h cycles of oxidation at 1100 °C on the investigated Pt- diffused  ɣ / ɣ ′  bond coating is presented in STEM-BF images ( Fig. 7 a,b). The microstructure of the RE-doped  α -alumina grain boundaries is pre- sented in  Fig. 7 c along with elemental mapping of Hf, Y and Ta present- ed in  Fig. 7 d.  

The TGO grown on the Pt-diffused  ɣ / ɣ ′  bond coating during 584 1.25 h cycles of oxidation at 1100 °C is around 8  μ m thick and consists of two distinctive zones ( Fig. 7 a). The outer zone (around 800 nm thick) consists of equiaxed and porous  α -alumina grains with NiAl 2 O 4 islands ( Fig. 7 b) that grew during the pre-oxidation treatment and no evolution of this particular region was observed during the cyclic oxida- tion test. The inner zone, around 7 – 8  μ m thick, consists of dense and co- lumnar  α -alumina grains that grew by nucleation at the metal-scale interface and inward diffusion of O 2 − with minor outward Al 3+   diffu- sion  [32] . At the interface between the inner and outer zones of the TGO a distinct porosity can be observed indicating vacancy injection at the initial metal-scale interface. Additionally, the columnar grain boundaries underneath the NiAl 2 O 4  contain numerous perturbation voids ( Fig. 7 b) that were most probably incorporated during the onset of alumina growth governed by inward O 2 − diffusion  [25] .  

The grain boundaries of the columnar  α -alumina formed on the in- vestigated Pt-diffused bond coating were found to be doped with reactive elements ( Fig. 7 c) such as Hf, Y and Ta ( Fig. 7 d) which is in agreement with the results obtained by Unocic et al.  [19]  for a similar bond coating. In contrast to our previous studies on Pd \ Pt-aluminide bond coatings [26]  no Zr was found to diffuse through the grain boundaries of the TGO studied in this paper. While Zr was also found to segregate to the grain boundaries of alumina scales formed on bare N5 superalloy at 1100 °C and at 1200 °C on La 2 O 3 -dispersed FeCrAl  [33]  it can be conclud- ed that the presence of Pt in the  ɣ / ɣ ′  bond coating facilitates the diffusion of Hf and Y in particular but not Zr at 1100 °C. Additionally, the presence of Ta is not believed to be as bene ﬁ cial for alumina scales as Hf and Y ac- cording to  [34] . The outward diffusion of reactive elements such as Hf and Y from the superalloy through the  α -alumina grain boundaries is driven by the oxygen potential gradient (OPG) according to the dynamic segre- gation theory (DST)  [35] . Recent papers on this phenomenon indicate that these elements reduce the Al ionization at the metal-scale interface leading to inhibition of outward Al 3+   diffusion and causing the alumina scale to grow mostly by inward O 2 − diffusion  [36] .  

Due to the supersaturation of Hf, Y and Ta on the TGO grain bound- aries the formation of particles (up to 200 nm in length) rich in these elements occurred during the growth of the columnar alumina scale ( Fig. 7 b). The formation of such particles can originate from the phe- nomenon described by a model suggested by Haynes et al.  [13]  where perturbation voids continuously form over discontinuities at the inter- face between the oxide scale and the substrate alloy. Such voids are encapsulated within the growing scale and can act as sinks for Hf and Y leading to their supersaturation.  

# 4. Summary  

The conducted study concerned the application of Pt-enriched surface layer of a 2nd generation SX Ni superalloy as a bond coating for EB-PVD Thermal Barrier Coatings. The deposition of Pt layer was conducted using a Closed Hollow Cathode Physical Vapor Deposition (CHC-PVD) method.  

The as-deposited bond coating consisted of Pt-enriched  ɣ  and  ɣ ′ phases (29 and 35.7 at.% of Pt respectively) with the latter phase exhibiting also Al enrichment (16.9 at.%) due to its uphill diffusion in the presence of Pt. The pre-oxidation treatment applied to the Pt- diffused bond coating prior to the YSZ deposition resulted in the forma- tion of a thin (500 – 800 nm) TGO layer that consisted of porous  α - alumina indicating initial growth of the transient  θ  polymorph which was accompanied by volumetric changes during the transformation to the  α  polymorph. Additionally, occasional formation of NiAl 2 O 4  islands was observed in the TGO.  

After 584 1.25 h cycles of oxidation at 1100 °C the Al content in the  ɣ ′ phase was retained at around 18 at.% while the Pt content decreased to 7 at.% due to interdiffusion with the substrate superalloy. The evolution of the TGO involved the nucleation and growth of columnar  α -alumina grains at the interface between the porous  α -alumina formed during the pre-oxidation treatment and the substrate superalloy. The grain boundaries of the TGO were found to be doped with Hf, Y and Ta while no Zr was detected.  

3D nondestructive evaluation of the investigated TBC system's degradation revealed the formation of concentrated point buckles which led to stress generation and accelerated YSZ top coat spallation. The observed buckles formed as a result of an unimpeded oxidation of Ni which started during the pre-oxidation treatment and continued in the regions where the growth of alumina did not become rate control- ling due to low Al level in the bond coating.
