# Abstract:  

The solidification paths, microstructures and mechanical properties of two Co-Al-W base superalloys  with different additions of tungsten and molybdenum were investigated. Mo addition does not  influence the volume fraction of the γ′ phase; however, the morphology of the γ′ phase changes from  cuboidal to near cuboidal. The μ phase and β phase were identified in both alloy systems. Although  the melting point of the μ phase is decreased by Mo addition, the solidification paths remain the  same: L→ L 1  + γ → L 2  + γ + μ → γ + μ + β → γ + μ + β + γ′. The precipitation of a γ′ phase envelop  around the interdendritic regions in Co-Al-W alloy systems has never been reported. The formation  of a surrounding γ′ phase was caused by the diffusion of elements between secondary phases and the  substrate during the heat treatments. The microhardness of the μ phase, which can be easily  influenced by Mo addition, is the highest in both alloys, followed by the microhardnesses of the β  phase and the substrate. In the fracturing process, microcracks were generated in the μ phase, the  number of which increased with Mo addition.  

Keywords: Co-base superalloys; Molybdenum; Microstructures; Tensile behavior; Solidification path  

# 1.   Introduction  

Nickel-base superalloys have been successfully used in gas turbine engine for decades because  they are strengthened by coherent L1 2 -orderd   γ′-(Ni 3 Al) phase in the FCC γ matrix. Conventional  Cobalt-base superalloys mainly depend on solid solution hardening and carbide strengthening;  however compared with the Nickel-base superalloys, their solidus and liquidus temperatures are  50-150  ℃  higher [1]. The discovery of the Co-Al-W ternary systems that are strengthened by  γ′-Co 3 (Al,W) with L1 2  structure by Sato et al. [2] demonstrates that this class of Co-Al-W  superalloys can provide a new and efficient high-temperature alloy system.  

To achieve high microstructural stabilities and superior mechanical properties of Co-base  superalloys, various alloying elements such as Ta, Ti and Cr have been investigated [3-5]. According  to Ref [6], when the concentration of Ta reaches to 2.2 at.%, the γ′ phase becomes metastable. In the  meantime, a small addition of Hf can make the γ′ phase stable. In Co-Al-W alloy systems, W is the γ′  formation element and it can increase the γ′ solvus temperature by 20  ℃  per 1 at.% increase; the  creep strength is also improved by the increased γ′ volume fraction [7]. However, a high  concentration of W (19.3 g·cm -3 ) leads to a high density, the densities of Co-base superalloys, which  are in excess of 9.0 g·cm -3 , are higher than the densities of most Ni-base superalloys [8]. To make  Co-Al-W base superalloys more competitive than Ni-base superalloys, we must reduce the W content  in the alloy systems. Substantial efforts have been made to find a substitute for W. The investigations  by Shinagawa et al. [9] demonstrate that the γ/γ′ field is enlarged with increasing Ni content and the γ′  phase continuously exists from the Co side to the Ni side. Since Mo is in the same group as W,  attempts to replace W with Mo are made by G. Feng [10]. The cellular and needle-like κ-Co 3 Mo  phase which is harmful to the alloy’s strength and ductility appears when at least 6 at.% W is  replaced by Mo.  

In this research, we aim at understanding the effects of Mo on microstructural characteristics and  tensile properties. Microstructural characteristics such as the solvus temperature, solidification path  and microhardness were investigated. Furthermore, the influence of Mo on the evolution of  microstructures during heat treatments was discussed.  

# 2.   Experimental procedure  

![Table 1  Nominal compositions of two experimental alloys (wt.%).  ](images/a3baf73d0b3d98fd66af0dc8f3a26a0b5fa08b539f804c8d2f547d8769e6bc7c.jpg)  

The nominal compositions of the experimental polycrystalline alloys are listed in Table 1. Both  alloys have the same compositions except for Mo: In the 5Mo alloy, 5 wt.% W was substituted by 5  wt.% Mo, the other unsubstituted alloy is denoted as 0Mo. The samples were encapsulated in quartz  tubes that were filled with argon and the heat treatments were carried out as follows: 1210  ℃ /4 h, air  cooling + 1050  ℃ /2 h, air cooling + 850  ℃ /48 h, and air cooling. The γ′ solvus temperatures were  determined via differential thermal analysis (DTA). As-cast and heat-treated samples were prepared  by standard metallographic methods and etched in a solution of 42 ml H 3 PO 4  +34 ml H 2 SO 4  +24 ml  H 2 O at 5 V for a few seconds. Microstructures were examined by using a scanning electron  microscope (SEM) in both backscatter electron (BSE) mode and secondary electron (SE) mode. The  SEM micrographs are used to calculate the area fractions of the secondary phases and the γ′ phase  which approximately represent the volume fractions of them. We use Image Pro Plus software to  calculate the area fractions of the secondary phases with 15 low magnification SEM images and the  area fraction of the γ′ phase with 15 high magnification SEM images. Vickers microhardness  measurements were carried out to estimate the mechanical behavior of different phases. X-ray  diffraction (XRD) with Cu-Kα was performed for phase identification. The equilibrium compositions  of the γ/γ′ phases and interdendritic areas were determined using an electron probe microanalyzer  (EPMA). Archimedes′ principle was used to determine the mass densities. Tensile specimens with a  nominal gage length of 25 mm and a diameter of 5 mm were used to measure the tensile behaviors of  the two experimental alloys at room temperature and 900  ℃ .

# 3.2.   Microstructure  

# 3.2.1.   γ′ phase  

Fig. 3(a) and (b) show the typical γ/γ′ microstructures in the matrix of two experimental alloys  after heat treatments (the secondary phases are not shown). The cuboidal γ′ precipitates of 0Mo are  coherent with the γ matrix and have an average size of \~1 μm. As shown in Fig. 3(b), the γ′  precipitates of 5Mo have an average size of \~ 0.8 μm and become near cuboidal due to a smaller  lattice misfit. Omori et al. [3] found that Mo atoms have tendency to partition to the γ′ phase and the  lattice misfit decreases when the Mo atoms occupy the sites of W atoms [12]. The volume fraction of  γ′ is listed in Table 3. It does not change significantly with 5% Mo addition, because the proportion  of the γ′ formation elements are kept at the same level.  

![](images/505ec2b4cb8d6be1755718f476419c3870f6dc1201512ea67f0384fa8296248a.jpg)  
Fig. 3  The typical γ′ microstructures of heat-treated 0Mo and 5Mo alloys: (a) 0Mo and (b) 5Mo.  

# 3.2.2.   Secondary phases  

The as-cast and heat-treated microstructures of 0Mo and 5Mo alloys were examined in BSE mode  via SEM. Fig. 4(a) and (b) show the microstructures of the two experimental alloys before the heat  treatments which consist of the dendritic structures. Two phases that differ in terms of contrast are  formed in the interdendritic regions: a dark-contrast phase and a bright-contrast phase. The EPMA  results are presented in Table 4 and we can find the bright-contrast phase is enriched in refractory  elements such as W and the dark-contrast phase is enriched with Al and only a few phases can be  chosen as candidates for the bright-contrast phase and the dark-contrast phase. They are Co 3 W (D0 19 )  or Co 7 W 6  (D8 5 , also known as μ) for the bright-contrast phase and CoAl (β) for the dark-contrast  phase. Only Co 7 W 6  (μ) and CoAl (β) match the results of XRD. The μ phase, which is a type of  topologically close pack (TCP) phases, known for their complex crystallographic structure and  physical properties, has an ideal A 6 B 7  stoichiometry, with its prototype W 6 Fe 7 , containing  rhombohedral cell with 13 atoms [13]. Based on the XRD results and the composition results, in both  0Mo and 5Mo alloys, the dark-contrast phase, which contains high level of Al and Ti, is the β phase.  The bright-contrast phase, which is enriched in refractory alloying elements W, Ta and Hf and  depleted in Al and Ni, is the μ phase. The carbides, which are enriched with carbon and hafnium, can  be found in Fig. 6. They do not appear in the XRD results because of their very small volume  fraction. Needle-shaped phase can be found in the β phase, possibly the D0 24  phase according to Ref.  [14, 15]. In addition to W, Ta and Hf, the bright-contrast phase in 5Mo is enriched with Mo. The  amount of the bright-contrast phase increases enormously with 5% Mo addition. Fig. 2 shows the  XRD results of the 0Mo and 5Mo alloys after heat treatment. In addition to γ/γ′ phases, peaks of μ  the phase and β phase were identified.  

![Table 3  Volume fraction of μ and γ′ precipitates in 0Mo and 5Mo alloys.   ](images/df31d4fe030852b0bb6fd99fdba9f10ab5f44d059306c3f4753a54948cb515b5.jpg)  

Fig. 4 (c) and (d) show the typical microstructure of the two experimental alloys after heat  treatments. A needle-like phase was formed in the β phase, which is probably γ + γ′ eutectic. All the  interdendritic regions are wrapped by a thin γ′ phase layer. After the heat treatments, the volume  fraction of the μ phase decreases from 8.6% to 7.9% for the 0Mo alloys and stays at the same level  for the 5Mo alloys. Hence, the volume fractions of the μ phase and β phase change little due to the  heat treatments. 5Mo addition promotes the precipitation of the μ phase and increase the volume  fraction of the μ phase from  

![](images/376aa5c80efd5a94db2195ae79e832a6279bc283c05ff28ab30b8ea6304e447e.jpg)  
Fig. 4  As-cast and heat-treated microstructures of 0Mo and 5Mo alloys:(a)(b) as-cast, (c)(d)  heat-treated, (a)(c) 0Mo, (b)(d) 5Mo.  

7.9% to 10.2% for heat-treated alloys. 5Mo addition can also decrease the volume fraction of the β  phase. Both of these alloys contain a large amount of the μ phase, with a small amount of the γ′  phase around it. Both the μ phase and β phase are observed in Xue et al. [16], the contrast and  morphology of the μ phase and β phase in the present research are the same as that in Xue et al.’s  result. However, no such γ′ phase around the μ phase was reported. Comparing the compositions of  the surrounding γ′ phase and the substrate in Table 4, this type of γ′ phase contains more Ni, Ta and  Ti and less W, Cr than the substrate. It is generally known that Ta and Ti are the formation elements  of γ′-Co 3 (Al,W) and Cr is partitioned into the γ phase [3]. This is the distinguishing feature of the γ′  phase. However, the surrounding γ′ phase has a lower W content, which is the result of diffusion  between the μ phase and the substrate. According to Table 4, the W content in the μ phase increased  after the heat treatments; hence W atoms diffused from the substrate to the μ phase. Therefore, a  tungsten depleted zone was formed around the μ phase. This zone was enriched with Ni, Ta and Ti.  Finally, the μ phase became embedded within a thin layer of γ′ phase. Moreover, according to the  result in Table 4, this type of γ′ phase has a higher γ′-Ni 3 Al content than the typical γ′ phase. Fig. 5  shows three distinct stages of the surrounding γ′ phase growth: After the process of solidification, the  secondary phases didn′t reach the equilibrium concentrations and were embedded within both the γ  and γ′ phases (stage A). To reach equilibrium concentrations, elements atoms such as Ni and W  diffused between the substrate and the secondary phases during the heat treatment. This resulted in a  gradient concentration zone of various elements around the secondary phases (stage B). Finally, the  secondary phases reached the equilibrium concentrations and were embedded within an envelope of  γ′ phase (stage C).  

![](images/86d270fbb5520fcd2dffff5cc503ac5b732167bd4483e8472dcb148937ce7fad.jpg)  
Fig. 5 Schematic illustration of the sequence of local microstructural changes around the  interdendritic regions  

This type of γ′ precipitate was also found in Nickel-base superalloys[17]. Based on their research,  an envelope of γ′ was formed due to the continued growth of the TCP phase, which causes  dissolution of the γ phase during an isothermal exposure. However, for both the 0Mo and 5Mo alloys,  the TCP phase didn′t grow and the volume fraction remained nearly constant. In the present work,  changes in elemental compositions such as W, Cr and Ta in the μ phase lead to the formation of the  surrounding γ′ phase. In addition, the analysis of the composition and morphology of the surrounding  γ′ phase, additional evidence is available to support the existence of this phase. Fig. 6 shows the r  EMPA results. A Cr-depleted channel was formed in the substrate and the μ phase and β phase which  constitute the surrounding γ′ phase. Other elements in the alloys were also measured; but none of  them has the same distribution as Cr (not shown). Hence, it is difficult to distinguish this type of γ′  phase from the distribution of others elements. In addition, some small phase in Fig. 6 contain high  concentration of Hf and C, which is apparently carbide. The carbide was observed in SEM images, in  which its contrast is higher than that of the μ phase. According to Fig. 4, the carbide was distributed  only in the γ matrix for as-cast alloys but in the surrounding γ′ phase and μ phase for heat-treated  alloys.

# 3.3.   Solidification paths  

We conducted heat preservation experiments to investigate the solidification sequence of all  phases in 0Mo and 5Mo alloys. The heat preservation temperatures are determined according to DTA  results. From Fig. 1, we conclude that only one phase is transformed from solid to liquid between  1200  ℃  and 1300  ℃ . Therefore, we choose 1200  ℃ , 1300  ℃  and the middle temperature 1250  ℃  for  the heat preservation experiments. Fig. 7 shows the microstructures of the 0Mo and 5Mo alloys after  heat preservation for 10 min followed by quenching. According to Fig. 7(a) and (d), the β phase and  γ′ precipitates are dissolved in the γ matrix, whereas the μ phase still remains its morphosis. When  the temperature reaches to 1250  ℃ , the μ phase begins to dissolve in the γ matrix. Moreover,  comparing Fig. 7(b) with Fig. 7(e), more of the μ phase is dissolved at the same temperature over the  same time periods, hence addition of 5% Mo contributes to a lower solvus temperature of the μ phase.  As exhibited in Fig. 7(c) and (f), the entire μ phase is dissolved and if heat preservation is carried out  for a sufficient duration, the μ phase will become completely dispersed throughout the γ matrix.  Based on the conclusion above, during the solidification process, the μ phase precipitated from the  matrix at a higher temperature than the β phase. In the interdendritic area in Fig. 4(a) and (b), some  of β phase are embedded with the μ phase which are closed to the edge of the dendrite area.  Therefore, the μ phase precipitate at the edge of the dendrite core and the β phase subsequently  precipitates in the residual liquid of the interdendritic region. Comparing the sizes of the γ′  precipitate close to and far from the interdendritic region in Fig. 4(a) and (b), the former is larger due  to a lower cooling rate.  

Above all, the solidification paths are determined as followed:  

The μ phase was identified in the 0Mo and 5Mo alloys. The μ phase precipitates before the β  phase in the interdendritic region due to the higher solution temperature of the μ phase. Moreover,  the surrounding γ′ phase precipitates during the cooling stage.

# 3.4.   Microhardness and tensile behavior  

The microhardnesses of different phases after heat treatments are presented in Fig. 8. The Mo  content has a substantial influence on the microhardness. With 5Mo addition, the microhardness of  the μ phase has a decreasing tendency while the microhardness of the β phase and γ/γ′ phases remain  approximately constant. This result is associated with the partitioning of Mo. Thus, most of the Mo  atoms are consumed by the μ phase which causes the large variation of the microhardness. In both  the 0Mo and 5Mo alloys, the microhardness of the μ phase is the highest, followed by the β phase  and the γ/γ′ phases. Due to the high volume fraction of the μ phase, the mechanical properties and  ductility are easily affected by its characteristics.  

![](images/695f578a9f4e120593453cee33402c05e6111bb1a80bc22215cdb0332fc701ca.jpg)  
Fig. 8  Microhardness of 0Mo and 5Mo after heat treatments.  

The tensile properties of the 0Mo and 5Mo alloys are shown in Fig. 9. The 0Mo and 5Mo alloys  have almost the same tensile strength; however, brittle fractures occurred in two samples of 0Mo  alloys, which may due to the higher microhardness of the μ phase. At room temperature, the ultimate  strength, the yield strength and the percentage of elongation are all decreased with addition of 5%  Mo because 5Mo addition increases the volume fraction of the μ phase. Hence, the μ phase plays a  negative role in ductility and strength at room temperature.  

![](images/46b58f6eebab7b0befd284c8c2dcb8feed511fae72a15562b05fc0a3ee19119f.jpg)  
Fig. 9  Tensile properties of heat-treated 0Mo and 5Mo alloys at room temperature and 900  ℃ .  

The SEM fractographic analysis results are exhibited in Fig. 10. As it can be seen in Fig. 10(a) and 

 (d), the material has failed in an interdendritic manner with a failure strain of 5 pct or less. According  to Fig. 10(b) and (e), internal cracks tended to occur in the μ phase, which exhibited the brittle nature  of the TCP phases. When the material was strained, the μ phase precipitates acted as crack initiation  sites and the cracks in the μ phase grew and joined together, leading to failure. As shown in Fig.10(f),  the proportion of the μ phase on the fracture surface in Fig. (f) is obviously higher than that in  Fig.10(c), which demonstrates that more cracks initiated in the μ phase at elevated temperature,  resulting in a reduction in the strength. However, in the rupturing process, the γ′ precipitates around  the μ phase play an important role. There are cracks that started in the μ phase and stopped at the  surrounding γ′ phase in Fig. 10 (b) and (e). Hence, this type of γ′ phase hinders the expansion of the  cracks.

# 4.   Conclusions  

The main effects of molybdenum on the γ′ solvus temperatures, volume fraction of the γ′ phase,  microstructures, solidification path and tensile behavior were investigated. The following  conclusions are drawn:  

(1)   The substitution of W by Mo in the 5Mo alloys resulted in an 18  ℃  reduction of the γ′ solvus  temperature and a decrease in the mass density from 8.94 g·cm -3  to 8.73 g·cm -3 . However, the  volume fraction of γ′ was not affected by Mo addition.  

(2)   The μ phase, β phase, γ/γ′ phase and carbide are identified in the 0Mo and 5Mo alloys. In  addition, thin layers of γ′ precipitates formed around the interdendritic regions in the heat-treated  alloys, which were caused by the changes in compositions in the μ phase and β phase during the  heat treatment.  

(3)   The solidification paths of the 0Mo and 5Mo alloys are as follows: L→ L 1  + γ → L 2  + γ + μ → γ  + μ + β → γ + μ + β + γ′.  

(4)   The μ phase has the highest microhardness value and microcracks are easily generated in it. The  yield strength decreased with Mo addition at room temperature because more of the μ phase  precipitated in the interdendritic regions. During the fracturing process, the γ′ precipitate around  the μ phase play important roles: blocking the extension of microcracks.
