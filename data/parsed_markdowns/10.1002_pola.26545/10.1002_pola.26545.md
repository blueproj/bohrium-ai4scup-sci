Polymer solar cells (PSCs) have attracted considerable atten- tion due to their unique advantages of low cost, light weight, ease of processing, and flexibility in large area applica- tions.   Thanks to the concept of bulk heterojunction (BHJ) which was composed of electron-donating conjugated poly- mers and electron-accepting fullerene derivatives (such as PC 61 BM, PC 71 BM, ICBA) to offer an inter penetrating network, the power conversion efficiencies (PCEs) of the PSCs have been improved significantly from less than 1% of 5   poly(phenylenevinylene) (PPV)/PC 61 BM in 1995 to nearly 9%   recently. The properties of the conjugated polymers such as band gap, energy levels, carrier-mobility, and solubility have great influence on photovoltaic performance. Fortunately, due to the diversity of the molecular structure in organic chemistry, the polymer properties can be tuned conven- iently.   Donor–acceptor (D-A) alternating conjugated poly- mers with low-band gap have received great interest for its absorption can be modulated by choosing different combina- tions of building blocks to match the solar photon flux well, which have been successfully developed to achieve high PCEs over 7%.   According to published models and experimen- tal results up to now, the PCEs of the device was proportional to the values of the short circuit current density ( J sc ), open cir- cuit voltage ( V oc ), and fill factors (FFs). For the device based on D-A copolymer, high  J sc  can be obtained by the strong absorption in the visible region via controlling the intramolec- ular charge transfer within the donor–acceptor backbone, but it usually results in the low  V oc  due to the elevated highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) level.  

Among various donor building blocks, benzo[1,2-b:4,5-b’] dithiophene (BDT), which has a large and planar molecular structure by fusing a benzene with two flanking thiophene units, exhibited the most promising properties. It was well- known for its weak donor strength to keep low HOMO level and fuse rings to obtain enhanced mobility profited.   For further improvements in the photovoltaic performance of the BDT-based polymers, structural modification has been brought about by introducing thiophene groups on the phe- nyl ring of BDT to afford two-dimensional alkylthienyl-sub- stituted BDT (TBDT). Due to the extended conjugation, poly- mers based on TBDT showed red-shifted and broadened absorption.   As for the acceptor building blocks, 4,7- dithien-2-yl-2,1,3-b enzo thi a diaz ole (DTBT) has been success- fully developed in many high efficiency polymers by virtue of their excellent electron-withdrawing property, and the typ- ical well-known polymer was reported by Heeger and co- workers.   Moreover, fluorinated DTBT has been found to be benefit to improve the performance, because electron-with- drawing fluorine has been proven to be very effective in decreasing the HOMO levels, subsequently resulting in a higher  V oc  in fluorine-based polymers, for example, PBDT- fTT,   PBDT-FTAZ,   PBDT-FBT,   PIDT-DfBT.   Especially, in those fluorine-based polymers, incorporation of fluorine did not cause any deleterious steric effects. The outstanding rep- resentation of fluorinated unit 5,6-difluoro-4,7-dithien-2-yl- 2,1,3-b enzo thi a diaz ole (ffDTBT) has been verified by some high-efficiency photovoltaic polymers lately, such as, PBDT- 27     DTffBT and PBDT-ffDTBT. Additionally, fluorine atom also has some other interesting functions, such as used in end- group-modification, self-organization.

The thermal stabilities of the polymers were measured by thermo grav i metric analysis (TGA). Seen from Figure 1, all of the polymers exhibited good thermal stability, and decomposition temperatures ( T d ) at 5% weight loss were nearly the same, approximately above 400  C, which was an important characteristic for device fabrication and application. Differential scanning calo- rimetry measurements on copolymers found neither endo- nor exo-thermic processes below 240    C, shown in Support- ing Information Figure S4.

At the same time, the red-shift (  63 nm) of PTBDTDTBT was more pronounced than that of PTBDTffDTBT (  17 nm), indicating that PTBDTDTBT ag- gregated in film much more strongly than PTBDTffDTBT. The maximum absorption of PTBDTDTBT in the solid state was 613 nm, which was 30 nm larger than that of PTBDTffDTBT, and the absorption edges of their films were at 756 nm and 712 nm, respectively, corresponding to optical band gaps of 1.62 eV and 1.74 eV. It could inferred that the introduction of fluorine atom heightened the band gap slightly, which was in agreement with other paper that the electron-withdrawing groups made the absorption blue-shift. Cyclic voltammetry (CV) was used to investigate the electro- chemical properties of the polymers (shown in Fig. 3). The HOMO and lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) energy levels of the polymers were deduced from the plot and listed in Table 1. CV revealed reversible oxidation behav- ior and inconspicuous reduction peak for both polymers. The fluorinated polymer (PTBDTffDTBT) was oxidized after non- fluorinated polymer (PTBDTDTBT), making the HOMO of PTBDTffDTBT lower, which also agreed with the reported works that the introduction of fluorine atom on the conju- gated backbone could decrease the HOMO energy level. Because the first reduction peak of the polymers was hard to defined, the LUMO energy levels were estimated from the optical band gap and the related data were summarized in Table 1. It could be found that the fluorine substitution not only influenced the absorption spectra and band gap but also lowered the HOMO energy level.

Combination with a much higher  J sc (12.17 mA cm  2 ) and slightly improved FF (45.6%), a over- all PCE of the device (PTBDTffDTBT/PC 61 BM ratio 1:1) with- out any optimization reached to 4.8%, which has been remarkably enhanced in contrast to that of nonfluorinated analogues. Unlike PBDTDTBT based device, thermal anneal- ing did not promote efficiency improvement, manifesting again that the inherent interchain interaction of polymer could not be transformed by common thermal treatment. Figure 6 showed the atom force microscopy images of the active layers, which gave the direct evidence to explain the morphology difference between the two polymer:PCBM blends. The surface roughness  S q  (root mean square rough- ness) of PTBDTffDTBT blend was 0.84 nm, much lower than nonfluorinated counterpart with  S q  of 2.87 nm. The active layer of fluorinated polymer exhibited a better morphology with less rough and smaller bicontinuous phase separation, which helped to prompt effective charge dissociation and transportation, consequently led to the enhanced  J sc  and FF.
