# Abstract  

Double-layer thermal barrier coatings (TBCs), including a top ZrO 2  layer and an inner CoNiCrAlY layer, were deposited on nickel-based superalloy using supersonic atmospheric plasma spraying (SAPS). Thermal shock resistance of the TBCs between 1200  1 C and room temperature was investigated. After thermal shock test, the adhesive strength of the coatings was evaluated through scratch test. The SAPS – TBCs present good thermal shock resistance, exhibiting only 0.26% mass gain up to 150-time thermal cycling. Before thermal cyclic treatment, SAPS – TBCs exhibited a strong adhesion with the absence of the thermally grown oxide (TGO) between out and inner layer. With the increasing of thermal cycles, the TGO layer was formed and its thickness  ﬁ rstly increased and then dropped down. The critical load fell down by about 32% for topcoat – bondcoat adhesion (up to 50 cycles) and 35% or so for TBCs – substrate adhesion (up to 150 cycles) compared to the counterpart of as- sprayed specimens. The strain introduced by the existence of TGO and mixed oxides resulted in a varied adhesion for TBCs on nickel-based alloy during thermal cycling.

# 1. Introduction  

Nickel-based superalloy has been widely used in aviation and navigation  ﬁ eld  [1 – 3] , and some applications require it to survive in a thermal cyclic environment. For example, the gas turbine often operates from the static environment of nearly room temperature to the working condition above 1000  1 C  [1] . Some hot-section devices even need to work in a cyclic combustion gas and wind tunnel environment  [4 , 5] . At high temperature, the poor oxidation resistance and relatively short thermal cycling life of nickel-based superalloy limit its further application. Employ- ing thermal barrier coatings (TBCs), by plasma spraying or vapor deposition, is a reliable approach to protect nickel-based superalloy against corrosion and oxidation  [1 , 6] .  

Owing to high temperature of plasma  ﬂ ame, high ef ﬁ ciency of energy consumption and high impact velocity of in- ﬂ ight particles [7] , supersonic atmospheric plasma spraying (SAPS) exhibits obvious superiority for obtaining coatings with high-quality structure (such as ultra ﬁ ne columnar crystal grain, compact splat layer, little fraction of pore  [8 , 9] ) and preferential performance (including good oxidation resistance and long thermal cycling life) on carbon substrate  [10]  and metal matrix materials  [11]  as compared with air plasma spraying(APS)  [8] . As for nickel- based superalloy substrate, the TBCs fabricated by SAPS (SAPS – TBCs) exhibit outstanding oxidation resistance against 1100  1 C for 1000 h with only 6.8 mg/cm 2   weight gain, about 43% lower than APS TBCs  [12] . Up to now, there are few literatures about SAPS – TBCs on nickel-based superalloy. The previous work about TBCs by SAPS concentrated mainly on their oxidation behavior in static air.  

As we known, coating performance closely depends on its adhesion to substrate. Therefore, the evolution of the coating adhesion during thermal cycling has a direct relation on its service life. It is reported that TBCs, fabricated by APS  [13] and high velocity air – fuel spray (HVAF)  [14] , shown a reducing tendency of bond strength during oxidation duration or thermal cyclic treatment. In the present work, a double-layer TBC was prepared on nickel-based superalloy by SAPS. The top layer was com- prised of partially stabilized ZrO 2  and the inner coating (or bond coating) consisted of CoNiCrAlY. The inner coating can relieve the mismatch of different coef ﬁ cient of thermal expend (CTE) between the top coating and substrate as well as play a signi ﬁ cant role for improving the coating adhesive strength. The thermal shock resistance of SAPS – TBCs at 1200  1 C was tested and the corresponding adhesive strength was investi- gated through micro-scratch test. The microstructural charac- terization was carried out by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) whereas the phase analysis was carried out by X-ray diffraction (XRD). The adhesion evolution after different thermal cycles was discussed.

# 2.3. Thermal shock and adhesion test  

Thermal shock test was performed in an electric furnace at 1200  1 C in air. After oxidation at 1200  1 C for 5 min, the specimens were immediately taken out of the furnace and cooled down in ambient environment for 5 min. Then, the specimens were put into the furnace again for the next cycle. The mass change was recorded after every 5-time thermal cycling using an electric balance with a sensitivity of 7 0.1 mg. At least three specimens were tested and the average value was calculated. The mass change of specimens was plotted as a function of thermal cycles in order to investigate the thermo- dynamic mechanism of the oxidation reaction.  

The adhesive strength was measured through the micro- scratch test using a MHT scratch/indentation system, USA. A diamond indent in conical shape was used as the scratch indent with a radius of 100  m m. During the test process, a linear load (0 – 30 N) with a load rate of 10 N/min was vertically con- ducted as the indent scratched parallel on the specimen surface for 1 mm distance. Moreover, the tangential scratch speed was 0.333 mm/min. In order to lower the statistical error,  ﬁ ve

The thermal shock test was performed on nickel-based super- alloy with and without TBCs. The mass change of the coated specimens was calculated and plotted as a function of thermal cycles in  Fig. 4 . It can be observed that the mass change of bare nickel-based superalloy was in accordance to a parabolic manner during thermal cycling treatment. In the thermal shock process, oxygen easily reacted with nickel-based alloy to form nickel oxide or other oxides (derived from the oxidation reaction of the alloy elements in substrate), resulting in a mass gain in  ﬁ rst 5 cycles. Subsequently, since the oxidation products had the CTE mismatch with the inner nickel-based superalloy, part of oxidation products splashed off quickly once the sample moved into ambient condition during the thermal cycling process, which led to a mass loss after 5-time thermal cycles.  

It could be seen from  Fig. 4  that as the thermal cycle increases, the mass of the specimens with SAPS – TBCs gradually increase, about 0.26% mass gain after 150 cycles. Furthermore, the corresponding mass change curve turns to follow a linear law with very low slope of 0.0012. Coated with SPAS – TBCs, the thermal shock resistance of nickel-based superalloy could be signi ﬁ cantly improved. The improved mechanism will be discussed below.  

# 3.3. Adhesion  

The micro-scratch experiment was employed to evaluate the adhesive strength between the coating and substrate. A typical scratch model and the real obtained curve of as-sprayed specimen are shown in  Fig. 5 . In principle, when the indent moves in a homogeneous medium material, a relatively stable depth – scratch curve can be obtained (such as the curve from point  m  to point  n  shown in  Fig. 5 b). When the indent encounters with another homogeneous medium, the slope of the curve will change. It is clear to record the corresponding load related to the turning point from the scratch length – load – depth curve ( Fig. 5 b). Nevertheless, a temporary transition or platform sometimes appears (located around the point  p  in Fig. 5 b) on the curve, which means that the indent gets across a protruding zone near the interface between two coatings.

# 4. Discussion  

The ZrO 2 – CoNiCrAlY double-layer coating system shows high thermal shock resistance to protect the nickel-based superalloy from oxidation. In order to clarify the protection mechanism, the morphology of coating and interface was observed.  

Fig. 7  shows the cross-section morphologies of four selective samples after different-time thermal cycles. It was dif ﬁ cult to identify clearly the TC – BC interface for as-sprayed specimens even under high magni ﬁ ed visual  ﬁ eld ( Fig. 7 b), from which a reciprocal locked interface was formed, which was bene ﬁ t to improve the interface bond capacity. This also agrees with the scratch results shown in  Fig. 6 . After 50-time thermal cycles, the TGO grew rapidly with a thickness about 2  m m. Whereas, the TGO thickness was found to become somewhat thicker after 102 cycles ( Fig. 7 e and f) and subsequently become thinner after 150 cycles ( Fig. 7 g and h).

# 5. Conclusions  

Double-layer coatings, the top ZrO 2  coating and the CoN- iCrAlY bond boating, were produced by the SAPS technique. The as-received SAPS – TBCs possess excellent thermal shock resistance between 1200  1 C and room temperature, and could ef ﬁ ciently protect nickel-based superalloy from oxidation with only 0.26% mass gain after 150 thermal cycles. The as-sprayed coatings  ﬁ rmly locked with the substrate, presenting relatively high bond strength. The coating adhesion decreases with the increasing of thermal shock cycles. The critical load for TC spallation was reduced by 32% (to 50-time thermal cycles) and for BC by 35% (to 150-time thermal cycles). The little mass gain of the coated samples during thermal shock tests mainly resulted from the oxidation of BC. As a result, the Al 2 O 3  TGO and some fast-growing mixed oxides formed, which intro- duced huge stress at TC – BC interface leading to a decline for adhesion of the coatings.
