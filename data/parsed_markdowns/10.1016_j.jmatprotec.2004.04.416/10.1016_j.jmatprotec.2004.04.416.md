# Prediction of nickel-base superalloys’ rheological behaviour under hot forging conditions using artiﬁcial neural networks  

P.F. Bariani, S. Bruschi ∗ , T. Dal Negro  

DIMEG, University of Padova, Via Venezia 1, 35131 Padova, Italy Received 20 February 2003; received in revised form 19 April 2004; accepted 26 April 2004  

# Abstract  

In this paper neural networks are utilised to represent the rheological behaviour of the Nickel-base superalloy Nimonic 80A under deformation conditions approximating thermo-mechanical cycles of industrial hot forging operations. A feed-forward back-propagation neural network has been trained and tested on rheological data, obtained from hot compression experiments, performed under single- and multi-step deformation conditions, both at constant and varying strain rate. The good agreement between experimental and calculated ﬂow curves shows that a properly trained neural network can be successfully employed in representing material response to hot forging cycles.  

Keywords:  Hot forging; Flow stress; Neural network  

# 1. Introduction  

In the last decade, many attempts have been made to model rheological behaviour of metals in hot forg- ing operations. The material response to hot deformation conditions is affected not only by the current values of the process parameters—equivalent strain, strain rate and temperature—but also by thermal and mechanical cycles before the current deformation step as well as by contin- uous variations of strain rate and temperature inside the deformation step  [1–4] .  

Currently, material behaviour is modelled through consti- tutive equations, taking into account only the dependence to the instantaneous values of process parameters; the material constants of these equations are usually calculated through non-linear regression analysis. Even under this approxima- tion of operating conditions, the deﬁnition of correct analyt- ical relationships involves the clear understanding of all the phenomena occurring while deforming—such as strain hard- ening, dynamic recovery, dynamic recrystallization—that, particularly in hot forging, are complex and not easy to model.  

Neural networks, instead, can be employed in representing material rheological behaviour under hot forging conditions, when a complete modelling in terms of material response dependence on previous histories is needed. They can repre- sent an alternative tool to describe material behaviour as the knowledge a priori of the phenomena and their interactions during forging is not needed when used to represent rheo- logical data  [5–7] . Recently, this technique has been applied to many areas where modelling is too complex and highly non-linear, as in several areas of manufacturing—process planning, process monitoring and control, tool design, etc. [8,9] . However, even if some applications of neural networks to predict metals ﬂow stress have been proposed in the last years  [6,7,10,11] , very few studies are recordable in scien- tiﬁc literature that utilise neural networks to describe ma- terial behaviour under varying hot deformation conditions. Material response in terms of ﬂow strength or microstruc- ture is predicted in case of well-deﬁned deformation condi- tions, mainly single-step deformation conditions; but, even if these operative conditions are near to those of conven- tional rheological testing, they can be very different from those of industrial hot forging operations  [1,2] .  

Therefore, the main objective of this work is to investigate the feasibility of utilising neural networks to predict mate- rial response under whatever processing conditions, demon- strating that neural networks can represent an alternative tool to constitutive equations in describing material behaviour in hot forging.  

The paper presents in its ﬁrst part the experiments for the generation of rheological data for the network training, conducted on the Nickel-base superalloy Nimonic 80A; then the development of the neural network model in terms of

# 2. Experimental procedures  

In the following the procedures to generate rheological data for the network training are presented. The rheologi- cal behaviour of the Nickel-base superalloy Nimonic 80A (its chemical composition is shown in  Table 1 ) in hot form- ing conditions has been investigated through three kinds of physical simulation experiments conducted on real-material samples.  

•  Single-step compression experiments, carried out at con- stant values of strain rate and aimed at evaluating material sensitivity to process parameters.

 •  Two-step compression experiments, consisting of a two-stage upsetting with an intermediate soaking time; these experiments are aimed at evaluating the inﬂuence on the material ﬂow strength of the previous thermal and mechanical history represented by the amount of strain of the ﬁrst deformation step and by heating and soaking time.

 •  Varying strain rate compression experiments, consisting of a single upsetting carried out with a continuous variation of strain rate according to deﬁned paths; these experiments are aimed at evaluating the inﬂuence of strain rate histories on the instantaneous value of the material ﬂow strength.  

All the experiments have been carried out on the com- puter-controlled dynamic system Gleeble TM   3800 equipped with Hydra-Wedge TM , capable to assure very accurate con- trol of strain and strain rate along the whole deformation stroke. The specimen is resistance heated and temperature is controlled through thermocouples welded on its surface. A layer made of alternate foils of graphite and tantalum is interposed between specimen and punches, preventing fric- tion and thus barreling and acting as thermal barrier.  

Samples employed in testing are cylindrical of 12.8 mm diameter and 14 mm long, machined from a drawn bar of 12.8 mm diameter.  

During single-step experiments, Nimonic 80A samples are heated up to testing temperature at 15   ◦ C/s, held at this temperature for 30 s and then compressed ( Fig. 1  on the left). The two-step experiment is characterised by the same heating rate and soaking time, but with different amount of strain and strain rate in the two steps; between the ﬁrst and the second steps, the sample is held in temperature for 30 s ( Fig. 1  on the right).

For fast optimisation, the Levenberg–Marquardt training algorithm has been utilised  [13,14] . The training typology is the batch mode: the network weights and biases are updated only after the entire training set has been applied to the network.  

Network inputs have been chosen closely related to pro- cess parameters. Instantaneous values of equivalent strain, strain rate and temperature have been ﬁrst employed as network inputs: strain and strain rate as logarithmic func- tions, ln( ε i ) and ln( ε i ), while temperature as inverse function (1/ T i ), taking into account the analytical relationship exist- ing among the considered process parameters  [7] .  

The effects of the previous thermal and mechanical cycles on material ﬂow stress have been taken into account adding to the network inputs the microstructural parameter grain size that has been proven to suitably represent this inﬂuence [15] .  

To improve the accuracy of the network predictions in the case of varying strain rate experiments, time-dependent pro- cess parameters  ˙ i − 1  and  ˙ i − 2 , as logarithmic functions, have been added as network inputs, representing varying strain rate material response  [12] . The equivalent stress is the only output. For the network training, the following deformation conditions have been utilised:  

•  ten conditions representative of single-step experiments;

 •  seven conditions representative of multi-step experiments;

 •  thirteen conditions representative of varying strain rate experiments (eight for always increasing strain rate and ﬁve for decreasing strain rate experiments).  

For each set of experimental data, 20 points have been picked at strain steps of 0.05. Therefore, 600 training it- erations have been repeatedly presented to the network for training.

# 5. Conclusions  

In this paper, the feasibility of utilising neural networks in modelling material rheological behaviour under hot de- formation conditions have been evaluated.  

The main advantage of this approach is that neural net- works can infer the complex and highly non-linear relation- ships between input process parameters and the output ma- terial ﬂow strength, without requiring any physical under- standing of the process.  

The developed neural network allows to give predictions of Nimonic 80A rheological behaviour under whatever pro- cessing conditions. The mean error in predicting material response is less than 6% in each testing case. The results on the agreement between experimental and calculated ﬂow curves demonstrate that properly trained neural networks can represent a valid alternative to constitutive equations to describe material behaviour under hot forging conditions.
