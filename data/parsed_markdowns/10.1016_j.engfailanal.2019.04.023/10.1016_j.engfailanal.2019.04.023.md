# A B S T R A C T  

Thermo mechanical fatigue (TMF) experiment with dwell times on a nickel-based superalloy turbine blade was conducted. The strain  ﬁ eld and temperature  ﬁ eld of the test section were simulated well through adjusting gripping  ﬁ xture and induction coil. In addition to the loading, heating, cooling and control subsystems, high temperature strain measurement subsystem was introduced into the experiment to ensure the accuracy of the simulation results of service con- dition for the test section. In the whole experiment, the service condition of the test section was reproduced. The experiment result showed that TMF crack initiated at the trailing edge of the test section and propagated along the leading edge direction. Using the scanning electron microscope (SEM), multiple crack sources at the surface of turbine blade were observed and the crack surface was oxidized seriously. In addition, based on the fractographic and metallographic observation, the mixed features of intergranular fracture and transgranular fracture were found. The inter- action of oxidation, creep damage and fatigue damage is an important reason for the TMF failure of the turbine blade.  

# 1. Introduction  

Turbine blades are subjected to the combined action of varying mechanical loads induced by the centrifugal force and thermal loads caused by the high temperature gas at service [ 1 , 2 ]. As a consequence, thermo mechanical fatigue (TMF) becomes one of the main failure modes for the turbine blades, which is the dominant life limiting factor to be considered in the design of the turbine blades [ 3 – 5 ]. Therefore, the investigation on the TMF failure by performing TMF experiments is of special interest in the gas turbine industry. Generally, mechanical properties and damage mechanisms for the turbine blade's material under thermo mechanical loading are experimentally investigated based on standard specimens [ 6 – 18 ] or blade-shaped specimens [ 19 , 20 ]. However, mechanical properties of these specimens are di ﬀ erent from the actual turbine blades due to the di ﬀ erent manufacturing process as well as the structural complexity of full-scale turbine blade, thus revealing a deviation in the fatigue life [ 21 – 23 ]. In order to investigate the TMF failure of actual turbine blades, a TMF test rig with loading, heating, cooling and control subsystems was proposed in our previous work [ 22 , 23 ], in which mechanical load of the test section was adjusted discretely by changing the combination of spacers in the joints. To adjust the mechanical load of the test section continuously, the gripping  ﬁ xture was improved in this paper. In addition,

# 2. Experimental details  

# 2.1. Material and specimen  

The specimen of interest is a low pressure gas turbine blade (see  Fig. 1 ) made of the nickel-based superalloy K444 [ 24 ]. The turbine blade su ﬀ ered a solution heat treatment at 1170 °C for 4 h, and then air-cooled. Following the solution heat treatment, the turbine blade underwent an intermediate heat treatment at 1050 °C for 4 h, and then air-cooled. Finally, the turbine blade experi- enced an aging heat treatment at 850 °C for 16 h, and then air-cooled. Main chemical compositions (in wt%) of K444 are listed in Table 1 .  

# 2.2. Experimental method  

The most dangerous section of the turbine blade is generally selected to conduct the experiment in the laboratory [ 23 – 25 ]. During simulation for the service condition for the test section, several test points of interest were selected and measured. Generally speaking, when the test results of the room temperature strains (or stresses) and temperatures for all of the test points were close to the real results, the service condition of the whole test section were thought to be simulated well [ 23 – 25 ]. Considering that the turbine blades operated at high temperature during the service, high temperature strain measurement subsystem was introduced to ensure the accuracy of the simulation results of service condition. The detailed process for the TMF experiment on a turbine blade is illustrated in  Fig. 2 .  

# 2.3. Simulation results of service condition  

In this paper, the temperature and strain distributions of the turbine blade under service condition are calculated using thermal and mechanical analysis. The positions and calculated results of the test points are shown in  Fig. 3  and  Table 2 .  

The mechanical load was applied by a computer-controlled servo-hydraulic testing machine together with a developed gripping ﬁ xture. In our previous work, joints were introduced into the gripping  ﬁ xture, and the discrete adjustment of the mechanical load was achieved through changing the combination of spacers in the joints [ 22 , 23 ]. However, this method can not adjust the mechanical load of the tested section continuously, and the  ﬁ xture needs to be reassembled at each adjustment process. In view of this, the adjusting screw was introduced to the joints, thus mechanical load of the tested section could be adjusted continuously without reassembling the gripping  ﬁ xture. In addition, an internal cooling passage in the gripping  ﬁ xture was designed to simulate the internal cooling process of the turbine blade. The details of adjusting screw and internal cooling inlet are shown in  Fig. 4 . During the strain  ﬁ eld (room temperature) adjustment, the testing machine provides uniaxial load, and the gripping  ﬁ xture simulates the ad- ditional bending moment through changing the screw-in depth of the adjusting screw. As a consequence, the strain distribution of the test section in laboratory conditions is close to the actual strain distribution under centrifugal force. The absolute errors of the peak strains (room temperature) for all test points were less than 5% (see  Fig. 5 ), demonstrating the developed loading device simulates the actual mechanical load for the turbine blade well.  

The thermal load was imposed by a high-frequency induction heater with a special induction coil. It is di ﬃ cult to simulate the temperature distribution of the test section due to the complex structure and temperature gradient. In our previous study [ 22 , 23 ], a 2D induction coil similar to the geometry of the test section is applied. However, when using this kind of 2D induction coil in this

# 2.4. TMF experiment description  

The experiment was conducted under stress control. Test point 5 was selected as the control point. The stress range of test point 5 was 15.4 – 154.0 MPa. During the loading process and unloading process, the stress rate was 4.62 MPa/s and  − 4.62 MPa/s, re- spectively. The dwell times at  T  = 818 °C were 30 s and the dwell times at  T  = 200 °C were 10 s. To meet the need of rapid cooling procedure in the TMF cycle and the internal cooling process of the turbine blade, an air compressor was used to provide su ﬃ cient compressed air. The internal cooling air  ﬂ ow was 2 g/s. The stress load was controlled precisely by the testing machine. To maintain the mechanical load and the thermal load synchronized and stable, a load synchronizer was used to control them. When the stress load changed, the load synchronizer controlled temperature load synchronously change with the stress load. The load spectrum of test point 5 is shown in  Fig. 10 . As we can see, the stress load and temperature load are in a good synchronization. During the experiment, a long-focus microscope with a 2  μ m resolution was used to monitor the surface of turbine blade. And the experiment was

# 3. Results and discussion  

# 3.1. Location of crack  

As shown in  Fig. 12 , the crack initiated at the trailing edge of the test section and propagated along the leading edge direction. Further observation shows that there were also cracks in the  ﬁ lm-cooling hole of the test section (see  Fig. 13 ). The propagation of the main crack and the low roundness of the  ﬁ lm-cooling hole caused the increase of the e ﬀ ective stress and the degree of stress concentration respectively, which accelerated the cracking of the  ﬁ lm-cooling hole.

Referring to [ 23 ], fractographic sample with well-preserved crack surface was obtained. As shown in  Fig. 14 , there were three regions on the fractographic sample and the region enclosed by red curves indicated the well-preserved crack surface. Compared with the other two regions, the crack surface was dark because of oxidation.  

SEM was employed to observe the TMF crack surface on the fractographic sample. It can be seen that almost everywhere of the TMF crack surface was covered by oxidation layer (see  Fig. 15  (a)), indicating that the oxidation might play an important role during the TMF crack initiation and propagation, which is also is con ﬁ rmed in the references [ 26 ,  27 ]. Three metallographic samples were observed under SEM, and the SEM photos were shown in  Fig. 19 . There were obvious intergranular cracks on the surface of sample 1 (see  Fig. 19  (a)), while there were no cracks on the surface of specimen 3 (see  Fig. 19 (b)). It indicated that the intergranular cracks initiated under the TMF loading. In general, the intergranular cracks are typical features of high temperature creep damage [ 29 ]. Although the results in the reference [ 27 ] show that the intergranular fracture may also be caused by the coupling e ﬀ ect of oxidation and fatigue under TMF loading, the intergranular cracks on metallographic samples can not be a ﬀ ected by oxidation because they are inside the specimens before grinding and polishing. Therefore, the intergranular cracks are caused by creep damage under TMF load with the dwell time. The surface of sample 2 also had intergranular cracks (see Fig. 19  (c) and  Fig. 19  (d)). And the number of intergranular cracks increased with the decrease of the distance from the tested section. Moreover, the closer to the tested section, the longer the intergranular cracks were.

The TMF experiment on an actual turbine blade was conducted based on a developed TMF experimental system. Furthermore, the failure mechanism was investigated through fractographic and metallographic observation under SEM. The main conclusions are summarized as follows:  

(1) The TMF experimental system for turbine blade was improved through improving the gripping  ﬁ xture and introducing high

 (2) TMF crack initiated at the trailing edge of the test section and propagated along the leading edge direction. The fracture analysis showed there were multiple crack sources at the surface of the test section, and the deep pits which are prone to stress con- centration were found on turbine blade surface. The deep pits might be one of the causes of crack initiation, therefore the surface quality has an important impact on TMF damage of turbine blade.

 (3) The TMF crack surface was dark because of oxidation, and almost everywhere of the crack surface was covered by oxidation layer, which indicated that the oxidation might play an important role during the TMF crack initiation and propagation. Moreover, both transgranular fracture (typical features of fatigue damage) and intergranular fracture (typical features of creep damage) were observed at the crack surface. Specially, the intergranular cracks were found on the surface of metallographic samples. Therefore, it is reasonable to believe that the interaction of oxidation, creep damage and fatigue damage results in the TMF failure of turbine blade.
