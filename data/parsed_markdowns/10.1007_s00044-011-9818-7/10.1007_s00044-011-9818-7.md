# The application of comparative molecular ﬁeld analysis for the design of  a -anilino substituted-3-phenyl propanoic acids as novel PPAR a / c  dual ligands  

Raman K. Verma •   Prithwish Ghosh •     Vijay Kumar •   Lalit K. Wadhwa  

Received: 28 June 2011 / Accepted: 11 October 2011   Springer Science+Business Media, LLC 2011  

Abstract Peroxisome proliferator-activated receptors (PPARs) are members of the nuclear hormone receptor family. Ligands which bind to PPAR a  and PPAR c  receptors are involved in the pathophysiology of type 2 diabetes mellitus. PPAR a / c  dual agonists provide superior therapy through lipid and glucose control simultaneously. Com- parative molecular ﬁeld analysis was applied to a set of PPAR a / c  dual activators to build three 3D-QSAR models which have been utilised for the design and prediction of novel PPAR ligands. Signiﬁcant models, namely,  a -model, c -model and dual model depending on the sum of the activities of the compounds at the individual receptors; were developed with  r 2 cv  [  0.5,  r 2 ncv [  0.9 and lower values of standard error of estimate. The predictive efﬁcacy of the models was established by the activity prediction of a test set and was observed to be of reasonable accuracy. New molecules were designed by analysis of the contours, obtained from the three models. Subsequent prediction of activities and docking at PPAR c  active site of the designed molecules gave good results compared to other standard PPAR dual agonists.

# Introduction  

Peroxisome proliferator-activated receptors (PPAR) have gained importance over the past recent years in the area of type 2 diabetes drug research, due to the discovery of their physiological roles in the regulation of glucose and lipid homeostasis (Wilson  et al .,  2000 ). The distinct tissue dis- tribution and physiological roles of PPARs are well docu- mented (Gani,  2008 ). PPAR a  mainly controls the expression of genes involved in  b -oxidation of fatty acids (Raalte  et al ., 2004 ), and activation of PPAR a  with endogenous or syn- thetic ligands reduces triglycerides and elevates high density lipoprotein cholesterol levels.Activation of PPAR c  is known to improve insulin sensitivity and thereby control glucose homeostasis (Feige  et al .,  2006 ). Two classes of compounds, namely, thiazolidine dione s (popular as glitazones, e.g. ros- iglitazone and pioglitazone, Scheme  1 ) as antidiabetic agents (Lehman  et al .,  1995 ; Hulin  et al .,  1996 ) and ﬁbrates as anti hyper lipid emi c agents (Robins  et al .,  2001 ; Naderali et al .,  2004 ; Ye  et al .,  2001 ) are currently marketed and are PPAR c  and PPAR a  agonists, respectively. However, treat- ment with glitazones is associated with cardiovascular, hepatic and other adverse effects like weight gain and edema, whereas ﬁbrates are poor activators of PPAR a  and high dose is needed to be administered to exert therapeutic effect (Krische,  2000 ; Alwai  et al .,  2004 ).  

Development of multiple targets modulating agents is very challenging as the molecules should have afﬁnity simultaneously for all the aimed macromolecular targets with similar binding pockets, and ﬁne tuning of the sub- stituents is required to obtain the optimum potency that ﬁnally may result in superior activity proﬁle and lessen the hazards of adverse effects as compared to the selective ligands. As this metabolic syndrome is known to be associated with defects in glucose as well as lipid metabolism, the concept of discovering dual agonists, which can activate both PPAR a  and PPAR c simultaneously has emerged as an interesting target because of a logical hypothesis that these dual agonists may not only control both glucose and lipid levels but also mitigate the weight gain induced by PPAR c  activation based on the observation that ﬁbrates in addition to their hypolipidemic effects, reduce body weight gain in rodents without affecting food intake (Carmona  et al .,  2005 ; Chaput  et al .,  2000 ). Several PPAR dual agonists, commonly termed as glitazars, were developed by various pharmaceutical companies. Computational methods can be effectively employed in designing such multiple receptor activating ligands, and X-ray crystal structural information of the 3D-geometry of targeted receptors is advantageous for this special effort.  

In this article, we report the development of three 3D QSAR models using CoMFA with the help of a series of N-substituted tyrosine-based molecules and  a -alkoxy/aryl- oxy-3-phenyl propanoic acid derivatives, for which activity of the single enantiomer at individual receptor site is reported, for the predictions of PPAR a , PPAR c  and PPAR a / c - dual activities of these classes of PPAR ligands and design of a set of new chemical entities whilst analysing the steric and electrostatic contours of the developed models. Moreover, the activities of the designed molecules have been predicted and their docking studies were carried out in the crystal structure of PPAR c  protein.  

# Materials and methods  

Molecular modelling, CoMFA and docking were per- formed using Sybyl (Sybyl,  2006 ) version 7.3 installed on RHEL5 workstation at our in silico Drug Design Lab.  

# Data set  

A set of thirty-four agonists of PPAR a  as well as PPAR c receptor subtypes including eighteen  a -alkoxy propanoic acid and ﬁve  a -aryloxy propanoic acid derivatives and eleven tyrosine-based molecules (Sauerberg  et al .,  2002 ; Xu  et al .,  2004 ; Liu  et al .,  2001a ,  b ; Collins  et al .,  1998 ; Mogensen  et al .,  2003 ) with their activities of single enantiomer was selected from the literature by an exhaustive survey as there is limited conﬁguration-based activity data available for PPAR ligands. The structures of the compounds with their corresponding in vitro human PPAR transactivation assay activity in terms of EC 50  are listed in Table  1  (part a and part b). The activity values have been converted into pEC 50  ( - log EC 50 ) values in molar terms wherever required and used as the dependent variables in the study. The activities of the compounds at PPAR a  constitute the dependent column for building the a -model. The activities of the compounds at PPAR c  con- stitute the dependent column for building the  c -model. The sum of the pEC 50  values for PPAR a  and PPAR c  have been taken as dual pEC 50  value for each compound, the dependent parameter for developing the dual model (Khanna  et al .,  2005 ; Sundriyal and Bharatam  2009 ). These molecules are composed of three major fragments: (i) the left hand side heterocycle, (ii) the linker and (iii) the right hand side  N -substituted tyrosine or  a -alkoxy/aryloxy-3- phenyl propanoic acid. The set of 34 molecules (Table  1 ) were sorted into a training set of 26 molecules and a test set of 5 molecules in the process of model reﬁnement and internal prediction of training set and test set for develop- ing all the three models. The training set was so chosen that identical set of molecules can be used for building the three 2  in each case. models with the most signiﬁcant values of  r cv Three molecules  4a ,  8a ,  26a  did not ﬁt well into either of the training sets and were dropped. The reported SAR studies also include a compound ( 26d ) for which the pEC 50 value in PPAR a  could not be speciﬁcally ascertained, but a minimum of a range was mentioned. For this molecule, the reported minimum value was employed in building the models.  

Generating the molecular structures and alignment  

All the dataset molecules were built onto energy minimised GI 262570/Farglitazar (extracted from the Ligand Binding Domain of 1FM9) using the ‘Sketch module’ whilst maintaining the U-shape essential for activity (Nolte  et al ., 1998 ). These were subjected to energy optimisation by Powell method after assigning Gasteiger–Marsili charges (Gasteiger and Marsili,  1978 ).

CoMFA 3D QSAR modelling  

CoMFA was performed using the QSAR option of SYB-   3 ˚ and a YL. A  sp carbon atom with a radius of 1.53 A charge of  ? 1.0 was used as a probe to calculate the steric and electrostatic energies between the probe and the mol- ecules using the Tripos force ﬁeld (Clark  et al .,  1989 ). The truncation for both the steric and the electrostatic energies were set to 30 kcal/mol.  

Docking studies  

Molecular docking studies of the designed new molecules (DNMs) were performed along with the template as well as some other reported PPAR dual activators into the PPAR c (PDB entry 1FM9) active site using Surﬂex dock program interfaced with SYBYL 7.3. The 3D coordinates of the active sites were taken from the X-ray crystal structure of PPAR c  (1FM9) reported as complex with the correspond- ing ligand Farglitazar (20) (Gampe  et al .,  2000 ). The active site was deﬁned as the area within 6.5 A ´ around the co- crystallised ligand. The DNMs along with the standard molecules were submitted to docking run to get the results in terms of docking score. The H-bonding interactions of the docked molecules with the active site were also found. The  G -score, PMF-score,  D -score and Chem-scores have also been explored to reveal the comparative differences in the binding interactions of the molecules at the active site of PPAR c .

# Result and discussion  

The major objective of the analysis was to build good predictive models that can be effectively used for designing novel PPAR activators. The dataset molecules have been so chosen that they cover a broad range of activity. The training and test set molecules align well with the template (Figs.  2 ,  3 ). In the CoMFA models, steric and electrostatic ﬁelds contribute to the QSAR equations by 58.5 and 41.5 in case of  a -model; 64.8 and 35.2 in case of  c -model and 57.5 and 42.5 in case of dual model in terms of percent relative contribution (Table  2 ). These CoMFA models were used to predict the respective pEC 50  values of the training set and test set molecules (Tables  3 ,  4 ). The contours of all the three models have been studied, and the optimum features for designing the new chemical entities were selected depending upon the observation of the favourable and unfavourable overlaps of the individual training set molecules with the steric and electrostatic contours of each model. Structural variations have been carried out on to the tyrosine scaffold, as that of the template, being kept intact, whilst designing the new molecules.  

The  a -carbon was kept unsubstituted, as substitution at this site was not an indispensable criterion for the PPAR c and overall PPAR dual activity. No ring extension or substitution was made at C 5  or C 6  positions (yellow maps, Fig.  5 a, c) of the ﬁrst benzene ring (linked to the nitrogen atom) on the right hand side as substitution at these sites may cause loss of PPAR a  and overall PPAR dual activity. A methyl group was placed at the 2-position of the same benzene ring, and the ketonic function was removed (which eventually removed the second benzene ring) to satisfy the need of a small electron releasing group at the site according to the steric and electronic requirements for the improvement of the activities (Fig.  6 ). Substitution with an isopropyl group (group of medium bulk) at the 3-position satisﬁes steric requirements of the  c - and dual models (Fig.  5 b, c). The steric interaction between the methyl and isopropyl groups pushes the isopropyl group somewhat towards the 4-position accounting for the steric require- ment of the  a -model (Fig.  5 a). Substitution with an iso- propyl group at the 4-position was also done to observe steric effects on the activities. The blue canopy over the middle linker part of the template in all the three models indicated saturated linker chain is favourable for both the activities. Therefore, two carbon saturated linker chain was kept intact whilst designing the new molecules. Tricyclic heterocycle was chosen for the left hand side part to follow the large green contour in all the three models for the design of new molecules (Table  5 ). Four different types of heterocycles were employed for satisfying the electronic requirements of the models for the improvement of the activities. The tiny red electrostatic contour (covered under the large blue canopy in all the three cases, Fig.  6 ) at the backside of the nitrogen atom of the oxazole ring of the phenyl oxazole moiety of the template in all the three models have been accounted for by the oxygen atom of the central ring of the tricyclic heterocycle in series II and III designed molecules and the ketonic oxygen of the same ring in series I designed molecules. As the contribution of this particular contour is very small to the overall ﬁeld, a tricyclic unit satisfying no such requirement was used in the series IV DNCEs to see the effect of ignoring the feature onto the activities. A small and electron rich group, triﬂuoromethyl, substituted at the nitrogen atom of the central ring of the tricyclic nucleus in series I, II and IV of the designed molecules, was kept to account for the pyra- midal red contour and the yellow contour that appeared in front of the oxygen atom of the oxazole ring of the tem- plate molecule (Fig.  8 ). # Conclusions  

Although there are several constraints in building a conﬁguration-based model for PPAR dual agonists, we obtained three 3D QSAR CoMFA models with statistical signiﬁcance and good predictive abilities.  

The proposed models gave important indications for structural variations for designing new chemical entities for PPAR agonistic activity. The contours of the models thus obtained were explored to design novel PPAR agonists. Moreover, PPAR activities of these newly designed mol- ecules were predicted with the developed models, and docking studies were carried out at the crystal structure of PPAR c  active site. Finally, some of them were selected as reasonably good, novel PPAR a / c  dual activators by com- paring the predicted activities and docking scores with respect to the standard molecules. These efforts will guide synthetic medicinal chemists to design new compounds and provide further insights to support structure-based design of anti-type 2 diabetic drugs to develop novel PPAR dual agonists with improved potency and safety proﬁles.
