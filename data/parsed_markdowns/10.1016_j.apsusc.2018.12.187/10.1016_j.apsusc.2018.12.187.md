# A R T I C L E I N F O  

# A B S T R A C T  

Generally, transient liquid phase (TLP) bonding is conducted with an overdose of filler metal in a vacuum furnace that necessitates excessive post machining. Recently, a new form of filler metal, the sintered brazing preforms (SBP) has been introduced to achieve a neat TLP bonding joint, which undergoes partial melting and retains its original shape. This paper studies the microstructure and mechanical properties of induction TLP bonding (IB) of the Inconel 718 using nickel-based SBP. Firstly, the surface roughness effect on the tensile bonding strength of the brazed joint was investigated. Further, a comparative study between IB and furnace TLP bonding (FB) on the microstructure and mechanical properties of the TLP joints was performed. The results clearly show that neat bonding joints can be obtained through the IB of Inconel 718 using nickel-based SBP. Besides, the strongest tensile bonding strength (240.76 MPa) of the SBP joint after IB was achieved with an optimal bonding surface roughness of around 0.9 μm ( R a ). The strength was approximately 16% higher than that of the SBP joint after FB. Furthermore, the IB could accelerate the diffusion process, leading to fewer porosities and borides in the SBP, and a lesser amount of precipitates in the precipitation layer than that after FB.  

Keywords: Induction TLP bonding Microstructure and mechanical properties Inconel 718 alloy Sintered brazing preform  

# 1. Introduction

Inconel 718 alloy is one of the widely used aerospace materials due to its excellent mechanical properties, corrosion resistance and oxida- tion resistance at high temperatures. After years of services under high temperature and high pressure environments, often some of the aero- space components experience in-service damages, such as cracks and erosions, and hence potentially result in a reduction of their perfor- mance and service life. Generally, gas tungsten arc welding (GTAW) has been widely used to repair these damaged components during Maintenance, Repair & Overhaul (MRO). However, intergranular and interdendritic cracks are often found in the fusion zone (FZ) and heat affected zone (HAZ) of these welds  [1] . Besides, high residual stresses are accumulated around the FZ and its adjacent areas. The high tem- perature arc during the TIG welding process also worsens the base material microstructures and mechanical properties adjacent to the welding area. To address these issues, transient liquid phase (TLP) bonding (also known as diffusion brazing) was explored, which  

TLP bonding is a relatively new joining process which produces a strong joint mainly due to the diffusion bonding, where the liquid in- terlayer forms at a lower temperature compared to base metal (BM) due to the inclusion of melting point depressants (MPDs) based on the binary alloy system, causing isothermal solidification  [3] . A number of research works have been devoted to investigating TLP bonding since Owczarski et al. patented this technology  [4–6] . The bonding time is an important parameter which has significant effects on the mechanical properties of TLP bonding joints. Many studies have been conducted on the bonding time effects on the mechanical properties of the TLP bonding joints of different alloys with varieties of filler metals  [7–11] . The vacuum furnace which has been traditionally used for these TLP bonding requires at least a few hours to heat up the component to the bonding temperature  [1,12] . Though the mechanical properties of the joints might be greatly improved with longer TLP bonding time due to

In comparison to furnace heating, the induction heating provides an efficient heating rate of more than 100 °C/s and minimizes the ad- verse effects on the BM. Besides, induction heating achieves highly localized heating (skin effect) and precise control of the bonding cycles [1] .  

Generally, excessive post machining is required after the TLP bonding with an overdose of filler metal  [13,14] . Thus, there is an in- creasing demand for near-net-shape manufacturing of a neat TLP bonding joint with minimum or no post machining. To solve this pro- blem, the sintered brazing preform (SBP), alternatively known as “pre- sintered preform (PSP)”, was developed  [15] . The SBP is a new sintered powder product which is a homogeneous mixture of the metallic alloy powders, preferably having similar characteristics as the BM, and brazing alloy powders. Unlike the traditional filler metal, the SBP is not fully melted during the TLP bonding process, thereby its original shape and dimensions can be ideally preserved after bonding. Thus, it can be adopted to dimensionally restore the critical components such as vanes, blades and repair cracks. TLP bonding using SBP as filler metal requires minimum to no post processing, which enables a relatively efficient, cost-effective and reliable repair technology. Despite all these ad- vantages, the research on induction TLP bonding (IB) of the Inconel 718 with the nickel-based SBP is still scarce.  

This paper aims at studying the microstructure and mechanical properties of induction TLP bonding of Inconel 718 with the nickel- based SBP. A new form of filler metal, nickel-based SBP was introduced to join the Inconel 718 through induction heating. The effect of surface roughness on the tensile bonding strength of the IB Inconel 718 joint using SBP was studied. Additionally, the microstructure and mechanical properties of the SBP sample after IB was investigated. The IB effects on the phase evolution of SBP, grain size distribution of SBP and bonded BM were analysed. Moreover, tensile fractographies of the joints after IB was investigated. Finally, the results were analysed and compared to that after furnace TLP bonding (FB).

# 3. Results and discussion  

# 3.1. Bonding surface roughness  

Surface roughness of the bonding surface of BM plays an important role in the TLP bonding process due to its non-neglectable effect on the diffusion and spreadability of filler metal. Most of these previous re- search focused on the surface roughness effect on the mechanical properties of the brazing or diffusion bonding joints, rather than the TLP bonding joint  [21–24] . So far, no research work has been reported on the influence of surface roughness on the mechanical properties of the IB joints of Inconel 718 with nickel-based SBP.  

The effect of bonding surface roughness on the tensile bonding strength of the induction SBP joints was studied in order to realize the optimal bonding surface. The roughness of the bonding surface after different surface treatments is shown in  Table 2 . The surface conditions vary from rough grinding to fine polishing using different grades of abrasive sandpapers and polishing cloths with polishing liquid as de- scribed in  Table 2 .  Fig. 3  compares the surface contour profiles (with a 300% magnification) corresponding to the different surface conditions. Besides, the bonding surface profiles acquired by OM are shown in Fig. 4 . It can be noticed from  Fig. 5 (a) that there was a thick oxidation layer on the rugged as-received surface. The depth of the surface crack is found to be decreasing as the surface roughness decreases from the surface A to D, as seen in  Fig. 5 . A mirror-like surface finish was achieved for surface E after polishing of surface D with × G1200 SiC sandpapers and polishing cloth (Chem) with colloidal silica suspension (OPS, 0.04 μm).  

Fig. 5  shows the tensile bonding strengths of the SBP joints with the bonding surfaces undergone different surface treatments. SBP joints with the as-received surface exhibited a weak bonding strength of 5.21 MPa, as seen in  Fig. 5 . A significant amount of oxidation humps on the as-received bonding surface weakened the diffusion process of TLP bonding. In addition, the porosities around the bonding interface de- crease the bonding strength. Thus, the as-received Inconel 718 surface was found to be unsuitable for bonding with the SBP. As the roughness ( R a ) decreased from 1.63 μm ( R a ,  surface A) to 0.90 μm ( R a ,  surface C),

the tensile bonding strength of the bonded joints increased from around 96.36 MPa to 240.76 MPa. The bonding strength improvement along with a reduction in surface roughness could be associated with the ability to achieve a full contact between BM and SBP as the peaks and valleys flattened out correspondingly. However, the bonding strength decreased to 137.40 MPa with the further reduction in the roughness of the bonding surface from 0.90 μm to 0.13 μm ( R a , surface E). The di- minishing bonding strength could be attributed to the reduction in wetting or surface tension due to the smaller contact area between the BM and the SBP as the bonding surface was getting smoother. The re- sults indicate that the strongest tensile bonding strength (240.76 MPa) of the SBP joint after IB can be achieved with an optimal bonding surface roughness of around 0.90 μm ( R a ). It is noted that the highest joint strength of the SBP joints after IB was around 16% higher than that (207.50 MPa) of the SBP joints produced by FB.  

Fig. 6  shows the macroscale tensile fractographies of the SBP joints after IB with different bonding surfaces. Two fracture modes were ob- served on the tensile fractographies of the SBP joints with different bonding surfaces; original bonding surface (OBS) failure and the bonded SBP failure. Quantitative analysis was conducted using ImageJ to study the fracture morphologies of the bonding joints. The fraction of different fracture modes is illustrated in  Table 3  and  Fig. 7 . Fracto- graphy of the SBP joint with the as-received surface revealed ( Fig. 6 (a)) that failure occurred entirely (100%) at the OBS, where large porosities and thick oxidation layers weakened the diffusion process of bonding between BM and the SBP, resulting in a weak bonding strength. For surface A, which has the bonding strength 18 times higher than that of the as-received surface, the fracture occurred at both the OBS and SBP with a slightly larger fraction at the latter, as noticed from  Fig. 6 (b). As the roughness of the bonding surface decreased from 1.63 μm ( R a , surface A) to 0.90 μm ( R a , surface C), the fraction of the OBS failure zone reduced from 44.51% to 12.18%, respectively. This was likely influenced by the improvement of bonding wetting as well as the dif- fusion process by the decreasing oxide humps on the bonding surface. However, as the roughness of the bonding surface decreased further to 0.13 μm ( R a , surface E), the fraction of the OBS failure increased gra- dually to 43.13%. As mentioned earlier, this behaviour was likely due to the weakened diffusion as a result of the reduced contact area be- tween the BM and the SBP. For the further investigation in this study, the bonding surface roughness of 0.90 μm (surface C) which yielded the highest bonding strength was used for all the samples.

A precipitation layer (PL) was observed in the BM adjacent to the bonding interface (PL layer belongs to the BM). Fine Ni grains were observed in the PL layer as seen from the enlarged images of  Fig. 9 . However, unlike the previous studies on TLP bonding of Inconel alloy with the BNi-2 filler metal  [4,25,26] , no obvious isothermal bonding layer (IBL) with upright columnar crystals were observed in the SBP samples after both FB and IB. In addition, fine Ni 3 B grains were ob- served around the boundaries of the Ni matrix of the bonded SBP. The CrB, which was found in the area close to the bonding interface, is lesser after IB compared to that after FB. This could be due to the lower concentration of boron in this area as more boron diffused from the SBP into the BM. Similarly, fewer precipitates were found in the PL of the bonded sample after IB than that after FB. Meanwhile, new borides were nucleated and coarsened in the SBP after both FB and IB, resulting in finer borides compared to that of the as-received SBP. Moreover, IB resulted in fewer porosities at SBP, in comparison to FB. This might be attributed to the fact that IB accelerated the diffusion process, leading to fewer and smaller porosities  [27,28] .  

Fig. 10  shows the band contrast images and EDS mapping of the PL layer of the SBP joints after FB and IB (corresponding to the PL images in  Fig. 9 ). It can be observed that the precipitates in the PL of the SBP bonded samples were rich in Cr, Mo and Nb after both FB and IB – this is understandable due to the higher nominal Cr content in BM. The EDS mappings further indicate that the precipitates in the PL after FB were mainly around the grain boundaries, while the precipitates in the PL after IB were not only around the grain boundaries but also within the Ni matrix grains with a smaller size. This could be due to the fact that the induction heating accelerates the diffusion process during IB which is coupled with a higher heating and cooling rate  [27,28] , resulting in the discrete distribution of boron in the PL of the BM. The growth of Ni matrix was impeded by the Cr, Mo and Nb-enriched precipitates, leading to fine Ni grains in the PL adjacent to the bonding interface after FB and IB.  

# 3.3. Phase analysis  

Fig. 11  compares the XRD patterns of the SBP before and after bonding. Obviously, a small amount of Ni 3 Si, Ni 3 B, CrB and a large amount of Ni matrix were found in the as-received SBP and the SBP after FB and IB  [4,29,30] . Combined with the distribution of Si in the SBP before and after FB, it can be confirmed that small blocky Ni 3 Si distributed discretely in the as-received SBP and the SBP after FB. The amount of Ni 3 Si decreased in the SBP after IB, as seen in  Fig. 11 . This is due to the fact that more Si diffused from SBP into BM during IB, re- sulting in a lower concentration of Si in the SBP after IB than that after FB. Although Ni 3 Si peak was observed in the XRD spectra, however, owing to its small volume fraction, the phase was not clearly dis- tinguished in microscopy. The XRD patterns reveal a small decrease in the borides (CrB, Ni 3 B) fraction that is evident from their reduced peak intensities after IB in comparison with as-received SBP, as observed in Fig. 11 . A slight increase in the (1 2 1) boride peak after FB possibly indicates a small increase in the CrB phase fraction in SBP after FB - this could be due to the melt-back of Cr from BM to SBP during FB.  

To study the phase evolution of the SBP during FB and IB, the as- received SBP, and the faces of the SBP which are around 0.5 mm above the bonding surface after FB and IB were characterized using EBSD. It is shown in  Fig. 12  that the area fraction of borides decreased from 3.83% (as-received SBP) to 3.72% after FB and 3.05% after IB. The depletion of borides in the SBP after bonding was due to the diffusion of boron from SBP to BM. Additionally, the borides in the SBP after IB was lesser than that after FB, which could be attributed to the fact that induction heating accelerates the diffusion process of boron from SBP to BM [27,28] .  

# 3.4. Grain size distribution

# 3.5. Nanohardness  

The effect of TLP bonding on the mechanical properties was studied through the nanohardness of SBP before and after TLP bonding. Experimental measurements indicate that there was no big difference between the nanohardness profile of SBP sample after FB and IB. Thus, only the nanohardness profiles of SBP before and after IB have been analysed in this study.  Fig. 15  shows that there was no obvious dif- ference between nanohardness of the as-received BM (3.28 GPa) and the BM (excluding the PL) after IB (3.21 GPa). However, there was a significant increase in nanohardness of the material at the PL (4.45 GPa) due to the formation of Cr, Mo, and Nb-enriched boride pre- cipitates in the PL during IB. Similar to BM, nanohardness of the Ni matrix of the SBP before bonding (4.78 GPa) and after IB (4.80 GPa) is found to be similar. The nanohardness of the material around grain boundaries (GB) of SBP before (around 10.89 GPa) and after IB (around 11.84 GPa) was obviously much higher than the nanohardness in other regions. This is due to the fact that the hard and brittle borides mainly accumulated around the GB.  

# 3.6. Fractography  

The SEM fractographies of SBP after FB and IB are shown in Fig. 16 (a) and  Fig. 16 (b), respectively. Fine dimples were observed on the tensile fractographies of SBP joints after both FB and IB. IB resulted in more dimples on the fractography of SBP joint as there were fewer borides in the joint after IB, in comparison to FB (refer to  Fig. 9  and Fig. 12 ). In addition, a large amount of transgranular fracture was ob- served on the fractographies of SBP joints after both FB and IB. This indicates the dominance of brittle fracture at the SBP joints after both FB and IB, though a combined brittle-ductile fracture mode was ob- served on the fractographies of the joints.

The stresses at the subgrain concentrated areas, the edge of cracks and voids are higher than that of other regions, where enough energy accumulates for dis- location motion and rearrangement to nucleate fine grains  [33] .  

The fractography of the SBP joint after IB is shown in  Fig. 18 . Ob- viously, the density of subgrain boundaries on the fractography of the SBP after IB is much less than that on the fracture morphology of SBP after FB ( Fig. 17 (a) &  Fig. 18 (a)). Besides, fewer borides were observed on the fractography of the SBP joint after IB compared to that after FB, which provides strong evidence for the higher tensile bonding strength of the SBP joint after IB. Similarly, the transgranular crack (circle 1), intergranular crack (circle 2) and the crack around the void (circle 3) were observed on the fractography of the SBP joint after IB ( Fig. 18 (c)).

# 4. Conclusions  

This paper studied the induction TLP bonding (IB) of Inconel 718 with nickel-based sintered brazing preforms (SBP) in detail. The IB process was conducted in a vacuum induction bell jar system. The effect of the bonding surface roughness on the tensile bonding strength has been studied. Additionally, the microstructure and mechanical prop- erties of IB specimens of the Inconel 718 using nickel-based SBP have been investigated thoroughly in this work. Moreover, the results were analysed and compared to that after furnace TLP bonding (FB). Based on the aforementioned discussion, the conclusions are summarized as below:  

1) The vacuum induction bell jar system was able to conduct the IB of Inconel 718 with nickel-based SBP and neat bonding joints were obtained. 2) The highest tensile bonding strength of 240.76 MPa of the SBP joint after IB was achieved with an optimal bonding surface roughness of 0.9 μm ( R a ). The strength was approximately 16% higher than that of the SBP joint after FB due to fewer borides in the SBP joint after IB compared to that after FB. 3) The IB could accelerate the diffusion process, leading to fewer porosities and borides in the SBP, fewer and smaller precipitates in the precipitation layer, in comparison to FB. Additionally, lesser adverse heat effect was induced on the BM during IB compared to that during FB. 4) Brittle fracture dominated the fracture mode of SBP joints after both FB and IB. In addition, it was found that the voids and borides-rich areas were likely to be the preferred locations for crack initiation and propagation for the SBP joints during the tensile testing.
