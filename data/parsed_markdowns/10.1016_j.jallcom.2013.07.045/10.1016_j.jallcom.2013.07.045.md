# Inﬂuence of sub-solvus solution heat treatment on  c 0   morphological instability in a new Ni–Cr–Co-based powder metallurgy superalloy

Article history: Received 27 February 2013 Received in revised form 6 July 2013 Accepted 8 July 2013 Available online 16 July 2013  

The inﬂuence of the sub-solvus solution heat treatment on the microstructure, especially the  c 0   morphol- ogy ( c 0   fan-type structure), and microhardness of a new Ni–Cr–Co-based powder metallurgy superalloy was studied by means of ﬁeld emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM) and microhardness test- ing. The results show that sub-solvus solution heat treatment changes the microstructure of an as-forged alloy. It makes large primary  c 0   phases at grain boundaries smaller and the distribution of secondary  c 0 phases in the interior of the grains more homogeneous. Moreover, the grain boundaries widen because of the supplementary precipitate. The sub-solvus solution heat treatment before the super-solvus solution heat treatment does not change nucleation sites of the  c 0   fan-type structures which precipitate during the super-solvus solution heat treatment. However, it inﬂuences the morphology of  c 0   fan-type structures. Length distribution of the secondary  c 0   dendrites in fan-type structures changes from a bimodal to a uni- modal distribution, which means the lengths of the secondary  c 0   dendrites become more uniform. Apply- ing a sub-solvus solution heat treatment after the super-solvus solution heat treatment causes the secondary  c 0   dendrites to be broken off in the fan-type structures and a reﬁnement of the  c 0   phases, and this improves stability of the  c 0   phases.

A new kind of heat treatment process (Dual Microstructure Heat Treatment, DMHT) has been developed by the NASA Glenn Research Center to obtain P/M turbine disks with dual properties [10] . It has been successfully demonstrated on three different ad- vanced powder metallurgy disk superalloys  [10–12] . The speciﬁc heat treatment process involved employs a sub-solvus solution heat treatment below the  c 0   solvus temperature before or after the DMHT treatment.  

# 1. Introduction  

Compared with cast and wrought superalloys, nickel-based powder metallurgy (P/M) superalloys are produced with more uni- form composition and microstructure, more consistent grain and precipitate size  [1–3] . Therefore, nickel-based P/M superalloys have been widely used as turbine disk materials in advanced en- gines with a high thrust-weight ratio  [4,5] . A ﬁne microstructure is required for the bore region of turbine disks, to ensure sufﬁcient tensile strength and fatigue resistance, as the bore usually operates at low temperatures (420–650   C) and endures the torsion and centrifugal forces of the turboshaft  [6,7] . At the same time, a coarse microstructure is needed for the rim region to ensure enough resis- tance to stress rupture, creep and fatigue crack growth, as the rim is close to the gas channel and it undergoes high service tempera- tures (>760   C)  [8,9] . Therefore, a dual grain microstructure turbine disk is required which has a ﬁne grain bore and coarse grain rim. Such turbine disks with dual properties belong to a new turbine- disk generation, which has dual grain microstructures and can meet the need of aircraft engines with a high thrust-weight.  

Several studies have undertaken to further improve mechanical properties of the DMHT disks. Fatigue life, failure modes and the microstructure of the transition zone of a DMHT disk made by the superalloy LSHR have been investigated by Gabb et al.  [13] . Very recently, Gabb et al. studied the inﬂuences of the sub-solvus and super-solvus solution heat treatments on the notch dwell fati- gue resistance of the LSHR superalloy  [14] . Collins et al. have stud- ied a candidate material for the DMHT disk, RR1000, focusing on the grain growth behavior with near  c 0   solvus temperature heat treatments  [15] . Ning et al. used a similar heat treatment method, called gradient temperature heat treatment (GTHT) on the FGH 4096 superalloy and it produced the dual grain microstructure [16,17] .  

Since nickel-based superalloys are mainly strengthened by  c 0 precipitates, the size and morphology of the  c 0   phases play a critical role in the alloy’s mechanical properties  [18] . The  c 0   fan-type struc- ture is a special and unstable morphology of the  c 0   phases, and it has been extensively studied  [19–25] . However, the inﬂuence of a sub- solvus solution heat treatment on the size and morphology of the secondary  c 0   phases when applied prior to the super-solvus solution heat treatment has not been reported. The primary goal of this paper is to report on the inﬂuence of a sub-solvus solution heat treatment on the precipitation behavior and morphological instability of the  c 0 phases. This could provide both the scientiﬁc basis and the technical support for selecting the optimum heat treatment for manufactur- ing new generation P/M turbine disks with dual properties.  

# 2. Experimental procedures  

We selected the new Ni–Cr–Co-based P/M superalloy (FGH 98I) as the experi- mental material; the chemical compositions is given in  Table 1 . The vacuum induc- tion melting (VIM) and electroslag remelting (ESR) methods were used for alloy smelting. We used the plasma rotating electrode process (PREP) for powder prepa- ration, and combined the vibration screening and electrostatic separation methods in order to remove inclusions and produce powders with particle sizes of 50– 150  l m. We ﬁrst encapsulated the powders in stainless steel capsules and sealed them, after vacuum degassing; then, after welding, they were hot isostatically pressed (HIP) and we then obtained ﬁne-grained billet by isothermal forging (IF).  

The specimens were then taken from the air-cooled forged billet and divided into three groups. We treated the ﬁrst group with the super-solvus solution heat treatment at 1190   C for 1 h and cooled them at rates of 0.1, 0.4, 1.4, 4.3, and 10.8   C/s, as shown in Part II of  Fig. 1 . The second group ﬁrst underwent the sub-sol- vus solution heat treatment (at 1135   C for 2 h, followed by air cooling), and then underwent the super-solvus solution heat treatment; this can be seen in Parts I and II of  Fig. 1 . The third group was treated in the reverse sequence: ﬁrst they underwent the super-solvus solution heat treatment, and then the sub-solvus one (at 1135   C for 2 h followed by air cooling); see Parts II and III of  Fig. 1 .  

Samples for microstructure observation ﬁrst need erosion and polishing treat- ments; we prepared these by using a chemical etchant of CuSO 4  + HCl + H 2 O and electropolish with 20%H 2 SO 4  + 80%CH 3 OH. The unstable morphology of the  c 0   fan- type structure was observed by a SUPRA-55 ﬁeld emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM). Images obtained by FESEM were subsequently analyzed to quantify the size and area fraction of the fan-type structures under different heat treatment processes. We estimated the mechanical property of the alloys under dif- ferent heat treatment processes by Vickers hardness testing, which was performed on a WILSON WOLPERT 430SVD microhardness tester with an applied load of 1 kg for 15 s, at room temperature. Five measurements were taken for each sample, and the arithmetic mean was calculated.  

We chose three parameters to compare the  c 0   fan-type structures under differ- ent heat treatment processes: the characteristic length, characteristic space, and area fraction; Image-ProPlus 6.0 was used to count and measure these. For each sample, at least 20 fan-type structures were measured randomly for length ( L ) and space ( S ), and these are shown in  Fig. 2 . We then identiﬁed a certain length or space in a certain range with a maximum frequency, and the size corresponding to the maximum frequency was chosen as the characteristic length or the charac- teristic space. We used origin 8.0 software to get the frequency-length histogram of secondary  c 0   dendrites in the fan-type structures. We then obtained the peak va- lue by the Gauss ﬁtting method, and chose the size corresponding to the peak value as the characteristic length. The characteristic space was obtained similarly. We determined the area fraction by measuring relative areas of the fan-type structures, from 30 pictures of every sample; we then calculated the arithmetic mean, which gave us the area fraction. Using the Image-ProPlus 6.0 software, we measured the average size of the  c 0   phases to get the equivalent diameter of 100–200  c 0   phases for each sample; we deﬁned equivalent diameter as the diameter of a circle which has the same area as the measured  c 0   phase. We then used Origin 8.0 to obtain the average size of  c 0   phases, using the same technique we used to obtain characteristic length. Standard deviation for all measurements was less than 0.05  l m.

Prior research on the formation mechanism of the  c 0   fan-type structure  [19–24]  has revealed that the  c 0   fan-type structure con- sists of ﬁnger-shaped secondary  c 0   dendrites with  c  matrix be- tween them. The dominating factor for formation of  c 0   fan-type structures was the chemical composition’s inhomogeneity on the grain boundaries. That is to say, the small-scale segregation regions with non-uniform Al and Ti contents on grain boundaries were found to be related to the nucleation of the  c 0   fan-type structure. These chemical composition segregation regions on grain boundaries could be the preferential nucleation sites of the precip- itation process. When the alloy was cooled from a super-solvus solution temperature, the grain boundaries were supersaturated before the interior of the grains. It was also found that the contents of Al and Ti in the previous  c 0   phases formed at high temperatures were higher than those in the  c 0   phases formed later at low tem- peratures, which resulted in the formation of concentration gradi- ent within the  c 0   phases. Accordingly, as the cooling process proceeded, the  c 0   phase forming elements such as Al and Ti made short-distance diffusion from the  c 0   phases previously formed at high temperatures to those formed at low temperatures. Mean- while, Al and Ti on the supersaturated grain boundaries were able to accomplish the short-distance diffusion to the nucleated  c 0 phases; solute atoms in the supersaturated  c  matrix were also able to diffuse in this way. Consequently, these processes led the  c 0 phases at grain boundaries to grow in a directional manner, result- ing in the formation of fan-type structures. The growth of the  c 0 fan-type structure terminated at a slightly lower temperature when the vast homogeneous nucleation of  c 0   phases happened in the interior of the grains. From these results, we know that during the cooling process, the key factors controlling formation and development of the  c 0   fan-type structure are the following: the de- gree of supersaturation on the grain boundaries; the concentration gradient of Al and Ti within  c 0   phases; and the concentration var- iation of solute atoms in the  c  matrix around the grain boundaries.

For the as-forged alloy, the chemical composition and micro- structure on the grain boundaries were not homogeneous. By applying the sub-solvus solution heat treatment, chemical compo- sition and microstructure of the as-forged alloy became more homogeneous. After the alloy was super-solvus solution heat trea- ted and cooled at a relatively slow rate, alloying elements such as Al and Ti within the secondary  c 0   dendrites were able to fully dif- fuse, and the dendrites easily grew in a directional manner. Hence the morphology of the  c 0   fan-type structures developed well. Growth rates of the secondary  c 0   dendrites, however, tended to be more uniform and their length distribution presented a uni- modal distribution. In contrast, for the alloy that underwent the super-solvus solution heat treatment without having been sub-sol- vus solution heat treated, the process of the super-solvus solution treatment was insufﬁcient to change the chemical composition’s inhomogeneity on the grain boundaries. Although the alloying ele- ments such as Al and Ti on the grain boundaries could accomplish full diffusion under the condition of the slow cooling, the inheri- tance of the chemical composition’s inhomogeneity on the grain boundaries still resulted in irregular growth rates of the secondary c 0   dendrites. Consequently, the  c 0   fan-type structure morphology became irregular and the length distribution of the secondary  c 0 dendrites presented a bimodal distribution. When the alloy was super-solvus solution heat treated and cooled at a relatively fast rate, the  c 0   fan-type structures could not develop well: a fast cool- ing rate led to difﬁcult diffusion of the alloying elements, and thus the concentration gradient of Al and Ti within the secondary  c 0 dendrites was small. 3.4. Morphology of the  c 0   fan-type structures in the alloy after the super-solvus solution heat treatment and the sub-solvus solution heat treatment  

Fig. 7  shows the morphology of the  c 0   fan-type structures in an alloy after being super-solvus solution heat treated and cooled at the rate of 0.1   C/s and then treated with the sub-solvus solution heat treatment. It can be seen from both  Figs. 5 a and  7  that apply- ing a sub-solvus solution heat treatment after the super-solvus one causes a change in  c 0   fan-type structure morphology. The edges of the ﬁnger-shaped secondary  c 0   dendrites have broken off, and the c 0   phases in the broken dendrites have transformed into rectangu- lar or square shapes, which arrange along the direction of the ori- ginal dendrites, as indicated by an arrow in  Fig. 7 . It is obvious that the sub-solvus solution heat treatment after the super-solvus solu- tion heat treatment has transformed the  c 0   fan-type structures from coarse  c 0   phases into an arrangement of ﬁne cubic  c 0   phases; this could be a more stable state of the  c 0   phases.  

It should be pointed out that the  c 0   fan-type structure is a spe- cial microstructure that usually presents during the heat treatment processes of the third generation high alloying P/M superalloys. Note that the presence of large  c 0   fan-type structures at grain boundaries or in the interior of the grains is harmful to the mechanical properties of the alloy. Large  c 0   fan-type structures forming at grain boundaries would promote the initiation and propagation of the creeping crack, and reduce the alloy’s resistance to fatigue crack propagation  [26–29] . However, we show in this study that it can be changed through proper heat treatment pro- cesses, such as the application of the sub-solvus solution heat treatment before or after the super-solvus one. The chemical com- position of the alloy would be more homogeneous, and the second- ary  c 0   dendrites in the fan-type structures would present a more uniform length. Furthermore, the coarse secondary  c 0   dendrites in the fan-type structures would be broken off. As a consequence, the stability of the  c 0   phases would be improved, which could ben- eﬁt the mechanical properties of the alloy.

# 4. Conclusions  

The sub-solvus solution heat treatment prior to the super-sol- vus solution heat treatment does not change the nucleation sites of the  c 0   fan-type structures which precipitate during the super- solvus solution heat treatment. However, it inﬂuences morphology of the  c 0   fan-type structures. The length distribution of the second- ary  c 0   dendrites in fan-type structures changes from a bimodal to a unimodal distribution, which means the lengths of the secondary c 0   dendrites become more uniform. Applying a sub-solvus solution heat treatment after the super-solvus solution heat treatment causes breaking off of the secondary  c 0   dendrites in fan-type struc- tures and the reﬁnement of  c 0   phases; this improves the stability of the  c 0   phases.
