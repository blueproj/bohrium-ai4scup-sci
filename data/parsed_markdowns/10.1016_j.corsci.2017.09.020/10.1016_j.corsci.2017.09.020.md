# A R T I C L E I N F O  

Ni-base superalloy C263 has been oxidized from 700 °C to 1100 °C in arti ﬁ cial dry air. A continuous Cr rich (Cr, Ti) 2 O 3  layer is formed during oxidation, while the oxidation kinetics deviated from the parabolic kinetics. The oxidation microstructures were di ﬀ erent in as-machined and polished samples. Recrystallization below the oxide layer occurred in as-machined samples together with severe internal degradation (internal oxidation and ni- tridation). Grain boundaries provide paths for fast di ﬀ usion of O and N into the substrate and outward di ﬀ usion of Ti and Cr. Surface deformation and recrystallization exhibit detrimental e ﬀ ects on the oxidation behavior of C263.

The internal oxides preferably grow along the grain boundaries which provide easy propagation paths for cracks during mechanical loading  [26,27] . The formation of a Cr 2 O 3  layer can be a ﬀ ected by surface  ﬁ nish, such as sand blasting, rolling and grinding [28,29] . # 1. Introduction  

The harsh environment in advanced turbine engines has led to the application of superalloy parts in hot sections  [1] . Besides the re- quirement of adequate mechanical properties (creep, fatigue etc.  [2] ), oxidation properties are also important  [3,4] . Cr and Al are usually added to form protective Cr 2 O 3  or Al 2 O 3  layers to improve the oxida- tion resistance of superalloys  [5 – 7] . Although Al 2 O 3  layers show a smaller growth rate at high temperatures than Cr 2 O 3  layers  [8] , they are more brittle and the adherence between the Al 2 O 3  layer and sub- strate is poorer compared to Cr 2 O 3  layers  [9 – 11] . Additionally, Al 2 O 3  is less protective under sul ﬁ dation conditions  [12] . A further considera- tion is as follows: Al, when added in su ﬃ cient quantity, leads to  γ '- formation and high strength, making rolling and forging much more di ﬃ cult  [13] . Since high strength is not always required, many wrought superalloys are designed to form a Cr 2 O 3  layer  [14] .  

The material investigated in the present paper, Nimonic C263 (C263 for short), is a solution and age-hardenable Ni-base superalloy con- taining 20 wt.% Cr, 2.0 wt.% Ti and 0.45 wt.% Al  [31] . According to the investigations on similar Ni-Cr-Al alloys  [32] , a chromia scale will form during the oxidation. Little is known about the oxidation behavior of this alloy. Previous research usually focused on its machining, mi- crostructures and deformation mechanism  [33 – 35] . In the present work, the oxidation behavior of C263 superalloy after machining was investigated from 700 °C to 1100 °C in arti ﬁ cial dry air.  

During oxidation, the substrate region close to the Cr 2 O 3  layer will be depleted in Cr and degraded by the penetration of oxygen and ni- trogen (internal oxidation or nitridation)  [15 – 17] . As a result, the mechanical properties are decreased. Crack propagation may be ac- celerated by the internal degradation process  [18 – 20] . Oxides such as Al 2 O 3 , and nitrides like TiN, AlN and even CrN phases could form during the internal degradation process  [21 – 23] . Once these oxides/ nitrides are formed, the volume change in the matrix will introduce high stresses in the oxide layer  [24]  and the layer may even be da- maged. At least three SEM images of dif- ferent areas were used for statistic. X-ray di ﬀ raction (XRD) measure- ments were performed on the fresh surfaces of machined and polished specimens oxidized at 800 °C for 60 h. Specimens for normal EBSD analysis were cut and polished metal lo graphically ( ﬁ rst grinding with #80 – #4000 grit SiC grinding paper, then  ﬁ nal polishing using OP-U and cleaning with demineralized water). The specimens for High Resolution-EBSD (HR-EBSD) analysis were additionally ion beam polished with a Leica EM TIC 3X Ion milling System (Leica Micro- systems GmbH, Germany) after metal lo graphical preparation. All EBSD measurements were conducted on a FEI Helios NanoLab 600i FIB Workstation equipped with an Oxford Instruments NordlysNano EBSD detector. The patterns were recorded at an acceleration voltage of 25 kV using the Oxford Instruments AZtechHKL software. The EBSD data analysis was done with the Oxford Instruments HKL CHANNEL5 software. For the HR-EBSD measurements the step size was set to 0.1  μ m, the patterns were recorded with a resolution of 1344 × 1024 Pixels and data analysis was done with the commercial software CrossCourt4 (CC4) from BLG Vantage Software Inc. CC4 performs cross- correlation analysis of the collected EBSPs to detect residual elastic and plastic strains. The method is described elsewhere in detail  [37 – 40] . Mapping of the element distribution in the oxidation areas of machined and polished specimens was performed using electron probe micro analysis (EPMA) (JXA-8100, JEOL, Japan).

# 3.3. Surface recrystallization  

Recrystallization is observed in a small band below the oxide layer after oxidation at 700 °C, 800 °C and 900 °C. EBSD measurements have been performed to reveal the recrystallized grain structure and the re- sults are shown in  Fig. 4 . In the recrystallized region, the grain sizes are much smaller than in the substrate. Additionally, the recrystallized grains are much  ﬁ ner when they are close to the oxide layer, and this becomes more pronounced at 900 °C. The penetration depths of the recrystallized region at the di ﬀ erent oxidation times and temperatures were also measured and are presented in  Fig. 5 a. The recrystallized grains will penetrate more than 60  μ m into the substrate after oxidation at 900 °C for 300 h, while at 700 °C, the penetration depth is less than 5  μ m. Recrystallization of superalloys usually happens at high tem- peratures due to the release of stored energy introduced by deforma- tion, for example, forging, extrusion, or shot peening followed by heat treatment. Although our specimens were heat treated in the as received state, they were then milled and ground to surface roughness below 0.3  μ m before the oxidation tests. Thus there is still some surface de- formation left which can induce the recrystallization at high tempera- tures.  

In order to con ﬁ rm that the surface recrystallization is due to the residual surface deformation, one side of a 2 mm thick specimen was ground using SiC grinding paper (#80 – #4000) to 1.85 mm and then mechanically polished to reduce the surface strain to the lowest value. After polishing and cleaning, the specimen was oxidized at 800 °C for 60 h, and the oxidation microstructures were analyzed. As expected, no recrystallization occurred in the samples as shown in  Fig. 6 . In addition to this, the surface residual strains of specimens before and after oxi- dation were measured using HR-EBSD which will be introduced in Section  3.7 .

# 4. Discussion  

# 4.1. Recrystallized grain size  

The recrystallized grains in the substrate, once formed, do not change their size during subsequent annealing (Figs.  1  and  2  a). Ap- parently, the time is too short and the temperatures are too low for the di ﬀ usion controlled grain growth. However, the recrystallized surface layer grows slowly inwards, as shown by  Fig. 5 . It should be noted that the driving force due to surface deformation will be highest at the immediate surface and drop as a function of the distance from the surface. As such, sections further away from the surface will need more time to recrystallize and the recrystallized layer thickness will gradually increase with annealing time. Some surface strain can still be observed in  Fig. 16 a ahead of the recrystallized region (RX region), which is 4 – 5  μ m deep from the surface. However, in  Fig. 16 b and c, the re- crystallization is already deeper than the external of the surface strain region, while the recrystallized grains are still growing. Furthermore, the recrystallized grain size becomes larger with the increase of the penetration depth. Some larger substrate grains are penetrated by these smaller surface recrystallized grains in  Fig. 4 b and c. In this case, sur- face strain is not high enough as the driving force for the growth of these recrystallized grains. Obviously, di ﬀ usion induced grain boundary migration (DIGM) happens during the growth of the re- crystallized grains especially when they exceed the surface strained region. DIGM is a phenomenon which combines the migration of grain boundaries and di ﬀ usion of solute elements along the migrating grain boundaries  [41] . In our case, once the surface recrystallized grains formed below the oxide layer, Cr and Ti could di ﬀ use along the re- crystallized grain boundaries to the oxide layer. Therefore, the front of

# 4.2. Oxide layer composition  

C263 superalloy contains 20 wt% Cr and forms a dense chromia layer on top of the substrate. Although there may be a transient oxi- dation stage during the formation of this chromia layer  [42] , oxide formation should be very fast based on the oxidation kinetics in  Fig. 3 a. The high Ti content in the substrate leads to a competition between Ti and Cr for the O during the oxidation process. Inferred from the El- lingham diagram  [43] , the Ti oxide is more stable than Cr oxide. Therefore, at the early stage of oxidation, TiO 2  could form together with Cr 2 O 3  [44] . Once the Ti in the matrix is depleted, formation of a Cr 2 O 3  layer will happen. If some remaining Ti enters the solid solution, this results in the oxide layer of (Cr 0.88 Ti 0.12 ) 2 O 3  which was presently observed. The grinding process may introduce some compounds, such as SiC in the present work, on the very top surface and may a ﬀ ect the passivity of the alloys at the very beginning as Wagner reported  [45] . However, in our investigation this e ﬀ ect should be rather low since the subsequent light microscopy did not indicate the presence of SiC compounds on the surface.  

Further growth of the oxide layer usually depends on the di ﬀ usion of oxide forming elements, such Cr and Ti, to the surface layer. Once the recrystallized surface grains are formed, the total grain boundary area also increases. The grain boundary could serve as fast di ﬀ usion paths for the oxide forming elements. Therefore, the concentrations of Cr and Ti at the oxide/substrate interface in machined specimens are higher than that in polished specimens.  Fig. 12  has indicated that much more Cr and Ti had been depleted in the substrate close to the oxide layer. Additionally, the higher composition gradient on the two sides of the chromia layer will accelerate the di ﬀ usion of elements through the chromia layer. And even Ti oxide also grows above the chromia layer due to further di ﬀ usion of Ti through the chromia layer.

More Cr vacancies will be generated when Ti is doped into the Cr 2 O 3  layer. This has provided more paths for the di ﬀ usion of Cr, Ti or even substrate elements such as Ni and Co. Therefore, some phases containing substrate elements above the chromia layer formed, such as (Co, Ni) 2 TiO 4  in machined specimens and (Ni, Ti)O in the polished specimen ( Fig. 8 ). As has been known, if there is only Cr di ﬀ using across the dense Cr 2 O 3  layer, the oxidation kinetics will follow a parabolic law. However, since the layer is already doped with Ti, the kinetics are accelerated. For one thing, outward di ﬀ usion of Cr is promoted and more Ti could also di ﬀ use through the vacancies to the outermost surface, this led to the deviation from parabolic oxidation kinetics. Additionally, severe internal oxidation and nitridation and porosities in the chromia layer accelerate the oxidation kinetics.

O di ﬀ uses faster along the grain boundaries as can be observed both in machined and polished specimens. There is always some internal oxidation along the grain boundaries in present research. In machined specimens, although the Al concentration is only 0.45 wt% in the substrate, the Al content is higher at grain boundaries. Therefore, Al oxides will form  ﬁ rst along the recrystallized grain boundaries. When Al is depleted along the grain boundary, Ti, whose oxide is less stable than Al oxide, will participate into the internal oxidation, resulting in the formation of a Ti, O and N rich envelope around the Al 2 O 3  oxides ( Fig. 11 a). Both the internal oxidation in machined and polished spe- cimens penetrates similar depth along the grain boundaries. No internal oxidation inside the grains is observed mainly because of the low concentration of Al and low di ﬀ usivity and concentration of O inside the grains. O has also been reported to be trapped in defects, such as dislocations  [46] . Although the solubility of O in Ni base alloys is higher than that of N, the di ﬀ usivity of N could be two orders of magnitude greater than that for O. Rubly and Douglass  [47]  reported that the di ﬀ usion coe ﬃ cient of O in Ni-10Cr alloy is about 1.20 × 10 − 9   cm 2 /s while the N is 6.7 × 10 − 7   cm 2 /s. When N di ﬀ uses across the chromia layer and enters the substrate, there is no chance to form nitrides along the grain boundaries because of the high O content there. Due to the high di ﬀ usivity in the grains and high Ti contents, Ti nitrides formed as a result as smaller plates or needles in the grains. The internal nitridation is much more severe in machined samples than polished ones, and this is possibly because the lower Cr content in the surface region since Rubly and Douglass  [46]  found the nitridation will decrease with the increasing Cr content. Once Cr was depleted in the recrystallized region, nitridation is more likely to take place.  

As discussed above, surface deformation due to machining plays a major role for the formation of surface recrystallized region. Once the surface recrystallization formed the di ﬀ usion of Cr or Ti to the surface region is accelerated. Additionally, severe internal nitridation hap- pened in the recrystallized region, consuming Ti which is a  γ ' forming element. This is not good for C263 alloy during mechanical loading.  

# 5. Conclusions  

The oxidation behavior of C263 superalloy specimens after ma- chining was examined systematically. The following conclusions can be drawn:  

1. The residual strain due to machining caused surface recrystallization during oxidation at 700 °C, 800 °C and 900 °C. The recrystallized region will grow in parallel with the oxidation process because re- crystallization further inside takes longer to occur due to a reduced driving force. Polishing of the samples will avoid the recrystalliza- tion.
