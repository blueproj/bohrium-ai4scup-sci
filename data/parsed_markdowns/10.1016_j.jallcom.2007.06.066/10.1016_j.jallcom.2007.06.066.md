# High temperature diffusion induced liquid phase joining of a heat resistant alloy  

N.P. Wikstrom, A.T. Egbewande, O.A. Ojo   ∗  

Department of Mechanical and Manufacturing Engineering, University of Manitoba, Winnipeg, Manitoba, Canada R3T 5V6 Received 15 June 2007; accepted 19 June 2007 Available online 22 June 2007  

# Abstract  

Transient liquid phase bonding (TLP) of a nickel base superalloy, Waspaloy, was performed to study the inﬂuence of holding time and temperature on the joint microstructure. Insufﬁcient holding time for complete isothermal solidiﬁcation of liquated insert caused formation of eutectic-type micro constituent along the joint centerline region in the alloy. In agreement with prediction by conventional TLP diffusion models, an increase in bonding temperature for a constant gap size, resulted in decrease in the time,  t f,  required to form a eutectic-free joint by complete isothermal solidiﬁcation. However, a signiﬁcant deviation from these models was observed in specimens bonded at and above 1175   ◦ C. A reduction in isothermal solidiﬁcation rate with increased temperature was observed in these specimens, such that a eutectic-free joint could not be achieved by holding for a time period that produced complete isothermal solidiﬁcation at lower temperatures. Boron-rich particles were observed within the eutectic that formed in the joints prepared at the higher temperatures. An overriding effect of decrease in boron solubility relative to increase in its diffusivity with increase in temperature, is a plausible important factor responsible for the reduction in isothermal solidiﬁcation rate at the higher bonding temperatures.

# 1. Introduction  

Heat resistant nickel base superalloys are used for manufac- turing hot section components of aero and land-based gas turbine engines, due to their remarkable high temperature mechanical properties and hot corrosion resistance. Exposure to harsh oper- ating conditions often causes these components to be damaged by cracking and surface erosion during service. It is usually more economically attractive to repair damaged parts instead of a complete replacement. Traditional repair techniques, such as welding are commonly used in the repair of many superalloy components.However,precipitation hardened nickel base super- alloys, like Waspaloy, containing relatively high amounts of Al and Ti are generally considered to be difﬁcult to weld due to their high susceptibility to heat-affected zone (HAZ) cracking  [1,2] . As a result, a process known as transient liquid phase (TLP) bonding  [3] , has evolved as an attractive alternate joining tech- nique for difﬁcult-to-weld superalloys due to its technological and economical advantages. The major advantage of this tech- nique over conventional brazing is that the process can achieve joints free of eutectic-type micro constituents that are usually detrimental to mechanical properties. Nevertheless, optimiza- tion of the process variables that control the bond microstructure is vital in achieving eutectic-freejoint.A number of studies [4–8] have been previously done to model microstructural develop- ment during TLP bonding. These studies normally model the isothermal solidiﬁcation stage of the process using a binary phase relationship between the base and ﬁller alloy. However, the phase relationships that are actually encountered in multi- component systems that are used in industrial applications may not comply with simple binary system approximation. Recent work  [9,10]  on numerical modeling of TLP isothermal solidiﬁ- cation process in ternary systems has suggested that the actual phase relationships encountered during TLP bonding of multi- component systems could result in a signiﬁcant modiﬁcation of isothermal solidiﬁcation behavior compared to conventional expectations. Therefore, experimental investigation of the inﬂu- ence of process parameters on microstructure of TLP bonded

# 2. Experimental procedure  

Wrought Waspaloy superalloy was used in this study. The chemical composi- tion of the alloy was 18.36 Cr, 14.02 Co, 3.73 Mo, 0.02 Nb, 0.02 W, 0.61 Fe, 1.49 Al, 3.12 Ti, 0.032 C, 0.005 B, balance Ni (wt.%). The ﬁller alloy used was Nicro- braz 150 foil with a composition of 15 Cr, 3.5 B, balance Ni (wt.%) at 100   m thickness. TLP bonding of the alloy was performed in a vacuum furnace at a pressure of 10 − 4   to 10 − 5   Torr, and within a temperature range of 1100–1225   ◦ C for 1–8 h of holding time. Subsequent to bonding, the samples were sectioned transversely by electro-discharge machining (EDM) and prepared by standard metallographic techniques for microscopic examination. Sections of the joint were subsequently etched in a solution of 40 mL H 2 O + 480 mL HCl + 48 mL CuCl 2  for 5 s. The microstructure of the samples was examined by optical micro- scope and by a JEOL 5900 scanning electron microscope (SEM) equipped with an ultra thin window Oxford energy-dispersive spectrometer (EDS).

During TLP bonding, an interlayer, which is sandwiched between the base alloys, melts and rapidly attains equilibrium at liquid–solid base alloy interfaces through a dissolution process. Following this, a solid-state diffusion of melting point depres- sant element occurs from the liquid interlayer to the solid base alloy. This results in a decrease in the volume of liquid that can be maintained at equilibrium, causing isothermal solidiﬁcation to proceed inward from the solid mating surface. Due to an absence of solute rejection at the solid–liquid interface during isother- mal solidiﬁcation under equilibrium condition, the only solid phase which forms is the solid solution phase, and formation of other phases is basically prevented  [3] . In situations where suf- ﬁcient time is not allowed, any residual liquid could transform during cooling from the bonding temperature into eutectic-type solidiﬁcation products.  

The solidiﬁcation transformation behavior of residual liq- uid during TLP bonding of nickel using a Ni–B–Cr ternary ﬁller alloy was studied and modeled numerically using Scheil simulation by Ohsasa et al.  [11] . A ternary centerline eutectic, consisting of Ni base solid solution phase (  ), Ni boride (Ni 3 B) and Cr boride (CrB), was predicted in the bonded specimens. Their simulation results showed that during solidiﬁcation of the residual liquid in a sample held at 1100   ◦ C, Ni-rich    phase formed as the primary phase, followed by the eutectic reac- tion L  →   + Ni 3 B at 1042   ◦ C. Solidiﬁcation was reported to be completed via a ternary eutectic reaction L  →   + Ni 3 B + CrB at 997   ◦ C. In addition, Gale et al.  [12]  in their work on TLP bonding of nickel using Ni–Si–B ternary ﬁller alloy observed, with the use of high temperature X-ray diffraction, a deposit of Ni plus Ni 3 B eutectic mixture in a sample joined at 1150   ◦ C for 5 min. The ternary centerline eutectic observed in the present work, which was similar to those reported by Ohsasa et al. [11] , is believed to be formed by solidiﬁcation reaction(s) dur- ing cooling due to an insufﬁcient diffusion time for complete isothermal solidiﬁcation to occur during holding at the bond- ing temperature. Similar eutectic reaction product consisting of nickel-rich boride, chromium-rich boride, and nickel base solid solution phase were also observed in alloy IC6 (Ni–15.9 Al–7.78 Mo–0.125 B–0.05C (at.%)) specimens that were bonded with the same Ni–Cr–B ﬁller alloy ( Fig. 3 (a), (b) and (c) and Table 2 ), despite the fact that the IC6 base alloy does not contain chromium. Therefore, the result of Scheil simulation reported by Ohsasa et al.  [11]  reasonably describes the solidiﬁcation behav- ior of residual liquid insert during TLP bonding of these alloys with Ni–Cr–B ﬁller alloy.

Analytical and numerical models have been developed for isothermal solidiﬁcation process during TLP bonding  [4–8,13] . These models were mostly based on the use of pseudo-binary phase relationships between liquid interlayer and solid base alloy, and depend on classic solutions to Fick’s laws of diffusion. According to these models, an increase in bonding temperature is expected to increase isothermal solidiﬁcation rate, and pro- duce a eutectic-free joint in a reduced time,  t f , compared to the time needed at lower temperatures. However, it has been suggested by Sekerka  [14]  that an increase in bonding tempera- ture may not necessarily result in a faster solidiﬁcation process, as the actual rate of solidiﬁcation will depend upon details of the phase relationships in complex alloy and on diffusivities of solutes across the solid–liquid interface. In agreement with this, Sinclair et al.  [9,10]  suggested that deviation from isother- mal solidiﬁcation rate predicted by conventional TLP diffusion models, which are generally based on binary phase relationships, might be encountered during TLP bonding of multi-component systems. It was proposed that if the solubilities and/or diffu- sivities of the two diffusing solutes (normally melting point depressants) capable of controlling the isothermal solidiﬁcation process are very different, the isothermal solidiﬁcation stage could be divided into two parabolic regimes. In the present work, diffusion of boron, which is essentially an interstitial atom in nickel and thus with a higher diffusivity compared to substitutional solutes, is believed to be isother- mal solidiﬁcation rate controlling factor during TLP bonding at 1100 and 1145   ◦ C. Thus, an increased diffusivity of boron with an increase in bonding temperature from 1100 to 1145   ◦ C resulted in a reduction in isothermal solidiﬁcation completion time. However, during the bonding process, continual interdif- fusion of elements between the liquid insert and the base metal would cause the composition of the liquid to be continually modiﬁed as solidiﬁcation progressed. Notably, a bonding tem- perature of 1175   ◦ C is close to the solvus temperature of Ti-rich  ′   precipitate particles, which are present in the matrix of solid base alloy. The dissolution of the particles would allow more Ti atoms to be available within the base alloy    matrix  [15] , result- ing in a steep concentration gradient between the base alloy   matrix and liquid interlayer. Subsequently, this can result in a signiﬁcant enrichment of the liquid interlayer with Ti, which is expected to increase with an increase in bonding temperature from 1175 to 1225   ◦ C. As shown in  Fig. 2 (b) and (c), the for- mation of Ni–Ti-rich intermetallic eutectic constituent within the samples bonded at 1175 and 1225   ◦ C, respectively, sug- gests an enrichment of the ternary liquated interlayer with Ti during isothermal solidiﬁcation process. The solidiﬁcation par- tition coefﬁcient of Ti in nickel alloy is less than one; therefore an increase in its concentration in the liquid phase would result in a depression of solidiﬁcation temperature  [16] . A consider- able enrichment of this element in the liquated insert during TLP bonding at 1175 and 1225   ◦ C could, as suggested by Sin- clair et al.  [9,10] , be responsible for the commencement of a solidiﬁcation regime characterized by a slower isothermal solid- iﬁcation rate that was observed in the present work. Although, manufacturers of ﬁller alloys tend to avoid the use of solute elements that can retard isothermal solidiﬁcation rate, a consid- erable enrichment of liquated insert with base alloying elements during TLP bonding of superalloys could result in an undesir- able sluggish isothermal solidiﬁcation rate, particularly at higher temperatures.  

Besides the possible role of the second solute, Ti, in reduc- ing the isothermal solidiﬁcation rates at higher temperatures, the decrease in solubility of boron with increase in temperature above its eutectic temperature in nickel could also be another important contributing factor. Therefore, it can be seen from this equation that the diffusion-controlled isothermal solidiﬁ- cation rate is not only dependent on diffusion coefﬁcient, but also on solubility, both of which can be inﬂuenced differently by bonding temperature. An increase in temperature will cause an exponential increase in solute diffusivity according to the Arrhenius equation. However, according to the Ni–B phase dia- gram the solubility of boron in nickel decreases with increase in temperature above the eutectic temperature. A reduction in the solubility of boron at higher temperatures (1175   ◦ C and above) in the Waspaloy superalloy used in the present work could cause a decrease in the rate of change of concentration gradient ( ∂ 2 C / ∂ x 2 ) in the base metal during bonding. This could, according to Fick’s 2nd law, reduce boron diffusion and, as such, decrease the rate of isothermal solidiﬁcation at these higher tem- peratures. Boron-containing particles were observed within the centerline eutectic product in joints prepared at the high tem- peratures where reduction in isothermal solidiﬁcation rate with increase in temperatures was observed ( Fig. 4 (b) and (c)). The presence of boron-rich particles within the centerline eutectic, suggests that boron diffusion out of liquated insert was reduced at these higher brazing temperatures. A decrease in solubility of boron in nickel from 320 ppm at 1125   ◦ C to 210 ppm at 1225   ◦ C has been previously related to reduction in isothermal solidiﬁca- tion kinetics during diffusion brazing of pure Ni, in the literature [5] .

Nevertheless, an increase in bonding temperature simultaneously reduces the solidus composition (solid solubility),  C  s , and, thus, will tend to increase the time required for complete isothermal solidiﬁcation. This indicates that time,  t , may not continue to monotonically decrease with increase in temperature but instead will tend to increase as the temperature reaches and exceeds a critical value where the inﬂuence of reduced solubility overrides higher dif- fusivity. Enrichment of the liquated inert with Ti, capable of depressing the melting temperature, may, thus, not be solely responsible for the reduction in isothermal solidiﬁcation rate during the TLP bonding in the present work. Interplay between increase in diffusivity and decrease in solubility of melting point depressant boron with increase in temperature could be another important factor contributing to the observed reduction in isothermal solidiﬁcation rate of the materials. It is plausible that the slower rate of isothermal solidiﬁcation at the higher temperatures (above 1175   ◦ C) was a consequence of overriding effect of lower solubilities of boron relative to its higher dif- fusivity at these temperatures. Therefore, the decrease in boron solubility with increase in temperature is an important factor that needs appropriate consideration in the selection of processing parameters for optimizing TLP bonding of Waspaloy to obtain a high quality and reliable eutectic-free joint.  

# 4. Summary and conclusions  

1. Isothermal solidiﬁcation of liquated Ni–Cr–B ﬁller occurred during TLP joining of Waspaloy superalloy and the extent of the solidiﬁcation increased with increasing holding time. Incomplete isothermal solidiﬁcation of the liquated insert resulted in formation of centerline eutectic-type product along the joint, the thickness of which increased with increase in gap size.  

2. Complete isothermal solidiﬁcation in 100   m joint, which precluded the formation of the centerline eutectic, occurred within 8 h and 6 h of holding time at 1100 and 1145   ◦ C, respectively.  

3. A deviation from conventional expectation was observed in joints bonded at 1175 and 1225   ◦ C. Contrary to the con- ventional expectation, a decrease in isothermal solidiﬁcation rate occurred with increase in temperature to these tempera- tures, which resulted in the formation of centerline eutectic along the joints even after 6 h of holding time that produced a eutectic-free joint at 1145   ◦ C.  

4. An increased interdiffusion induced enrichment of base alloying elements, particularly Ti, in the liquated insert at 1175 and 1225   ◦ C and decrease in boron solubility were suggested as plausible factors contributing to the observed reduction in isothermal solidiﬁcation rate at the higher bond- ing temperatures.
