# Abstract  

Creep crack growth (CCG) tests on Ni-base superalloys were carried out in the Japanese VAMAS group as part of a round-robin program in order to assist the standardization of the CCG test method for creep-brittle alloys. The eect of temperature, load, specimen thickness and material microstructure on CCG behavior was investigated. The applicable range of fracture mechanical parameters to evaluate the CCG rate was evaluated. The CCG rate was characterized by the  C \*  parameter independent of testing conditions in the range where the CCG rate accelerated. However, the acceleration stage occupied only a small portion of life time for the creep-brittle superalloys. In the range where the CCG rate was constant, the CCG rate and the fracture life could be predicted approximately by the  Q \* method based on the thermally activated process.  #  1998 Elsevier Science Ltd. All rights reserved.  

Keywords:  Creep crack growth; Ni-base superalloy;  C \*  parameter; Thermally activated process;  Q \*  parameter  

# 1. Introduction  

It is important to predict the creep crack growth (CCG) rate and life for the reliability assessment of high temperature structural components. There are many fracture mechanical studies [1±5] on creep crack growth for creep-ductile low alloy steels and stainless steels, however, relatively little research has been performed on creep-brittle alloys [6±9]. The stress intensity factor  K  was considered for characterizing the CCG rate for these brittle materials. However, the applicable range of the fracture mechanical parameters  K  and  C \*  has not been established.  

Authors have been participating in the VAMAS TWA19 international collaborative research project that aims to pre-standardize the testing and evaluation method of high temperature fracture of brittle materials. In this project, the Japanese group has conducted the round-robin tests of CCG on Ni-base superalloys. The Ni-base superalloy can maintain good creep resistance at very high temperatures, but its creep ductility is relatively small.  

In the present work, the creep crack growth tests were conducted using CT specimens of IN100 and Inconel 713C superalloys in three laboratories: National Research Institute for Metals (NRIM), Tohoku University and Ishikawajima-Harima Heavy Industries (IHI) Co. Ltd. The CCG rates were correlated with  K ,  C \*  and  Q \*  parameters [10, 11]. The eects of load, temperature and specimen thickness were investigated.

The d a /d t  of creep brittle IN100 superalloy was about 10 times higher than that of ductile 1Cr-Mo-V steel. According to Ref. [16], d a /d t  at constant  C \*  value was inversely proportional to the creep ductility. Because the creep ductility was found to be about 80% for Cr-Mo-V steel [17] and about 10% for IN100 from the creep tests of round bar specimens, the dierence of d a /d t  between these materials could be attributed to the dierence of creep ductility. In the ductile 1Cr-Mo-V steel, the increase in specimen thickness accelerated the creep crack growth rate [17], but the eect of thickness was small for IN100, as seen in Fig. 7. It is considered that a thickness of 6.35 mm is sucient for developing plane strain conditions for this creep brittle alloy, even without side grooves. There was no evidence of dimpling at the crack tip as is commonly observed when plasticity or creep occurs at the crack tip.  

At the initial stage of CCG, nose parts [18, 19] were also observed in the brittle superalloys and d a /d t  could not be uniquely characterized by  C \* . Fig. 8 shows the typical transient behavior in 713C superalloy and 1Cr-Mo-V steel. In the superalloy, this portion accounts for a longer period of life time than that for ductile materials. In the Ni-base superalloy, d a /d t  could be correlated with  C \*  for only 30% of life time, while in ductile 1Cr-Mo-V steel, d a /d t correlated with  C \*  for 60% of the life. In the brittle alloys the creep deformation area ahead of the crack tip was smaller, and the small scale creep conditions were considered to occupy the larger period of the life time. In order to standardize the CCG evaluation method for creep brittle alloys, it should be noted that the applicable range of  C \*  is quite limited.

3.3. The evaluation of da/dt and life based on the thermally activated process  

The CCG rate during the accelerating portion could be characterized by the  C \*  parameter, however, the applicable range of  C \*  in the life time was shorter for these creep brittle superalloys. In the superalloys, the constant CCG rate region occupied a larger part of the life time, that is about 50%, as shown in Fig. 3. Therefore, it is important to characterize d a /d t  in the constant rate region. In the early stage of crack growth, the crack may propagate under almost small-scale creep condition for these brittle alloys, and d a /d t  may be characterized by K .  

The relationship between d a /d t  at constant rate region and the initial stress intensity factor when the test was started,  K in , is shown in Fig. 9. Because the increase in the  K  value during the early stage was comparatively small, d a /d t  was plotted against the initial  K  value. The short-term data, for which the fracture time was less than 100 h, are plotted with round brackets as ( w ), ( q ) in Fig. 9. Except for the short-term data, the systematic dependence of d a / d t  on temperature and  K in  was observed as shown with the dashed lines in Fig. 9. In the short-   term test region at lower temperatures, in which  g 0 microstructure remained cubic as explained in Fig. 4, a dierent dependence on  K  was observed.  

By extrapolating the relations in Fig. 9, the d a /d t  at constant  K in  value was Arrenius plotted in Fig. 10. The activation energy for CCG in the higher temperature and lower load region was obtained as 496 kJ/mol. The result indicates that the CCG rate is dominated by the thermally activated process. From the tests conducted at constant  K in  at lower temperatures, where the microstructure is dierent, a larger value of activation energy, 1106 kJ/mol, was obtained [11].  

The activation energy for CCG was compared with the activation energy of creep deformation and that of self-diusion in Table 3. The activation energy for creep deformation was obtained from the data of NRIM Creep Data Sheet [20, 21]. In the creep ductile 1Cr-Mo- V steel, these values were virtually the same, therefore the creep crack growth was controlled by creep deformation. In the creep brittle superalloy, for which the creep deformation was small, the activation energy for crack growth was larger than that for creep deformation or for self-diusion of Ni. In the higher temperature region, this value approached that of creep deformation, but was still larger.

# 4. Conclusions  

CCG tests on Ni-base superalloys were carried out using CT specimens in a round robin sponsored by the Japanese group VAMAS TWA19 project for assisting in the standardization of the creep crack growth test and data evaluation methods. The eect of temperature, load, specimen thickness and microstructure on creep crack growth behavior was investigated. The applicable range of fracture mechanical parameters was discussed. The results are summarized as follows:  

1. Consistent CCG data between various laboratories which participated in the round-robin program on Ni-base superalloys were developed.

 2. The CCG behavior and activation energy of Ni-base superalloys were dependent on the  g ± g 0 microstructures.

 3. The d a /d t  could be characterized by the  C \*  parameter which was independent of temperature, load, specimen thickness and microstructure of materials in the region where CCG rates accelerated. For the Ni-base superalloys, however, the initial transition region in d a /d t  versus  C \*  relation occupied a larger part of life than that for creep ductile materials.

 4. The constant CCG rate can be represented by Eq. (7) based on a thermally activated process. The  Q \*  parameter for the Ni-base superalloy was derived. The crack growth life can be predicted approximately by using the  Q \*  method.
