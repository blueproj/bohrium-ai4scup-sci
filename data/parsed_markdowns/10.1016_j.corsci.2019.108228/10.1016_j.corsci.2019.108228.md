# A B S T R A C T  

# A R T I C L E I N F O  

Previous  ﬁ ndings demonstrated roughness increase in the oxide/ferritic Mn-depleted zone interface in an aus- tenitic FeMnSiCrNi shape memory stainless steel (SMSS) cyclic oxidized, generated by plastic deformation. The main aim here was to assess the causes of roughness evolution during cyclic oxidation of an FeMnSiCrNiCe SMSS at 950 °C. Thus, isothermal and cyclic oxidation tests were conducted, metal/oxide interface roughness was measured and the oxide layers characterized. The isothermal tests on the FeMnSiCrNiCe SMSS samples de- monstrated increasing roughness, however less than after cyclic tests, allowing the identi ﬁ cation of a more complete mechanism, where oxide growth and chemical e ﬀ ects also take place.

# 1. Introduction  

FeMnSi alloys are known by their shape memory e ﬀ ect. They have also recently become the most explored shape memory steel due to their workability, machinability, weldability and low-cost [ 1 ]. The addition of chromium to these alloys improved their corrosion resistance and interesting results have been achieved [ 2 – 5 ]. Rovere et al. [ 4 ] studied a FeMnSiCrNi shape memory stainless steel (SMSS) in oxidizing en- vironment (boiling nitric acid solution) and observed a lower corrosion rate in this alloy than in 304 austenitic stainless steel. On the other hand, high-temperature isothermal and cyclic oxidation have demon- strated that these alloys have higher mass gain than commercial stainless steel [ 6 , 7 ]. Souza et al. [ 7 ] documented manganese oxide formation and explained that this higher mass gain was due the pre- ferential Mn oxidation. Recently, a FeMnSiCrNiCo SMSS, with less manganese (8.26) and high content of chromium (12.8) and cobalt (11.84) presented a better oxidation resistance considering this kind of alloys, although with higher cost [ 8 ].  

Malafaia and Oliveira [ 9 ] noticed that this ferrite to austenite transformation also helps to explain an anomalous behavior of new mass gain after oxide spallation observed during cyclic tests at high temperature. Two phenomena related to the Mn-depleted zone forma- tion explained this anomalous behavior. First, as a result of ferrite ’ s coe ﬃ cient of thermal expansion (CTE) being smaller than austenite ’ s and closer to that of oxide ’ s CTE, the stresses generated during cooling decreased, preventing oxide spallation and promoting new mass gain. Second, on account of the austenite-ferrite CTE mismatch, compressive stress generated plastic deformation in the ferrite layer during cyclical cooling. Ferrite plastic deformation was also a metal/oxide interface roughening mechanism and was considered responsible for better oxide mechanical anchoring and stress relief in the oxide layer. These results were also better explained by  ﬁ nite element analysis [ 15 ], proposing that the roughening at metal/oxide interface also could take part in the spallation onset. When FeMnSiCrNi alloys are oxidized, the formation of a Mn-de- pleted zone near the metal/oxide interface is observed [ 6 , 7 , 9 , 10 ]. In the Mn-depleted zone, the austenitic matrix transforms into ferrite due to the selective Mn oxidation, which is an austenite stabilizer element in iron-based alloys. Previously, Coccia Lecis et al. [ 10 ] used this trans- formation to improve the corrosion resistance in an FeMnSiCr alloy, oxidizing and then removing the oxide before performing the corrosion  

isothermal study in an FeMnSiCrNi alloy also veri ﬁ ed a  “ very irregular ” interface [ 6 ].  

Metal surface roughness is an important subject in oxidation tests and applications, both in isothermal and cyclic conditions. Several studies reported roughening behavior in the thermal barrier coating (TBC) system, which is a well-known occurrence called rumpling and can provoke spallation in the top coat [ 16 – 18 ]. Regarding general oxidation, the oxide layer stresses can be induced by mechanical, thermal and/or oxide growth mechanisms [ 19 ]. In addition to studies related to the understanding of roughening generated by oxidation, other studies have revealed that initial surface roughness also in ﬂ u- ences the stresses generated [ 20 , 21 ], which can contribute to oxide spallation if at a high intensity [ 22 ]. Through  ﬁ nite element analysis, Chao and González-Carrasco [ 23 ] found that on a rough surface, the average oxide layer stress is smaller than the average stress on a  ﬂ at surface. Nevertheless, the maximum stress value is higher for the rough surface and is localized in the peaks of the roughness pro ﬁ le. Thus, these points, where stress concentrates, facilitate cracking and spalla- tion in the oxide layer.  

Currently, few studies have approached the roughness evolution at the metal/oxide interface for SMSS. Thus, the present study aimed to understand in depth the mechanism of roughness increasing at the metal/oxide interface of an FeMnSiCrNiCe SMSS during oxidation tests. Through isothermal and cyclic oxidation, the goal here was to compare the results and de ﬁ ne which parameters are behind roughness increase. AISI 310 was used as a comparison, due to it being a referential com- mercial alloy used at high temperatures.  

# 2. Experimental procedures  

# 2.1. Alloys  

AISI 310 austenitic stainless steel and an austenitic FeMnSiCrNiCe SMSS were tested. The AISI 310 steel was donated by Villares Metals© and the FeMnSiCrNiCe SMSS was melted in the casting laboratory of the Materials Engineering Department at the Federal University of São Carlos (DEMa/UFSCar). The SMSS used for this study was prepared in an induction furnace with an alumina crucible, under an argon atmo- sphere. The casting was performed through gravity in a sand mold with resin. AISI 1010 low carbon steel was used as the iron source, AISI 304 austenitic stainless steel and high-purity Mn, Si and Ce were used in the alloy melting. The alloy was solution treated at 1050 °C for a period of 1 h and quenched in water at room temperature. To reveal the micro- structure phases and cross-section after oxidation tests, etching with Vilella reagent (5 ml HCl +2 g picric acid +100 ml ethanol) was per- formed.  

The FeMnSiCrNiCe SMSS was proposed based on how cerium ben- e ﬁ ts oxidation resistance, i.e., lower oxidation rate and better oxide layer adherence [ 24 – 26 ]. Similarly, Souza et al. [ 7 ], in a previous study from our research group, proposed that cerium decreases the oxidation rate in FeMnSiCrNiCe SMSS, although no cerium oxides are formed. AISI 310 austenitic stainless steel was used for comparison, allowing the authors to determine the roughness increase at the metal/oxide inter- face of a conventional austenitic stainless steel after the cyclic test. The chemical composition of the alloys (wt.%) were previously determined [ 7 ] by energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) analysis and the data is presented in  Table 1 .

# 2.2. Oxidation tests  

Cyclic oxidation tests were performed on both alloys and isothermal oxidation tests were performed solely on the FeMnSiCrNiCe SMSS. Since the roughness increase had previously been associated with stress generated by cyclic tests [ 9 , 15 ], the isothermal tests were performed to evaluate if roughness increase could also be generated with the iso- thermal test conditions. For both the cyclic oxidation test and the iso- thermal test, four samples were prepared and tested for 2, 10, 30 and 76 cycles/hours. The samples were machined based upon the cyclic oxi- dation test guidelines published by Nicholls and Bennett [ 27 ], with a surface area of approximately 5 cm 2 . To standardize the sample sur- faces, they were sanded to a 600 grit on SiC paper, cleaned in ethanol with an ultrasonic bath for 2 min and then dried with hot air.  

The cyclic oxidation tests were performed in static air at 950  ° C in an automatic furnace that allowed the cycles to be programmed. The furnace moved vertically itself to expose the samples to either high or room temperature. The cycles lasted 1 h at experimental temperature and then cooled for 10 min in the mild air. During the tests the samples were hang by a hole. The isothermal oxidation tests also were per- formed in static air at 950  ° C and then the samples were removed from the furnace into room temperature. Before the oxidation tests, the samples were weighed and their dimensions (length, width and height) were measured. The changes in weight for oxidized specimens were measured using an electronic balance with an accuracy of 0.1 mg. For both tests the net mass variation was evaluated, as the possible spalled oxides were not collected.  

# 2.3. Layers characterization and measurement of roughness at the metal/ oxide interface  

The characterization of the oxidized layer was performed using scanning electron microscopy (SEM) with energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) and X-ray di ﬀ raction (XRD) with Cu K-alpha ra- diation. The SEM/EDS analyses were performed at the cross-section of oxidized specimens, which were previously cut, then sanded until 1200 grit on SiC paper, polished using an alumina solution and  ﬁ nally, chemically attached. The XRD was performed on the oxide layer ’ s surface.  

Finally, the initial surface roughness was measured  ﬁ ve times for each sample before the oxidation tests, using a pro ﬁ lometer, to assess the changes in the roughness of the metal/oxide interface. Considering that the metal/oxide interface is internal, after oxidation, the roughness was analyzed from images obtained at the cross sections using a scan- ning electron microscope (SEM) and an optical microscope (OM). The roughness parameters Rz and Ra were used to measure the roughness pro ﬁ le from the images. In both cases, the analysis length was 400  μ m and the cuto ﬀ was 80  μ m for Rz roughness.  

ImageJ ®  software was used to measure roughness in the metal/ oxide interface based on the images made by SEM and OM. To measure Rz roughness, the analysis length was divided into  ﬁ ve sections ( ﬁ ve cuto ﬀ s). The distance between the highest peak and the lowest valley of each cuto ﬀ was measured and the arithmetic mean of these  ﬁ ve values was calculated. To measure Ra roughness, a medium line was drawn in the images using the metal area. The metal area above the medium line plus the oxide area below the medium line divided by the analysis length is the Ra roughness. The Ra and Rz roughness measurements were repeated three times in di ﬀ erent areas of each oxidized sample.

# 4. Discussion  

# 4.1. Characterization: SMSS alloy, oxidation tests and oxide layer  

The microstructure observed at  Fig. 1 , as also the phases austenite and martensite determined by XRD are the typical ones, as also noticed by Rovere et al. [ 3 ] in an FeMnSiCrNi SMSS, showing that the alloy has the microstructure expected in shape memory stainless steels. Ad- ditionally, the high mass gain observed for the SMSS is in agreement with other authors [ 6 – 9 , 14 ], explained by the high manganese content in the alloy that increases the amount of weight gained due to the re- activity of this element with oxygen. Ma et al. [ 6 ] also compared SMSS with conventional stainless steel, isothermally at 800 °C for 100 h, showing a mass gain twelve times higher for the SMSS (2.88 mg/cm 2 against 0.23 mg/cm 2 ), similarly the magnitude observed here. Finally, the higher mass gain observed after isothermal oxidation for SMSS in comparison with cyclic tests can be related to the fact that cyclic tests imposes stresses that can generate some spallation [ 28 ], behavior al- ready reported to a SMSS alloy [ 9 ].

The di ﬀ usion of this element to the oxide layer destabilizes part of the austenitic matrix and promotes the transfor- mation of austenite to ferrite, which changes the microstructure of the alloy in the metal/oxide interface [ 7 , 9 , 10 ]. Although Mn-depleted zone is a well-known behavior, one important cause is that Mn di ﬀ usion is faster in ferrite than in austenite [ 31 , 32 ], promoting the ferrite layer increase.  

The  Fig. 5  showed the formation of Mn 3 O 4  (ICDD 96-900-1964) and Cr 0.5 Mn 2.5 O 4  (ICDD 04-002-3813) by XRD, both presenting tetragonal structure. The Mn-containing oxides do not present the same oxidation resistance than Cr 2 O 3  and because of this, the FeMnSiCrNiCe SMSS presented a high oxidation rate, compared to AISI 310. Previous studies demonstrated that SMSSs has less oxidation resistance than conven- tional stainless steels due to the Mn oxides and absence of pure Cr 2 O 3 layer [ 6 , 7 ] and Hua et al. [ 33 ] demonstrated that increasing manganese (from 0.0 to 0.5, 1.0 and 3.0 wt.%) in Fe-17Cr stainless steels decreases their oxidation resistance at 750 °C. Furthermore, it was observed that for higher oxidation time exposure the spinel peak intensity also in- creased and after 76 cycles, almost no evidence of Mn 3 O 4  was detected, result corroborated by  Fig. 6 , where Cr EDS map demonstrates that this element is present in all oxide layer, although more concentrated close to the metal/oxide interface. Indeed, two di ﬀ erent M 3 O 4  structures are possible considering Mn-Cr-O spinels, cubic or tetragonal, both (Mn,Cr) 3 O 4  [ 34 , 35 ]. The cubic phase can assume a complete solid so- lution, often denoted by Mn x Cr 3-x O 4 , while the tetragonal phase only allows a small amount of chromium in solid solution. Furthermore, the tetragonal phase is more stable for low Cr/(Cr + Mn) ratio values [ 34 , 35 ]. The Mn 3 O 4  and Cr 0.5 Mn 2.5 O 4  present the same structure, thus, the higher Mn 3 O 4  presence in the  ﬁ rst hours occurs because of the faster di ﬀ usion of manganese and the higher stability at higher oxygen pressure [ 6 , 32 ]. After the Mn-depleted zone formation, the Cr/ (Cr + Mn) ratio increases at the metal/oxide interface and increases the tendency of chromium solubilization in the tetragonal oxide. On the other hand, the Mn and Cr EDS measurements after 76 cycles ( Fig. 6 and  Table 2 ) demonstrated that content of chromium in the oxide layer near the metal/oxide interface (point 2) is higher than manganese, and the atomic proportion is close to the MnCr 2 O 4  spinel, while in the point 1, the composition presents less chromium. These results suggest that near the metal/oxide interface, the MnCr 2 O 4  (cubic) was formed and a less Cr-containing oxide, as Cr 0.5 Mn 2.5 O 4  (tetragonal), was only formed at the outer scale, similar to the predicted by Cr-Mn-O diagrams, ac- cording to high and low Cr/(Cr + Mn) ratio regions, respectively [ 34 , 35 ].

Roughness increase at metal/oxide interface after isothermal and cyclic oxidation tests, presented at  Fig. 7 , demonstrated that the roughness increase could not be explained only by plastic deformation and some chemical and or mechanical e ﬀ ect was missed in previous studies [ 9 , 15 ]. Based on literature, the stresses (mechanical e ﬀ ects) at the oxide layer can be induced by mechanical, thermal and/or oxide growth mechanisms [ 19 ]. Thus, the oxide growth stresses could con- tribute or be responsible for increasing roughness during isothermal oxidation, as no mechanical constriction or thermal variation occurred, and can explain the increase of roughness for all materials at 2 h/cycles. Tolpygo and Clarke [ 16 , 17 ] and Maurel et al. [ 18 ], evaluated nickel aluminides used in TBC by means of comparative isothermal and cyclic tests, where there was also a di ﬀ erent metallic layer (bond coat) be- tween the oxide and metallic matrix, aiming to understand metal/oxide interface roughening. The  ﬁ rst researchers observed that the cyclic test in air promoted an increase in roughness, but when an isothermal oxidation test was performed for an equal amount of time, the rough- ness increase was very small. The second group of researchers added that at the beginning of the process, the roughness increased in both conditions, but after some time (more than 50 h), the roughness maintained constant in the isothermal condition, but not in the cyclic one. This result was explained as a  “ natural ”  roughening generated by growth oxide stresses during the isothermal condition. This could help to explain why the isothermal condition presents considerable rough- ness in the present study, but here no stabilization was noticed, as some increase in roughness also happened between 30 and 76 h.  

A chemical e ﬀ ect could be another contribution to the increase in roughness at the metal/oxide interface. Douglass et al. [ 37 ] reported a phenomenon that produced a wavy interface and was unrelated to stresses. When oxidizing an FeMnCr alloy, they observed that the formed oxide grew inward into the Mn-depleted zone, due to the fact that the manganese interdi ﬀ usion in the alloy was much slower than the cation di ﬀ usion in the oxide. Therefore, the result presented here under the isothermal condition brings forth the hypothesis that an in- crease in roughness at the metal/oxide interface could be due to the combined e ﬀ ect of di ﬀ erent oxide growth and chemical phenomena. As presented before, more prominent valleys pointed by arrows at  Fig. 8 could be an indicative of the chemical phenomena observed by Dou- glass et al. [ 37 ], and described by Wagner [ 38 ]. As the ferrite layer increases, the Mn content near the surface decreases, as demonstrated at  Fig. 3  and  Table 2  and also previously reported [ 6 ]. Thus, because Mn di ﬀ usion in the oxide scale is faster than in the metal substrate, some inward oxides begin to be formed. The Mn di ﬀ usion coe ﬃ cients, determined experimentally from literature [ 31 , 39 ], at austenite, ferrite and spinel MnCr 2 O 4  at 900 °C are presented in  Table 3 , in accordance with this mechanism. For Mn 3 O 4  and spinel with less Cr amount oxides the di ﬀ usion data were not found. However, MnCr 2 O 4  is probably the oxide at metal/oxide interface as demonstrated by EDS results in  Fig. 6 . As already cited, the Mn-depleted zone is related to the slower Mn di ﬀ usion in austenite than in ferrite. Furthermore, the faster Mn dif- fusion at spinel explains, according Wagner theory [ 38 ], the irregular metal/oxide interface observed here.  

Finally, the sharper peaks presented only after cyclic oxidation ( Fig. 9 ) are in accordance with previous  ﬁ ndings [ 9 , 15 ], related to ferrite plastic deformation in stress concentration regions generated by the protuberances. Thus, the analysis performed here on isothermal oxidation allowed to understand that, although ferrite plastic de- formation occurs during cyclic oxidation, increasing roughness at metal/oxide interface, other e ﬀ ects as oxide growing and mainly che- mical e ﬀ ect also take important part in the mechanism of roughening at metal/oxide interface during SMSSs oxidation at high temperature.  

# 5. Conclusions  

The mechanism of roughness increasing at the metal/oxide interface in the FeMnSiCrNiCe SMSS during isothermal and cyclic oxidation tests at 950 °C was investigated. Cyclic tests on conventional AISI 310 stainless steel demonstrated that roughness at the metal/oxide interface of an FeMnSiCrNiCe SMSS was substantially higher after cyclic or iso- thermal tests. The continuous increase in roughness during isothermal and cyclic tests was explained. The main  ﬁ nding was that a chemical e ﬀ ect, generated by the formation of the Mn-depleted zone and fast manganese di ﬀ usion through the oxide layer compared to ferrite, also contributes to roughness increase during the FeMnSiCrNiCe SMSS oxi- dation. Finally, the higher roughness increase observed during cyclic tests was related to previous  ﬁ ndings concerning ferrite plastic de- formation.
