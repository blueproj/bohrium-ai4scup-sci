ABSTRACT:  Enamides and their derivatives are prominent bioactive pharmacophores found in various bioactive molecules. Herein we report a palladium-catalyzed oxidative  N - α , β -dehydro- genation of amides to produce a range of enamides with high yields and excellent tolerance toward different functional groups. Mechanistic studies indicate that the reaction involves allylic C(sp 3 ) − H activation followed by  β -H elimination. The effective- ness of this approach is demonstrated through late-stage function aliz ation of bioactive molecules and the synthesis of valuable compounds through product elaboration.  

T he physicochemical properties and the presence of multiple active reaction sites of enamides/dienamides have rendered them versatile motifs in organic synthesis.   They have been extensively used in cycloaddition reactions, asymmetric hydrogenations,   and transition-metal-catalyzed function aliz ation reactions 4   since their first report in 1963. Diels − Alder (DA) cycloadditions of 1-amido-dienes have proven to be a powerful reaction manifold. Enamides are also present in natural products and drug molecules, showcasing anticancer and antibacterial activities.   Numerous classical methods have been utilized for the synthesis of enamides and their derivatives, including the Wittig reaction, Curtius rearrangement, and transition-metal-catalyzed coupling reactions.   However, these approaches often involve complex preparation procedures or harsh reaction conditions. The low yield and narrow substrate range also limit the application of these methods. The state-of-the-art method for the synthesis of enamides is the isomerization of  N -allyl amides reported by Trost.   The chelation of high unsaturated CpRu catalyst and substrates determined the configuration of the products. While this approach allowed for the rapid construction of a range of polysubstituted  Z -enamides,  N -alkyl or other  N -substituted amides were not available substrates in this system. Therefore, developing an efficient and environmentally friendly method for generating structurally and functionally diverse enamides is of practical significance.

These catalytic pathways for dehydrogenation typically passed through metal enolates from ketones, followed by  β - H elimination to generate the enone products ( Figure 1 a). The acidity of the  α -H of carbonyl compounds is critical for the formation of metal enolates. Consequently, dehydrogenation of acid and other substrates with weak acidity of  α -H is far more challenging in enolate chemistry. Recently, Yu and co- workers described ligand-controlled dehydrogenative reactions of carboxylic acids via  β -methylene C − H activation.   The use of bidentate pyridine-pyridone ligands not only facilitated C(sp 3 ) − H activation but also prevented product inhibition. Nevertheless, this strategy of dehydrogenation through  β -C − H activation faces two challenges: (i) the formation of the five- membered cyclic metal complex between the directing group and metal catalyst is not conducive to subsequent  β -H elimination; (ii) C − H activation of methylene usually requires high temperatures and strong oxidants. Therefore, the design of substrates and control of reaction conditions also pose limitations to the advancement of this method. Our group has a longstanding dedication to the palladium- catalyzed oxidative allylic C(sp 3 ) − H activation of unactivated olefins.   The highly active  π -allyl complexes could be captured by alkylamines, alcohols, and other nucleophiles under the tailored catalytic system. Despite recent advancements in this area, the study of dehydrogenation reaction via palladium- catalyzed allylic C − H activation was relatively rare ( Figure 1 b). There are only a few examples of dehydrogenation of  π - allyl species to dienes.   One of the primary challenges in achieving this transformation is the susceptibility of  π - allylpalladium intermediates to being quenched through three main pathways: (i) rapid attack by nucleophiles, such as H 2 O in the reaction; (ii) direct C − O reductive elimination of hydroquinone; (iii) protonation of  σ -palladium species resulting in the formation of inert internal alkenes. Accordingly, in order to overcome the above competitive reactions and realize the dehydrogenation reactions through allylic C − H activation, the coordination form of allyl- palladium and its electrophilic it y must be well controlled. On the basis of previous studies, phosphorus-based ligands have been identified as a class of effective ligands that could promote palladium-catalyzed allylic C − H activation through ligand participation.   Steric environment, electronic proper- ties, and coordination mode could be well-tuned by ligand modifications, thereby significantly impacting the chemical selectivity and stereo selectivity of the reaction.

With the optimal reaction conditions in hand, we next investigated the substrate scope in this transformation ( Table 2 ). A variety of aryl amides were examined first, substituting the phenyl ring with electron-rich and electron-withdrawing groups were well-tolerated. The electronic effects on the aromatic ring had minimal impact on the dehydrogenation yield. For a  para -substituted aryl amide containing a halogen atom, a yield of 52% was achieved ( 5 ). Substituents at  ortho - and  meta -positions on the aryl amide could be accommodated ( 6  and  7 ). Moreover, polysubstituted substrates, exemplified by compounds  8 − 10 , were successfully utilized. However, a noticeable decrease in yield was observed when  ortho -double- substituted substrates were employed. For 2,6-bis-halogen substituted amide, the dehydrogenation yield decreased to 34% ( 9 ). Heterocyclic amides, such as furan and thiophene carboxamides, were also found to be suitable for the reaction,

This protocol provided a conventional and efficient approach for late-stage modification of bioactive molecule derivatives containing an  N -(but-3-en-1-yl)-amide group. For example, two analgesics and anti-inflammatory drugs ketorolac and ibuprofen derivatives could undergo the dehydrogenation reaction under the standard conditions and gave the desired products  35  and  40  with moderate to good yields. The late- stage modification of chlorambucil successfully afforded the product  36  in 63% yield. Additionally, adapalene, a drug used to treat gout could also proceed with the modification process without difficulty. To further unravel the underlying synthetic value of this method, a 1.0 mmol scale experiment was successfully conducted to deliver dehydrogenative product  15 with 55% yield under the optimized reaction ( Figure 2 ). The addition of the corresponding ester or ketone instead of amide under the standard condition failed to deliver the desired dehydrogenation product ( Figure 3 b), demonstrating that the electron cloud density of alkenes and coordination between the catalyst and nitrogen atom were important to the reaction. Subsequently, the cleavage of the allylic C − H bond forms the highly active allyl-Pd electrophile intermediate II , followed by  β -hydride elimination in the presence of base to yield the target enamides. It is worth noting that when the substituent R 2   on the amide is an H atom,  E -enamides could be obtained, and when the substituent is methyl or other alkyl, the dehydrogenative product was delivered as a mixture of  Z / E isomers in a 1:1 ratio. The possible reason is that when R 2   is an H atom, the six-membered cyclic metal complex is not conducive to subsequent  β -H elimination. The enamide of formula  E  will be preferentially generated in the dominant conformation ( Figure 4 b). When R 2   is a bulky substituent, the resulting six-membered ring palladium intermediate is unstable due to steric hindrance, making subsequent  β -H elimination more likely. Additionally, the elimination process in coordina- tion mode requires higher energy, resulting in lower yields of substituted amides compared to unsubstituted ones.  

In conclusion, a general protocol for the construction of dienamides from readily available amide substrates has been disclosed. The combination of a monodentate phosphine ligand, palladium catalyst, quinone oxidant, and base was crucial for the dehydrogenation reactions. The mild reaction condition and straightforward procedure allowed excellent functional group compatibility and broad substrate scope. The potential utility of this method was demonstrated by late-stage modification of bioactive molecules and drugs as well as by elaborating the corresponding products to access various synthetically valuable compounds. A series of mechanistic investigations preliminarily revealed the importance of the position of the amide group relative to the alkene for the reaction and the influence of the substituents on the amide on the stereo selectivity of this dehydrogenation reaction.
