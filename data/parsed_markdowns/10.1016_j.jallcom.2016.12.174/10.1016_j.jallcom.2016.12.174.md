In this paper, we have implemented a speci ﬁ c hot rolling and solution treatments on Incoloy 800H to examine the evolution of texture and microstructure during this process. We also focused on the analysis of local misorientation (LM) and grain boundary character distribution through electron backscattered diffraction (EBSD) method. The results showed that the hot-rolled microstructure is mainly composed of elongated grains with typical texture components of B, Cu and S at the mid-layer of plates, while sub- stantial inhomogeneous  ﬁ ne grains can be observed between the deformation grains at the surface-layer showing a combination of R-C and R-Cu components. Following solution treatment, the original deformed grains are fully substituted by the  ﬁ ne recrystallized grains and the grain size increases signi ﬁ cantly at higher solution temperatures. An alternant lamellar structure of  ﬁ ne and coarse grains forms along the rolling direction at 1050    C due to the pinning effect of TiC at  ﬁ ne grains boundaries. Besides, C and G are the dominant texture components after solution treatment. With increasing solution temperature, the overall level of LM and micro-hardness decrease stably and a higher ratio of n   S 3: S (9  þ  27) indicates that  S 3 boundaries proliferation mechanism transforms from  “ S 3 regeneration ” to  “ new twinning ” .

# 1. Introduction  

In metalworking, rolling is termed as the process of plastically deforming metal through passing it between rolls. It is well known that rolling is the most widely used forming process, which pro- vides high production and close control of  ﬁ nal product. Generally, the rolling process can be categorized into hot rolling and cold rolling according to the metal-rolling temperature. Hot rolling is a metalworking process that occurs above the recrystallization temperature of metallic material. The initial breakdown of ingots into billets is usually accomplished by hot rolling which is followed by further processing into plate, sheet, rod and bar and also pro- vides raw stuff for consequently cold rolling. In order to obtain the desired microstructure and improved mechanical properties, so- lution treatment is often performed after hot rolling through holding a material long enough at a suf ﬁ ciently high temperature to allow the constituents to be dissolved into the solid solution  [1 e 3]  

Our previous investigations  [9 e 12]  examined the microstruc- ture evolution and hot working properties during hot deformation of Incoloy 800H through uniaxial compression tests. We discussed the in ﬂ uence of deformation parameters on the microstructure (dynamic recrystallization  [9] , precipitation behavior  [10]  and grain boundary character distribution  [11] ) and also established the hot

# 2. Experimental procedure  

Incoloy 800H plates in the form of hot rolled and annealed condition with a thickness of 26 mm were used as initial materials in this study. The chemical composition of the investigated alloy is given in  Table 1 . These plates were soaked at 1200    C for 2 h and then subjected to hot rolling with the accumulated reduction of 80% through seven passes reverse rolling on a laboratory rolling mill. The details of rolling procedure are presented in  Table 2  and the hot-rolled samples were labeled according to the start rolling temperatures, namely HR1050 and HR950. The hot-rolled plates with a target thickness of 5 mm were quenched into water immediately to retain deformation microstructure. Samples with 3   the dimensions of 15    15    5 mm were cut from the central part of hot-rolled plates and further subjected to different heat- treatment schedules, during which the samples were solution treated for 30 min at 950    C, 1050    C, and 1150    C before water cooled to the room temperature.  

Microstructure observations were carried out on the plane consisting of rolling direction (RD) and normal direction (ND). The electron backscattered diffraction (EBSD) analysis was performed using an Oxford HKL Channel 5 system attached to a Zeiss UltraPlus analytical  ﬁ eld emission gun scanning electron microscope (FEG- SEM). The samples for EBSD were electrochemical polished in a solution of 4% HClO 4  with alcohol at 25 V for 25 e 30s and Channel 5 software by HKL technology was used to analyze EBSD data. For the standard noise reduction of EBSD data, wild spikes were extrapo- lated  ﬁ rst and then a medium level of zero solution extrapolation was conducted and iterated until no zero solution remained. A minimum misorientation threshold of 2    was set for the EBSD analysis. The grain boundaries with misorientation angle of 2 e 15  and greater than 15    were de ﬁ ned as low-angle grain boundaries (LAGBs) and high-angle grain boundaries (HAGBs), respectively. The local variation in orientation was evaluated by the local misorientation (LM) component based on the  ﬁ ltered crystal orientation. The LM value was determined through calculating the average misorientation between a pixel and its surrounding pixels and then assigning the mean value to the pixel. The distribution of LM was illustrated with a  ﬁ lter size of 5    5 and sub-grain angle of 5  . The boundaries with misorientation angles exceeding 5    were discarded in this calculation. Brandon's criterion was applied to identify the  “ coincidence site lattice ”  (CSL) grain boundary  [13] . Orientation distribution functions (ODFs) were calculated after correction and symmetrization using the coef ﬁ cient calculation (series expansion method) with expansion to  L max  ¼  22 and a cluster size of 5  . The distribution of different crystallographic orientations over the entire scanned region was displayed by ODFs insider the  4 2  ¼  45    and  4 2  ¼  65    sections of the Euler space. Thin foils for transmission electron microscope (TEM) investigation were prepared by jet polishing with perchloric acid and methanol solu- tion (1:9) at   30    C and \~40 mA. The TEM examination was per- 2   formed on a FEI Tecnai G F20 microscope operated at 200 kV. The distribution of different chemical elements was characterized using a JXA-8530F electron probe microanalyzer (EPMA). The samples for EPMA were prepared by standard mechanical polishing and chemically etched in a solution of 4 g CuSO 4 , 20 mL HCl and 20 mL H 2 O. Vickers hardness testing was conducted under a load of 50gf with a dwell time of 10s on a FM-700 micro-hardness tester. An average value of ten points was used to determine the hardness of each sample.

# 3.4. Grain boundary character distribution  

It is widely acknowledged that grain boundary character dis- tribution (GBCD) that describes the proportions of random and special grain boundaries (termed as coincidence site lattice, CSL) can effectively change the performance and property of metallic materials at high-temperature service  [21 e 23] . Through altering the GBCD, grain boundary engineering (GBE) can generate a high fraction of low- S  CSL boundaries with low boundary energy, which has good resistance to high temperature fracture and lower rate of grain boundary sliding during creep  [24 e 27] . It has been reported n   that a large number of  S 3 boundaries can be introduced into microstructure of Incoloy 800H associated with the formation of annealing twins  [28] . As shown in  Fig. 14 , it signi ﬁ es that the n   fraction of  S 3 boundaries increases steeply after solution treat- ments compared to the hot rolling state and the number of these boundaries drops continually with the growth of recrystallized grains at higher solution temperatures. It is important to realize n   that  S 3 boundaries are incorporated into the HAGBs network as convoluted boundaries and extensively disrupt the connectivity of this network, particularly when solution treatment is performed at 950    C. In contrast, most of the  S 3 boundaries are observed to be long, straight parallel pairs or multiples and not embedded in the HAGBs network at a higher solution temperature of 1150    C.  

The length fractions of  S 3 ( f S 3 ),  S 9 and  S 27 ( f S (9 þ 27) ) with different solution treatments were measured and the results were presented in  Fig. 15 . As expected, these fractions are at the lowest level following hot rolling due to the fragmentation of twin boundaries after severe deformation. Besides, it can be seen that the variation of  f S 3  and  f S (9 þ 27)  shows a similar decreasing trend with larger grain size at higher solution temperature irrespective of start rolling temperature. V. Randle et al.  [29]  proposed two  

n main mechanisms accounting for the proliferation of S 3 boundaries, namely  “ S 3 regeneration ”  and  “ new twinning ” . In practice, these two mechanisms will take effect simultaneously for all twin-related GBE metals, such as Cu-alloy, Ni-alloy and austenitic stainless steel (ASS), but typically one mechanism will be a mainstay. Speci ﬁ cally, the  “ S 3 regeneration ”  contributes to the main formation of convoluted  S 3 boundaries replacing part of the HAGBs network while long and straight parallel pairs/multi- ples of  S 3 boundaries can be largely obtained via  “ new twinning ” with few  S 3 boundaries incorporated into the HAGBs. The ratio of S 3: S (9  þ  27) can be rationally used to determine the twin pro- liferation mechanism with the criteria that a lower ratio implies the predominant effect of  “ S 3 regeneration ”  whilst a higher ratio mostly corresponds to the function of  “ new twinning ”  [24,29,30] . Fig. 15  also depicts that the  S 3: S (9  þ  27) ratios progressively rise with the increasing solution temperature, which suggests that the main twinning mechanism is transformed from  “ S 3 regeneration ” to  “ new twinning ”  during this process. The previous research  [29] also proposed that the S 3: S (9 þ 27) ratio indicates the n   morphology of  S 3 boundaries. All of these points are in good agreement with the aforementioned microstructure observations n   for  S 3 boundaries morphology.  

# 4. Conclusion  

The focus of this study is to investigate the evolution of texture and microstructure on hot-rolled Incoloy 800H plates with different solution treatments. The principal conclusions of this work are listed as below:  

(1) The hot-rolled microstructure primarily consists of severely elongated deformation grains. The LM tends to be larger near  

grain boundaries, particularly along the intragranular shear bands. At the mid-layer of hot-rolled plates, a strong texture of B, Cu and S components can be observed and the intensity decreases signi ﬁ cantly at lower start rolling tem- perature. At the surface-layer of hot-rolled plates, some inhomogeneous  ﬁ ne grains between deformation grains can be found and the R-C and R-Cu components become inten- si ﬁ ed instead.  

(2) Fine recrystallized grains completely substitute the original deformed grains after solution treatment and the grain size rises distinctly with increasing solution temperature. An alternant lamellar structure of  ﬁ ne and coarse grains along RD can be observed after solution treatment at 1050    C due to the pinning effect of TiC on the  ﬁ ne grains boundaries. The overall intensity of texture is weakened obviously after so- lution treatment. A strong C component with a fairly weak Cu component can be detected in the HR950-S1050 sample, while the G component becomes strong for the HR1050- S1150 sample and a weak C component can be observed in both of the samples after solution treatment at 1150    C.

 (3) The overall level of LM following solution treatment is rela- tively lower compared to that after hot rolling. Besides, LM p values drop smoothly with increasing solution temperature, indicating a continual decrease of LM level. The micro- hardness of samples at different start rolling temperatures both decreases with increasing grain size at higher solution temperature. n  

 (4) The fraction of  S 3 boundaries has a signi ﬁ cant increase after solution treatments compared to that of hot rolling state. As solution temperature increases, these fractions are reduced n   steadily and the morphology of  S 3 boundaries also changes evidently with a higher ratio of  S 3: S (9  þ  27), which suggests that proliferation mechanism of  S 3   boundaries mainly changes from  “ S 3 regeneration ”  to  “ new twinning ” .
