# A study on the microstructure and cyclic oxidation behavior of the pack aluminized Hastelloy X at 1100 °C  

⁎   c Jyh-Wei Lee , Yu-Chu Kuo  

a  Department of Mechanical Engineering, Tung Nan Institute of Technology, #152, Section 3, Pei-Shen Road, Shen-Ken, Taipei 222, Taiwan b  Research Center for Micro/Nanotechnology, Tung Nan Institute of Technology, #152, Section 3, Pei-Shen Road, Shen-Ken, Taipei 222, Taiwan c  Institute of Mechatronic Engineering, Tung Nan Institute of Technology, #152, Section 3, Pei-Shen Road, Shen-Ken, Taipei 222, Taiwan  

Available online 7 September 2006  

# Abstract  

A pack aluminizing process at 950 °C for 9 h has been employed on the nickel-base superalloy Hastelloy X to deposit a 75  μ m thick  β -NiAl aluminide layer on the surface. A nanoscale dendritic structure is observed on the surface of the aluminide coating. A finger-like interdiffusion zone is found between the aluminide layer and the substrate. Fine precipitates with complex phases are distributed in the NiAl layer. The cyclic oxidation tests of aluminized alloys and untreated substrates were conducted at 1100 °C for 196 h. It was observed that the aluminizing process greatly enhances the cyclic oxidation resistance of Hastelloy X at 1100 °C due to a dense and protective alumina layer formed on the surface. Complex phase transformation reactions occurred in the aluminide layer. Owing to the oxidation and interdiffusion reactions at high temperature, the Al content of the NiAl layer was depleted to form some low Al containing  γ -substrate grains on the surface and a continuous  γ  layer between the aluminide layer and substrate. Thermal stress induced, transverse cracks in the interdiffusion zone, were observed possibly due to the difference of thermal expansion coefficients among the substrate, aluminide layer and interdiffusion zone.

# 1. Introduction  

The nickel-base superalloy, Hastelloy X, has wide use in gas turbine engines for combustion zone components, afterburners, tailpipes and etc. In gas turbine applications, the components are subjected to repeated thermal cycles during operations. Consider- ing the engine efficiency and performance, the operation tempe- rature of modern gas turbine engines is getting higher and higher [1] . Thus, the oxidation resistance of superalloys under cyclic high temperature environments is becoming an important issue. For the nickel-base superalloys, aluminum is the most commonly depo- sited element to produce nickel aluminide coatings by the pack cementation  [2 – 4] , chemical vapor deposition  [5,6]  and physical vapor deposition  [7]  processes. At high operation temperature, the aluminide surface will form a dense and adhesive alumina scale to provide sufficient oxidation and hot corrosion resistance.  

Some effects influencing the oxidation resistance of the aluminide coating include the interdiffusion of the coating and substrate, the spalling of the protective alumina layer and the cracking of the coating  [8] . To improve the performance of the nickel aluminide coatings, a better understanding of the com- plex changes in structures and properties during high temper- ature service is required. In this study, the structures, properties and possible failure mechanisms of a nickel aluminide coating provided by the pack cementation process on Hastelloy X after cyclic oxidation testing at 1100 °C for 196 h are investigated.  

# 2. Experimental procedure  

Rectangular plates of dimensions 15 mm×15 mm×1 mm for Hastelloy X were used as the substrate material. The nominal chemical composition of Hastelloy X is Ni – 22% Cr – 18% Fe – 9% Mo – 1.5% Co – 0.6% W – 1% Mn – 1% Si – 0.1% C (in wt.%). The substrates were abraded with # 1200 SiC paper, washed in distilled water and ultrasonically degreased in acetone. Pack cementation aluminizing was conducted at 950 °C for 9 h. The details of the pack aluminization process have been reported elsewhere  [9] .

Fig. 1. The granular morphology and fine dendritic structures (on the upper left corner of this image) on the surface of Hastelloy X specimen pack aluminized at 950 °C for 9 h.  

Cyclic oxidation tests of two aluminized specimens and two substrates were conducted at 1100 °C in an automatic box furnace for 96 cycles (196 h). Each cycle included 110 min at 1100 °C, followed by air cooling and holding for 13 min at ambient. Weight changes were measured every 4 cycles with a precision electronic − 4   balance with 1×10 g accuracy.  

Surface morphologies of each specimen were further analyzed with a field emission electron probe microanalyzer (FE-EPMA, JXA-8500F, JEOL). The cross-sectional elemental distributions of specimens were also detected by FE-EPMA with the aid of a ZAF-corrected program. The phases in the specimens were identified with an X-ray diffractometer (XRD, Rigaku, Model DMAX-B) equipped with a Cu target.  

# 3. Results and discussion  

# 3.1. Microstructures and phases of the aluminide layer  

Compared with other aluminide coatings in the literature [6,7] , very fine granular surface structures are observed for the specimen aluminized at 950 °C for 9 h in this work. As shown in Fig. 1 , the average grain size of the surface aluminide layer is

 3.0±0.6  μ m. According to the research work by Anderson et al.

 [4] , very fine grain size of the aluminide coating is attributed to the precipitated substrate elements entrapped during the inward diffusion of aluminum. In the upper left corner of  Fig. 1 , an insert image at higher magnification, 10,000×, depicts the nanoscale dendritic structures. The width of the dendritic arms is ranges from 100 to 300 nm. Although the formation mech- anism differs, a nanoscale terraced substructure on the surface of an aluminide layer provided by a direct vapor deposition process was also reported by Yu et al.  [7] . The X-ray diffraction pattern of the as-aluminized surface is shown in  Fig. 2 . The XRD peaks of Al 2 O 3  appear due to some alumina filler particles entrapped on the surface during the pack aluminizing process. It is noticeable that the Al 3+ x Mo 1 − x  phase is also detected, which was seldom reported in the literature. It is suggested that the rather high Mo concentration of Hastelloy X compared to most nickel-base superalloys is the possible reason for the formation of the Al 3+ x Mo 1 − x  phase.  

A cross section view of the aluminide layer is shown in  Fig. 3 (a). The thickness of the aluminide layer is around 75  μ m. Fine precipitate phases are interspersed throughout the outer zone of the coating. A complex and finger like interdiffusion zone, as indicated by an arrow in  Fig. 3 (a), between the aluminide layer and substrate is obvious. The corresponding elemental distri- bution of the aluminide layer is shown in  Fig. 3 (b). In general, the aluminum content decreases from the surface, while nickel increases. Some scatter of the concentration profiles of alu- minum, chromium and nickel are found due to the precipitates, for example, Al 3+ x Mo 1 − x  phase and Cr – Mo rich carbides ob- served in  Fig. 3 (a). The chemical composition of the aluminide layer is 52.90% Al – 26.75% Ni – 9.91% Fe – 8.05% Cr – 2.03% Mo (in at.%), which is identified to be  β -NiAl phase by XRD. Apparently, the  β -NiAl phase is a solid solution with iron and chromium dissolved. The finger like structure in the inter- diffusion zone has considerably different composition. The average chemical composition of this structure is 36.90% Cr –

# 3.3. Microstructures and phases of oxidized specimens  

# 3.3.1. Hastelloy X substrate  

Large oxide nodules are found on the surface of the substrate after the cyclic oxidation test at 1100 °C. A rough surface is observed due to a severe oxidation reaction and spallation of oxide scales. The thickness of the residual scale is around 10 to 25  μ m. Through X-ray mapping analysis, it is found that the surface oxide is mainly rich in Cr and contains smaller amounts of Ni and Fe. Based on the X-ray diffraction patterns of the substrate before and after cyclic oxidation testing, it is clear that the surface oxides are composed of Cr 2 O 3  and FeCr 2 O 4 .  

# 3.3.2. Aluminide coating  

It is found that only some oxide scales are still adherent to the surface of the aluminide coating after test. Apparently, spallation of the protective oxide is responsible for the higher mass loss. Through the XRD patterns depicted in  Fig. 2 , the retained surface oxide of the cyclic oxidized aluminide coating is  α -Al 2 O 3 . In addition to the  β -NiAl phase, the  γ -substrate phase is also shown. The backscattered electron (BSE) image of the cross section of the aluminide layer after the cyclic test is shown in  Fig. 5 (a). Some large grains gray in color and small grains white in color are randomly distributed in the outermost region of the coating. A discrete layer is found between the outer zone and the interdiffusion zone. Some vertical pores are also obvious in this layer. The finger- like structure, Cr 4.6 MoNi 2.1  phase, in the interdiffusion zone coalesces into a continuous and irregular shaped layer. A more detailed feature of the outer zone and the interdiffusion zone is illustrated in  Fig. 5 (b) and (c), respectively. In  Fig. 5 (b), some pores are obvious in the outer zone, which are formed due to the condensation of vacancies. The contrast difference in the BSE image indicates that there are three different phases shown. The chemical compositions of six selected regions are analyzed by FE- EPMA and listed in  Table 1 . Combining with the XRD results shown in  Fig. 2  and the chemical compositions listed in  Table 1 , the gray color grains near the outermost surface (region #1, #4 and #5 in  Fig. 5 (b)) are the Al alloyed  γ -substrate phases, which are transformed from the  β -NiAl due to the selective oxidation of Al in aluminide layer at high temperature. In regions # 2 and #6, the average Al contents are around 28.5 at.%, which are confirmed to be the  β -NiAl phases by XRD. In region #3, a Mo and Cr rich equiaxed precipitate, the Cr 18 Mo 42 Ni 40  phase is found which is possibly transformed from the Al 3+ x Mo 1 − x  phase due to the out- ward diffusion of Al. In  Fig. 5 (c), the secondary electron (SE) image reveals a continuous and irregular shape layer (region #7) between the substrate and the outer zone. The chemical compo- sition of this layer is almost identical to that of region 3 in  Fig. 5 (b). It is believed that the Cr 4.6 MoNi 2.1  phase had transformed into Cr 18 Mo 42 Ni 40  phase after long time exposure. The carbides were gradually densified and coarsened into large spherical particles adjacent to the continuous layer. Electron microprobe data suggests that these particles in regions #8 and # 10 are M 6 C type carbides containing Fe, Ni, Cr, W and Mo. In region #9, the chemical composition is also identical to that of Al alloyed  γ -substrate in  Fig. 5 (b), which was formed due to the inward and outward diffusion of Al in the aluminide coating.  

# 3.4. Failure mechanism of aluminide  

It is well known that  β -NiAl is the most desirable phase due to its stability and superior oxidation resistance. The lower aluminide phases, such as Ni 3 Al and Al alloyed  γ -substrate are undesirable due to their inferior oxidation resistance  [1,2,10] . The depletion of Al from the coating is considered as a rate- controlling factor in the assessment of coating life at high temperature. For oxidation tests at temperatures higher than 1000 °C, the loss of Al by inward and outward diffusion must be considered  [10] . Due to both oxidation and interdiffusion, the Al concentration decreases and correspondingly, the  β -NiAl transforms to Al-depleted phases. Although the Ni 3 Al phase in not detected in this study, the transformation of  β -NiAl into Ni 3 Al phase and finally into the Al alloyed  γ -substrate is a possible degradation mechanism of the coating. In addition, the rapid increase in the mass loss after 70 cycles is caused by the spallation of protective  α -Al 2 O 3  scale, which may be caused by the strain energy generated by the growth of oxide scale at high temperature and the thermal expansion mismatch upon cooling to room temperature  [11] .  

The effect of precipitates in the interdiffusion zone on coating life is quite contradictory. Godlewesk and Godlewski [12]  suggested that the Cr-rich precipitates could act as diffusion barriers to enhance the long-term durability, whereas propagation of cracks parallel to the surface between the NiAl outer layer and the interdiffusion zone has been reported by Totemeier et al.  [13] . In this work, the Cr 18 Mo 42 Ni 40  phase is detrimental to the coating life due to some cracks parallel to the surface which are found in the interdiffusion zone and might be a starting point of spallation after long-term service at repeated thermal cycles. It is believed that the thermal expansion mis- match between the precipitate layer, Cr 18 Mo 42 Ni 40  phase, in the interdiffusion zone and the substrate is the possible reason for the cracking.  

# 4. Conclusions  

A nickel aluminide coating on Hastelloy X has been pro- duced by pack cementation aluminizing at 950 °C for 9 h. A very fine grain size with nanoscale dendritic structure is ob- served in the aluminide surface. Complex precipitates and in- termetallic compounds are found in the outer and interdiffusion zones. A protective  α -Al 2 O 3  scale formed on the aluminide surface to improve the cyclic oxidation resistance of the alloy after testing at 1100 °C for 196 h (96 cycles). The Al 3+ x Mo 1 − x phase decomposed into Cr 18 Mo 42 Ni 40  grains in the outer zone. Large M 6 C carbide nodules and an agglomerated Cr 18 Mo 42 Ni 40 layer in the interdiffusion zone are also investigated. The de- graded Al alloyed  γ -substrate grains appeared in both the surface region and the interdiffusion zone of the aluminide coating due to outward and inward diffusion of Al, respectively. The outward and inward diffusion of Al, spallation of oxide scale and transverse cracks in the interdiffusion zone are the main degradation mechanisms of the aluminide coating.
