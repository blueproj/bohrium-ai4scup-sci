# 1. Introduction  

Turbine blades in aircraft engines are exposed to severe service conditions. The creep properties  are of great importance to the service safety of turbine blades by the reason of the combination of  high temperature and complex stress distribution [1-3]. Therefore, turbine blades are usually made of  Ni-based superalloys because of the excellent creep properties. Wrought superalloys with low  alloying degree are widely used as turbine blades which operate at intermediate temperature range.  The creep properties of wrought alloys are generally influenced by the solid solution strengthening  ’ phase within grain interiors as well as the  

Since the service temperature of blades varied greatly during a typical flight cycle [1] and  ACCEPTED during the emergency regimes, such as one engine inoperative [5], normal service exposure and  overheating exposures could be introduced to turbine blades. Therefore, the blades would undergo a  series of microstructural changes associated with time, temperature and stress during service,  resulting in degradation of mechanical properties of serviced blades [6-10]. These microstructural  changes can be classified in normal and overheating microstructural degradation according to the  service condition. Previous studies have typified normal microstructural degradations in turbine  blades by the morphology evolution of grain boundary (GB) and GB precipitates, coarsening and  rafting of  γ ’ precipitates, decomposition of primary MC carbides as well as the precipitation of  topological closed packed (TCP) phases [7, 8, 11, 12]. Overheating exposure was a typical kind of  abnormal service condition. Typical overheating microstructural degradation emerged immediately  during the overheating exposure and were in terms of dissolution of  γ ’ precipitates and secondary  carbides, grain growth and even incipient melting in the blade alloy [9, 13, 14]. The re-precipitation  of  γ ’ phase and GB carbides in degraded morphologies would occur during the cooling procedure  and the subsequent normal service exposure at lower temperatures [5, 9, 15]. Although normal and  overheating microstructural degradations were reported in previous studies [7, 9], comparative  investigation on both kinds of microstructural degradation has been rarely reported in blades made of  wrought superalloys after different service histories.  

The microstructural degradations resulted in decreased mechanical properties of turbine blades,  such as the creep properties [6, 8, 16]. It is well accepted that the creep properties of superalloys  would be deteriorated by the change of grain size, morphologies of GB precipitates and  ’ phase  [17-19]. The creep resistance would be enhanced by increasing grain size for wrought superalloys [4,  17]. The fine discrete GB precipitates were preferred over the continuous and coarse discrete ones  along grain boundaries for the sake of creep properties [20-23]. The creep properties were also  ACCEPTED ’ phase. Significant increase in creep resistance was observed in  γ ’ phase [15, 19, 24, 25]. Besides  creep properties, Vickers hardness of blades were believed to be sensitive to the dissolution of  γ ’  precipitates. Therefore, Vickers hardness tests were taken into the regular inspections to examine  overheating operation for turbine blades made of wrought superalloys with low volume fraction of  γ ’  phase [10]. Nevertheless, the impacts of microstructural degradation, especially the overheating  microstructural degradation on mechanical properties of serviced blades were rarely investigated.
