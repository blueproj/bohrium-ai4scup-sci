# A R T I C L E I N F O  

# A B S T R A C T  

Selective laser melting (SLM) provides an economic approach to manufacturing Ni-base superalloy components for high-pressure gas turbines as well as repairing damaged blade sections during operation. In this study, two advanced processing routes are combined: SLM, to fabricate small specimens of the nonweldable CMSX-4, and hot isostatic pressing (HIP) with a rapid cooling rate as post-processing to heal defects while the target γ/γ´ microstructure is developed. An initial parametric study is carried out to investigate the influence of the SLM process parameters on the microstructure and defects occurring during SLM. Special emphasis is placed on understanding and characterizing the as-built SLM microstructures by means of high-resolution characterization techniques. The post-processing heat treatment is then optimized with respect to segregation and the γ/γ´ mi- crostructure.  

Keywords: Selective laser melting CMSX-4 Repair Hot isostatic pressing Processing  

The second-generation single-crystal (SX) Ni-base superalloy CMSX- 4 exhibits the typical microstructural heterogeneities associated with dendritic growth during casting, such as strong segregation between dendritic and interdendritic regions, large eutectic areas, topologically close-packed (TCP) phases as well as porosity [ 1 , 2 ]. To reduce these heterogeneities, complex and long heat treatments are required to achieve a homogeneous chemical composition, to dissolve large γ/γ′ eutectics and to develop a coherent γ/γ′ microstructure with about 70 vol.% of γ′ [ 1 , 2 ]. In addition to the high cost of these thermal treatments, another drawback of them is the increase in porosity [ 5 ], which has a negative effect on the material properties [ 5 , 6 ]. The only way to remove pores and to considerably improve the material prop- erties is through the use of hot isostatic pressing (HIP) [ 5 , 6 ]. Recent works have shown that an integrated heat treatment during HIP has beneficial effects on the creep life of the SX superalloy ERBO/1 (same chemical composition as CMSX-4) [ 7 , 8 ]. This integrated heat treatment comprises a standard solution-annealing heat treatment within a HIP facility under a certain amount of isostatic pressure, followed by a rapid cooling rate. The resulting material shows no segregation or porosity with a very fine γ/γ´ microstructure that is suitable for the subsequent aging heat treatment.  

# 1. Introduction  

Nickel-base superalloys are one of the most widely used materials for high-temperature applications in aero engines and stationary gas turbines. They have excellent thermal and mechanical stability at high temperature, which allows them to operate at temperatures close to their melting point. Their extraordinary properties are associated with their complex chemical composition, which contains high amount of refractory elements such as W, Ta and Re, as well as their micro- structure, consisting of coherent secondary γ′ precipitates embedded in the solid-solution γ matrix [ 1 , 2 ]. However, due to the rotational movements of hot-section components at very high temperatures, they suffer from material losses due to creep, thermal induced stresses, thermal abrasion, corrosion, and oxidation. As consequence, these components must be repaired or, in the worst case, completely replaced during the overhaul, which is very expensive [ 3 ]. Hence, there is great commercial interest in developing repair processes based on additive manufacturing (AM) that are capable of restoring the metallurgical microstructure and geometry of the parent material at damage locations [ 3 , 4 ]. On the other hand, the potential to produce turbine components with a complex shape from Ni-base superalloys makes AM technology very attractive.

In this process, equiaxed or columnar grain structures are ob- tained by epitaxial growth from the substrate. Parameters such as scanning velocity, hatch distance, and power input play a significant role in the presence of cracks, pores, and building defects as well as on the resulting microstructure [ 9 , 10 ]. The high-temperature gradients inherent to the SLM process lead to high solidification rates and, therefore, to the formation of small cells [ 4 ]. Consequently, segrega- tion, γ/γ′ eutectics, or TCP phases may be present in SLM parts on a very small scale [ 9 , 11 ]. They can be removed by a short post-build solution heat treatment, which is typically carried out in a HIP facility under pressure to reduce not only segregation, but also the presence of pores and cracks [ 10 , 11 ].  

This work investigates the influence of the SLM manufacturing parameters – scanning velocity and hatch distance – on the micro- structure of CMSX-4. Owing to the high content of γ´-forming elements Al and Ti in this alloy, CMSX-4 is prone to cracking and is considered to be nonweldable [ 12 ]. Subsequently, a post-processing heat treatment at atmospheric pressure is optimized with respect to homogenization and γ/γ´ microstructure. The optimum conditions are then applied under hot isostatic pressure to heal defects (residual porosity, cracks, or binding faults) and to obtain a homogeneous γ/γ´ microstructure at the same time. The impact of the SLM parameters as well as the different heat treatment conditions with or without pressure, on the segregation and microstructure are evaluated using high-resolution characterization techniques (energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX), scanning electron microscopy (SEM), electron backscatter diffraction (EBSD) and atom probe tomography (APT) in combination with thermodynamic calculations and structure maps.  

# 2. Material  

Prealloyed CMSX-4 bars, delivered by Cannon Muskegeon, were atomized with argon using electrode induction-melting gas atomiza- tion. The particle fraction with a size between 25 and 45 μm was used.  

Inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) was used to analyze the chemical composition of the powder [ 10 ], which is given in Table 1 . Additionally, the powder contains about 133 ppm of oxygen and < 1 ppm of nitrogen.  

The powder shows a majority of the particles with a regular and spherical morphology with a small amount of irregular particles and satellites formed during atomization ( Fig. 1 a–b). The powder contains a very small amount of gas porosity formed during the gas atomization process, as well as a small amount of irregularly shaped pores within the powder particles, generally located between the solidified den- drites. Large irregularly shaped pores are rarely found within irregu- larly shaped particles caused by particle collisions during solidification. The irregular dendritic structure, within the powder particles, shows a slight segregation of Ta and Al to the interdendritic areas and a lower content of Re, W, Co and Cr in those regions. The maximum segregation distance within the powder particles is about 10 μm, measured by EDX. Small precipitates are found between dendrites ( Fig. 1 c), with a slightly higher Ta content than the CMSX-4 nominal composition. The nature of these precipitates was not addressed in the framework of this study due to the fact that the powder particles will melt during SLM melting and, consequently, so will these small particles.

# 3.2. SLM processing and laser scan strategy  

All SLM powder-net samples were fabricated using the Aconity One SLM system that uses a continuous wave IPG fiber laser with a max- imum output of 400 W that was directed at the powder bed using a Raylase Superscan III-30 SC 3D-scanner. The Aconity ONE uses a fixed laser spot focus of 85 μm diameter at the bed and has a maximum built plate size of 200 mm diameter and a buildable height of 400 mm. Austenitic stainless steel 316 L was used as the plate material. The laser output power (P) was fixed to 100 W and the slice thickness (Z incre- ment) to 30 μm. The samples were built without any preheating and no contour melting was applied. The temperature in the building chamber was measured to be around 50 °C. The focus distance was 146 mm. All builds were carried out under an argon atmosphere maintaining oxygen levels below 1 ppm. For a parametric study, the scanning speed (v s ) was varied between 200 mm/s and 1200 mm/s, and three values of the hatch distance (d h ) were used, 80, 100 and 120 μm ( Table 2 ). Com- bining the different parameters results in different values for the line energy (E L ), defined as E L  = P / v s , and for the energy input per area (E A ), defined as E A  = P / (v s  ∙d h ) ( Table 2 ). The same scan strategy was used to build all samples. A back and forth scan pattern was used for each layer. The whole pattern is rotated by 90° (hatch angle) for each subsequent layer, which means that after the deposition and melted rows of four layers, the orientation of the next melted row will be the same as the rows of the first layer.  

# 3.3. Post-processing heat treatments  

Following fabrication, the samples built with an energy input per area higher than 2.5 J/mm 2 , which showed a small amount of building defects or pores, were subjected to different heat treatments with dif- ferent intentions. First, representative samples were heated to the γ´- supersolvus temperature of 1300 °C for a holding time of 15 min, 30 min, and 60 min in an inert argon gas atmosphere furnace at at- mospheric pressure (P atm ) in order to find the optimum holding time that leads to full homogenization of the SLM-built samples. To optimize the length of the first step of the conventional aging heat treatment, the previously homogenized samples were subsequently subjected to heat treatments at 1140 °C for 1 h, 2 h, or 3 h under an argon atmosphere at P atm . In all cases, after the heat treatments, the samples were quenched in water.  

Once the holding times for homogenization and the first aging step were optimized, an integrated HIP heat treatment was performed in a laboratory HIP unit of type QIH-9 (Quintus Technologies AB), which allows very fast cooling rates (≈2000 K/min). The integrated HIP treatment was performed at the supersolvus temperature of 1300 °C with 100 MPa and 15 min holding time. The samples were then quen- ched to 1140 °C and maintained at this temperature for 2 h to perform the first step of the typical aging heat treatment for this alloy [ 5 , 7 ]. Finally, a short version of the second aging step of 4 h at 870 °C was applied.  

# 3.4. Microstructural characterization  

The characterization of the microstructure of the specimens in the as-built state and after the different heat treatments was carried out both transversal and longitudinal to the build direction. Standard me- tallographic techniques (mounted, ground and polished) were used to prepare the specimens for microstructural characterization in the SEM. SEM Leo 1530 V P (Zeiss) was used to study the as-built and the pro- cessed microstructures. EDX was used to verify the extent of homo- genization of the different samples and to analyze the chemical com- position of the observed TCP phases. EBSD was used to study the grain structure and its orientation. The accurate compositions of found pre- cipitates in the as-built microstructures and after post-processing, as well as of the cell and subcell structures found in the as-built micro- structures were measured by atom probe tomography. The required needle-shaped specimens were prepared using a FEI Helios Nanolab 600 dual-beam SEM/focused-ion-beam (FIB) following the protocol outlined in ref. [ 16 ]. The analyses were performed on a reflectron-fitted local electrode atom probe (LEAP 3000 XR), from Cameca Instruments Inc. in the laser-pulsing mode, where the specimen was maintained at a base temperature of 70 K and the detection rate was maintained at 5 ions per 1000 pulses on average. The processing and reconstruction of the raw data, as well as the analysis of the reconstructions were done by using the software package IVAS™3.8.2.

# 4. Results  

4.1. Phase transformation and phase stability: nonequilibrium solidification  

During the cooling phase of the SLM process, the temperature gra- dients are in the order of several 1000 K/min [ 22 , 23 ], which is con- sidered to be rapid solidification in which the cooling conditions are undoubtedly nonequilibrium. This kind of rapid solidification results in a cellular solidification structure [ 4 ]. In the course of nonequilibrium solidification, considerable segregation of alloying elements takes place and is responsible for heterogeneities and TCP phase precipitates. The segregation behavior in CMSX-4 during solidification was estimated by the CALPHAD method using the TCNi9 database [ 14 ] and the Scheil- Gulliver approach for solidification. The assumed infinitely fast diffu- sion in the liquid phase and absence of diffusion in the solid phase mimic the rapid solidification during SLM processing. The determined precipitation temperatures (T solvus ) of intermetallic phases are listed in Table 3 . According to the Scheil-Gulliver calculation, during solidifi- cation several phases form in the following order: γ, B2 (body-centered cubic), Ni 3 Ti (D0 24 ) and σ (tetragonal). The B2-phase and Ni 3 Ti – phase have as main elements Ni, Al, Ta and Ti. B2-phase contains more Al, while Ni 3 Ti phase contains more Ta. The Sigma-phase is characterized by high contents of W, Re, Cr and Co. The absence of back diffusion within the Scheil-Gulliver assumption leads to an extremely low solidus temperature (solidification range of about 237 °C) due to over- estimation of solute segregation.  

During nonequilibrium solidification, the variation in the compo- sition of the liquid phase results in chemical segregation within the solid and also leads to the precipitation of different TCP phases. The variation in the chemical composition of the liquid phase was calcu- lated using the Scheil-Gulliver approach and is plotted in  Fig. 2 . Their corresponding structure map coordinate values ( N  and  Δ V/V ) are plotted in  Fig. 3 a. In the last stages of the solidification, Scheil-Gulliver predicts the enrichment of Ti, Ta, Cr, W, and Hf in the liquid phase, with respect to the nominal composition of CMSX-4. Consequently and taking into account the cell-type solidification, it is expected that en- richment of these elements is found in the cell boundaries. The calcu- lated relatively high local concentration of the transition metals in the remaining liquid may be responsible for the precipitation of inter- metallic phases. The change in the composition of the remaining liquid during Scheil-Gulliver solidification favors the enrichment of elements with a higher volume factor and lower band filling. In the structure map, this causes the system to move towards the region of TCP phase stability ( Fig. 3 a). The composition of the phases B2, Ni 3 Ti and σ, predicted by the Scheil-Gulliver approach, are in accordance with the corresponding stability regions in the structure map ( Fig. 3 a).  

In order to estimate the maximum segregation level that can be found during nonequilibrium solidification, the chemical composition of the first γ-phase that solidifies at 1397 °C and the composition of the last remaining liquid at 1160 °C, according to Scheil-Gulliver calcula- tions, are listed in  Table 4 . The first γ-phase that solidifies is considered to correspond to the cell core, whereas the last composition of the liquid phase is considered to form the cell boundaries. Therefore, the main segregated element to the cell core is Re, whereas Ti, Ta, Cr, W and Hf segregate to the cell boundaries.  

From DTA experiments, the identified temperatures of solidus (T sol ) and liquidus (T liq ) are in good agreement with the corresponding temperatures calculated with Thermo-Calc ( Table 3 ). However, due to the small amount of intermetallic precipitates in the as-built CMSX-4 specimens, the solvus temperatures for the different precipitates could not be detected.

# 4.4. Optimization of the post-processing heat treatment  

Optimization of the post-processing heat treatment consists of op- timizing the holding time at the maximum temperature during solution- annealing and that for the first step of the aging heat treatment. Based on (i) the conventional solution-annealing heat treatment for the ERBO/1 alloy,  see dotted line in  Fig. 4 , and (ii) the calculated phase transformation temperatures using Thermo-Calc,  Table 3 , a tempera- ture of 1300 °C was selected for the homogenization heat treatment. As- cast materials, such as ERBO/1, need a long solution-annealing heat treatment of around 10 h due to the strong dendritic segregation during casting and the relatively large size of the dendrites. Specimens built in this work by the SLM process show a much lower segregation level, due to the fast solidification process, which generates a cell structure with a size of about 1–2 μm in diameter. In this work, holding times of 15 min, 30 min, and 60 min at the selected temperature of 1300 °C were applied at P atm  to homogenize the chemical composition of the CMSX-4 speci- mens that were generated by different SLM processing conditions. # 4.5. SLM microstructures after post-processing by HIP  

The samples built with energies input per area greater than 2.5 J/ mm 2   that exhibit a small amount of building defects were subjected to an integrated HIP cycle in order to remove these defects while the material was completely homogenized. The degree of homogenization was investigated by means of EDX line scans, and no segregation was observed in the HIPed samples. It is believed that voids and building defects that are sufficiently large or that are connected to cracks in the as-built samples will survive the short HIP treatment, as is shown in Fig. 12 a–b. Unmelted powder particles can also be found within pores ( Fig. 12 a). In this regard, the HIP treatment was not satisfactory. The amount of cracks present after HIP is noticeable. The origin of the cracks after HIP has not yet been determined. It is assumed that all initial as-built cracks are not completely interconnected and end in the surface of the samples and, therefore, at least some of them should be closed by HIP. In addition, it is presumed that at least some of these cracks may have formed during quenching from 1300 °C. Finally, it is believed that the absence of a contour around the SLM samples means that almost all the cracks or pores remain after the HIP treatment. With respect to the γ/γ′ microstructure, a very fine and uniform γ/γ′ phase distribution was obtained after the applied HIP treatment, regardless of the parameters used for SLM processing ( Fig. 12 c). Small amounts of two types of precipitates were found in all samples heat-treated under isostatic pressure (solution-annealing and first aging step) with the additional second aging step at P atm . The first type of precipitate, de- noted here as A-type, contains large amounts of Re and W, and these precipitates are surrounded by the γ´-phase ( Fig. 12 d). This kind of precipitate, exclusively lined up along the grain boundaries, was al- ready found and described previously,  Fig. 11 c ,  after the first aging step. The second type, denoted B-type, contains large amounts of Ta and Ti and is surrounded by the γ-phase ( Fig. 12 e). B-type precipitates were found only at the edges of the samples, which seems to indicate that their presence is associated with a reaction of the specimens with the used atmosphere during the post-processing heat treatments. All these precipitates have a maximum size length of about 2 μm.  

The exact chemical composition of the A- and B-type precipitates have been measured by APT and the results are listed in  Table 6 . The respective  N  and  Δ V/V  value for the case of the A-type precipitate is depicted as pink circle in  Fig. 10 ,  inside the stability region for the μ and Laves phases, indicates its possible nature. The measured chemical composition for the B-type precipitates indicates that beside Ta and Ti as described above, C is also highly enriched, making this a (Ta,Ti) 3 C 2 type carbide phase.  

# 5. Discussion  

# 5.1. Microstructure after SLM processing  

During SLM processing, local regions begin to melt to produce a micron-size melt pool. Then, the molten pool experiences a fast and nonequilibrium solidification process due to the complex thermal his- tory resulting from the repeated passes of the laser [ 28–30 ]. The nonequilibrium solidification behavior controls the distribution of so- lutes as well as the grain size and its morphology ( Fig. 7 ) [ 31 ]. Within the grains, a fine and regular cellular structure with diameter sizes between 1 and 2 μm is observed, regardless of the selected building conditions ( Fig. 8 ) [ 31 ]. The extremely fast melting of the powder particles as well as of part of the already solidified material together with the nonequilibrium solidification that takes place during rapid cooling is responsible for the non homogenization of the liquid composition. Consequently, low- melting precipitates surrounding the cells are found. These precipitates have been identified as Laves phase through the combined use of APT and structure map techniques ( Table 5 ,  Figs. 9, and 10 a).  

The experimental identification of these small precipitates sur- rounding the cells does not match that predicted by Scheil-Gulliver (nonequilibrium solidification). Scheil-Gulliver predicts the presence of phases B2, Ni 3 Ti and σ, as minor phases. However, of these, only the σ- phase is characterized by containing large amounts of W, Re and Co, which partially coincides with the measurement experimentally with ATP, although the found precipitates are also characterized by con- taining high amounts of Ta.  

A subcell structure with a diameter of about 10 nm is found within the cells by using APT ( Fig. 9 c). Different chemical composition be- tween the subcells center and boundaries are distinguishable. Although APT does not provide access to the crystal structure of the phases be- cause the lack of diffraction patterns, it seems that the subcell centers, which are enriched in Ta and Al, and depleted in W, Re, Mo, Cr and Co in comparison to the subcell boundaries ( Table 5 ), can host small nuclei of the γ´-phase. This possibility is also supported by the position of the subcell center within the structure map ( Fig. 10 ), in the region where the γ´-phase is typically located ( Fig. 3 b). Verification of this assumption requires transmission electron microscopy.  

The selection of adequate values of the SLM processing parameters, namely scanning velocity and hatch distance, has a direct influence on the as-built microstructure. Only the combination of both parameters that results in energies input per area greater than or equal to 4.17 J/ mm 2   suppresses the formation of large building defects and voids so that only cracks are found. No combination of the scanning velocity and hatch distance values used in this work avoids the high cracking sus- ceptibility of CMSX-4 during SLM processing. Therefore, all the as-built SLM samples in this work contain cracks, which indicates the presence of thermal residual stresses during solidification and subsequent fast cooling [ 32 ]. The low thermal conductivity and high specific heat ca- pacity of CMSX-4, 7.8 W m-1 K-1 and 500 J kg-1 K-1 respectively at room temperature according to [ 33 ], prevent the conduction of the heat effectively, leading to the generation of large thermal gradients inside the built specimen and consequently to the formation of large residual stresses [ 26 , 31 , 34 ]. Additional stresses are produced during rapid cooling due to the large thermal expansion coefficient of CMSX-4, which leads to a large shrinkage of the material [ 26 , 31 , 34 ].  

# 5.2. Microstructure evolution during heat treatments  

A post-processing heat treatment has been optimized with the main objectives of minimizing segregation, dissolving precipitates that have formed during rapid solidification, reducing the number of cracks, and promoting a uniform γ´ particle distribution, which are required for good mechanical properties.  

Holding times at the maximum temperature of the solution-an- nealing treatment and for the first step of the aging heat treatments were optimized by performing different heat treatments at P atm . The selected optimum conditions were then applied within a HIP facility under 100 MPa. This optimized heat treatment consists of a short so- lution-annealing heat treatment at 1300 °C, 100 MPa for 15 min fol- lowed by rapid quenching to 1140 °C. A holding time of 15 min is considered sufficient to homogenize the specimens and to dissolve the small TCP phases found in the as-built structures. After HIP, a large number of cracks was observed in all specimens and, therefore, the application of isostatic pressure did not have the expected success. The absence of a contour around the SLM specimens allows at least some the as-built cracks to end up on the surface of the specimens and, therefore, these cracks do not close during HIP. Formation of a suitable contour during the SLM process would help to densify the specimens as well as heal pores and cracks.  

The first step of the aging heat treatment was carried out at 1140 °C with a holding time of 2 h. This temperature was selected because it is the one used in the conventional thermal treatment of ERBO/1 (similar to CMSX-4) ( Fig. 4 ). Although at this temperature, the presence of TCP phases was not expected according to the thermodynamic calculations, a small amount of precipitates enriched in W, Re, and Cr ( Table 6 ) are found in the grain boundaries ( Fig. 11 c). These precipitates are prob- ably a type of μ or Laves phases according to their position in the structure map ( Fig. 10 ). After the first aging step, the specimens were subjected to a short version of the second stage of aging at 870 °C for 4 h, instead of the 16 h corresponding to the standard heat treatment ( Fig. 4 ). A very fine and uniform γ/γ´ phase distribution was obtained after the second aging step regardless of the parameters used for SLM processing. Two different kinds of precipitates, with a maximum size length of about 2 μm, were distinguished after performing this complete heat treatment. The first type of precipitate, contains high amounts of Re and W, and these precipitates are surrounded by the γ´-phase, Fig. 12 d. The second type contains high amounts of Ta and Ti and is surrounded by the γ-phase,  Fig. 12 e. According to the chemical com- positions measured by APT, these precipitates are μ or Laves phase for the first type and (Ta,Ti) 3 C 2  for the second type. Precipitates of μ or Laves phases with similar size and chemical composition were recently observed at grain boundaries of plasma-sprayed ERBO/1 after HIP treatment [ 35 ].  

The presence of these precipitates is presumably not related to the application of pressure during the HIP process, but to the two steps of aging heat treatment carried out at 1140 °C and 870 °C. This can be deduced from the recent observation of stress-induced TCP phase for- mation at regions of high local tensile stresses in high-temperature low- cycle fatigue tests of a CMSX-4 type superalloy [ 36 ]. The chemical compositions of these stress-induced TCP phase precipitates do not correspond to the bulk TCP phase stability of the structure map, an effect that would also be expected for compressive stresses during HIP but that is not observed in this work. The presence of residual stresses generated during SLM processing leads to a high dislocation density, which may affect the nucleation of the observed TCP phases. Therefore, this type of AM-SLM specimens requires the development of thermal treatments with new strategies to relieve residual stresses and, conse- quently, avoid precipitation of such brittle TCP phases.  

# 5.3. Comparison between SLM and cast material  

Three main parameters, namely segregation, porosity, and γ/γ′ microstructure, are compared and discussed with respect to the CMSX-4 samples produced by SLM and by conventional casting.  

The high solidification rates of the SLM process lead to materials with a greater homogeneity than the conventionally cast CMSX-4 ma- terial. Consequently, a very short solution-annealing heat treatment of 15 min holding time at the supersolvus temperature is sufficient to homogenize the material.  

Selection of appropriate values of the scanning speed and hatch distance allows the production of samples with a low porosity, as ex- plained above. The conventional as-cast ERBO/1A material (which has similar chemical composition to CMSX-4) has a porosity of about 0.21 vol.%, which is mainly located between the secondary dendrite arms and is aligned along the primary dendrites [ 5 ]. In SLM samples containing porosity, no clear trend has been found with respect to the position of the pores because most of them can be related to building defects.  

The integrated HIP heat treatments applied in this work, which include a solution-annealing step, a subsequent first aging step lasting two hours and a second aging step lasting four hours, result in a very fine and uniform γ/γ′ microstructure ( Fig. 12 c) compared to the con- ventionally full heat-treated cast ERBO/1 ( Fig. 12 f). It is believed that application of a longer second step of the aging heat treatment will not significantly modify the average size of the γ′ precipitates, but never- theless, will lead to a more cuboidal shape and regular organization. Previous studies on conventionally cast SX ERBO/1 superalloy show that the presence of a fine γ/γ′ microstructure significantly improves the creep properties [ 8 ]. Therefore, an improvement in the mechanical properties can be expected for SLM parts subjected to the integrated HIP heat treatment. Additional work is necessary to identify appro- priate HIP aging conditions, accompanied by fatigue and creep ex- periments.  

# 6. Conclusions  

This work investigates the influence of the two SLM processing parameters – scanning velocity and hatch distance – on the micro- structure of the Ni-base superalloy CMSX-4. In addition, a post-pro- cessing heat treatment is optimized at atmospheric pressure. Finally, the optimized post-processing heat treatment is performed within a HIP facility that provides rapid cooling rates. The following conclusions can be drawn from the results:  

•   The as-built SLM CMSX-4 samples contain defects like pores, cracks, segregation, and TCP phases. These defects are on a smaller scale compared to conventionally cast CMSX-4 material. The selection of adequate manufacturing parameters suppresses the formation of voids and large building defects. However, the high propensity of this material to cracking during SLM could not be avoided.

 •   The fine cell microstructures obtained by SLM allows to shorten the holding times for the complete homogenization of the material and the dissolution of the primary precipitates. A holding time of 15 min at 1300 °C is sufficient to homogenize the alloy.

 •   A short aging heat treatment consisting of 2 h at 1140 °C and 4 h at 870 °C is sufficient to develop a very fine γ/γ´ microstructure with a unimodal γ′-precipitate size distribution.

 •   According to the structure map analysis based on chemical com- position from APT, the observed TCP precipitates in the as-built microstructures are expected to be Laves phases.

 •   SLM specimens subjected to an integrated HIP heat treatment and subsequent aging show two kinds of TCP precipitates located at the grain boundaries and at the edges of the specimens respectively. By combining APT and structure maps, it is proposed that the first kind of precipitate is the μ or Laves phase and the second kind are Ta/Ti carbides ((Ta,Ti) 3 C 2 .) The formation of the first kind of precipitate seems to be promoted by defects like grain boundaries and dis- locations.

 •   The large amount of cracks observed after HIP makes it tough to evaluate the success of the HIP treatment for closing cracks. It is believed that the absence of a contour around the SLM samples re- sults in the retention of almost all the cracks and pores after the HIP treatment. Therefore the formation of a suitable contour during the SLM process would help to densify the samples as well as to heal pores and cracks.

 •   The temperature for the first step of the precipitation hardening must be adjusted to relieve thermal stresses and to avoid the for- mation of TCP phases.
