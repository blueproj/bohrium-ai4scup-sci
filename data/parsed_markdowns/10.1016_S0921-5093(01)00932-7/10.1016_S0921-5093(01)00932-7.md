# Abstract  

The structure evolution of commercial DD3™ 1   single crystal superalloy has been systematically investigated within the achieved range of undercooling 0–210K. The grain structure of the alloy can be reﬁned notably if it solidiﬁes in a range of lower undercooling 30–70 K or above the critical undercooling (  T \* = 180 K). These two kinds of grain reﬁnements arise in completely different ways. Based on current dendrite growth theory, a thermodynamic concept, dimensionless superheating, is adopted to evaluate the tendency of the dendrite remelting. With the increase of undercooling, the dimensionless superheating of the alloy increases ﬁrst and then decreases, which suggests that the dendrite remelting driven by recalescence superheating should only be responsible for the grain reﬁnement at lower undercooling range. Whereas the decrease of grain size above   T \* is attributed to the high strain energy and lattice deformation energy that originate from the extremely rapid solidiﬁcation, and lead to the dendrites distortion and disintegration. Subsequently, the recrystallization occurring in cooling could enable the grain size to decrease further. Dislocation morphology evolution in as-solidiﬁed structure is also provided by TEM technique to verify the recrystallization mechanism. © 2001 Elsevier Science B.V. All rights reserved.

# 1. Introduction  

The grain reﬁnement in undercooled metals was ﬁrst found in pure nickel by Walker [1], in which the grain size abruptly decreased by about 1–2 orders of magni- tude when the undercooling   T  prior to nucleation exceeded a critical value   T \* . Since the occurrence of the grain size reduction was accompanied by the emis- sion of sound waves, he concluded that the reﬁnement resulted from the copious homogeneous nucleation in- duced by the pressure pulse generated from the collapse of shrinkage cavities. Horvay [2] and Glicksman [3] also supported this argument. However, Jackson and Flem- ings [4,5] suggested that the ﬁne-grained structure of the Ni–Cu alloy should be attributed to the dendrite remelting during recalescence and post-recalescence.  

Recently, a physical mechanism was proposed, that suggests that the break-up of dendrites under the action of remelting causes the grain reﬁnement both at low undercoolings and at high undercoolings, and it occurs when the plateau duration   t pl  exceeds the time for dendrite break-up by Rayleigh instability   t bu  [6]. Pow- ell and his co-workers [7–9] also conﬁrmed the exis- tence of   T \*   when they investigated the structural evolution of Ag-, Cu- and Ni-based alloys with under- cooling. However, they argued that the recrystallization in post-recalescence period should be responsible for the reﬁnement. Herlach et al. [10,11] measured crystal growth velocities in many undercooled melts, and found that the velocities no longer increased rapidly with undercooling when   T   T \* . Thus   T \*   was cor- related to a critical velocity, which is about 20 m / s. However, there has been no reliable theoretical or experimental basis to determine which one is the best mechanism for grain reﬁnement to date. Therefore, the present paper is focused on the investigation of the grain reﬁnement mechanism for multi-component DD3 single crystal superalloy.  

# 2. Experimental procedure  

Commercial DD3 single crystal superalloy was studied (see Table 1) in the bulk undercooling experi- ment, which was carried out by the application of molten salt ﬂuxing and high frequency induction heating under the protection of argon atmosphere. Prior to melting, the surfaces of the metal charges were cleaned by mechanical grinding and chemical etching. Initially, the alloy charges were placed in a cleaned and dried non-catalytic nucleation coated mold [12], and covered with a 5 mm layer of salt granules. The preparation and characterization of the non-catalytic nucleation coated mold has been de- scribed elsewhere in detail [12]. Then the mold was sealed, evacuated and subsequently back-ﬁlled with 99.999% argon gas. Each sample was melted, super- heated and solidiﬁed several times, in order to obtain large undercoolings. After the high frequency power source was turned off, the alloy sample was sponta- neously cooled, while thermal behavior of samples was monitored by an infrared pyrometer with an ab- solute accuracy, relative accuracy, and response time of less than 10 K, 3 K, and 5 ms, respectively [13]. Before the experiment the pyrometer was calibrated with a standard PtRh 30 –PtRh 6  thermocouple. Chemi- cal composition analysis, performed in the alloy sam- ples achieved and on the inner surface of the coated mold, clariﬁed that no chemical reaction occurred be- tween the alloy melt and the mold materials and the salt granules. Therefore, the composition of the as-so- lidiﬁed specimens was taken to be the same as the original composition. Each of the samples had a weight of 10–15 g and a diameter of 8–12 mm, and was sectioned through the triggering spot, then pol- ished, and etched with aqueous solution of FeCl 3  and HCl. Structure observation was carried out with an optical microscope, scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM).

Therefore, a sub- stantial number of side-arms must certainly be de- tached by coarsening and remelting processes [25] and the smallest of these will be free to move and rotate in the melt during post-recalescence, thus leading to the reﬁned structure (Fig. 2c). In contrast, the dimen- sionless superheating of the dendrites undercooled be- low   T 1 , decreases to its lowest level (Fig. 6). The remelting is not severe enough to change the original morphology of dendrites, thus it can be retained to the room temperature instead (Fig. 2a,b).  

# 4.3.  The reﬁnement mechanism in high undercooling range  

When undercooling of the melt exceeds   T 4 , the solidiﬁcation process is primarily thermal-diffusion controlled. The contribution of   T t  to total under- cooling   T  increases signiﬁcantly with undercooling, while that of   T c  to   T  decreases gradually (Fig. 4). The contribution of   T k  become pronounced when undercooling is sufﬁciently large (  T   T \* ), where the crystal growth velocity become so high that the interface attachment kinetic effect has played an im- portant role in effecting the solidiﬁcation behavior (Fig. 5a). Severe solute trapping will consequently take place, and the solute content in the liquid at the dendrite tip substantially decreases [21] (Fig. 4, Fig. 5b). This indicates that the solute prerequisite for dendrite ripening is not guaranteed in this under- cooling range. Also, at the undercooling above   T 5 the dimensionless superheatings of the dendrite de- crease to a low level, so the remelting is not severe enough to make the dendrite disintegrate into ﬁne granule. The grain reﬁnement at high undercooling must be induced by other factors rather than remelt- ing.

Therefore, high internal stress (i.e. the lat- tice deformation energy) can also be caused by the lattice deformation resulting from the incorporation of solute atoms and other lattice defects.  

In connection with the microstructure shown in Fig. 2e, Fig. 3b and c, and Fig. 9, substantial grain boundary movement, associated with twin propagation, occurs in the complete solid state and determines the grain boundary pattern ﬁnally observed at room tem- perature. Furthermore, the internal grain structure, re- vealed by transmission electron microscopy (Fig. 9a), conﬁrms the lack of regular dendrite arms although a number of low angle boundaries (Fig. 8d) are found within grains. So we suggest that the grain reﬁnement at high undercooling should result from the recrystal- lization process as argued by Powell et al. [7–9], which appears to occur during, or immediately after, solidiﬁ- cation, while the solid metal is very close to the melting point. According to the present nomenclature [28], the whole annealing process, from the deformed state to the fully annealed state, is classiﬁed into three main stages, that is recovery, recrystallization and grain growth respectively. It is herein the high SCSE and lattice deformation energy (LDE) arising from rapid solidiﬁcation process that ﬁrstly leads to plastic defor- mation (e.g. dendrite distortion and fragmentation) in the dendritic network. Both LDE and SCSE can be temporarily stored in the aforementioned distorted dendrite fragments, which indicates that the distorted fragments must be in a ‘state of unstable condition’. During rapid recalescence and the relatively long post-recalescence, this instability then provides a high temperature recovery annealing and a recrystallization annealing for the so-called cold-deformed structure. In the meantime, the stored energy is released upon annealing the deformed metal. Finally, grain growth is driven by the surface free energy of the grain boundaries, which is much smaller than the strain energy for recovery and recrystallization.

After rapid recalescence, the aforementioned deformed structure will be subjected to recovery annealing. The tangled dislocation in the deformed cell (Fig. 8c) tends to move from the interior to the cell wall, where the direct and regular lineage of dislocation is being formed progressively (Fig. 8d). This indicates that the sub-boundary is constructed by two-dimensional dis- location network, which should be highly immobile according to the theory of dislocation [30]. When re- covery annealing further proceeds (i.e. in relatively longer post-recalescence), some dislocation networks annihilate, dissociate and merge into more stable net- works, and the sub-crystalline grains grow up. The merging and growing of sub-crystalline crystal will enlarge the orient difference among sub-grain boundaries, thus leading to the formation of new grains. Following recovery, recrystallization takes place by the nucleation and growth of new grains at the expense of the recovered matrix. These nuclei of newly formed grains are relatively strain-free, and when reaching the critical size, are surrounded or par- tially surrounded by high-angle boundaries with high mobility (Fig. 9a). They are then energetically capable of growing into the matrix by the migration of their high-angle boundaries. As recrystallization proceeds to completion, impingement among these new grains occurs with increasing frequency. Finally, the entire specimen is replaced by an aggregate of the recrystal- lized grains (Fig. 1e and Fig. 9b).  

As is widely known, the microstructure of a cold- worked metal is highly heterogeneous, and the recrys- tallization nuclei are formed preferentially at speciﬁc sites, which in general are regions of relatively small volume where the lattice is highly distorted, having a high LDE [29]. As for the multi-component DD3 sin- gle crystal superalloy, various solute atoms (such as Al, Ti, Co, Cr), because of solidiﬁcation segregation, are accumulated in the original grain boundary, i.e. the dendritic intersections. This phenomenon must be responsible for the high LDE stored there, which fa- vors the occurrence of recrystallization. The critical size for a viable nucleus to form in these regions is relatively small, so it can be attained more readily. Furthermore, the nucleus only needs to grow through a relatively short distance to form a high-angle boundary within the matrix. Therefore, the recrystal- lized grains have different sizes, as shown in Fig. 1e and Fig. 3b.  

On the other hand, recrystallization proceeding through the growth of these new grains by high-angle boundary migration will inevitably be subjected to pin- ning and drag force [31], especially in the multi-compo- nent superalloy. Pinning force results from the presence of a particle, whereas the drag force is due to the existence of solute or impurity, on the grain boundary. In view of Fig. 3, many     particles are precipitated during the cooling process after solidiﬁcation. In fact, the incorporation of defects into the as-solidiﬁed struc- ture is in favor of the     precipitation, and moreover, increases in the terminal solubility of additions (e.g. Al, Ti, Co) obtained in rapid solidiﬁcation provides higher volume fractions of     precipitates [32]. The existence of    particles on the grain boundary will consequently lessen grain-boundary area and hence grain-boundary energy, thus inhibiting or retarding its migration (Fig. 9b). On the other hand, the complex alloying element of DD3 single crystal superalloy will be deﬁnitely ag- gregated on the grain boundary owing to the unavoid- able solute segregation occurring in slow solidiﬁcation after recalescence. It was suggested that a force of interaction exists between the dissolved solute and grain boundary [33,34], and grain-boundary migration is con- siderably retarded. Therefore, the recrystallized grain obtained here is obviously smaller than that achieved in single-phase alloy [13,22].  

# 5. Conclusions  

(1) DD3 single crystal superalloy melt was bulk undercooled by adopting molten salt ﬂuxing and ther- mal cycles. The highest undercooling up to 210 K was achieved.  

(2) The grain reﬁnements of the undercooled single crystal superalloy are found to be dependent on the degree of undercooling. The two main mechanisms are stated as follows. First, in the range of undercooling 30–70 K, solutal diffusion plays the important role in the crystal growth and the ﬁrst reﬁnement results from the dendrite break-up or ripening owing to remelting. With the increase of undercooling, solute trapping re- sulting from the extremely rapid solidiﬁcation becomes more and more effective. Then the second reﬁnement occurring in the undercooling range above the critical undercooling   T \* , 180 K, is attributed to the solidiﬁca- tion contraction strain energy and lattice deformation energy originating from the extremely rapid solidiﬁca- tion process, which result in the dendrite distortion, disintegration and recrystallization ﬁnally.  

(3) Employing the information obtained from SEM and TEM, the recrystallization mechanism was system- atically investigated by adopting the dislocation analy- sis. The rapid solidiﬁcation and the rapid recalescence as well as post-recalescence could be interpreted as the prior cold deformation and the high-temperature recov- ery, and recrystallization annealing treatment, respectively.  

(4) The solute or impurity aggregation owing to complex alloying elements and precipitation existences on the grain boundary inhibit the boundary migration.
