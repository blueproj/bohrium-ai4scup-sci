# Inertia Friction Welding of Dissimilar Superalloys Mar-M247 and LSHR  

OLEG N. SENKOV, DAVID W. MAHAFFEY, S. LEE SEMIATIN, and CHRISTOPHER WOODWARD  

The solid state inertia friction welding (IFW) process was used for the ﬁrst time to join two dissimilar Ni-based superalloys, LSHR, a powder metallurgy alloy, and Mar-M247, a direc- tionally solidiﬁed alloy. Extensive studies of the microstructure, phase composition, re-distri- bution of the alloying elements between the welded alloys, microhardness, and welding defects were conducted at diﬀerent distances from the weld interface, and the results were correlated with the loading and friction conditions during IFW. Possible reasons leading to the formation of the welding defects were discussed and directions for the further improvement of the quality of the IFW of these two dissimilar alloys were outlined.

# I. INTRODUCTION  

D UE  to their inherent ability to withstand deforma- tion at temperatures approaching their melting temper- atures, nickel-based superalloys are among the most important structural materials for use in extreme high temperature applications. Application areas include land-based power generation, rocket propulsion, and aero-propulsion. In particular, Ni superalloys are tradi- tionally used for making turbine components in the hottest sections of turbine engines as these alloys have the best combination of required properties, such as creep resistance, temperature capability, environmental resistance, and damage tolerance, compared to other 1 , ]   commercial alloys. The fuel economy, reduction of carbon emission, and the eﬃciency of generating power of a turbine engine are known to improve considerably with an increase in the turbine inlet temperature and the 2 , ]   turbine rotating speed. Therefore, numerous eﬀorts have been made to improve the temperature capability of Ni superalloys through the development of new alloy grades, modiﬁcations of the processing parameters, and design modiﬁcations of the turbo-machinery compo- 3 – 9 ]   nents. Two main grades of superalloys are currently available. These are (i) ﬁne-grained powder metallurgy (PM) alloys, such as Rene 88, IN100, or LSHR, which possess very high strength at temperatures up to 973 K to 1033 K (700   C to 760   C), but have insuﬃcient creep resistance at higher temperatures, and (ii) single-crystal or coarse-grained cast alloys, such as Rene N6, CMSX- 10, or Mar-M247, which have outstanding creep resis- tance at temperatures up to  \~ 1273 K (1000   C), but have almost twice lower strength than the PM superal- loys at lower temperatures. At the same time, many advanced applications require diﬀerent sections of a single structural component to operate in very diﬀerent temperature and loading conditions. An attractive way to fulﬁll this requirement is thought to design and fabricate a hybrid structure consisting of several super- alloys joined together. In such design, sections operating under high loading conditions, but at lower tempera- tures, are made of a PM alloy, while sections requiring outstanding creep resistance at higher temperatures are made of a cast alloy.  

One method that can be utilized to produce complex geometries and hybrid structures from Ni superalloys is welding. Unfortunately, traditional fusion welding tech- niques generally cannot be used for Ni superalloys, which have high volume fractions of  c ¢  particles, as these result in welding defects such as liquation cracking, 10 , ]   strain age cracking,  etc . Inertia friction welding (IFW) is considered suitable to join the dissimilar nickel- base superalloy materials together as the defects related to porosity and solidiﬁcation that can occur during 12 , ]   fusion welding are eliminated. IFW oﬀers a quick, readily reproducible, and contaminant-free solid state joining process and involves only a small number of welding parameters. This solid state welding process involves steep thermal gradients, large microstructural changes, and high levels of hot working over a weld process zone, which thickness depends on the welding 14 parameters and alloy thermo-mechanical properties. It is therefore very important to understand the micro- structural changes in the IFW region, since these changes 15 – 17 ] may signiﬁcantly aﬀect mechanical properties.  

In this work IFW was used to weld two dissimilar Ni- based superalloys, LSHR, and Mar-M247. LSHR is a high-strength PM alloy developed by Allied Signal in collaboration with NASA for operation up to temper- atures between \~ 973 K and 1013 K (700   C to  

 [ 18 , ] 740 C) and Mar-M247 is a casting alloy developed by the Martin-Marietta Corporation for applications requiring high strength and creep resistance at elevated    \~   20 ] temperatures up to 1310 K (1037 C). Strength of both alloys is mainly controlled by  c ¢  particles and also enhanced by solid solution and grain-boundary strengthening. Having much ﬁner grains and  c ¢  precip- itates, LSHR has almost twice the tensile strength of Mar-M247 at temperatures below 1023 K (750   C). However, the c ¢ solvus temperature of LSHR,     21 ] T LS _ S  = 1430 K (1157 C), is much lower than that     22 ] of Mar-M247,  T M _ S  = 1498 K (1225 C). As a result, the tensile strength of LSHR decreases more rapidly at higher temperatures than that of Mar-M247, and above  \~ 1173 K to 1273 K (900   C to 1000   C) tensile strength and creep resistance of Mar-M247 be- 18 – 20 , ]   come higher than LSHR. Diﬀerent mechanical properties make IFW of these superalloys challenging. Because IFW of these alloys was carried out for the ﬁrst time, diﬀerent IFW conditions were tried in eﬀort to develop suitable welding parameters. Several IFW samples were produced and their microstructure and properties were examined. In this work, the microstruc- ture and microhardness of the welded superalloys are reported.

# III. RESULTS  

# A.  Selection of the IFW Processing Conditions  

To select IFW conditions which could provide sound joining of the LSHR and Mar-M247 materials, ﬁve trials were conducted under the welding conditions given in Table  II . There are four adjustable parameters in the IFW process. These are the moment of inertia and rotation speed of the ﬂywheel, which provide the amount of kinetic energy to be transformed into heat at friction surfaces, sample geometry, and the axial forging force, which controls the friction force and the temperature range at which the joining alloys deform plastically. Trial 1 was conducted as a starting point 24 ]   using typical Inconel 718 welding parameters. These parameters, however, provided very little upset and no joining occurred. This was probably due to higher strength levels of both LSHR and Mar-M247 alloys than IN 718 at temperatures higher than 1023 K (750   C). In Trial 2, the rotation speed was increased and the axial welding force slightly decreased in order to increase the peak temperature in the weld joint and thus increase the upset of the alloys at the joint region. The increased speed resulted in a higher kinetic energy of the ﬂywheel and higher rate of transformation of the kinetic energy to heat at the friction surfaces, while the reduced force allowed the joint to reach a higher temperature before the material began to ﬂow and ﬂash formation occurred. The results of this trial did show an increase in the upset to 1.1 mm, but it was predominantly in the LSHR material and no bonding was created between the alloys. Additionally, apparent liquation at the LSHR side of the joint was noticed, which could be due to insuﬃcient axial force to keep the process in solid state. In Trial 3, the axial force was increased to lower the peak temperature in the joint and increase the forging upset. The upset gain of 2.2 mm was achieved, predom- inantly on the LSHR side, but it was still insuﬃcient to weld the alloys through the whole thickness. Welding Trial 4 examined the feasibility of increasing the rota- tion speed to put more energy into the part and thus increase the peak temperature, with the same welding force used in Trial 3. A further increase in the upset to the level of 2.3 mm, as well as slight upset on the MAR- M247 side, was observed and the alloy samples were welded to the level allowing metallographic examination of the weld interface. The inspection, however, showed that the bond did not extend to the outer diameter of the rods in certain areas. Therefore, an increase in energy was required to achieve full width welds. In welding Trial 5, more signiﬁcant adjustments were made to the process in order to slow the rate of heating, but to increase the total energy input. This involved increasing the moment of inertia and decreasing the rotation speed of the ﬂywheel. The axial forging force was held unchanged as it maintained an appropriate temperature in the joint in the previous trial. The adjusted param- eters resulted in a longer frictional heating time and an increased amount of upset. The upset was observed in both the LSHR and MAR-M247, and the weld joint extended slightly beyond the original outer diameter of the rods. The weld conditions in Trial 5 appeared to produce better quality weld joint (relative to the other trials), and it was used to produce additional weld samples for detailed analysis. The results of the analysis are described and discussed in the following sections.

# C.  Microstructure of the IFW Samples  

# 1.  Microstructure of the parent alloys  

The cast Mar-M247 alloy has a coarse-grained micro- structure (Figure  3 ). The elongated grains of  \~ 1 to 6 mm in length and  \~ 0.5 to 1.0 mm in width are oriented about 30 deg to the axial direction of the welded sample (Figures  3 (a) and (b)). The grains con- tain near-equiaxed dendritic cells of the  c  matrix and evenly distributed carbide particles (Figure  3 (c)). The dendritic cells are  \~ 0.75 mm in diameter and the carbide particles are up to 25  l m in size. According to the EDS analysis, the carbide particles are rich with tantalum, titanium, and/or tungsten. Cuboidal-shaped primary  c ¢ particles, which the average size,  d c ¢ , is 1.25  l m and the volume fraction,  f c ¢ , is 64 pct, are present inside the  c matrix (Figure  3 (d)).  

The forged LSHR alloy has a ﬁne-grained duplex microstructure consisting of the mixture of  c  grains and primary  c ¢  particles (Figures  4 (a) and (b)). The average size of the  c  grains is  \~ 3.5  l m and that of the primary  c ¢ particles is  \~ 1.3  l m. The volume fraction of the primary c ¢  particles is  \~ 35 pct. Because these constituents have very similar lattice parameters, similar sizes and random crystallographic orientations, the EBSD method is not able to separate these phases and the inverse pole ﬁgure map of grains shown in Figure  4 (c) contains both the  c grains and primary  c ¢  particles. The EBSD-determined grain size ( i.e. , the average size of the mixture of the  c grains and primary  c ¢  particles) is 2.7  l m in the as- forged LSHR. The combined particle/grain size distri- bution has a long tail indicating log-normal-type behav- ior (Figures  4 (d)). Fine, nanometer-sized coherent secondary and tertiary  c ¢  particles are present inside the  c  grains. The secondary  c ¢ , which size is  \~ 70 to 100 nm, are clearly seen in Figure  4 (b). The total volume fraction of the  c ¢  particles in the LSHR alloy is  \~ 50 to 55 pct. The microstructure of the forged LSHR 21 ] has been described in more details elsewhere.

# IV. DISCUSSION  

IFW trials conducted in this work allowed identiﬁca- tion of the IFW parameters suitable for the solid state joining of the LSHR and Mar-M247 alloys. Three stages of the IFW process characterized by diﬀerent deceleration of the angular rotation and diﬀerent rate of plastic upset of weld samples were identiﬁed (Figure  1 ). During the ﬁrst and second stages, the angular rotating speed of the ﬂywheel rapidly decreased due to transfor- mation of the kinetic energy into heat and plastic deformation. The angular deceleration was in the range 2   of 47.8 to 61.7 rad/s during the ﬁrst stage, which occurred within ﬁrst 5.05 seconds, and it rapidly 2   increased to 900 rad/s during the second stage, which duration was only 0.15 seconds and resulted in complete stop of the rotation. A sample upset of 2.88 mm was registered at the time when the ﬂywheel stopped rotating. During the third stage, further sample upset occurred under the applied axial force, without addi- tional heat input (no angular rotation).

The rate of formation of these particles is probably maximal at the beginning of the IFW and it decreases with a decrease in the angular rotation speed. When the material at the contact surfaces softens by heating, the plasticized surface layer is extruded (by the applied axial forging force) into ﬂash together with many of these particles and the contact surfaces are self-cleaned, starting from the center-axis toward the outer surface. The transition from the ﬁrst stage to the second stage of deceleration is likely to occur when the central-middle regions of the mating surfaces become almost free of the brittle nano- particles allowing the clean surfaces to have stronger bonding, which results in a more-than one order increase in the friction coeﬃcient. Sticking to the slower deformed surface of the Mar-M247 alloy and accumulating in the rim region (near the outer surface), they become the main welding defects after the IFW process completes.

The value of this critical torque has to be determined in future work.  

It is generally thought that as soon as the alloys at the friction surfaces start to plastically deform and form a ﬂash under the applied axial force, the friction condi- tions change dramatically, torque (or shear stress) rises and torsional plastic deformation of the contacted 34 – 36 ] regions occurs together with the surface sliding.  

Our experimental results, however, show almost no correlation between the kinetics of the axial upset and the coeﬃcient of friction. Indeed, the upset started in \~ 0.73 seconds after the axial force was applied and it continued within another  \~ 5 seconds covering the ﬁrst and second deceleration stages and  \~ 0.7 seconds after the ﬂywheel stopped. At the same time, no dramatic changes in the angular deceleration and, therefore, in the friction coeﬃcient and torque, occurred during the ﬁrst stage (ﬁrst 5 seconds). The angular deceleration, friction coeﬃcient, and friction torque rapidly increased during the second stage, while the axial upset rate did not change. These observations indicate that no macroscopic bonding between the friction surfaces occurs during the ﬁrst deceleration stage in spite of a considerable axial/radial plastic ﬂow of the LSHR alloy surface layer. This does not mean of course that the local surface regions would not experience sticking and shear deformation during the ﬁrst stage. Even if the average value of the torsion torque (and shear stress) is low, the local values can be high enough to provide plastic shear, which would also be responsible for the surface milling/grinding and grain reﬁnement.

Oxidation behaviors of other disk alloys with similar compositions of Al and Cr were studied at temperatures not exceeding 1088 K (815   C) and formation of both Al 2 O 3  and Cr 2 O 3  was re- 9 , ]   ported. However, no Cr 2 O 3  was detected in our case. It is quite possible that at high temperatures, due to higher diﬀusivity of Al, only Al 2 O 3  scale forms on the surface of LSHR during the ﬁrst few seconds of oxidation. Assuming the oxidation rate of LSHR to be similar to Mar-M247 at 1423 K (1150   C), the total thickness of the oxide layer grown on both mating surfaces during static holding at 1423 K (1150   C) for 5 seconds is estimated to be 40 nm. The thickness of the oxide layer observed in the rim region of the IFW interface is  \~ 200 to 400 nm,  i.e. , about an order of magnitude thicker than estimated. The discrepancy can be due to higher interface temperature, resulting in quicker oxidation, incorrect scaling of the long-time oxidation data to the short-time oxidation, enhanced diﬀusion due to concurrent deformation, and/or fric- tion-enhanced oxidation. Indeed, it is well known that the parabolic dependence of the weight gain on time takes place only after a continuous oxide layer forms on the surface. At the ﬁrst stage of oxidation of a virgin surface, the weight gain generally occurs considerably 39 ]   faster and follows linear time dependence. Friction of the mating surfaces can be another reason for acceler- ated oxide formation, as it may result in fracturing ( e.g. , by tear and wear) of the oxide scale, thus creating fresh surfaces for oxidation.  

# V. CONCLUSIONS  

1. Two Ni-based superalloys, a PM LSHR and a directionally solidiﬁed Mar-M247, were joined in solid state using the IFW process. The Mar- M247 alloy had higher tensile strength than the LSHR alloy at the applied IFW conditions. The true plastic forging strain rate and total forging strain of the LSHR alloy at the IFW interface oc- curred under the applied axial forging force were 1.94 and 0.39/s, respectively, this resulted in the for- mation of a characteristic ﬂash. The true plastic forging strain and strain rates of the Mar-M247 alloy at the IFW interface were about 18 times lower, only 0.11 and 0.022/s, respectively.  

2. Three stages of the IFW process occurring at diﬀer- ent angular deceleration and friction conditions were identiﬁed. During the ﬁrst stage, which started 2   when the ﬂywheel with  I  = 0.379 kg m had the angular rotation speed  x 0  = 345.6 rad/s and a con- stant axial force  P  = 55.2 kN was applied, the coefficient of friction of mating surfaces was esti- mated to be very low,  l 1  = 0.067 to 0.051, and friction torque  M 1  = 18 to 23 N m, shear stress s 1  = 30.2 to 43.7 MPa, and angular deceleration 2   a 1  = 53.3 rad/s were registered. During the second stage, which started after the rotation speed de- creased to  x 1  = 79.2 rad/s, the friction coefficient and angular deceleration rapidly increased to their 2 peak values,  l p  = 0.9 and  a p  = 900 rad/s , forcing the flywheel to stop rotating. Respectively, the peak values of the friction torque  M p  = 377 N m and shear stress  s p  = 535 MPa were registered. The ra- pid increase in  l  and  s  was explained by the forma- tion of strong bonding between the two alloys during the second stage. Therefore, while the angu- lar deceleration at the first stage was likely caused by sliding friction between the mating surfaces, at the second stage it was mainly caused by the surface sticking and shear plastic deformation of the already welded joint. A total upset of 2.9 mm was observed during these two stages. The third stage occurred under the applied axial force at no rotation and was associated with further sample upset and diffusion bonding under cooling conditions.  

3. Transition from the composition of the Mar- M247 alloy to the composition of the LSHR alloy occurred in a narrow,  \~ 8 to 23  l m thick layer located mainly on the LSHR side of the weld inter- face. It is likely that both mechanical mixture due to severe plastic deformation at the mating surfaces and grain-boundary diffusion of the elements dur- ing the IFW process were responsible for the observed distribution of the alloying elements at the weld interface.  

4. The HAZ was recognized through a strong depen- dence of the microhardness and the volume fractions of the primary  c ¢  particles on the distance from the weld interface. The HAZ thickness was  \~ 4.5 to 6.0 mm and consisted of  \~ 2.0 to 3.5 mm thick layers on both the Mar-M247 and LSHR alloy sides from the weld interface. In the HAZ, microhardness of the Mar-M247 alloy continuously increased with a decrease in the distance from the weld interface from Hv = 405 typical to the property of the virgin alloy to Hv = 527 at the interface. At the same time, microhardness of the LSHR alloy was 464 Hv out- side the HAZ, showed a local minimum of 439 Hv at L  = 1.1 mm from the interface and rapidly increased to Hv = 568 at the weld interface. The microhardness dependence on the distance from the weld interface was explained by the evolution of the  c ¢  particles during and after IFW.  

5. Noticeable dissolution of the primary  c ¢  particles and grain refinement occurred in both alloys inside the HAZ. The size and volume fraction of the pri- mary  c ¢  rapidly decreased with a decrease in the dis- tance from the interface from their original values of 64 pct (Mar-M247) and 35 pct (LSHR) outside HAZ to  \~ 30 pct (Mar-M247) and 0 pct (LSHR) at the weld interface. A 60 to 100  l m thick precipi- tate-free region was detected on the LSHR side of the weld interface. The grain size in the LSHR alloy was \~ 3.5  l m outside the HAZ, decreased to \~ 1.4  l m at  L  = 0.33 mm and slightly increased to 1.9  l m at the weld interface. Large elongated grains of the cast Mar-M247 alloy transformed into finer and more equiaxed grains inside the HAZ. A 5 to 30  l m thick layer of very fine ( \~ 0.5  l m in diameter) grains was formed on the Mar-M247 side of the weld interface.  

6. Nanometer-sized oxide and carbide particles agglomerated/clustered into semi-continuous thin ﬁlms were observed at the ﬂat regions of the weld interface in the middle and rim sections of the weld sample. These nano-particles seemed to form during the ﬁrst IFW stage due to the surface oxidation and grinding/milling by friction. Their clustering was likely to occur due to insuﬃcient plastic ﬂow of the Mar-M247 alloy in the radial direction, which did not allow these particles to move into the ﬂash.  

7. A radial crack contaminated with nanometer oxide and carbide particles was observed in the rim sec- tion of the welded sample on the Mar-M247 side adjacent to the well-welded interface. The analysis of the weld conditions and microstructure suggested that the crack formed during the second stage of the angular deceleration, when the alloys were al- ready welded, but the ﬂywheel still had remaining kinetic energy to plastically shear the weld interface region and for the crack to initiate and propagate inside the lower ductility Mar-M247 alloy. It was suggested that the rotation should be stopped as soon as the friction torque reaches a critical value to prevent the torsional-deformation-induced after- welding fracture.
