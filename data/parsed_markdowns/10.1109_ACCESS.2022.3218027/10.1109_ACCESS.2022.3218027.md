ABSTRACT  High-throughput studies of biological systems are rapidly generating a wealth of ‘omics’-scale data. Many of these studies are temporal collecting proteomics and genomics data capturing dynamic observations. While temporal omics data are essential to unravel the mechanisms of various diseases, they often include missing (or incomplete) values due to technical and experimental reasons. Data prediction methods,  i.e. , imputation and forecasting, have been widely used to mitigate these issues. However, existing imputation and forecasting techniques typically address static omics data representing a single time point and perform forecasting on data with complete values. In this paper, we propose a graph-based method for temporal omics data imputation and forecasting that handle omics data containing missing values at multiple time points. The method takes advantage of topological relationships (e.g., protein-protein and gene-gene interactions) among omics data samples and incorporates a graph convolutional network to ﬁrst infer the missing values at different time points. Then, we combine these inferred values with the original omics data to perform temporal imputation and forecasting using a long short-term memory network. Evaluating the proposed method on two real-world datasets demonstrated a distinct advantage over existing data imputation and forecasting methods. On the omics dataset, the average mean square error of our method improved 11.3% for imputation and 6.4% for forecasting compared to the baseline methods.

# I. INTRODUCTION  

The development of high-throughput biology allows the production of large-scale omics datasets of varying levels ranging from genomics and epigenomics to transcriptomics and proteomics. In the past decades, the wide availability of various omics-scale data has greatly advanced our bio- logical understanding of complex diseases such as breast cancer, neuro degenerative diseases [1], and inﬂammatory bowel disease [2]. Many of these advances have beneﬁted from integrating multiple omics data making it possible to obtain comprehensive knowledge of the studied biological systems [3]. For example, an integrative multi-omics analysis study of brains affected by Alzheimer’s disease has revealed associations of histones H3K27ac and H3K9ac in dysregu- lating transcription- and chromatin–gene feedback loops [4]. In addition, an increasing number of computational studies, such as disease subtyping and biomarker prediction, have beneﬁted from the integration of multiple omics data to achieve better classiﬁcation and prediction accuracy [5].  

A common challenge of integrative and computational analyses is that omics data often contain missing values and limited observations, especially for sequential omics data observed from dynamic systems. These in turn hinder multi-omics data integration and many downstream analyses that require complete omics data representations. Missing values in the omics data may occur due to various experi- mental and technical reasons. For example, proteomics data

show missing values due to the imperfect identiﬁcation of coding sequences within a genome and the limited sensi- tivity of current peptide detection technologies [6]. A large portion of miRNAs is expressed below the detection limit in miRNA arrays, resulting in missing values in the output [7]. In addition to missing or incomplete values, shortage of data is another problem, especially for temporal omics data collected from dynamic observations. For example, capturing the dynamic interactions among growth promoting ligands, signaling proteins, histone modiﬁcations, and genes in the breast cancer micro environment requires integrating multi- ple temporal omics data (e.g., proteomics and genomics). Typically, these temporal datasets do not align perfectly,  i.e. , each omic data only have observations in few time points. Therefore, it becomes challenging to incorporate multiple temporal omics datasets and capture their biological dynam- ics. Omics data imputation and forecasting methods are there- fore required to ﬁll this gap.  

Existing works on data imputation typically focus on static omics data as shown in the top panel of Fig.1a, including the Markov afﬁnity-based graph imputation of cells (MAGIC) [8], singular value decomposition impu- tation (SVDimpute) [9],  k -nearest neighbor imputation (KNNimpute) [9], local least square imputation (LLS) [10], and other methods. However, an obvious limitation of these methods is that they are inefﬁcient at handling tem- poral omics data in which the time-ordered nature (e.g., temporal dependencies in data) of the data needs to be considered. To address this limitation, we propose here a novel network-based approach for temporal omics data imputation. As shown in Figure 1a, our method is able to model time-sequence information (e.g., temporal depen- dency relations between data representations) and allows for missing values at different time points while performing the target data imputation. We additionally model the structure-dependency relations (topology) across data sam- ples from their interaction networks (e.g., protein-protein or gene-gene interactions) to mitigate the inﬂuence of missing values at different time points and enhance the target imputa- tion. Apart from the missing data imputation, the proposed method can also be used for omics data forecasting at the future time points. For the forecasting task, Fig.1b shows that our method allows the presence of missing values at different time points, unlike other existing methods that require that all observed input data representations are complete.  

To address the missing values at different time points while performing target data imputation and forecasting, we ﬁrst build a graph (e.g., gene-gene or protein-protein interaction networks) among omics data samples for each time point, and leverage the neighborhood relationships between samples to ﬁll the missing values of omics data before the target time point using a graph neural network (GNN). Then, to impute the missing omics values at the target data point or forecast the target omics data at a future time point, we use all omics data before the target time point to build prediction models based on a Long Shot-Term Memory Network (LSTM) for temporal omics data imputation or forecasting. We evaluate the proposed method for temporal imputation and forecast- ing using three real-world datasets including a proteomics dataset and a genomics dataset, and compare against ﬁve strong baseline methods in situations where the raw omics data contain different ratios of missing values. Comparative results show that our method for temporal incomplete omics data imputation and forecasting outperforms other methods.  

In summary, our contributions in this work are summarized as follows :  

1) We formulated a omics data prediction problem in the dynamic setting, which is prevalent in the computational  

biomedical studies, including the longitudinal pro- teomics and genomics data analyzing research. 2) We proposed an efﬁcient graph-based deep learning model which incorporates GNN and LSTM for temporal omics data imputation and forecasting. 3) We evaluated the promising imputation and forecasting performance of the proposed model on two real-world datasets. It is expected the generated data by our method would enhance the biomedical research especially when available data are limited.

# II. RELATED WORK  

This section survey existing work on omics data prediction techniques and graph neural networks (GNN) based data prediction methods in general.  

# A. OMICS DATA PREDICTION METHODS  

Understanding of human diseases requires molecular data analysis at various levels such as genome, epigenome, tran- scriptome, proteome and metabolome [5]. These different data modalities are referred to omics data broadly. The past decades has witnessed an explosive rise of omics studies which focus on the speciﬁc type of omics [11] or the inte- gration of multiple types of omics [12]. Many omics studies have identiﬁed that incomplete or missing values observed on the omics data are one of the major obstacles which obstruct relevant analyses contributing to biological ﬁnd- ings [13], [14], especially for many multi-omics integrative studies [7], [15]. Troyanskaya et al. [9] evaluated a Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) based method (SVDimpute) and weighted K-nearest neighbors (KNNimpute) for missing values imputation for DNA microarrays. The SVD tries to remove the missing values through matrix decomposition and reconstruction processes, while KNN-based method selects genes with expression proﬁles similar to the gene of interest to impute missing values. Dijk et al. [8] proposed a Markov Afﬁnity-based Graph Imputation of Cells (MAGIC) which leveraged the shared information across similar cells and ﬁlls in missing transcripts by imputing gene expression for any given cell, taking advantage of the rich structure imposed by biology. Ni et al. [16] proposed an imputation method called scLRTD, which uses low-rank tensor decomposition based on nuclear norm to impute scMO-seq data and single- cell RNA-sequencing (scRNA-seq) data with different stages, tissues or conditions. Although above similarity-based or decomposition-based methods are simple and effective to capture the dynamic interactions of samples especially for longitudinal omics data in the dynamic environment.  

In contrast to existing imputation methods for static omics data, we propose to study temporal omics data imputation by explicitly modeling the dynamic interaction of samples and the time-ordered information. In addition, we further extend imputation for existing observed omics data to data forecasting which aims to predict the unseen omics data at the future time points.  

# B. GNN-BASED DATA PREDICTION METHODS  

Data are often not independent but correlate with each other in either explicit or implicit ways. For example, proteins can establish communication in the biological system to form a protein-protein interaction network [17]. Recently, many GNN based methods have been proposed by taking advantage of the complex interactions between data samples for missing value imputation [18], [19]. GNN is efﬁcient to learn the explicit and hidden relations between samples by information propagation with a spectral-based convolution ﬁlter operating over the graph of data samples. For examples, Spinelli et al. [19] adopted a GNN-based autoencoder which ﬁrst builds intermediate representations for each example by interleaving classical projection layers and locally combining information between neighbors. Then, it learns to reconstruct the full imputed dataset from this intermediate embedding.  

Inspired by the recent work in graph learning, we pro- pose to perform missing omics data imputation by exploring graph-based correlations between omics samples. The graphs structure can be constructed from the inherent interaction between omics samples such as protein-protein interaction networks and gene-gene co-expression networks. Different from existing data imputation methods which typically work on static data, we focus on the omics data imputation and forecasting in the dynamic environment.  

# III. METHODOLOGY  

# A. OVERVIEW  

As shown in Figure 1, this work aims to propose a uniﬁed framework which can be used for both omics data imputation and forecasting. The data imputation task seeks to predict the absent values at a target time point  k  based on the partially observable values. In the dynamic setting, all historical omics data before time point  k  can be fully utilized in the prediction. In comparison, the data forecasting task seeks to predict the entire data representation at a target time point  k  based on all historical data before  k . It is common that the historical data may also contain missing value. We propose to impute the missing values for historical data based on GNN. Then, we use LSTM to model the time-series historical data for target omics data imputation and forecasting. The GNN and LSTM are trained and optimized to achieve optimal historical missing value prediction and the target data imputation or forecasting.

# C. THE PROPOSED METHOD  

The developed network-based approach for temporal omics data imputation and forecasting (NeTOIF) contains two major components: (1) a graph convolutional network (GCN) based structure dependency relationships modeling, and (2) a long short-term memory network (LSTM) based tempo- ral dependency relationships modeling. The two components are trained in the uniﬁed framework (Fig.2).  

# 1) GCN-BASED STRUCTURE DEPENDENCY RELATIONSHIPS MODELING  

Our rationale for modeling the structure dependencies among omics data samples is twofold. First, network connections explicitly reveal rich functional relevance, such as physi- cal pathway associations and orthology relations between samples, which can be used as reliable auxiliary information to strengthen the target data imputation and forecasting [20]. Moreover, structure relations can help infer the missing val- ues in data representations at different time points, resulting in a more robust target imputation and forecasting. The sec- ond reason is based on the assumption that genes or proteins with neighborhood relationships on the network tend to have similar biological responses or observations. For instance, in a gene-gene co-expression network, all linked gene sample nodes should likely to have similar expression values [21]. The missing values of genes at different time points can therefore be estimated from the known expression values of their linked genes.

# B. COMPARATIVE METHODS  

We compare the developed NeTOIF with ﬁve baseline meth- ods used for either static or temporal data imputation and forecasting as follows  

MAGIC (MAGIC)  [8]: is a graph-based method orig- • inally designed for imputing missing values of the static single-cell gene expression data. The main idea of MAGIC is to share information across similar cells and ﬁll in missing gene expression values of a given cell based on the neighborhood cells. This method is only used for static data imputation at a single time point.  Bayesian Bridge Regression (Bayesian)  [32]: is a • regression model with an additional regularization parameter for the coefﬁcients, where the prior for the coefﬁcient is given by a spherical Gaussian. This method adopts the same training scheme as our method for temporal imputation and forecasting shown in Fig.3.  Support Vector Regression (SVR)  [33]: is extended • from the support vector machine model for regression problems. In SVR the inputs are mapped into a higher dimensional space in a non-linear manner based on the kernel function. This method uses the same imputation and forecasting schemes shown in Fig.3.  Long Short-Term Memory Networks (LSTM)  [34]: • is a neural network model that requires temporal data as inputs. LSTM is able to model the long-time depen- dency relations between temporal data samples and has been widely used for temporal regression and prediction tasks. This method uses the same imputation and fore- casting schemes shown in Fig.3.  Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN)  [35]: is a neu- • ral network model widely used for image and text pro- cessing. The hidden layers of a CNN typically consist of a series of convolutional layers that convolve with a multiplication or other dot product. This method uses the same imputation and forecasting schemes shown in Fig.3.  LSTM-CNN  [36]: is a neural network model widely • used for temporal prediction. It uses CNN to extract fea- tures from data and uses LSTM to capture dependencies between at various time points.  DCRNN  [37]: is a deep learning framework for trafﬁc • forecasting that incorporates both spatial and temporal dependency in data. DCRNN captures the spatial depen- dency using bidirectional random walks on the graph, and the temporal dependency using the encoder-decoder architecture with scheduled sampling.  

Among all compared methods in the experiment, NeTOIF, Bayesian, SVR, LSTM, CNN, LSTM-CNN and DCRNN are methods which are able to capture the temporal dependency relationships between omics samples, whereas MAGIC can only model the static relations between samples at a single time point.

# D. EXPERIMENTAL SETTINGS  

We adopted a single-layer GCN in NeTOIF to learn the omics sample networks in both imputation and forecasting tasks. We used  Adam  [30] to train NeTOIF with a learning rate equals to 5-e3. We perform extensive experiments to test the sensitivity of the balance parameter  α  and the window size  s  used in the forecasting task. In addition, we used parameter  λ  to represent the ratio of missing values in each of previous data representations before k  while performing the target data imputation or forecasting. We ran the experiments 20 times and reported the average results and their standard deviations. We used a pairwise Student’s  t -test to determine if the results of a given pair of methods are statistically different from each other. In this paper, we evaluated NeTOIF for temporal data imputation on both RPPA and GE datasets. Since the time intervals between different time points in RPPA are not equivalent, we only used GE data to test the temporal data forecasting performance of NeTOIF.

# V. DISCUSSION  

Omics data have been widely generated which contribute to biological understanding of various human diseases. It is very common in biomedical experiments that the omics data, such as gene expression and biomarker protein expression array, are very sparse or subject to missing values. The NeTOIF model is proposed to handle the temporal omics data imputa- tion and forecasting which is beneﬁted from the topological structure and interactions between omics data samples. The advantages of NeTOIF over existing methods can be summa- rized in three aspects. First, NeTOIF is generic for both tem- poral data imputation and forecasting tasks which are tolerant to missing values at any time states. Second, The topological dependencies between omics samples can be incorporated to enhance the imputation, which complies with the nature that biological data are widely networked to inﬂuence each other, i.e., gene co-expression. Third, the model is ﬂexible to handle any missing omics data imputation even though there is not an explicit graph (e.g., protein-protein interaction network) to capture the interactions between samples. One can assume the underlying graph is fully connected, where the interactions between samples can be efﬁciently learned by the graph neural networks.  

From the comparative experiments, we can conclude that NeTOIF performs generally better imputation performance than other baselines especially when the ratio ( ρ ) of values to be imputed is relatively small. In addition, NeTOIF signif- icantly outperforms all baseline methods in the forecasting task. It is worthy to mention that in some cases, especially when higher fraction of missing values need to be imputed, that NeTOIF has no obvious advantage. We argue this is because that our model is more complex (e.g., involving more trainable parameters) than other baseline methods, which desire more training data to avoid the over-ﬁtting problem. This also explains why a smaller value of  ρ  is beneﬁcial to NeTOIF because it means that more observed data are available for fully training the weight parameters in NeTOIF.  

# VI. CONCLUSION  

Missing values are common in temporal omics data such as proteomics and genomics data, which disrupt many down- stream analyses and applications. This further complicates analyses of temporal data which not only contain missing values at multiple time points but also present a shortage of data observations (e.g., omics data at more time points are desired). In this paper, we proposed a novel network-based method called NeTOIF that incorporates both structural and temporal dependency relationships between omics samples to perform data imputation and forecasting. For our study, we used proteomics (RPPA) and genomics (GE) temporal datasets to validate the utility of the NeTOIF against ﬁve baseline methods. Our NeTOIF generally outperformed other baseline methods for temporal omics data imputation and forecasting, where we believe the performance gain was achieved by incorporating topological structures of genes and proteins interactions.  

In this study, we mainly focused on temporal omics data where a network of interactions was available for capturing the topological relationships between samples. In the future, we plan to extend our method by automatically learning the topology structures between the omics samples and develop efﬁcient models that can handle omics data without topology dependency.
