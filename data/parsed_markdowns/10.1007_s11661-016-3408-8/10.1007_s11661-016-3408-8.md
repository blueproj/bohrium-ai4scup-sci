# Microstructure, Lattice Misﬁt, and High-Temperature Strength of  c ¢ -Strengthened Co-Al-W-Ge Model Superalloys  

CHRISTOPHER H. ZENK, ALEXANDER BAUER, PHILIP GOIK, STEFFEN NEUMEIER, HOWARD J. STONE, and MATHIAS GO ¨ KEN  

The quaternary alloy system Co-Al-W-Ge was investigated and it was found that a continuous  c = c 0 two-phase ﬁeld extends between the systems Co-Al-W and Co-Ge-W. All alloys examined comprised cuboidal L1 2  precipitates coherently embedded in an A1 matrix. Diﬀerential scanning calorimetry measurements revealed that the liquidus, solidus, and  c 0 -solvus temperatures decrease when the Ge content is increased. The lower liquidus temperature and the capability of c 0 -strengthening in the Ge-rich alloys make them interesting as potential candidates for brazing   applications of Co-base superalloys. The  c = c 0 lattice misﬁt was determined by high-resolution X-ray diﬀraction and found to be positive for all alloys investigated, decreasing with increasing Ge content. The mechanical properties of the Al-rich alloys surpass those rich in Ge.

# I. INTRODUCTION  

N ICKEL - BASE  superalloys derive their excellent mechanical properties at high temperatures from strength- ening by a cuboidal and coherently embedded L1 2 \* phase  

\*L1 2  and A1 denote crystal structures in Strukturbericht notation  

within an A1 (see footnote \*) matrix phase. In 2006, [ 1 ]   researchers working with K. Ishida found a new ternary L1 2 phase in the Co-Al-W system. This discovery has generated renewed interest in the devel- opment of c 0 -strengthened cobalt-base superalloys 2 – 7 ]   worldwide. A year later, Chinen and co-workers reported the discovery of another L1 2 phase, Co 3 (Ge,W). 8 ]   They found that the  c 0 -solvus tempera- ture of this phase in a Co-11.0Ge-5.7W alloy is 1180 K (907   C), which is lower than that of Co 3 (Al,W) at 1263 K (990   C). This ﬁnding implies that the addition of Ge to alloys based on the Co-Al-W system may lower the  c 0 -solvus temperature, thereby potentially enlarging the processing window and increasing the ease with which wrought alloys could be forged.  

Germanium can be used as a melting point depressant 9 ]   in brazes for Ni-base superalloy single crystals. Such brazes allow epitaxial single-crystal joints to be pro- duced by an isothermal heat treatment. Importantly, Ge forms Ni 3 Ge precipitates that are isomorphous to the Ni 3 Al-based precipitates of Ni-base superalloys. This enables these brazes to avoid the deleterious, brittle 10 ]   phases produced with boron-based brazes. However, the mechanical properties, c = c 0 lattice misﬁt, and microstructural evolution during creep have been observed to change in the brazing-aﬀected zone com- 11 ] pared to the base material.  

If new Co-base A1-L1 2  superalloys are successfully realized, Co-Ge brazes oﬀer a method by which repair and joining operations may be performed. Such technologies will potentially improve the eco- nomic case for Co-based A1-L1 2  superalloys and allow them to be considered for a wider range of applications.  

To understand the potential for Co-Ge brazes, it is necessary to know the properties of alloys intermediate between Co-Al-W and Co-Ge-W, and this is the aim of the present study. Speciﬁcally, it is important to acquire data on phase equilibria and transformation tempera- tures in Co-Al-Ge-W alloys. Brazing would only be feasible if the liquidus temperature of a braze alloy is suﬃciently low,  i.e. , lower than the solidus temperature of the alloy that is to be processed. Additionally, it is desirable that  c 0 -strengthening is also possible in the   braze alloy and that this  c = c 0 two-phase region has a compositional stability that extends to the composition of the base material. If this is not the case, other deleterious intermetallic phases might form instead of   the  c 0 and hence weaken the joint. But even if the base and joining alloys apparently exhibit a similar   microstructure, the  c = c 0 lattice misﬁt might diﬀer. This would introduce a stress gradient through the joint, which should be avoided. The mechanical properties of the joint, base material, and interdiﬀusion zone might also diﬀer, which is generally undesirable.  

In order to quantify all these properties, ﬁve alloys intermediate between Co-Co 3 (Al,W) and Co-Co 3 (Ge,W) have been studied. In this quaternary alloying space, their compositions are equidistant and vary linearly between Co-9Al-9W and Co-11Ge-5.7W. The latter composition was chosen as data on this alloy 8 ]   are already available in literature. A derivative of the Co-Ge-W alloy with higher tungsten content was also examined in order to ascertain the inﬂuence of this element. The alloys were subjected to a homogenization heat treatment to reduce elemental micro segregation and an aging heat treatment to obtain the typical  c = c 0 microstructure. Transition temperatures were deter- mined using diﬀerential scanning calorimetry. High-res- olution X-ray diﬀraction measurements were performed   to determine the lattice parameters of the  c  and  c 0 phases and hence the  c = c 0 lattice misﬁt. The mechanical properties were evaluated through uniaxial compression testing and compressive creep tests.  

# II. EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURES  

The compositions of all the alloys investigated in this study are listed in Table  I  along with their respective designations, which are used throughout this paper. The alloys without boron were arc-melted using raw ele- ments of at least 99.9 pct purity. The melting was performed under an Argon back-ﬁlled atmosphere and the samples were ﬂipped and re-melted at least 3 times to improve their homogeneity.  

For mechanical testing, new alloys with the same concentration of elements were produced, however this time containing 0.12 at. pct B. The B was added at the expense of Co in order to increase the grain boundary 12 , ]   strength. These alloys are henceforth referred to by a subscripted B. Similarly, alloys containing elevated W additions are referred to by a subscripted +W.  

To minimize solidiﬁcation-induced micro segregation and allow the controlled precipitation of  c 0 , the samples were subjected to a solution and homogenization heat treatment. This was performed for 12 hours at 1573 K (1300   C) for alloys 0Ge through 50Ge and for 24 hours at 1473 K (1200   C) for alloys 75Ge, 100Ge, and 100Ge þ W . The solution heat treatment for all alloys was followed by a precipitate aging heat treatment for 200 hours at 1073 K (800   C) with the exception of alloy 0Ge. As this was the ﬁrst alloy to be investigated, a precipitate aging heat treatment from previous studies was used, which was 200 hours at 1173 K (900   C). In the following sections, these heat treatments are referred to as the standard heat treatment. The respective B-containing alloy variants were heat treated in the same way.  

The microstructures of all the alloys were examined after each heat treatment step by SEM using a  Zeiss Crossbeam 1540 EsB . Prior to microstructural examina- tion, the samples underwent standard sample prepara- tion consisting of grinding by SiC paper up to 4000 grit, followed by polishing using a diamond suspension. To determine the actual chemical composition and conﬁrm homogeneity, samples of all alloys were characterized by energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) using the same device. Pure element standards (Co, Al, Ge, W) were used for the EDS system calibration.  

To determine the critical phase transition tempera- tures,  i.e. ,  c 0 -solvus, solidus, and liquidus temperatures, diﬀerential scanning calorimetry was performed on samples of the alloys in the standard heat-treated state using a  Netzsch STA 409 . For these measurements, heating and cooling rates of 5 K/min were used. The characteristic phase transition temperatures,  i.e. ,  c 0 -sol- vus, solidus, and liquidus temperatures, were determined by peak analysis of the DSC curves. The transition temperatures determined from the peak onsets in both heating and cooling segments were averaged to approx- imate the actual transformation temperature, which would correspond to an inﬁnitely slow cooling/heating rate.  

The arc-melted and heat-treated samples were observed to have grain sizes between several hundred l m and a few mm. This large grain size does not allow powder diﬀraction measurements of lattice constants. As a consequence, to measure the lattice constants of the c  and  c 0 phases, high-resolution X-ray diﬀraction (HRXRD) experiments were performed using a

Compressive creep tests with stress jumps were performed on cylindrical specimens with a diameter of 5 mm and a height of 7.5 mm at 1023 K and 1123 K (750   C and 850   C). The load in each test was varied to obtain the minimum creep rate at diﬀerent stress levels (150 to 550 MPa) from one test. After reaching the secondary creep stage at a certain stress level, the samples were deformed at least 1 pct before changing the applied load.  

# III. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION  

# A.  Microstructure  

The  c = c 0 microstructures after the standard precipitate aging (200 hours at 1173 K (900   C) for alloy 0Ge and 200 hours at 1073 K (800   C) for alloys 25Ge to 100Ge) are shown in Figure  1 .  

It is apparent that the  c 0 precipitate size in alloy 0Ge (ca. 150 nm) is much larger than those in the other alloys. This may be attributed to the higher heat treatment temperature experienced by this alloy. In the remaining alloys, the precipitate size slightly increased from ca. 75 nm for alloy 25Ge to 100 nm for alloy 100Ge. This is probably caused by the decreasing W content, as W is known to be a slowly diﬀusing element [ 15 ]   in fcc Co and would therefore decelerate precipitate   coarsening kinetics. At the same time, the  c 0 volume fraction decreased from alloy 25Ge to alloy 100Ge. This is to be expected for all alloys of this type, as the   c 0 volume fraction generally decreases when the temper- ature approaches the  c 0 -solvus temperature. # B.  Thermophysical Properties  

The characteristic phase transition temperatures obtained from the DSC measurements are listed in Table  II .  

Plotting these data versus the alloy composition produces a vertical section of the metastable phase diagram between Co-9Al-9W and Co-11Ge-5.7W, see Figure  3 .  

It can be clearly seen that all characteristic temper- atures monotonically decrease with increasing Ge con- tent. The  c 0 -solvus temperature decreases from 1286 K to 1166 K (1013   C to 893   C), which would enable wrought alloys to be forged at lower temperatures. The freezing range broadens while both the solidus and liquidus temperatures decrease towards the right-hand side of the phase diagram. The lower solidus tempera- ture of these alloys results in a narrower forging window. Additionally, it potentially impedes homoge- nization as the lower possible heat treatment tempera- ture may be expected to necessitate longer annealing durations.  

The use of alloy 100Ge as a braze material, for example for alloy 0Ge, would be possible. At a temperature between 1680 K and 1713 K (1407   C and 1440   C), the former alloy is completely liquid, while the latter is still entirely solid. However, the relatively broad solidiﬁcation range restricts brazing applications. The low ﬂuidity of the alloy in the ‘‘mushy’’ state impedes high penetration depths in narrow gaps and might cause 23 ] liquation; however, larger gaps can be bridged better. To decrease melting temperature of the braze alloy further and hence increase the temperature range where brazing would be possible, the Ge content could be increased further, or W could be completely omitted. A binary Co-20 at. pct Ge braze with a Ge content similar to the Ni-Ge brazes used in Reference  9  would, according to Reference  24 , have a liquidus temperature of around 1500 K (1227   C) [Ni-19.45 at. pct Ge: 1503 K (1230   C)]. Additionally, other alloying ele- ments, which are known to decrease the melting point, could be considered for brazing alloys. Titanium is particularly interesting, as it is known to decrease the solidus and liquidus temperatures, while simultaneously 3 , , , ] improving the mechanical properties.

# D.  Mechanical Properties  

The results of the compression tests are summarized in Figure  7 .  

The room-temperature yield strength of alloys 100Ge B , 75Ge, B  and 50Ge B  surpasses those of alloys   25Ge B  and 0Ge B , despite their lower  c 0 volume fraction.   That alloys containing a low  c 0 volume fraction may possess a higher ﬂow stress at low temperatures than those with higher fractions was reported in 1969 by 30 ]   Beardmore  et al . Their explanation for this was that upon cooling from the heat treatment, ﬁnely dispersed   secondary  c 0 precipitates in the former  c  matrix. These precipitates are more eﬀective in low-temperature strengthening than the primary  c 0 . As the alloy compo- sitions they investigated are situated on a tie line   between  c  and  c 0 in the Ni-Al-Cr system, they concluded   that a lower  c 0 volume fraction at the heat treatment temperature causes a higher volume fraction of matrix   containing such ﬁnely dispersed  c 0 precipitates after cooling. While no secondary precipitates could be observed in alloys 50Ge B  to 100Ge B , they might well be present (but too small to be resolved in an SEM) and contribute to a high room-temperature yield strength. A more detailed study on the precipitation kinetics of these alloys would be necessary to address this issue. Other factors which would inﬂuence the room-temperature yield strength are the grain size, solid solution strength-   ening, and shear resistance of the  c 0 precipitates. The aforementioned grain size itself of several hundred micrometers is not expected to have a signiﬁcant impact on the yield strength, although the morphology of the borides and the width of the particle-depleted zones can 31 ]   have detrimental eﬀects. However, no signiﬁcant diﬀerences in the present alloys 0Ge B  to 100Ge B  were observed. As W is presumably the most eﬀective solid solution strengthening element in this system and alloys 0Ge B  and 25Ge B  contain more of it than the other alloys, this would also imply a behavior opposite to the one observed,  i.e ., they would be expected to show a higher strength. If cutting of the precipitates is respon- sible for the low room-temperature yield strength of, for example, alloy 0Ge B , this would imply a lower shear resistance of its precipitates. The shear resistance of the precipitates correlates with the precipitate size and the planar fault energies. Given the large precipitates in this alloy, this would mean that the energy of the planar faults (presumably anti-phase boundaries), which are generated by cutting dislocations, are lower in the Ge-poor section of the alloying space. Also the elastic   properties of the  c  and  c 0 phases contribute. According to Reference  32 , the  Co 3 ð Al ;  Ge Þ  phase should be more eﬀective in strengthening than the  Co 3 ð Al ;  W Þ  phase, because its Young’s modulus and shear modulus have a larger diﬀerence to the corresponding values of the Co-matrix phase. To clarify the actual origins of the lower room-temperature yield strength of alloys 0Ge B and 25Ge B  requires detailed TEM studies, which are beyond the scope of this study.  

Following a decrease in yield strength up to 873 K (600  C), all alloys show an anomalous increase of the yield strength with temperature, as it is typical for many alloys [ 33 ]   strengthened by L1 2  phases including the  Co 3 ð Al ;  W Þ 7 , – 36 ]   phase. The temperature at which the local maximum   of this anomaly occurs scales roughly with the  c 0 solvus temperature,withalloy0Ge B  having it speak strength at the highest temperature,while the peak for alloy 100 Ge B  occurs at the lowest temperature,  i.e. , the Ge-rich alloys oﬀer superior strength in the low-temperature regime.At1123 K (850   C), the alloy behavior can probably be attributed to   the remaining  c 0 volume fraction at this temperature. It is decreasing in the same sequence as the corresponding yield strength (0Ge B , 25Ge B , 75Ge B , 100Ge B ).  

The performance at intermediate temperatures cannot be explained as easily. At 873 K and 948 K (600   C and 675   C), the notional secondary precipitates might not have dissolved completely yet and still contribute to strengthening in alloys 50Ge B  and 75Ge B . Additionally, it is possible that the thermally activated processes that cause the yield strength anomaly start at lower temper- atures for the Ge-rich alloys. These processes are sessile   dislocation formations that form in the  c 0 precipitates 37 ]   and were ﬁrst discovered by Kear and Wilsdorf. In the same manner, the processes responsible for the subsequent drop of strength, often attributed to a change of the dominating slip system to the 38 ]    f 100 g  cube planes, seemingly start at lower temperature. The latter eﬀect is, of course, superimposed with the decrease   of the  c 0 volume fraction, which also happens at lower temperatures for the Ge-rich alloys because of their lower  c 0 -solvus temperature. A complete change of the dominant slip system to the cube planes does not occur   in single-phase  Co 3 ð Al ;  W Þ , because the  c 0 phase dis- 36 ]   solves before that can happen. The same mechanism is likely responsible for the decrease in yield strength for temperatures above the maximum anomalous increase in ﬂow stress for the alloys investigated here.

All alloys follow a Norton-type creep law with a similar stress exponent of about 15. It is apparent that the creep strength deteriorates markedly when moving from alloy 0Ge B  to alloy 100Ge B . The minimum creep rate of alloy 100Ge B  is more than 4 orders of magnitude faster than that of alloy 0Ge B  (factor 35000 at 450 MPa). One reason for this is probably the diﬀerence in   c 0 volume fraction. A second reason is the homologous temperature,  i.e. , the test temperature related to the melting temperature, which is higher for the Ge-rich alloys, although this diﬀerence is rather small (58 to 60.9 pct). Furthermore, the content of W may contribute, as diﬀusion plays an important role in creep deformation. As already stated, W is known to be a slowly diﬀusing element in Co. Hence, its increasing amount in the alloys towards the Ge-poor side of the alloy system might impede diﬀusional processes and thus enhance the creep strength, as it was already shown in Reference  19 . To ﬁnd out more about the role of W on the creep properties, alloy 100Ge þ W ; B , whose nominal W content equals that of alloy 0Ge B , was included. The data obtained from this alloy indicated that W actually improves the creep rate of alloy 100Ge B , probably both   by increasing the  c 0 volume fraction and decreasing the diﬀusivity. However, the creep strength is still worse   than that of alloy 75Ge B . Despite a similar  c 0 volume fraction, the creep rates of alloys 100Ge þ W ; B  and 0Ge B diﬀer by about 3 orders of magnitude. The reasons for this could be a change of deformation mechanisms. Either, as speculated above, the dominating slip system in alloy 100Ge þ W ; B  already changed towards the  f 001 g planes, whereas the dislocation locks are still active in alloy 0Ge B , or the planar fault energies in the Ge-rich alloy are substantially lower, making it easier to get sheared by dislocations. The latter case would translate into a transition from strongly coupled dislocations for the Ge-poor to weakly coupled dislocations for the Ge-rich alloys. Clarifying this issue is beyond the scope of this study. Qualitatively, the same creep behavior was also observed at 1123 K (850   C) and even more pronounced, Figure  8 (b).  

The stress exponents of alloys 0Ge B  to 75Ge B  have values of ca. 12, whereas those of alloy 100Ge B  and especially alloy 100Ge þ W ; B  are somewhat lower. This indicates a diﬀerence in deformation mechanisms. It is worth emphasizing that the addition of 3.3 at. pct W (from alloy 100Ge B  to 100Ge þ W ; B ) decelerates the creep rate by a factor of more than 300. In comparison, [ 19 ]   Pyczak and co-workers only observed an improve- ment of a factor of 5 with the addition of 3 at. pct W in the Co-Al-W system at 1123 K (850   C) (Co-9Al-8W  vs Co-9Al-11W). The reasons for this remains yet to be clariﬁed. The arguments used for explaining the creep behavior at 1023 K (750   C) are also believed to be valid for explaining the diﬀerences in behavior observed during testing at 1123 K (850   C).  

# IV. CONCLUSIONS  

The microstructure, thermophysical properties,  c = c 0 lattice misﬁt, and high-temperature mechanical proper- ties of quaternary Co-Al-Ge-W alloys were character- ized and the following conclusions were drawn:  

 There is a continuous  c = c 0 two-phase region of the form Co-Co 3 (Al,Ge,W).

  Borides and a precipitate-depleted zone were found at the grain boundaries of the B-containing alloy variants.

  Liquidus, solidus, and  c 0 -solvus temperatures de- creased with an increasing amount of Ge.

  The liquidus temperature of alloy 100Ge was lower than the solidus temperature of alloy 0Ge, suggest- ing that it could be used as a braze alloy.  

  The  c = c 0 lattice misfit decreased towards the Ge-rich side of the alloying system.

  The creep strength at high temperature strongly deteriorated with an increasing amount of Ge.
