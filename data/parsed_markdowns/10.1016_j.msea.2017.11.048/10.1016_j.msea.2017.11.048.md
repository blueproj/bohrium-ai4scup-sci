# E ﬀ ect of minor additions on the microstructures and stress rupture properties of a directionally solidi ﬁ ed Ni-based superalloy  

H.W. Zhang a , , X.Z. Qin b , X.W. Li a , , L.Z. Zhou b ,  

a  Department of Materials Physics and Chemistry, School of Materials Science and Engineering, and Key Laboratory for Anisotropy and Texture of Materials, Ministry of Education, Northeastern University, Shenyang 110819, PR China b  Institute of Metal Research, Chinese Academy of Sciences, Shenyang 110016, PR China  

# A B S T R A C T  

# A R T I C L E I N F O  

The e ﬀ ects of C, Zr and Y/Ce on the microstructures and stress rupture properties of a directionally solidi ﬁ ed Ni- based superalloys were investigated. The morphology of MC carbides does not change in as-cast alloys, as the C content is increased or the Zr is added. However, the Y/Ce additions promote the transformation of MC carbide morphology from blocky to script-like type. Compared with that in as-cast alloys, there is no obvious change in the morphologies of MC carbide or chemical compositions after heat treatment. The increasing of C content and the addition of Zr or Y/Ce signi ﬁ cantly decreases and increases the amount of the  γ / γ ′  eutectic, respectively. After a solid solution treatment and subsequently a two-stage aging treatment, the  γ ′  phase is characteristic with two kinds of shapes and sizes, and the volume fraction of cuboidal  γ ′  phase is much larger than that of spherical γ ′  phases. More MC carbide and residual eutectic result from the decreasing of cuboidal  γ ′  phase volume fraction. The decrease in volume fraction of cuboidal  γ ′  phase is principally responsible for the reduction of the stress rupture property. In addition, script-like carbide and residual eutectic remaining after heat treatment play the secondary role in the degradation of the stress rupture properties. The less regularly arranged cuboidal  γ ′  phase can damage the stress rupture life to some extent. The spherical  γ ′  phase re-dissolves into  γ ′  matrix widening the spacing between large cuboidal  γ ′  phase, and the addition of Zr inhibits grain boundary sliding and cracking; these two facts are bene ﬁ cial to the rupture elongation.  

Keywords: Ni-based superalloy Minor addition Microstructure MC carbide Eutectic Stress rupture  

Minor additions are cited for a variety of possible strengthening mechanisms including decreased grain boundary di ﬀ usivity  [14] , in- creased grain boundary interfacial strength  [15] , modi ﬁ ed morphology of  ﬁ ne  γ ′  or carbides  [16,17] , and removed tramp elements by pre- cipitating them as stable compounds  [18,19] . Although minor additions are of great signi ﬁ cance, the underlying mechanism about their e ﬀ ects on mechanical properties still remains unclear. In the present work, the e ﬀ ects of C, Zr and Y/Ce additions on the microstructures and stress rupture properties of a directionally solidi ﬁ ed Ni-based superalloys are explored, and the probable mechanisms are discussed.  

# 1. Introduction  

The importance of minor additions to the mechanical properties of Ni-based superalloys has been well recognized  [1,2] . It is reported that the addition of minor elements such as C and Zr could improve stress rupture properties of Ni-based superalloys  [3 – 8] . The C content a ﬀ ects the morphology, size and distribution of carbides, which have an im- portant e ﬀ ect on the mechanical properties of IN792 alloy  [9] . The  ﬁ ne particle-like carbides inhibit grain boundary sliding and dislocation moving, which was thought to be the main reason for the better creep resistance with the alloy  [10] ; However, coarse or continuous script- like carbides are detrimental because they provide the easy crack in- itiation sites and propagation paths  [11,12] . The increase of Zr en- hances the volume fraction of  γ / γ ′  eutectic, and it causes the risk of incipient melting during solution treatment  [4] . The additions of Y/Ce are reported to be capable of improving the oxidation and hot corrosion resistances  [13] . However, the e ﬀ ects of their additions on the micro- structure and mechanical properties of Ni-based superalloys are not yet clearly studied.

Their contents were analyzed as follows: 0.05 wt%C (Alloy 1), 0.11 wt%C (Alloy 2), 0.11 wt%C+0.03 wt%Zr (Alloy 3), 0.10 wt%C+0.01 wt%Y+0.02 wt%Ce (Alloy 4). As C is

The size and volume fraction of various phases were measured using an image analysis software. Each measured value is a statistical average of 5 (for  γ ′  precipitate) or 30 (for MC carbide and  γ / γ ′  eutectic) read- ings.

The MC carbides of the heat-treated alloys are blocky, rod-like or script-like, and tend to distribute in the interdendritic regions, which has no obvious change compared with that in as-cast alloys ( Fig. 1 ). The corresponding SEM-EDS indicates that the MC carbides are mainly rich in Ti (64 at%), Ta (7 at%), W (11 at%) and Mo (7 at%), etc, which are almost the same as those in as-cast alloy ( Fig. 2 ). In addition, the MC carbides still keep intact after heat treatment and no degenerations take place. The volume fraction and size of MC carbides are larger in Alloy 2 than in Alloy 1, which is exactly the re ﬂ ection of increased C content. Most of MC carbides have a high concentration of Ti, Ta and W. Besides, some Ti atoms in MC carbides are substituted by Zr in Alloy 3 or Y in Alloy 4. Zr or Y with large atomic radii is strong MC carbide former  [24] . This leads to the increased volume fraction and size of MC carbides in Alloy 3 or Alloy 4.  

# 3.1.2.  γ / γ ′  eutectics  

Fig. 4  shows the optical micrographs of the  γ / γ ′  eutectics in the as- cast alloys. It can be seen that the rose-shaped  γ / γ ′  eutectics, which are solidi ﬁ ed at the end of the solidi ﬁ cation process, are located in the interdendritic regions, and their size is larger in Alloy 1, Alloy 3 and Alloy 4 rather than in Alloy 2; the volume fraction and size of  γ / γ ′ eutectics decrease with increasing C content, and rise with the addition of Zr or Y/Ce ( Fig. 5 ). Actually, as the solidi ﬁ cation proceeds, the MC carbides precipitate, followed by the formation of  γ / γ ′  eutectics at the end of the solidi ﬁ cation  [25 – 28] . The MC carbides occupy the forma- tion places of the  γ / γ ′  eutectics and the formation of the MC carbides consumes the  γ / γ ′  eutectic forming elements like Ti, Ta, etc. Therefore, the amount of  γ / γ ′  eutectics is reduced with the increase of C content. Generally, the increase of  γ / γ ′  eutectics in Alloy 3 or Alloy 4 can be caused by the extension of the solidi ﬁ cation interval and segregation behavior of Al, Ti and Ta elements. However, as shown in  Table 2 , the solidus and liquidus temperatures of the alloys do not alter signi ﬁ cantly with the addition of Zr or Y/Ce; namely, the solidi ﬁ cation interval is not a ﬀ ected by the addition of Zr or Y/Ce. # 3.2. Stress rupture properties  

Fig. 11  shows the stress rupture properties of the investigated alloys under the condition of 930 °C/275 MPa. It can be seen that with the increase of C as well as the addition of Zr or Y/Ce, the rupture life decreases slightly. Meanwhile, the addition of Zr in Alloy 3 improve the rupture elongation.  

Generally, the stress rupture property of Ni-based superalloys mainly depends on the size and volume fraction of  γ ′  phase  [30] . Considering that the cuboidal  γ ′  phase is far greater in volume fraction

Fig. 12  shows the dislocation con ﬁ gurations of the stress ruptured specimens. Obviously, the cuboidal  γ ′  phase rafts after stress rupture, and a number of dislocations are blocked on the  γ  and  γ ′  interfaces and form dislocation networks, which can prevent the dislocations from cutting into the  γ ′  rafting. However, only a few dislocations exist in the γ ′  rafting, indicating that the shearing mechanism has just occasionally occurred. The more regular the cubic shape of  γ ′  precipitate, the more homogeneous the size of  γ ′  precipitate, and the longer the creep rupture life after heat treatment is  [31] . It is well known that the uniform distribution of cuboidal  γ ′  phase promotes the continuous rafting of  γ ′ under stress rupture conditions. This structure can e ﬃ ciently improve the creep resistance by providing e ﬀ ective barriers to the climb of the matrix dislocations around the  γ ′  particles  [32] . Therefore, the reduc- tion in stress rupture life ( Fig. 11 a) should be mainly attributed to the decreased volume fraction of cuboidal  γ ′  phase in Alloys 1 – 4 ( Fig. 9 a). Meanwhile, it is worth noting that with the decreasing of the volume fraction of cuboidal  γ ′  phase ( Fig. 11 a), the size distribution of cuboidal γ ′  phase becomes more homogeneous ( Fig. 10 ), which can improve the stress rupture property of the present alloy to some extent.  

The spherical  γ ′  phase with a relatively lower volume fraction and a smaller size can somewhat provide strengthening e ﬀ ect to the alloy, and it is easy to re-dissolve into  γ  matrix at the high testing temperature of 930 °C. and the spacing between large cuboidal  γ ′  phase widens  [33] . In this way, the dislocation moves more easily in  γ  matrix channel, thus leading to a large deformation before fracture. This is bene ﬁ cial to the elongation of the alloys. It is noted that the reasonable combination of the size and volume fraction of cubic and spherical  γ ′  phases may im- prove the stress rupture property.  

SEM observations on the longitudinal section of stress ruptured specimens at 930 °C/275 MPa are shown in  Fig. 13 . It can be seen that noticeable cracks mainly form in the MC carbide zone. On one hand, the volume fraction of cuboidal  γ ′  phase ( Fig. 9 a) decreases with increasing volume fraction of MC carbides from Alloy 1 to Alloy 4 ( Fig. 3 a), which is the major reason for the degradation in stress rupture property ( Fig. 11 ). On the other hand, MC carbides can be the initial site of cracks ( Fig. 13 ), which is detrimental to the stress rupture property; however, since its volume fraction is small ( Fig. 3 a), its e ﬀ ect on the stress rupture property should be limited. Besides, the microcracks prefer to initiate and propagate near script-like carbides rather than block carbides  [34] . Therefore, the script-like carbide in Alloy 4 has a detrimental e ﬀ ect on the stress rupture life, as shown in  Fig. 13 d.  

As aforementioned, the residual eutectic still remains in Alloys 1, 3 and 4 after the same heat treatment ( Fig. 6 ). Firstly, the residual eu- tectic partially consumes  γ ′  phase forming elements. Secondly, the or- ientation of  γ ′  rafts around the residual eutectic changes, and the con- tinuity of  γ ′  rafts is also disturbed duo to the residual eutectic. Thirdly, the residual eutectic becomes the site of crack initiation due to the

As mentioned above, the stress rupture life ( Fig. 11 a) decreases with the addition of Zr. It has been reported that the segregation of Zr at grain boundaries decreases the grain boundary energy and signi ﬁ cantly increases the grain boundary cohesion  [4,36 – 38] , and thus inhibits grain boundary sliding and cracking. Therefore, the rupture elongation of Alloy 3 is enhanced by the addition of Zr, which plays an important role in strengthening grain boundaries during the stress rupture tests.  

# 4. Conclusions  

(1) The increase in C content or the Zr addition nearly do not change the morphology of MC carbides in as-cast alloy, while the additions of Y/Ce promote the transformation of MC carbide morphology from blocky to script-like type. After heat treatment, the morphol- ogies or chemical compositions of MC carbides almost do not change, but their volume fraction and size increase with increasing C content and the additions of Zr or Y/Ce.  

(2) The carbides occupy the formation places of the  γ / γ ′  eutectics and the formation of the carbides consumes the  γ / γ ′  eutectic forming elements like Ti, Ta, etc. Therefore, the resulting amount of the  γ / γ ′ eutectics is reduced with the increase of C content. The addition of Zr or Y/Ce can aggravate pile-up of the eutectic elements during the solidi ﬁ cation, and thus leads to the rise of  γ / γ ′  eutectics. The re- sidual eutectics with a low volume fraction and a small size still partially remain in Alloys 1, Alloy 3 and Alloy 4 after the same heat treatment.  

(3) The  γ ′  phase displays a bimodal distribution after heat treatment. A number of cuboidal  γ ′  phases become aligned along certain direc- tions but a few spherical  γ ′  phases randomly distribute in the  γ matrix. The volume fraction of cuboidal  γ ′  phases decreases but the homogeneity of size distribution slightly increases from Alloy 1 to Alloy 4.  

(4) Decreasing volume fraction of cuboidal  γ ′  phase is determined to be the principal reason for the degradation of stress rupture property. In addition, the script-like carbide morphology or residual eutectic are remained after heat treatment, which plays the secondary role in the degradation of the stress rupture properties duo to the crack initiation and propagation along carbide/matrix interfaces or re- sidual eutectic. The spacing between large cuboidal  γ ′  phase be- come widened during stress rupture tests due to the re-dissolution of the spherical  γ ′  phase into  γ  matrix, and the grain boundary sliding and cracking can be inhibited by the addition of Zr, which are bene ﬁ cial to the rupture elongation.
