# Development of Grain Boundary Precipitate-Free Zones in a Ni-Mo-Cr-W Alloy  

JIE SONG, ROBERT FIELD, DOUG KONITZER, and MICHAEL KAUFMAN  

In this study, the morphology and development of precipitate-free zones (PFZs) near grain boundaries (GBs) in low coeﬃcient of thermal expansion (CTE) Ni-Mo-Cr-W alloys (based on Haynes 244) have been investigated as a function of thermal history and composition using electron microscopy techniques. It is shown that the formation of wide, continuous PFZs adjacent to GBs can be largely attributed to a vacancy depletion mechanism. It is proposed that variations in the vacancy distributions that develop after solution heat treatment (SHT) and subsequent quenching and aging greatly inﬂuence the development of the  c ¢ -Ni 2 (Mo,Cr) precipitates during the aging process and result in the development of PFZs of varying sizes. The relatively large (5 to 10  l m) PFZs are distinct from the smaller, more common PFZs that result from solute depletion due to GB precipitation that are typically observed after prolonged aging. During the course of this investigation, heat treatment parameters, such as aging time, SHT temperature, cooling rate after SHT, and heating rate to the aging temperature—all of which change vacancy concentration and distribution adjacent to GBs—were investigated and observed to have signiﬁcant inﬂuence on both the size and morphology of the observed PFZs. In contrast to results from other Ni-based alloys studied previously, PFZ development in the current alloys was observed across a broad range of aging temperatures. This appears to be due to the high misﬁt strain energy of the  c ¢  precipitates, resulting in a nucleation process that is sensitive to vacancy concentration. It is also shown that a slightly modiﬁed alloy with higher Mo concentrations develops smaller, more typical PFZs; this is presumably due to an increased driving force for  c ¢  precipitation which overshadows the inﬂuence of misﬁt strain energy, thereby decreasing the sensitivity of precipitation on vacancy concentration.

# I. INTRODUCTION  

P RECIPITATION  hardening is one of the most important strengthening mechanisms in structural alloys, such as steel, aluminum alloys, and Ni-based 1 ]   superalloys. Precipitate-free zones (PFZs) adjacent to grain boundaries (GBs) are common in precipita- tion-hardened alloys and are detrimental as they act as ‘‘soft areas’’ compared to the grain interiors thereby 1 – 4 ]   leading to localized deformation and fracture. Since PFZs also reduce yield strength and fatigue 2 , – 10 ] strength, understanding and controlling their development are important to materials scientists and engineers.  

PFZs adjacent to GBs are normally attributed to two diﬀerent mechanisms: solute depletion and vacancy 11 , ]   depletion. The eﬀects of these two mechanisms on PFZ width with increasing aging time have been 11 ]   summarized by Chang  et al . as shown schematically in Figure  1 . If PFZ development is driven by solute depletion, the PFZ width follows the blue-dotted line in Figure  1  and the PFZ has several characteristics: (1) the width of the PFZ increases with increasing aging time, (2) the width of the PFZ is normally less than a micron, and (3) the solute depletion is due to GB precipitation 11 , ]   during aging. In contrast, if PFZ development results from vacancy depletion (red solid line in Figure  1 ), the PFZs usually develop at shorter aging times, and their widths decrease with increasing aging time. This phenomenon is exacerbated in systems in which nucleation of intragranular precipitates is not only aﬀected by diﬀusion rates within the matrix, but also by the formation of low-energy solute/vacancy clusters which signiﬁcantly reduce the activation energy for nucleation, as will be discussed below. In addition, the PFZ widths can vary from a few microns to over 10 11 , , ]   l m. In some systems, PFZ development is inﬂuenced by vacancy depletion at short aging times and solute depletion at longer times, as GB precipitates form and grow. This is represented by the green dash-dotted line in Figure  1 .

For the vacancy depletion mechanism shown in Figure  2 , GBs with misorientations larger than  \~ 2 pct act as sinks for the excess vacancies formed during 13 , – 25 ]   solution heat treatment. Edge dislocations can also serve as sinks for vacancies, as well as heteroge- neous nucleation sites for precipitates, depending on the 13 , ]   heat treatment conditions. Therefore, the vacancy concentration is not uniform within the grains,  i.e ., the areas adjacent to GBs and edge dislocations will have lower concentrations compared to the grain interiors. In addition, vacancies can help to reduce the lattice misﬁt strain between the matrix and the precipitates, thereby 13 , ]   decreasing the nucleation barrier. Thus, vacancy concentration can have a profound inﬂuence on the nucleation of misﬁtting precipitates. Given this sensitiv- ity, the nucleation rate can be expected to have kinetics that vary signiﬁcantly with vacancy concentration. During aging, precipitates form readily in areas with [ 13 ] vacancy concentrations exceeding some critical value for a particular aging treatment, whereas precipitation only occurs after prolonged aging times in areas with below-critical vacancy concentration. It should be noted that the ‘‘critical’’ vacancy concentration in Figure  2 actually is not a ﬁxed value but a value that depends on both aging temperature and time due to the C-curve kinetics of vacancy-assisted nucleation. In the other worlds, areas near GBs may have insuﬃcient vacancy concentrations to result in precipitation for a particular aging temperature and time, resulting in PFZs. The inﬂuence of vacancy concentration on precipitation kinetics will be discussed in greater detail later.  

An interrupted quenching process performed by Unwin  et al . was used to show the signiﬁcant eﬀect of vacancy concentration on PFZ development in an 20 ]   Al-Mg-Zn alloy. Speciﬁcally, a hold time at an intermediate temperature before ﬁnal quenching to room temperature resulted in the loss of excess vacancies present at the solution temperature  via  diﬀusion and annihilation at the GBs. The area of vacancy concen- tration below some ‘‘critical’’ value increased in size with longer hold times, resulting in increased PFZ width. At long hold times, the entire width of the grain fell below the critical vacancy concentration and no 20 ] intragranular precipitation was observed upon aging. Quench rate also inﬂuences PFZ formation, especially through the temperature range where vacancies diﬀuse 13 ]   more quickly. For example, the PFZ width in an Al-6Zn-2.2Mg alloy was shown to vary linearly with the inverse square root of the quenching rate when PFZ 11 ] formation is dominated by the vacancy mechanism. To decrease quench rate sensitivity, one can add trace elements into an alloy to stabilize the vacancy distribu- tion during quenching. These stabilizing elements have strong interaction energies with vacancies ( e.g ., Mg in Al 13 , , ]   alloys). Three-dimensional atom probe tomogra- phy results from a study by Ogura  et al . indicated that there was no solute depletion within PFZs in an Al-Mg-Zn alloy, and the authors concluded that vacancy depletion is the key mechanism for PFZ 5 , ] development in this system.  

PFZs in conventional Ni-based alloys are dominated by the solute depletion mechanism. For example, the PFZ width with respect to  c ¢  phase precipitates in a Ni-based superalloy (NIMONIC PE16) was observed to    \~ 28 ] be 1.5  l m after typical aging treatments. The PFZs in this alloy were shown to be due to the Ti depletion in the zones adjacent to GBs due to the formation of TiC 12 , ]   along the GBs. Governed by diﬀusion of Ti, the width of the PFZ increased linearly with the square root of aging time reaching a maximum width that was dictated by the amount of carbon available to form 1 , , ] TiC.  

Haynes alloys are used widely in aerospace and 30 , ]   turbine applications. For example, Haynes 282 is resistant to high-temperature fatigue in steam turbine 32 ]   applications. Solid-solution-strengthened Haynes 230 exhibits longer creep life compared to other Haynes alloys at high temperatures and has a low primary creep 33 , ]   rate due to pinning eﬀects. Finally, Haynes 242 is a precipitation-strengthened alloy where the strengthening phase is the metastable  c ¢ -Ni 2 (Mo,Cr) phase; this phase will eventually decompose during long-term aging treat- ments to a mixture of the stable Ni 3 Mo and Ni 4 Mo phases. These stable phases further increase the strength (yield and tensile), but decrease ductility at both room 35 , ] and high temperatures.  

Despite the eﬀorts mentioned above, there have been only a few investigations of PFZ formation in Ni-based alloys. In the current study, the inﬂuence of both thermal history and composition variations on the development of PFZs has been analyzed in the Haynes 37 ] 244 alloy in some detail.  

# II. EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURES  

The base alloy used in this study was Haynes 244 (Ni-15.6Mo-10.2Cr-2.2 at. pct W), which was kindly provided by Haynes International. Two slightly modi- ﬁed alloys—Ni-13.4Mo-8.8Cr-1.9 at. pctW and Ni-17.9Mo-10.0Cr-2.1 at. pctW—were also prepared by taking pieces of the base Haynes 244 alloy and remelting them together with pieces of pure Ni (99.0 pct)  

or Mo (99.9 pct). All modiﬁed alloys were produced as buttons by non-consumable arc melting in a puriﬁed Ar atmosphere. Each button was ﬂipped and remelted 5 times in an eﬀort to ensure complete melting and mixing. The buttons were then homogenized at 1423 K (1150   C) for 24 hours to produce a uniform microstruc- ture followed by 50 pct reduction by cold rolling to produce 1.1-mm-thick sheet. The samples were then solution heat treated at 1423 K (1150   C) for 1 hour (SHT), and water quenched.

# A.  Aging Temperature Effects  

The microstructures of the  c ¢  precipitates and the PFZ in the Haynes 244 alloy after aging for 4 hours at diﬀerent temperatures are shown in Figure  6 . At the lowest aging temperature, 993 K (720   C), the entire specimen has a uniform distribution of the  c ¢  precipi- tates. For the higher aging temperatures [1023 K to 1053 K (750   C to 780   C)], PFZs were observed and the width of the zones increased with temperature. Further- more, the  c ¢  precipitates near the GBs tended to be coarser and sparser for aging temperatures above 1003 K (730   C) (Figure  6 (a)). The two high-magniﬁcation images (insets) in Figure  6 (a) clearly show the size variation of the  c ¢  precipitates from inside the grain to near the GBs for the specimen aged at 1003 K (730   C). The coarseness of the  c ¢  precipitates near the GBs becomes signiﬁcant at 1013 K (740   C) even though the PFZs are still quite narrow for this treatment. The PFZs are more pronounced for specimens aged at 1023 K (750  C) (Figure  6 (b)), and increase in width as the aging temperature is increased to 1033 K (760   C), 1043 K (770   C) (Figure  4 (b)), and 1053 K (780   C). When the aging temperature is increased to 1063 K (790   C) (Figure  6 (c)), no  c ¢  precipitation is observed within the grains, although there are clearly GB precipitates with solute-depleted zones adjacent to them, as evidenced by the enhanced etching in these areas. Based on above results, the solvus of the  c ¢ -Ni 2 (Mo,Cr) precipitation should be between 1053 K and 1063 K (780   C and 790  C).  

# B.  Aging Time Effects  

The microstructural evolution of the  c ¢  precipitates and the PFZ development as a function of aging time and temperature are shown in Figure  7 . When the specimen is aged at 1023 K (750   C), the  c ¢  precipitates and the PFZ develop within a short time ( i.e ., 1 and 4 hours). The PFZ near the GB in Figure  7 (a) is observed to essentially disappear after aging for 16 hours (Figure  7 (b)). As mentioned above, the  c ¢  precipitates adjacent to the PFZ are larger and more sparsely distributed than in the grain interiors. Meanwhile, small isolated PFZs can be observed along the GBs even when the PFZs appear to be absent adjacent to most of the boundary (Figure  7 (b)). When the aging temperature is increased to 1043 K (770   C), the PFZ becomes wider compared to that at 1023 K (750   C) for the same aging time (Figures  7 (a) through (d)). With increased aging time, the PFZ becomes narrower, as shown in Figures  4 (b) and  7 (d) (4, 16 hours). In contrast to the samples aged at 1023 K (750   C), 16 hours aging is insuﬃcient to eliminate the PFZ at 1043 K (770   C).  

The isolated PFZs shown in Figure  7 (b) are depleted in Mo, which is not the case for the large PFZs in the reference sample. In other words, there were two types of PFZs observed in this study: those with and those without solute depletion. EDS line scans for the two cases are shown in Figure  8 . The development of these isolated Mo-depleted PFZs appears to be  via  the diﬀusion-induced grain boundary migration (DIGM) 40 , ] mechanism.

Slight composition variations play an important role in changing the  c ¢  precipitation behavior as shown in Figure  12 . # IV. DISCUSSION  

As shown in the previous section, the development of PFZs during aging may be dominated by one of two distinct mechanisms—(a) vacancy depletion or (b) solute depletion (Figure  1 ). The most signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the two mechanisms is that the PFZ width decreases with increasing aging time if dominated by vacancy depletion but increases with aging time if dominated by solute depletion. The experimental obser- vations made in this study indicate that the vacancy depletion mechanism is operative in the Haynes 244 alloy given that the PFZ width decreases steadily with aging time, as shown in Figure  13 . Second, the GB precipitates formed during the SHT process at or below 1323 K (1050   C) have little eﬀect on subsequent  c precipitation during aging (Figure  10 ). The GB precip- itates in the samples solution treated at 1323 K and 1223 K (1050   C and 950   C) are quite large compared with those in the sample treated at 1123 K (850   C). However,  c ¢  precipitates are observed upon aging for both the 1323 K and 1223 K (1050   C and 950   C) SHT samples, but not the 1123 K (850   C) sample. Third, EDS analysis of the large PFZs ( e.g ., that shown in reference sample) indicated negligible concentration variations across the PFZs,  i.e ., there is little or no solute depletion in these PFZs.  

In samples aged for the longest times (Figure  7 (b)), the initially wide PFZs decrease in size until they are negligible. However, isolated PFZs adjacent to the GB precipitates were observed ( e.g ., Figure  7 (b)). EDS results from these smaller PFZs, forming at longer times, are consistent with solute depletion (Figure  8 ). For aging at 1023 K (750   C) (Figures  6 (b) and  7 (a) and (b)), the PFZs disappeared nearly completely after aging for 16 hours but again with isolated PFZs surrounding some of the GB precipitates at prolonged aging times. Again, these smaller, isolated PFZs have an appearance that is consistent with their being formed  via  a DIGM mechanism rather than the classical solute depletion mechanism. In both cases, the PFZs become depleted in the solute associated with precipitate formation.  

Since vacancy concentration and depletion appear to inﬂuence the precipitation of  c ¢  and the development of PFZs, it is important to understand their role on both nucleation and growth of this phase. It is well known that excess vacancies retained during the quenching process can increase the diﬀusivity of substitutional elements during precipitation. In other words, vacancies can inﬂuence the nucleation and growth kinetics signif- icantly simply due to their eﬀect on solute mobility. In addition, it is clear that, in this particular system, the vacancies assist the nucleation of the  c ¢  phase signiﬁ- cantly, presumably by lowering the activation barrier for nucleation ( D G \*). # V. CONCLUSION  

The development of PFZs in the Haynes 244 (Ni-Mo-Cr-W) superalloy is dominated largely by the vacancy depletion mechanism. The morphology of this

type of PFZ is wide and continuous, with relatively straight boundaries parallel to the grain boundaries. These PFZs exhibit no solute depletion relative to the matrix and are observed to decrease in width with increasing aging times. Exceptions were observed at longer aging times in which case, local, solute-depleted PFZs were observed adjacent to GB precipitates; in these cases, these zones are consistent with a diﬀu- sion-induced grain boundary migration (DIGM) mech- anism. For the majority of PFZs observed in this study, the vacancies impact precipitation in two ways: (1) by increasing substitutional atom diﬀusivity, and (2) by decreasing the misﬁt strain energy ( D G s ) thereby reduc- ing the nucleation barrier for the metastable, coherent  c ¢ precipitates. Thus, vacancy concentration gradients between the grain interiors and the areas adjacent to the GBs can cause a gradient in the precipitation rate. Because of the inﬂuence of vacancies on precipitation, the eﬀect of thermal history on the resulting vacancy concentration proﬁles across the grains can be quite signiﬁcant. For example, increasing the aging tempera- ture reduced the undercooling below the coherent  c ¢ solvus, resulting in a lower driving force such that precipitation is less likely within the grains. Increasing the SHT temperature leads to higher vacancy concen- trations, and promotes precipitation and smaller PFZs for a given aging temperature. Rapid cooling rates after solution heat treatment provide less time for vacancy annihilation at GBs and were shown to result in smaller PFZs, as expected. Slow heating rates to the aging temperature also impact the PFZs due to the fact that nuclei are formed and vacancies are trapped during the heating process; this results in small PFZs. Finally, the alloy composition has a strong inﬂuence on precipita- tion and PFZ formation by altering the driving force for nucleation, ( D G V ).
