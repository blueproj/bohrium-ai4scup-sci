# Abstract  

Phase compositions of matrix and  g %  phase in the third-generation superalloy CMSX-10 containing 6 wt.% Re were measured locally by energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy in a transmission electron microscope. The corresponding lattice misﬁts between matrix and  g %  phase were determined by convergent beam electron diffraction. All measurements were performed in dendrites and interdendritic regions. Comparing the as-cast condition to the standard heat treated condition, remarkable enrichments of Re in the  g %  phase of dendrites and segregation of W and Ta in the matrix are detected. After standard heat treatment, an enrichment of Re remains in the matrix of dendrites. This results in differences in amount and sign of lattice misﬁt. Coherency stresses were calculated using the Finite Elements Method. © 2000 Elsevier Science S.A. All rights reserved.  

Keywords : Nickel-base superalloys; Phase composition; Dendritic macrostructure; Lattice misﬁt; Convergent beam electron diffraction; Coherency stresses  

# 1. Introduction  

CMSX-10, with the composition given in Table 1, belongs to the third generation of nickel-base superal- loys designed for aircraft turbines [1]. The high content of refractory elements, especially that of 6 wt.% Re, improves the high-temperature creep performance of the alloy but requires careful heat treatment in order to equalize inhomogeneous element distributions prevalent in the macrostructure of the as-cast condition. As has been observed in CMSX-4, which contains 3 wt.% Re, different phase compositions in dendrites and interden- dritic areas [2] are connected with local differences in the lattice parameters of the two phases and cause gradients of lattice misﬁt values in the macrostructure even after standard heat treatment [3].  

In nickel-base single crystal superalloys the L1 2  or- dered  g %  phase is embedded coherently in the fcc  g matrix phase. If the lattice parameters  a g  of the matrix and  a g %  of the  g %  phase are slightly different, a lattice misﬁt  d = 2( a − a ) / ( a + a ) results for the uncon- u g % g g % g strained condition. Knowledge of the misﬁt is impor- tant, as it inﬂuences internal stresses and deformation mechanisms of single crystal superalloys [4,5] and there- fore is a parameter in mechanical behavior modeling. In most experiments, the constrained misﬁt  d c  is deter- mined either as an average value by neutron diffraction

 [6] or semi-locally by X-ray diffraction measurements

 [7]. The highest lateral resolution is obtained by conver- gent beam electron diffraction (CBED) [3,8–13].  

Owing to the dendritic solidiﬁcation of the alloy, which causes composition gradients, averaging methods are not adequate for the correct determination of misﬁt values. In our investigation, we used the high lateral resolution of CBED for the local measurement of the  g and  g %  lattice parameters. Thereby misﬁt values in den- drites and interdendritic regions could be distinguished. The corresponding chemical compositions of the two phases were also measured locally in a transmission electron microscope by energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDX).

# 2. Experimental  

# 2.1.  Material  

A   001   oriented single crystal CMSX-10 had been supplied by HOWMET (Whitehall, MI, USA) and heat treated by the CANNON-MUSKEGON Corp. (Muskegon, MI, USA). The nominal composition of the alloy [1] together with the heat treatment is given in Table 1. Investigations were performed in the as-cast and in the fully heat treated condition. After casting, the alloy has a marked dendritic macrostructure (Fig. 1). The inhomogeneous element distribution can be directly concluded from back-scattered electron (BSE) contrast, not only between dendrites and interdendritic areas, but also inside the dendrites. After standard heat treatment, the gradient between dendrite and interden- dritic BSE contrast has lowered considerably (Fig. 2).  

# 2.2.  Specimen preparation  

In order to produce equally thin TEM foils for transmission electron microscopy (TEM), a multistep procedure was applied. Two hundred micrometer thin disks were cut perpendicular to the   100   growth direc- tion of the crystals. The disks were thinned electrolyti- cally (83% ethanol, 10% perchloric acid and 7% glycerin) until a hole appeared followed by 2 h of ion milling with Ar ions. For the BALTEC RES 010 ion mill, the optimal conditions were 8° inclination angle, 2.8 mA beam current, 3.5 kV acceleration voltage and a specimen potential of  − 0.5 kV. The mean thickness of TEM foils was 200 nm.

# 2.4.  Lattice misﬁt determination by CBED  

In a previous investigation [3,13] an incident beam direction of   1 1 19   for the CBED patterns was found best to ensure that no overlapping of matrix and  g % phase occurs during the analysis and that dynamic effects are reduced. The effective (302.1 9 0.3) kV accel- eration tension of the TEM was calibrated with a high-purity Ni single crystal. The stability of the high tension was checked and no deviation within the 0.3 kV error was found.  

CBED patterns were recorded with a GATAN TV- camera and integrated over several TV-frames. Digi- tization and integration were performed with the program ‘Image  CBED + + ’ developed by Vo ¨lkl [16]. For details of the applied CBED method see [3] and [13].

# 3.3.  Thin foil lattice constants determined by CBED and misﬁt  6 alues  

The CBED patterns of the  g %  phase indicate cubic symmetry in dendrites and interdendritic regions. Fig. 7 is an example in the center of a dendrite. In contrast, CBED patterns of the matrix taken in the middle of the matrix channels deviate from a mirror symmetry (Fig. 8). Arrows in Figs 7 and 8 mark triangles formed by three Higher Order Laue Zone lines. As compared to the  g %  pattern, the triangle in the matrix is smaller. This is evidence of the larger lattice constants in the matrix, because the distance between two Higher Order Laue Zone lines is reciprocal to the corresponding lattice constant. For CBED measurements close to the foil hole, we ﬁnd a dependence between lattice parameters and the orientation of the matrix channel relative to the hole. Due to stress relaxation, the misﬁt value is smaller for measurements in matrix channels running perpen- dicular to the hole as compared to those running paral- lel. It is therefore necessary to do CBED measurements in matrix channels parallel to the foil hole and at sufﬁcient distance to it.  

The results of lattice parameter determinations in matrix and  g %  phase are summarized in Fig. 9. They come from CBED measurements in four foils for places running through dendrite centers, dendrite arms and interdendritic areas. It is seen that the lattice constant of the  g %  phase  a g %  is almost constant in the different parts of the macrostructure while the lattice parameter a g Þ  changes. In dendrites the lattice parameter of the  g % phase is smaller than that of the matrix. On the con- trary,  a g %  is larger than  a g Þ  in interdendritic areas. The standard deviation of any lattice constant at one point is  9 0.0002 nm.

# 4. Discussion  

In agreement with the strong segregation of elements W and Re in dendrites of the as-cast condition, a high negative misﬁt is determined in dendrite centers. After standard heat treatment, where only a small enrichment of Re remains in the matrix of dendrites, the misﬁt is strongly reduced. In interdendritic areas of the as-cast condition we ﬁnd an enrichment of Ta in both phases. The misﬁt is slightly positive. In conformity with the minor changes in phase compositions in interdendritic areas during standard heat treatment, the positive misﬁt of the as-cast condition remains in interdendritic regions.  

The very effective heat treatment that levels out all element gradients between dendrites and interdendritic areas except that of Re, makes it possible to study the inﬂuence of Re on the magnitude of  d . # 5. Conclusions  

The dendritic solidiﬁcation of superalloy causes inho- mogeneities in  g %  dimensions and alignment, phase com- positions, misﬁts and coherency stresses.  

  The applied heat treatment improves morphology and alignment of  g %  cubes. Concentration gradients between dendrites and interdendritic areas are lev- eled out, except for Re, which remains more highly concentrated in the dendrite matrix.

   The volume fraction of the  g %  phase is increased by the applied heat treatment.

   The high reduction of Ta in the matrix during standard heat treatment is connected with secondary g %  precipitation and the formation of an intermetallic (Ta 70 Ti 9 Al 7 )Ni 12  phase.

   Phase compositions in interdendritic areas of the as-cast condition are close to phase compositions after standard heat treatment. In conformity with the minor changes in phase compositions in inter- dendritic areas during standard heat treatment, the positive misﬁt of the as-cast condition remains in interdendritic regions.

   The smaller misﬁt of CMSX-10 as compared with CMSX-4 is discussed as being due to the smaller partitioning ratio of Ta in CMSX-10.

   The calculated values of unconstrained misﬁt are in good agreement with the plate approximation by Mu ¨ller et al. [21].

   Owing to the variation of lattice misﬁt in dendrites and interdendritic areas, the alloy experiences con- siderable gradients of coherency stresses in the macrostructure.
