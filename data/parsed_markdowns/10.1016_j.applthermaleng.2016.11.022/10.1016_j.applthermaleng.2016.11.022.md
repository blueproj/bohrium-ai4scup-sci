# Normal spectral emissivity of GH536 (HastelloyX) in three surface conditions  

Bo Kong, Ting Li, Qitai Eri \*  

School of Energy and Power Engineering, Beihang University, Beijing, 100191,  

China  

Abstract  

The normal spectral emissivity of GH536, a nickel-based superalloy, is  measured using a FT-IR spectrometer in the wavelength range 1\~15μm at moderate  and high temperature (400\~1200K), as well as the samples with partially oxidized and  flame treatment processed. It is found that the emissivity of GH536 decreases with the  increasing of emission wavelength, which is well predicted by the electromagnetic  wave theory, and increases linearly when the temperature rises. The emissivity of the  other two samples with partially oxidized and flame treatment processed is also  investigated in details. The results of the partially oxidized sample indicate that  oxidation can enhance the emissivity significantly and alter the wavelength  dependence trend. And the results of the flame treatment processed sample shows that  the impurities and defects caused by the fire treatment process moderately increase  the emissivity. The experimental data of GH536 is compared with the results  calculated using Hagen-Rubens equation, which shows an inconsistence at high  temperature condition. Therefore, a modified emissivity model is proposed, which has  a good agreement in measured wavelength range at moderate and high temperature  condition.  

Key words: High-temperature alloy; Infrared emissivity; Surface conditions;  

Hagen-Rubens equation  

# 1.   Introduction  

Thermal radiation is increasingly important in heat transfer at medium to high  temperature [1]. In order to accurately calculate thermal radiation in relevant     industrial applications, radiative properties are essential to acquire. Ideally, classical  electromagnetic wave theory can be used to predict all radiative properties, emissivity,  absorptivity and reflectivity, of any substance. However, optical constants required in  equations are very difficult to obtain and the theory incorporates some assumptions  that many materials cannot meet. What’s more, the surface conditions, such as the  degree of roughness, oxide layers and contaminants, having great effects on the  radiative properties, are not taken into account in the electromagnetic wave theory [2].  Therefore, the direct measurement of radiative properties, especially in certain surface  conditions, is significant for high fidelity radiation computation and improving the  understanding of radiation.  

L. del Campo investigated the normal emissivity dependence of three kinds of  Ni and Co based aeronautical alloys, Inconel 718 ,Rene41 and Haynes 25 ， on  wavelength (2\~22μm),sample temperature (200\~650 ℃ ) and emission angle (0\~85°) 。 The effects of surface conditions and oxidation were taken into consideration [3].  Benjamin P. Keller measured the hemispherical emissivity of Inconel 718 in four  different surface types from 650K to 1275K, and found that the hemispherical  emissivity increased with the increasing of temperature and oxidation [4]. The major  element of GH536, nickel, was widely investigated. Setién-Fernández studied the  normal spectral emissivity of nickel in the range of 3\~21μm between 250\~900 ℃   and  found the Hagen–Rubens equation predicted poorly for nickel [5].   Total hemispherical  emissivity was obtained in the 300\~1000K range using calorimetric techniques [6].  Additional spectral emissivity values were obtained between 1167 and 1332 ℃  for a     high-purity nickel sample and at its melting point with a pulse-heating technique [7].  In terms of the effect of surface conditions on radiative properties,   Tai al. studied total  hemisphere emissivity of pure nickel at different oxidized degrees. They found the  quantitative effects of the growth scale and rate of NiO film on the total hemispherical  radiation properties [8]. However, no data related to hemisphere or spectral emissivity  of GH536, which is a kind of nickel-based superalloy widely used in combustor and  other hot parts of aero-engine, can be found in literatures. In this paper, we  parametricly investigate the normal spectral emissivity of GH536 in different surface  conditions at medium to high temperature.  

The normal emissivity of GH536 in wavelength range of 1\~15μm at  400\~1200K is carefully measured. After that, we investigate the normal emissivity of  the superalloy at two typical surface conditions when is used in aero-engine, which  are oxidation and heating by the flame. In addition, we compare the experimental data  with the data calculated by Hagen–Rubens equation according to electromagnetic  theory. Finally, a modified emissivity model is proposed.  

# 2.1 Samples  

The composition of GH536 is shown in Table 1 [9]. First, we manufacture three  thin cylindrical samples with the size of 30mm in diameter and 2mm in thickness  using the same finish turning process to ensure the nearly same surface  micromorphology. Then these samples are washed by acetone to eliminate impurities     and contaminants on the surfaces and then dried in the air. One sample is kept after  finish turning and named as S1. The rest two of them are carefully processed to  acquire specific surface conditions. One is heated in a high-temperature air muffle  furnace at the temperature of 900 ℃   for 720 min and named as S2. The other is heated  in flame of a kerosene diffusion flame for 20 min and named as S3. We can see that  the color of the oxidized sample turns black and its surface becomes rough and the  color of the burnt sample turns brown. Then we measure the roughness of these  samples, using a conventional roughmeter. The roughness of each sample is shown in  Table 2. And X-ray diffraction (XRD) is used to detect generated products on the  surfaces of S2 and S3. The results show that the oxidation products formed on S2 are  Cr 2 O 3  and NiCrMnO 4  and the proportion of Cr 2 O 3  is higher than NiCrMnO 4 . There is  no sign of oxidization found on S3.The reason is that the flame consumed the most of  oxygen and the flame processing time is not long enough so the oxidization is not  detected. The XRD result of S2 is displayed in Fig. 1. A scanning electron microscope  (SEM) is used to observe the micromorphology of three sample’s surfaces. Fig. 2 is  SEM micrographs of each sample in different magnification factors. It can be seen  that the micromorphology of these samples varies a lot from each other. The surface  of S1 is relatively clean and smooth and the tiny grooves on it are due to the  manufacture process. The surface of S2 is covered by a compact and continuous oxide  crystal film. Since the oxide crystals do not distribute uniformly, there are many pores  on the film. So the surface was more complex and rough than S1. The surface of S3  has some impurities and tiny defects, caused by the flame process. The effect of     micromorphology on emissivity will be discussed later.

2.2 Apparatus and measurements  

The normal spectral emissivity is measured by a high-temperature normal  spectral emissivity measurement system shown in Fig. 3, which mainly consists of a  FT–IR spectrometer, a sample heating furnace, a blackbody chamber and a vacuum  system.  

The FTIR spectrometer (Nicolet iS50) is used to rapidly and accurately detect and  process the signals from the sample and blackbody. Some important parameters of the  FTIR spectrometer are list: (1) wavelength range: 0.9-26μm; (2) resolution: 1/16 cm -1 ;  (3) signal to noise ratio: 55000:1; (4) wavenumber accuracy: 0.005 cm -1 .  

The heating furnace heats up the sample by a Silicon Nitride plate and the  temperature is measured by a  S -type thermocouple wire with the diameter of 0.5mm  which is in contact with the heating plate. Temperature is controlled by a PID device  with the accuracy of 1 ℃ . To avoid the direct contact of the sample and thermocouple  that influence the temperature field of the sample, this temperature is also regarded as  the sample’s temperature. Therefore, the thickness of the sample is preferable in  1-2mm to ensure a limited temperature gradient. The sample is fixed by a high  temperature ceramic sample holder in a disk sharp with an adjustable diameter from  30-50mm.  

The blackbody source is a cavity blackbody furnace (ISDC, IR-564) with the  aperture diameter of 25.4 mm. Its emissivity is beyond 0.99. The temperature is also     controlled by a PID device with the accuracy of 0.2 ℃ .  

The vacuum system is used to eliminate the influence of oxidation during the  heating process and the absorption by CO 2  and vapor. The vacuum pump can evacuate  the chamber to 10 -2 Pa.

The uncertainty of this system comes from three parts: (1) temperature  (temperature measurement of the sample and blackbody, temperature stability of the  sample and blackbody); (2) the blackbody cavity effective emissivity; (3) the  none-linearity of FTIR and the noise signal.  

The uncertainty of temperature is evaluated at two temperatures, 200 ℃  and  1200 ℃ , for the sample and blackbody.   The uncertainty values of sample temperature  at 200 ℃  and 1200 ℃  are 0.0121 and 0.0077. The uncertainty value of blackbody  temperature at the same temperature   is 0.0009 and 0.0006, respectively. The  blackbody cavity effective emissivity uncertainty is 0.0065. The none-linearity of  FTIR and the noise signal vary from wavelength. Here we give the maximum values  provided by the FTIR spectrometer manufacturer. The none-linearity uncertainty is  0.0043 and the noise signal is 0.0029. The combined standard uncertainty is  calculated by summing up all the square uncertainties. Therefore, the combined  uncertainty of this system at 200 ℃  and 1200 ℃  is 0.0147 and 0.0113, respectively. The  corresponding expended uncertainty values are 0.0294 and 0.0226 at the 95%  confidence level.  

According  to the  researches  conduct  by  González-Fernández  and  Setién-Fernández, the surface stresses generated during the machining process  increase the emissivity, while this effect can be eliminated by annealing. Higher  annealing temperature is good to increase the rate of surface stresses relieve [5][11].  So before the experiment, we heat the sample S1 for 1 hour at 900 ℃  in vacuum. For  samples S2 and S3, the oxidation and flame process have already severed as a  relieving step, therefore, we can measure their emissivity directly. After all  preparations, the emissivity of three samples is measured from 400K\~1200K in the  interval of 100K. Every temperature step is kept for 20 minutes to ensure thermal  equilibrium before the FT-IR begins to acquire the sample radiative spectrum.  

3. Results and Discussion  

The normal emissivity of GH536 in three surface processing methods is  acquired between 1\~15μm at medium to high temperature (400\~1200K).  

3.1 The normal emissivity of GH536  

Fig. 4 shows the normal spectral emissivity of the finish turning sample as a  function of wavelength at different temperatures. It can be seen that the emissivity  decreases as the wavelength increases and the variation in short wavelengths is more  significant than that in long wavelengths. These behaviors are in agreement with  electromagnetic wave theory for metal [2]. In addition, the emissivity increases with  increasing temperature. In order to see the relation between temperature and  emissivity clearly in different wavelengths, the emissivity at different temperature in     three specific wavelengths (2μm, 8μm and 14μm) is shown in Fig. 5. It can be seen  that the emissivity is proportional to the temperature in measured range and the slopes  of the lines are almost independent of wavelength. The deviation of experimental data  and the fitting line is within the uncertainty of the measurement system.

3.2 Effect of surface conditions on the normal emissivity  

Fig. 6 shows the normal spectral emissivity of the oxidized sample as a  function of wavelength at different temperatures. The emissivity increases  significantly compared to the finish turning sample in all wavelength range, especially  in λ<8μm where the average emissivity is larger than 0.7. The wavelength dependence  of emissivity also behaves irregular that there is a sharp decrease and slight increase  of the emissivity in the wavelength range from 9μm to 15μm.  

As shown in the XRD result, the oxidation products of GH536 formed on the  surface consist of Cr 2 O 3  and NiCrMnO 4 . Although the mass fraction of Ni accounts  for the most proportion in all composition, the main products are Chromium oxides.  The reason is that Cr is more active than Ni according to thermodynamics and the  outward transmitting capacity of Cr element through grain boundary is stronger.  What’s more, Cr 2 O 3  is more stable than the oxides of other elements in the Ni-Cr  alloy systems [12][13]. Therefore, at the beginning of the oxidation process, the Cr 2 O 3  is generated faster on the surface of the GH536 sample than other oxides. Then these  oxides interact with each other, forming the NiCrMnO 4  in spinel structure and a  compact and continuous oxidation film, which can block the transmission of  superalloy elements, resulting in a decrease of oxidation rate. This is also the reason  why this kind of superalloy has a good ability of oxidization resistance.

Metals are with an abundance of free electrons that are high reflectance and poor  absorption to thermal radiation, resulting in low emissivity, according to the  electromagnetic wave theory. While the radiative properties of nonconductors are  dominated in the infrared by photon–phonon interaction, i.e., by the photon excitation  of the vibrational energy levels of the solid’s crystal lattice [2]. The relative vibration  can produce some electric dipole moment which can interact with electromagnetic  wave, triggering strong absorption in infrared [14]. This is one reason that the  oxidation film raises the emissivity significantly. The other reason is the increase in  surface roughness and complexity of micromorphology. The SEM image in Fig. 2  shows that the surface of S2 is much rougher and more complicated than S1.  Electromagnetic wave is reflected and absorbed more times between the oxide  crystals on the surface of S2, so more radiation is absorbed. Then the sharp decrease  of emissivity in long wavelength is high possible due to the sharp decrease of  absorptive index  k  on both side of the absorption band of the oxidation film at this     range of wavelength.  

The temperature dependence at different wavelength is shown in Fig.7. As seen,  the temperature and emissivity are almost in linear relation with a small and same  slope at all three wavelengths.

The SEM image in Fig.2 shows that there are multiple grooves  as the S1 sample and small impurity particles and defects left on the surface of the  superalloy after flame treatment process. The most of the radiation is emitted from the     superalloy itself, which can also be proved by the fact that the wavelength and  temperature dependence of emissivity of S3 are quiet similar with that of finish  turning sample. However, the soot and other combustion residual particles which have  a much higher value of emissivity increase the emissivity of S3 significantly. In  addition, the defects of the surface caused by flame treatment process increase the S3  surface roughness which leads to the increase of the reflection and absorption on the  surface. Then the increase of surface roughness affects the emissivity at longer  wavelengths more than at shorter wavelengths [3][15][16]. So the emissivity of S3  sample increases more in long wavelengths, resulting in a smaller variation of  emissivity between long wavelength and short wavelength.

The  comparison between experimental data and the analytical results using the  Hagen-Rubens equation is shown in Fig.11. As seen, although there is a relative good  agreement at 500K, the significant inconsistence exists at 1000K is unaccepted. These  results demonstrate that the Hagen-Rubens equation is not capable to be used to  predict the emissivity of GH536, especially at high temperature. These discrepancies  are highly possible coming from the assumption of an abundance of free electrons that  ignores the resonance frequency in the Lorentz model. Meanwhile, the optically  smooth surface condition can also lead to mistakes. Similar phenomenon has been  found and reported for nickel [5]. Nickel does not follow the free electron model for  λ< 20μm [17][18]. As Ni is the major element in GH536, it is very likely that the  Hagen-Rubens equation is not capable to predict all Ni-based superalloys correctly.

Hagen-Rubens equation simplified from Drude theory can’t well predict the  emissivity of metals that do not follow the free electron model. Lorentz-Drude (L-D)  model incorporates the free electron model and the oscillator model used to describe  the intraband and interband absorption of the complex dielectric function, respectively  [19]. Zhang measured the reflective index  n  and the absorptive index  k  of Ni in the  temperature range of 300\~773K and used L-D model to fit the complex dielectric  function based on the experiment data [20]. There was a good agreement between the  fitting results and the experiment data. In this research, the oscillation damping factor  in the Lorentz part increases with increase of temperature, which leads to the decrease  of absorptive index  k  caused the increase of the emissivity. Obviously, this fact is not  considered in the Hagen-Rubens equation. 4. Conclusions  

In this paper, the normal emissivity of GH536 with different surface     conditions has been studied in detail. It is found that the emissivity of GH536  decreases with the increase of wavelength as the electromagnetic wave theory predicts.  In additional, the temperature dependence of emissivity is nearly linear with small  positive slope.  

An oxide layer is formed on the surface of the oxidized sample, which consists  of Cr 2 O 3  and NiCrMnO 4 . Oxidation process has considerable effects on the emissivity  that significantly increases the average emissivity and makes the trend of wavelength  dependence irregular. These effects are due to by the oxide layer formed on the  surface of GH536. First, the radiation is mainly emitted from the oxide layer so the  emissivity is similar to that of nonconductor rather than metal. In addition, the  oxidation process increases the roughness and complexity of the surface, which also  contributes to the increase of emissivity. Furthermore, the emissivity also increases  linearly with increasing temperature.  

There is limited oxide products generated after the flame treatment process  since the concentration of oxygen is quiet low inside the diffusion flame. The surface  turns coarse and has some impurity particles and small defects. The wavelength  dependence is similar to the finish turning sample but the emissivity is higher.  

The comparison between experimental data and calculated data using  Hagen-Rubens equation shows an obvious disagreement. The reason is that this  Ni-based superalloy does not follow the assumptions of the equation. Therefore, a  modified emissivity model with temperature correction term is proposed, which has a  good agreement from near IR to far IR and moderate and high temperature conditions.  

These experimental data and the modified Hagen-Rubens equation can be used  in radiative heat transfer computation with the similar surface conditions. But it  should be always kept in mind that small changes in surface conditions may lead to  considerable changes in emissivity.
