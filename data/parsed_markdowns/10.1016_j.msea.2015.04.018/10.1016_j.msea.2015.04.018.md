The microstructure and tensile behaviour of Mo-doped Co – 9Al – (9  x )W – 2Ta – 0.02B – x Mo alloys (where x 0, 2, and 4 at% is hereafter referred to as 0Mo, 2Mo, and 4Mo alloys, respectively) at room and high ¼ temperatures have been investigated. Aged at 800  1 C for 100 h, all alloys consist of a Co solid-solution γ -Co SS  matrix and nano-scale cuboidal  γ 0 -Co 3 (Al, W) intermetallic precipitates. The  γ  matrix is coherent with the  γ 0 -Co 3 (Al, W) precipitates. With a coherent  γ / γ 0 -Co 3 (Al, W) microstructure, the aged alloys show tensile yield strength anomalies at temperatures above 700  1 C, and the 0Mo alloy always exhibits the highest strength at 600 – 800  1 C. It is interesting that the alloys also possess anomalously low elongation of 0.4 – 3.3% at 600 – 800  1 C, which is signi ﬁ cantly below the   10% elongation at room temperature. Intergranular cleavage is found to be the dominant failure mode of the  γ / γ 0 -Co 3 (Al, W) microstructure at high temperatures, resulting in tensile plasticity anomalies. At room temperature, however, slip bends are observed on the aligned  γ  and  γ 0   phases, and the  γ / γ 0 -Co 3 (Al, W) coherent microstructure fails in a dimple mode with excellent elongation.

# 1. Introduction  

Since the discovery of a  γ 0 -Co 3 (Al, W) phase in the Co – Al – W ternary  [1] , signi ﬁ cant interest in the phase stabilisation, micro- structural evolution, and mechanical and oxidation behaviours of the aforementioned ternary by multi-alloying has developed  [2 – 8] . Because the  γ 0   phase precipitates coherently from the  γ  matrix, the Co – Al – W ternary material exhibits a microstructure similar to that typical of Ni-based alloys  [9,10] . This introduces the coherent interface strengthening mechanism and enhances the high- temperature strength to a level considerably above that of tradi- tional Co-based alloys, strengthened only by solid solution ele- ments and carbides  [11,12] .  

Frequently, alloying elements such as Ta, Cr and/or Re, Mo [5,7,8,13]  have been added to the Co – Al – W ternary to increase strength at room and high temperatures. Systemic research under compressive loading has revealed that with Ta, Cr and/or Re, and Mo additions, Co – Al – W alloys exhibit excellent ductility and  ﬂ ow stress anomalies above 600  1 C, whereas the anomalous peak strength values appear at 700 – 800  1 C. Ta is found to be the most ef ﬁ cient strengthening element for a Co – Al – W ternary alloy  [2] . For example, with the addition of 2 at% Ta, the solvus temperature  

Recently, the tensile behaviour of the Co – Al – W ternary-based alloys at room temperature was investigated  [7,19] . An elongation larger than 11.5% was obtained in Co – 9.2Al – 9.5W alloys  [7]  and Co – 9Al – (5 – 9)W – 2Ta – (0 – 4)Mo  [19]  when doped with 0.02 at% B. This mechanism of ductility enhancement is explained by the suppression of any intergranular fractures with B, which have been discovered in Ni 3 Al single-phase polycrystalline alloys with brittle behaviour and grain boundary failure  [18] . In this study, Co – 9Al – 9W – 2Ta – 0.02B is used as the base composition, and 2 at% and 4 at% Mo are added to replace the same amount of W. A detailed investigation with regard to room- and high-temperature tensile behaviours of the Co – 9Al – (9  x )W – 2Ta – 0.02B – x Mo alloys (where  x ¼ 0, 2, and 4 at%, hereafter referred to as 0Mo (Co – 9Al – 9W – 2Ta – 0.02B), 2Mo (Co – 9Al – 7W – 2Ta – 0.02B – 2Mo) and 4Mo (Co – 9Al – 5W – 2Ta – 0.02B – 4Mo) alloys, respectively) is performed because the properties, particularly the plastic elongation and fracture mode, are thought to be key behaviours for an alloy intended for structural applications, however, to the knowledge of the authors, systemic reports on this topic are not available.  

# 2. Experimental procedures  

Button ingots of the 0Mo, 2Mo and 4Mo alloys, each with a mass of 150 g were prepared by arc melting through  ﬁ ve or six cycles in an argon atmosphere. The purity of all raw elements was 99.99% by mass or higher. The ingots were solution-treated at 1350  1 C for 8 h in a vacuum, followed by furnace cooling, and were then sealed in an evacuated quartz capsule that was back- ﬁ lled with argon and aged at 800  1 C for 100 h before cooling in air. X-ray diffraction (XRD) analysis was performed on the bulk samples to identify the phase constitutions using Cu K α  ( λ 0.15405 nm) ¼ radiation at 40 kV and 40 mA (Rigaku D/Max 2500PC). Back- scattered electron (BSE) images were taken to investigate the microstructures of the as-cast samples and the aged samples, and the fracture surfaces of the tested samples using an SEM (JEOL JXA-8100). The grain sizes were measured by the crystal orienta- tion map using the electron backscattering diffraction (EBSD) function of the SEM at the scanning step sizes of 1 – 3  μ m. Phase

# 3.2. Tensile deformation and damage behaviour of the aged alloys at room temperature  

In an earlier work  [19] , we found that this coherent  γ / γ 0 microstructure produces a tensile elongation larger than 11% at room temperature. To understand the mechanisms allowing the coherent  γ / γ 0   microstructure to have such a signi ﬁ cant ductility at room temperature, the deformation and failure behaviours of the material were investigated by an in-situ tensile test using an SEM in this work.  Fig. 5  shows the deformation morphologies of the 2Mo alloy with a typical  γ / γ 0   microstructure at different tensile plastic strains (i.e., elongation), in which the tensile stress – strain curve is shown. Two groups of slip lines with different orientations appear, between which the angle is approximately 45 1 , as shown in  Fig. 5 b. When the nominal plastic strain reaches 2.5%, the parallel slip lines near the notch tip becomes thicker and denser, and a furrow-like morphology develops, indicating severe plastic deformation (see  Fig. 5 c). # 3.3. Tensile behaviour of the aged alloys at high temperatures  

The tensile stress – strain curves of the three aged alloys at 600  1 C, 700  1 C and 800  1 C are shown in  Fig. 6 , in addition, the temperature dependence of the 0.2% yield strength,  σ 0.2 , is plotted to determine the effect of Mo on strength ( Fig. 6 d). It is clear that with the coherent  γ / γ 0   microstructure, the 0Mo, 2Mo and 4Mo alloys all show similar tensile stress – strain characteristics: the stress quickly reaches a peak value after yielding, followed by failure, without a noticeable plastic elongation at 600 – 800  1 C. Data of the average 0.2% yield tensile strength ( σ 0.2 ) in  Fig. 6 d reveal that the 0Mo alloy always has the highest strength near 800  1 C, in which the  σ 0.2  of the three alloys at room temperature was taken from reference  [19] . At each temperature,  σ 0.2  decreases monotonically as the Mo content increases, which is consistent with the dependence of the  γ 0   phase solvus temperature and the  γ 0   phase fraction versus the Mo content. It is shown that under tension, the three alloys also show yield strength anomalies:  σ 0.2  decreases with temperature and reaches a minimum value at 700  1 C before rising again as the temperature increases further. Therefore, the temperature at which the strength anomaly occurs for the three alloys is near 700  1 C.  

It is interesting to note that the three aged samples have very low tensile plastic elongation at 600 – 800  1 C (see  Fig. 6 a – c).  Table 2 summarises the compiled elongations of the three alloys as functions of temperature with the elongations of the three alloys at room temperature for comparison. It is shown that the materi- als have outstanding ductility (i.e., greater than 11.5%) at room temperature for the dog-bone specimens without a notch (9% for the in-situ dog-bone specimens with a notch, see  Fig. 5 ); however, their ductility is signi ﬁ cantly lower (0.4 – 3.3% in elongation) at 600 – 800  1 C. This phenomenon is called the anomalous tempera- ture dependence of the tensile ductility in this work.  

# 3.4. Fractographic observations on the fracture surfaces at high temperatures  

To understand the mechanism of why the coherent  γ / γ 0 microstructure shows such a low tensile ductility at high tem- peratures, a fractographic observation of the fracture surface was performed. Fig. 7 shows the deformation and damage morpholo-gies of the  γ / γ 0   microstructure in the position away from the fracture surface for 0.2 – 0.3 mm along the tensile axis direction. The aligned cuboidal  γ 0 -Co 3 (Al, W) precipitates have been altered (see  Fig. 7 a), the arrangement of the  γ  and  γ 0   phases is not as regular as that in the samples in the aged state ( Fig. 3 ). However, the cuboidal shape of the  γ 0 -Co 3 (Al, W) precipitates remains, and no slip lines are found in the  γ / γ 0   microstructure. In addition, small voids can be observed occasionally in the  γ  phase, as identi ﬁ ed with the arrows A in  Fig. 7 a and b, which show the locations of preferential damage at small deformations (i.e., the channel of the γ  phase). It is evident that the deformation and damage of the coherent  γ / γ 0   microstructure is not severe before fracture because the total elongation at 600 – 800  1 C is as small as of 0.4 – 3.3%. Typical morphologies of the fractured surfaces in  Fig. 8 a – c suggest that at temperatures of 600 – 800  1 C, the coherent  γ / γ 0   microstruc- ture fractures completely along the grain boundaries. The enlarged SEM images of the separated grain boundaries shown in  Fig. 8 a 0 – c 0 demonstrate smooth surfaces with slight river lines, revealing the dominant fracture mode to be the intergranular cleavage. In addition, a small amount of dimples is found to scatter on the cleavage surface and is believed to result in the 3.3% plastic   elongation observed. It is concluded that the anomalous tempera- ture dependence of the tensile ductility corresponds to the fracture mechanism changing from dimple mode (see  Fig. 5 e and f) at room temperature to the intergranular cleavage mode at high temperatures.  

# 4. Discussion  

A series of works  [3,7]  have con ﬁ rmed that under compressive conditions, the Co – Al – W ternaries and those alloys with the B and Ta additions show  ﬂ ow stress anomalies beginning at temperatures of approximately 650 – 700  1 C and that the peak  ﬂ ow stress appears at 750 – 850  1 C. These results are consistent with those measured under tension in the Co – Al – W – Ta – B – Mo alloys studied here (see Fig. 6 (d)). This implies that the  ﬂ ow stress anomalies of the Co – Al – W based alloys are independent of the loading mode, whether compression or tension, and indirectly reveals that there are similar dislocation mechanisms for the  ﬂ ow stress anomalies at high temperatures. The 0.2% compressive  ﬂ ow stress of the referred Co – 8.8Al – 9.8W – 2Ta alloy at 800  1 C  [3]  is found to be nearly identical to the 0.2% tensile stress ( σ 0.2 ) in the current work. For

It was con ﬁ rmed that the tensile elongation of the polycrystal- line  γ 0 -Ni 3 Al is sensitive to the grain size and environment and that it decreases dramatically at temperatures between 700  1 C and 800  1 C for relatively large grain sizes; this results from the change of the fracture mode from transgranular at room temperature to intergranular at high temperatures  [21] . Intergranular cleavage at high temperatures is found to be common in those alloys with a  γ 0 phase or a  γ / γ 0   microstructure, such as Ir-based alloys  [22] . The dense slip lines and dimple morphology on the aligned  γ / γ 0 coherent microstructure at room temperature ( Fig. 5 ) and the intergranular cleavage failure at high temperatures ( Fig. 8 ) strongly suggest that both the temperature dependence of the tensile fracture mode and the plastic elongation of the Co – Al – W – Ta – B – Mo alloys ( Table 2 ) are consistent with those of the poly- crystalline Ni 3 Al with additions of B.  

It has been reported that under compression, the B-free Co – Al – W – based alloys have no noticeable plastic ductility at temperatures above 760  1 C. However, both the compressive anomalous  ﬂ ow stresses and the ductility were found to be signi ﬁ cantly improved (i.e., by 50% to 100% and 5% to 30%, respectively) at temperatures of 760 – 980  1 C when the alloys were doped with B  [7] . The additions of B can improve both the bulk strength and the ductility of the  γ / γ 0 coherent microstructure at room and high temperatures under compression. Under tensile conditions, however, the bulk strength can be enhanced with B additions, whereas the plastic elongation cannot. This indicates that even though the strength of the grain boundary  [18]  and the bulk coherent  γ / γ 0   microstructure  [7]  can be improved by additions of B at high temperatures, the strength of the inner grain is still above that of the grain boundary, which results in intergranular fracture occurring prior to transgranular fracture. This temperature-dependent change in the fracture mode might also be an integrated effective of the grain boundary character distribution (GBCD)  [23] , the dislocation movement in the coherent  γ / γ 0   micro- structure and those factors that relate to the grain boundary, such as grain size  [21]  and impurities in the grain boundary. Because the slip plane {0 0 1} is active in addition to the {1 1 1} planes as the temperature increases up to the anomalous  ﬂ ow stress regime [2,3] , the interaction between the dislocation and the GBCD may be complex, causing failure in the transgranular dimple mode with a higher elongation at room temperature and failure in the intergranular-cleavage mode with a lower elongation at high tem- perature. Therefore, the intergranular cleavage is believed to be the dominant fracture mode of the coherent  γ / γ 0   microstructure at high temperatures. The coated samples still exhibited low tensile elongations less than 3% at temperatures of 600  1 C and 800  1 C, and the specimens failed by intergranular cleavage fracture mode, as same as that shown in  Fig. 8 . # 5. Conclusions  

This study investigates the room- and high temperature tensile behaviours of Co – Al – W – Ta – B – Mo alloys with a coherent  γ / γ 0   microstructure, and the following conclusions are drawn.  

1) The microstructure of the as-cast 0 – 4Mo alloys consists of the γ -Co SS  matrix and the Co 3 (Ta, Me) chins, and exhibits a dendritic-like morphology. After aging, the Co 3 (Ta, Me) phase disappears, and the microstructure is composed of the  γ -Co SS matrix and the nano-scale cuboidal  γ 0 -Co 3 (Al, W) precipitates, while the  γ -Co SS  and  γ 0 -Co 3 (Al, W) phases cohere with each other; 2) Tensile yield strength anomalies are observed when the tem- perature exceeds 700  1 C. Additionally, an increase in the nominal amount of W replaced by Mo decreases the tensile yield strength at 600 – 800  1 C. 3) The dimple failure mode at room temperature and the inter- granular cleavage fracture at high temperatures are typical failure modes for polycrystalline Co – Al – W – Ta – B – Mo alloys with the coherent  γ / γ 0   microstructure. With the synergistic slip property, the  γ / γ 0   microstructure exhibits an outstanding plastic elongation of approximately 9% at room temperature. 4) Due to the change in the failure mode from the dimple mode to intergranular cleavage as the temperature increases, the  γ / γ 0 coherent microstructure shows an anomalously low plastic elongation of 0.4 – 3.3% at 600 – 800  1 C.
