# Long-term corrosion behavior of the 7A85 aluminum alloy in an industrial-marine atmospheric environment

# a b s t r a c t  

The atmospheric corrosion behavior and mechanism of the 7A85 aluminum alloy exposed to the Qingdao industrial-marine atmospheric environment for ﬁve years was investigated using the weight-loss method, mechanical property analysis, morphology observations, scanning Kelvin probe force microscopy, and corrosion product analysis. The results showed that the mechanical properties of the exposed 7A85 Al alloy deteriorated consid- erably, which was mainly induced by pitting corrosion and intergranular corrosion. Al 2- CuMg and Al 7 Cu 2 Fe intermetallic particles were the main precipitates in the 7A85 Al alloy, and the particles not only induced a defective ﬁlm but also acted as cathode phases in the precipitate e matrix galvanic corrosion couple, which led to pitting corrosion initiation during atmospheric corrosion in Qingdao. Moreover, intergranular corrosion initiated and then propagated owing to micro-galvanic corrosion along the grain boundaries.

# 1. Introduction  

7000 series (Al e Zn e Mg e Cu) aluminum alloys have been increasingly applied in the aerospace, rail transit, and ship- building industries for their high speciﬁc strength, high spe- ciﬁc stiffness, low density, and good corrosion resistance [ 1 e 3 ]. However, as the service scope continues to expand and corrosive environments are more complex, aircraft and ships are faced with severe corrosion problems. At present, corrosion data and research on Al alloys in natural environ- ments are relatively sparse, particularly in harsh industrial-  marine atmospheres with industrial pollutants [ 4 ], high Cl concentrations [ 5 , 6 ], and high humidity [ 7 ]; therefore, the corrosion of Al alloys may become an important technical bottleneck restricting development in the naval and aero- space industries. Thus, it is of great signiﬁcance to study the corrosion behavior of and accumulate data for 7000 series Al alloys in harsh industrial-marine environments, which can then guide major engineering applications [ 8 , 9 ].  

Many studies have investigated the corrosion behavior of Al alloys exposed to different atmospheres [ 10 e 12 ]. Gao et al. [ 13 ] showed that the coexistence of NaCl and SO 2  has a syn- ergistic effect on the atmospheric corrosion of the 7B04 Al alloy. When they are simultaneously present, the corrosion caused by the combined effect is greater than that caused by each single component. Zhou et al. [ 14 ] suggested that the climate and pollutant impurities in the atmosphere are the main factors affecting the atmospheric corrosion of Al alloys. The climate factors mainly include the relative humidity, temperature, rainfall, amount of sunshine, wind speed, dust falling from the atmosphere, and atmospheric corrosive pol-  lutants, mainly SO 2 , Cl , NO x , CO 2 , and solid soot particles. Sun et al. [ 15 ] performed a ﬁeld exposure test on 2024 and 7075 Al alloys in urban, coastal, and industrial environments. The results showed that the relationship between weight loss and exposure time followed a power law, and exfoliation corrosion occurred on extruded 2024 and 7075 in coastal and industrial environments, which led to a signiﬁcant reduction in me- chanical properties.  

Furthermore, the corrosion resistance of Al alloys during long-term exposure is closely associated with their intrinsic characteristics, such as oxide ﬁlms [ 16 , 17 ] and secondary phases [ 18 e 20 ]. Fratila-Apachitei et al. [ 18 ] reported that Al e Fe and Al e Fe e Si particles in Al alloys are relatively inert and are doped or enclosed in an anodic oxide. In contrast, Al 2 Cu par- ticles are preferentially oxidized, and the presence of the secondary phase can affect the anodic oxidation response (voltage or current density), promote an uneven thickness of the anodic oxidation ﬁlm, and produce ﬂawed oxides [ 19 ]. In addition, Wang et al. [ 20 ] veriﬁed that the Fe e Si e Al or Fe e Si(Mn) e Al secondary phases that form in Al alloys act as the cathode phase, and the surrounding matrix Al dissolves preferentially, thus forming a pit. However, few studies have focused on the combined inﬂuence of environmental factors and microstructure in the long-term corrosion process, including the oxidation ﬁlm destruction, rust layer charac- terization, and pitting and intergranular corrosion (IGC) behavior of Al alloys.  

In this work, the long-term corrosion behavior of a 7A85 Al alloy exposed to the harsh Qingdao industrial-marine atmo- spheric environment for ﬁve years was investigated. The 7A85 Al alloy, with the characteristics of high strength, high tough- ness, and low quenching sensitivity, was developed based on the 7085 Al alloy by China [ 21 , 22 ]. The corrosion rate was determined by weight loss, and the morphology, composition, and protectiveness of the corrosion were characterized via microscopic analysis and electrochemical measurements. In addition, the origin and mechanism of pitting and IGC were addressed. This is of great signiﬁcance for providing reference corrosion data for 7000 series Al alloys in service in marine atmospheric environments with industrial pollutants.  

# 2. Experimental  

# 2.1. Material preparation  

The experimental samples used in this research were 7A85 Al e Mg e Zn e Cu series Al alloys, and their chemical composition is listed in  Table 1 . The sample surface was ground through successive grades of SiC paper from 120 to 2000 grit, followed by polishing with 1  m m diamond paste. The surface was then examined using scanning electron micro- scopy (SEM, Quanta 250) to observe the distribution and chemical composition of the secondary phases. The crystal- lographic orientation of the Al matrix and the microstructure of the 7A85 Al alloy were characterized using electron back- scatter diffraction (EBSD). The EBSD specimens were prepared by argon ion polishing, and the EBSD measurements were conducted using an FEI Quanta 650F SEM equipped with an HKL Channel 5 EBSD detector. The accelerating voltage and step size were 0.3  m m and 20 kV, respectively. In addition, transmission electron microscopy (TEM, FEI TECNAI G20) was used to further investigate the precipitates in the 7A85 Al alloy, and the TEM specimens were prepared by slicing, grinding, and ion thinning.  

# 2.2. Field exposure test  

Sheet specimens with dimensions of 100    50    3 mm and round bar tensile specimens with dimensions of  F 5    70 mm were cut from a 7A85 Al alloy sheet. Prior to exposure, the specimens were sequentially ground with 1000 grit SiC paper and then degreased with acetone. According to the Chinese standard GB/T 11112-89, non-ferrous metal atmospheric corrosion testing method, the specimens were set up on the    atmospheric corrosion exposure frame at a 45 angle to the     horizontal and facing south in the Qingdao (36.05 N, 120.29 E) industrial-marine atmospheric environment, and the exposure duration was ﬁve years. The environmental pa- rameters and atmospheric pollutant concentrations at the Qingdao experiment station during the exposure duration are shown in  Table 2 ; they were consistent with the features in our previous study [ 23 , 24 ]. Four parallel sheet specimens were retrieved after ﬁve years of exposure, three of which were used to calculate the corrosion rate, and the other one was used for morphology observation and corrosion product analysis. In addition, ﬁve parallel tensile specimens were retrieved, and all of them were used to determine the me- chanical properties.

# 2.4. Morphology observation and corrosion product analysis  

The microscopic surface and cross-sectional corrosion mor- phologies were analyzed using SEM and energy-dispersive X- ray spectroscopy (EDS). In addition, X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was used to identify the composition of the corrosion products on the Al alloy. An ESCALAB 250Xi spec- trometer equipped with an Al K a  X-ray was used for the XPS analysis, with an accelerating voltage of 15 kV and a current intensity of 12 mA. A charge compensation (in-situ correction) was applied, and the positions were corrected with reference to the spectroscopic state of C 1s ( h n  ¼  1486.6 eV) to eliminate any perturbations (charge effects and surface states). The position of the hydrocarbon C 1s peak was assumed to be 284.6 eV and used as an internal standard to determine the binding energy of other photoelectron peaks. Curve fittings were performed using the XPS Peak software.  

An SKPFM test was performed to investigate the relative po- tential between the Al matrix and the secondary phases at RT in air. Prior to the SKPFM test, a specimen with dimensions of F 10    2 mm was ground with SiC paper to 2000 grit and then mechanically polished with 1  m m diamond paste. SKPFM measurements were conducted using a Multimode 8 atomic force microscope with PeakForce KPFM capability from Bruker Instruments Inc. The PFQNE-AL probe model was used. The tips were nanoprobe silicon tips on a silicon nitride cantilever  1   with a force constant of 0.8 N m and a resonant frequency of 300 kHz.  

# 3. Results and discussion  

# 3.1. Characterization of the 7A85 Al alloy  

An EBSD map of the 7A85 Al alloy is shown in  Fig. 1 . Based on the inverse pole ﬁgure map, the microstructure of the Al alloy mainly consisted of elongated deformed grains; part of the deformed grains underwent recrystallization and presented polygonal shapes. In addition, the size of the grains with no distinct orientation was more than 100  m m.  

The distribution and composition of the secondary phases in the 7A85 Al alloy were investigated using SEM and EDS on the mechanically polished surface, as shown in  Fig. 1 b and c. The particles were mainly intermetallic secondary phases with sizes varying between 5 and 30  m m. As shown in  Fig. 1 b, there was a large number of Al e Cu e Fe intermetallic particles and a small number of Al e Cu e Mg intermetallic particles. In addition, a high-resolution image of the P1 region is shown in Fig. 1 c, and the atomic ratios of the elements in intermetallic particles A and B are shown in  Table 3 . As shown in  Table 3 , the particles were indexed as Al 2 CuMg and Al 7 Cu 2 Fe. There- fore, the majority of the secondary phases in the 7A85 Al alloy were Al 2 CuMg and Al 7 Cu 2 Fe. It is widely accepted that the morphology and thickness of the oxide ﬁlms formed on Al alloys are greatly affected by the presence of intermetallic particles [ 19 , 25 ], and oxide ﬁlms composed of granular nano- scale oxides/hydroxides formed on the secondary phase and Al matrix. Moreover, the granular oxides/hydroxides on the Al matrix were compact and tiny, while the barrier layer on the secondary phase was relatively loose and ﬂawed, which, in the industrial-marine environment, allowed the penetration of corrosive ions and led to oxide ﬁlm thinning and break- down at the ﬂawed site [ 17 ]. Thus, the oxide ﬁlm on the Cu- rich secondary phases was much weaker than that on the

To further study the inﬂuence and mechanism of the sec- ondary phases on the corrosion resistance of the matrix, SKPFM was used to measure the surface potential of the sec- ondary phase.  Fig. 2 a and b shows the surface potential maps and line-proﬁle potentials of the different phases. As shown in Fig. 2 , it is clear that the potential of Al 2 CuMg was 200 mV higher than that of the Al matrix, and the potential of the Al 7 Cu 2 Fe phase was more than 400 mV higher than that of the Al matrix, which indicates that both secondary phases acted as cathodes in the galvanic cell comprising the intermetallic precipitates and Al matrix. The results illustrate that pitting corrosion initiated from the matrix around the Al 2 CuMg and Al 7 Cu 2 Fe particles, and the matrix around the secondary phases was prone to dissolution. As corrosion developed, the surrounding matrix dissolved completely, and the secondary phases fell off, which led to the formation of corrosion pits. Therefore, the presence of secondary phases, such as Al 2 CuMg and Al 7 Cu 2 Fe, was the origin of the pitting corrosion, which signiﬁcantly decreased the corrosion resistance of the 7A85 Al alloy [ 27 , 28 ].  

The TEM image of the ﬁne precipitates along the grain boundaries is shown in  Fig. 3 , and EDS was used to determine the composition of these grain boundary precipitates (GBPs). The precipitates were composed of Al, Mg, Zn, and Cu, and the precipitate-free zones (PFZs) on either side of the grain boundary (30 e 60 nm wide) were expected to be the Zn, Mg, and Cu depletion zones [ 29 ].  

# 3.2. Analysis of the mechanical properties and corrosion behavior  

The mechanical properties of the 7A85 Al alloy before and after ﬁve years of exposure are listed in  Table 4 . As shown in Table 4 , after outdoor exposure, the mechanical properties decreased dramatically. The yield strength and tensile strength decreased by 24.5% and 21.1%, respectively, and the elongation loss was 79.2%, which indicates that the mechan- ical properties were greatly inﬂuenced by industrial-marine atmospheric corrosion.  

Previous studies have illustrated that the deterioration of the mechanical properties of Al alloys induced by atmospheric corrosion is closely associated with the rust layer character- istics, pitting corrosion, and IGC depth [ 23 ]. It is well acknowledged that the corrosion rate by weight loss suggests that corrosion is uniformly distributed over the surface [ 30 , 31 ]. In this study, the large gap between the weight loss and degradation of the mechanical properties indicate that the weight-loss measurement was not appropriate to explain the degradation of the mechanical properties, and penetrative corrosion, such as pitting and IGC, played a more important role. Therefore, pitting corrosion and IGC were analyzed further.

To further analyze the localized corrosion behavior of the exposed 7A85 Al alloy, cross-sectional morphologies from different regions of the exposed sample were analyzed after ﬁve years of exposure in Qingdao, as shown in  Fig. 5 . In this ﬁgure, a rust layer with the thickness of up to 136.81  m m covered the 7A85 matrix, and numerous cracks were seen in the rust layer, particularly at the interface between the rust layer and the matrix, indicating poor cohesion between them. Then, crack propagation along the corroded grain boundaries occurred more easily [ 33 ], which signiﬁcantly reduced the strength and elongation of the exposed specimens.  

# 3.3. Corrosion mechanism of the 7A85 Al alloy in industrial-marine atmospheric environments  

To investigate the corrosion mechanism of the exposed 7A85 Al alloy, the elemental distributions of S, O, and Cl via EDS analysis are shown in  Fig. 5 . Both O and S were enriched not only in the rust layer but also in the corrosion pits and IGC region, while Cl showed no aggregation in any of the areas. Therefore, the Cl in the industrial-marine atmosphere was mainly associated with the destruction of the oxide ﬁlm and the initiation of pitting corrosion. However, S might have directly participated in the chemical reactions and formed S- containing corrosion products.  

For the rust layer on the surface of the sample, XPS analysis was used to verify the compositions of the corrosion products. Fig. 6 a shows the XPS survey spectrum, and the peaks for Al 2p, O 1s, S 2p, and Zn 2p are distinct. In addition, high- resolution images of Al 2p 3/2, Zn 2p 3/2, and O 1s are shown in  Fig. 6 b-d, respectively. The Al 2p 3/2 spectrum can be deconvolved into three peaks: Al(OH) 3  (74.20 eV), AlO(OH) (74.60 eV), and Al 2 (SO 4 ) 3  (74.90 eV), and the Zn 2p 3/2 spectrum can be divided into Zn(OH) 2  (1021.80 eV), ZnO (1022.40 eV), and ZnSO 4  (1023.00 eV). Speciﬁcally, the relative amounts of Al(OH) 3 , AlO(OH), and Al 2 (SO 4 ) 3  in the surface corrosion products were 30.6%, 28.2%, and 41.2%, respectively, and the relative amounts of Zn(OH) 2 , ZnO, and ZnSO 4  were 42.2%, 26.5%, and 31.3%, respectively. The XPS results imply that S- containing pollutants in the environment were involved in the formation of the surface rust layer.

The OH in Al(OH) 3  was replaced step by step through a series of chemical reactions, and ﬁnally stable AlCl 3  was formed [ 25 , 35 ], thereby gradually destroying the barrier effect of the oxide ﬁlm. Once the oxidized ﬁlms on the surface were destroyed, the sec- ondary phases and the matrix were exposed to the corrosive media. Combined with  Fig. 2 , the surface potentials of the intermetallic particles (such as Al 2 CuMg and Al 7 Cu 2 Fe) were much higher than those of the Al matrix, and the Al 2 CuMg and Al 7 Cu 2 Fe particles acted as the cathodic phases in the atmo- sphere, which led to the preferential dissolution of the Al matrix around the intermetallic particles. As corrosion pro- gressed, the matrix around the secondary-phase particles completely dissolved, thus causing the secondary-phase par- ticles to fall off and form corrosion pits. As the pits grew larger, the generated corrosion products increased, and the accu- mulation of corrosion products led to stress cracking.  

In addition, the XPS and EDS mapping results imply that S- containing pollutants in the environment were involved in corrosion propagation. When pitting occurred at the grain boundaries, IGC was initiated. It is well acknowledged that the IGC of Al alloys is related to elemental segregation at the grain boundaries [ 36 ]. As shown in  Fig. 3 , noticeable Al e Cu e Mg e Zn precipitates were observed along the grain boundaries, and near both sides of the boundaries were Mg, Zn, and Cu depletion associated with these zones. The chemical composition of the PFZs (30 e 60 nm wide) was similar to pure Al, and they possessed the lowest potential compared with the GBPs and 7A85 Al alloy matrix prior to corrosion in the micro-galvanic couples [ 37 , 38 ]; then, IGC pathways were formed. With increased exposure time, IGC extended to the matrix, and the pathways were more distinct, indicating more severe IGC [ 39 ]. In addi- tion,  Fig. 5  shows that S enrichment occurred in the IGC area, which veriﬁed that the sulﬁde pollutants in the atmospheric environment greatly inﬂuenced the IGC of the Al alloy. Because sulﬁde dissolved in the TEL to form sulfate ions, the sulfate ions penetrated deep into the grain boundary area with the corrosive media, which provided advantageous corrosive media for galvanic corrosion along the grain boundary, thus aggravating the propagation of IGC [ 40 ].  

# 4. Conclusion  

(1) The mechanical properties of a 7A85 Al alloy decreased considerably after ﬁve years of exposure in an industrial-marine atmospheric environment; the yield strength and elongation decreased by 24.5% and 79.2%, respectively, while the weight-loss ratio was only 1.13%, which indicates that the weight-loss measurement was not appropriate for explaining the degradation of the mechanical properties, and the pitting and intergran- ular corrosion (IGC) inside the Al alloy played a decisive role.  

(2) Al 2 CuMg and Al 7 Cu 2 Fe intermetallic particles were the main precipitates in the 7A85 Al alloy, and the particles not only induced a defective ﬁlm, but also acted as cath- ode phases in the precipitate e matrix galvanic corrosion couple, which led to the initiation of pitting corrosion during industrial-marine atmospheric corrosion. (3) IGC initiated and then propagated owing to the micro- galvanic corrosion among the grain boundary pre- cipitates, precipitate-free zones and matrix. In addition, the sulﬁdes in the atmosphere converted into sulﬁte ions and penetrated deep into the grain boundary re- gion, which accelerated pitting and IGC.
