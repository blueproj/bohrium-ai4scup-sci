Ge Song,   Zonglin Yi,   Fangyuan Su, \*  Lijing Xie, and Chengmeng Chen \*

ABSTRACT:  The high impedance of the solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) is one of the important factors that deteriorate the charge behavior of lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) at low temperatures, which hinders their practical application in portable electronic products and electric vehicles under extreme conditions. Based on this consideration, a LiF-rich SEI  ﬁ lm with low impedance, using lithium di ﬂ uorobis(oxalato)phosphate (LiDFBOP) as an electrolyte additive and a blank electrolyte without commercial additives, is constructed on a graphite surface. The decomposition mechanism of LiDFBOP is further deduced by density functional theory calculations. This additive inhibits the decomposition of the electrolyte and then forms a thin SEI  ﬁ lm with more LiF. LiF, possessing high Young ’ s modulus, makes the SEI  ﬁ lm dense and stable. At the same time, more LiF/Li 2 CO 3  interfaces are formed to increase the ionic conductivity. # 1. INTRODUCTION  

Lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) have been widely used due to their high energy density, long cycle life, and zero memory e ﬀ ect. However, LIBs su ﬀ er from severe energy and power density losses when the temperature falls below 0  ° C, which limit their applications in portable electronic products and electric vehicles in the polar and subpolar zones. The essential limitations at low temperatures can be summarized into four 1 − 3   aspects: (a) high impedance of the solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) and the cathode electrolyte interface (CEI), (b) increased viscosity and poor Li +   conductivity of the electrolyte solvent, (c) the low solid di ﬀ usion of Li +   in the graphite anode and cathodes such as LiCoO 2 , and (d) Li + plating on the anode surface at a high rate (greater than C/5). It is generally acknowledged that the limitation of LIB charging at low temperatures is attributable to the graphite anode rather than cathodes such as LiCoO 2  and LiFePO 4 .   Moreover, one of the performance inhibitors is fundamentally tied to the electrolyte − anode interphase 5   because of the slow charge- transfer process at the SEI and the sluggish Li +   di ﬀ usion through the SEI at subzero temperature.

Regarding the subzero temperature condition, the SEI should be thin, robust, and should have low ionic resistivity but high electronic resistivity. This not only ensures the battery life but also improves the charge-transfer kinetics during the electrode. The additive lithium di ﬂ uorobis(oxalato)phosphate (LiDFBOP) has been gradually studied in recent years, which ascertained that LiDFBOP would be reduced prior to the  ﬁ , − 15   carbonate-based solvents to form a SEI/CEI lm. Zhao et al.  reported that the addition of LiDFBOP additive to the electrolyte could increase the content of organic ingredients and reduce the inorganic substance, thereby reducing the impedance of the SEI  ﬁ lm derived by LiDFBOP and increasing the capacity retention of MCMB/Li half cells at room temperature. In particular, the inorganic content of LiF goes up and Li 2 CO 3  goes down, which are not in accordance with other reports.   For instance, LiDFBOP was also used in LiCoO 2 /Li cells. However, the presence of LiF is not detected in the CEI  ﬁ lm, which leads to the decrease of CEI  ﬁ lm impedance.   Liao  et al.  explored the in ﬂ uence of LiDFBOP as an electrolyte additive on the electrochemical performance of the LiNi 0.5 Co 0.2 Mn 0.3 O 2 /graphite cell at low temperature. The introduction of Li +   from LiDFBOP results in surface  ﬁ lms with high ionic conductivity, which improves the low-temperature performance.   Apparently, the e ﬀ ects of LiDFBOP on the SEI are still debatable, and how LiDFBOP a ﬀ ects the ionic conductivity and low-temperature performance needs to be further studied. Researchers then put forward di ﬀ erent views on the mechanism of subsequent decomposition of LiDF- ,   BOP, where there is no consensus.  

In this work, we investigate the e ﬀ ect of the components and the structure of the SEI  ﬁ lm on the electrochemical performances of LIBs at low temperatures. In order to do so, a thin SEI  ﬁ lm with high structural stability and ionic conductivity on the graphite − electrolyte interphase is constructed using LiDFBOP as an electrolyte additive. According to transmission electron microscopy (TEM), Fourier-transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy,  ex situ  X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), and the corresponding etching technology, the structure and the composition of the SEI in electrolyte systems that contain di ﬀ erent LiDFBOP additive contents are analyzed. Combined with density functional theory (DFT) calculations, a possible decomposi- tion mechanism of LiDFBOP is proposed. This work presents an e ﬀ ective strategy for developing a functional electrolyte to meet the requirement of LIBs with enhanced low-temperature performance and provide a theoretical insight for the structural design of the additive.  

# 2. EXPERIMENTAL DETAILS  

2.1. Electrolytes and Electrodes.  LiDFBOP was purchased from CHEMFISH CO., LTD in China. The prepared LiDFBOP was added to the baseline electrolyte (BE) of 1.0 M LiPF 6  in EC (ethylene carbonate)/DMC (dimethyl carbonate)/diethyl carbonate (1:1:1 by volume) at concentrations of 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, and 2.0 wt % to the whole electrolyte; these samples are simply referred to as BE0.5, BE1.0, BE1.5, and BE2.0 in this work, respectively.  

Graphite was obtained from Jiangxi Zichen Technology Co., Ltd. The graphite electrode was prepared by mixing graphite powder, acetylene black conductive, styrene-butadiene-rubber (SBR) adhesive, and sodium-carboxyl-methylcellulose (CMC) thixotropic agent in the weight ratio of 85:8:2:5 on a Cu current collector. Then, the electrode was dried at 85  ° C for 12 h in a vacuum oven. The graphite electrodes were punched into 10 mm diameter disks, which made the average loading of graphite on the Cu current collector to be about 4.5 − 5 mg cm − 2 .  

LiNi 0.6 Mn 0.2 Co 0.2 O 2  was obtained from BTR (Jiang Su) New Material Technology Ltd. The LiNi 0.6 Mn 0.2 Co 0.2 O 2  electrode was prepared by mixing LiNi 0.6 Mn 0.2 Co 0.2 O 2  powder, acetylene black, and polyvinylidene  ﬂ uoride (PVDF) binder in the weight ratio of 85:8:7 on an Al current collector. Then, the electrode was dried at 85  ° C for 12 h in a vacuum oven. The LiNi 0.6 Mn 0.2 Co 0.2 O 2  electrodes were punched into disks with a diameter of 12 mm.  

Each CR2032 coin-type graphite/Li half cell was fabricated using a prepared composite electrode disk, a lithium foil disk, and a sheet of polypropylene separator (Celgard) with the prepared electrolyte. For each cell, 50  μ L of electrolyte was used. The open circuit voltage of the assembled coin type half cells was initially approximately 2.5 − 3 V. The coin type full cells were fabricated using graphite as the anode and LiNi 0.6 Mn 0.2 Co 0.2 O 2  as the cathode (N/P = 1.05). 60  μ L of electrolyte was used in each full cell.  

2.2. Electrochemical Measurements.  For graphite/Li half cells, cyclic voltammetry (CV) tests were performed from 0.005 to 3 V with a scan rate of 0.1 mV s − 1 . Before the rate performance and cycle performance tests, the graphite/Li cells were  ﬁ rst charged/discharged at 0.1 C (1 C = 372 mA g − 1 ) in the potential range of 0.005 − 2.0 V. These activation processes of the initial three cycles were accomplished at room temperature with a LAND system

 (CT2001A, Wuhan, China). Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy

 (EIS) of the graphite/Li cell was performed using an electrochemical workstation (Autolab, Metrohm, the Netherlands) in a frequency range from 100 kHz to 0.01 Hz using an AC signal of 5 mV.  

For LiNi 0.6 Mn 0.2 Co 0.2 O 2 /graphite full cells, the rate performance and cycle performance were tested after the initial three cycles at room temperature, with a program of a charging step to 4.2 V until the current decreased to 0.025 C and then discharging to 2.75 V (1 C = 170 mA g − 1 ).  

2.3. Material Characterization.  To analyze the elemental composition and microstructure of the surface of the graphite after cycling, all the cycled cathodes were rinsed with DMC solvent three times and then left overnight in a glovebox. The surface compositions of the graphite cathode were analyzed by XPS (AXIS Ultra DLD, Thermo Fisher Scienti ﬁ c, UK) under extreme vacuum with monochromatic Al K α  radiation ( h ν  = 1486.6 eV). The micro- structure of the SEI  ﬁ lm on the surface of the graphite was performed using a transmission electron microscope. The sample was placed in acetone and sonicated for 5 min to obtain a uniform dispersion. Then, two drops of the solution were placed on a standard copper net.

3.1. Formation and Structural Characterization of the SEI.  The SEI  ﬁ lm forms during the initial discharge of LIBs and stabilizes after subsequent several cycles.  Figure 1 a shows the  ﬁ rst CV curves of the graphite/Li half cells in di ﬀ erent electrolytes. The CV curve of the BE shows a peak around 0.7 V, which is related to EC, one of the electrolyte components, undergoing a reduction reaction to form the SEI  ﬁ lm. The peaks between 1.9 and 2 V appear in electrolytes with di ﬀ erent contents (0.5, 1.0, 1.5, and 2.0 wt %) of LiDFBOP, which is because LiDFBOP will be reduced around 1.9 V (vs Li + /Li) preferentially compared with that of EC. What is more, the intensity of the reduction peaks increases as the content of LiDFBOP grows, indicating that more LiDFBOP participates in the reaction to form the SEI  ﬁ lm. According to the  ﬁ rst charge − discharge curves in  Figure 1 b, the Coulombic e ﬃ ciency (CE) is calculated, as shown in  Table S1 . The CE of LIBs in the BE is only 80.8% due to the participation of more LiPF 6  and electrolyte solvents in the formation of the SEI ﬁ lm. When LiDFBOP was added, the CE signi ﬁ cantly improved, possibly because LiDFBOP reduced to form the SEI  ﬁ lm which then prevented the further decomposition of LiPF  and solvents.  

In order to understand the microstructure of the SEI, the electrodes after three cycles of activation at 0.1 C (1 C = 372 mA g − 1 ) are observed by TEM. Combined with the phenomena mentioned earlier, we speculate that LiDFBOP would sacri ﬁ cially decompose to form more LiF and then prevent the reaction of electrons with LiPF 6 , resulting in the decrease of  − Li x PF y  (the main decomposition product of ,   LiPF 6 ). The situation gets complicated as the additive content increases from 0.5 to 2 wt %. The C − C/C − H peak in C 1s spectra gradually decreases after reaching the maximum value in the electrolyte BE1, indicating that more LiDFBOP can promote the formation of a thicker SEI, which is consistent with the results observed by TEM.   At the same time, the content of LiF increases with the increase of LiDFBOP, further verifying that LiF comes from the decomposition of the additive LiDFBOP. Generally, LiF plays a positive role in improving the properties of the SEI layers because of the high Young ’ s modulus ( ≈ 64.9 GPa) and low electronic conductivity (10 − 31 S cm − 1 ).   However, LiF shows poor ionic conductivity compared to porous organic substances; the e ﬀ ect on the electrochemical performance of excessive LiF will be discussed later. In addition, the  − Li x PO y F z  content increased and subsequently decreased as the LiDFBOP content was increased from 0.5 to 2 wt %, as observed in Figure 2 b − d, showing the highest value when LiDFBOP is 1%. It is interesting to note that the variation pattern of  − Li x PF y  is exactly opposite to that of  − Li x PO y F z : when the content of LiDFBOP is 1% in BE, the content of  − Li x PF y  in the SEI is the least. The increase of  − Li x PF y  and the decrease of  − Li x PO y F z from BE1.0 to BE2.0 seem to be confusing. As we mentioned earlier, LiF derived from LiDFBOP can block the contact between the electron and the lithium salt LiPF 6 , which will reduce the decomposition of LiPF 6  to form  − Li x PF y . The increase of  − Li x PF y  here will not be due to the decomposition product of LiPF 6 . Combining the opposite trends of  − Li x PF y and  − Li x PO y F z , we speculate that the above phenomenon may be caused by the breaking of the P − O bond in  − Li x PO y F z . To further verify the decomposed mechanism and products of LiDFBOP, DFT calculation will be used.

Two possible initial structures were constructed since both F- and O-terminals can be the possible binding sites for the Li atom in  Figure S3 .  − 110.25 eV, while that of Li binding with the F-terminal is

 − 109.15 eV. Therefore, LiDFBOP with the Li atom attached with terminal O atoms is considered as the stable state for further analysis. Then, DFT calculations were applied for establishing the reaction network step by step as shown in Figure 3 . The binding energy of each chemical bond in the molecule is listed in  Table S5 , which is the key factor for determining the reaction network, since the binding energy represents the stability of a certain chemical bond. First, the P − O bond (Li side) is found to achieve the lowest binding energy of 2.34 eV and thus is considered as the weakest chemical bond in the molecule and would  ﬁ rst break, as inferred by XPS. Therefore, with two Li side P − O bonds being broken, the molecule accepts one electron and splits into a − LiC 2 O 4  radical and a DFOP (di ﬂ uoro(oxalate)phosphate, C 2 O 4 PF 2 ) molecule.  − LiC 2 O 4  could accept a Li +   in the electrolyte and form Li 2 C 2 O 4 , whose existence has been veri ﬁ ed by previous research studies.   The second step of LiDFBOP decomposition is the further decomposition of DFOP. After another optimization of DFOP, the P − F chemical bond has the lowest binding energy of 2.87 eV. Therefore, it is reasonable to conclude that the second step of the reaction occurs when the DFOP molecule accepts one − electron once more and is followed by the detachment of F − from DFOP. The detached F is capable of forming LiF with Li +   in the electrolyte, which is the main component of the SEI ﬁ lm. What is left for DFOP is a FOP ( ﬂ uor(oxalate)phosphate, C 2 O 4 FP) radical without one F atom. It is inferred that the ﬁ nal step of the reaction could be multiple FOP molecules reacting with each other, followed by the possible formation of organic polymers. Interestingly, the variation pattern of R SEI  and  R ct  is consistent with the results of rate performance, showing that the SEI can be rationally designed and controlled by using LiDFBOP to a ﬀ ect the electrochemical performance. As shown in  Figure 4 c,e,  R SEI  and  R ct  also greatly improved after 200 cycles. Similarly, the total impedance of cells in the BE grows signi ﬁ cantly, while those of the electrolyte with LiDFBOP (such as BE1.0, BE1.5, and BE2.0) increases less signi ﬁ cantly, con ﬁ rming the stabilizing e ﬀ ect of LiDFBOP on the SEI  ﬁ lm. Finally, the diagonal of the low frequency region mainly represents the di ﬀ use Warburg impedance ( W ). In this study, only the electrolyte is replaced, thus  W  is similar, so it will not be elaborated here.  

3.4. Mechanisms of LiDFBOP A ﬀ ecting the Electro- chemical Performance.  To further understand the in ﬂ uence of LiDFBOP on the electrochemical performance, the BE1 electrolyte with an optimal performance and the BE electrolyte without LiDFBOP are selected for EIS at di ﬀ erent voltages, as shown on the left side of  Figure 5 a,b. The results of  ﬁ tting analysis are shown on the right. The  R ct  and  R s  of LIBs in the electrolyte BE are higher than those in BE1, especially below 0.2 V. As we all know, the energy storage of graphite in LIBs mainly relies on the lithium intercalation/deintercalation below 0.2 V where there is a long platform.   Therefore, the SEI  ﬁ lm formed by additives reduces the  R SEI  and  R ct  during lithium intercalation/deintercalation, which e ﬀ ectively im- proves the electrochemical performance.  

XPS spectra of the SEI in the electrolytes BE and BE1 were recorded at di ﬀ erent depths and re shown in  Figure 6 a,b, and their corresponding areas below the peaks are summarized in Figure 6 c and  Tables S6 − S9 . As the sputtering time increases, C − O, C  O, and  − Li x PO y Z z  show a decreasing trend, while LiF, Li 2 CO 3 , and  − Li x PF y  show an increasing trend. This means the SEI  ﬁ lm holds the multilayer structure.   The inner layer is mainly composed of inorganic substances such as LiF, Li 2 CO 3 , and  − Li x PF y , while the outer layer is made of organic polymeric species, such as ROCO 2 Li and Li n (FOP) n . After sputtering for 18 s, the contents of inorganic components LiF and Li 2 CO 3  remain unchanged basically in BE, and a large amount of organic contents still exist as the inner layer of the SEI is not dense enough ( Figure 6 a). Such a loose inner layer containing more Li 2 CO 3  and organic components cannot e ﬀ ectively inhibit the transport of electrons and the electrolyte, resulting in the continuous decomposition of the electrolyte and LiPF 6 . Thus, the formed SEI  ﬁ lm in BE is uneven in thickness, as shown in  Figure 1 d. By contrast, the organic contents in BE1 are low after sputtering for 18 s, indicating that the inner SEI layer is continuous, compact, and impermeable to the electrolyte solution and LiPF 6  ( Figure 6 b). The LiF-rich inner layer is bene ﬁ cial for stabilizing graphite.   Meanwhile, the combination of appropriate content of LiF and Li 2 CO 3  reduces the battery resistance and facilitates the lithium ion transfer. The outer layer contains more organic components Li x PF y O z , which further promote the transport of lithium ions.   To make it easier to understand, the above- mentioned mechanism is shown in  Figure 6 d. In the BE electrolyte without the additive LiDFBOP, EC will be reduced ﬁ rst, which will generate a lot of ROCO 2 Li, leading to a decreasing  ﬁ rst cycle CE. What is more, the decomposition reaction of LiPF 6  also occurs during this process. The formed SEI in the electrolyte BE will not be dense and contain a small content of LiF and more Li 2 CO 3 , further causing the decrease in CE and reversible capacity. When LiDFBOP was added to BE, the additive will be reduced  ﬁ rst, thereby inhibiting the decomposition of the electrolyte and the lithium salt. Thus, the

According to previous literature reports, due to the lower glass-transition temperature ( T g ), higher ionic conductivity, and better absorption of electrolyte, PVDF which helps reduce the impedance and accelerate the lithium ion di ﬀ usion might be more suitable for low-temperature applications.   There- fore, these discharge capacities of the graphite electrode are based on PVDF binder. It can be found that the insertion of lithium ions at 25  ° C is divided into four stages: 30   (a) C − LiC 36 ; (b) LiC 36 − LiC 27 ; (c) LiC 27 − LiC 12 ; and (d) LiC 12 − LiC 6 . The decreasing temperature hinders the process of insertion of lithium ions into graphite, thereby reducing the speci ﬁ c capacity greatly. At 0  ° C, all four stages of lithium conversion can be completed, but not all lithium can be successfully embedded in graphite, resulting in a loss of energy compared to that at room temperature. As the temperature goes down to  − 10  ° C, the LiBs with BE only contain two stages, and the conversion of LiC 27  to LiC 12  and LiC 12  to LiC 6 seem to be very di ﬃ cult. However, LiDFBOP makes things better. The electrolytes with LiDFBOP (such as BE0.5, BE1, BE1.5, and BE2) can help more lithium ions to be inserted into graphite and promote the conversion of LiC 27  to LiC 6 , thus a higher speci ﬁ c capacity can be obtained. Among them, BE1 shows the highest speci ﬁ c capacity of 80 mAh g − 1 , which is nearly twice the value of the BE at  − 20  ° C, indicating that LiDFBOP is a good choice to improve the low-temperature performance by forming the thinner SEI  ﬁ lm with higher Li + conductivity. Regrettably, though low-temperature perform- ance with the additive LiDFBOP is superior to, or at least similar to, that of other reported LIBs, ,   the speci ﬁ c capacity below 0  ° C is unsatisfactory, which is because the electrolyte and electrode materials are not optimized. EIS is used to analyze the impedance of LIBs at di ﬀ erent temperatures, as shown in  Figure 7 e − h. It can be seen that both  R SEI  and  R ct improved greatly as the temperature decreases, suggesting that the decrease of electrochemical performance is because of the great increased  R SEI  and  R ct  at low temperatures. As we mentioned earlier,  R SEI  represents the impedance of the SEI ﬁ lm.  R SEI  of the SEI  ﬁ lm derived by LiDFBOP is much lower than that by EC, especially at  − 20  ° C, which is one of the reasons why electrolytes with LiDFBOP show a higher speci ﬁ c capacity.  R ct  refers to the charge-transfer resistance and the desolvation process, where the electrode materials are the same, indicating that the SEI  ﬁ lm formed by the additive is more conducive to the desolvation process of lithium ions.  

The cyclic stability of the  ﬁ ve electrolytes is also tested with the current density of 0.2 C at  − 20  ° C in  Figure 8 a. Compared with BE, the addition of LiDFBOP can not only improve the capacity but also improve the cycle stability. There are two reasons for this. First, the SEI  ﬁ lm formed by LiDFBOP contains LiF and Li 2 C 2 O 4 , which improves the stability of the SEI  ﬁ lm. Second, the SEI  ﬁ lm with LiF and  − Li x PO y Z z  shows higher ionic conductivity, which inhibits Li +   plating at low temperature and reduces the generation of lithium dendrites. In order to further evaluate the practical application of LiDFBOP, full cells with/without LiDFBOP were fabricated using graphite as the anode and LiNi 0.6 Mn 0.2 Co 0.2 O 2  as the cathode (N/P = 1.05). Upon cycling at 0.2 C and  − 20  ° C, as shown in  Figure 8 b, full cells with 1.0 wt % LiDFBOP display a reversible capacity of 85.6 mA h g − 1   after 125 cycles, which is 87% of its initial capacity (98.3 mA h g − 1 ). In contrast, the speci ﬁ c capacity of the electrolyte without LiDFBOP dropped signi ﬁ cantly after 100 cycles, showing an unstable state. The rate performance of full cells is illustrated in  Figure 8 c. BE1.0 has always shown superior performance to that of the BE. Full cells with LiDFBOP can deliver reversible capacities of 107, 95, 72, and 41 mA h g − 1   at the rates of 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, and 1 C, respectively. When the current rate was reset to 0.2 C, the initial capacity (60 mA h g − 1 ) was almost fully restored, indicating the superior lithiation/delithiation kinetics and reversibility at  − 20  ° C. Regrettably, at the rate of 2 C, only a discharge capacity of 21 mA h g − 1   can be achieved, which may be limited by the solvent system of the electrolyte. Many factors might a ﬀ ect the battery performance at low temper- atures, such as the ionic conductivity of electrolytes and the interfacial impedance of the electrodes. Although the application of LiDFBOP can increase the ionic conductivity of the SEI, the modi ﬁ cation of the electrolyte is also particularly important, which we will discuss further discuss in our next works.  

# 4. CONCLUSIONS  

To investigate the e ﬀ ect of the SEI  ﬁ lm (component and structure) on the low-temperature performance of LIBs, the SEI  ﬁ lm formed in electrolytes with di ﬀ erent LiDFBOP contents is studied systematically. The results show that LiDFBOP can be reduced prior to the addition of the electrolyte, and the formed SEI with a multilayer structure contains more LiF, Li 2 C 2 O 4 , and  − Li x PO y F z . The presence of LiF and Li 2 C 2 O 4  ensures the stability of the SEI  ﬁ lm, thereby improving the cycle stability of LIBs. LiF and  − Li x PO y F z ensure the fast ion conductivity of the SEI  ﬁ lm. Therefore, this SEI enables the graphite anode to couple with higher energy density, excellent cycling stability, rate capability, and low- temperature performance. Finally, the decomposition mecha- nism of LiDFBOP is veri ﬁ ed by means of DFT calculations, identifying the key products that improve the electrochemical performance. The results of this study will further guide the design of electrolyte additives that can remove undesirable side reactions and contribute to the construction of a controlled and stable electrolyte − electrode interphase.
