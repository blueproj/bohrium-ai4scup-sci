# 1.   Introduction  

The market demand of micro-parts has become more and more extensive due to product  miniaturization, such as micro-cups in the field of microelectronics, the chip lead frames, micro- t / Journal Pre-proof  

high reliability, and special medium corrosion. However, traditional materials such as pure  copper and pure nickel cannot meet their comprehensive properties. Nickel-based superalloys  have been recently applied in the processing field at the mesoscopic scale and become the  critical materials of micro-parts due to high-temperature strength, outstanding oxidation and  

corrosion resistance, decent fatigue property [11-16]. Inconel 718 is one of the most widely  applied nickel-based superalloys in modern aircraft engines, gas turbine disks, nuclear reactors  and power generators. The primary precipitate that contributes to high-temperature strength is  the coherent nano-scale disc-shaped γ″ phase with the chemical composition of Ni 3 Nb. The  assistant strengthening phase is coherent nano-scale cubic or spherical γ′ phase with the chemical  composition of Ni 3 (Al, Ti) [17-21]. The mechanical and thermal properties are strongly  associated with the microstructure, especially the grain size and various second phase particles  [22-24].

# 2.   Experimental  

Inconel 718 used in present study is annealed cold-rolled sheets with the thickness of  200 µm. The chemical composition (mass fraction, %) of Inconel 718 is 52.80Ni-18.73Cr- 5.24Nb-3.02Mo-0.52Al-0.95Ti-0.03C-0.03Co-Fe rest. The dog-bone tensile samples were  machined to 12 mm gauge length and 5 mm gauge width. The size schematic diagram and  machining process of the dog-bone tensile sample can refer to the previous research [12, 16]. A  vacuum heat treatment method was adopted to prevent specimen oxidation. The quartz tubes  Journal Pre-proof performed four times at least to establish the reproducibility.  

The samples for OM observation were prepared using mechanical grinding and polishing  methods with 200 - 1200 grits metallographic sandpapers and 0.5 – 2.5 μm grits diamond  suspensions. Then the samples were etched chemically in a mixed solution containing 5 g  

CuCl 2 +100 ml HCl+100 ml C 2 H 5 OH [31]. The metallographic structures were characterized by  optical microscope (DSX 510, OLYMPUS, Japan). The fine microstructural details of  precipitates were observed by TEM (Tecnai G 2  F30, FEI, USA). The slices for TEM observation  were progressively mechanical ground to the thickness of approximately 50 μm by using a 4000  grit metallographic sandpaper. The discs with diameters of 3 mm were mechanically punched  from the thinned slices. These discs then were electrolytic thinned in a mixed solution containing  10 ml HClO 4  + 190 ml C 2 H 5 OH using a twin jet polisher., cooled to −25 °C, at 25 V [12].

3.2. Mechanical properties  

The engineering stress - engineering strain curves are demonstrated in Fig. 9. The flow  stress decreases with the increase of the grain size due to the reduction of grain boundary  strengthening. Additionally, the flow stress increases with the aging time due to the precipitation  strengthening of γ′ and γ″ phases. Due to the presence of grain boundaries, precipitates, etc., the  microstructures of the alloy change during the deformation process, causing nucleation, growth  and aggregation of the micro defects, resulting in changes in the mechanical properties of the  alloy.
