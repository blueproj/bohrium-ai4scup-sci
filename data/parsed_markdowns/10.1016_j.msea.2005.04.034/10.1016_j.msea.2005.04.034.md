# On the role of liquated   ′   precipitates in weld heat affected zone microﬁssuring of a nickel-based superalloy  

O.A. Ojo, M.C. Chaturvedi   ∗  

Department of Mechanical and Manufacturing Engineering, University of Manitoba, Winnipeg, Man., Canada R3T 5V6  

# Abstract  

The role of   ′   precipitate particles, which is the principal strengthening phase of most of the precipitation hardened nickel base superalloys, on weld heat affected zone (HAZ) cracking was investigated. The HAZ microstructures around welds in a commercial nickel-based superalloy IN 738LC were simulated using Gleeble thermo mechanical simulation system. Microstructural examination of the simulated HAZs and those present in actual tungsten inert gas (TIG) welded specimens showed that   ′   particles persisted during heating to the welding simulation temperatures, where they reacted with the surrounding    matrix producing liquid ﬁlm by a eutectic-type reaction, which subsequently inﬁltrated the grain boundary regions. The on-cooling ductility of the alloy was signiﬁcantly reduced as shown by the comparatively low ductility recovery temperature. Correlation of simulated HAZ microstructures with hot ductility properties of the alloy revealed that HAZ cracking resistance was damaged by liquation reaction involving the   ′   precipitate particles. The results support previously reported observations which indicated that   ′   precipitate particles could contribute to HAZ microﬁssuring in superalloys through liquation reaction besides the generally reported rapid solid-state re-precipitation of   ′   phase.

# 1. Introduction  

The continued drive towards increasing the overall efﬁ- ciency of gas turbines in modern aircraft engines and power generation systems by the higher operating temperatures has lead to a signiﬁcant progress in the development of nickel-based superalloys containing increasing amounts of volume fraction of the main strengthening phase, ordered L1 2 intermetallic Ni 3 (Al, Ti or Ta)-  ′ . Complex shaped compo- nents of these superalloys are required with suitable elevated temperature mechanical properties and good hot corrosion resistance in order to withstand the stringent operating con- ditions encountered in the hot sections of these engines. Unfortunately, cost efﬁcient commercial application of these materials has been largely restricted due to the difﬁculty in joining them by conventional welding techniques dur- ing manufacture and repair. This is because   ′   precipitation hardened nickel-based superalloys are highly susceptible to heat affected zone (HAZ) microﬁssuring during welding and subsequent post weld heat treatments  [1–7] . It has been recognized that this crucial weldability problem becomes increasingly worse with an increase in concentrations of   ′ forming elements in these highly alloyed materials  [2,4,6,7] . The role of   ′   precipitate particles in promoting susceptibil- ity to HAZ microﬁssuring has been reported to be through their rapid re-precipitation behavior during cooling from the welding temperatures, which induces large shrinkage stresses along with a signiﬁcant intragranular strengthening. The latter resists stress relaxation thereby causing welding stresses to be concentrated across grain boundaries and, thus, increases the driving force for cracking  [2–4,6,7] . Hence, attempts in reducing inﬂuence of   ′   particles on HAZ crack- ing in these alloys have been mainly based on the effects of theirsolid-statere-precipitation reaction during weld cooling. In contrast, however, very limited information is available  

about the actual on-heating dissolution behavior of   ′   precip- itate particles during welding cycle, which can be expected to not only inﬂuence their mode of re-precipitation during cooling but also contribute to grain boundary embrittlement through other potentially important mechanisms.  

In general, it has been implicitly assumed that   ′   precip- itate particles in nickel-based superalloys undergo complete solid-state dissolution in the HAZ regions which experience peak temperatures above   ′   equilibrium solvus temperature during welding cycles. Nevertheless, a very important pos- sibility that on-heating to the welding temperatures, these particles could persist to temperatures at which they could react with the austenitic    matrix producing a liquid phase by a eutectic-type reaction, along with its concomitant conse- quences, has apparently not received due consideration. Ojo et al.  [8,9]  have observed that, besides the generally reported coarsening and complete solid-state dissolution of   ′   par- ticles, these particles liquated in the HAZ of nickel-based superalloy IN 738LC weldment. A review of micrographs in published literature shows that constitutional liquation of  ′   precipitates most likely occurs in other   ′   strengthened nickel-based superalloys, yet was not recognized. Consid- ering that   ′   precipitate particles are essential and are the principal strengthening phase of most of the precipitation hardened nickel-based superalloys, and new generation of nickel-based superalloys continue to contain even higher vol- ume fraction of these particles, it is deemed appropriate to understand better the role of these precipitates in weld HAZ microﬁssuring. In the present work, the HAZ microstructures were examined and correlated with the hot ductility property of IN 738 superalloy in order to evaluate the effect of consti- tutional liquation of   ′   precipitates on cracking resistance of the alloy during weld cooling. The results of this investigation are presented and discussed in this communication.

# 3.2. Non-isothermal solid-state dissolution and constitutional liquation of  γ ′   particles  

The phenomenon of constitutional liquation of second phase particles was ﬁrst proposed by Pepe and Savage  [15] and has been observed by different investigators in vari- ous alloy systems  [1,15–17] . It occurs generally below an alloy’s equilibrium solidus temperature by a eutectic-type reaction between a second phase particle and the surround- ing matrix producing a metastable solute rich liquid ﬁlm at the particle/matrix interface. The basic requirement for the occurrence of constitutional liquation of an intermetallic compound A x B y  in an alloy is the existence of A x B y  particle at temperatures equal to or above the eutectic temperature of the matrix-A x B y  system  [15] . Therefore, the susceptibility of an A x B y  type second phase to constitutional liquation should primarily be related to its solid-state dissolution behavior, as complete dissolution prior to reaching the eutectic temper- ature will preclude the occurrence of liquation. An attempt has been made to model particle dissolution under rapid heat- ing condition by analytical technique based on isokinetic and additivity concept and also by control volume numer- ical calculations  [18] . The results of the two methods, which were found to be in good agreement, show that the degree of particle dissolution depends on interplay between the heat- ing rate and the initial particle size. Soucail and Bienvenu [19]  in a separate work, studied dissolution of   ′   phase in a nickel-based superalloy at equilibrium and under rapid heating condition. Following a different analytical approach, developed by Ashby and Easterling  [20] , they derived a particle dissolution model and veriﬁed it with experimen- tal results. Their analytical results, which are reported to be in good agreement with the experimental results, are quali- tatively consistent with those of Bjorneklett et al.  [18] . They showed that there is a signiﬁcant departure from equilibrium under rapid heating condition, in that the temperature of com- plete solid-state dissolution increased with increasing heating rate and this departure was dependent on the initial parti- cle size. The increase in complete dissolution temperature was found to be more pronounced with increase in particle size. An increase of about 120   ◦ C in the complete dissolution temperature at a heating rate of 8   ◦ C/s was reported for   ′   pre- cipitates with an initial size of 0.8   m. They also indicated that the delay in   ′   dissolution was more pronounced during the early stages of dissolution than during towards the end of dissolution process. No reasons were given for the appar- ent increase in the rate of dissolution towards the terminal dissolution stage. Similar effect of heating rate on   ′   solvus temperature has also been reported by DTA experiments  [21] . Therefore, depending upon the initial particle size and heat- ing rate, limited integrated time available for homogenisation by diffusion process during continuous heating can cause   ′ precipitate particles in nickel-based superalloys to persist to temperatures well above their equilibrium solvus tempera- ture. Pepe and Savage  [15]  have proposed that existence of such second phase intermetallic particles at a temperature where they could react with the matrix by a eutectic-type reaction will result in constitutional liquation.  

It is generally accepted that   –  ′   eutectic reaction occurs during solidiﬁcation of most   ′   precipitation hardened super- alloys. Ni–Al–Cr and Ni–Al–Ti are the two ternary systems mostly used to represent these type of superalloys. The con- ﬁguration for low Ti concentrations in Ni–Al–Ti is similar to that of Ni–Al–Cr system  [22] . Therefore, from a more theo- retical point of view, the Ni–Al–Ti system is a good reference for the discussion of  –  ′   equilibriaandthesolidiﬁcationpath for this class of superalloys. Willemin and Durand-Charre [23]  have studied liquid-solid equilibria in the nickel rich cor- ner of the Ni–Al–Ti system and have proposed a projection of the liquid surface for this system. According to the Ni–Al–Ti ternary system data, the eutectic reaction between    and   ′ phases are found to be monovariant in nature. This implies that   –  ′ eutectic reaction in the nickel superalloys based on this system occurs over a range of temperature. Microstruc- tural examination of directionally solidiﬁed and quenched IN 738LC from different temperatures has been reported to show that   –  ′ eutectic transformation occurs over a range of temperatures which could be below 1180   ◦ C  [13] . Likewise, carefully designed DTA experiments have also been reported to show formation of   –  ′ eutectic over a range of tempera- tures in PWA 1480 single crystal superalloys  [24,25] . Hence, there exists a temperature range in   ′   precipitation hard- ened nickel-based alloys, within which   –  ′ eutectic reaction occurs and persistence of   ′   particles to this temperature range during continuous heating could result in their con- stitutional liquation. Nonetheless, it is useful to consider the kinetics of such a liquation reaction, i.e. whether liquation could occur almost immediately on reaching an eutectic tem- perature or a signiﬁcant time delay is required for such a reaction to take place, which is discussed next.  

# 3.3. Interface reaction and constitutional liquation of  γ phase  

In the consideration of constitutional liquation Pepe and Savage  [15]  implicitly assumed that thermodynamic equi- librium exists at the precipitate–matrix interface during the precipitate dissolution, as well as at the precipitate–liquid and liquid–matrix interface during liquation. This implies that the precipitate dissolution and liquation reactions are diffusion controlled. It has, however, been suggested that in a situation where the solid-state dissolution is fully interface controlled (such as of coherent precipitates) the enrichment of solutes at the particle–matrix interface required for constitutional liqua- tion might be signiﬁcantly restricted  [26] . Considering that  ′   particles are coherent with    matrix in nickel base super- alloys, it is reasonable to consider if the dissolution behavior of these particles would limit their liquation by reducing the solute concentration at the particle–matrix interface to a level which is below the equilibrium value, when these particles are heated to a temperature where they are thermodynamic ally capable of liquating.  

It is generally assumed that in superalloys the   ′   phase precipitates are fully ordered up to their solutionzing temper- ature and that dissolution is accompanied by migration of the interface between the ordered   ′   phase and    matrix. In such a situation, the above deviation from equilibrium solute con- centration at the particle–matrix interface during dissolution can be expected. However, a recent high temperature X-ray micro diffraction study of dissolution behavior of   ′   phase in AM1singlecrystalnickelbasesuperalloyhasshownasigniﬁ- cant variation from the above assumption [27] .Itwasreported that partial structural disordering of   ′   phase occurs at   /  ′ interface at temperatures above 800   ◦ C. As the temperature increases, the layer of disordered   ′   increases in thickness from the   /  ′   interface inwards within the   ′   particle, reduc- ing the volume fraction of the ordered phase without changing the composition enough to transform the   ′   to    phase. The disordering reac- tion was observed only in the superalloy in an experiment conducted up to 1300   ◦ C. They also concluded from their micro- chemical composition analysis that solute composition at the  /  ′   phase boundary at each annealing temperature was in an equilibrium condition. Therefore, the concept of solid-state dissolution of   ′   in which the ordered   ′   phase is surrounded by solute rich    phase transformed directly from it, and the reports indicating local equilibrium at the migrating   /  ′ coherent interface show that, contrary to the aforementioned prediction based on interface control dissolution of coher- ent particles, solute concentration at the   /  ′   interface can be expected to be at least commensurate with that expected on the basis of near thermodynamic equilibrium. This implies that liquation can be expected once a   ′   particle survives to a temperature where a   –  ′   eutectic-type reaction is possi- ble, as dictated by local thermodynamic equilibrium. The   ′ solvus temperature ( T SV ) in IN 738LC superalloy has been reported to be around 1160   ◦ C  [30] , while   –  ′   eutectic reac- tion was reported to occur below 1180   ◦ C in the same alloy. In the present work, rapid continuous heating at 111   ◦ C/s caused the primary   ′   precipitate particles to persist beyond their  T SV  to 1165   ◦ C and above, and resulted in constitutional liquation within a holding time of 0.5 s. Likewise, this could probably be contributing to the observed deviation from the dissolution rate predicted by the solid-state diffusion based model, during continuous heating of Astroloy alloy  [19] . It is possible that the   ′   particles in their work  [19]  did undergo some solid-state dissolution on reaching and exceeding the equilibrium solvus temperature during heating, but they did survive to a temperature where they could have constitution- ally liquated. Since diffusion through liquid is of orders of magnitude faster than in solid-state, dissolution of   ′   particles through liquation can be expected to occur more rapidly than the rate predicted on the basis of solid-state diffusion. This might be responsible for the faster rate of dissolution that they observed during the last stages of dissolution in their work [19] .  

# 3.4. Stability of liquid produced by constitutional liquation of  γ ′   during cooling  

Heat affected zone microﬁssuring is generally considered to be caused by the presence of low melting liquid on the grain boundaries. The liquid enables the boundaries to sepa- rate under the thermal and shrinkage stresses resulting from weld cooling cycle. It has, however, been recognized through many studies on liquation cracking that the mere occurrence of liquation is not sufﬁcient to produce a crack susceptible microstructure. To incur cracking, continuous or semi contin- uous grain boundary liquid should persist until a time when sufﬁcient thermal and mechanical restraints have developed during cooling. Rapid elimination of grain boundary liquid prior to the existence of sufﬁcient level of tensile stresses would improve the alloy’s resistance to liquation cracking. One fundamental mechanism by which a metastable grain boundary liquid ﬁlm can be effectively removed through rapid solidiﬁcation is by liquid ﬁlm migration (LFM)  [31] . This solidiﬁcation mode, which is controlled by a high diffu- sion rate in the liquid, is an alternative to lattice diffusion con- trolled and normal dendritic solidiﬁcation types, and as such has been reported to be beneﬁcial to HAZ liquation crack- ing resistance  [32] . There are two major driving forces that have been reported to be responsible for LFM. They are (i) diffusional coherency strain energy, which requires sufﬁcient size difference between the diffusing solute in a metastable liquid and the matrix atoms in order to develop substantial coherency strains resulting from lattice mismatch  [31–33] , and (ii) asymmetry of surface tension at the two solid–liquid interfaces. The latter requires the occurrence of appreciable curvature at grain interfaces in order to set up a substantial concentration gradient within the liquid needed for solute diffusive ﬂux during LFM  [32–34] . As mentioned earlier, LFM was observed in simulated and TIG weld HAZs in the present work ( Figs. 5, 6 and 8 ). This could be attributable to (i) substantial atomic size mismatch between   ′   forming ele- ments and the    matrix atoms and (ii) signiﬁcant local grain boundary curvature exhibited by its well rounded/curved ser- rated grain boundaries.Inaddition,the inherent concentration gradient in liquid ﬁlm produced by constitutional liquation of   ′   particles  [15]  could also assist in driving the LFM process.  

Richards et al.  [35]  have suggested that the beneﬁt of LFM in precluding HAZ microﬁssuring will be effective pro- vided the thickness of intergranular liquid ﬁlm is such that a complete solidiﬁcation of grain boundary liquid occurred via LFM before enough thermal and mechanical restraints could be developed on weld cooling to induce cracking. Ojo et al. [9]  have reported that high volume fraction of   ′   particles capable of liquating and contributing to the intergranular liq- uid in IN 738LC superalloy limited the occurrence of LFM in the alloy.  

# 3.5. Effect of constitutional liquation of  γ ′   on HAZ microﬁssuring  

The susceptibility of structural alloys to weld HAZ microﬁssuring is often quantiﬁed by Gleeble hot ductility testing  [11] . The test is based on the premise that the deforma- tion behavior of a material, as evaluated by its hot ductility, reﬂects its capability to accommodate tensile stresses and resist cracking during welding thermal cycle. According to this test, a material loses all ductility when the temperature reaches the on-heating nil-ductility temperature (NDT) and exhibits practically no ductility at all the temperatures above the NDT. Several investigations  [36–39]  have shown that in a number of different materials NDT occurs as a result of grain boundary liquation. Lin et al.  [39]  have developed a methodology, which characterizes a crack susceptible region (CSR) in the HAZ. The CSR was theoretically derived on the basis of the ductility of a material during welding, as obtained from Gleeble hot ductility tests, and the criteria assumed in the development of liquation cracking theories. It can be described using the results of hot ductility tests by the temperature range between the liquidus  T L  and NDT, which is represented by on-heating zero ductility temper- ature range (ZDTR). The ZDTR, within which the material exhibits practically zero ductility, was found to correlate with the width of HAZ regions in which grain boundary liqua- tion may occur (crack susceptible region) during welding cycle  [39] . The ZDTR is a material speciﬁc constant. This parameter often allows the HAZ regions that are susceptible to liquation cracking, to be quantiﬁed and facilitates compar- ison of cracking susceptibility of different alloys  [39] . The alloy recovers its ductility during cooling from ZDTR when the temperature is lowered to the ductility recovery temper- ature (DRT). The temperature range between the on-heating peak temperature  T p  and the corresponding DRT during cool- ing is usually referred to as brittle temperature range (BTR). This temperature range is equally important in describing sus- ceptibility of an alloy to HAZ cracking, since tensile strain (or stress) which causes cracking does not generally develop until the weld begins to cool  [40] . BTR can be related to the period of time, at a given cooling rate, during which the ductility of a particular HAZ region, within the CSR, expe- riencing the peak temperature  T p,  remains negligible during cooling. It has been found that when the peak temperature exceeds the NDT, the values of on-cooling ductility are less than those of the on-heating values at the same temperature [39] . This is due to the grain boundary liquation, which tends to delay the ductility recovery to temperatures much lower thanNDT.The larger this brittle temperature range,thehigher is the propensity to HAZ cracking. Therefore, any factor that causes grain boundary liquation at a temperature much lower than an alloy’s equilibrium solidus temperature during heat- ing is not only potentially detrimental in terms of increasing the HAZ CSR (i.e. ZDTR) of the alloy but would also lead to increased BTR during cooling.  

The critical hot ductility data in the present work, in terms of nil-ductility temperature (NDT), nil-strength temperature (NST), ductility recovery temperature (DRT), on-heating zero ductility temperature range (ZDTR) and on-cooling zero ductility temperature range, also known as brittle tempera- ture range (BTR), for a peak temperature of 1245   ◦ C were determined by Gleeble hot ductility testing, and are given in Table 2 . It is interesting to note that the temperature range at which constitutional liquation of   ′   particles was observed in the present work falls within the on-heating zero ductility temperature range (ZDTR) as determined by the hot ductility tests. The on-cooling ductility of the alloy was also signiﬁ- cantly reduced due to the on-heating constitutional liquation of   ′   particles, as reﬂected by the substantially lower DRT (1000   ◦ C) compared to NDT (1160   ◦ C) with a corresponding BTR of 245   ◦ C for a peak heating temperature of 1245   ◦ C. The relatively wide ZDTR compared to other austenitic alloy systems known to be resistant to HAZ cracking like Type 310 stainless steel (61   ◦ C  [37] ) and alloy IN 625 (102   ◦ C  [37] ), coupled with a considerable loss of on-cooling ductility as reﬂected by low ductility recovery temperature (DRT), could be an important factor contributing to the high susceptibility of IN 738LC to HAZ microﬁssuring.

As stated earlier, HAZ microﬁssuring in fully austenitic steel and nickel base superalloys has been recognised to involve the presence and persistence of low melting liq- uid phase on HAZ grain boundaries to temperatures at which sufﬁcient welding stresses develop during cooling. Several mechanisms have been proposed to explain HAZ grain boundary liquation. The most widely accepted being the grain boundary penetration mechanism, wherein liquid ﬁlm from liquating particles inﬁltrates and spreads along the grain boundary regions. The metastable intergranular liquid produced by constitutional liquation of   ′   reacts with the adjacent solid grain through back diffusion of solute atoms across the solid–liquid interface. In this situation the non- equilibrium solid–liquid interfacial energy is considerably low  [41] , which would make the liquid to effectively wet the grain boundaries and spread along the intergranular regions. This was observed in the simulated HAZ microstructures as well as in the actual weld HAZs in the present work, as the liquid from constitutional liquation of   ′   particles penetrated grain boundary regions and resulted in liquid ﬁlm migration (LFM) which requires intimate contact at the solid–liquid interfaces ( Figs. 5, 6, 8–10 ). According to Chadwick’s calcu- lations  [42] , the tensile stress required to overcome surface tension γ L  on a grain boundary containing liquid lm of thick- ness  h , is given by,  σ  = 2 γ L / h . This indicates that an increase in intergranular liquid ﬁlm thickness through liquation of   ′ particles would lower the stress required to cause decohe- sion at such a solid–liquid interface. Besides, the limiting effect of constitutional liquation of large volume fraction of  ′   particles on LFM  [9]  could promote persistence of inter- granular liquid to lower temperatures during cooling at which sufﬁcient welding stresses could develop to cause the grain boundary to separate. HAZ grain boundary microﬁssuring was often observed closely associated with liquated   ′   parti- cles in the present work ( Figs. 9 and 10 ). The present results showing the damaging effect of constitutional liquation of   ′ particles on hot ductility of the present alloy, complements earlier reports  [8,9]  on the role of   ′   particles in weld HAZ microﬁssuring.  

Increase in concentration of   ′   forming elements have been long recognized to result in increased HAZ crack- ing susceptibility of nickel-based superalloys  [2,4,6,7] . DTA analysis of these alloys  [21]  have shown that increase in   ′ forming elements produces not only an increase in volume fraction of   ′   phase but also a signiﬁcant increase in the   ′ solvus temperature,  T SV,  which causes a resultant decrease in the temperature range between  T SV  and   –  ′   eutectic tem- perature. New generations of single crystal and directionally solidiﬁed nickel-based superalloys containing higher volume fraction of   ′   phase are known to exhibit signiﬁcantly higher values of   ′   T SV  [22] , which drives it closer to the   –  ′   eutec- tic temperatures. Another factor that has been recognized to affect   ′   T SV  is the Co content, a reduction in Co con- centration producing a substantial increase in  T SV  [43] . It is interesting to note that a decrease in the temperature differ- ence between  T SV  and   –  ′   eutectic reaction temperature, either due to an increase in   ′   forming elements or decrease in Co concentration, would result in a decrease in the mini- mum heating rate required for the   ′   particles of a given size to persist to a temperature where it could react with    matrix to produce liquation via a eutectic-type reaction. That is, it would reduce the value of minimum heating rate required to cause constitutional liquation of   ′   particles. Likewise, it will also reduce the maximum   ′   particle size that can be tolerated without liquating during continuous heating at a particular heating rate. Therefore, increase in   ′   forming elements in nickel-based superalloy would not only increase the volume fraction of   ′   particles capable of liquating during contin- uous heating process but could also increase susceptibility to constitutional liquation of   ′   by reducing the minimum heating rate and minimum   ′   particle size required for such occurrence.  

Based on the concept of rapid on-cooling   ′   re-precipit- ation, it has been suggested that in order to prevent HAZ microﬁssuring,   ′   particles in nickel-based superal- loys should be overaged prior to welding  [4,6] . However, based on the present discussion, for a speciﬁc heating rate, increase in   ′   particle size would increase their tendency to survive during continuous heating to a temperature where they could constitutionally liquate and contribute to grain boundary liquation. It should be noted that liquation of   ′ phase in nickel-based superalloys is not only potentially dele- terious in relation to HAZ microﬁssuring during welding, but also during subsequent post weld heat treatment (PWHT), which is known to commonly affect these alloys. Liquated HAZ grain boundary regions have been recognised to be most susceptible to PWHT cracking and also to the formation of deleterious TCP phases during service  [44] . Liquation of   ′ precipitates has not been generally considered, perhaps unin- tentionally, in discussions on metallurgical processes taking place during heating of nickel-based superalloys to elevated temperatures. It is suggested that appropriate consideration be given to the likelihood of its occurrence, which could have signiﬁcant consequences, particularly on the weldability of superalloys and on the chemistries of the next generation of superalloys.  

# 4. Summary and conclusions  

1. IN 738LC superalloy was observed to exhibit a relatively wide crack susceptible region depicted by high on-heating zero ductility temperature range, estimated to be around 150   ◦ C. This coupled with a considerably high brittle temperature range are suggested to be plausible factors contributing to high susceptibility of the alloy to HAZ microﬁssuring.  

2. Correlation of microstructures of simulated HAZs and of those observed in actual TIG welded specimens, of IN 738LC with hot ductility properties of the alloy showed that the temperature range at which constitutional liqua- tion of   ′   particles was observed in the present work falls within the on-heating zero ductility temperature range. In addition, the on-cooling ductility of the alloy was also signiﬁcantly reduced due to the on-heating constitutional liquation of   ′   particles  

3. The results of the present study conﬁrmed previous reports which indicated that besides the generally reported effects of solid-state rapid re-precipitation reaction of   ′   precip- itates during cooling, these particles could contribute to HAZ cracking susceptibility by liquation reaction.  

4. It is suggested that higher concentration of   ′   forming elements in nickel base superalloys could promote con- stitutional liquation tendency of   ′   particles by reducing the minimum required heating rate for particles of a given size to liquate, and by reducing the maximum   ′   particle size that can withstand a particular heating rate without liquating.
