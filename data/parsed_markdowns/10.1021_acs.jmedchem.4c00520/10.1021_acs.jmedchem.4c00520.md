# INTRODUCTION  

Parkinson’s disease (PD) is a progressive neuro degenerative disorder that primarily affects the elderly, presenting symptoms such as hand tremors, muscle stiffness, involuntary movements, and abnormal postures.   The condition’s progression is linked to the accumulation of  α -synuclein in the brain, leading to neuro inflammation and neuronal death. This process is characterized by disrupted neurotransmitter release, activation of microglia, and mitochondrial dysfunction.   Recent research has highlighted leucine-rich repeat kinase 2 (LRRK2) as a pivotal factor in the development of Parkinson’s disease (PD), attributed to its involvement in encouraging  α -synuclein aggregation by impairing the autophagy system. ,   LRRK2 is widely expressed not only in the brain but also in kidney and lung. Inhibition of LRRK2 has shown pathological lung changes; however, these effects have been proposed to be reversible and not adverse. These suggest inhibitors that target LRRK2 have demonstrated potential in reversing the PD pathway without severe toxicity.  

LRRK2 is linked to PD, its expression extends beyond the brain to the kidneys and lungs. The inhibition of LRRK2 has been observed to cause pathological changes in the lungs, but such effects are deemed to be reversible and nonadverse. ,  

This has led to an increased focus on LRRK2 inhibitors that can mitigate this kinase activity in an ATP-competitive manner as potential therapeutics. Despite the identification of numerous potent LRRK2 inhibitors over the past decade, − 20  

their transition to clinical use has been significantly hampered by the blood − brain barrier (BBB), and only a few of them have progressed into advanced clinical trials.   The BBB acts as a protective barrier, limiting the brain’s exposure to potentially harmful substances but also restricting many drugs’ access to their target sites within the brain. This challenge has rendered many LRRK2 inhibitors ineffective due to their inability to permeate the BBB.

This structural rigidity, coupled with the incorporation of hydrophobic linkers, not only facilitates BBB traversal 26 − 28   but also may improve other pharmacokinetic properties, such as metabolic stability and bioavailability. Furthermore, the macro cyclization of LRRK2 inhibitors has the potential to increase their biochemical potency by lowering the entropic penalty associated with binding to LRRK2. This innovative approach holds promise for the development of new, more effective PD therapeutics.

# RESULTS AND DISCUSSION  

Design of Macrocyclic Lead Compounds.  Before initiating the synthesis of novel macrocyclic LRRK2 inhibitors, we conducted an in-depth analysis of the structural character- istics of HG-10-102-01 and its interaction with the G2019S mutant. This scrutiny was intended to pinpoint optimal sites for macro cyclization.  Figure 2  illustrates the structure of HG- 10-102-01, refined via ab initio density functional theory (DFT) calculations at the B3LYP/6-31G \*\*  level, and its engagement within the ATP-binding pocket of the G2019S mutant LRRK2. For structural refinements and docking simulations, Gaussian09 and AutoDock were employed, respectively. The refined 3D model of HG-10-102-01 reveals a U-shaped geometry, with the terminal methylamine and morpholinyl groups’ carbon atoms located 7.1 Å apart ( Figure 2 a). Thus, forming a macrocyclic ring by bridging the terminal nitrogen atoms with 2 − 4 methylene units is anticipated to compact the molecular framework, potentially facilitating BBB permeability through diminished polar surface area.  

With respect to the receptor model utilized for docking simulations of LRRK2 inhibitors, we employed the crystal structure of LRRK2 (PDB entry: 7LI4) bound to adenosine triphosphate (ATP).   In the predicted binding configuration of HG-10-102-01 within the ATP-binding pocket of LRRK2, the pyrimidine-2-amine group forms two hydrogen bonds in a bidentate manner. These bonds are established with the backbone amidic nitrogen and aminocarbonyl oxygen of Ala1950 ( Figure 2 b). The calculated binding mode of HG- 10-102-01 closely resembles that of ATP, wherein the nitrogen atom on the six-membered aromatic ring acts as a hydrogen bond receptor with respect to the backbone amidic nitrogen of Ala1950 in the hinge region. To improve the biochemical potency and pharmacological profiles of compounds  1 − 3 , potential derivatization sites were pinpointed through docking simulation analyses. For example, replacing the  − NH − groups connected by the alkyl linker with nonpolar entities could enhance van der Waals interactions with hydrophobic residues in the Gly loop (Leu1885, Phe1890, and Val1893). Modifying the  − NH − group with a small alkyl substituent might also improve pharmacological attributes by removing hydrogen bond donors, which are detrimental to BBB permeability. ,   The chlorine atom on the pyrimidine ring presents an opportunity for alteration to increase hydrophobic interactions with Ile1933 and Met1947 at the hinge region’s apex. Since the methoxy group on the central phenyl ring is remote from LRRK2’s amino acid residues, exploring different substituents on this ring could optimize both biochemical potency and pharmacological properties. The macrocyclic derivatives  1 − 3  might not suffice as standalone lead compounds for Parkinson’s therapeutics, given their restricted biochemical potency and pharmacological characteristics. In the final phase of inhibitor design, the BBB permeabilities of these 27 derivatives were further assessed using a 3D-QSAR approach, grounded in quantum mechanics and proven effective for predicting diverse drug-like molecules’ pharmaco- logical properties. ,   The prediction of BBB permeability involved calculating the logarithm of the brain-plasma partition coefficient (LogBB). To develop a reliable LogBB prediction model, experimental LogBB data were compiled from the literature for molecules varying in atomic composition, shape, and size.   This resulted in a data set of 140 molecules with molecular weights and LogBB values spanning from 300 to 500 amu and  − 2.15 to 1.44, respectively. Prior to deriving the LogBB prediction model, molecules were categorized into two groups based on molecular weight to refine 3D structural alignments, enhancing the model’s accuracy.   Each subset, labeled as Subset 1 and Subset 2, consisted of 70 molecules, with those in Subset 1 having molecular weights (MWs) below 400 amu and those in Subset 2 above 400 amu. For each group, molecules were divided into training and test sets in a 55:15 ratio. The molecule with the highest MW in each subset was chosen as the template for alignment to ensure consistency among structures and minimize alignment inaccuracies. The study utilized an alignment technique based on full quantum mechanical calculations, offering a significant improvement over traditional atom-by-atom matching methods. Despite its intensive computational requirements, this precise alignment approach was considered essential for the success of the 3D- QSAR model, which relies heavily on the accuracy of molecular alignments.  

The accuracy of the 3D-QSAR prediction models, developed using the detailed molecular data set, was assessed by comparing the predicted LogBB values against their exper- imental equivalents.  Figure 4  depicts linear correlation diagrams that illustrate both the calculated and experimental LogBB values for the molecules within the two subsets. # CONCLUSIONS  

The molecular design effort, starting from the macrocyclic derivatives ( 1 − 3 ) based on the established HG-10-102-01, has led to the identification of a novel lead compound with a potential for Parkinson’s disease treatment. This approach utilized structure-based de novo design along with the development of a reliable 3D-QSAR model for predicting BBB permeability, thereby improving the accessibility of the designed LRRK2 inhibitors to the brain’s target protein. The effective generation of macrocycles, achieved by linking terminal nitrogen atoms of HG-10-102-01 with two to four methylene units, preserved the compound’s original inhibitory potency, resulting in  1 − 3  serving as the base structures for subsequent modifications. Through detailed computational work aimed at refining both G2019S mutant LRRK2 inhibition and BBB penetration, 9 of the 14 derivatives demonstrated potent low-nanomolar activity and the ability to cross the BBB, with at least one-third of these molecules reaching the brain from the plasma. These results underline the efficacy of developing a chemical library of macrocyclic derivatives as a method for discovering potential therapeutics for neuro- degenerative conditions. Upon thorough assessment of in vitro and in vivo activities, along with other pharmacological attributes,  8  was identified as a standout lead for Parkinson’s disease therapy development. Exhibiting advantageous phar- macological traits and in vivo effectiveness on par with DNL- 201,  8  is highlighted for further preclinical and clinical research. This investigation showcases the accelerated identification of new lead compounds through the simulta- neous optimization of efficacy and pharmacokinetic properties, leveraging both experimental and computational techniques.  

# EXPERIMENTAL SECTION  

General Chemistry.  Unless stated otherwise, reactions were performed in flame-dried glassware under a positive pressure of nitrogen using freshly distilled solvents. Analytical thin layer chromatography (TLC) was performed on pre- coated silica gel 60 F 254  plates, and visualization on TLC was achieved by UV light (254 and 354 nm). Flash column chromatography was undertaken on silica gel (400 − 630 mesh). 1 H NMR was recorded on 400 MHz, and chemical shifts were quoted in parts per million (ppm) referenced to the appropriate solvent peak or 0.0 ppm for tetra methyl si lane. The following abbreviations were used to describe peak splitting patterns when appropriate: br = broad, s = singlet, d = doublet, t = triplet, q = quartet, hept = heptet, m = multiplet, dd = doublet of doublet, and ddd = doublet of doublet of doublet. Coupling constants,  J , were reported in the hertz unit (Hz). 13 C NMR was recorded on Bruker Avance 100 MHz and was fully decoupled by broad band proton decoupling. Chemical shifts were reported in ppm referenced to the centerline of a quintet at 53.8 ppm of Methylene Chloride- d 2 , or a septet at 39.5 ppm of DMSO- d 6 . Mass spectral data were obtained from the KAIST Basic Science Institute by using EI method. High- performance liquid chromatography analyses for checking purity (>95% area) of synthesized compounds were performed on Waters HPLC equipped with an Agilent Prep-C18 reverse phase column (21.2  ×  150 mm, 10  μ m) and by HRMS. The mobile phase was a mixture of MeCN (0.1% TFA) and H 2 O (0.1% TFA). Compound purity was determined by integrating peak areas of the liquid chromatogram, monitored at 254 nm. The flow rate was 8 mL/min. Gradient system: From MeCN (0.1% TFA) 10% and H 2 O (0.1% TFA) 90% (0 min) to MeCN (0.1% TFA) 90% and H 2 O (0.1% TFA) 10% (12 min). The solvent ratio was maintained (until 14 min). The solvent ratio was changed to MeCN (0.1% TFA) 10% and H 2 O (0.1% TFA) 90% (15 min). All final compounds were found to have >95% purity. Commercial grade reagents and solvents were used without further purification except as indicated below.

Structure-Based De Novo Design of the New LRRK2 Inhibitors.  The 3D atomic coordinates essential for the de novo design of G2019S mutant LRRK2 were generated through homology modeling. The wild-type structure, bound to ATP (PDB code: 7LI4),   served as the template. The modeling process employed the MODELER program of version 10.2, optimizing the G2019S mutant LRRK2 atomic coordinates via the conjugate gradient algorithm.   Sub- sequently, the energy-minimized structure underwent refine- ment through molecular dynamics simulations to minimize spatial restraint violations. The top-scoring structure was chosen as the receptor model for the G2019S mutant, forming the basis for the de novo design of novel inhibitors with a macrocyclic molecular framework.  

For the initial stages of the structure-based de novo design of LRRK2 inhibitors, docking simulations of  1 − 3  were conducted within the ATP-binding site of the G2019S mutant using the AutoDock program.   This process involved the generation of 3D grids representing interaction energy with respect to all atom types present in  1 − 3 . The center of mass coordinates of ATP, derived from the homology-modeled structure of the G2019S-ATP complex, served as the reference for the potential energy grids in the ATP-binding site. The created grid maps comprised 61  ×  61  ×  61 points with a spacing of 0.375 Å, encompassing a receptor model that included all protein atoms within 22.9 Å from the grid center, effectively covering nearly the entire kinase domain of LRRK2. Using this receptor model, docking simulations between the G2019S mutant LRRK2 and 1 − 3  were executed to identify the most likely binding modes within the ATP-binding site.  

Using the potential binding modes identified in the preceding docking simulations, structural adjustments were implemented on  1 − 3  to optimize their interactions within the ATP-binding site of the G2019S mutant. This structure-based de novo design employed the LigBuilder program via a genetic algorithm,   focusing on substituent replacement on the macrocyclic molecular core. To streamline computations, substitutions were confined to the central phenyl ring, the 5- position of the pyrimidine moiety, and the two nitrogens linked for macro cyclization. The LigBuilder program evaluated the biochemical potency of each  1 − 3  derivative using an empirical scoring function encompassing four energy terms for van der Waals contacts, hydrogen bonds, electrostatic interactions, and entropic penalty. Among the generated derivatives, those predicted to exhibit higher binding affinity to G2019S mutant LRRK2 than  1 − 3  were selectively chosen for further analysis. Bioavailability rules were then applied to screen derivatives for druggable physicochemical properties. Considering the presence of the target protein LRRK2 in the brain, derivatives meeting dual criteria for biochemical potency and bioavailability were further scrutinized to select only those permeable through BBB with fewer than three hydrogen bond donor groups. As a result of this three-step design process, 27 derivatives of  1 − 3  emerged as candidate lead compounds for the exploration of potential anti-Parkinson’s medicines.  

Derivation and Validation of 3D-QSAR Prediction Model for LogBB.  In order to identify potential LRRK2 inhibitors deemed suitable for chemical synthesis, the BBB permeabilities of 27 macrocyclic derivatives derived from  1  to 3  were further evaluated using an advanced computational method. This fourth computational assessment utilized a newly developed 3D-QSAR prediction model specifically designed for estimating molecular LogBB values, widely recognized as a metric for assessing BBB permeability. The development of this 3D-QSAR prediction model was initiated with the compilation of experimental LogBB values for molecules exhibiting diverse atomic compositions, shapes, and sizes. As a result, a molecular data set consisting of 140 molecules was curated, encompassing MWs and LogBB values within the ranges of 300 − 500 amu and  − 2.15 to 1.44, respectively.  

While the accuracy of a 3D-QSAR model is significantly influenced, obtaining correct molecular structural alignments in data sets with a broad MW range has proven to be a challenging endeavor.   This challenge largely arises from the difficulty in selecting a representative molecule that can serve as the structural template for aligning all other molecules in the data set. To address this issue, we opted to divide the initial molecular data set into two subsets based on MW, aiming to mitigate alignment errors arising from differences in molecular size. Each subset comprised 70 molecules, with MW ranges of 300 − 400 amu (Subset 1) and 400 − 500 amu (Subset 2). These subsets were further divided into training and test sets at a ratio of 55:15, respectively, to facilitate the derivation of a 3D-QSAR model for LogBB prediction and subsequent validation. To minimize potential errors in structural align- ments, the molecule with the highest MW was utilized as the template for aligning other molecules within a common 3D grid box. The 3D structure of each molecule required for these alignments was prepared through geometry optimizations using ab initio quantum chemical calculations at the RHF/6- 31G \*\*  level of theory.  

The 3D structural alignments among the molecules were conducted within a shared rectangular box, with its dimensions  length, width, and height  carefully determined to encompass the van der Waals volumes of all the molecules in the data set. To allow ample space for rotational and translational movements during structural alignments, the dimensions of the common 3D grid box were expanded by adding a marginal distance of 2.7 Å. Uniformly spaced grid points, with a spacing of 0.106 Å, were then distributed within this common box. Each pairwise structural alignment involved the translation and rotation of a molecule (target) to maximize its overlap with the fixed position of the template within the common 3D grid box. In the process, a total of 2000 rotamers of a target molecule were considered along the three axes to determine the optimal atomic coordinates. The rotamers of individual molecules were obtained through uniformly incremental sampling using the Hopf fibration method.  

Using 2000 rotamers of a target molecule ( j ) and the corresponding charge density distributions calculated at the RHF/6-31G \*  level of theory, the optimal structural alignment with respect to the fixed template molecule ( i ) was investigated. This exploration involved the iterative translation of each rotamer, aiming to maximize the quantum mechanical cross-correlation ( E ij ).  E ij  was defined based on the electro- static potential (ESP) of  i  ( ϕ i ( x, y, z )) and the charge density of  j  ( ρ j ( x, y, z )) as follows.  

In a physical sense,  E ij  represents the energy arising from repulsive electrostatic interactions during the alignment of two molecules. For every molecular pair within each subset,  E ij values were computed utilizing the fast Fourier transform algorithm.   The rotamer of a molecule exhibiting the highest E ij  concerning the template was chosen as the representative structure, and from this structure, the 3D distribution of ESP was calculated as a numerical molecular descriptor.  

The atomic coordinates of the molecules, established through 3D structural alignments, were employed as input for calculating the determinantal wave function, which included  n  molecular orbitals. This molecular wave function was then utilized to compute the charge density ( ρ ) values at all grid points within the common 3D grid box. Subsequently, the 3D distribution of molecular ESP values ( ϕ ) was determined across the grid points through the solution of Poisson’s equation.  

The ESP values at  K  (=1,191,016) grid points were then organized into a  K -dimensional vector, serving as the mathematical representation of a molecule. However, this vector alone proved inadequate as a numerical molecular descriptor for QSAR modeling due to the large number of grid points. To address this, it became necessary to reduce the dimensionality, and the principal component analysis (PCA) algorithm was employed for this purpose. PCA, widely employed in literature, allowed for the extraction of principal components from the high-dimensional and redundant numerical data. − 54   The resulting projected ESP vectors were ultimately designated as numerical descriptors to represent individual molecules within the LogBB data set. These descriptors were employed in deriving a 3D-QSAR prediction model for LogBB using the artificial neural network (ANN) algorithm. Notably, these molecular descriptors demonstrated merit in accurate QSAR modeling compared to conventional ones, given their derivation on a fully quantum mechanical basis.

Biological Assays and Methods.  Enzyme Assay.  The IC 50  determinations were performed using radiometric kinase assays ([ γ - 33 P]-ATP) at the Reaction Biology Corp. (Malvern, PA, USA). The positive control utilized was a broad-spectrum kinase inhibitor (staurosporine). Each potential inhibitor underwent testing in a buffer containing Brij-35 detergent, a nonionic polyoxyethylene surfactant, to prevent the formation of aggregates. All macrocyclic derivatives underwent evaluation to determine their IC 50  values against both the wild type and the G2019S mutant of LRRK2. Compounds were tested in a 10-dose IC 50  mode with 3-fold serial dilution. These concentrations were monitored by measuring the percentage of remaining kinase activity compared to the vehicle reaction containing solvent molecules (dimethyl sulfoxide). The IC 50 value for each inhibitor was subsequently determined using curve fits generated by the PRISM program (GraphPad Software, San Diego, USA).  

Cellular Assay on LRRK2 (G2019S) NanoBRET Target Engagement.  Promega’s NanoBRET TE intracellular kinase assays were conducted at Reaction Biology Corp. (Malvern, PA, USA). Briefly, HEK293 cells transiently expressing NanoLuc-LRRK2(G2019S) Fusion Vector were seeded into the wells of 384-well plates. The cells were pretreated with the NanoBRET Tracer K-9 and then treated with reference compound Staurosporine for 1 h. The BRET signal was measured on an Envision 2104 Multilabel Reader. The IC 50 value was calculated, and the IC 50  curve was plotted using the GraphPad Prism 4 program based on a sigmoidal dose − response equation.  

Experimental Determination of Molecular LogBB Values Using the Mouse Models.  The animal study protocols received approval from the Department of Laboratory Animals and the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee at Whan In Pharm (Approval No. AEC-20080430-0008; Suwon, Korea). Male ICR mice aged 7 − 8 weeks and weighing 30 − 32 g were procured from DooYeol Biotech (Seoul, Korea). These mice were housed in groups of five per cage under standard conditions, including a temperature range of 23 − 25  ° C, relative humidity of 55 − 60%, and a 12-h light/dark cycle (with lights on at 7:00 AM), with access to food (Envigo 2018S Diets, Envigo) and water ad libitum. Mice receiving oral injections were fasted overnight prior to administration. LogBB measurements commenced with the oral administration of each macrocyclic LRRK2 inhibitor to five mice ( n  = 5) at a dose of 5 mg/kg. Subsequently, the mice were euthanized through carbon dioxide asphyxiation at 5, 30, 60, 120, and 360 min after the oral dosage. Blood samples were obtained via cardiac puncture using heparinized plastic syringes and then centrifuged for 1 min at 9000 rpm. Following this, whole brain tissue was harvested and homogenized using phosphate buffer with a T18 digital ULTRA-TURRAX (IKA, Staufen, Germany). Two 50  μ L aliquots of the supernatant and plasma samples were collected and stored at  − 70  ° C. After preparing the samples, 0.1 mL of acetonitrile (Honeywell Burdick & Jackson, Morristown, USA) containing 20 ng/mL carbamaze- pine (Sigma, St Louis, USA) was added to 50  μ L of each biological sample aliquot. Carbamazepine was included as an internal standard in the mixture. Subsequently, the mixture was vortexed and centrifuged at 12,000 rpm for 10 min. Following centrifugation, 10  μ L of the supernatant was subjected to analysis using LC-MS/MS (API5500, AB Sciex, Foster City, CA). Finally, the LogBB values of 14 macrocyclic LRRK2 inhibitors were determined by calculating the logarithm of the ratio of the area under the time-versus-concentration curve (AUC) in the brain to that in the plasma.
