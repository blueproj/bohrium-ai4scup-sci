A low cost Ni e Fe-based superalloy due to its good mechanical properties and good workability has been evaluated as a promising candidate boiler material for advanced ultra-supercritical coal- ﬁ red power plants with temperature up to 700    C. The major concerns with Ni e Fe-based alloys are the fundamental deformation mechanisms at intermediate temperatures. In this paper, great emphasis is placed on the dislocation con ﬁ gurations induced by tensile deformation in this low cost Ni e Fe-based superalloy. Two typical deformation-induced microstructure features, Orowan looping and precipitates shearing, are present during tensile deformation. Careful identi ﬁ cation of dislocation-based deformation mechanisms by transmission electron microscopy was performed in tensile strained specimens at 20    C, 500    C, 650    C, 700    C and 750    C in order to correlate the macroscopic behavior with the tension controlling mechanisms.

# 1. Introduction  

The new generation of coal- ﬁ red power plants, termed advanced ultra-supercritical (A-USC) coal- ﬁ red power plants, have been designed to operate at higher steam temperatures (700    C) and higher steam pressures (37.5 MPa) than current generation ultra-super-critical (USC) plants, which operate around 600    C and 24 MPa  [1 e 6] . It is projected that A-USC boilers will achieve above 50% process ef ﬁ ciency over current generation plants and are ex- pected to release 40 e 50% less CO 2  than current generation boilers [7] . Novel materials, such as advanced ferritic and austenitic steels, Ni-based superalloys and thermal barrier coatings, have contrib- uted signi ﬁ cantly to increase the ef ﬁ ciency of fossil-fueled power plants for reducing the emission of greenhouse gases and improving energy conservation  [8 e 12] . Special requirements have been proposed for the materials used in A-USC boiler according to the operating conditions, i.e. 100,000 h creep strength greater than 100 MPa at 750    C and 200,000 h coal-ash corrosion resistance of  

A low cost austenitic Ni e Fe-based alloy designated as GH2984 (developed by the Institute of Metal Research, China) has been used as superheater tubes for marine application. After 10-year service, GH2984 tubes were well in shape without any damage and which can be continuously used  [18] . Guo et al. also pointed 5   out that stress rupture strength for 10 at 700    C can be higher than 100 MPa to meet the superheater material requirement for long term service at 700    C. This alloy contains a high amount of Fe (33 wt %), Cr (19 wt %), no Co, and low amount of Mo (2.2 wt %).  

GH2984 alloy characterizes with high oxidation and corrosion resistance because of its high content of Cr (19 wt %), which can form a tight and adherent Cr 2 O 3  ﬁ lm, that guarantees this alloy having better corrosion resistance. Structure stability study of GH2984 alloy has been carried out at 700  C from 1000 to 18000 h. After 18000 h long time aging at 700    C, the radius of  g 0 precipitates increase linearly with aging time. The amount of  g 0 precipitates gradually increases. GH2984 alloy not only charac- terize with good high temperature strengths, oxidation and corrosion resistance but also easy formability for tube making. Therefore, GH2984 alloy has been considered to be a promising 700    C tube candidate for superheaters and reheaters of A-USC boilers [18 e 22] . Recently, the Ti/Al ratio was moderately decreased to improve the thermal stability of microstructure and mechanical properties of GH2984 alloy for satisfying the require- ment of A-USC plants  [7] . Further microstructural analysis during tensile deformation is required to fully understand the detailed structural evolution of GH2984 alloy. Generally, the motion of dislocations allows plastic deformation to occur. As with many dislocation-based deformation mechanisms commonly observed in  g 0   precipitates strengthened Ni-based superalloys, the following dislocation-precipitate interactions have been identi ﬁ ed, such as weakly and strongly coupled dislocations cutting  g 0   precipitates, cross-slip of super dislocations in  g 0   precipitates and dissociation of dislocations into partials, Orowan looping, as well as dislocation climb and microtwinning  [23 e 27] . The deformation mechanisms in precipitation strengthened materials are known to be strongly dependent on the precipitate size. The current understanding and theories of precipitation strengthening in polycrystalline alloys were developed for materials with low precipitate volume frac- tions. The dislocation-based deformation may become more complex and interesting because of the interactions between dislocations and precipitates. Therefore, dislocation con ﬁ gurations in GH2984 alloy resulting from the tensile deformation at 20    C, 500    C, 650    C, 700    C and 750    C were examined and investigated systematically by transmission electron microscopy (TEM). These research data were then used to study the effect of  g 0   precipitates size on the deformation mechanisms of GH2984 superalloy.  

# 2. Experimental procedures  

The nominal chemical composition of GH2984 alloy is, in wt pct, 33Fe, 19 Cr, 2.2Mo, 1.0 Ti, 0.4Al, 1.0 Nb, 0.06 C, and the balance Ni. Solution heat treatment was conducted at 1100    C for 1 h, followed by air cooling. Aging heat treatment was carried out at 750    C for 8 h and furnace cooling to 650    C, and then holding at 650    C for 16 h with subsequent air cooling. Tensile tests were performed on a Shimadzu test machine at an initial strain rate of 2.0    10  4   s  1   up to about 3 pct deformation; the strain rate was then increased to 5.0    10  3   s  1   until the specimen fractured. Tensile tests were conducted at 20    C, 500    C, 650    C, 700    C and 750    C in order to characterize the deformation behaviors. The temperature region from 700    C to 750    C is of particular interest, because it is considered to be near the maximum temperature that an advanced Ni e Fe-based superalloy for current generation USC power plants application can sustain for an extended period of time. The testing conditions and related tensile properties of GH2984 alloy are given in our previous report  [23] . Tensile specimens (4 mm in gage diameter and 30 mm in gage length) were machined form the heat- treated plates. These specimens were polished using 600 grit sili- con carbide paper in the gage section. Discs of 3 mm in diameter were cut out of the tensile specimens' gage sections and mechan- ically thinned down to about 40  m m  in thickness. TEM samples were prepared by a standard electro-polishing technique using a twin-jet electro-polisher. Electro-polishing was carried out at 15    C at a

# 3. Results and discussion  

Fig.1  (a)-(c) summarize the tensile properties of GH2984 alloy at 20    C, 500    C, 650    C, 700    C and 750    C. It can be seen clearly that yield strength decreased gradually form 20    C to 700    C and then decreased drastically above 700    C. Similar to the temperature dependence of yield strength, tensile strength decreased gradually with increasing temperature to 650    C and thereafter decreased steeply. Both elongation and reduction in area were found to be relatively insensitive to temperatures up to 500    C. Beyond 500    C, the elongation and reduction in area decreased with increasing temperature, though to different extents, and both reached mini- mum levels at 700    C, which is 17.3% and 21.45, respectively. Clearly, compared with other candidate Ni-base alloys such as CCA617  [9,16,17] , Nimonic263  [11] , and Inconel740  [8,15] , the Ni e Fe-base superalloy GH2984 has great cost bene ﬁ ts for super- heater and reheater applications in A-USC power plants.

The average size of  g 0   precipitates is around 10 e 15 nm in  Fig. 4  (a). Thermo-calc indicates that theoretical  g 0 solvus temperature for GH2984 alloy is around 900    C. So the decrease of the  g 0   precipitates size may result from the elevated tensile test temperature which is close to the theoretical  g 0   solvus temperature. The  g 0   precipitates with smaller size may cause a markedly decrease in the tensile strength at 750    C. Physically, the precipitation strengthening effects can be attributed both to size and interfacial or surface energy. At this point, the  g 0   precipitates could not act as effective obstacles to dislocation motion. In addi- tion, Dislocation cutting through  g 0   precipitates seems to be a general tendency at 750    C. In  Fig. 4  (b) and (c), many stacking faults could be observed clearly instead of Orowan dislocation loops in the g/3 g weak-beam dark- ﬁ eld images, which result from dislo- cation shearing of  g 0   precipitates. At this point, the dislocations no longer bow out between the  g 0   precipitates but readily cut or shear them. The smaller  g 0   precipitates are different to impede disloca- tions effectively. In addition, the reduction of  g 0   precipitates size combines along with the stacking faults occurrence to can explain why the yield strength and ultimate tensile strength decreased markedly at higher temperatures (above 700    C). Thus, dislocation shearing of  g 0   precipitates becomes the dominant deformation mechanism after tensile test at 750    C.

# 4. Conclusions  

The dislocation con ﬁ gurations in GH2984 alloy deformed dur- ing tensile test were studied as a function of temperature at 20    C, 500    C, 650    C, 700    C and 750    C. Two typical deformation- induced dislocations features, Orowan looping and precipitates shearing, are present after the tensile deformation. Below 700    C, Orowan looping is dominant; above 700    C,  g 0   precipitates shearing becomes dominant. Both Orowan looping and  g 0   precipitates shearing occur at 700    C. Thus, the transition from Orowan looping to precipitates shearing may started around 700    C. The critical size of  g 0   precipitates for this transition is 18 nm, which is very close to the experimental observation. Below 700    C, the larger  g 0   pre- cipitates impeded the dislocation motion effectively to improve the tensile strength. The decreasing of the  g 0   precipitates size at 750    C may result from the higher temperature which is close to the theoretical  g 0   solvus temperature. The decreasing of the  g 0   pre- cipitates size combine the stacking faults generated by the pre- cipitates shearing in the  g 0   precipitates make the yield strength and ultimate tensile strength decreased markedly above 700    C.
