ABSTRACT:  The cycling performance of batteries is largely determined by the electrode-electrolyte interphase associated with the chemical and electrochemical properties of electrolyte salts and solvents. In this work, the correlations between the electrode (cathode/anode)- electrolyte interphase, ion coordination structure, and di ﬀ usion kinetics were analyzed systematically using localized high-concentration electro- lytes (LHCEs) as a model system. The fundamental mechanism behind the enhanced cycling stability of silicon (Si)-based lithium (Li)-ion batteries using optimized LHCEs is investigated. Among all the LHCEs tested, 1.8 M lithium bis( ﬂ uorosulfonyl)imide with carbonate solvents and  ﬂ uorinated diluents (1:2 molar ratio) had the highest proportion of solvent-separated ion pairs and contact ion pairs and the fastest ion

# INTRODUCTION  

Lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) have enabled a high energy density rechargeable world with the state-of-the-art LIBs with graphite anodes and lithium transition metal oxide cathodes (LiNi x Mn y Co 1 ‑ x ‑ y O 2 , LiNi x Co y Al (1 ‑ x ‑ y ) O 2 , LiCoO 2 , LiMn 2 O 4 , ,   etc.). To date, these batteries have eventually reached their practical limits on energy densities after more than three decades of development.   To break this bottleneck, silicon (Si)-based anodes have been considered as one of the most promising candidates to replace graphite anodes in the next generation of LIBs. The theoretical capacity of Si is 4200 mA h g − 1 , which is ten times higher than that of graphite. However, poor service life, a large volume change of Si upon lithiation, and high initial irreversible capacity have greatly hindered its − 7 large-scale applications.  

Tremendous e ﬀ orts have been devoted to mitigate the volume change of Si through the reduction of Si particle size, − 13   the development of nanostructured materials, and the use − 16   of binders with tailored functionalities. It is a general understanding that the electrode-electrolyte interphase plays a key role on the cycle life of the batteries. Therefore, improving the solid-electrolyte interphase (SEI) of Si anodes with surface coating/modi ﬁ cation and electrolyte/electrolyte additives has − 24   attracted great attention. Conventional LIB electrolytes, such as lithium hexa ﬂ uorophosphate (LiPF 6 ) in ethylene carbonate (EC) and ethyl methyl carbonate (EMC) and/or diethyl carbonate (DEC), work well for graphite anodes. However, they cannot form a stable SEI layer on Si particles during repeated cycling due to the large volume change of ,   Si. Fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC) has been proposed as an e ﬀ ective electrolyte additive that can signi ﬁ cantly enhance the stability of the SEI, and it has been proven to be an ﬀ − 29   e ective additive to extend the service life of Si anodes. In the presence of FEC, which decomposes before EC and EMC (or DEC), a denser, more conformal, and robust SEI is formed on the surface of Si.   This SEI, to a certain degree, can suppress the decomposition of EC/EMC or EC/DEC. However, FEC will be consumed, and the decomposition of EC/EMC or EC/DEC cannot be fully controlled because there is a large amount of free/uncoordinated EC and EMC (DEC) molecules in the conventional electrolytes.  

Recently, our group developed localized high-concentration electrolytes (LHCEs), which can signi ﬁ cantly improve the quality of the SEI on Li metal anodes that also exhibits large

volume expansion. The LHCEs are electrolytes prepared by adding diluents, such as bis(2,2,2-tri ﬂ uoroethyl) ether (BTFE) or 1,1,2,2-tetra ﬂ uoroethyl-2,2,3,3-tetra ﬂ uropropyl ether (TTE) − 33   into high-concentration electrolytes (HCEs). The diluent usually is intermixable with the solvents, while it has no solubility toward the salts nor involves itself in the formation of Li + -solvent/additive pairs. The LHCEs, with extra volume introduced by the diluent, can have a salt concentration comparable to or slightly higher than that of conventional electrolytes. LHCEs not only can retain the excellent electrochemical stability of HCEs against Li metal and Si anodes but also exhibit much lower cost and viscosity than the corresponding HCEs. These LHCEs can retain the advantages of HCEs (e.g., a stable SEI layer formed by preferential decomposition of a Li salt prior to solvent decomposition and a wide electrochemical window) but can avoid disadvantages of HCEs (e.g., low conductivity, high viscosity, and high cost). Recently, we adopted the non ﬂ ammable LHCE used in Li metal batteries and demonstrated good performance for Si- graphite (Si-Gr) composite electrodes.   The Si anodes in full cells with a LiNi 1/3 Mn 1/3 Co 1/3 O 2  cathode demonstrated  ∼ 90% capacity retention over 600 cycles. However, the cells with this electrolyte still su ﬀ er from higher overvoltage, and the fundamental mechanism for LHCE design/optimization for high-performance Si anodes is still not well understood.  

Similar to the case of Li metal anodes, lithiated Si anodes experience a large volume change and are highly reactive with the electrolyte. However, unlike in the case of Li metal anodes where 2 − 3 times of excess Li is typically used, no excess Li source is available in Si anodes. To accelerate the application of Si-based anode materials, it is critical to develop novel electrolytes that can form a stable SEI and enable high Coulombic e ﬃ ciency (CE) (ideally >99.9%) cycling of Si anodes. In this work, a series of LHCEs based on EC/EMC carbonate solvents have been developed for Si anodes. Electrolytes with di ﬀ erent salt concentrations and carbonate solvents to  ﬂ uorinated diluent molar ratios are screened for optimized performance. The correlation between the elec- trode-electrolyte interphase and ion coordination structure and di ﬀ usion kinetics were revealed. The results indicate that the salt concentration and the solvent-to-diluent molar ratio have optimized values for the best performance of the next- generation Si-Gr || NMC532 cells. The LHCE with 1.8 M LiFSI concentration and a 1:2 molar ratio of carbonate solvents and  ﬂ uorinated diluents has the highest proportion of solvent-separated ion pairs and contact ion pairs and fastest ion di ﬀ usion among all the LHCEs. It enables a stable electrode- electrolyte interphase in both the Si anode and high-voltage cathode (NMC532 at 4.5 V). These stable interphases exhibit good ionic conductivity and low overvoltage and lead to high CE ( ≈ 99.8%) cycling of Li || NMC532 cells at 4.5 V. This work also presents an in-depth understanding of LHCEs and reveals fundamental mechanisms behind the optimization of electro- lyte composition.

The formation of the SEI/CEI relies on the decomposition of the electrolyte at the cathode/anode surface at appropriate potentials. E-101 has a very low ion di ﬀ usion coe ﬃ cient. The decomposition mainly comes from LiFSI salts and FEC-EC/ EMC solvents close to the electrode surface. In contrast, the existence of a large amount of BTFE, a  ﬂ uorinated ether contained in E-102, E-104, and E-106, mitigated the contact of LiFSI-EC/EMC-FEC with the electrode surface. Hence, the species decomposed to form CEI/SEI layers in E-102, E-104, and E-106, which not only depends on the electrolyte components near the electrode surface but it is also a ﬀ ected by the ion coordination structure and di ﬀ usion kinetics. It is natural to expect the variation of the LiF ratio in the CEI/SEI with di ﬀ erent electrolyte compositions. E-104 has the highest proportion of SSIP and CIP and the fastest di ﬀ usion via carrier di ﬀ usion. Therefore, the electrolyte decomposition at the electrode surface mainly comes from the SSIP and CIP species that contain some BTFE. Hence, the composition of the CEI/ SEI in E-104 and E-101 are similar, as is their performance with E-104 having higher speci ﬁ c capacity and rate perform- ance.  

The electrochemical impedance spectra (EIS) ( Figure S10 ) of Si-Gr || NMC532 cells were measured and further analyzed by distribution of relaxation time (DRT) analysis ( Figure 6 ) to corroborate the ion di ﬀ usion kinetics. The numbers (1, 10, 20, and 50) marked in the left upper corner of each panel indicates the cycle number when the EIS were measured. As shown in Figure S10 , the cell impedance after the 1st cycle in the order from highest to lowest is E-101 > E-102 > E-baseline > E-104 > E-106. After 50 cycles, the impedance from highest to lowest is E-baseline > E-101 > E-106 > E-102 > E-104. Therefore, we conclude that the electrolyte E-104 has a very low initial impedance value and the lowest impedance increase, which is consistent with the battery performance shown in  Figure 1 . DRT analysis can e ﬀ ectively unveil the details of electro- chemical processes (i.e., new SEI/CEI and charge transfer at the evolving interface) that the EIS spectra are unable to reveal easily. DRT spectra were calculated from the obtained impedance spectra to make a clear separation of electro- chemical processes with di ﬀ erent time constants. As shown in Figure 6 , four distinct peaks are observed in the frequency range of 10 4   to 0.1 Hz (e.g., the relaxation time range of 10 − 4 to 10 s), which can be ascribed to Li-ion migration ( R SEI ) in two di ﬀ erent SEIs (ca. 10 4 − 10 3   Hz) and corresponding charge transfer processes of  R ct  (ca. 10 2 − 0.1 Hz). DRT analysis of the Si-Gr || NMC532 full cells using the baseline electrolyte showed a substantial increase in the second peak of  R ct  and a higher  R ct compared to that obtained in LHCEs. DRT analysis of the full cell using E-104 showed a clear decrease in the  ﬁ rst  R ct  as well as smaller  R ct  and  R SEI  compared to E-101 and E-102. It suggests that the di ﬀ usion kinetics of Li ions in the E-104/ electrode interphase increases as the amount of BTFE increases. However, the full cell using E-106 exhibited an increase in both peaks of  R SEI  and  R ct  compared to E-104 at the 50th cycle, which means that the transport of Li ions becomes slower when the molar ratio of BTFE to solvent decreases to 3. This indicates that E-104 can protect both the cathode and anode. This is consistent with the remarkable improvement of cycling performance and suppression of structure swelling of the Si anodes in  Figures 1  and  3 .  

# CONCLUSION  

In this work, LIFSI-EC/EMC-FEC-BTFE-based LHCEs have been developed, and the formulation has been optimized for Si-based anodes. An LHCE with a controlled overall salt concentration of 1.8 M and a molar ratio of 1:2 between carbonate solvents and  ﬂ uorinated diluents enables very good battery performance in terms of speci ﬁ c capacity, cycle life, and electrode swelling. The LHCE has the highest proportion of SSIPs and CIPs and the fastest ion di ﬀ usion. The Li-ion coordination structure and di ﬀ usion kinetics are closely related to the CEI and SEI quality and hence battery performance. Our work reveals a fundamental understanding on tuning the electrolyte composition of LHCE systems and electrode- electrolyte interphases. It also provides guidance for future electrolyte design.  

# EXPERIMENTAL SECTION  

Preparation of Electrolytes and Electrodes.  Battery grade LiPF 6 , EC, EMC, and FEC were purchased from BASF. LiFSI of battery grade was obtained from Nippon Shokubai and dried overnight at 120  ° C before use. BTFE (99%) was ordered from SynQuest Laboratories and dried with molecular sieves. The Si-Gr composite anode was provided by the CAMP facility. It was composed of 15 wt % nanosilicon (Paraclete Energy), 73% graphite (Hitachi MagE3) as an active material, 2 wt % C45 carbon (Timcal) as a conducting agent, and 10 wt % LiPAA (H 2 O solution, LiOH titrated) as a binder. The electrode disks with a diameter of 15 mm and an average mass loading of 3.0 mg cm − 2   were prepared and vacuum dried at 150  ° C overnight. The cathode consisted of 90 wt % NMC532 (Toda), 5 wt % C45 carbon (Timcal), and a 5 wt % 5130 polyvinylidene di ﬂ uoride (PVDF, Solvay) binder provided by the CAMP facility. The electrodes with a diameter of 14 mm and a mass loading of 11.4 mg cm − 2   were punched and vacuum dried at 120  ° C for 12 h. The LHCEs were prepared by the introduction of a dilute solvent (e.g., BTFE) to HCE (6 M LiFSI in EC/EMC, 5 wt % FEC, named as E-101 in this work). The LHCEs with EC-EMC/BTFE molar ratios of 1:1 (denoted as E-102), 1:2 (denoted as E-104), and 1:3 (denoted as E-106) have LiFSI concentrations ranging from 2.9 to 1.2 M. The conventional electrolyte of 1.2 M LiPF 6  in EC/EMC (3:7 by wt) with 10 wt % FEC, denoted as E-baseline, is also evaluated for comparison.  

Electrochemical Tests.  Electrochemical tests were performed using CR2032-type coin cells on Land BT2000 battery testers at 30 ° C in environmental chambers. The electrolyte amount is controlled at 40  μ L for all coin cell full cells. A  ﬂ ooded electrolyte was used in half cell tests. Al-clad stainless-steel cans (purchased from MTI) with an additional Al foil disk (19 mm in diameter) were used to avoid the corrosion of stainless steel by LiFSI-based electrolytes when the cells were charged over 4.0 V. A polyethylene (PE) separator was used for the conventional carbonate electrolyte and LHCEs, while a Celgard 3501 separator with big pores was used for the HCE for good wettability. The voltage window for full cell tests is 3.0 − 4.1 V. The full cells were cycled at 0.05C for 3 cycles and then cycled at 0.33C (1C = 180 mA h/g). Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurements were performed on a 1255B Solartron frequency response analyzer and a 1287 electrochemical workstation in the frequency range from 100 kHz to 1 mHz. The perturbation amplitude is  ± 5 mV. The DRT method was used to further analyze EIS data to separate information of the charge transfer and SEI. The DRT value from EIS data is a mathematically ill-posed problem. DRT data were interoperated by MatlabR2017a with a toolbox of DRTTOOLS developed by Professor Francesco Ciucci ’ s group.   DRTTOOLS is free to use and available under the GNU license from the website of https://sites.google.com/site/drttools/ .  

Characterization.  The 17 O liquid-state NMR spectra of the solvents and electrolytes were collected using a 600 MHz NMR spectrometer (Agilent, USA) with a 5 mm HX probe (Doty Scienti ﬁ c, USA) tuned to 81.27 MHz. All other NMR experiments including pulsed- ﬁ eld gradient nuclear magnetic resonance (PFG-NMR) were performed with a 500 MHz NMR spectrometer (Agilent, USA) with a 5 mm dual-broadband probe. The di ﬀ usion coe ﬃ cients of Li + , FSI − , and EC and EMC were measured using   7 Li, 19 F, and   1 H PFG-NMR, respectively, using a vendor-supplied PFG sequence (Dbppste in VNMRJ, Agilent, USA). The details for the PFG-NMR measurements can be found from previous reports.   The ionic conductivity was evaluated using a BioLogic MCS 10 system with cells, which is made of two parallel Pt electrodes. The electrolyte viscosity was measured on a Brook ﬁ eld DVII+ Pro viscometer, which was equipped with a circulating bath for temperature control. The cell constants were measured using standard solutions from Oakton. Morphologies of the Si anodes were performed on a Helios focused ion beam SEM at 5.0 kV. The cross sections were obtained by cutting the electrodes with a razor blade. XPS analysis of the electrodes was performed on a Physical Electronic Quantera scanning X-ray microprobe with a focused monochromatic Al K α  X-ray source. For the measurements of XPS and SEM, the cycled electrodes were washed with a pure dimethyl carbonate (DMC) solvent overnight to remove the remaining electrolytes and then dried naturally in a glovebox. The electrode samples were sealed in an airtight container in the glovebox before being transferred for XPS characterization.  

An FEI Helios Dual Beam system was used to prepare the NMC lamellar for TEM characterization. The beam is operated at 2 − 30 kV. A randomly selected NMC secondary particle was coated with a Pt capping layer of  ∼ 2  μ m thick. The covered NMC cathode with a width of  ∼ 3  μ m was then lift out and brought to the TEM grid. After that, the as-lifted out cubic was thinned to reach an electron transparency thickness at an operation voltage of 30 kV. For the initial surface polish, the ion beam voltage was then reduced to 5 kV. After that, by using a 2 kV, the  ﬁ nal polishing was done to remove the surface damaged layers. After the 2 kV polishing, the thickness of surface damage was generally reduced to be less than 2 nm. The as- prepared NMC lamellar is ready for TEM measurements by a JEOL JEM-ARM200CF. For the STEM imaging, the convergence angle was set to be 20.6 mrad, and the signals were collected within the range from 90 to 370 mrad for HAADF.  

Theoretical Calculation.  All-atom MD simulations were carried out by GROMACS.   These systems were  ﬁ rst pre-equilibrated at 298 K and 1 atm for 10 ns in an  NPT  ensemble. Then, 100 ns NVT simulations were performed at 298 K for data collection. The details about theoretical calculation are described in the  Supporting Information .
