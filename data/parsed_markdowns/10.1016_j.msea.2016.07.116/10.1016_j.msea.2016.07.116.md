# Effect of heat treatments on microstructure evolution and  mechanical properties of blended Nickel-based superalloys  powders fabricated by laser powder deposition  

Y. Tian a , A. Gontcharov b , R. Gauvin a , P. Lowden b  and M. Brochu a \*   a Department of Mining and Materials Engineering, McGill University, 3610  University Street, Wong Building, Montreal, Quebec, Canada, H3A 0C5  b Liburdi Turbine Services, 400 Highway 6 North, Dundas, ON, Canada, L9H 7K4  

Corresponding author: Mathieu Brochu  Email address: mathieu.brochu@mcgill.ca  

Abstract: Microstructure evolution and mechanical properties of sample produced by  laser powder deposition using blended powders of Mar M247 and Amdry DF3 were  studied in the as-deposited and heat treated conditions. In the as-deposited condition,  continuous (Cr, W) borides and γ-Ni 3 B eutectics were observed to segregate in the  interdendritic regions. After the two-step aging treatment, the low melting point γ- Ni 3 B eutectics re-melted. Small size γ-γ   eutectics and discrete W borides formed  during re-solidification. The γ   strengthening phase that precipitated out  homogeneously in the dendritic cores had an average diameter of 102±26 nm. When  annealing was attempted before the aging treatment, both γ-Ni 3 B eutectics and the  surrounding dendrites re-melted, contributing to the coarsening of the γ-γ   eutectics  and the discrete W borides. Gamma prime precipitates with average diameter of  106±30 nm were observed. This microstructure evolution from the re-melting of γ- Ni 3 B eutectics and development of γ-γ   eutectics eradicated the risk of strain-age  cracks during the two tested heat treatments by backfilling. The coarsening kinetics of  γ   precipitates in the two heat treated conditions was modeled and showed a good  agreement with the experimental observation. The microhardness results showed that  the as-deposited condition possessed the highest hardness attributed to the continuous  feature of the secondary phases, while the two heat treated conditions presented  similar hardness owing to the similar size and volume fraction of γ   precipitates. The  tensile test results at both ambient and elevated temperatures indicated that the two  types of heat treatments increased the tensile properties, especially ductility.  

# 1.   Introduction  

Nickel-based superalloys have been widely applied for high temperature components  such as gas turbine blades and vanes owing to their good tensile properties, corrosion  resistance and oxidation resistance [1]. The repairing techniques, mainly based on  fusion welding process, are highly used due to the high cost for replacement of gas  turbine components. However, nickel-base superalloys are prone to hot cracking  (solidification cracking [2] and HAZ liquation cracking [3]) during welding  solidification and strain-age cracking during heat treatment (for γ   based superalloys)  [1]. To circumvent the weldability problem, Alexandrov  et.al  [4] proposed one  strategy to heal the cracks through backfilling effect by sufficient eutectic phase. In  this context, Liburdi Turbine Services developed the self-healing laser beam welding  technology [5, 6] by using blended powders that are typical for wide gap brazing. In a  previous research [7], the authors have studied the microstructure and the rapid  solidification behavior of as-deposited sample made by laser powder deposition from  blended Mar M247 and Amdry DF3 powders. A large amount of eutectics were  formed during the terminal solidification stage and were sufficient to support the  backfilling effect discussed above. The solidification front velocity was calculated by  the measurement of dendrite tip radius from KGT model, and was correlated with  solid solution supersaturation and partial solute trapping.  

Of all the repairing techniques, laser powder deposition process has become more and  more popular [8-10], due to its ability of re-manufacturing damaged gas turbine  blades with little distortion and dilution as well as providing near net-shape  construction. After deposition, the heat treatments are usually utilized to precipitate  the strengthening phases and homogenize the microstructure along the deposits.  Significant research has been conducted to study the influence of heat treatment on  microstructure and mechanical properties of laser additive manufactured γ   nickel- based superalloys in the recent years [11-15]. Li and Wang [11] revealed the  microstructure and mechanical properties of Rene   41 made by laser melting  deposition after aging process and found along the longitudinal direction that the  build exhibited promising high temperature mechanical properties at 800°C. Vilaro  et.al  [12] proved that standard heat treatment enabled to solutionize the out-of- equilibrium as-processed microstructure and precipitate the strengthening phases in  

selective laser manufactured Nimonic 263. Bi  et.al  [14] observed large fraction of  coarsening γ   precipitates on post heat-treated IN100 sample fabricated by micro-laser  aided additive manufacturing. They also obtained ultimate tensile strength and yield  strength in the heat treated conditions at both room temperature and 600˚C above the  minimum requirements specified in aerospace material specification AMS 5397 for  cast IN100. Nevertheless, the above literature considered only standard compositions  and thus neither the effect of using B-contained braze powder in blended nickel-based  superalloy powder manufactured by laser powder deposition, nor the strategy to  address potential strain-age cracking issue during heat treatment of γ   nickel-based  superalloy.  

Thus, the objective of this paper is to understand the effect of two different types of  heat treatments on the microstructure evolution and the mechanical properties of  blended nickel-based superalloys powders made by laser powder deposition process.  The investigation of the microstructure evolution during heat treatments of an alloy  that favored crack healing was assessed. The coarsening kinetics of the γ   precipitates  during heat treatments was also evaluated.  

# 2.   Experimental Procedure  

The samples for the present research were produced by Liburdi Turbine Services.  Laser powder deposition was made using blended powders of 80 wt% Mar M247 and  20 wt% Amdry DF3 on Inconel 738 substrate. The nominal compositions of the  materials used are given in Table 1. The powder size distribution was measured by a  LA-920 Horiba laser particle size analyzer. The powder morphology and powder size  distribution curves of Mar M247 and Amdry DF3 are presented in Fig. 1. Fig. 1a and  b depict that the spherical Mar M247 powders have nearly homogeneous size  distribution with an average diameter of 65 μm; while Fig. 1c and d shows that the  spherical Amdry DF3 powders have a relatively large size distribution with the  average diameter of 76 μm. The two powders were blended using a V-shape mixer for  24 hours prior to the deposition process.  

The laser powder deposits consisted of about 50 layers built on the substrate, with  approximate dimensions of 8.5 mm in height, 6 mm in width, and 50 mm in length.  

During the deposition, the laser head was oscillated with an amplitude of ± 3-3.5 mm  at a speed of approximately 17 mm/s; the scanning speed was about 1.5 mm/s; the  laser beam power range used was 420-475 W; and the powder feed rate was kept  between 3.5-4 g/min. Argon was employed as a shielding gas during the deposition.  

After deposition, one sample was subjected to a standard primary aging at a  temperature of 1120˚C for two hours followed by a secondary aging at a temperature  of 845˚C for 24 hours. The other tested heat treatment was a standard annealing at a  temperature of 1205˚C for two hours, and followed by the same two-step aging  process. The heat treatments were performed in a VAH 3448 HV vacuum furnace  made by VAC AERO International Inc. Regarding the heat treatment cycles, the  heating rate was 15 ˚C/min to the target temperature, the cooling ramp from primary  aging to secondary aging or from annealing to primary aging was fixed as 15 ˚C/min,  argon quenching was used after secondary aging. Both annealing and aging processes  are standard for repairing of IGT engine components manufactured of IN738 and  GTD111 superalloys [6].  

The as-deposited and heat treated samples were sectioned along the transverse  direction, followed by sequential grinding steps up to 800 grit using silicon carbide  abrasive papers, and standard diamond polishing steps up to 1 μm. Polished samples  were electrolytic ally etched in a solution of 12ml H 3 PO 4  + 40ml HNO 3  + 48ml H 2 SO 4   at 6V for 5s to reveal the microstructure [16]. The microstructures of all the samples  were characterized using a SU3500 scanning electron microscope (SEM) equipped  for electron dispersive spectrometer (EDS) analysis, and a high resolution SU8000  FE-SEM for high magnification microstructural observations. Both secondary  electron mode (referred to as SE) and backscattered electron mode (referred to as BSE)  were employed to observe the morphology and chemical contrast of the different  phases, respectively. Five different regions were chosen in order to measure the  dendrite arm spacing and the area fractions of the different phases using ImageJ  image analysis software [17] and the average results are provided in this discussion.  Quantitative chemical analysis in both the inter-dendritic regions and the dendritic  cores was obtained using a JEOL 8900 electron probe microanalysis (EPMA). The  reported values are the average of at least five measurements. Interaction volumes of  

16 μm 3  and 23 μm 3  for EDS and EPMA respectively were calculated using Monte  Carlo simulation from Casino [18].  

The microhardness for different conditions were measured by a CM-100AT Clark  Microhardness Indenter with a Vickers hardness scale using 200-g load. Five different  locations were chosen to obtain the microhardness values for each condition, and the  average results were reported, the deviations of the data is within 5 %. Tensile tests  were conducted along the longitudinal direction in order to avoid heterogeneity  problem along the building direction [10]. Based on ASTM E-8, the layers with 2 mm  in thickness in upper section and adjacent to the substrate were removed before  machining the subsided tensile samples. Prior to test, the samples were subjected to a  radiographic inspection according to ASTM E-1032, and samples with discontinuities  exceeding 0.1 mm in size were discarded. Five samples for each condition were  tensile tested and the average results are presented. The deviations of ultimate tensile  strength (UTS) and elongation were within 5 and 10 %, respectively. Mechanical  properties of materials were compared to that of equiaxed Mar M247 superalloy in  aged condition [19].  

# 3.   Results and Discussion  

# 3.1   Microstructure Evolution  

# 3.1.1   Microstructure of As-deposited Condition  

Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) was employed to reveal the microstructure and  the results are shown in Fig. 2. Fig. 2a depicts that there is no crack in the deposits. It  exhibits the dendritic microstructure possessing average dendrite arm spacing of  4.5±0.8 μm in the deposits. The dendrites grew along the vertical direction of the  deposits. The microstructure presents continuous secondary phases segregated in the  inter-dendritic regions. Fig. 2b shows that the secondary phases consist mainly of  eutectics (circle A in Fig. 2b) and discrete phases (circle B in Fig. 2b). From image  analysis, it was measured that the area fractions of the eutectics and the discrete  phases are 8.4% and 4.3%, respectively. This high amount of eutectics arising from a  higher volume fraction liquid at terminal solidification permitted backfilling and  contributed to the development of crack free samples in the deposits.  

EDS maps of the major alloying elements (Ni, Cr, W, Al, Ti and B) were acquired in  order to observe the elemental constitution of the different phases in the deposits and  the results are shown in Fig. 3. It is shown in Fig. 3 that the γ matrix is rich in Ni, Cr  and Al, and the eutectic phases (see circle A in Fig. 3a for an example) are mainly  composed of Ni, Ti and B, depleted with Cr, W and Al, and identified as Ni-B rich  eutectics. It demonstrates that this high amount of eutectics came from the addition of  B, which represent significant difference after solidification of traditional Mar M247  [20]. The discrete phases (see circle B in Fig. 3a for an example) are mainly  composed of Cr, W and B with a lack of Ni and Al, and identified as (Cr, W) rich  discrete borides. Moreover, some fine carbide like phases (see circle C in Fig. 3a),  mainly concentrate in the inter-dendritic regions, are found to be enriched in W and Ti  and depleted of Ni and Cr, corresponding to the typical elemental constitution for MC  carbides [1].  

EPMA was performed to quantitatively obtain the compositions of the secondary  phases and dendritic cores in the deposits, and the results are presented in Table 2.  The EPMA results are in good agreement with the EDS mapping results shown earlier.  Results from Table 2 depict that Ni rich eutectics are ascribed to γ-Ni 3 B eutectics with  a B content of roughly 3.5 wt% (around 15.5 at%). The measured composition for the  eutectics is a combination of the pure eutectic phases and the surrounding γ matrix  due to the interaction volume of around 23 μm 3 for EPMA, so the overall B content in  eutectics is lower than the nominal 25 at% B in pure Ni 3 B eutectic. Table 2 also  confirms that the (Cr, W) discrete borides are (Cr, W)B with a B content of roughly  7.9 wt% (around 45 at%) [20]. A mass balance was calculated among γ solid solution,  γ-Ni 3 B eutectics and (Cr, W) discrete borides, and the result was correlated well with  the nominal composition.  

# 3.1.2   Microstructure of Two-Step Aged Condition  

The microstructure after the two-step aging treatment is revealed in Fig. 4. It depicts  from Fig. 4a that no crack is observed in the deposits. It exhibits that the  microstructure in the deposits presents a similar dendritic structure to the as-deposited  sample. Higher magnification SE micrograph shown in Fig. 4b suggests the absence  of γ-Ni 3 B eutectics in the inter-dendritic regions. It is understood from Ni-B binary  phase diagram [21] that the melting temperature of γ-Ni 3 B eutectics is 1093˚C and  

lower than the temperature used during primary aging (1120˚C). This results in the re- melting of γ-Ni 3 B eutectics corresponding to the observed microstructure. The BSE  micrograph in same location is shown in Fig. 4c and depicts that different phases with  different chemical contrast agglomerated with each other and segregated in the inter- dendritic regions.  

EDS maps of the major alloying elements (Ni, Cr, W, Al, Ti and B) were acquired to  observe the elemental constitution of the different phases in the aged condition, and  the results are presented in Fig. 5. As depicted, the γ matrix is rich in Ni, Cr and Al.  The discrete (Cr, W) rich borides (see circle A for an example) are mainly composed  of Cr and W, with a lack of Ni and Al. The MC carbides (see circle D for an example)  are enriched in W and Ti, and absent of Cr and Ni. The MC carbides have larger size  than those in the as-deposited condition, which is attributed to the coarsening effect  during the aging process. These three phases in the aged condition have similar  chemical constitution than in the as-deposited condition. However, two types of  newly formed phases are also revealed in Fig. 5. The first one, see circle B for an  example, is mainly composed of Ni and Al, and depleted with Cr, W and B, which is  the typical elemental constitution for γ-γ   eutectics [1]. The other discrete phase, see  circle C for an example, is mainly composed of W and B with a lack of Ni, Cr, Ti and  Al, and is identified as W rich discrete borides. As discussed above, the primary aging  temperature is higher than the melting temperature of γ-Ni 3 B eutectics. This resulted  in the disappearance of the elemental segregation existing in the as-deposited  condition by re-melting the γ-Ni 3 B eutectics. The primary aging time of two hours  was sufficient to re-melt all γ-Ni 3 B eutectics, leading to the formation of γ-γ   eutectics  and W discrete borides during the re-solidification. The reaction for this phase  transformation is shown as follows: γ + Ni 3 B     γ + γ   + W(B). Other work have also  shown that γ-γ   eutectics can be observed to form during relatively slow solidification  rate [22, 23]. Advantageously, the re-melting of γ-Ni 3 B eutectics and formation of γ- γ   eutectics is capable of backfilling the potential strain-age cracks formed during the  aging process, and this phase transformation has not been observed during heat  treatment of superalloys without B addition before [1]. As a result, no trace of cracks  was observed in the deposits in the aged sample.  

EPMA results of the discrete (Cr, W) borides and the dendritic cores in the aged  condition are given in Table 3. The results indicate a similar compositional  distribution to the previously presented EDS maps. Unfortunately, the proper signal  interpretation of the γ-γ   eutectics and W discrete borides failed due to the small size  of γ-γ   eutectics and W discrete borides. The similar composition of the discrete (Cr,  W) borides between the aged condition and the as-deposited condition shown in Table  2 suggests that (Cr, W)B borides remain unchanged after aging treatment.

# 3.1.3   Microstructure of Annealed and Aged Condition  

The microstructure after annealing and two-step aging treatment is shown in Fig. 7.  The deposits are free of cracks. Fig. 7a depicts a non-dendritic microstructure,  suggesting that the high temperature annealing treatment (1205˚C) re-melted not only  γ-Ni 3 B eutectics, but also the surrounding dendrites. This local re-melting with large  area also led to the formation of large size γ-γ   eutectics and discrete phases. Fig. 7b  shows the γ-γ   eutectics with typical umbrella-shape morphology [33]. Fig. 7c  indicates that the near cuboidal γ   precipitates, with a diameter of 106±30 nm (see Fig.  7d) and an average area fraction of 31.7±2.1%, are evenly distributed in the γ solid  solution upon annealing and aging. It was obtained from DSC measurement that the  solvus temperature of γ   precipitates is 1168.6˚C, so only the primary aging process  contributed to the coarsening effect of γ   precipitates, explaining the similar size and  morphology of γ   precipitates after annealing and aging treatment to the aged sample.  

EDS maps of the major alloying elements (Ni, Cr, W, Al, Ti and B) were employed to  observe the elemental constitution of the discrete phases and γ-γ   eutectics, and the  results are shown in Fig. 8 and Fig. 9, respectively. Fig. 8 depicts that the γ matrix is  mainly composed of Ni, Cr and Al, (Cr, W) discrete borides (see circle A for an  example) enriched with Cr, W and B, and depleted with Ni and Al, and MC carbides  (see circle C for an example) which are rich in W and Ti and poor in Ni and Cr. The  MC carbides in the annealed and aged sample are larger than those in the aged sample,  as shown from the comparison between Fig. 5 and Fig. 8, which is owing to the  coarsening effect during the additional annealing treatment. Moreover, W rich  discrete borides (see circle B for an example), with enrichment of W and B, and  absence of Ni, Cr, Al and Ti, are the large discrete phases shown before. These large  size W rich discrete borides present similar chemical constitution to the small size  ones in the aged condition. The EDS maps of γ-γ   eutectics are presented in Fig. 9  (see region A in Fig. 9 a), where it indicates that γ-γ   eutectics are mainly composed  of Ni and Al, and depleted with Cr and W, showing similar chemical constitution to  the aged sample presented in Fig. 5. Moreover, similar as the aged condition, the re- melting of the γ-Ni 3 B eutectics and the formation of the γ-γ   eutectics during  annealing and aging treatment could heal the potential strain-age cracks by backfilling  

effect, contributing to the crack free build in the deposits of the annealed and aged  sample.  

EPMA results of the compositions of the different phases after annealing and aging  treatment are given in Table 4. The EPMA results are in good agreement with the  EDS mapping results presented earlier. Results from Table 4 indicate that both γ  matrix and (Cr, W)B discrete borides in annealed and aged condition have similar  chemical compositions to the as-deposited and aged conditions, as shown in Table 2  and Table 3, respectively. Moreover, the γ-γ   eutectics possess lower Cr content and  higher Al content than the γ matrix. EPMA results also show that the W discrete  borides are proved to be WB borides with B content of around 5.6 wt% (around 47  at%). The area fractions of (Cr, W) discrete borides and W rich discrete borides are  calculated to be around 4.2% and 3.1%, and it is in good agreement with the B mass  balance of the as-deposited condition.  

# 3.2   Mechanical Properties  

# 3.2.1.   Micro-hardness  

The average microhardness values in the as-deposited condition, the aged condition,  and annealed and aged condition are measured to be 550, 450 and 448 HV,  respectively. The hardness in the as-deposited condition is mainly controlled by the  continuous secondary phases in the inter-dendritic regions, while the γ   strengthening  precipitates are believed to dominate the hardness in both heat treated conditions. The  reduction of microhardness after heat treatment is owing to the breaking down of the  continuous feature of the secondary phases by the elimination of elemental  segregation as well as the coarsening of the γ   precipitates. The microhardness of both  heat treated samples is similar associated with the similar size and morphology of the  γ   precipitates. It was reported that the hardness of equiaxed Mar M247 superalloy in  the aged condition was about 450 HV [19]. So the hardness of the both heat treated  conditions is comparable with that of equiaxed Mar M247 superalloy in the aged  condition.  

# 3.2.2.   Tensile properties  

The ultimate tensile strength (UTS) and elongation of the different conditions at both  room temperature and 926°C are presented in Table 5. The corresponding  fractography for each condition is shown in Fig. 10. Table 5 indicates that, at room  temperature, the two types of heat treated samples possess better tensile properties  than in the as-deposited sample. The average UTS for as-deposited, aged, and  annealed and aged conditions were 787, 1183, and 1142 MPa, respectively. The two  heat treated samples have similar UTS owing to the similar microstructure. The  elongation has a more severe difference, where the average elongation values of 0.9,  5.0 and 6.5 % for as-deposited condition, aged condition, and annealed and aged  condition, respectively, were measured. At room temperature, the fractography of the  as-deposited sample (see Fig. 10a) clearly shows intergranular feature, where the  fracture propagated along the dendrites. The fractography of both heat treated samples  (see Fig. 10c and e) indicates dimple fracture, which explains the larger elongation.  The dimples in the aged sample and the annealed and aged sample possess average  diameters of 2.4±1.2 and 4.5±1.6 μm, respectively. Fig. 10e depicts that the large size  secondary phases (see circle A in Fig. 10e for an example) can be observed in  annealed and aged condition, which corresponds to the microstructure as shown in Fig.  8a.  

The UTS and elongation for equiaxed Mar M247 in the aged condition at ambient  temperature are 977 MPa and 5.9 % [19], respectively. The two heat treated  conditions have higher UTS than the equiaxed Mar M247 in the aged condition. The  elongation of the two heat treated conditions is similar to the equiaxed Mar M247 in  the aged condition. It demonstrates that the tensile properties of the two types of heat  treated conditions are comparable to the equiaxed Mar M247 in the aged condition at  room temperature.  

Table 5 also depicts that at 926°C, the average UTS for as-deposited condition, aged  condition, and annealed and aged condition is 622, 578, and 537 MPa, respectively.  The corresponding average elongation is 3.0, 12.0 and 11.0 %, respectively. The UTS  of the as-deposited sample is slightly higher than that of the two heat treated samples  attributed to the continuous feature of secondary phases. However, its elongation  value is much lower than the other two samples. At 926°C, the fractography of as- deposited condition (see Fig. 10b) exhibits no formation of dimples after fracture,  

explaining its low elongation value. While the fractography of two heat treated  conditions at the same temperature (see Fig. 10d and f) clearly shows dimples that are  responsible for their higher elongation values. The average diameters of dimples for  the aged sample, and the annealed and aged sample at 926°C, as depicted in Fig. 10d  and f, are 3.4±1.4 and 5.6±2.1 μm, respectively. The large size secondary phases, as  shown in circle A in Fig. 10f for an example, are observed in annealed and aged  condition and consistent with microstructure observation (see Fig. 8a).  

The UTS and elongation for equiaxed Mar M247 in the aged condition at 926°C are  547 MPa and 6.4 %, respectively. It depicts that the two types of heat treated  conditions have similar UTS to equiaxed Mar M247 in the aged condition at elevated  temperature. However, the two heat treated conditions possess much higher  elongation values than the equiaxed Mar M247 in the aged condition at the same  temperature, showing their promising mechanical properties at elevated temperature.  Based on both microstructure evolution and mechanical properties, this work  demonstrates the compatible output of the two types of heat treatments with the  blended Mar M247 and Amdry DF3 powders through laser powder deposition.  

# 4.   Conclusion  

In the current research, the effect of different heat treatments on microstructure  evolution and mechanical properties of blended nickel-based powders manufactured  by laser powder deposition was investigated. The main findings can be summarized as  follows:  

1.   In the as-deposited condition, continuous secondary phases including γ-Ni 3 B  eutectics and discrete (Cr, W)B borides segregated at the inter-dendritic regions.  The high amount of B rich eutectics resulted in the crack free deposits. 

 2.   After two-step aging treatment, γ-Ni 3 B eutectics were re-melted into small size γ-γ    eutectics and discrete W rich borides in the inter-dendritic regions.  

 3.   In annealed and aged condition, both γ-Ni 3 B eutectics and surrounding dendrites  were re-melted, resulting in non-dendritic microstructure with coarsened γ-γ    eutectics and discrete WB borides.  

 4.   The two types of heat treatment removed the potential strain-age cracking problem  from the phase transformation of eutectic re-melting and development.  

5.   Near cuboidal γ   precipitates with similar size and morphology were observed in  the two types of heat treated samples, leading to the similar microhardness and  tensile properties in these two conditions.  

6.   The two types of heat treatments increased tensile properties of as-deposited  condition at both ambient and elevated temperature, especially with respect to the  ductility.  

7.   The two types of heat treated conditions have similar mechanical properties at both  ambient and elevated temperature, and they both show superior mechanical  properties than the equiaxed Mar M47 in the aged condition. It demonstrates the  feasibility of applying the two heat treatments for the blended Mar M247 and  Amdry DF3 powders fabricated by laser powder deposition.
