From a material-design standpoint, one feasible design method for producing low-bandgap polymers is to construct alternating donor–acceptor (D–A) copolymers, because the light-absorption ability and energy levels of a copolymer can be readily tuned by controlling the intramolecular charge transfer from the donor unit to the acceptor unit.   Re- cently, we demonstrated that naphtho[1,2-c:5,6-c]bis(1,2,5- thiadiazole) (NT) was very promising as a strong acceptor building block for D–A polymers.   Compared with 2,1,3-b enzo thi a diaz ole (BT), NT has an enlarged planar aro- matic structure that contains two fused BT rings, which facil- itates stronger interchain packing of the resulting polymers and enhances the carrier mobility, which consequently leads to a high PCE. Several PSCs based on NT have shown ex- cellent photovoltaic performance. For example, a conjugated polymer that contained NT and benz odi thi oph ene (BDT), PBDT-DTNT, exhibited a much-improved PCE to 6.0% compared to the analogous polymer with BT as an accept- or.   By using a conjugated polyelectrolyte interfacial layer within an inverted-solar-cell device, the PCE of PBDT- DTNT was further enhanced to 8.4%.   More recently, Osaka et al. reported a series of polymers that contained NT and different donor units; the highest PCE of 8.2% was achieved with active lays of 300 nm thickness.  

Over the past decade, polymer solar cells (PSCs) have at- tracted extensive attention owing to their light weight, low cost, and great potential for the fabrication of large-area flexible devices.   Significant improvements in the power- conversion efficiencies (PCEs) of PSCs have been achieved, owing to the invention of new donor polymers and to the in- troduction of new interfacial materials and device struc- tures.   Recently, high PCEs of 8–9% for single-junction devices   and 10% for tandem devices   have been re- ported. Nevertheless, to meet the requirements for the large-scale commercialization of PSCs, the further develop- ment of high-efficiency solar cell materials is still of great importance.

In addition, the introduction of spacer groups between the donor and acceptor units can also effectively tune the properties of a polymer to generate high-performance solar cells. In most of the previous reports, electron-rich thio- phene was selected as the spacer group, thereby resulting in a red-shift of the absorption spectrum of the polymer and, hence, improved light-harvesting and device perfor- mance.   Recently, it was found that the bithiophene spacer exhibited even better properties than the thiophene spacer.   By adding another thiophene moiety into the spacer group, the absorption spectrum of the polymer would typically be further red-shifted, owing to the longer conjuga- tion length, which is often favorable for a high short-circuit current ( J sc ).   In addition, bithiophene could effectively en- hance the absorption coefficient of a conjugated polymer and also the charge-carrier mobility, thus leading to an im- proved PCE. Wang et al. studied a series of D–A copoly- mers that contained different thiophene spacers. On replac- ing the thiophene spacer by a bithiophene spacer, a huge improvement in the PCEs from 1.49% to 5.05% was ach- ieved, owing to enhanced charge-carrier mobility.   Further- more, it is also feasible to introduce alkyl side-chains onto the bithiophene spacer to improve the solubility of the re- sulting polymers; as a result, high-molecular-weight poly- mers with improved properties can be achieved.   There- fore, a combination of the outstanding properties of the NT unit and the bithiophene spacer may lead to the develop- ment of a new series of promising donor materials for high- performance PSCs.

# Optical and Electrochemical Properties  

To investigate the photophysical properties of the polymers, UV/Vis absorption spectra of the polymers as solutions in  o - DCB and as solid films were recorded (Figure 2, Table 2). All of the D–A polymers exhibited two absorption bands both in solution and as thin films: short-wavelength peaks, located at about 400–500 nm, which originated from  p – p \* transitions, and long-wavelength peaks, located at about 590–670 nm, which could be ascribed to intramolecular charge transfer (ICT) between the donor and acceptor units. In solution,  PAF-C12NT  and  PBDT-C12NT  exhibited clear- ly red-shifted ICT absorbance peaks compared to  PF- C12NT  and  PCz-C12NT , which could be explained by stron- ger ICT interactions between the donor and acceptor units in  PAF-C12NT  and  PBDT-C12NT . The absorption maxima ( l max ) of  PF-C12NT ,  PAF-C12NT ,  PCz-C12NT , and  PBDT- C12NT  in solution were 585, 621, 592, and 662 nm, respec- tively. In the films, all of the polymers showed a slightly bathochromic shift compared with their corresponding spec- tra in solution, thus indicating the presence of intermolecu- lar interactions in the solid state. However, for  PAF-C12NT and  PBDT-C12NT , the similar absorption onsets in the spec- tra of the solutions and films indicated that strong packing of the polymers might have already taken place in solution.

PSC devices with an inverted structure of ITO/PF 3 N  OX/ active layer/MoO 3 /Al were fabricated to investigate the pho- tovoltaic properties of the polymers. A thin layer of PF 3 N  OX (thickness: about 5 nm) was placed on the top of ITO to improve the electron selectivity at the cathode inter- face. As shown in Table 3, active layers with different polymer/ PC 61 BM (p:n) ratios were tested and the optimal p:n ratios for these polymers were 1:1.5 for  PF-C12NT  and  PAF- C12NT , 1:2 for  PCz-C12NT , and 1:1 for  PBDT-C12NT . # Mobility  

The charge-transport properties of the donor polymer in a BHJ film are an important factor in determining the effi- ciency of the PSC. To evaluate the hole mobility of the re- sultant polymers, their corresponding hole-only devices with the configuration ITO/poly(3,4-ethylene di oxy thi oph ene):p- oly-(styrene s ulf on ate) (PEDOT:PSS)/active-layer/MoO 3 /Al were fabricated and the hole mobilities were obtained by using the space charge limited current (SCLC) model, in which the active layers were prepared under the optimal device conditions (see above). The mobilities were deter- mined by fitting the dark currents to the model of a single- carrier SCLC, according to the equation  J = (9/8) e 0 e r m h (( V 2 )/ ( d 3 )), where  J  is the current,  m h  is the zero-field mobility,  e 0 is the permittivity of free space,  e r  is the relative permittivity of the material,  d  is the thickness of the polymer layer, and V  is the effective voltage. The effective voltage was obtained by subtracting the built-in voltage ( V bi ) and the voltage drop ( V s ) from the series resistance of the substrate from the ap- plied voltage ( V appl ), according to  V = V appl  V bi  V s . The mo- bilities were extracted by modeling the dark currents in the SCLC region and calculated from the slope of the  J 1/2 – V curves (Figure 6). The hole mobilities of  PF-C12NT ,  PAF- C12NT , and  PCz-C12NT , as extracted by using the SCLC model, were 1.3410  4 , 1.5410  4 , and 2.63 10  4   cm 2 V  1 s  1 , respectively, whereas  PBDT-C12NT  exhibit- ed a slightly lower hole mobility of 2.7410  5   cm 2 V  1 s  1 . Nevertheless, these results indicated that the differences be- tween the  J sc  and FF values for different PSCs were not seri- ously affected by the hole mobilities of the polymers.

# Conclusions  

Four new low-bandgap polymers based on a naphtho[1,2- c:5,6-c]bis(1,2,5-thiadiazole) scaffold and different donor units bridged by a bithiophene spacer were synthesized. Owing to the difference in the electron-donating ability of the donor units, the energy levels, absorption spectra, bandgaps, and carrier mobilities of the resulting polymers were systematically tuned. Under optimized conditions, high J  values (above 10 mAcm  2 ) were observed in PSCs based sc on all of the different polymers, which indicated efficient photocurrent generation from the NT-based polymers. The best devices were based on the active layer of  PF-C12NT mixed with PC 71 BM in a 1:1.5 ratio, which yielded a  V oc value of 0.87 V, a  J  value of 12.19 mAcm  2 , a FF value of sc 61.36%, and a high PCE of 6.51% under simulated sunlight. Other polymers also showed good photovoltaic properties, with PCE values of 3.64%, 4.85%, and 5.00% for the corre- sponding devices with PAF-C12NT , PCz-C12NT , and PBDT-C12NT , respectively. These results demonstrate that NT-based polymers are promising donor materials for high- performance solar cells.

Fabrication and Characterization of Polymer Solar Cells  

ITO glass substrates were used as the cathode in the polymer solar cells. The ITO-coated glass substrates were cleaned by sonication in detergent, deionized water, acetone, and 2-propanol and dried in a stream of N 2 . Then, the cross-linkable PF 3 N  OX (0.5 mgmL  1 ) was spin-cast from MeOH/acetic acid (100:1 v/v) onto an ITO substrate to form a thin film (thickness: 5 nm) and the cross-linking process was performed by heating the half-dried films at 140 8 C for 25 min. Then, the bulk heterojunction composite (thickness: 80–100 nm) was spin-coated onto a PF 3 N  OX layer from a hot solution in  o -DCB of the polymer (5 mgmL  1 ) and PC 61 BM or PC 71 BM. Molybdenum oxide (MoO 3 , about 10 nm) was ther- mally deposited on top of the active layer through a shadow mask in a vacuum chamber, with an evaporation rate of 0.1 s  1   under a vacuum of about 310  4   Pa. The effective area of the device was 0.16 cm 2 , as de- termined by the shadow mask that was used during the deposition of the Al electrode. Power-conversion efficiencies (PCEs) were measured under an AM 1.5 G XES-40S1 150W AAA Class Solar Simulator. The power of the sun simulation was calibrated before the testing by using a standard silicon solar cell, thus giving a value of 100 mWcm  2   during the test. The current-density/voltage ( J – V ) characteristics were recorded on a Keithley 2400 Source Measure Unit. The spectroscopic response was measured by using a commercial photomodulation spectroscopic setup (Oriel). A cali- brated Si photodiode was used to determine the photo sensitivity. The ex- ternal quantum efficiencies of the inverted PSCs were measured by using a commercial photomodulation spectroscopic setup (DSR100UV-B), in- cluding a xenon lamp, an optical chopper, a monochromator, and a lock- in amplifier that was operated by a PC computer (Zolix Instruments); a calibrated Si photodiode was used as a standard.
