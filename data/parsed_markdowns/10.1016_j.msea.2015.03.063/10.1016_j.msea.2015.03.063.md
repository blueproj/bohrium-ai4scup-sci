Article history: Received 2 February 2015 Received in revised form 17 March 2015 Accepted 17 March 2015 Available online 8 April 2015  

The creep deformation behavior of a single-crystal Co – Al – W – Ni – Cr – Ta alloy with low tungsten content has been studied at stresses between 275 and 310 MPa at 900  1 C. The alloy exhibits comparable creep strength with that of Co – Al – W-base alloys containing more tungsten. The creep deformation consists of three stages, the primary stage, the steady-state stage and the tertiary stage, when described by the creep strain rate versus time curve. At 900  1 C,  γ 0   precipitates tend to raft along the direction of applied tensile stress in the steady-state creep stage and a topologically inverted and rafting  γ / γ 0   microstructure is formed in the tertiary stage. The main deformation mechanism in the primary creep stage is dislocation shearing of  γ 0   precipitates, and in the following creep stages, the dominant deformation mechanism is dislocations bypassing  γ 0   precipitates.

# 1. Introduction  

The discovery of a relatively stable  γ 0 -Co 3  (Al,W) phase with L1 2 structure in the ternary Co – Al – W system by Sato et al.  [1]  provides a possibility to develop a new class of  γ 0 -strengthened Co-base super- alloys. Solidus and liquidus of Co – Al – W-base alloys are about 100 – 150  1 C higher than those of advanced Ni-based single-crystal super- alloys  [2] . Compared to Ni-base single-crystal alloys, segregation of constituent elements in the dendritic structure in single-crystals Co- base alloys is very limited  [3] , resulting in a higher resistance to the formation of convective instabilities and freckles. A pronounced  ﬂ ow stress anomaly is observed in the quaternary or ternary Co – Al – W system and their strength is much higher than that of conventional Co-base superalloys strengthened by refractory elements and car- bides  [4] . These studies provide further motivation to investigate the high temperature creep properties of the new Co-base alloys  [5,6,8 – 10] , which is a dominant mode of deformation for single crystal turbine blades used in gas turbine engines. The primary researches on the effect of alloying elements on the creep strength suggest that the addition of Ta or Ti to the ternary Co – Al – W system is bene ﬁ cial to the creep properties  [5]  while the creep strength of Co – 7.8Al – 7.8W – 1.5Ta (at%) is decreased obviously with a 4.5 at% Cr addition  [5] . It seems that the addition of Cr is detrimental and the addition of Ta may not overwhelm the negative Cr effect. # 2. Experimental procedure  

The master alloy with a nominal composition of Co – 10Al – 5W – 17Ni – 6Cr – 2.7Ta (at%, named as 5W) was melted in a vacuum induction furnace, and then directionally solidi ﬁ ed into [001] single crystal rods with a length of about 220 mm by HRS (Conventional High-rate-solidi ﬁ cation Bridgeman Process) at a constant withdraw rate of 6 mm/min. Before heat treatments, the single-crystal bars were sectioned and sealed in several evacuated quartz capsules back- ﬁ lled with argon. The heat treatments were carried out as follows: 1310  1 C/10 h, furnace cooling þ 1000  1 C/36 h, air cool- ing þ 750  1 C/24 h, air cooling. In order to reveal both the dendrites and  γ / γ 0   microstructure, heat-treated samples were electrolyzed in a solution of 42 ml H PO 34 ml H SO 24 ml H O at 10 V. The  γ 3 4 þ 2 4 þ 2 phase is etched by this etching method. A conventional Rigaku D/ MAX 2500 X-ray diffractometer was used to measure the  γ / γ 0   lattice mismatch. The intensity pro ﬁ les of {004} re ﬂ ection were collected and the peak  ﬁ tting was done by using the MDI Jade software (Materials Data Inc., Livermore, CA), Pearson VII functions being used. Tensile creep specimens with a nominal 32 mm gage length and a diameter of 5 mm were machined from the heat-treated bars. The constant-load tensile-creep tests were conducted in air at stresses between 275 and 310 MPa at 900  1 C. The crept micro- structures were analyzed by using a scanning electron microscope (SEM). The volume fraction of  γ 0   precipitates and  γ  channel width were analyzed by image analyzer. Some samples were run to failure, and some were interrupted for dislocation observations. Specimens were cut transversely or at about 45 1  angles to the tensile axis by wire electrical discharge machining. # 3. Experimental results  

# 3.1. Heat-treated microstructure and  γ / γ 0   lattice mismatch  

Fig. 1 a shows the TEM bright  ﬁ eld image of 5W alloy after heat treatments. The  γ ´  precipitates exhibit cuboidal morphology, aligned along the  〈 100 〉 direction. The interfaces between  γ 0   and  γ  phases are coherent, and interfacial dislocations are not observed. Tiny  γ ´  pre- cipitates can be observed in channels ( Fig. 1 b), which are formed during cooling after the aging treatments. The frequency size distribu- tion of  γ ´  precipitates as well as the  γ 0   volume fraction was already published in a previous work  [8] .  

The lattice mismatch of the heat-treated sample at room tempera- ture was determined by X-ray diffraction. Because 5W alloy consists of pure  γ 0   and  γ  phases which have nearly equal lattice parameters, the diffraction peaks of  γ 0   and  γ  phases in XRD curve overlap and form a convoluted curve. The key point of the determination of  δ  is the peak ﬁ tting of strongly overlapping  γ 0   and  γ  sub-peaks. The  ﬁ t and experimental data are shown in  Fig. 2 . The lattice parameters of  a γ 0 and  a  is 0.35956 nm and 0.35808 nm, respectively, and the lattice γ mismatch  δ  is 0.412%. The partition coef ﬁ cient of W between  γ  and γ 0   phase can be depressed by Ni additions, resulting in a decrease of  γ / γ 0   lattice mismatch  [12] . In view of W occupying B sites of the L1 2  A 3 B ordered phase, the decrease of W content can decrease the lattice parameter of  γ 0   phase, which may cause the decrease of the  γ / γ 0   lattice mismatch as well. As a result, the composition characteristics of 5W results in a relatively lower  γ / γ 0   lattice mismatch at room temperature compared to these ternary Co – Al – W alloys  [6,10] . The different thermal expansion coef ﬁ cients of  γ  and  γ 0   phases and the redistribu- tion of alloying elements between  γ  and  γ 0   phases at higher temperatures result in a change of the  γ / γ 0   lattice mismatch with increasing temperature. It is reported that the  γ / γ 0   lattice mismatch of Co – 9 at%Al – 9 at%W – 0.1 at%B at room temperature is positive with 0.8% and it is decreased to 0.35% at 850  1 C and 0.1% at 900  1 C  [6,10] . As for Co – 9 at%Al – 7 at%W – 2 at%Ta – 20 at%Ni, the lattice mis ﬁ t at 1000  1 C is lower than the value at room temperature as well  [7] . These results indicate that the  γ / γ 0   lattice mismatch of 5W alloy at high temperature may be lower than the value at room temperature.

# 3.3. Microstructures after creep tests  

According to the creep curves in  Fig. 3 d, interrupted creep tests were carried out to show  γ 0   phases evolution during creep deforma- tion.  Fig. 4  reveals the morphological evolution of the  γ / γ 0   two phases microstructure during the creep deformation at 900  1 C. It is clearly seen that in the primary stage  γ 0   precipitates remain cuboidal and large amount of tiny  γ 0   precipitates are frequently observed in  γ channels. For specimens crept at 900  1 C for 60 h, the  γ 0   precipitates are not fully rafted. After creep for 400 h, complete directional rafting of the  γ 0   precipitates parallel to the applied tensile axis is observed. According to Nabarro's prediction  [13] , for  σ  ∙ δ 4 0 the raft structure is parallel to the external stress axis while the situation is reversed for σ  ∙ δ o 0, where  σ  is the externally applied stress. From the rafting direction, it can be inferred that the 5W alloy still possesses a positive γ / γ 0   lattice mismatch at 900  1 C. In comparison with  Fig. 4 c and f, the aspect ratio of directionally coarsened precipitates is somewhat decreased with increasing the applied stress from 275 to 310 MPa. Since specimens were crept for similar times, the variation in aspect ratio of directionally coarsened precipitates is presumably due to the reduced driving force (lower applied stress) for the development of the rafts  [14] .

Cavities are pre-existing in single-crystal alloys as interdendri- tic casting porosities. During creep deformation, these defects lead to local stress concentrations in their vicinity. Therefore, cracks are much easier to nucleate and grow from these defects during creep deformation. As shown in  Fig. 5 , cracks tend to be initiated at pores and propagate through the  γ  and  γ 0   phases. Recently, an analysis has been made into the stress distribution in the region near cavities during tensile creep tests of a single crystal nickel- based superalloy by means of the  ﬁ nite element method  [18] . The results  [18]  show that the lowest stress appears in the top region of the round cavity and a bigger value of the Von Mises stress distribution occurs in the regions along the direction of about 45 1  angles relative to the stress axis, which results in rafted  γ 0   phase being arranged along the direction of 45 1  angles, while the maximum value of the stress distribution appears in the regions along the direction perpendicular to the stress axis and leads to the initiation and propagation of cracks in this direction as well  [18] .  Fig. 5 b shows that the initiation and propagation of the cracks occur along the direction perpendicular to the stress axis and the raft behavior of  γ 0   phases in the region along the direction of about 45 1  angles relative to the stress axis is much similar with the observations in Ref.  [18] . This suggests that the stress distribution in the region near cavities is similar with the situation in Ni-base superalloys  [18] . For Ni-base superalloys, generally possessing a negative  γ / γ 0   lattice mismatch, the direction of  γ 0   rafting as well as the microcracks propagation near pores is perpendicular to the tensile stress axis, which means that microcracks tend to propagate along the γ / γ 0   interfaces. The propagation of microcracks through the  γ  and  γ 0 phases is more dif ﬁ cult compared to the propagation along the  γ / γ 0 interfaces. This indicates that the rafting microstructure in Co – Al – W- base alloys is more effective to suppress the propagation of micro- cracks, which is bene ﬁ cial to increase creep resistance especially in the tertiary creep stage.

# 3.4. Dislocation structures  

# 3.4.1. The primary creep stage  

Fig. 6  shows dislocation con ﬁ gurations of the primary creep stage at 900  1 C/275 MPa and 900  1 C/310 MPa. It can be seen that the main features are dislocations shearing of  γ 0   precipitates accompanied by stacking faults (SFs). The dislocation density in channels in both conditions is low and dislocations pileup at  γ / γ 0 interfaces is seldom observed. This suggests that the dissociation of a matrix dislocation in the interfaces is favored  [19] . It is reported by Eggeler et al.  [9]  that stable APBs in  γ 0   precipitates of new Co-base single-crystal superalloys have been observed for tensile creep tests performed at 900  1 C under vacuum at stresses between 275 and 310 MPa, which is attributed to the relatively lower antiphase domain boundary (APB) energy  [20] . They also pointed out that APBs were observed in nearly every precipitate beyond 0.5% creep strain for the compositions they investigated. A lower APB energy represents a lower resistance for dislocations shearing  γ 0   precipitates. Besides, a large number of tiny  γ 0 precipitates can be observed in channels ( Fig. 1 b). These tiny particles can add resistance to the normal dislocation bowing mechanism that would tend to  ﬁ ll the  γ  channels with dislocations and create large networks of interfacial dislocations  [21] , which may illustrate that no dislocation activity in channels was obser- vable in the primary creep stage. Moreover, these tiny  γ 0   pre- cipitates can decrease the spacing between  γ 0   precipitates. A small mean surface-to-surface spacing between  γ 0   precipitates can restrict dislocation motion signi ﬁ cantly, which could add  “ pres- sure ”  to dislocations at the  γ / γ 0   interface and enable dislocations to overcome the energy barrier preventing their entry into the ordered precipitates  [21] .  

# 3.4.2. The steady-state creep stage  

The dislocation con ﬁ gurations of steady-state creep at 900  1 C/ 275 MPa and 900  1 C/310 MPa are shown in  Fig. 7 . For both creep conditions, dislocations shearing  γ 0   precipitates (marked by dark arrows) and dislocations bypassing  γ 0   precipitates (marked by white arrows) can be observed at this stage. For Ni-base super- alloys, as stresses increase in the  γ 0   precipitates due to plastic  ﬂ ow of the surrounding  γ  matrix, pairs of  a /2 〈 011 〉 dislocations shearing the precipitates will occur. In the new Co-base single-crystal superalloys  [9] , the  a /2 〈 011 〉 dislocations shearing the precipitates are more frequently observed at 2% creep strain compared to the observations at a lower creep strain (0.5%). Thus, the increasing stress due to subsequent plastic deformation probably results in the occurrence of  a /2 〈 011 〉 dislocations shearing the precipitates in the present study.  

# 3.4.3. The tertiary creep stage  

Fig. 8  shows the dislocation con ﬁ gurations in the tertiary creep stage at 900  1 C/275 MPa and 900  1 C/310 MPa. In view of the heavy plastic deformation, the stresses are relatively higher compared to the initial stage. For Ni-base superalloys, a higher stress could enable  a /2 〈 011 〉 dislocations shear into  γ 0   precipitates, while at this stage for Co-base superalloys, the predominant deformation mechanism is dislocations bypassing  γ 0   precipitates. For such behavior, we will give a preliminary discussion in the later section.

# 4.2. Creep deformation behaviors  

Based on observations from SEM and TEM, microstructure evolution and deformation behaviors during creep at 900  1 C can be summarized as follows: (i) in the primary creep stage,  γ 0 precipitates are still cuboidal and the main deformation mechan- ism is dislocation shearing of  γ 0   precipitates; (ii) in the steady-state creep stage,  γ 0   precipitates tend to raft along the direction of applied stress and dislocations begin to bow in  γ  matrix and bypass  γ 0   precipitates; and (iii) in the tertiary stage, a topologically inverted  γ / γ 0 -microstructure is formed, and the main deformation mechanism is dislocations bypassing  γ 0   precipitates.

The resistance for the motion of dislocations in  γ  channels is made up of three contributions  [23] , the Orowan stress, solid solution resistance and mis ﬁ t stresses. In the steady-state creep stage,  γ 0   precipitates begin to raft. It is known that the directional diffusion of elements responsible for rafting  [14] .  Fig. 11  displays the schematic illustra- tion of directional coarsening by preferential dissolution along the horizontal matrix channels and coalescence in the horizontal matrix channels in the case of applied tensile stress parallel to vertical matrix. Elements such as Al, W, Ta, Ni prefer to enrich in  γ 0 phases, while Cr atoms are partitioned to  γ  phases . During directional coarsening, Ta and W atoms tend to diffuse to  γ 0 phases, which will result in the decrease of solid solution resis- tance for dislocations motion. On the other hand, the  γ 0   precipi- tates volume fraction is almost constant during creep tests, which leads to the increase of  γ  channel width, shown in  Table 2 . The Orowan stress is decreased with the increase of the  γ  channel width  [23] . Besides, the tiny  γ 0   precipitates dissolved, which may decrease the resistance to the normal dislocation bowing in the  γ channels. Dislocations gathering at the  γ / γ 0   interfaces can decrease the mis ﬁ t stresses. Thus, it can be seen that the resistance for the motion of dislocations in  γ  channels is decreased in the steady and tertiary creep stages. It is proposed by Kear et al.  [25]  that changes of SF energies or APB energies generated by elements redistribu- tion may be of partial in ﬂ uence on the deformation mechanism. Since the rafted microstructure is caused by directional diffusion of elements, elements such as W, Ta may be concentrated in  γ 0 phase more heavily compared to the initial state  [13] . It is possible that the diffusion of W and Ta atoms during the process of directional diffusion increases APB energies. The associated APB energy,  γ APB , represents a barrier which must be overcome if cutting of particle should occurs, and the precipitate-cutting stress is expected to be in the order  γ APB /b , where  b  is the Burgers vector. The increase of the APB energy will result in a higher resistance for dislocations shearing into  γ 0   precipitates. Therefore, at the begin- ning of steady-state creep stage, the resistance for dislocations bypassing  γ 0   precipitates and dislocations shearing  γ 0   precipitates may be comparable and both deformation modes can be observed. However, with creep deformation going on, the directional diffu- sion is heavier, and the resistance for dislocations bypassing  γ 0 precipitates is decreasing while the resistance for dislocations shearing of  γ 0   precipitates is increasing, which leads to the transformation of deformation mechanism.

# 5. Conclusions  

The creep deformation behavior of a single-crystal Co – Al – W- base alloy with  γ / γ 0   two-phase microstructures has been investi- gated in tension at 900  1 C. The results obtained are summarized as follows.  

(1) Compared to other Ni-free Co – Al – W-base alloys, the minimum creep rate of 5W alloy at 900  1 C is relatively higher, while its creep life is longer than those alloys. Alloying with high Ni and high Ta can overwhelm the negative Cr effect as well as the negative low W effect in the present alloy system.  

(2) The creep deformation consists of three stages, the primary stage, the steady-state stage and the tertiary stage, when described by the creep strain rate versus time curve. At 900  1 C, the main deformation mechanism in the primary creep stage is dislocation shearing of  γ 0   precipitates; during the subsequent creep deforma- tion,  γ 0   precipitates tend to raft along the direction of applied stress, and dislocations begin to bow in  γ  matrix and bypass  γ 0 precipitates; in the tertiary stage, a topologically inverted  γ / γ 0 - microstructure is formed, and the main deformation mechanism is dislocations bypassing  γ 0   precipitates.
