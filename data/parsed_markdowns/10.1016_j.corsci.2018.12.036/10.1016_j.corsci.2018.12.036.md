# The e ﬀ ect of cooling rate on the  γ ' composition, morphology and corrosion behaviour of IN738LC  

⁎ H.T. Mallikarjuna , W.F. Caley, N.L. Richards  

University of Manitoba, R3T 2N2, Canada  

# A B S T R A C T  

# A R T I C L E I N F O  

The in ﬂ uence of  γ ' size and composition on the corrosion behaviour of IN738LC superalloy was investigated while maintaining a monomodal microstructure. Experimental results showed that smaller precipitates ( ∼ 50 nm), obtained by a rapid water quench (3000 °C/min), were spherical with an Al content of 6.2 wt.-%. Slower-cooled specimens (2.5 °C/min) exhibited larger cuboidal shaped  γ ' precipitates ( ∼ 800 nm) with reduced Al content (4.5 wt.%). Furthermore, a negative in ﬂ uence on the corrosion resistance of IN738LC was found with increasing rate of cooling, which is attributable to an increase in volume fraction and Al content of the  γ ' precipitates.  

Keywords: A. Ni-based superalloy B. Cooling rate C. Precipitates D. Microstructure

At higher undercooling, the thermodynamic driving force for nucleation (chemical free energy) increases and the di ﬀ usivity of  γ ′  forming elements decreases, which creates local supersaturation resulting in other bursts of nucleation forming secondary and tertiary  γ ′  precipitates.  

Ni-based superalloys are used as a material for turbine blades in aerospace gas turbine components because of overall strength at high- temperature. These alloys were developed to resist mechanical prop- erties such as fatigue, creep deformation and surface deformation at temperatures in the range of 750 – 900 °C [ 1 ]. Such properties are at- tained through the development of a high volume fraction of an ordered strengthening gamma prime ( γ ') L1 2  precipitate phase, which has a composition Ni 3 (Al,Ti) inside a disordered gamma ( γ ) phase matrix [ 2 , 3 ]. The  γ ' precipitate morphologies and their size distribution have been well-documented [ 4 ]. Ni-based superalloys generally consist of a bi-modal  γ ' precipitate size distribution with a volume fraction of about 35 – 60% [ 5 ]. The small secondary and tertiary  γ ' precipitates are formed upon quenching, are called cooling precipitates and the ratio of these cooling precipitates exceeds 60% of the total volume. Coarser  γ ' pre- cipitates are often formed during slow cooling from the solvus tem- perature.  

Results from Sajjadi et al. [ 8 ] showed an increase in volume fraction of larger  γ ′  precipitates with a decrease in cooling rate. It has also been reported that the volume fraction of the overall  γ ′  precipitates can be suppressed at high cooling rates. Babu et al. [ 9 ] studied the cooling rate dependent microstructure and composition of  γ  and  γ ′  phases. It was observed that there was no compositional change in the larger pre- cipitates compared to the equilibrium composition; however, a higher Al concentration was noted for smaller precipitates.  

A typical industrial heat-treatment for superalloys often leads to the formation of multiple size ranges of  γ ' precipitates during continuous cooling from solutionizing temperature. The morphology, distribution and composition of these  γ ′  precipitates in ﬂ uence the mechanical properties of the materials; therefore, a study of the thermodynamic and kinetic factors a ﬀ ecting the evolution of these precipitates is vital for the fundamental understanding of the underlying phase transfor- mations that occur.  

It has been reported that the precipitate size, distribution and their morphologies depend strongly on cooling rate [ 6 ]. The growth of a precipitate is limited at faster cooling rates; therefore, these precipitates attain spherical morphology. Additionally, if the cooling rate is high enough, the  γ ' will precipitate in a monomodal morphology. In contrast, the slow-cooled materials exhibit a multimodal precipitate size dis- tribution, which has been attributed to multiple bursts of  γ ′  precipitates at various temperatures on cooling [ 7 ]. The  ﬁ rst bursts of  γ ′  nucleation or primary  γ ′  precipitates occur at high temperatures, i.e., below  γ ′  

Apart from high-temperature mechanical properties, corrosion be- haviour at room temperature is another important property that can a ﬀ ect the service life and durability of aerospace engine parts during downtime. Aluminum and its alloys are more susceptible to pitting corrosion due to the presence of intermetallics formed during material processing [ 10 , 11 ]. The extent of corrosion rate of Al alloys depends on their composition and morphology [ 12 ].  

It should be noted that most of the reported works on  γ ′  precipita- tion have focused on bimodal precipitation during various cooling rates, whereas little work has been done to understand the monomodal precipitation and their corrosion properties under continuous cooling rates. The purpose of this work is to study the monomodal  γ ′  pre- cipitation and their morphological evolution during di ﬀ erent cooling rate conditions. This work is also extended to observe the size-depen- dent composition variation from di ﬀ erent cooling rates and their e ﬀ ect on corrosion behaviour.  

# 2. Experimental procedure  

# 2.1. Monomodal precipitate preparation  

The samples used in the present work were prepared from poly- crystalline alloy IN738LC, supplied by PCC Airfoils, Minerva in ingot form. The chemical composition of the alloy is 16Cr, 3.4Al, 3.44Ti,

 1.7Ta, 0.9Nb, 8.47Co, 1.77Mo, 2.58 W, 0.028 Zr, 0.009B and balance Ni

 (wt.%). The equilibrium weight fractions of  γ ,  γ ' and liquid phase for IN738LC were obtained over the range 700 °C to 1400 °C using the thermodynamic simulation JMatPro R   version 4 software [ 13 ], which is shown in  Fig. 1 . From  Fig. 1 , the  γ ' solvus temperature for this alloy is approximately 1134 °C. The standard solution heat treatment tem- perature for this alloy is 1120 °C [ 14 ], which is below the predicted  γ solvus temperature; this temperature is termed as the partial solution treatment temperature.  

In this investigation, the blank specimens for the cooling tests were cut by a wire electrolytic-discharge machine (EDM) to a size of 12 × 10 × 3 mm. All cooling tests were performed in a Carbolite box furnace, CWF-1300, in static air and a  γ ' solutionizing temperature of 1200 °C was used (supersolvus temperature). Specimens were held at 1200 °C for up to 10 min and then cooled to 800 °C at di ﬀ erent cooling rates. On reaching 800 °C, the specimens were rapidly quenched by immersing in water. The primary objective of this experiment was to produce a mono-modal precipitate size distribution by avoiding the second and third burst of nucleation below 800 °C. Four di ﬀ erent cooling rates were obtained (from 1200-800 °C) by water quenching, air cooling and furnace programming; these gave cooling rates of 3000, 50, 5 and 2.5 °C/min respectively.  

The samples were mounted and polished using standard metallo- graphic techniques. In order to obtain a clear threshold between matrix

The compositional analysis was done using a Super-X EDS system.  

# 2.2. Electrochemical testing  

The di ﬀ erent  γ '-sized monomodal specimens were mounted in ba- kelite and polished to 1  μ m using diamond paste. These specimens were used as working electrodes with a surface area of 1 cm 2   in contact with the electrolyte. Electrochemical tests were conducted in di ﬀ erent electrolytes, both acidic and chloride-containing solutions, to simulate di ﬀ erent types of exposure conditions; the electrolytes used were 0.1 M sulfuric acid (H 2 SO 4 ), 0.5 M sodium chloride (NaCl) and 0.5 M hydro- chloric acid (HCl). The electrochemical tests were conducted in a three- electrode electrochemical cell using a saturated calomel electrode (SCE) as a reference electrode and graphite rods as the counter electrodes.  

Speci ﬁ cally, the test specimens were subjected to potentiodynamic polarization testing scanned at a rate of 1 mV/s from  − 1 V to 1.5 V (SCE). The stable corrosion potential was achieved by running an open circuit potential for one hour before the electrochemical test. The po- larization and current measurements were performed with a Princeton Applied Research Potentiostat and the potential and current were re- corded on a VersaSat3 software. The results from these experiments were used to analyse corrosion current density (i corr ), corrosion po- tential (E corr ), passive current density (i pass ), critical current density (i crit ) and passivation potential range ( Δ E pass ). All tests were conducted at room temperature. The corroded samples were examined using an optical microscope to evaluate the pitting.

# 3.3. Corrosion behaviour  

The potentiodynamic polarization measurements for the corrosion of WQ and slow furnace cooled specimens were carried out in three di ﬀ erent aqueous solutions: 0.5 M NaCl, 0.5 M HCl and 0.1 M H 2 SO 4 solutions at room temperature; these are shown in  Fig. 8 a – c respec- tively. From  Fig. 8 , it is clear that the polarization curves for the 3000 °C/min specimen are shifted to the higher current density region compared to slow furnace cooled (2.5 °C/min) specimen in all three solutions. A summary of the corrosion data is tabulated in  Table 3 . From  Fig. 8 a, it is clear that neither specimen exhibited a passive layer in 0.5 M NaCl solution. However, they showed small passivation re- gions in 0.5 M HCl, as shown in  Fig. 8 b. Even though the 3000 °C/min specimen had lower critical current density, i crit  and passivation current density, i pass  than the 2.5 °C/min specimen in 0.5 M HCl, it did not display any passivation region for up to 0.03 A/cm 2 . However, the 3000 °C/min specimen later showed small passivation range of 0.265 V against 0.7 V for 2.5 °C/min. On the other hand, both specimens dis- played good passivation range of 0.71 V and 0.8 V for 3000 °C/min and 2.5 °C/min respectively, in 0.1 M H 2 SO 4  ( Fig. 8 c). Overall, the chloride environment was much more aggressive compared to the sulphate en- vironment for both specimens.

The  γ ' precipitate size distribution in the 3000 °C/min specimen was monomodal, with size measured to be approximately 50 ± 5 nm. For such a small precipitate size to occur, the specimen must undergo maximum undercooling so that the amount of time required for the growth of  γ ' precipitates is reduced, but at the same time the thermo- dynamic driving force for the nucleation will be high. As a precipitate nucleates at maximum undercooling, one would expect a high nuclea- tion rate. It is well known that an increase in undercooling will increase the rate of nucleation exponentially [ 17 ]. Langer-Schwartz [ 18 ] explained this mechanism using phase- ﬁ eld modelling. The model incorporated the strain, di ﬀ usion  ﬁ elds around the precipitates and overlapping di ﬀ usion  ﬁ eld parameters to predict a microstructure, which was similar to that found experimentally. Both low di ﬀ usivity and high nucleation density impedes the  γ ' growth and maintains the spherical morphology. The high undercooling prevents further nucleation and growth even though a non-equilibrium condition exists in the system. Hence, the monomodal distribution of the  γ ' pre- cipitates was observed.  

In contrast, the results for the slowest cooled condition (2.5 °C/min) suggest a di ﬀ erent mechanism. These precipitates nucleate at high temperatures and have higher di ﬀ usion rates and lower undercooling compared those of the 3000 °C/min specimen, where di ﬀ usion rates are slow. This allows equilibrium to be established at the  γ / γ ' interface resulting in near equilibrium precipitate composition. The nucleation rate decreases at high temperature resulting in a lower volume fraction of precipitates and thus an increase in inter-precipitate distance and was shown in  Fig. 6 c and d.

Since the time at each tem- perature is limited during cooling, it becomes di ﬃ cult to achieve the equilibrium volume fraction of precipitates; the precipitate can main- tain equilibrium with the matrix not more than a few tens of nan- ometers from the interface [ 6 , 20 ]. Additionally, there will not be su ﬃ cient driving force for new nucleation to occur between these precipitates.  

The slow-cooled  γ ' precipitates displayed deviation from the equi- librium concentration, i.e., 4.5 ± 0.3 wt.% of Al vs 6.1 ± 0.9 wt.% of Al at equilibrium. From  Fig. 7 , it is clear that a decrease in cooling rate decreased the Al concentration in the  γ ' precipitates. In contrast, the decrease in cooling rate increased the concentration of  γ -forming ele- ments such as Co and Cr in larger  γ ' precipitates. The non-equilibrium compositions can be attributed to the low Al concentration in the slow- cooled specimen. However, with increased Al di ﬀ usion into a pre- cipitate with further decrease in cooling rate, equilibrium composition could be attained. According to equilibrium defect concentration stu- dies in ordered phases [ 21 ], the deviation in precipitate composition is due to the presence of antisite defects, which is accommodated by Ni 3+x Al 1-x , i.e., Ni atoms on Al sites for Ni-rich compositions. Similarly, the formation of larger precipitates during slow cooling was assumed to be due to vacancy-mediated antisite-bridge di ﬀ usion of elements such as Co and Cr into the  γ ' precipitate, resulting in non-equilibrium across the interface [ 22 ].  

# 4.2. In ﬂ uence of cooling rate on corrosion performance  

Ni-based superalloys exhibit excellent corrosion resistance due to the formation of passive layers such as Cr 2 O 3  and Al 2 O 3 . However, the corrosion resistance can vary depending on the corrosive environment or corrosive medium. For instance, the alloy can perform well in a chloride-free electrolyte but show severe corrosion in a chloride-con- taining solution. Therefore, knowledge of the chemical composition of the alloy is critical to understand the formation of passive  ﬁ lms and subsequent corrosion behaviour. In chromium-containing alloys, it is often observed that the external layer is mainly Ni-hydroxides over an inner chromium-rich oxide layer [ 23 ]. The amount of chromium pre- sent in the alloy plays a signi ﬁ cant role in corrosion performance. Craig et al. [ 24 ] suggested that an alloy should contain a minimum of 10.5 wt. % Cr to exhibit corrosion resistance and a further increase in chromium will enhance the corrosion resistance. The alloy used for the present study consisted of 16 wt. % chromium, which is high enough to provide a good corrosion resistance. Previous studies by the present authors revealed that IN738LC is a chromia former with multiple oxide layer formation [ 25 ].  

The electrochemical behaviour of a Ni-based superalloy is complex and is highly dependent on composition, size, volume fraction and the morphology of the  γ ' precipitates. To understand the in ﬂ uence of these factors, potentiodynamic tests were conducted on IN738LC and the results were shown in  Fig. 8 . The specimens described earlier as derived from di ﬀ erent cooling rates of 3000 and 2.5 °C/min displayed a high corrosion resistance in sulphate medium (H 2 SO 4 ) compared to chloride media (NaCl and HCl). In the present study, the corrosion current density was strongly in ﬂ uenced by the size and volume fraction of the  γ ' precipitates indicating a decrease in the precipitate size increased the corrosion rate. It was also noted that the composition of  γ ' precipitates had in ﬂ uenced the corrosion performance.  

Since the  γ -matrix contains more chromium than the  γ ' precipitates, the matrix exhibits more corrosion resistance than the  γ ' precipitates. Some studies have shown that an increase in Al content can have a negative in ﬂ uence on the corrosion resistance. For example, Castadena et al. [ 26 ] and Osorio et al. [ 27 ] showed that an increase in Al content decreases the corrosion resistance in Ni-Fe-Al and Fe-Al alloys respec- tively by forming localised corrosion such as pitting. In the present work, both Al content (6.1 ± 0.9 wt. %) and precipitate number den- sity (84 ± 3.27 precipitates/ μ m 2 ) in a specimen cooled at 3000 °C/min was higher than that found in a slow-cooled specimen (2.5 °C/min) with Al of 4.5 ± 0.3 wt. % and precipitate number density of 12,800 ± 500 precipitates/ μ m 2 . Hence, the 3000 °C/min specimen with the smaller  γ ' precipitates would be more susceptible to corrosion than the 2.5 °C/min specimen with larger  γ ' precipitates.  

# 5. Conclusions  

The experimental results and microstructural analyses suggest that cooling rate in ﬂ uences the  γ ′  precipitate size, morphology and com- position. The  γ ′  precipitate size was found to decrease with an increase in cooling rate due to limited di ﬀ usion time and reduction in trans- portation of  γ ′  forming alloying elements.  

Compositionally, the smaller precipitates were found to be richer in Al than the larger precipitates, which can be attributed to the presence of Cr and Co-rich antisite defects.  

The WQ specimen (3000 °C/min) with small spherical  γ ′  precipitates (50 nm) was found to have a higher corrosion current density compared to the slow-cooled specimen (2.5 °C/min) with larger cuboidal pre- cipitate (800 nm). The improvement in corrosion resistance for the 2.5 °C/min specimen was attributed to an increase in inter-precipitate distance that exposed more matrix to the corrosive environment and a decrease in volume fraction of  γ ′  precipitates.
