# 1. Introduction  

In recent years, in order to increase the fuel-use efﬁciency of gas turbine engines, nickel-base single-crystal (SX) superalloys have been widely used as the material of hot-section components such as blades, blisks, and vane seal segments [ 1 ]. The main advantage of SX over polycrystalline nickel-base superalloy is their improved high-temperature creep and thermal fatigue resistance, due to the suppression of grain boundary strengthening elements and the elimination of grain boundaries [ 2 ]. Under extremely severe work- ing conditions of high-temperature and high-pressure, the service life of these high-cost SX components is limited by the thermal fatigue cracks and tip erosion, leading to a loss of turbine efﬁ- ciency [ 3 ]. The replacement of the these high-value SX components contributes largely to the operation cost of modern gas turbines [ 4 ]. Therefore, effective refurbishment and repair techniques which extend the lifetime of SX components are of great economic inter- est and desirable [ 5 ]. A successful repair technique should retain a SX solidiﬁcation microstructure which is identical to the crystalline structure of the base material in the repaired zone [ 6 ].  

Laser powder deposition (LPD) process, which allows rapid and accurate addition of controlled amounts of material at the required locations with minimal heat input, has been proven to be a very promising and effective technology in restoring the worn SX com- ponents [ 7 ]. However, the major challenge in the LPD process of SX superalloy is the stray grain formation which appears as mis- oriented and equiaxed grains in the deposited microstructure [ 8 ]. Stray grains, once form, suppress the epitaxial growth of columnar dendrites and, furthermore, cause the formation of low melting point grain boundaries which act as the easy path for crack initia- tion and propagation [ 9 ]. Thus, the nickel-base SX superalloys are vulnerable to columnar-to-equiaxed transition (CET).  

The CET has been studied by theoretical modeling [ 10–12 ] and experimental study [ 13 ]. During the low-penetration LPD process of SX superalloy on a substrate with similar material, the crystal growth behaviors are determined by the constitutional supercool- ing (CS) at the solidiﬁcation interface. Hunt [ 14 ] showed that the CS can be described by the G/V ratio, where G is the thermal gradient and V is solidiﬁcation velocity at the crystal tip. Gäumann et al. [ 7 ] developed Hunt’s analytical model for the LPD process of CMSX-4 superalloy on a substrate with [001]/<100> crystallographic orien- tation normal to the depositing surface. For the worn-tip repair process of SX turbine blades, [001]/<100> crystallographic orientation is usually normal to the laser depositing surface. Layers of SX material are deposited on the top surface of blades to regenerate a new tip. In order to achieve continuous SX microstructure in multi-layer tip-repair process, the formation of stray grain must be avoided or completely remelted and resolidiﬁed to the base crystal orientation. Increasing the ther- mal gradient G along the [001]/<100> crystallographic orientation (normal direction of blade tip) of substrate can effectively increase the ratio of | G 001 | n /| V 001 |, which can prevent stray grain formation and promote the remelting of stray grain between layers. Mean- while, the primary dendrite arm spacing (PDAS) also relates to the thermal gradient G and solidiﬁcation velocity V at the solid- liquid interface [ 15 ]. For the rapid solidiﬁcation processing of LPD of CMSX-4 superalloy, the PDAS follows the proportional relation w ∼ G − 0.5 V − 0.25   [ 16 ], where w is the PDAS. Increasing thermal gradi- ent G can decrease the PDAS. Small PDAS restrains the segregation and beneﬁts the homogenization of chemical elements. During the LPD processing, the thermal gradient G is determined by many factors such as laser power, scanning speed, substrate preset tem- perature. Decreasing the laser power or increasing the scanning speed can not only effectively increase the thermal gradient, but also decrease the linear laser energy absorbed by molten pool and correspondingly changes deposited bead geometrical size. For the speciﬁc applications such as worn-tip refabrication of SX turbine blades, the geometrical size of deposited bead should keep sta- ble. Adjusting the substrate preset temperature can change the thermal gradient with little effect on the linear laser energy of molten pool. Meanwhile, adjusting the substrate temperature has been proven to be an effective method to inﬂuence the microtex- ture and morphology of thin ﬁlm materials [ 17 , 18 ]. Therefore, for given processing parameters such as laser power, scanning speed and powder feeding rate, controlling the substrate preset tempera- ture is a useful method to optimize the SX-processing window and microstructure. and the effect of substrate preset temperature on CET and PDAS in LPD process of SX superalloy has never be analyzed in detail.  

In this study, the effect of substrate preset temperature on the crystal growth and microstructure formation in the deposited bead was analyzed by an improved mathematical model. LPD experi- ments with speciﬁc SX superalloy were conducted to verify the computational results. The results of simulation and experiments were compared and discussed.

# 3. Experimental procedure  

A nickel-base SX superalloy developed by the Powder Metal- lurgy Research Institute of the Central South University in China was used in the LPD experiments. The composition of the SX superalloy is shown in Table 1 . The particle size of SX powder was 65  m–100  m. A SX ingot with [001]/<100> crystallographic orientation along the axial direction, which was produced by a traditional casting method followed by standard heat treat- ment, was machined into plate samples. The size of samples was 25 mm × 15 mm × 6 mm in X , Y and Z directions, respectively. The surface of samples was grounded with 300-grit SiC paper and cleaned by acetone and alcohol before laser deposition.  

An Ytterbium ﬁber laser with maximum 2 kW power was used as the heat source for all experiments. The laser beam has a Gaus- sian model intensity proﬁle and generates a 0.7 mm circular spot on the (001) surface ( X - Y plane) of samples with a 2 mm defocus- ing length. The LPD process was protected by argon shielding gas (purity of 99.99%) fed through the coaxial nozzle with a ﬂow rate of 10 L/min. The carrier gas of powder was 6 L/min. The laser power, scanning speed, powder feeding rate and layer height  h were set constantly as 350 W, 10 mm/s, 3.5 g/min and 0.2 mm, respectively. The environmental temperature of LPD experiments was +30   ◦ C. The substrate preset temperature, represented by T sub , varied from − 30   ◦ C to +210   ◦ C with an interval of +60   ◦ C. The preheating of substrate (above +30   ◦ C) was done with an induction, while the precooling (below +30   ◦ C) was done by a semiconductor chilling plate, as shown in Fig. 1 .  

The samples for microstructure analysis were sectioned, mounted and polished using standard metallographic techniques for nickel-base superalloy, and etched by a solution contain- ing CuCl 2  (5 g), HCl (100 mL) and CH 3 CH 2 OH (100 mL). Optical microscopy and electron backscattered diffraction (EBSD) were used for microstructure analysis in the study. # 4.1. Effect of T sub  on molten pool morphology  

Fig. 4 shows the variation of molten pool morphologies under different substrate preset temperature T sub . As shown in Fig. 4 (a)–(c), when the T sub  increases from − 30   ◦ C to +210   ◦ C, the molten pool width W and height H change little, while the melt- ing depth D increases from 0.105 mm to 0.131 mm. This is because for the given processing parameters, the linear laser energy input is constant. The variation of T sub  has little effect on the process of laser-powder interaction and powder capture of molten pool. Meanwhile the molten pool width is mainly determined by the laser spot diameter and linear laser energy. Thereby the molten pool width W and height H ﬂuctuate in a narrow range. While the variation of T sub  changes the energy for melting substrate. The precooling of substrate means more energy is needed to melt unit volume of substate, leading to the decrease of melting depth D of molten pool. While for the preheating, the situation is opposite. Thus, the melting depth D of molten pool shows an almost linear increase trend with the increase of T sub  from − 30   ◦ C to +210   ◦ C. In the real processing condition, the transport phenomena are very complex, and the melting depth is also affected by factors such as ﬂuid ﬂow of liquid metal and energy loss caused by heat radiation in molten pool. These transport pheonmena can not be completely and accurately considered in the simualtion model. Thereby the simulated melting depth D of molten pool shows a larger increase rate than that of experimental results.

# 4.3. Effect of T sub  on PDAS  

Fig. 7 shows the dendritic microstructure perpendicular to the [001]/<100> crystal dendrite trunk orientation in deposited bead under different substrate preset temperature T sub . Columanr den- drites growth parallelly with no obvious secondary dendrite arm formation. Under the environmental temperature ( T sub  = +30   ◦ C), the average PDAS is 9.73  m. Due to the ﬂuctuation of molten pool size and corresponding soldiﬁcation conditions at the solidifcation interface, the PDAS also ﬂuctuates in a range. When the substrate is precooled ( T sub  = − 30   ◦ C), the average PDAS is 8.55  m. While when the substrate is preheated ( T sub  = +210   ◦ C), the average PDAS is 18.32  m. As shown in Fig. 8 , with the increase of T sub  from − 30   ◦ C to +210   ◦ C, the averge PDAS shows a near-linear increasing trend, and the ﬂuctuation range becomes larger. This is because the pre- cooling of substrate enhances the |G 001 | 3.4 /|V 001 | ratio and weakens the disturbance caused by molten pool ﬂuctuation. While the pre- heating of substrate reduces |G 001 | 3.4 /|V 001 | ratio. The disturbance caused by molten pool ﬂuctuation becomes important and differs obviously the soldiﬁcation conditions at each point of the soldiﬁca- tion interface. The results indicate the substrate preset temperature T sub  can effectively inﬂuence the PDAS.  

# 5. Discussion  

During the multi-layer repair or even multi-track and multi-layer manufacturing processing of SX components, the dis- continuity of epitaxial columnar dendrites caused by the formation of residual stray grain should be avoided. For the multi-layer tip- repair process of SX turbine blades, methods to increase the melting depth and decrease the stray grain formation promote the ability of complete remelting of stray grains between layers. The sub- strate preset temperature has a predominant effect on the CET and associated stray grain formation. As shown in Fig. 9 , under the normal environmental condition ( T sub  = +30   ◦ C), the HR s  is about 44.5%, while the HR m  is 50.1%, indicating that the melting depth is larger than stray grain portion and stray grains can be totally remelted. When the substrate is precooled ( T sub  = − 30   ◦ C), the HR s decreases to 40.5%, while the HR m  is 46.1%. The complete remelting of stray grain can also be achieved. When the substrate is pre- heated, the situation becomes complex. When the T sub  = +90   ◦ C, the HR m  = 52.9% is still larger than HR s  = 50.5%. When the T sub  = +150   ◦ C, the HR s  increases to 55.3%, while the HR m  is 54.5%, indicating that the melting depth is smaller than stray grain portion and some stray grains will be retained. In other words, under the given process- ing parameters, T sub  = +90   ◦ C can be regared as critical condition for continuous epitaxial growth. When the substrate preset temper- ature is not larger than +90   ◦ C, the HR m  is larger than HR s , which means the stray grain of previous layers can be totally remelted by above layer. While when the substrate temperature is above +90   ◦ C, the HR m  may be larger than HR s , which means that the stray grain of previous layers may not be totally remelted by above layer.  

Fig. 10 shows the EBSD color mapped microstructure in the transverse section of 40-layer SX wall structure deposited with continuous preheating and precooling respectively. As shown in Fig. 10 (a), under preheating conditions ( T sub  = +210   ◦ C), some stray grains which form near the outside surface of thin-wall bead are not totally remelted by above deposition layer. These stray grains expand layer by layer and then completely suppress the epi- taxial growth of columnar dendrites. Under precooling condition ( T sub  = − 30   ◦ C), the columnar dendrites grow continuously from the substrate to the top of the thin-wall bead without any resid- ual stray grains and cracks. The misorientation angle of thin-wall bead is below 10 ◦ . Compare Fig. 10 (a) and (b), it is obvious that precooling of substrate can effectively enhance the continuity of epitaxial columnar dendrites in multilayer LPD process. Low sub- strate preset temperature advantages the stability of repair process and optimization of processing window.

# 6. Conclusions  

In this study, the effect of substrate preset temperature on crys- tal growth and microstructure formation in LPD processing of SX superalloy was investigated by mathematical and experimental approaches. The results between simulation and experiments show a reasonable agreement.  

The substrate preset temperature affects the molten pool mor- phology little, but obviously changes the CET condition and PDAS.
