# ON THE KINETICS OF RAFTING IN CMSX-4 SUPERALLOY SINGLE CRYSTALS  

N. MATAN, D. C. COX, C. M. F. RAE and R. C. REED {  

University of Cambridge/Rolls-Royce University Technology Centre, Department of Materials Science and Metallurgy, Pembroke Street, Cambridge CB2 3QZ, U.K.  

(Received 22 December 1998; accepted 9 March 1999)  

Abstract ├ÉThe kinetics of  g '  rafting in CMSX-4 single crystals at 950 8 C has been studied using scanning and transmission electron microscopies. The crept material was subjected to further heat treatment in the absence of applied stress, in order to deduce the threshold strain  E th  for rafting to continue; this is shown to be 0.10 2 0.03%. For strains smaller than  E th , rafting occurs exceedingly slowly; once  E th  is exceeded the kinetics are largely una┬Ćected by the absence of applied stress. The magnitude of  E th  confers a reduction in g / g '  interfacial coherency and a relaxation of interfacial mis┬«t stresses. It is concluded that the threshold strain is a quantity suitable for distinguishing between rafting occurring in the \`\`elastic'' and \`\`plastic'' regimes. The results con┬«rm conclusively that plasticization of the horizontal  g -channels by  ┬ģ a = 2 ┬åh 110 if 111 g creep dislocations and their subsequent adsorption at the  g / g '  interfaces, with a concomitant loss of perfect coherency and reduction in elastic mis┬«t strains, is responsible for providing the kinetic path which enables rafting to occur at a reasonable speed. An argument is put forward to explain the magnitude of  E th .  # 1999 Acta Metallurgica Inc. Published by Elsevier Science Ltd. All rights reserved.  

# 1. INTRODUCTION  

Ever since the ┬«rst observations in the 1960s of directional coarsening of the g ' precipitates in nickel-based superalloys [1] the so-called  rafting e┬Ćect has received a considerable amount of atten- tion. It is well known that for a given alloy the direction of rafting can be reversed by changing the sign of the applied load [2, 3]. The morphological evolution is also in┬»uenced by di┬Ćerences in the lat- tice mis┬«t [4, 5]. The early observations were inter- preted in a quantitative sense by Pineau [6] who used the equivalent inclusion method of Eshelby [7] to design maps which identify the conditions of lowest elastic energy. However, as has been noted [8, 9], there are many simplifying assumptions introduced in this analysis; for example the elastic interaction between particles remains unaccounted for, the elastic properties are taken to be isotropic and no allowance is made for the Poisson contrac- tion. In fact, it appears that Pineau's model is in agreement with the experimental observations only if the  g '  particles are elastically sti┬Ćer than the matrix and this is not necessarily the case [3, 10]. Recently, Nabarro  et al . [11] have presented an ad- aptation of Pineau's model which removes a num- ber of the simplifying assumptions.  

However, in the past ┬«ve years or so there has been a growing realization that, at least in commer- cial superalloys, the elastic regime is very di┬ücult to detect and that rafting is always associated with an appreciable amount of plastic strain (e.g. Ref. [5]). This seems to have been appreciated by Carry and Strudel [12, 13] who observed that, in a negatively mis┬«tting alloy, coherency stresses are relaxed on the horizontal (more highly stressed)  g / g '  interfaces due to the dislocations created by creep defor- mation. Indeed the role of plasticity-driven ┬»ow was accounted for, at least in a macroscopic sense, in the model developed by Socrate and Parks [9]; the  g / g '  interfaces were, however, assumed to remain coherent. Ignat and co-workers [14, 15] appear to have been the ┬«rst to acknowledge the need for a micro-mechanical interpretation in which account is taken of the interaction between creep dislocations and the initially coherent  g / g '  interfaces, the relax- ation of mis┬«t stresses via the occurrence of mis┬«t dislocations and the evolution of the stable dislo- cation cell structures which have been observed in the  g  matrix after appreciable amounts of strain [16┬▒ 18]. This picture of rafting has been developed in a mathematical sense by Veron  et al . [19, 20]. Recent evidence [21] has con┬«rmed that a measurable extent of rafting coincides with the formation of mis┬«t strain-relieving dislocation networks. Recent theoretical considerations [22] have recognized the possibility of a transition from the elastic into a plastic regime, in which the thermodynamic driving force for rafting depends upon the product of the lattice mis┬«t and the applied stress, with the di┬Ćer- ences in elastic moduli appearing only as a second- order e┬Ćect.

# 2. EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURE  

The CMSX-4 single crystal material used here was provided by Rolls-Royce plc in the form of 1 cm diameter directionally solidi┬«ed rods, in the fully heat-treated condition. The chemical compo- sition of the CMSX-4 alloy is given in Table 1. The orientations of the rods were determined via the automatic indexing of back Laue patterns using the SCORPIO method [23]; only rods with axes within 10 deg of 001  were used, in order to minimize the e┬Ćects arising from misorientation. The initial microstructure after a standard heat treatment con- sists of about 70%  g '  precipitates with an average length of 0.35  m m on the (001) plane.

Creep strain testing was carried out using 20 kN constant load creep testing machines; such testing was compliant with the British Standard UDC 629.7 [24], to which the interested reader is referred. Interrupted creep tests were performed on the creep specimens under a ten- sile stress of 185 MPa at 950 8 C; the tests were inter- rupted at 150, 280, 550, 695, 1060 and 1170 h (samples A, B, C, D, E and F, respectively). For the determination of the e┬Ćect of stress on the kin- etics of the  g / g '  morphological instability, a further series of tapered specimens was tested, see Fig. 1(b). The testpiece geometry was chosen such that the stress varied by a factor of two along the specimen gauge length. 3.2. A stereological procedure for characterizing the degree of rafting  

Image analysis was carried out using a technique adapted from that reported elsewhere [14, 26]. The FEGSEM images were imported into a commercial image analysis software package (Kontron Elektronik KS300 version 2.00) and a regular square grid superimposed onto them. Intersections of the grid lines and  g / g '  interfaces were then used to de┬«ne a number of stereological parameters. These include  L  and  T  which represent the average mean linear length of precipitate in the directions normal and parallel to the tensile axis, respectively, and the mean  g  interlamellar spacing along the ten- sile axis which is denoted  S . Typical images with the grid lines superimposed are given in Figs 4(a) and (b).  

With this technique care needs to be taken to ensure that the periodicity of the square grid is opti- mal, so that systematic errors are kept to minimal levels. In order to determine an optimum period- icity, micrographs of sample C (crept for 550 h at 950 8 C and 185 MPa) were used as test images. Figures 5(a)┬▒(c) illustrate the e┬Ćect of grid period- icity on the measured parameters  L ,  T  and  S , re- spectively. The error bars quoted represent one standard deviation from the mean values. From Fig. 5, it can be concluded that periodicities greater than 1  m m yield signi┬«cant error bars for parameters L  and  S  whilst having little e┬Ćect on the parameter T . The grid periodicity chosen for future use was 0.10  m m (about ┬«ve times the pixel size and about one third of the initial  g '  size). Care also needs to be taken to ensure that the mesh is well aligned with the tensile axis of the sample. Figures 5(d)┬▒(f) illustrate the way that the values of the intercept parameters  L ,  T  and  S  vary when the mesh is rotated by angles up to 45 deg from perfect align- ment. The values of  L ,  T  and  S  vary within the range of the error bars provided that the misalign- ment is no greater than 10 deg; it is believed that this can easily be achieved in practice.

# 4. DISCUSSION AND RATIONALIZATION OF RESULTS  

The results from the experimentation raise var- ious points which warrant further discussion and rationalization. The various issues are taken in turn.  

# 4.1. On rafting in the elastic regime  

The thermodynamic driving force for rafting in the \`\`elastic'' regime arises from the di┬Ćerence between the elastic strain energy in the horizontal and vertical  g -channels, and has been discussed in some detail in Refs [11, 22]. The results reported here have shown conclusively that for accumulated creep strains of less than  0 0.1%, the rafting kinetics is almost immeasurably slow. Moreover, one cannot be sure that any rafting detected in this regime

# 4.2. On rafting in the plastic regime  

It would appear that plasticization of the hori- zontal g -channels by ┬ģ a = 2 ┬åh 110 if 111 g creep dislocations [5] and their subsequent adsorption of dislocations at the  g / g '  interfaces, with a concomi- tant loss of perfect coherency and reduction in elas- tic mis┬«t strains, is responsible for providing the kinetic path which enables rafting to occur at a reasonable speed. Thus, it would appear that models for rafting in the plastic regime [9, 22], which do not account for the micro-mechanical in- teraction of dislocations with  g / g '  interfaces, are incapable of describing adequately the phenomena which are responsible for the e┬Ćect. On the other hand, the model outlined by Veron  et al . [19, 20] is very realistic; it assumes that the mass transport is driven by gradients in potential arising from (i) di┬Ćerences in the elastic energy densities in the highly stressed horizontal channels in which the  g / g ' interfaces were taken to be relaxed and those in the vertical channels, which were assumed to remain coherent and (ii) creep ┬»ow of matter between the two types of channel driven by disparities arising from the di┬Ćerent states of stress developed. However, the present paper suggests that it lacks a treatment of (iii) the incubation period associated with multiplication of  ┬ģ a = 2 ┬åh 110 if 111 g  creep dislo- cations and their adsorption on the  g / g '  interfaces to form mis┬«t dislocations and (iv) the spacing of mis┬«t dislocations to be expected as a function of applied stress, accumulated strain and temperature. However, since it has been shown than  t th  is reason- ably small the assumption that rafting in the plastic regime occurs instantaneously is likely to be a good one.  

However, a question which remains concerns the kinetics of rafting in the plastic regime. Whilst the mechanism of rafting outlined in the previous para- graph can reasonably explain why rafting proceeds in the absence of an applied stress ┬«eld, why should the rate of rafting be largely independent of whether the applied stress is acting or not, as indi- cated by the data in Fig. 8(b)? One might have reasonably expected that the action of the applied stress for further time would allow the stable cellu- lar dislocation structures to be formed, of the kind reported in Ref. [16]. Moreover, it is well known that the mis┬«t dislocation networks produced during creep deformation have di┬Ćerent con┬«gur- ations and dislocation Burgers' vectors compared with those of mis┬«t dislocation networks produced entirely by lattice mismatch strains during ageing at zero applied stress [16, 17]. These factors should act to increase the kinetics of rafting in the plastic regime, over and above the rate seen when the stress is removed, since it is reasonable to expect the mass transport for rafting to be accomplished at or near the  g / g '  interface [28┬▒30], possibly by pipe or core di┬Ćusion. However, the rate of rafting is not slowed by the removal of stress, see Fig. 8(b). This observation can only be rationalized if any in- cubation period is associated with su┬ücient time and/or creep strain for relaxation of interfacial mis- ┬«t, with the spacing of mis┬«t dislocations not increasing markedly with further creep strain.  

The ideas outlined here suggest that the thermo- dynamic driving force and the kinetics of rafting in the plastic regime should depend rather strongly on the lattice mis┬«t  d . In the following section an attempt is made to relate the magnitude of the criti- cal strain  E  to  d  and  f , the volume fraction of the th g   0 g '  phase.

# 5. CONCLUSIONS  

The following conclusions can be drawn from this work:  

1. A procedure for the characterization of rafting kinetics has been presented. It makes use of a dimensionless surface ratio  R . Provided that suit- able precautions are taken,  R  appears to be a suitable and reliable quantitative measure of the extent of rafting.  

2. Subsequent annealing at 950 8 C of pre-crept (950 8 C, 185 MPa) CMSX-4 single crystals has revealed conclusively that there is a threshold strain  E th  necessary to induce signi┬«cant rafting. The magnitude of  E th  is 0.10 2 0.03%. Beyond this value, any extra strain appears to be redun- dant; rafting occurs even after the external load is removed.  

3. Even at the very smallest stress levels which were applied, the rate of rafting in material subjected to strains smaller than  E th  was barely detected during the experimentation.  

4. Transmission electron microscopy was used to con┬«rm that the  g / g '  microstructure deformed to strains smaller than  E th  remained coherent, even in regions where creep dislocations of Burgers vector  ┬ģ a = 2 ┬åh 110 i  had formed. Thus this may be termed the \`\`elastic'' regime. Beyond  E th , i.e. in the \`\`plastic'' regime, interface mis┬«t dislocations were present on all  g / g '  interfaces indicating that mis┬«t stresses had become at least partially relaxed.  

5. The results con┬«rm conclusively that plasticiza- tion of the horizontal  g -channels by  ┬ģ a = 2 ┬åh 110 i  f 111 g  creep dislocations and their subsequent adsorption of dislocations at the  g / g '  interfaces, with a concomitant loss of perfect coherency and reduction in elastic mis┬«t strains, is responsible for providing the kinetic path which enables raft- ing to occur at a reasonable speed.

 6. An argument has been put forward to explain the magnitude of  E th . This is based upon an esti- mate of the number of  ┬ģ a = 2 ┬åh 110 if 111 g  dislo- cations required to relax the interfacial  g / g ' mis┬«t.
