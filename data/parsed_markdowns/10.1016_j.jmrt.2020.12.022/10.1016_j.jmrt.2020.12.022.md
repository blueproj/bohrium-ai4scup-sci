# a b s t r a c t  

A novel near  a  high temperature titanium alloy (Ti55) was produced from powder compact through hot isostatic pressing. Electron beam welding (EBW) was used to join the Ti55 alloy powder compact and effects of annealing treatment on the microstructure and mechanical properties of EBW joints were investigated. The residual stress distribution of as-welded joint was calculated using ﬁnite element method (FEM). The results show that the micro- structure and microhardness proﬁles are non-uniform across the EBW joints. The tensile residual stresses are conﬁrmed across the as-welded joints based on FEM analysis. All of tensile specimens failed at the base metal, indicating that the mechanical properties of EBW joints are equal to those of base metal, or better. The tensile strength of as-welded and as-annealed joints increases slightly compared with base metal. An obvious reduc- tion on the reduction of area of EBW joints was obtained after annealing at 500    C. Related mechanisms were discussed based on the residual stress, tensile deformation and the strain rate sensitivity.

# 1. Introduction  

Titanium and its alloys are the promising structural materials for crucial load-bearing components in modern aerospace and chemical industries due to their high speciﬁc strength, outstanding corrosion resistance and excellent fatigue prop- erties [ 1 , 2 ]. However, it is very difﬁcult to join Ti-based alloys due to their high melting points and high reaction activity with oxygen at high temperature [ 3 e 5 ]. Therefore, high energy welding methods are needed to fabricate such structural components. Recently, a lot of studies [ 6 e 12 ] have been  

carried out and various welding methods of Ti-based alloys such as linear friction welding, friction stir welding and high energy-density beam welding have been developed to fabri- cate high-quality welding joints.  

Both linear friction welding (LFW) and friction stir welding (FSW) are solid-state joining processes [ 13 , 14 ]. The LFW pro- cess is an established technology for the fabrication of tita- nium alloy bladed disks and aircraft structural components [ 13 ] due to the economic beneﬁts compared with other weld- ing methods. However, it is noted that only the planar sur- faces can be joined by the LFW process so far [ 15 ]. Friction stir welding (FSW) process can produce various high-quality metal joints and ultraﬁne-grained materials [ 16 , 17 ]. Howev- er, it is still challenging to obtain sound Ti-based alloy joints by conventional FSW process due to the problem of over- heating [ 7 ]. Most previous studies have focused on the FSW joints of CP e Ti [ 18 ] and Ti e 6Al e 4V alloy [ 16 ], and quite a few investigations have been conducted on the joining of other Ti- based alloy such as Ti 2 AlNb alloy. Up to now, the fusion welding methods are still main techniques to join the Ti-based alloy components [ 19 ]. Compared with tungsten inert gas (TIG), the high energy-density beam welding such as laser and electron beam welding can provide high energy density and welding speed, low heat-input and produce the joint with narrow fusion zone and heat-affected zone, small distortion and low residual stress [ 20 e 23 ]. Besides, the high energy- density beam welding is usually performed under the vac- uum environment or the protective inert gas.  

Lots of recent studies on microstructure and mechanical properties of laser or electron beam welded CP e Ti, Ti e 5Al e 2.5Sn, Ti e 6Al e 4V, Ti e 6.5Al e 3.5Mo e 1.5Zr e 0.3Si, Ti- 6242, Ti-6246, Ti55531, Ti 2 AlNb alloys [ 11,19,20,24 e 27 ] have been conducted. The dissimilar joints and effects of post heat treatment have also been well investigated [ 28 e 31 ]. However, to the authors’ knowledge, the majority of the previous work have focused on the as-forged or as-rolled Ti-based alloys, and limited data have been reported for the powder metallurgy Ti- based alloys. Powder metallurgy (PM) through hot isostatic pressing can reach near-net-shape manufacturing, and the mechanical properties of Ti-based alloy powder components are comparable to those of wrought materials [ 32 e 34 ]. In our previous study, the weldability of PM Ti e 6Al e 4V alloy has been investigated [ 35 ]. In this work, a novel near  a  high tem- perature titanium alloy powder compact was prepared through PM-HIP route, and the effect of post-welding heat treatment on the microstructure and mechanical properties of EBW joints were investigated.

# 4. Discussion  

In this study, the fracture locations were found at BM for all the tensile specimens of EBW joints. The tensile specimens were cut across the transverse direction. According to the FEM analysis shown in  Fig. 9 , it is found that the tensile residual stress has been conﬁrmed in the parallel segment of tensile samples. It is expected the tensile strength is close to that of as-HIPed alloy. However, the tensile strength is a little higher than that of as-HIPed alloy. In addition, the reduction area of as-annealed tensile specimen at room temperature is only a half to that of as-welded joint. Therefore, the following dis- cussion will focus on tensile strength and the reduction of area of EBW joints.  

# 4.1. Tensile strength  

In this work, the tensile samples were prepared according to ASTM E8. The dimensions of tensile specimens were: diam- eter of 3 mm and gauge length of 15 mm. However, it is found that the microstructure across the EBW joint is non-uniform. In general, the EBW joint is divided to three zones: BM, HAZ and FZ. The microstructure of as-welded joint at FZ is made up of martensitic  a 0   phase. Previous studies [ 3,24 ] have proved that the microhardness of martensitic  a 0   phase is much higher than those of  a  and  b  phases for titanium alloys, which are consistent with the results obtained in this study. It is ex- pected that the tensile strength of FZ is higher than those of HAZ and BM.  

Zhao et al. [ 39 ] has reported that the yielding stress of FZ is higher than the fracture stress of BM, and the fracture stress of FZ is above 120 MPa higher than that of BM of laser welded joints of Ti e 6Al e 4V alloy using  in situ  tensile tests. In  Fig. 12 (b and c), the equivalent diameter at FZ of tensile specimens is still \~3 mm after tensile tests. It indicated that only elastic deformation occurs during the tensile tests for the FZ of the EBW joints. Here, we assume that the yielding stress of FZ is

higher than the fracture stress of BM. Therefore, the tensile specimens will be not always under the uniaxial load during the tensile tests. For the tensile specimens, when the tensile stress is below the yielding stress of BM, the elastic defor- mation occurs at BM, HAZ and FZ of EBW joint. The strain of FZ equals to that of BM. When the tensile stress is above the yielding stress of BM, the plastic deformation starts to occur at BM, while the elastic deformation occurs at FZ of the EBW joints. At the same time, the strain of BM is higher than that of FZ. As known, the volume of the material increases during the elastic deformation, but keeps a constant during the plastic deformation. Therefore, the  D S (reduction of the correspond- ing cross section) of FZ is less than that of BM. At this point, the EBW joints will be not under the uniaxial tensile stress during the further deformation. In order to keep the  D S continuously changing along the axial direction (from BM, HAZ to FZ), the extra compressive stress and tensile stress will be generated in the radial direction of FZ and BM, respectively. Fig. 13  shows the probable schematic of the stress analysis of tensile specimens during the tensile tests. When the applied stress is above the yielding stress of BM, the stress analysis of BM will transform from uniaxial tensile stress into biaxial tensile stress. An excessive stress should be loaded to ensure the plastic deformation due to the negative Poisson’s ratio. Therefore, the non-uniform deformation of BM, HAZ and BM will make a compromise, resulting in the slight increase of the tensile strength of EBW joints.  

Compared with the as-welded samples, the elongation of as-annealed tensile specimen increases and the tensile strength decreases slightly ( Table 4 ). This may be related to relief of the small-value tensile residual stress of EBW joint and the coarsening of  a  lamellae at FZ after annealing treatment.  

# 4.2. Reduction of area  

As shown in  Table 3 , it is interesting that the average reduc- tion of area of as-annealed tensile specimens shows an obvious reduction compared with the as-welded samples. However, the average elongation of as-annealed joints (14.2%) is a little higher than that of as-welded joints (11.5%). Ac- cording to the fracture surface examination, both the as- welded and as-annealed joints show the typical ductile frac- ture mode, and the dimples spread all over the fracture sur- faces. In  Fig. 11 , the working hardening and uniform plastic deformation occur for both as-welded and as-annealed tensile specimens before reaching the maximum tensile stress. The reduction of area of tensile specimens is mainly related to the necking. In the present work, multiple necking was found during the tensile tests of as-annealed samples, which may decrease the reduction of area of tensile specimens.  

As known, large neck-free elongation and high strain rate sensitivity (SRS) can be obtained in the tensile tests for su- perplastic alloy [ 40 ]. In general, the higher strain-rate sensi- tivity, the less necking. Therefore, the SRS exponent is considered as an essential issue on understanding the obvious difference on reduction of area of as-welded and as-annealed tensile samples.

# 5. Conclusions  

PM Ti55 alloy was welded using electron beam welding, and microstructure and mechanical properties of EBW joints were investigated for the ﬁrst time. The main results are summa- rized as follows:  

(1) The microstructure shows an obvious change across the EBW joints. The microstructure of BM is mainly made up of lath-like and equiaxed a phase, martensitic  a 0   phase for FZ, a mixture of lath-like and equiaxed  a  phase, martensitic  a 0 phase for heat affected zone of EBW joints. After annealed at 500    C, the martensitic  a 0   phase is transformed to lamellar  a and  b  phases. (2) The microhardness proﬁles show a non-uniform dis- tribution for Ti55 alloy EBW joints. The microhardness value of FZ is higher than that of BM due to the martensitic  a 0   phase and ﬁne lamellar  a  phase for as- welded and as-annealed EBW joints, respectively. (3) The tensile residual stresses are conﬁrmed across the as-welded joints based on FEM analysis. All the tensile specimens fail at BM of EBW joints. The slight increase of tensile strength of EBW joints can be attributed to the strengthening effect of FZ on BM compared with the as- HIPed alloy. (4) In this study, the obvious reduction of reduction of area of as-annealed joint compared with the as-welded joint is related to the multiple necking and higher strain rate sensitivity exponent.
