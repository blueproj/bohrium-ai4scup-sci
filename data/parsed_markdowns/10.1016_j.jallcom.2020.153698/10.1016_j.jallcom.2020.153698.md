# Abstract:  

In this paper, cyclic expansion extrusion with an asymmetrical extrusion cavity  (CEE-AEC) was proposed as a novel severe plastic deformation process for  fabricating bulk ultrafine-grained (UFG) metals. Increasing the size of the processed  sample and introducing the shear strain by attaching an asymmetrical extrusion cavity  are the core advantages of this technology. The CEE-AEC process was applied to  Mg-13Gd-4Y-2Zn-0.4Zr alloys for four passes deformation with the decreasing  temperature, and then the microstructure evolution, texture analysis, and mechanical  properties were investigated. Microstructure results show that a dramatical grain  refinement is achieved from initial value of 24 ± 0.3  µ m to 1.4 ± 0.3  µ m which is  mainly led by continuous dynamic recrystallization (CDRX) and discontinuous  dynamic recrystallization (DDRX) as well as the particle-stimulated nucleation (PSN)  induced by interdendritic long-period stacking ordered (LPSO) phases. With  increasing CEE-AEC passes, the intensities of basal texture are gradually weakened,  and the basal plane tends to incline to transverse and normal directions with different  degrees of different passes, leading to a remarkable increase in Schmid factor for the  activation of basal slip system. Tensile tests at room temperature (RT) indicate that the  samples after three-passes of CEE-AEC exhibit the best comprehensive mechanical  properties with tensile yield strength (TYS) of 302 MPa, ultimate tensile strength  (UTS) of 330 MPa, and fracture elongation of 6.1%, which are mainly ascribed to the  grain refinement, broken of interdendritic block-shaped LPSO phases, and texture  modification.  

Keywords:  Cyclic expansion extrusion with an asymmetrical extrusion cavity;  ultrafine-grained materials; Mg-Gd-Y-Zn-Zr; long-period stacking ordered phases;  texture; mechanical properties.  

# 1.   Introduction  

Severe plastic deformation (SPD) attracted more and more attention due to its  essential contributions to the preparation of ultrafine-grained (UFG) materials with  high strength-toughness [1-3]. Various SPD methods, like equal channel angular  pressing (ECAP) [4], high-pressure torsion (HPT) [5], accumulative rolling bonding  (ARB) [6] and cyclic expansion extrusion (CEE) [7], were developed in the last  decades, and lots of studies were carried out to prove their practicability and  feasibility. But, few of them were applied to serve the industrial application.  

Compared with other SPD methods, equal channel angular pressing (ECAP) and  high-pressure torsion (HPT) are two of the most mature technologies. Both of them  can impose high shear strain and produce the UFG metals with high strength [8-10].  However, it was apparent of their disadvantages in deformation characteristics, i.e.,  billets in small dimensions and the high requirement for equipment. Thus, they are not  the ideal technique in industrial application. But it was noteworthy that the  introduction of shear strain can influence the grain orientation and improve the  comprehensive mechanical properties [11, 12]. Based on the above consideration, we  can innovate an SPD method that combines the tensile stress, compression stress and  shear stress.  

Cyclic expansion extrusion (CEE) was a relatively new SPD process, which had  the advantages of simple dies set up and no need for external back pressure system [7,  13, 14]. Meanwhile, it is an effective way in fabricating the hard-to-deform materials  due to its three-dimensional compression stress [15, 16]. However, the processed  samples of CEE in researched papers were still in a relatively small size, and the lack  of shear strain also restrained the improvement of comprehensive mechanical  properties [17, 18]. The current study aims to resolve the limitation of CEE and  introduce the shear strain simply by attaching the asymmetrical die geometry while  still implementing the advantage of CEE which is shown to be combination of  compression and tensile stress [7]. We entitled the method introduced an  asymmetrical cavity to the CEE process as the cyclic expansion extrusion with an  asymmetrical extrusion cavity (CEE-AEC). The specific process is introduced in Fig.  1.  

In present work, new quinary hard-to-deform metals Mg-Gd-Y-Zn-Zr alloys  were selected to carry out the CEE-AEC process to complete consecutive four passes  deformation. This kind of alloy has been implemented in high end equipment  manufacturing due to its low weight, excellent performance and moderate corrosion  resistance [19-21]. Numerous researchers have applied the Mg-Gd-Y-Zn-Zr alloys to  various SPD process. Yu et al. [22] studied the DRX behavior of Mg-Gd-Y-Zn-Zr  alloy under torsion deformation, both deformation temperature and strain rate had a  significant influence in DRX and texture. Li et al. [23] investigated the  Mg-Gd-Y-Zn-Zr alloy processed by ECAP, and the results showed that the impact of  14H-LPSO phases on microstructure and properties. However, CEE-AEC, as a novel  SPD process, has not been studied on the Mg-Gd-Y-Zn-Zr alloy, and its effect on  microstructure and mechanical properties continue to be uncharted. Thus, we selected  hard-to-deform Mg-Gd-Y-Zn-Zr alloy to complete consecutive four passes, and the  resultant microstructure, texture evolution and mechanical properties were  investigated systematically.  

# 2.   Principles of the CEE-AEC  

The prominent characteristic of the new process is the introduction of the  asymmetrical cavity in the bottom die. The schematic diagram of the CEE-AEC  process is illustrated in Fig. 1. Compared with CEE, the main difference is the flow  rule of materials. It is known that the asymmetric extrusion can introduce the shear  strain, and the magnitude of strain is closely related to value H, which is shown in Fig.  1(a). Meanwhile, expansion and contraction angles  α   also influence the shear strain.  

The CEE-AEC process consists of five steps, and the sequences are illustrated in  Fig. 1. Firstly, the bulk-shaped block was located at the bottom of the die to carry on  expansion deformation. Then, the billet was placed into the die and expanded to fill  the cavity with the movement of the punch. Secondly, the block was removed, and  another billet with the same statement was put in. With the pressing going on, billet 2  gradually replaced the initial billet and filled the expansion chamber, as illustrated in  Fig. 1(c, d). Lastly, billet 2 filled the expansion zone, and the initial billet was  extruded out of the room. The shape of initial chamber   (L 1 ) was the same as the  extrusion room (L 3 ). Furthermore, the billet after one-pass CEE-AEC would be  rotated 180° along the extrusion direction (ED), and then be deformed repetitively to  achieve a required number of passes.

# 3.   Experiment procedures  

# 3.1 Materials and steps  

The  as-received  material  used  in  this  study  was  the  as-extruded  Mg-13Gd-4Y-2Zn-0.4Zr (wt. %) magnesium alloy with 480 mm in diameter and 500  mm in length. The chemical composition is shown in Table 1. The billets with  dimensions of 50 mm × 100 mm × 250 mm were wire cut from the center of the  as-extruded alloy. The decreasing temperature from 480 ℃  to 420 ℃  by a drop of 20 ℃   every step was selected to carry out the CEE-AEC process, and the schematic is  shown in Fig. 2. Tool steel with 50  Rockwell Hardness  ( HRC) was applied to  manufacture the main parts of the die, and the fastening connection was completed by  annealing screw. Geometric die parameters are L 1  = 50 mm, L 2  = 100 mm, L 3  = L 1 ,  α   = 138° and H = 5 mm like shown in Fig. 1(a). The 6300 KN hydraulic press machine  was applied to complete the process at a speed of 1 mm/s, and the friction between  billet and dies was reduced by oil-based graphite. Upon the completion of each pass,  the water quench was performed on the deformed samples to retain the microstructure.  The equivalent strain of one pass CEE-AEC was about 2.2, and the detail analysis is  shown in Fig. 6.

# 3.2 Finite element method (FEM)  

Finite element method is a critical approach to understand the stress-strain  distribution of materials, and some constructive suggestions on physical experiments  can be put forward. In our work, DEFORMTM-3D V 11.2, developed by SFTC  Company, was applied to simulate the deformation process of CEE-AEC. The  stress-strain curves of Mg-13Gd-4Y-2Zn-0.4Zr were imported into the software and  customized the required database. Then, the molds were imported through third-party  interfaces. The top die and bottom die were defined as rigid objects, and the  workpiece was defined as a plastic object with a mesh number of 80000. After that,  the constant friction of dies and workpiece was 0.3, the speed of top die was 1 mm/s,  and deformation temperatures were determined with the range of 480 ℃ -420 ℃  from  one-pass to four-passes.  

# 4. Results and discussions  

# 4.1 Microstructure of as-received alloy  

The microstructure of the as-received alloy is demonstrated in Fig. 5. As seen in  Fig. 5(a, b), the grain structure and phase distribution are heterogeneous, and the  average grain size is about 24 ± 0.3  µ m by calculating through linear intercept method.  The as-received alloy is mainly composed of the Mg martix, intragranular  lamellar-shaped phases, interdendritic block-shaped phases, white eutectic phases, and  cuboid-shaped phases. The lamellar-shaped phases and block-shaped phases have the  long period stacking ordered (LPSO) structure by the definition of TEM, and thirteen  additional spots between transmission spots shown in the SAED pattern is proved a  14H structure (Fig. 5(c, d)) [24]. The white eutectic phases have the face centered  structure, which is the report of  β -Mg 5 (Gd, Y) in Mg-RE alloys (Fig. 5(e)) [21]. The  high content of Gd and Y in the cuboid-shaped phases examined by EDS is well  match with the component of RE-rich phases (Fig. 5(f)) [25]. The texture based on  EBSD measurement of as-received alloy is shown in Fig. 5 (g, h). The coarse grains  show a trend of <21 1 0>//ED while the fine grains present a dispersed orientation. The  maximum intensity of basal texture is 6.7, and the contribution is mainly from the  coarse grains.

# 4.2 FEM analysis  

Fig. 6 illustrates the FEM simulation of the CEE-AEC process. A complete  process of CEE-AEC is represented by Fig. 6(a, b). It can be noticed that the strain  after one-pass of CEE-AEC shows an asymmetric hierarchical distribution along ED  where the central region has strain concentration, but the bottom zone more disperses.  Furthermore, the metal flow in the asymmetrical cavity zone bears a higher strain  concentration than the non-asymmetrical cavity region along ND and forms a strain  distribution migration. Besides, metals in these regions can be subjected to the shear  strain caused by the staggered flow. This non-uniformity strain distribution is  determined by the die structure and will be inhibited after multi-passes deformation.  The load evolution of top die during the CEE-AEC process is demonstrated in Fig.  6(c). As illustrated, the load in the expansion process has a relatively low value, and  the highest pressure reaches about 220 kN in the process of extrusion. Based on the  theory of plastic forming, the appearance of the highest load value is the point where  the sample has extruded through the die land. The strain distribution of cross-section  of the CEE-AEC processed sample shown in Fig. 6(d) proves the strain  non-uniformity of one-pass deformation. It can be observed that the side B, which  represents the asymmetrical zone shows a higher strain value than side A, and the  center zone has the lowest. The average equivalent strain is about 2.2. This  non-uniformity can influence the whole performance of materials; thus, multi-passes  of CEE-AEC are dramatically needed.

# 4.3 Microstructures of CEE-AEC processed samples  

Fig. 7 illustrates the optical microstructures after different passes of different  deformation zones. It can be observed that bimodal structures are obtained after  one-pass of CEE-AEC at 480 ℃  with the strain of 2.2 (mentioned at section 4.2), in  which the coarse grains are surrounded by fine DRX grains and form the “necklace”  structure. Meanwhile, the interdendritic block-shaped LPSO phases and intragranular  lamellar-shaped LPSO phases are broken and refined through the kink deformation,  which is commonly investigated in Mg-RE-Zn alloys with LPSO phases, which can  be ascribed to that basal slip is impeded and dislocations accumulate along lamellar  phases [26, 27]. Besides, the kink mechanism is operated to accommodate the strain  concentration. The microstructures in different zones vary a lot in average grain size  and the orientation. It can be observed that the grains in the asymmetrical cavity zone  receive higher deformation strain, and the grain refinement is stronger, which can be a  proof of FEM. After two-passes of CEE-AEC at 460 ℃  with the strain of 4.4, as  illustrated in Fig. 7(d, e, f), the limited number of coarse grains can be detected with  the increasing volume fraction of DRX grains. The refinement of secondary phases is  further intensified, including the block crushing and lamellar separation. More  significantly, the homogeneity of the microstructure is improved due to the route of  180° rotation of billet to complete the follow-up deformation, which can offset the  strain inhomogeneity caused by the former deformation. The coarse deformed grains  are completely consumed by the fine grains after three-passes of CEE-AEC and  four-passes of CEE-AEC, indicating the DRX progress is nearly complete. The  equiaxed DRX grains and broken LPSO phases are the main characteristics and  demonstrated after four-passes of CEE-AEC process. Due to the condition of  decreasing deformation temperature and the pining of broken LPSO phases at DRX  grain boundaries, the growth of grains is inhibited and remained a slightly increase of  1.6  µ m. Furthermore, the size of interdendritic block-shaped LPSO phases declines  with increasing CEE-AEC passes.

# 4.4 Texture characteristics  

The EBSD microstructures of CEE-AEC processed samples after different  deformation passes are depicted in Fig. 8. The dark regions illustrated in IPF maps  and grain orientation spread (GOS) maps represent the interdendritic block-shaped  LPSO phases, which cannot be defined due to the lack of phase parameters [28].  Meanwhile, the critical GOS value for DRX grains is identified as 1.8° [29], as shown  in Fig. 6(c, g, i, l). The low angle grain boundaries (LAGBs, misorientation angles  between 2° and 15°) marked with the red lines and the high angle grain boundaries  (HAGBs, misorientation angles higher than 15°) shown by the green lines are  demonstrated in Fig. 8(b, e, h, k).  

As seen in Fig. 8(a, d, g, j), a significant grain refinement with an initial grain  size of 24  ±  0.3  µ m reducing to 1.4 ± 0.3  µ m and a relatively dispersed grain  orientation is obtained after four-passes of CEE-AEC. The achievement of grain  refinement is dominated by DRX. After one-pass of CEE-AEC at 480 ℃ , a mixed  crystal structure, with about 25% of grains having sizes more than 20  µ m and 40%  less than 5  µ m, which represent deformed grains and DRX gains respectively, are  obtained. The deformed grains are elongated with a degree of 30° deviation from ED  which may be influenced by the shear strain, and a color gradient from grain  boundary to the center in the deformed grains also indicates a strong distortion [30,  31]. Furthermore, LAGBs are primarily distributed in the coarse deformed grains,  while HAGBs are mainly composed of DRX grain boundaries and some coarse  deformed grain boundaries. A few DRX grains can be observed near LAGBs in the  deformed grains, as highlighted by red arrows in Fig. 8(a). It is widely known that the  CDRX mechanism is characterized by progressive subgrain rotation [32, 33].  Furthermore, the serrated grain boundary can be observed along the deformed grains,  and it can be the sites where the occurrence of nucleation through grain boundary  bulging. This is the characteristic of DDRX [34]. From Fig. 8(d, g), it can be found  the grain structure are dramatically refined from 8.3 ± 0.4  µ m to 1.4 ± 0.3  µ m.  Meanwhile, the DRX volume fraction increase markedly up to 49.1% and 80.2%,  respectively, as illustrated in Fig. 8(f, i). Fewer and fewer LAGBs can be observed in  the scanned area with the refinement of coarse grains, as shown in Fig. 8(e, h). It can  be attributed to the increasing of incubation time for the nucleation and growth of  DRX grain due to the accumulative strain and lower deformation temperature. As  shown in Fig. 8d, small DRX grains can be observed at the bulged grain boundary,  which indicates the taken place of DDRX. Grain boundary bulging is strain-induced  grain boundary migration (SIGBM) [35]. Besides, the existence of DRX fine grains  around the block-shaped LPSO phases proves the taken place of the RE-related  particles-stimulated nucleation (PSN) (marked with red circles in Fig. 8(d, g)) [36, 37].  After four-passes of CEE-AEC at 420 ℃ , the coarse grains completely disappear, and  some DRX grains have slightly grown up, as shown in Fig. 8(j). It is because the  relatively high interpass time spent at deformation temperature and lower propensity  to recrystallization given by the fine grains. The similar results were also reported in  previous research works [38]. Meanwhile, it can be found that a significant  misorientation evolution happened with the increasing deformation strain, resulting in  a decreasing volume fraction of DRX. In comparison with three-passes deformation,  an increasing volume fraction of LAGBs in the equiaxed grains is obtained, as shown  in Fig. 8(k). The high-density dislocations in the grain boundaries will induce the  migration of subgrain boundaries and the nucleation of subgrains, then newly DRX  grains are obtained [30, 39].

Fig. 9 gives the corresponding EBSD-based (0001) pole figures of Fig.8 on the  ED-TD plane. The texture characteristics are divided into two kinds, i.e., deformed  grain orientations (Fig. 9(b, e, h)) and DRX grain orientations (Fig. 9(c, f, i)). The  alloy after one-pass of CEE-AEC shows a strong basal texture with the (0001) planes  of most grains are almost turned to parallel to ED (illustrated in Fig. 9(a)). From Fig.  

8(a), it can be found that 80% of the scanned area is occupied by the deformed grains  with the orientation of c-axis perpendicular to ED, while the DRX grains exhibit a  dispersed orientation. The resultant texture intensities of deformed grains and DRX  grains are 11.1 and 1.6, respectively. In final, the texture of one-pass of CEE-AEC is  still strong (8.0) due to the relatively low level of DRX. Meanwhile, in can be proved  that the DRX grains can effectively weaken the texture. With the processes going on,  the texture intensities have a considerable weakening, as shown in Fig. 9(d, g). This is  because that the increasing volume fraction of DRX grains can offset the strong  deformation texture of the deformed grains and make the texture more random.  Meanwhile, the same results have been reported that the weaken of the intensity due  to the DRX dispersed orientations were essential to the boost of properties [40-42]. As  the research of Zhang et al.   [43]   told that the accumulation of strain induced the high  fraction of DRX followed by the weakening of texture. Moreover, the (0001) planes  of more grains are inclined from ED, which can be related to shear strain introducing  from the asymmetrical cavity just like the investigation of Chang et al. [44]. The study  of Li et al. [23] in the Mg-Gd-Y-Zn-Zr alloy after ECAP process also told that shear  texture was obtained after 2p-ECAP due to the shear strain. The same results were  also referenced to the work of Tong et al.   [45] .  After four-passes of CEE-AEC, as  shown in Fig. 9(j), the texture intensity is basically unchanged (2.1). The decreasing  volume fraction of DRX inhibits the further weakening of the texture to a certain  degree. Still, the dispersed orientation of DRX grains is the main component of the  final texture, as shown in Fig. 9(l). Moreover, the texture feathering the alignment of  the basal planes locating at about 45° from ED towards ED is obtained.

The Schmid factor for (0001) <112 0> basal slip of CEE-AEC processed samples  in the center zone loading along ED is depicted in Fig. 10. It can be observed that the  value of the Schmid factor demonstrates a tendency improvement with the increasing  of CEE-AEC process. As widely known that the basal slip is the predominant mode at  room temperature deformation [46, 47]. The Schmid factor value in (0001) <112 0>  can reflect the tensile yield strength (TYS) [48]. To be specific, a higher average  Schmid factor value determines a lower TYS under the same condition. High Schmid  factor means the low stress for the operation of basal (0001) <112 0> slip and  materials yielding. In our investigation, samples after three-passes CEE-AEC and  four-passes CEE-AEC with higher Schmid factor are more favorable for the  dislocation glide on (0001) plane.

The schematic diagram was illustrated in Fig. 11 for better understanding of the  microstructure evolution of CEE-AEC. The as-received alloys with the heterogeneous  structure exist at the early stage. After one-pass of CEE-AEC, the bimodal structure  can be observed, the intragranular lamellar-shaped LPSO phases are kinked, and the  interdendritic block-shaped LPSO phases are broken. The kink of lamellar-shaped  LPSO phases facilitates the CDRX. Furthermore, PSN mechanism induced by  interdendritic block-shaped LPSO phases further increase the fraction of DRX. With  increasing CEE-AEC passes, the limited fraction of deformed grains can be obtained,  and the broken of interdendritic block-shaped LPSO phases are intensified due to the  accumulation of strain. At last, the equiaxed grains with the homogeneous distribution  of interdendritic block-shaped LPSO phases are observed. The S-type kink of lamellar  LPSO phases gives the proof of the strong accumulation of strain.

The tensile properties after different processing passes are illustrated in Fig. 12.  Table 2 gives the corresponding values of tensile yield strength (TYS), ultimate  tensile strength (UTS) and fracture elongation (A%). It can be observed that the TYS  increased from 202 ± 3 MPa to 345 ± 5 MPa, UTS increased from 235 ± 3 MPa to  386 ± 7 MPa after one-pass of CEE-AEC. However, slightly increasing of fracture  elongation was detected. The dramatically strengthening effect can be partially  ascribed to grain refinement from as-received (24 ± 0.3  µ m) to one-pass (12.2 ± 0.2  µ m) condition. However, the effect of fine grain strengthening plays a minor role due  to the lower volume fraction of DRX grains and the heterogeneous grain structure.  Besides, it is worthy noted that a strong basal fiber texture (8.0) with (0001) planes of  most deformed grains being parallel to the tensile direction was obtained after  one-pass of CEE-AEC compared with the texture intensity of 6.7 in the as-received  alloys. This results in the grains staying in the hard orientations, and a low Schmid  factor of 0.2 is obtained. The increase in the stress for the operation of (0001) <112 0>  slip system causes the development of TYS. Therefore, it can be known that the net  increase in TYS is mainly dominated by the effect of texture strengthening, while the  slightly increment may come from the DRX fine grains. Fig. 13(a) demonstrates the  fracture morphology of the tensile sample after one-pass CEE-AEC. It can be noticed  that the characteristic of the tensile sample is cleavage fracture with full of cleavage  steps and tearing edges in the scanned area. One hand, the damage of fracture  elongation is partially due to stress concentration caused by the accumulation of strain  and high-density dislocation at the interface between the coarse deformed grains and  DRX grains. On the other hand, interdendritic block-shaped LPSO phases which have  the characteristic of high strength and low ductility are easy to be the source of cracks,  and the heterogeneous of interdendritic block-shaped LPSO phases intensifies the  effect.  

After two-passes of CEE-AEC, interestingly, both TYS and UTS remarkably  drop from 345 ± 5 MPa and 386 ± 7 MPa to 296 ± 2 MPa and 323 ± 4 MPa,  respectively. Nevertheless, the fracture elongation has a significant increase from 2.9  ± 0.2% to 5.6 ± 0.3%. Considering the samples at two-passes condition have refined  grain structure to 8.3 ± 0.4  µ m, the Hall-Petch criterion effect is obviously not the  primary factor to drive the performance. It was reported that the TYS of Mg alloys at  RT was mainly determined by the operation of basal dislocation slip [49]. As seen in  Fig. 9(d), the inclination of about 20° from TD towards ED arranges a soft orientation  of most grains, corresponding to the increase in Schmid factor to 0.3 (Fig. 10(b)).  Therefore, it is suggested that the decreasing of tensile strength and increasing of  fracture elongation can be ascribed to the formation of new texture feathering the  inclination of basal planes towards ED, with notably higher Schmid factor (0.3),  resulting in lower yield stress for the operating of (0001) <112 0> slip system. In sum,  it can be concluded that the texture softening appreciably overwhelms the fine grain  strengthening. Fig. 13(b) illustrates the fracture morphology of samples after  two-passes of CEE-AEC, where numerous dimples and limited cleavage are obtained.  The spread of volume fraction of DRX grains releases the stress concentration, and  the increasing number of finer grain boundaries hinders the propagation of cracks.  

After three-passes of CEE-AEC, which has the higher accumulative strain (about  6.3) and the lower deformation temperature (440  ℃ ), it can be seen that the fracture  elongation has a further improvement (6.1 ± 0.2%), while no obvious increase in TYS  and UTS. It is remarkable that the UFG structure did not bring any substantial  increment of tensile strength. This is because the formation of more random texture  with a rotation of 45° of (0001) planes from TD towards ED, which contributes to the  increased ductility of Mg alloys by providing dramatically soft orientation with the  operation of basal <a> slip [50]. However, the flip side of this effect is the reduction  of tensile strength. Therefore, it can be stated that the strengthening in tensile strength  coming from the fine grain strengthening keeps a balance with the weakening  providing from the texture modification. However, the increasing of fracture  elongation may not eventuate in the case of Mg alloys containing RE elements due to  precipitation of secondary phases on basal planes [51]. This effect will cause even the  decreasing of ductility, which is in agreement with the ductility change after  four-passes of CEE-AEC. Considering the similar grain structure and texture intensity  as well as the almost identical values of TYS and UTS, the competitive balance  relationship between fine grain strengthening and texture modification weakening  keeps going on during the four-passes deformation. However, the excessive number  of particle phases precipitate along the boundaries due to the low deformation  temperature (420 ℃ ). These dynamic precipitations can worsen the ductility of the  samples after four-passes of CEE-AEC. The fracture morphologies after three and  four-passes of CEE-AEC are illustrated in Fig, 13(c, d), and the fracture zone is  occupied with numerous dimples which contribute to the significant improvement of  fracture elongation. Meanwhile, a relatively high amount of dynamic precipitations  and broken LPSO phases are obtained in the fracture zone. Garces et al. [52] found  that the reason for poor ductility in extruded Mg 88 Y 7 Zn 5  was the relatively high  content of Y and Zn elements. In our study, lots of white particle phases are  precipitated along the grain boundaries during the deformation at low temperature,  which results in the decline of fracture elongation, especially after four-passes of  CEE-AEC. As shown in Fig. 13(d), the fracture morphology after four-passes of  CEE-AEC proves the strong dynamic precipitations, corresponding to the poor  ductility shown in the stress-strain curves.

# 5. Conclusions  

In the present study, a novel SPD process entitled cyclic expansion extrusion  with an asymmetrical extrusion cavity (CEE-AEC) is introduced. The most core  advantage are the shear strain developed by attaching the asymmetrical extrusion  cavity to achieve the superior ability of grain refinement and improvement of  properties as well as the ability to fabricate large-size bulk metals for industrial  applications. The process was carried out on Mg-13Gd-4Y-2Zn-0.4Zr Mg alloy and  processed the 50 mm×100 mm×250 mm specimens, and analyzed preliminarily, with  several conclusions being observed.  

(1)   The CEE-AEC process promotes the development of DRX behavior of  Mg-Gd-Y-Zn-Zr alloy, resulting in a significant grain refinement, and a relatively  homogeneous grain structure with average grain size of 1.4 ± 0.3  µ m was obtained  after three-passes.  

(2)   The CEE-AEC process accumulated a high strain with the kink of  intragranular lamellar LPSO phases and broken of interdendritic block-shaped LPSO  phases, which facilitated the transformation from LAGBs to HAGBs and induced the  PSN mechanism.  

(3)   The texture characterization revealed that the basal texture was strengthening  at the first CEE-AEC stage due to the lower volume fraction of DRX grains. With  increasing CEE-AEC passes, the weakening of texture occurred, corresponding to the  higher volume fraction of DRX grains. Besides, with different extent of inclination or  spread of basal texture owing to the shear strain introduction by the asymmetrical  cavity was observed after two-passes of CEE-AEC.  

(4)   Tensile tests at room temperature revealed a noticeable enhancement in the  mechanical properties. After one-pass of CEE-AEC, the strong texture was a crucial  factor affecting the enhancement of the TYS and UTS. With increasing CEE-AEC  passes, the relatively steady of TYS and UTS can be attributed to the competitive  balance between fine grain strengthening and texture weakening. The improvement of  ductility was mainly ascribed to the new texture feathering the spread and titling of  (0001) planes of most grains from TD towards ED. Nevertheless, the dramatically  strong dynamic precipitations at relative low deformation temperature (420 ℃ )  worsened the ductility after four-passes of CEE-AEC.  

# 6. Outlook  

In this paper, we give an introduction of the CEE-AEC process and investigate  its effect on microstructure and mechanical properties of Mg-Gd-Y-Zn-Zr alloy. The  introduction of shear strain is the core of this method, and it is possible to control the  kinematic of metal flow by varying the structure of the chamber. Two examples of the  possible change in the container are illustrated in Fig. 14. In particular, the higher  altitude difference in working land, as demonstrated in Fig. 14(a), will increase the  shear strain and provides a more homogeneous strain distribution sample. Besides, the  tubular samples will be obtained by changing the die shape from bulk to cylinder and  adding the mandrel, as shown in Fig. 14(b).

·   Continuous and discontinuous dynamic recrystallization s took place during  CEE-AEC.  

·   The comprehensive mechanical properties were contributed from the competitive  of fine grains strengthening and texture modification.
