# A B S T R A C T  

The microstructural evolution, stress rupture properties and deformation mechanisms of Nimonic 105 alloy at  800   C within stress range of 350-200 MPa were investigated through a combination of scanning electron mi­ croscopy (SEM), transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and stress rupture tests. It is found that the rupture  time of the alloy increased from 201 to 9591 h as the stress decreased from 350 to 200 MPa at 800   C. During the  stress rupture tests, evident coarsening of  γ 0   precipitate and M 23 C 6  carbide occurred while the morphology and  distribution of MC carbide had no significant change. The growth of  γ 0   precipitates follows the LSW theory. TEM  observations showed that the deformation mechanisms of the alloy were Orowan bypassing, strongly coupled  dislocations (SCD) shearing and microtwinning during the stress rupture tests at 350-200 MPa/800   C, and  Orowan bypassing was the dominant deformation mechanism. In addition, the fracture mechanism of the alloy  was also studied. The results revealed that cavities nucleated mainly at the interface of carbides (M 23 C 6  and MC)  on the grain boundary (GB). Growth and coalescence of these cavities resulted into the formation of cracks, and  fracture of the alloy occurred by the cracks propagation along the GBs.

# 1. Introduction  

The rising demand for electric power and the strong desire to reduce  environmentally damaging gases (CO 2 , NO x , and SO x ) emissions require  the use of advanced ultra-supercritical (A-USC) power plants with  higher thermal efficiency than current power plants. The present ultra-  supercritical (USC) power plants operate at steam conditions of up to  about 620   C and 25 MPa with a maximum thermal efficiency around  45% [ 1 ]. In order to further enhance the thermal efficiency and decrease  the release of environmentally damaging gases, the next generation of  A-USC power plants is designed to operate at steam temperatures of  700 – 760   C and pressures up to 37.5 MPa [ 2 , 3 ]. This combination of  steam temperatures and pressures is expected to achieve over 50%  thermal efficiency. The current materials used for steam turbine blades  are mainly made of ferritic or austenitic heat-resistant steels, and their  maximum operating temperatures are around 620   C and 675   C [ 4 ],  respectively. Nimonic 105 is a wrought Ni – 15Cr – 20Co – 5Mo-4.7Al-1.3Ti – B – Zr  alloy principally strengthened by the precipitation of coherent, L1 2  type  ordered  γ 0   [Ni 3 (Al, Ti)] precipitates within grains and additionally by  grain boundary (GB) carbides precipitation [ 5 – 7 ]. The alloy has a good  combination of high temperature strength, creep rupture property and  resistance to corrosion and oxidation, and has been developed for ser­ vice up to 950   C. It has been primarily used for gas turbine blades, discs,  bolts and fasteners. Due to its superior high temperature mechanical  properties, the alloy has also been considered as one of the prime  candidate materials for A-USC steam turbine blades. High temperature  stress rupture property is a major concern for a blade material operating  in more severe A-USC environments. The stress rupture property of  Nimonic 105 alloy is largely dependent upon the precipitation of  γ 0 precipitate since it acts as strong barriers for dislocation movement.  Generally, the interaction of dislocations with  γ 0   precipitate is complex  and many deformation mechanisms have been identified, including  weakly or strongly coupled dislocations shearing, Orowan bypassing,  cooperative climbing and stacking fault shearing as well as deformation  microtwinning, etc. [ 8 – 12 ]. These mechanisms of deformation can vary  significantly relying on the  γ 0   precipitate size, applied stress and testing  temperature [ 13 – 16 ]. For example, Fu ’ s work [ 16 ] on a Co-rich nick­ el-based superalloy indicates that with increasing the precipitate size,  the deformation mechanism transfers from shearing the  γ 0   precipitates  by 1/2 < 110 >  dislocation pairs to stacking fault shearing and then to  Orowan bypassing during tensile tests at 500   C. Similar transitions of  deformation mechanism are also found in superalloy IN100 at 650   [ 17 ]. Viswanathan et al. [ 18 ] found that at low applied stress, the main  deformation mode of Ren e 88 DT superalloy at 650   C is microtwinning,  while it changes to shearing by 1/2 < 110 >  dislocations and Orowan  looping at higher applied stress. Therefore, it is necessary to understand  the deformation mechanism for improving the stress rupture perfor­ mance of nickel-based superalloys and for developing new advanced  superalloys. Previous studies [ 6 , 19 , 20 ] have reported the tensile prop­ erties and short-time creep properties of Nimonic 105 alloy. Presently,  however, long-term stress rupture properties and relevant deformation  mechanisms and fracture behavior of the alloy at intermediate temper­ ature have not been investigated yet.  

In present study, the microstructural evolution and stress rupture  properties of Nimonic 105 alloy subjected to a combination of tensile  stress and temperature have been investigated. Furthermore, an attempt  has also been made to analyze the deformation and fracture mechanisms  of the alloy in detail.  

# 2. Material and experimental procedure  

The Nimonic 105 alloy used in this study was supplied by Fushun  Special Steel Co., LTD. (Fushun, China) in the form of hot rolled bar of  45 mm in diameter and 2000 mm in length. The chemical composition  (in wt.%) of the alloy is given in  Table 1 . Samples were cut parallel to the  rolling direction from the bar and were then given a standard heat  treatment (SHT): 1150   C/4 h/water cooling þ 1060   C/16 h/water  cooling þ 850   C/16 h/air cooling. Cylinder specimens for stress rupture  tests with a gauge diameter of 5 mm and a gauge length of 25 mm were  machined from the standard heat-treated samples. These specimens  were then tested in air at 800   C with constant stresses of 350, 300, 250  and 200 MPa, respectively using a GNCJ-30 creep rupture strength  testing machine. After stress rupture tests, the stress rupture properties,  including rupture time, elongation (El) and reduction in area (RA) were  measured.  

Slices having a thickness of 2 mm were cut from the longitudinal  section of the stress rupture specimens, and then ground and polished.  These slices were etched either chemically with an etchant containing 5  g CuCl 2 , 100 mL HCl and 100 mL ethanol to reveal carbides and GBs or  electrolytic ally with an aqueous solution containing 40% HNO 3  to  reveal  γ 0   precipitates. The examination of microstructure was performed  using an Olympus GX51 optical microscope (OM) and a Zeiss Supra 55  scanning electron microscope (SEM). Fractographic observations were  carried out on the stress rupture specimens employing SEM. Trans­ mission electron microscope (TEM) observation was conducted using a  TECNAI G20 TEM operated at 200 kV. The TEM specimens were pre­ pared by a standard electro-polishing technique using a RL-I-type twin-  jet electropolisher. Slices of 0.3 mm in thickness were cut from the  longitudinal sections of the stress rupture specimens and ground to a  thickness of 30 – 50  μ m. Discs of 3 mm in diameter were punched out  from these thinned slices. Subsequently, these discs were electro-  polished in an electrolyte of 10% perchloric acid and 90% ethanol at  temperatures of -25 to -15   C. The size and volume fraction of  γ 0   phase  were determined quantitatively on the SEM images utilizing an image  analysis software, Image-Pro Plus. For each specimen, eight SEM images  were used to measure the  γ 0   size and volume fraction.

In order to determine the deformation mechanisms of the alloy at  different stresses, the deformation microstructures of the stress rupture  specimens were observed by means of TEM.  Fig. 6  shows the TEM mi­ crographs of the microstructures of the specimen after stress rupture test  at 800   C/350 MPa. It can be seen from  Fig. 6 (a) that a large quantity of  dislocations were induced in the matrix after stress rupture test at 800   C/350 MPa. During the stress rupture test, first of all, dislocations were  generated and glided in the matrix, and then stopped at the interface  between the  γ  and  γ 0   since the  γ 0   precipitates were very strong barriers  for the glide of dislocations [ 26 ]. As the deformation proceeded, a  number of dislocations bypassed the  γ 0   precipitates and many disloca­ tion loops were left behind, as seen in  Fig. 6 (a) and (b). The formation of  these dislocation loops decreased the effective interparticle spacing and  made it more difficult for the subsequent dislocations to move, thereby  improving the stress rupture properties of the alloy. Meanwhile, several  dislocation pairs were found in  Fig. 6 (b). In general, when dislocations  cut through the ordered  γ 0   precipitates, they must travel in pairs because  shearing of  γ 0   precipitates by the first a/2 < 110 >  matrix dislocation will  create an anti-phase boundary (APB) in the precipitates. Subsequently,  the second a/2 < 110 >  dislocation eliminates the APB when it passes  through the  γ 0   precipitates and the order in the  γ 0   precipitates is restored  again. Depending on the precipitate size and the spacing of two paired  dislocations, the  γ 0   precipitates can be sheared by weakly and strongly  coupled dislocations. For small precipitates, the spacing between the  paired dislocations is larger than the diameter of precipitate. This situ­ ation is considered to be weakly coupled dislocations (WCD) [ 27 ].  Conversely, strongly coupled dislocations (SCD) takes place when the  γ 0 precipitates become coarsened and the spacing of the dislocation pairs is  smaller than the precipitate diameter. In  Fig. 6 (b), the spacing of  dislocation pairs was about 10 – 40 nm, which was smaller than the  γ 0 precipitate size, and they could be considered as strongly coupled  dislocation pairs. Besides, some deformation microtwins developed in  the specimen after stress rupture test at 800   C/350 MPa, as illustrated

With the stress decreasing to 250 MPa, the TEM micrographs of  deformation microstructures were presented in  Fig. 8 . Dislocation  bypassing of  γ 0   precipitates by Orowan process could be clearly seen in  Fig. 8 (a) and (b) and dislocation loops with different sizes were left  behind, as denoted by red arrows. At the same time, several strongly  coupled dislocation pairs (denoted by black arrows) were also observed  in  Fig. 8 (a) and (b). It is well accepted that after a short thermal expo­ sure, when the  γ 0   precipitates are very small and coherent with the  matrix, pairs of mobile dislocations can cut through them. As the ther­ mal exposure prolongs, both the size of  γ 0   precipitates and the spacing  are increased, the shearing process becomes more difficult and less  possible, and Orowan bowing prevails [ 28 , 29 ]. In such case,   dislocations might bypass the  γ 0   precipitates more easily than shear  them. Moreover, some microtwins were found in  Fig. 8 (c). It is worth  noting that some ellipsoidal carbides were present on the microtwin  boundaries. SAED analysis indicated that these carbides were M 23 C 6  ( Fig. 8 (d)). Kim et al. [ 30 ] also reported the precipitation of M 23 C 6  carbides along microtwins in Ni-base superalloy 263. Hence, Orowan  looping combining SCD shearing and microtwinning were the defor­ mation modes at 800   C/250 MPa.

Although the  specimen had the longest rupture time at 800   C/200 MPa, the  γ 0   pre­ cipitates in the specimen were significantly coarsened (273.8   9 nm)   and the interparticle spacing became very large. As a result, the stress  required for dislocations to bypass the  γ 0   precipitates becomes quite low.  From  Fig. 9 (a), it can be clearly seen that dislocation loops surrounding  the  γ 0   precipitates were mainly observed in the matrix. In some cases,  there was a tendency for the moving dislocations with long segment to  bypass the  γ 0   precipitates which had been surrounded by loops before,  tending to form loops again around these precipitates, as indicated by  white arrows in  Fig. 9  (a). Disloca­ tions piling up at the interface of  γ 0   precipitates with the matrix were  also observed in  Fig. 9 (c). In  Fig. 9 (d), some microtwins were noticed in  the matrix. Accordingly, the mechanisms of deformation were also  Orowan looping, SCD shearing and microtwinning at 800   C/200 MPa.  

The above results of TEM investigation revealed that both Orowan  bypassing and SCD shearing as well as microtwinning mechanisms  existed simultaneously during stress rupture tests. Dislocation loops and  dislocations bowed around the  γ 0   precipitates were frequently observed  after stress rupture tests at 350-200 MPa/800   C, while strongly coupled  dislocation pairs and microtwins were observed with much less fre­ quency. Moreover, with the stress decreasing and the rupture time  increasing, dislocation loops were observed more frequently. Thus,  Orowan bypassing was the main deformation mechanism. During stress rupture tests, as dislocations bypass the  γ 0   precipitates,  dislocation loops are left around the precipitates. The formation of  dislocation loops reduces the effective spacing between the  γ 0 precipitates and makes subsequent movement of dislocation more  difficult, thus improving the stress rupture properties of Nimonic 105  alloy. When SCD shearing occurs, the stress is required for the disloca­ tion pairs to cut through the  γ 0   precipitates, which helps to enhance the  stress rupture properties. Based on the operative deformation mecha­ nisms in Nimonic 105 alloy, some methods can be employed to enhance  the stress rupture properties of the alloy. For instance, reducing the  γ 0 precipitate size and the spacing between  γ 0   precipitates by optimizing  heat treatment in order to increase the Orowan stress. Increasing APB  energy through controlling chemical composition to increase the CRSS  required for the coupled dislocations to cut through the  γ 0   precipitates  [ 8 ]. Furthermore, it has been reported that deformation microtwins can  enhance the strength of TMW-4M3 superalloy at high temperatures  without deteriorating other mechanical properties [ 32 ]. Thus, defor­ mation microtwins may contribute to improve the stress rupture prop­ erties of Nimonic 105 alloy.  

3.6. Fracture mechanism

In nickel-based superalloys, GBs and secondary phase particles are  the common locations for cavity to nucleate. During the stress rupture  tests, the equiaxial  γ  grains can slide with respect to one another, namely  GB sliding, leading to formation of GB ledges [ 33 ] ( Fig. 13 (a)). GB  sliding can give rise to stress concentrations both at the M 23 C 6 /GB and  MC/GB interfaces. These stresses concentrations may be sufficient to  nucleate cavities on the GBs, as shown in  Fig. 12 (a), (b), (d) and (e) and  13(b). On the other hand, the adhesion between carbides and the matrix  is relatively weak and separation easily occurs by decohesion at the  interface of carbide/matrix on application of the tensile stress, thereby  causing the formation of cracks at the GBs [ 22 ]. It is suggested that the  formation of wedge-type cracks results from GB sliding ( Fig. 13 (c)). The  stress state at GB triple points is close to three-dimensional tensile stress  condition. When GB sliding takes place, stress concentration will occur  at the triple junction of GBs if the deformation inside the grains is  uncoordinated with GB sliding. As the value of local increased stress  exceeds the GB cohesive force, wedge-type cracks can develop at the  triple point of GBs, as displayed in  Fig. 12 (c) and (f). Furthermore, a  large quantity of dislocations were generated and piled up around M 23 C 6  carbides at the GBs during stress rupture tests, as shown in  Fig. 5 (i).  Dislocation pile-ups against hard M 23 C 6  carbides will bring about stress  concentration at carbide/matrix interface and may also cause cavities  nucleation on the GBs, which is conformity with the Zener-Stroh  mechanism [ 34 ], as illustrated in  Fig. 13 (d). After the formation of  cavities, a number of factors such as diffusion, GB sliding and plasticity  [ 35 ] could offer a condition for the growth and coalescence of these  cavities, thus resulting into the formation of cracks. These cracks mainly  propagated along the GBs under the tensile stress and finally led to the  occurrence of fracture of the alloy.  

# 3.7. Stress rupture properties compared with other superalloys  

Fig. 14  compares the stress rupture properties of Nimonic 105 alloy  with other candidate alloys (Waspaloy and USC 141) for A-USC steam  turbine blades [ 36 , 37 ]. All these alloys are principally strengthened by  the precipitation of  γ 0   phase within grains. From  Fig. 14 , it can be esti­ mated that the 10 5  h stress rupture strength of USC 141 alloy at 750   C is  around 100 MPa, while the 10 5  h stress rupture strength of Waspaloy  alloy at 760   C is about 130 MPa, which is smaller than that (143 MPa)  of Nimonic 105 alloy at 800   C. Therefore, it can be concluded that  Nimonic 105 alloy has higher stress rupture properties than Waspaloy  and USC 141 alloys. This is because Nimonic 105 alloy has higher  γ 0 volume fraction (45 – 51%) compared with Waspaloy (25 – 30%) and USC  141 (10 – 15%) [ 36 , 38 – 40 ]. It should be noted that since Nimonic 105  alloy contains a high  γ 0   volume fraction and large amounts of alloying  elements ( Table 1 ), the deformation resistance of the alloy is large and it  is difficult to process as large forgings. Besides, the alloy is not easily  weldable. Consequently, suitable processes should be used to prevent  the alloy from cracking during hot deformation.
