# Building a rigid-soft coupling interphase by a reduction – oxidation collaborative approach with lithium di ﬂ uorobis(oxalato) phosphate additives †  

abc  Shumin Wu,  Jingni Li,  Yulong Zhang,  Yu Zhang, Peng Wang, Xiaoling Cui, abc  Chunlei Li,  Dongni Zhao abc  and Shiyou Li \* abc  

Optimizing the properties of solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) layers on graphite electrodes is a crucial method for in ﬂ uencing the performance of cells. However, most studies ignore any potential synergistic e ﬀ ects of the reduction and oxidation products of electrolytes and only focus on their individual properties. In this study, we have formatted the pure reduction and oxidation products of lithium di ﬂ uorobis(oxalato) phosphate (LiDFBOP) on highly oriented pyrolytic graphite (HOPG) and lithiation HOPG surfaces and proved that there is rigid and high viscoelasticity, respectively. Accordingly, a cracked reduction product forming layer is built at a high discharge current. During the following charging process, a low current is used to oxidize LiDFBOP, generating oxidation products that strongly attach to the crack inside. Finally, a multifunctional SEI layer with a gomphosis-like structure is constructed, which prevents lithium dendrite degradation, improves the stability of the interphase layer, and signi ﬁ cantly increases the capacity retention of graphite half cells (from 50.2% to 96.7% at 0.5C after 200 cycles). This research opens up new possibilities for logically controlling the SEI production process and clari ﬁ es the high-power LIB design strategy.

# 1. Introduction  

In recent decades, lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) have had a stranglehold on the marketplace of portable devices, electric vehicles, and base stations due to their high energy density and security. – 3   Since almost all electrochemical reactions occur at the electrode – electrolyte interface, the properties of the solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) have a signi  cant impact on how well cells work. – 6   An e ﬃ cient SEI layer should typically have both Li +   conductivity and electronic insulation to prohibit side reactions between electrodes and electrolytes, stop hydro  uoric acid from eroding electrode materials, and prevent electrolysis of electrolytes on electrode surfaces. – 11   Additionally, SEI layers should be mechanically strong enough to prevent the growth of Li dendrites, which could result in safety risks and cycle life degradation.   Commonly, Li dendrites can be e ﬀ ectively suppressed when the Young's modulus of SEI layers is up to 4 GPa, while most SEI possesses only 50 – 400 MPa.   Great  

Researchers may have lopsided knowledge because the feature of oxidation products of additives on graphite anodes has always been ignored, which would prevent them from taking full advantage of their advantages. We attribute these problems to the challenge of preparing pure electrolyte oxida- tion products on graphite electrode surfaces. Speci  cally, during the   rst discharge process of half cells, blocks of reduction products of electrolytes are generated on the graphite electrode surfaces. This prevents electrodes from coming into contact with electrolytes, weakening the oxidation reaction of electrolytes and producing an undetectable oxidation product signal in measurement   ndings, even in  in situ  spectral anal- ysis. In addition, the oxidation pathway of electrolytes may di ﬀ er on di ﬀ erent electrodes; thus it is hard to analyze the properties of oxidation products by using an electrode other than graphite.  

In this study, we prepared pure reduction and oxidation products by an electrochemical method. It supported us in engineering electrolytes to create a rigid-so  coupling SEI layer with a gomphosis-like structure. Due to its strong SEI-forming capabilities at both cathodes and anodes, di  uorobis(oxalato) phosphate (LiDFBOP) was chosen as the ideal lithium salt additive in the controlled electrolyte.   Then, during the   rst discharging at a high current density, a LiF-rich incipient SEI layer that is cracked and rigid was built by the reduction of LiDFBOP in LiPF 6 -based electrolytes. And in the following initial charging process in the low-current mode, the preferred LiDFBOP additive was oxidized in the speci  c region where it could come in direct contact with the surface of the graphite electrode. Accordingly, the cracks of the incipient SEI layer were  lled with so  phosphates, generating a favorable SEI layer simultaneously containing reduction and oxidation cross- linked products. Compared with the traditional multi-layer structure, the as-generated SEI layer with a gomphosis-like structure combines multiple functions of high modulus and good viscoelasticity. Precisely bene  ting from this multifunc- tional SEI layer, the capacity retention of the cell has increased from 50.2% to 96.7% at 0.5C during the   rst 200 cycles. This work proposes a rational design strategy for constructing a stable SEI and provides guidance for further electrolyte design and interfacial property optimization.

# 2. Results and discussions  

# 2.1. Preparation of reduction and oxidation products  

Fig. 1A shows the preparation process of pure reduction and oxidation products of the LiDFBOP-added electrolyte. Compared with the original sample (Fig. 1B), a large number of crystalline reduction products cover the HOPG electrode surface a  er the discharging process, as shown in Fig. 1C. In addition, a rhagadiform surface with obvious cracks is observed, illus- trating that the reduction-products-dominated   lm is incapable of resisting the pulling stress of graphite expansion. Corre- spondingly, a  er scraping o ﬀ the reduction products layer and continued charging, Fig. 1D shows that the electrode surface is coated with a crossing gel-like sediment   lm without obvious cracks, which is interpreted to represent the oxidation products of the LiDFBOP-added electrolyte (Fig. 1D and S2 † ). In contrast, both reduction and oxidation products of controlled electrolytes are disorganized and riddled with numerous cracks (Fig. S3 † ), indicating that the decomposition products of the controlled electrolyte hardly maintain their own integrity during the expansion and contraction. In addition, this suggests that LiDFBOP may be the most important element in the mechanical property changes of SEI layers. These distinct morphological di ﬀ erences inspire our increasing exploration to identify the decomposition products in di ﬀ erent electrochemical processes and illustrate their interaction mechanisms in   lm formation.  

# 2.2. Characteristic property di ﬀ erences of the reduction and oxidation products  

The atomic force microscopy (AFM) analysis was used to further distinguish the mechanical properties of di ﬀ erent products. AFM height images in Fig. 2A show the test area, which, according to the SEM images in Fig. 1C and D, displays the spotted particles of reduction products and gel-like sediment of oxidation products. The force curves as a function of tip-surface distance during the approach-retract process are shown in Fig. 2B. For the reduction products, the approach-retract curves are almost overlapped, suggesting that it is sti ﬀ and cohesion- less.   Additionally, its elastic modulus is greater than 160 GPa, as shown in Fig. 2C, showing that the reduction-product- dominated SEI layer is not susceptible to distortion and may inhibit lithium dendrite piercing.   In stark contrast, the oxidation products exhibit a substantial irreversibility between approach and retreat curves and deliver a low elastic modulus of just about 900 MPa. This suggests that the oxidation products are highly viscoelastic and more   exible.   In other words, the oxidation-product-dominated SEI layer can adhere to the elec- trode surface and deform when the graphite particles expand, rather than brittle failure and cracking. The discrepancies between the mechanical characteristics of the reduction and oxidation products are consistent with the SEM   ndings.  

By analyzing the composition of reduction and oxidation products with X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), the major constituent factor in  uencing their mechanical proper- ties was identi  ed (Fig. 2D). In the C 1s pro  le, in comparison with the reduction products, a new peak centered at 285.3 eV

In the P 2s spectra, both products contain the peaks of Li x PF y O z  at  ∼ 137 eV. In particular, the P – O stretching in Li x PO y F assigned to the band at around 1057 cm − 1   in the FTIR spectrum is observed in both the reduction and oxidation products, which is consistent with the XPS   ndings,   while the peak at about 1192 cm − 1   corresponding to the P – O vibration in P sp 3   reveals that the P atom in the oxidation products of LiDFBOP-added elec- trolytes likely exists in PO 4 -like forms.   Moreover, compared with the FTIR results of the reduction and oxidation products (Fig. S5 † ), the parts of phosphate and   uorate phosphate are generated by the oxidation and reduction of LiDFBOP. TOF- SIMS surface pro  les of oxidation products (Fig. 2F) support this conjecture, in which the PO, PO 2 , PO 3 , PF 2 O 2 , Li 2 PO 2 , Li 3 PO 3 , and Li 4 PO 4  fragments are detected. Therefore, we determine the unique phosphate (PO 4 3 − ) really exists in the oxidation products, which has been proved to have excellent ion conductivity and electron insulation.   EIS and leakage current measurements shown in Fig. S6 †  demonstrate that phosphates in the oxidation product SEI layer contribute to stabilizing SEI and decreasing interfacial resistance. Moreover, the C 2 O 2 H 3  fragment in Fig. 2F and the CO characteristic peak in Fig. 2E indicate that there are more organic compounds in oxidation products to increase the   exibility of SEI layers, which is consistent with the C 1s of XPS data. From the above component analysis, it is reasonable to conclude that the di ﬀ erences in LiF, organics and phosphate content between the reduction and oxidation products are responsible for the various attributes.

Furthermore, the candidate decomposition pathways of LiDFBOP were calculated using DFT calculations conducted at the B3LYP/6-31G(d,p) level. During the single- electron reduction of LiDFBOP, the P – O and P – F bonds will break to release unstable PF 2 2 − anions, which will continue to react with Li +   to form LiF. However, two pathways exist for the oxidation of LiDFBOP. First, the DFBOP-anion will lose an electron and then the C – C single bond will break, forming S1. And it has been demonstrated that S2 reacts spontaneously with EC to produce organic polymers containing acetoxyl (CH 3 COO – ) n (path 1). On the other hand, S1 can also undergo C – O bond breaking, releasing CO and forming the S3 free radical, thus preserving the structure of PO 4 − and   nally generating phos- phates in the subsequent reaction (path 2).  

# 2.4. Building an SEI with a gomphosis-like structure  

To fully utilize the advantages of both the reduction and oxidation products of LiDFBOP, we built an SEI layer with a gomphosis-like structure. Initially, the graphite half-cell was discharged at a high current in the LiDFBOP-added electrolyte to produce an initial SEI layer with numerous cracks.   Then, the cell was charged at a low current to allow the oxidation products of the electrolyte to completely   ll the fractures in the initial SEI.  

As illustrated in Fig. 4A, the initial discharge curves at various current densities were measured to optimize the discharge current. Meanwhile, FESEM images illustrate the di ﬀ erence in morphology between SEI layers created at di ﬀ erent current densities. When the current density increases from 0.1 mA cm − 2   to 0.2 mA cm − 2 , both discharge curves display the same two separate platforms; however, the SEI layer at 0.2 mA cm − 2   appears to contain more holes and   ssures. Compared with the SEI in the controlled electrolyte, SEM images in Fig. 4C reveal that the graphite surface in the LiDFBOP-added electrolyte is uniformly covered with a thicker and denser SEI layer.  

Next, TOF-SIMS was utilized to display the composition and spatial distribution of the planned SEI. Fig. 4D depicts the normalized content pro  les of the primary ion fragments in the various SEIs generated in the electrolyte with and without LiDFBOP, both of which display a double layered structure as the LiF percentage in the outer layer is signi  cantly lower than that in the inner. This is attributesd to the LiF rich reduction products of LiDFBOP, which are formed preferentially on the graphite electrode surface.   Furthermore, as the principal oxidation products, the ion fragments associated with PO 4 3 − (PO –  and P 2 O 4 – ) account for about 0.2% in the outer layer and less than 0.1% in the deeper layer in the SEI with controlled electrolyte. But for the SEI of LiDFBOP-added electrolyte, the proportion of such two fragments decreases from 0.9% to 0.5% and 0.3% with an increase in the sputtering time , respectively. The above data indicate that the majority of the phosphates in the outer layer are the products of oxidation decomposition of LiDFBOP. And in the inner layer, a few phosphates are gener- ated in part by the decomposition of LiPF 6  and others by the oxidation of LiDFBOP in the cracks in the initial SEI layer.  

Additionally, for the SEI layer of the LiDFBOP-added sample, the organic fragments of CO 2 H –  are more prevalent in the outer layer and less in the interior layer. The number of organics in the outer layer of the LiDFBOP-added SEI (2.7%) is signi  cantly higher than that of the controlled electrolyte (0.14 – 0.23%). This clari  es the bene  cial e ﬀ ect of LiDFBOP oxidation on increasing the content of organics in SEI layers.  

The 3D spatial distribution pro  les of the main oxidation products in the SEI are shown in Fig. 4E. The CH –  groups have been considered as the marker of the SEI   lm because they are common in electrolyte decomposition products but absent in graphite. Interestingly, PO 4 3 − (PO –  and P 2 O 4 – ) and organic compounds (CO 2 H – ) in the SEI layer are wedge-shaped in the longitudinal direction, indicating that LiDFBOP was oxidized and its oxidation products   lled in the cracks of the initial SEI layer. In other words, we believe that we have constructed an SEI layer with a gomphosis-like structure on the surface of graphite electrodes, which is formed by wedging the viscoelastic oxida- tion products of LiDFBOP into its rigid reduction products.

# 3. Experimental section  

# 3.1. Materials preparation  

The controlled electrolyte (1 M LiPF 6  in 1 : 1 ethylene carbonate (EC)/dimethyl carbonate (DMC)) was purchased from Chaoyang Yongheng Chemical Co., Ltd. 0.1 M LiDFBOP (Macklin Inc.) was added to the controlled electrolyte to constitute the electrolyte required. Highly oriented pyrolytic graphite (HOPG, XFH16) was purchased from Nanjing XFNANO Materials Tech Co., Ltd. 2025-type coin cells were assembled in an Ar-  lled glovebox. Graphite-based electrodes consisted of 84 wt% active graphite, 8 wt% conductive acetylene black, and an 8 wt% polyvinylidene  uoride (PVDF) binder.  

# 3.2. General characterization  

Morphology observations were obtained using   eld-emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM, SU8010, Japan). Atomic force microscopy (AFM, Dimension Icon, BRUKER) was used to investigate the surface morphology and analyze the mechanical properties of the SEI layer on graphite anode surfaces. The reduced modulus of surface layers was obtained in peak force QNM mode with sharp AFM tips (BRUKER RTESPA-150). The scan area size was for AFM 2  m m  ×  2  m m. Surface elemental analysis was performed with Axis Ultra DLD XPS equipment (Kratos Analytical Ltd.) and Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy in the frequency range of 400 – 4000 cm − 1 . The surface element information was collected using a time-of-  ight secondary ion mass spectrometer (TOF- SIMS, PHI nanoTOF II Time-of-Flight SIMS).  

# 3.3. Theoretical calculations  

The quantum chemical calculations were employed using the Gaussian 16 so  ware to study the structural changes during electrochemical reactions.   The molecular geometry optimiza- tion was used with the B3LPY nonlocal correlation function at the 6-31G(d,p) level. The frequency calculations were performed at the same level to validate whether the optimized structure was at the lowest-energy state. The energy and wavefunction of molecules were considered for the 6-311G(d,p) basis set. Moreover, the Multiwfn program was used for wave function analysis, including Laplacian analysis of electron density distribution analysis.   First principles calculations were per- formed to analyze the molecular structure and di ﬀ erential charge density distribution of adsorbed structures by using MedeA-VASP so  ware  via  plane-wave pseudopotential forma- tion and with the generalized gradient approximation of the Perdew – Burke – Ernzerhof (PBE) form. For the optimization of structures, a 500 eV cuto ﬀ energy, 10 − 6   eV energy convergence accuracy, and 3  ×  5  ×  1 Monkhorst – Pack  k -mesh are utilized.  

# 3.4. Electrochemical evaluation  

Electrochemical performance tests were carried out on a LAND CT2001A tester (Wuhan, China) with a potential range of 2.00 – 0.01 V in the CR2025 cells. The LSV determinations were measured on a DONGHUA DH7000 electrochemical worksta- tion (Jiangsu, China) with a three-electrode electrochemical system, in which the as-prepared graphite electrode was used as the working electrode, lithium foil as the counter electrode and a lithium ring as the reference electrode, respectively. The leakage current test method of the battery was that the graphite half cells a  er the initial SEI formation cycle were   rst charged to 2.0 V at 0.1C, and then charged at 2.0 V for 3 hours. And the EIS measurements were performed over a frequency range from 10 5   to 10 − 2   Hz with an amplitude of 5 mV.  

# 4. Conclusions  

Based on the electrochemical and mechanical property di ﬀ er- ences between the reduction and oxidation of LiDFBOP, we devised a gomphosis-like structure for a rigid-so  coupling SEI layer. In such layers, a crack-containing initial SEI was formed by the reduction products of LiDFBOP-added electrolyte that was rich in LiF and su ﬃ ciently rigid to restrict dendrite growth. The LiDFBOP additive was then oxidized in the cracks of the initial SEI layer to generate a high viscoelasticity, organic- and phosphate-rich wedge cover that could improve the mechanical properties of the interphase layer. This multifunctional SEI could guarantee outstanding electrochemical performance for LIBs, with capacity retention improving from 50.2% to 96.7%, and resistance and leakage current drastically lowered. This study provides an approach to the design of functionalized SEI layers. Future research will focus on identifying electrochemical activity additives, understanding their   lm-forming mecha- nisms, and realizing their e ﬃ cacy-enhancing properties.
