# The Investigation of the Fatigue Failure of Nickel-Based Superalloy 718  

# Choke Valve Stems Used in Offshore Subsea Water Injection Service  

Muhammad Ejaz 1 , Essam R.I. Mahmoud , Sohaib Z. Khan  

1  Corrosion and Integrity Department, REAN Consultants Ltd, 15 Concraig Place Aberdeen,  AB158LH, UK 2  Department of Mechanical Engineering, Faculty of Engineering, Islamic University of  Madinah, 41411, Saudi Arabia 3  Central Metallurgical Research and Development Institute (CMRDI), Cairo, Egypt 4  Department of Engineering Sciences, PN Engineering College, National University of Sciences  and Technology, Karachi 75350, Pakistan  

# Abstract  

This paper analyse the failure of two choke valve inserts from offshore subsea service in the  Northern North Sea. The choke valve stems were manufactured from Nickel-based  superalloys 718 and coated with WC. The design life was 25 years. Treated seawater was  injected at a rate of 5,600 m 3 /d for about 3 years, and then water injection rate was increased to  7,360 m 3 /d in next 4 years with an addition of produced water from the field. Maximum design  flowrate for the choke was 8,320 m 3 /d. The first choke was failed after about 7 years in  operation. The choke was removed and replaced by a second choke of same design and material  of construction, which failed after less than 12 months service. The failure occurred almost at the  same location at the hollow stem section where pressure balancing port holes were drilled. The  analyses were concentrated on the fractography as well as materials chemical, mechanical and  metallurgical evaluation. All the materials examination demonstrated that the failed stem  material complied with API 6A718 and had been heat-treated correctly. The diagnose shows that  fatigue was the primary failure mechanism. Fatigue initiation occurred at a location on a port  owing to particularly high local tensile stress concentration. The main drive of fatigue crack  initiation and propagation, in this case, appears to be the loading cycle. Fractography (beach  marks) indicated that high frequency low amplitude fatigue mechanism was active. The balance  port is recommended to be moved to the top of plug where local tensile stress concentration is  inticepated to be minimum.  

Keywords:  Nickel-based superalloys 718; Subsea Water injection; Fatigue Failure; Stress  concentration; Cyclic Loading.  

# 1. Introduction  

Nickel-based superalloys, especially Alloy 718, is widely used in various severe applications,  such as rotating parts for aircrafts and spacecraft, nuclear reactor core components, power  generation turbines, and oil and offshore gas equipment, due to its good toughness, superior  corrosion resistance and exceptional mechanical properties at elevated temperatures even close  to its melting point [1-2]. To use Alloy 718 in the oil and gas industry, it needs single-step ageing  heat treatment to withstand the high pressure, fracture toughness and temperature and more  corrosive environment in sour oilfield conditions [3-5].  These superior properties depend on the  precipitation of many carbides/intermetallics during the complex and accurate heat treatment  cycle. Any poor control of this heat treatment can easily lead to the formation of undesirable  brittle phases within the microstructure, principally the niobium-rich phase, delta (Ni 3 Nb), which  will degrade the toughness of the alloy and can lead to brittle fracture [4]. However, most of the  catastrophic oilfield failures of Ni-alloy components, by two main modes; fatigue failure under  cyclic loading [6-7] and hydrogen embrittlement in components subjected to cathodic protection  [1-2]. Fatigue failures considered one of the most catastrophic modes of failure as an  accumulation of damage at the micro-structural scale occurs even under subcritical loads [8]. The  phenomenon of fatigue, which involves two main phases of crack nucleation and cracks  propagation, is very complex [9]. Geometrical discontinuities can lead to localised stress  concentrations, which drive fatigue cracks initiating and propagating [10-11]. Also, the fatigue  failure is more easily nucleated at the highest stress concentration sites (stress raisers) on the  surface at any defects such as notches, holes, scratches, corrosion pits, weld undercut and even  high roughness [6,12-13]. In some cases, fatigue failures can be induced from the interior at  material imperfections such as non-metallic inclusions, pores or non-homogenous  microstructures on the one hand and stress concentration areas, e.g., cladding interface, on the  other hand [14]. In this case, interior cracks can be initiated and propagated by the help of stress  concentration and the cyclic loadings. However, the crack propagation rate can increase by one  or two orders of magnitude when the magnitude of cyclic loading is increased [15-16].  

This work presents results of the failure analysis of two choke valves that made from Nickel- based superalloy 718 that fractured almost in the same location during offshore water injection  before serving a fraction of its design life. The attention was focused on analyses of the material  properties/quality and the fracture zone.  

# 2. Failure History  

Two 4" choke valve inserts were failed during offshore water injection service in the Northern  North Sea. One failed after about 7 years of service and the other one failed just after less than 12  months of service. The design life was 25 years. The choke valves (34.5 MPa rated) were  designed in accordance with API 6A with class HH internals for specified flowrates of 5,000 –  8,320 m 3 /d (cubic meters per day) at 16.9 MPa and 4 – 76   C [17]. All equipment was required to  be suitable for sour service. For a subsea water injection well, treated seawater was injected at a  rate of 5,760 m 3 /d and then increase to 7,360 m 3 /d by adding produced water into a water  injection facility. Water comes from the bottom of the choke where it is choked by plug shown in  Fig 1. Balance ports are provided in the plug/stem to balance water pressure above and below the  plug. Water coming from bottom apply hydrodynamic forces on the plug, which is controlling  water flowrates. Slight variations in these hydrodynamic forces may cause cyclic condition for  fatigue to occur. The first choke insert (indicated by the first choke) was failed after about 7  years in operation. The choke insert was removed and replaced by the second choke insert  (indicated by the second choke) which failed after less than 12 months service. The choke valves  were manufactured as one-piece; stem and plug from Nickel-based superalloys Alloy 718 round  bar. After machined to the dimensions, tungsten carbide coating was applied to the stem and an  overlay clad of “Stellite HS6” was deposited on the plug. The choke valves, as shown in Fig. 1,  were fractured almost at the same location at the hollow stem section where pressure balancing  port holes were drilled.  

# 3. Methodology and Results  

# 3.1 Visual Inspection  

A visual examination for the two as-received failed choke inserts was carried out with the  assistance of a stereo microscope. The principle dimensions were measured using digital callipers  and thread gauges. Fig. 1 shows a photograph of two as-received failed choke (stem/plug). The  lower one is the first choke and the upper one is the second choke. The plug had a length of 174  mm and a diameter of 101 mm, and the stem was 353 mm long with a diameter of 38 mm. The  fractures in both stems were similar, as shown in Fig. 2, exhibiting flat fracture features through  the majority of the stem cross-section with no necking or, reduction in area that indicates a  ductile overload fracture. The fracture location was close to a change in section and is situated at  a drilled hole (pressure balancing port) in the hollow section of the stem. Close examination of  the stems with the assistance of a low-powered stereo microscope revealed no further cracking of  the stems. Dye penetrant inspection (DPI), in accordance with the ASME V standard, carried out  on the stems and plugs revealed no crack like indications, as illustrated in Fig. 3.  

An area with features characteristic of fretting was detected in the coated area of the second  choke valve stem, 240 mm away from the fracture, as shown in Fig.4 (a). Fig. 4 (b) shows  corresponding fretting marks reported on the Gland (seal fixture), which was installed over the  second choke valve stem. Fretting features, shown in Fig. 4 (a), were studied at a higher  resolution Scanning Electron Microscope and the results illustrated in Fig. 5.  

# 3.2 Materials chemistry  

Chemical analysis of the two as-received failed choke materials was performed by Optical  Emission Spectroscopy (OES). Inductively coupled plasma atomic emission spectroscopy (ICP  OES) was also used to determine a deleterious alloying element ‘tin’ in the choke insert material  and the results together with API 6A718 composition limits [17] were shown in Table 1. All the  alloying elements for both stem samples were matching the API 6A718 alloy standard. Even the  tin (Sn) has been reported <0.005 wt.%, which indicates that the alloy was made from good  quality material.  

An EDX analysis was carried out on scrapings taken from the plug external surface to check the  composition of the overlay coating, and the results are shown in Fig. 6. The composition of  overlay weld was representative of “Stellite HS6” coating.  

The X-ray powder diffraction (XRPD) was used to characterise solid particles embedded in oily  deposits found in plug Sample (inset photograph in Fig. 7). The oily deposits were characterised  as oilfield-specific degraded crude. The pattern was recorded from 2-75° 2 Ɵ  using cobalt Kα  radiation for phase identification, as shown in Fig. 7. Phases identified within the sample were  halite, quartz and barite.  

# 3.3 Macro/microstructure  

The microstructures of the two as-received failed choke materials were investigated in transverse  and longitudinal directions using an optical microscope and Scanning Electron Microscope  (SEM) after standard methods of metallography. Prepared surfaces were examined using a  metallurgical optical microscope in the as-polished condition (up to 1-micron finish) and after  subsequent etching with Kalling’s No. 2 reagent (200 ml Methanol, 200 ml HCl, 10 g CuCl 2 ).  Grain size measurement was performed in accordance with ASTM E112 standard– comparison  method.  The micrographs shown in Fig. 8 indicated that the structure in the transverse and  longitudinal directions has almost annealed equiaxed twinned grains. The grain boundaries were  clean and free from any precipitates in all cases. Some carbides stringers in irregular and  discontinuous morphology were found in the grain interiors especially in the second choke.  

An EDX line scan and EDX mapping analyses were performed to detect preferential segregation  of the alloying elements ( e.g.  on grain boundaries) and to detect deleterious intermetallic phases.  

Fig. 9 shows the SEM micrograph of the investigated material with superimposed EDX analyses  along the dotted-pink line for the alloying elements. The concentrations detected for nickel,  chromium, iron, titanium and niobium are represented by blue, yellow, green, red and purple  lines, respectively. Also, Fig. 10(a) shows the SEM micrograph of Sample 1 with a  superimposed rectangle representing an area selected for the EDX analyses. Fig. 10(b) to (f)  show corresponding EDX maps for nickel, chromium, iron, niobium and titanium respectively.  No segregation was detected, and no deleterious underlying precipitates were revealed in the  EDX line scan or EDX maps.  

Fig. 11 shows optical micrographs used to establish grain size numbers for the as-received first  and second failed choke materials stems, respectively. For both samples, the average grain size at  the fracture (closer to the core) was larger (2–3 µm) compare to the grain size observed away  from the fracture (3–5 µm). The average grain size measured for both of the choke inserts were  large but acceptable to API 6A718.  

The overlay coatings were also examined, and the results were shown in Fig. 12. Micro cracking  was noted in the heat-affected zone (HAZ) in some areas adjacent to the overlay coating/weld.  

# 3.4 Fractography  

The fracture surfaces were cleaned of deposits, and/or corrosion products by immersion in an  ultrasonic bath, filled with dilute hydrochloric acid inhibited with hexamine. Detailed  fractography was carried out using a Scanning Electron Microscope. Fig. 13(a) shows a  schematic diagram photograph of the first failed choke fracture being prepared for the  microscopic examination and the fracture micrograph was illustrated in Fig. 13(b). There are few  secondary cracks visible, as pointed by arrows in the micrograph (Intra-granular cracking). To  achieve a better understanding of the fracture mechanism, a scanning electron microscope was  used to analyze the fracture surface, and the results were shown in Fig. 14. The fracture surfaces  reveal the striations formation. These striations were formed by the changing of loading cycles,  which generate micro-plastic stains, which is a prove of fatigue failure. The striations of the  second choke look courser than that of the first choke. This is maybe due to the higher cyclic  loadings. The SEM examination attempted to identify a specific feature at the crack initiation  location at the pressure balancing port nearest to the plug. A small, well-machined chamfer was  found and examined closely. No specific precipitates or other features were found at the  initiation site, indicating that the most likely cause of initiation would be high local stress. There  was evidence of local relaxation following fracture, indicating that some of the stress would have  been residual stress from the manufacturing route. Fig. 15 shows SEM micrograph of the stem  showing proposed fatigue crack initiation site (pointed by a red arrow) and machining  (chamfering) patterns in the background. Examination of the fracture path showed continuous  indications of high-cycle fatigue during propagation.  

# 3.5 Mechanical properties  

The tensile samples were prepared and tested in accordance with ASTM A370. Longitudinal test  specimens were prepared for each stem; three samples taken from the core and three from the  subsurface (just below the surface). The tensile samples had a 6.35 mm diameter parallel length  of 35 mm and the manual measured gauge length was 25 mm. The tests were performed using an  Instron 100 kN test machine ‘AM07’ with a bespoke digital controller which was calibrated in  accordance with BS EN 7500-1:2004 and ASTM E4:2014. The samples were tested between  self-aligning pull bars at ambient temperature, using dual averaging 12.5 mm gauge length  extensometers AE01 & AE02 calibrated in accordance with BS EN ISO 9513:2012 and ASTM  E83-10a. Charpy impact testing was carried out on 10 x 10 mm standard samples in accordance  with the ASTM E23 - 12c at a minimum design temperature of -46   C for the choke insert. For  each choke insert, three samples were taken from the stems for impacting in longitudinal to the  rolling direction, and three samples from the plugs for impacting in transverse to the rolling  direction. The tensile and impact testing results are presented in Table 2 and Table 3,  respectively. The impact test results are acceptable for Alloy 718, and this was supported by the  microstructure evaluation. The mechanical (tensile and impact) testing results also support the  evidence on the examinations of the fracture faces with no indication of brittle fracture. The  failure mode was predominantly fatigue failure throughout.  

Hardness testing was carried out in accordance with the Rockwell C method (ASTM E18-15)  with 150 kgf. Vickers method (ASTM E384 – 11E1) was also employed on the plugs closer to  the fracture faces and at the weld overlays, using a diamond pyramid indenter with a load of 98  N (10 kgf). In this study, Vickers hardness measurements are converted to HRC and reported for  comparison [18]. Rockwell C hardness was measured on the stem disks that were taken well  away from the fracture surface, as shown in Fig. 16. The hardness testing results are presented in  Table 4 for the two failed choke stems materials. Vickers pyramid hardness measurements were  taken on the plugs closer to the fracture faces and at the weld overlays. For the first choke, the  average hardness recorded on the stem was HRC 40, on the plug near the fracture face was HV  387 (\~HRC 40) and on the overlay coating was HV 322 (HRC 33). For the second choke, the  average hardness recorded on the stem was HRC 39, near the fracture face on the plug was HV  378 (\~HRC 39), and on the overlay coating was HV 381 (HRC 39).  

# 4. Discussions  

Alloy 718 is age precipitation-hardened nickel alloy. It was developed for severe applications [4]   due to its exceptional mechanical and corrosion properties in sour service environments and high  temperatures [4]. Regarding the investigated case, the chemical analysis, shown in Table 1,  demonstrated that the material complied with API 6A718 specification/standard. The niobium  content was below the optimum for delta phase formation (6 - 8%). The measured value was  slightly less than the minimum specified by API 6A718.  Very low levels of elements likely to  promote segregation/ precipitation/ liquation cracking. However, the metallographic examination  demonstrated that the heat treatment had been carried out correctly, and the microstructure was  matching the alloy optimum structure, except for some small aligned segregations. It is obvious  to record here that there was no evidence of acicular delta phase or laves phase in the areas  examined. For the mechanical properties, the tensile tests demonstrate uniform homogenous  properties in various locations, sub-surface and mid-thickness. The ultimate tensile strength  record more than 1200 MPa and the 0.5% proof stress was around 900 MPa. Although these  extreme high strength values, the elongation was more than 30%, which prove the high quality of  the materials, and it confirms with API 6A718 as presented in Table 2. The toughness of the  materials was in its acceptable range in all the impact tested samples (more than 97 Joules), as  demonstrated in Table 3. These Impact tests were performed at minimum design temperature (- 46   C) in both longitudinal and transverse orientations, with correspondingly high lateral  expansion values (Table 3). Also, the hardness records less than or equal to 40 HRC. This  hardness value complies with the specifications of API 6A718 standard. These results exclude  the sharing of material properties in the failure and exclude the possibilities of brittle fracture.  

Visual examination and fractography of both choke stems identified clear evidence of fatigue  failure. Fine curved crack arrest marks (beach marks) were present on the fracture faces  suggesting high-cycle low-stress unidirectional bending fatigue cracking.  It is well known that  fatigue occurs in 3 stages: initiation, propagation and catastrophic failure after crack reaches a  critical size. For both choke inserts failures studied, the initiation point was the outer edge of the  port nearest the plug at a point where stress concentration is believed to be greatest. Close  examination of the fracture faces by SEM showed initiation from the surface with no particular  material defects such as inclusions. The surface was machined to a high standard, and there was  a \~200 μm taper around the portends (Fig. 15). In the absence of a specific feature at the  initiation sites, it was anticipated that the initiation site was a location of particularly high local  tensile stress concentration. In this localised area, many factors share in increasing stress  concentration. Firstly, the change in the cross-section had led to the establishment of a high- stress concentration. Secondly, the cladding process and the subsequence machining operations  may also increase the stress concentration of the whole material (Cracks that observed in the  heat-affected zone under Stellite overlay as shown in Fig. 12 is clear evidence).  Lastly, the  critical factor was the existence of a drilled hole, which concentrates the stress in this area.  

The propagation of the fatigue cracks through the material was by a high cycle low amplitude  mechanism. Fatigue crack propagation is influenced by the loading history, the environment and  certain metallurgical features, principally grain size. The large grain size would favour low  propagation rate but would not halt the mechanism at loads below the fatigue limit if sufficient  driving force is present. The environment for the choke inserts is expected to be corrosive. The  combination of treated seawater and produced water would provide a combination of chlorides.  However, the data supplied from the operator show low but adequate levels of oxygen (up to 100  μg/l) and elevated temperature (up to 85   C).  Also, there was no evidence of any contribution  from corrosion or stress corrosion cracking on the fracture faces. Therefore, it is concluded that  significant cyclic loading was present. As the loading amplitude is high, the fatigue crack  propagation becomes faster. This can explain the lifetime of each choke. The first choke was in- service when the injected water flow rate was 5,600 – 7,360 m 3 /d for about 7 years, and the  failure occurred. When the second choke of the same design was replaced and used at this high  flow rate (7,360 m 3 /d) it failed after less than 12 months service. The higher cyclic loading  accelerates the fatigue crack propagation to a level to reach the critical size of catastrophic  failure in a shorter time. We don’t know the exact values of stresses applied. However, we  hypothesis that the location of the hole was initially on both side of the stem at the weakest  location. That’s causing it to fatigue. We believe the possible location where the stress would be  less being at the plug directly to equalise the hydrostatic pressure inside and above the plug. The  result worked properly, which vindicated the hypothesis (See schematic views in Fig. 17).  

It is understood that the choke designer, is considering modifying the design by relocating  balance ports to minimise local tensile stress concentration and thereby eliminate initiation. The  cladding of the stellite overlay must be re-evaluated to eliminate the cracking in the heat-affected  zone and to relieve the stresses.  

# 5. Conclusions  

The failure analysis of a two choke valve inserts from offshore field service in the Northern  North Sea was performed through the evaluations of the materials analysis, mechanical  properties, material microstructure, visual examination, and fractography to identify the root  cause of failure. The diagnose shows that fatigue was the primary failure mechanism. Chemical  analysis, mechanical testing (in longitudinal and transverse orientations) and metallographic  examination demonstrated that the failed stem material complied with API 6A718 and had been  heat-treated correctly. Visual examination and fractography of both choke stems identified clear  evidence of fatigue as the primary failure mechanism. Fatigue initiation was not attributable to a  specific feature. It was concluded that initiation occurred at a location on a port owing to  particularly high local tensile stress concentration. The main drive of fatigue crack propagation,  in this case, appears to be the high-cycle low-stress unidirectional bending fatigue cracking  

# 6. Recommendations  

A new stem design was recommended to drill the balance ports to minimise local tensile stress  concentration to reduce susceptibility to fatigue initiation. Details of modifications should be  reviewed for suitability of design through finite element analysis. The flow conditions through  this device are complex. An extensive trial programme would be required to define precise  interactions between flow rate, density and fluid chemistry and to identify actual operational  limits for fatigue crack propagation. Computational fluid dynamics simulation may provide an  initial assessment. Experience has shown a clear trend between flowrate and the incidence of  failure. In the absence of empirical or sufficient statistical data, flow conditions that gave no  failure over a prolonged period should be maintained.

# Highlights  

 Two choke valve inserts manufactured from 718 Ni superalloys  were fractured in  offshore field service.  The materials and mechanical properties were correct and complied with API 6A718.   The diagnose shows that fatigue was the primary failure mechanism.   Fatigue was initiated at a high local tensile stress concentration at pressure balancing  holes.  

 The main drive of fatigue crack propagation was the high-low-stress cycles.
