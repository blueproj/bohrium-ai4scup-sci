# Visible-Light-Induced Siloxycarbene Addition to N  N of A zod i carboxyl at es: Synthesis of Acyl Hydrazides from Acylsilanes  

Mohammad Saleem, Akash Ratwan, Pokhriyal Yamini, and Dongari Yadagiri  

Cite This:  Org. Lett.  2024, 26, 2039−2044  

![](images/4ee83048f25acddacfb09cb55fa42ed5999e10e2c03981cafa4d749a2bc75c22.jpg)  

ABSTRACT:  We report the synthesis of acyl hydrazides from acylsilanes in the presence of visible light without the aid of additives or transition metals. Acylsilanes underwent [1,2]-Brook rearrangement to generate the nucleophilic siloxycarbenes which on further addition to N  N of a zod i carboxyl at es produced the acyl hydrazides. Control experiments indicate that the reaction proceeds through the singlet carbene intermediate. Transformation of the acyl hydrazide functionality to other functional groups was demonstrated, including the synthesis of the drug candidate Moclobemide.  

C arbenes are versatile synthetic intermediates in organic chemistry for C − C, C − heteroatom bond formation, etc., and for the construction of different carbocyclic and heterocyclic compounds in a single step. − 3   Addition of carbenes including metallo carbenes to N  N bonds is a reaction that is scantily addressed in the literature. In 1977, Stang’s group reported that the vinyl carbenes derived from vinyl triflate add to azoarenes to yield 2-indazoles.   Shortly after, in 1984, Hegedus reported the metathesis reaction between azoarenes and Fischer carbenes such as the chromium carbene complex, which yields the imidates ( Scheme 1 a). Similarly, McElwee-White reported the reaction of tungsten carbene complex with azoarenes ( Scheme 1 a).   The diaziridine as the major product and the metathesis product as the minor product were observed with electron deficient azo com- pounds,   in the case of Pd-carbene formation of hydrazone was observed.   Recently, Fu rstner reported the metathesis of azoarene derivatives using a catalytic amount of half-sandwich Rh(III), Ir(III)-donor − acceptor electrophilic carbenes ( Scheme 1 b).   The reaction of azo compounds with carbenes either requires a stoichiometric amount of Fischer carbene complexes or a catalytic amount of highly expensive transition metal catalysts. The common drawback of these diazo compounds is that they are potentially explosive, hazardous, and need strong electron-withdrawing groups to stabilize the carbene. Acylsilanes are known to undergo 1,2-silyl migration to generate siloxycarbenes under thermal or photochemical conditions, known as Brook rearrangement.   Recent studies show that in situ generated siloxycarbenes would undergo X − H (X = B, C, N, O, and Si) insertion,   reactions with alkyl boronates, addition to unsaturated bonds such as alkenes, imines, and alkynes via inter- or intramolecular reactions,   and reactions with aldehydes, siloxycarbene addition to N    ketones, N yielding the acyl hydrazide has   and CO 2 .   However, not been documented.   Acyl hydrazide motifs are privileged compounds in various fields, including pharmaceuticals, biologically active molecules, and agrochemicals and are also valuable intermediates in synthetic chemistry.  

Prior syntheses of acyl hydrazides were accomplished from the alcohols,   aldehydes,   alkanes in the presence of carbon monoxide,   ketoacids,   carboxylic acids, and carboxamides. However, to the best of our knowledge, there is no report for the synthesis of acyl hydrazides from ketones or acylsilanes. Herein, we report the synthesis of acyl hydrazides under visible light from a zod i carboxyl at es and acylsilanes ( Scheme 1 c).  

The investigation commenced with the reaction of acylsilanes and a zod i carboxyl ate under visible light. Treatment of acylsilane  1a  (2.5 equiv) with a zod i carboxyl ate  2a  (1 equiv) under blue LED irradiation led to the formation of acyl hydrazides  3aa  in 91% isolated yield ( Table 1 , entry 1).

Reducing the amount of  1a  to 1.5 equiv under the standard conditions furnished  3aa  in reduced yield (68%) ( Table 1 , entry 2). Next, solvents like benzene, tri flu oro methyl benzene, acetonitrile, DCM, and THF were screened and all of them gave an inferior result compared to toluene  3aa  (entries 3 − 6). Next, the role of the light sources was examined with 390 and 427 nm, which gave diminished yield (entries 8 and 9). In 440 nm LEDs, a comparable yield was observed (entry 10). Furthermore, when a reaction was performed under conven- tional heating at 40 and 100  ° C (entry 11), formation of product was not observed; instead all of the starting materials were recovered. These observations suggest that blue LEDs are essential for the present transformation.  

With optimized conditions, we first explored the substitution of silicon in acylsilanes  1 . The substitution on the silicon atom played a significant role in the outcome of the acyl hydrazide product formation. For example, methyl substitution as on  1a , ethyl as on  1b , isopropyl as on  1c , and dimethyl phenyl as on 1d  afforded the product  3aa  in 91%, 82%, 52%, and 51% yield, respectively ( Scheme 2 ). In the case of bulky TBDMS derived acylsilane  1e  gave 59% of  3aa  along with a mixture of  N -silyl substituted and  O -silyl substituted acyl hydrazides  4  and  5  with the combined yield of 35% ( Scheme 2 ). On the other hand, treatment of the above reaction mixture with 6 M HCl in EtOH gave  3aa  in 72% yield ( Scheme 2 ). Interestingly, this reaction can be easily scaled up to 1 mmol scale without compromising the yield.  

Having studied the different substitutions on silicon, we next turned our attention to the scope of the a zod i carboxyl at es  2 . iso- Propyl-containing a zod i carboxyl at es reacted very well and gave the acyl hydrazide  3ab  with 86% yield ( Scheme 2 ). The structure of  3ab  was unambiguously confirmed by X-ray crystallography.   We also tested the  tert -butyl, benzyloxy, and p -chlorobenzyloxy substituted a zod i carboxyl at es. All of them reacted well with acylsilane  1a  and gave the corresponding acyl hydrazides  3ac − 3ae  in good yields ( Scheme 2 ). Next, the influence of substituents on the aryl moiety of acylsilane was examined. In general, the reaction worked well with electron- rich acylsilanes such as methoxy substituted derivative and produced the acyl hydrazide  3ba  in 87% yield. However, alkyl substituted acyl hydrazides  3ca  and  3da  were isolated in slightly diminished yield.  p -Halogen-containing, such as fluoro ( 3ea ), chloro ( 3fa ,  3fb ), as well as phenyl substituted ( 3ga ), acyl hydrazides are easily accessible from the corresponding acylsilanes  1  ( Scheme 2 ). Various ester-containing acylsilanes reacted smoothly under the optimized conditions to provide the desired products  3ha − 3ma  in 51 − 70% yield, which possess synthetically useful functional groups for further synthetic manipulation. Not only  p -substituted acylsilanes but m -substituted (e.g.,  m -Me,  m -CF 3 ,  m -Cl,  m -F) and sterically hindered  o -substituted acylsillanes (e.g.,  o -OMe,  o -Me) also underwent the reaction to give the corresponding acyl hydrazides ( 3sa ,  3ta ) in good yields. Interestingly, 2-naphthyl acylsilanes gave a moderate yield of  3ua . Notably, 3-thiophene derived acylsilane gave the desired product  3va  in good yield. Importantly, this concept also applies to the late-stage function aliz ation of biologically active molecules such as Ibuprofen ( 3wa ), Naproxen ( 3xa ), and Indometacin ( 3ya ) with good yield. Under our optimized conditions, azoarenes and aliphatic acylsilanes failed to produce the corresponding acyl hydrazides (see the  Supporting Information (SI)) .

As mentioned in the optimization, in the presence of blue light 95% of acyl hydrazide  3aa  was observed, but no product was detected without light (see the  SI  for more details). Next, to check the involvement of carbene in its singlet or triplet carbene, the reaction was performed in the presence of a radical quencher such as TEMPO. Based on the mechanistic insights and literature reports,   we proposed the following mechanism. Two pathways could explain the formation of acyl hydrazide  3  from acylsilane  1  and a zod i carboxyl at es  2 . Excited acylsilane  A  would undergo 1,2- Brook rearrangement to form the siloxycarbene  B  ( Scheme 3 c), which can be in equilibrium with nucleophilic carbanion C . The carbanion  C  would attack the electrophilic a zod i carboxyl at es  2  in path a to form intermediate  D . Transfer of the silyl group from O to N would give unstable  N -silyl acyl hydrazide  E , which then loses the silyl group to form acyl hydrazide  3 . Formation of intermediate  E  ( Scheme 2 , compound  4 ) was observed in the reaction between  tert - butyldimethyl derived acylsilane ( 1e ) and  2a . Alternatively, the formation of acyl hydrazide  3  could be rationalized through the [2 + 1]-cycloaddition of in situ generated siloxycarbene  B  with a zod i carboxyl at es  2  to form an unstable 3-membered compound, diazridine  F  (path b). Concomitant ring-opening of  F  followed by silyl group migration from O to N and, finally, loss of the silyl group delivers the acyl hydrazide  3 .

The synthesis of heterocyclic compounds such as oxadiazole  15  and 16  was also achieved in good yield. Furthermore, we have demonstrated the synthesis of the Moclobemide drug in two steps, which is used as an antidepressant for social anxiety. Thus, amide  17  in 54% yield was synthesized from acyl hydrazide  3fb , and substitution with morpholine afforded Moclobemide  18  in 40% yield.  

In conclusion, we have successfully accomplished the visible- light-induced synthesis of acyl hydrazides from the acylsilanes and a zod i carboxyl at es. Using this methodology, a library of acyl hydrazides was synthesized in high yield from electroni- cally and sterically diverse substituted acylsilanes, including pharmaceutical active ones. The developed reaction involves the addition of nucleophilic siloxycarbene to N  N of a zod i carboxyl at es harvesting the visible light under transition- metal-free conditions. The proposed mechanism is well- supported by the control experiments. The application of this methodology was shown through the synthesis of ketones, esters, amides, and oxadiazoles from the synthesized acyl hydrazides. Furthermore, synthesis of biologically active Moclobemide was also achieved in two steps.
