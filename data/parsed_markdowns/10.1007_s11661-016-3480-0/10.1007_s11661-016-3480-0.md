#   Predicting Coherency Loss of  c 00 Precipitates in IN718 Superalloy  

YANZHOU JI, YUCUN LOU, MENG QU, JOHN DAVID ROWATT, FAN ZHANG, TIMOTHY W. SIMPSON, and LONG-QING CHEN  

Precipitate hardening through coherent particles is an important strengthening mechanism in Ni-based IN718 superalloy. The change of coherency state,  e.g. , loss of coherency, can aﬀect the precipitation kinetics and the eﬃcacy of precipitate hardening. In this work, we predict the critical size of  c 00 -Ni 3 Nb precipitates in IN718 to trigger coherency loss using the phase-ﬁeld approach with available materials parameters from ﬁrst-principles calculations and from the literature. The predicted critical size lies within the range of experimentally reported values. The eﬀect of coherency loss on precipitate morphology and kinetics, as well as the aging time to reach this critical size during heat treatment, is also predicted and discussed. These predictions   provide guidance in controlling the size of  c 00 precipitates and optimizing the strength of IN718 alloys.

# I. INTRODUCTION  

P RECIPITATE  hardening is one of the key strength- ening mechanisms in various alloys. Through precipitate reactions, the precipitates, either shearable or shear-re- sistant, can act as obstacles of dislocation motion in the alloy matrix, enhancing the strength of alloys. The eﬃcacy of precipitate hardening is largely determined by the coherency state of the precipitates within the 1 ]   matrix. When the precipitate is coherent with the matrix phase,  i.e. , when there are well-deﬁned lattice correspondence (LC) and orientation relationship (OR) between the crystal structures of the precipitate and the matrix, and the misﬁt strains between the two phases are within the elastic range, then precipitate hardening is the most eﬀective. In contrast, when the precipitates are semi-coherent or incoherent with the matrix phase,  i.e. , when crystal structures of the precipitate and the matrix partially ﬁt or do not ﬁt at all, line defects (such as dislocations and structure ledges) can develop at the precipitate/matrix interface, and the eﬀect of precipitate hardening is impaired.  

Initially coherent precipitates can lose coherency during continued growth when the elastic strain energy becomes dominant. The elastic energy density is higher for coherent phase than the incoherent one,  i.e. , the elastic energy function is a penalty term for the precipitation process and it scales with the volume of the precipitates. When the precipitate reaches a critical size, the coherent phase becomes energetically unfavor- able. Consequently, line defects on the precipitate/ma- trix interfaces are developed to relax the stresses arising from misﬁt, which lead to coherency loss. This type of coherency loss has been observed in many diﬀerent types of alloys, including  h 0 precipitates in Al-Cu-based alloys, 2 ]   c 0   and  c 00   precipitates in Ni-based alloys, 2 , ] ¢ 2 ] and  b  precipitates in Al-Mg-Si alloys.  

The theoretical analysis of the mechanisms of coher- ency loss of a growing particle has been established by 2 ]   Weatherly  et al. Three mechanisms have been pro- posed: (i) the punching of dislocation loops near the precipitate/matrix interface due to coherency stresses in the matrix, (ii) the climb of dislocations from the matrix, and (iii) the nucleation of dislocation loops inside a precipitate. For each of these mechanisms, there is a correlation between the dimensions of the precipitate and the amount of dislocations at the interface, indicating a critical size of the precipitate where there is no nucleation barrier for creating dislocations at the interface.  

To determine this critical precipitate size, it is essential to evaluate the energetic contributions to the precipi- tate-matrix system before and after coherency loss. Currently, many computational methods have been 1 , – 7 ] attempted to predict this critical size quantitatively. 1 ] For example, using the phase-ﬁeld approach, Shi  et al. simulated the morphology evolution of  a  precipitates in Ti-alloys and predicted the critical size of  a  precipitates for coherency loss using an energy criterion,  i.e. , precipitate can transfer from coherent phase into semi-coherent phase if the total free energy of the former one is larger than the latter one. This energy criterion is of a thermodynamic nature, indicating that the coherency loss can happen spontaneously. However, kinetically, the onset of the transformation ( e.g. , the coherency loss process) can happen as long as the energy barrier for the kinetic process is overcome, which is referred to as the ‘‘stability criterion.’’ One notable ex- ample is the nucleation of new phases during solid-state 8 ]   transformations. Once the embryo of a new phase exceeds some critical size, the energy barrier of its growth no longer exists, and the embryo becomes a nucleus and can continuously grow. Relative to the energy criterion, the stability criterion can generally better indicate the nature of the transformation in terms of stability and kinetics.  

In this work, we are interested in the coherency loss for a particular type of Inconel 718 (IN718). This alloy has been widely used for manufacturing tools for oil ﬁeld operation due to its high strength, high corrosion resistance to downhole gases such as CO 2  and H 2 S, and good weldability. Depending on the particular applica- tions, many diﬀerent heat treatment approaches have been applied,  e.g. , to optimize its toughness, fatigue resistance, or corrosion resistance. Therefore, it is of practical importance to quantitatively predict the time for the coherency loss for Inconel 718 as a function of temperature.  

The IN718 with major alloying elements of Fe, Cr, Nb, Al, Ti, and Mo, is a traditional Ni-based superalloy strengthened by precipitates. Among the observed   precipitate phases,  c 00 is considered as the main strength- ening phase in IN718, since the morphology of  c 00 precipitate is plate- or disk-like shape, which can eﬀectively block the dislocation motion in  c -matrix with 9 ] an fcc structure. As proposed by Sundararaman  et al. ,   the coherency loss of  c 00 precipitates is mainly due to the formation of stacking faults within the precipitate, which can be classiﬁed into the mechanism (iii) men- 2 ] tioned previously (Weatherly  et al. ).  

Critical diameters for the coherency loss of  c 00 precipitates have been measured experimentally. For 10 ]   example, Slama  et al. reported the coherency loss of   c 00 when the diameter of a precipitate reached 100 nm. [ 11 ]   Similar results were reported by Cozar and Pineau for 3 ]   a Fe-Ni-Ta alloy. Notably, Devaux  et al. summarized various measurements for the thickness/diameter ( e / L )   ratio evolution of  c 00 in IN718, having noticed a change of the tangent of the  e / L – L  curve when the diameter of   c 00 reached 50 nm, which was attributed to the onset of coherency loss of  c 00 . The coherency loss can be not only detrimental to the alloy strength, as discussed previ- ously, but also considered as aiding the nucleation of the 9 ]   incoherent  d  phase, which can further decrease the strength of the alloy.  

In order to predict the coherency loss of  c 00 in IN718, [ 12 – 16 ]   we develop a phase-ﬁeld model to simulate the   morphology evolution of  c 00 precipitates in IN718. We combine the stability criterion and the energy criterion   to predict the critical size of  c 00 for coherency loss. The governing factors for this critical size, as well as the aging time to reach this critical size during heat treatment, are discussed. In addition, the change of precipitate kinetics before and after the coherency loss   of  c 00 precipitates is predicted.

[ 12 ]   The phase-ﬁeld approach has been widely applied in simulating precipitate morphologies and kinetics in   various alloy systems. Notable examples include  c 0 and c 00   precipitates in Ni-alloys, 14 , , ]   b 0   and  b 1  precipitates in Mg-rare earth alloys, 18 , ]   h 0   precipitates in Al-Cu- 20 , ]   1 ]   based alloys, and  a  precipitates in Ti-alloys. The fundamental components in a phase-ﬁeld model include: (i) a set of continuous order parameters to describe microstructures, (ii) free energies of the system as a function of these order parameters, and (iii) kinetic equations for the evolution of the order parameters.

Although at a size between  d c  and  d e , the semi-coherent state is still energetically less favorable than the coherent state, but the dislocations, once developed, tend to persist. This is because at the critical diameter  d c , once the coherency loss happens, the further growth of semi-coherent precipitates would lead to a decrease of energy, while the recovery of the coherency loss would lead to an increase of the energy. This approach is analogous to the determination of the critical nucleus 8 ]   size during solid-state transformations. The diﬀerence between  d c  and  d e  is the probability of the coherency loss: at  d  =  d e , the coherency loss has higher probability to take place since the energy barrier is fully overcome; at  d  =  d c , the highest energy barrier for coherency loss is overcome and the coherency loss already has certain probability to take place. # D.  Discussions of Coherency Loss Mechanisms  

The critical diameters,  d c  and  d e  predicted according to the stability criterion and energy criterion, may correspond to diﬀerent formation mechanisms of the interfacial dislocations that lead to the coherency loss. For precipitates larger than  d e , since the semi-coherent precipitate is energetically more favorable, the coherent c 00   ﬁ semi-coherent  c 00   transition can happen sponta- neously: the dislocations can spontaneously develop on the  c / c ¢¢  interface. On the other hand, when the precipitates are larger than  d c  but smaller than  d e , the probability of coherency loss depends on how the interface dislocations form, for example, the punching of dislocation loops, the climb of dislocations from the matrix, and the nucleation of dislocation loops inside precipitates. Therefore, we speciﬁcally discuss the pos- sibility of these three mechanisms for coherency loss.

# IV. SUMMARY  

We develop a phase-ﬁeld model to simulate the   morphology evolution of  c 00 precipitates and combine the ‘‘stability criterion’’ and the ‘‘energy criterion’’ to   predict the critical diameter of  c 00 for coherency loss in the IN718 superalloy. The predicted critical diameter agrees well with existing experimental observations. Furthermore, the eﬀect of coherency loss on the morphology and kinetics of the precipitates is discussed. To provide better guidance for controlling the heat   treatment time to avoid coherency loss of the  c 00 phase, we estimate the heat treatment time when the diameter   of  c 00 precipitates exceeds the critical values for coher- ency loss at various temperatures. The work is expected to provide practical guidance to optimize the strength of the IN718 superalloy, and the proposed model can be extended to other material systems.
