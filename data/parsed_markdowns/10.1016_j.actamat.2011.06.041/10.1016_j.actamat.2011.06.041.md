# Abstract  

A series of Al x Co 1.5 CrFeNi 1.5 Ti y  high-entropy alloys with diﬀerent Al and Ti contents were designed, and their phase and microstruc- ture were investigated. The adhesive wear behavior and mechanism were also studied and compared with the conventional wear-resistant steels SUJ2 and SKH51. The amounts of Al and Ti strongly aﬀect the phase and microstructure, particularly the amount and morphol- ogy of the hard  g -(Ni, Co) 3 Ti phase. It was found that the wear resistance of the Co 1.5 CrFeNi 1.5 Ti and Al 0.2 Co 1.5 CrFeNi 1.5 Ti alloys is at least two times better than that of conventional wear-resistant steels with similar hardness. The excellent anti-oxidation property and resistance to thermal softening in these high-entropy alloys are proposed to be the main reasons for the outstanding wear resistance.   2011 Acta Materialia Inc. # 1. Introduction  

Most metallic materials, such as steel and aluminum alloys, are based on one or two major elements. Various alloying elements are added to the materials to improve their properties. Diﬀering from conventional alloy design, Yeh et al.  [1,2]  proposed a new alloy design concept in 1995 in which more than ﬁve major elements, each having an atomic percentage (at.%) in the range 5–35  [2–4] , are combined. This new approach to alloy design with multiple principal elements leads to the formation of  “ high-entropy alloys ”  (HEAs), in which certain eﬀects not occurring in conventional alloys are observed; these include high- entropy, sluggish-diﬀusion, lattice-distortion and cocktail eﬀects  [5] . Among these core eﬀects, the high-entropy eﬀect is the most important (hence the name). The high-entropy eﬀect originates from the signiﬁcantly higher conﬁgura- tional entropy in HEAs compared to that in conventional alloys. Such high mixing entropy can favor the formation of disordered solution states and suppress the formation of intermetallic compounds, which is of paramount impor- tance in the application of these alloys  [2,6] .  

Previous studies  [2,3,5–18]  have shown that HEAs have many attractive properties, such as high hardness, good oxidation resistance, high elevated-temperature strength and good age-softening resistance. However, the cost of HEAs may be higher than that of conventional alloys (such as steel and Al alloys) because of the use of multiple ele- ments; though it is still cheaper than most superalloys and Ti alloys. Thus, HEAs show great potential for use in tools, molds and structural components.  

Previous research has indicated that Al x CoCrCuFeNi

 [9,12,19] , Al 0.5 CoCrCuFeNiTi x  [11]  and AlCoCrFeNiTi x

 [13]  HEAs have a variety of microstructures and mechani- cal properties. These alloys have body-centered cubic (bcc) or face-centered cubic (fcc) crystal structures, or the mix- ture of both. In general, the bcc-based alloys have higher strength but lower workability than the fcc-based alloys and vice versa  [6,9] . Based on these facts, a Co 1.5 CrFe- Ni 1.5 Ti 0.5 HEA having an fcc crystal structure was designed. In this alloy, the molar ratios of strong fcc form- ers, Co and Ni, are increased to 1.5 to ensure that the fcc structure is retained as the primary phase  [20] . This renders the alloy workable by plastic deformation, while remaining quite strong and tough. In addition, the strength can be enhanced by precipitation hardening like in Ni-based superalloys. In this study, four Al x Co 1.5 CrFeNi 1.5 Ti y alloys, with diﬀerent Al and Ti contents, based on the Co 1.5 CrFeNi 1.5 Ti 0.5 , were designed, and the eﬀects of Al and Ti concentrations on the phase, microstructure, precip- itate and mechanical properties were investigated.  

Wear is an important phenomenon in many applica- tions. However, information on the wear behavior of HEAs is very limited, and most reported studies have been conducted under abrasive conditions  [7,10–12,21] . In this study, we performed adhesive wear tests on Al x Co 1.5 CrFe- Ni 1.5 Ti y  alloys to study the wear mechanism and compare the results with the conventional wear-resistant steels SUJ2 and SKH51.  

# 2. Experimental procedure  

Four alloys with diﬀerent Al and Ti contents were studied: Co 1.5 CrFeNi 1.5 Ti 0.5  (Co 27.3 Cr 18.2 Fe 18.2 Ni 27.3 Ti 9.1 ), Al 0.2 Co 1.5 CrFeNi 1.5 Ti 0.5 (Al 3.5 Co 26.3 Cr 17.5 Fe 17.5 Ni 26.3 - Ti 8.8 ), Co 1.5 CrFeNi 1.5 Ti (Co 25 Cr 16.7 Fe 16.7 Ni 25 Ti 16.7 ) and Al 0.2 Co 1.5 CrFeNi 1.5 Ti (Al 3.2 Co 24.2 Cr 16.1 Fe 16.1 Ni 24.2 Ti 16.1 ); these alloys are henceforth referred to as Al00Ti05, Al02Ti05, Al00Ti10 and Al02Ti10, respectively. All alloy ingots were prepared by arc-melting the mixtures of the constituent elements with purities higher than 99.5 wt.% in an argon atmosphere, and the melting procedure was repeated several times to improve the homogeneity of the ingots. As-cast alloys were homogenized at 1100   C for 4 h and air cooled. The alloys were then aged at 800   C for 10 h followed by air cooling. Both homogenization and aging treatments were conducted in air.  

For microstructure investigations, the specimens were ground and polished using standard metallographic tech- niques, and to reveal grain boundaries and ﬁne precipi- tates, some specimens were etched with dilute aqua regia. The wear behavior of the alloys was investigated by pin- on-disk sliding tests based on the ASTM G99 standard under dry sliding conditions. SUJ2 (AISI 52100) bearing steel and SKH51 (AISI M2) high-speed steel were selected as reference materials in the wear test because they have good mechanical properties and are proven anti-wear materials. The reference materials and HEAs were tested under identical conditions. The wear test specimens were cylindrical in shape, and each had a diameter of 8 mm and height of 3 mm. Each specimen was ﬁxed in a holder to wear against a 75 mm diameter SKH51 (AISI M2) steel disk, which had a hardness value of HV 890. The distance between the specimen and the center of the disk was 20 mm. The sliding speed was 0.5 m s  1 , with a normal load of 29.4 N. To obtain suﬃcient wear loss, the required sliding distance for harder alloys was greater: the sliding distance for the softer alloys (Al00Ti05 and Al02Ti05) was 5400 m, while that for the harder alloys (Al00Ti10, Al02Ti10, SUJ2 and SKH51) was 43,200 m. The anti-wear property is measured by wear resistance, which is deﬁned as  W r  =  L / D V , where  W r  is the wear resistance and  D V  is the volumetric loss of the specimen after it has slid through a distance  L , and is obtained by dividing the weight loss by the sample density. Crystal structures were identiﬁed using an X-ray diﬀractometer (Rigaku ME510-FM2) under radi- ation conditions of 30 kV, 20 mA, a Cu target and a scan- ning speed of 2   min  1 . The microstructure and chemical composition of the alloys, the wear surface and wear debris were analyzed using a scanning electron microscope (SEM; JEOL JSM-5410) with an attached X-ray energy dispersive spectrometer(EDS).Room-temperaturemacroandmicrohardness measurements were performed using Mitu- toyo HV-115 and HM-115 hardness testing machines with loads of 5 and 25 g, respectively. Hot hardness measure- ments were obtained using a high-temperature hardness tester (Akashi AVK-CO) equipped with a heating and vac- uum part (Akashi AVK-HF), using a load of 5 kg.

# 3.2. Wear test results  

Fig. 6  compares the hardness and wear resistance of the six alloys tested in this research. HEAs with higher hard- ness (Al00Ti10 and Al02Ti10) have better wear resistance than the softer alloys (Al00Ti05 and Al02Ti05). Al02Ti10 has the highest wear resistance among the four HEAs, which is about twice that of Al00Ti10. It is worthwhile to note that the hardness of Al02Ti10 is close to that of SUJ2 and apparently lower than SKH51, but the wear resistance of Al02Ti10 is signiﬁcantly higher than these two reference conventional alloys (3.6 and 2 times higher than those of SUJ2 and SKH51, respectively). This indi- cates that Al02Ti10 has outstanding anti-wear properties.

# 3.3. Oxidation weight gain test and hot hardness measurement  

As previously mentioned, some signiﬁcant oxidational features are observed on the sample surfaces after the wear test. This means that during the dry sliding wear process the temperature of the wear surface increases substantially due to frictional heating. Indeed, our estimation of the sur- face ﬂash temperature according to Ashby et al.’s research [36]  suggests that during our sliding wear test conditions the ﬂash temperature of the local contact areas on Al00Ti10 and Al02Ti10 may be higher than 800   C. For SUJ2, which has a higher thermal conductivity, the ﬂash temperature can also be higher than 600   C. At these tem- peratures, both oxidation and softening become signiﬁcant enough to inﬂuence the wear properties of the alloys. To understand the eﬀects of high-temperature oxidation and softening, oxidation weight gain tests and hot hardness measurements were carried out.  

Fig. 10  shows the oxidation weight gain of SUJ2, SKH51 and HEAs after being heat-treated at 600 and 800   C for 24 h in air. The weight gains of the HEAs are much smaller than those of SUJ2 and SKH51 at both 600 and 800   C. The hot hardness measurements of SUJ2, SKH51, Al00Ti10 and Al02Ti10 as a function of temperature are shown in  Fig. 11 . The hardness of Al00Ti10 and Al02Ti10 alloys is higher than SKH51 at 700   C and above. Both the oxidation weight gain test and the hot hardness measurement results indicate that HEAs have better thermal stability than SUJ2 and SKH51 steels. Further discussions on the oxidation resis- tance and hot hardness and their eﬀects on the wear behav- ior are included in Section 4.2.

The wear mechanism of the Al00Ti05 and Al02Ti05 alloys is delamination wear (i.e. severe plastic deformation)  [37–40] . This can be conﬁrmed by the obvi- ous scratches on the wear surface and the plate-like wear debris whose composition is similar to the alloys. The dom- inance of delamination wear is due to the signiﬁcantly higher hardness of the wear plate (SKH51) relative to Ti05 alloys, which are composed mainly of a soft fcc phase with a hardness of   HV 500. When slid against the SKH51 disk (whose hardness is HV 890), the Ti05 alloys are plas- tically deformed and scratched. Further, when the degree of plastic deformation exceeds a certain threshold, the alloys begin to form cracks and split from the surface, forming the metallic plate-like wear debris.  

In contrast, the wear mechanism of the Al00Ti10 and Al02Ti10 alloys is mild oxidational wear  [40–42] . This can be judged from two reasons. The ﬁrst is the shallow wear surface scratches, the signiﬁcant oxidation feature on the wear surface, and the oxide-based wear debris.  

The second is the apparently higher Fe content in the deb- ris relative to that of other elements, which indicates that the SKH51 disk is seriously worn. Although the overall hardness of Al00Ti10 and Al02Ti10 is lower than the SKH51 disk, the ID microhardness is over HV 900, which is higher than the SKH51 disk. Therefore, the Ti10 alloys are strong enough to resist the wear against the disk, and the alloy surface will not be worn oﬀquickly during the wear test. In fact, they are able to withstand being worn for a long time and are therefore continuously heated due to friction. Such frictional heating raises the temperature of the local contact areas on the surface, leading to surface oxidation. Indeed, as mentioned previously, oxidized layers were observed on worn ID regions, and their compositions were similar to that of the alloy except for the presence of oxygen. This oxidized layer protects the specimen from direct metal–metal contact and reduces the degree of adhe- sive wear  [41–43] ; thus, the wear rate of the specimen is fur- ther decreased.  

# 4.2. Mechanism of the outstanding wear resistance in Al00Ti10 and Al02Ti10  

Compared with the conventional wear-resistant steels SKH51 and SUJ2, Al00Ti10 and Al02Ti10 exhibit even higher resistance to adhesive wear. The wear resistance of SKH51 and SUJ2 originates from their hard carbide phases  [44–46] . Similarly, the hard  g  phase in Ti10 alloys should also be an important factor in their anti-wear prop- erties. However, the carbides in SKH51 and SUJ2 are harder than the  g  phases in Ti10 alloys  [45] . Therefore, some other factors must also play important roles in the wear of Ti10 alloys. The higher wear resistances of Al00Ti10 and Al02Ti10 are attributed to their better oxida- tion resistances and superior hot hardness values.  

Oxidation resistance plays a critical role in the mild oxi- dational wear mechanism. In this mechanism, the surface oxide layer prevents direct metal–metal contact and adhe- sive wear. Thus, the volume loss comes mainly from the wear of the oxide layer  [47,48] . It has been shown that delamination of the oxide layer takes place due to internal stress when it reaches a critical thickness  [42] . Therefore, in mild oxidational wear, the better the oxidation resistance of the alloy (i.e. forming thin, dense and well-attached oxide layers), the less the wear loss.  

As seen from the weight gains at 600   C ( Fig. 10 ), the HEAs are almost not oxidized, but SKH51 and SUJ2 both show apparent weight gain. At 800   C, unambiguous oxi- dation signals are observed in HEAs. However, the weight gains of SKH51 and SUJ2 at 800   C are 26 and 39 times higher, respectively, than those of the HEAs. This is espe- cially so for SUJ2, whose oxide layer peels oﬀfrom the specimen surface after heating for 24 h at 600 and 800   C. These results indicate that the surfaces of the steels may oxidize rapidly under the wear test condition of the present research. However, the HEAs have very good oxi- dation resistances at elevated temperature, and the dense and well-adhering oxide layers formed during the wear test can protect the underlying materials from wear loss.  

Another reason for the superior wear resistance of Al00Ti10 and Al02Ti10 is their high hot hardness values. Under the oxidational wear mechanism, the wear mainly occurs at the real contact areas on the surfaces. As previ- ously mentioned, the temperature of these local areas (i.e. ﬂash temperature) could be as high as 800   C because of frictional heating, and the temperature of adjacent areas can also increase to 500–600   C. Such high temperatures can soften the alloys considerably, resulting in a decrease in wear resistance. The hot hardness values of SUJ2, SKH51, Al00Ti10 and Al02Ti10 as a function of tempera- ture are shown in  Fig. 11 . It can be seen that although SUJ2 and SKH51 have very high hardness values at room temperature, their hardness decreases rapidly as the tem- perature increases. For example, the hardness of SUJ2 decreases from HV 723 to less than HV 250 at 450   C. SKH51 has a better resistance to thermal softening, but its hardness still decreases rapidly above 600   C. In con- trast, the softening rate of Al00Ti10 and Al02Ti10 is appar- ently slower. In fact, both Al00Ti10 and Al02Ti10 have hardness values higher than HV 500 even at 800   C. This implies that at 800   C HEAs are at least 2.5 times harder than SKH51! Such good resistance to softening in HEAs has also been reported in another HEA system  [16] , and originates from the enhanced solution hardening eﬀect in multi-principle-element solutions. The wear surfaces of Al00Ti10 and Al02Ti10 will be apparently stronger than SUJ2 and SKH51 during the wear test, and their better wear resistance is thus reasonable.  

In this work, the Al02Ti10 alloy, which has high hard- ness, superior oxidation resistance and remarkable hot hardness, shows the highest resistance to adhesive wear among all the tested materials. Under mild oxidational wear mechanism, the well-adhering surface oxide layer on Al02Ti10 protects the underlying material. The better resis- tance to thermal softening implies that the local contact areas will have a higher hardness against adhesive wear. The superior wear resistance of this alloy thus has potential application in wear parts, especially at elevated temperatures.  

# 5. Conclusions  

1. Four HEAs designed in this study are categorized into two groups based on their Ti content. The main phase in Ti05 alloys (Al00Ti05 and Al02Ti05) is a gamma phase with an fcc structure. A small amount of plate-like g  phase precipitate is observed at the grain boundaries of Al00Ti05.  g  precipitation is suppressed in Al02Ti05 due to the addition of Al. The hardness values of Al00Ti05 and Al02Ti05 are HV 509 and HV 487, respectively.

 2. Increasing the molar ratio of Ti from 0.5 to 1.0 induces a dendritic structure in the Ti10 alloys (Al00Ti10 and Al02Ti10) and signiﬁcantly increases the volume frac- tion of  g  precipitates. A large amount of coarse  g  is observed in the ID region of Al00Ti10. The addition of Al in Al02Ti10 decreases the amount of the coarse g  phase in the ID regions and triggers the formation of the needle-like  g  phase with Widmansta ¨tten struc- tures in the Al-rich regions of the ID. This occurs because of the diﬃculty in eﬀectively relocating Al. The hardness values of Al00Ti10 and Al02Ti10 become HV 654 and HV 717, respectively, because of the strengthening eﬀect from the hard  g  phase.  

3. Due to the lower hardness of Al00Ti05 and Al02Ti05 compared to the wear disk, they undergo severe delam- ination wear. Al00Ti10 and Al02Ti10 alloys have a higher hardness and are much more resistant to sliding wear. The shallow scratches and oxidation features on the wear surface and the Fe-rich wear debris of Al00Ti10 and Al02Ti10 show that their wear behavior belongs to mild oxidational wear.  

4. The wear resistance of Al00Ti10 and Al02Ti10 are markedly higher than that of the two reference conven- tional steels. The hardness of Al02Ti10 is similar to that of SUJ2, but the wear resistance of the former is 3.6 times that of the latter. The wear resistance of Al02Ti10 is twice that of SKH51, but the hardness of the latter is a lot higher. The superior wear resistances of Al00Ti10 and Al02Ti10 relative to that of conventional steels orig- inate from their higher oxidation resistance and lower high-temperature softening rates.
