# Abstract  

The high-temperature strength and deformation behavior of  c / c 0   two-phase Co–Al–W-base alloys have been studied with polycrys- talline and single-crystal materials. The ternary, quaternary and higher-order alloys containing Ta, Cr and/or Re exhibit ﬂow stress anomalies above 873 K due to slip of pairs of 1/2 h 110 i  superpartial dislocations on {001} planes, in addition to {111} planes, in the  c 0   precipitates. Compression tests on the single-crystal specimens reveal a true anomalous peak temperature of 1073 K for both ternary and Ta-containing quaternary alloys. Above the peak, the ternary alloy exhibits a rapid decrease in strength with temperature, as 1/2 h 110 i  dislocations bypass the  c 0   precipitates without signiﬁcant shearing. Conversely, the Ta-containing quaternary alloy sustains strength to higher temperatures due to the activation of 1/3 h 112 i  partial dislocation slip that introduces a high density of stacking faults in the  c 0   precipitates.

# 1. Introduction  

The development of superalloys has been driven by the demand to increase the operating temperatures of gas tur- bines in power plants and aircraft engines. Among various classes of superalloys, including Fe-base, Co-base and Ni- base superalloys, Ni-base superalloys strengthened with the L1 2  compound ( c 0   phase) possess the highest heat resis- tance. For the highest-temperature applications, they are solidiﬁed into a single-crystal form, and alloyed to achieve a high volume fraction of the  c 0   precipitates, good solid- solution strengthening of the  c  and  c 0   phases along with environmental resistance. The superior strength of  c / c 0 Ni-base superalloys at elevated temperature can be attrib- uted to a high volume fraction of regularly aligned coher- ent  c 0   precipitates with a cuboidal shape that inhibit the dislocation glide in the  c  channels  [1,2] . In the most advanced Ni-base single-crystal superalloys, the volume fraction of the  c 0   phase reaches approximately 70%.  

Recently, a stable ternary Co 3 (Al,W) phase with L1 2 structure was discovered by Sato et al.  [3] . The  c 0 -Co 3

 (Al,W) phase thermodynamic ally equilibrates with  c -Co

 (fcc) solid-solution phase in the Co–Al–W ternary alloy system at 1173 K. In addition, the small misﬁt between the  c  and  c 0   phases permits the formation of a coherent two-phase microstructure where submicron cuboidal  c 0 precipitates align regularly in a continuous matrix phase. This is morphologically identical to the microstructure of c / c 0   two-phase Ni-base superalloys. Conventional Co-base superalloys lack eﬀective precipitation strengthening by intermetallic compounds, and depend on solid-solution ele- ments and precipitation of low volume fraction of carbides for their strength. Despite their inferior strength compared to the Ni-base superalloys, Co-base superalloys generally oﬀer better oxidation, corrosion and wear resistance, and have therefore been utilized in components where environ- mental resistance is required, such as gas turbine vanes, rather than in rotating components  [4] . However, the dis- covery of the Co 3 (Al,W)- c 0   phase oﬀers new possibilities for development of novel  c / c 0   Co-base superalloys that possess both high-temperature strength and environmental resistance. Such alloys are of interest since there is a grow- ing demand for high-temperature materials that are usable in severe environments, due to utilization of gasiﬁed coal, low-grade oil and gas with high sulfur content in power- generation systems.  

To explore the potential mechanical properties of this new alloy system, the temperature dependence of the 0.2% ﬂow stress of the Co–Al–W ternary and Ta-contain- ing quaternary alloys has been investigated  [5] . These polycrystalline alloys exhibit anomalous temperature dependence above 873 K, and have peak strengths at 973 K and 1073 K, respectively. Due to the ﬂow stress anomaly, the Ta-containing alloy exhibits strength compa- rable to a commercial polycrystalline Ni-base superalloy at 1173 K. Transmission electron microscopy studies on deformed specimens indicated that the activation of multi- ple slip modes is responsible for the occurrence of the ﬂow stress anomalies in the Ta-containing alloy  [5] .  

In this study, polycrystalline and single-crystal Co–Al– W-base alloys containing additional alloying elements were used to obtain further understanding of the deformation behavior and strengthening mechanisms in this new  c / c 0 system. The temperature dependence of the strength was examined in compression, and detailed examination of dis- location substructure was conducted by transmission elec- tron microscopy. The role of alloying elements and future approaches for alloy improvement are discussed.  

# 2. Experimental procedures  

The nominal compositions of polycrystalline and single- crystal alloys are listed in  Table 1 . Ternary alloys are here- after referred to with reference to their W concentration, and the others are referred to according to their quaternary element concentration or elements added to the ternary system, as listed in  Table 1 . Two ternary alloy composi- tions were chosen based on the reported phase diagram [3]  to vary the volume fractions of the  c 0   phase; the volume fractions are low in the 9W alloy and high in the 11W alloy. Ta was added to improve the strength and stability of the  c 0 phase  [3,5] . Re was chosen to enhance the solid-solution strength of the  c  matrix phase  [4] . The combined addition of Cr and Ta aimed to improve both strength and environ- mental resistance. The 11W and 2Ta alloys were selected for further study using single-crystal specimens.  

The polycrystalline alloys were melted as 65 g button ingots using a Crystalox induction levitation melting sys- tem under an Ar back-ﬁlled atmosphere after evacuation. Pure 99.9% Co and 99.99% Al pellets and a combination of compressed high-purity elemental powders of 99.5% Co, 99.95% W, 99.9% Ta, 99.99% Re and 99.95% Cr were utilized. These alloys except for CrTa were homogenized at 1623 K for 43.2 ks. CrTa was homogenized at 1573 K for 21.6 ks. Following this, the 2Ta alloy was aged at 1273 K for 360 ks, and the others were aged at 1173 K for 540 ks. For these heat treatments, the alloys were sealed in quartz capsules, which were argon back-ﬁlled after evacuation.  

For single-crystal material preparation, polycrystalline ingots of 11W and 2Ta alloys 2.5 kg in weight were fabri- cated by Sophisticated Alloys Inc., Butler, PA, USA. These ingots were directionally solidiﬁed as cylindrical bars (19 and 16 mm in diameter and 150 mm in length) in ceramic cluster molds in a laboratory scale Bridgman furnace (ALD Technologies Inc., Hanau, Germany) at the Univer- sity of Michigan  [6] . A withdrawal rate of 200 mm h  1   was utilized. Prior to the initiation of the withdrawal process, the charge alloys were induction-melted and poured into the investment molds preheated to 1823 K. Conventional helical starters were utilized to initiate single-crystal growth. To macroscopically investigate the presence of solidiﬁcation defects, macro-etching was conducted by sub- merging the directionally solidiﬁed bars into a solution of 50% HCl and 50% H 2 O heated at 343 K. The single-crystal specimens were homogenized at 1623 K for 43.2 ks. Aging treatments were conducted at 1223 K for 432 ks and 1273 K for 360 ks for 11W and 2Ta, respectively.  

Diﬀerential thermal analysis (DTA) was conducted for polycrystalline alloys using a Seteram Setsys 18 system with a Pt reference. The system was calibrated with high-purity Ni and Ag using prior to experiment  [6] . Cylindrical spec- imens with mass ranging from 250 to 300 mg were pre- pared, and transformation temperatures were determined during heating at 5 K min  1 .

These transforma- tion temperatures for the polycrystalline alloys are listed in Table 2 . Compared with the liquidus temperature of 9W at 1763 K, those of 2Ta and CrTa are about 30 K lower. # 3.2. Temperature dependence of the ﬂow stress  

The 0.2% ﬂow stress for each of the polycrystalline alloys is plotted as a function of temperature in  Fig. 3 a. For comparison, the 0.2% yield stresses of conventional Ni-base cast and superalloy (MarM247), sheet Ni-base superalloy (IN617), cast Co-base superalloy (MarM302) and wrought Co-base superalloy (Haynes 188) are also included  [7] . All examined alloys exhibited a similar tem- perature dependence of the ﬂow stress with three regimes of behavior. The ﬁrst stage is the normal decrease of ﬂow stress with temperature up to 873 K. The second stage dis- plays anomalous positive temperature dependence, and the third stage again exhibits negative temperature depen- dence. The ternary alloys, 9W and 11W, exhibit almost equivalent strength, regardless of the varying volume frac- tions of the  c 0   precipitates. They have a ﬂow stress of about 520 MPa at room temperature. With increasing tempera- ture, these alloys show a minimum of 420 MPa at 873 K, and then show a maximum of about 460 MPa at 973 K, followed by a rapid decrease with temperature. The 2Re exhibits the same trend as the ternary alloys, but the strength level is 150–200 MPa lower, probably due to the formation of the  c  + Co 3 W two-phase layer along the grain boundaries ( Fig. 1 d). On the other hand, the Ta-containing alloys, 2Ta and CrTa, exhibit an extended anomalous strengthening stage. These alloys possess similar strength up to 1073 K. The peak temperature is located at 1073 K and the ﬂow stress reaches close to 580 MPa. Above the peak, the 2Ta alloy exhibited a slight decrease in the ﬂow stress up to 1173 K, followed by a rapid decrease to 430 MPa at 1210 K. In the CrTa alloy, the onset of the third stage occurs above 1073 K. It is apparent that addi- tions of Ta signiﬁcantly improve the high-temperature strength in the Co–Al–W alloy system. The earlier onset of the third stage in CrTa compared with 2Ta can be attrib- uted to the lower  c 0   solvus temperature. Compared with the conventional superalloys, the ternary alloys exhibit almost same strength as cast Co-base superalloys. The strength of the Ta-containing alloys exceeds the conventional Co-base cast superalloys above 973 K due to the large ﬂow stress anomalies. Importantly, the ﬂow stress of 2Ta is compara- ble to that of the Ni-base superalloy above 1173 K.  

Fig. 3 b shows 0.2% ﬂow stresses of the single-crystal 11W and 2Ta alloys. The crystal orientation of the 2Ta is about 3   away from [001], and that of the 11W is about 7   away from [011]. The single-crystal 11W shows equiva- lent strength to the polycrystalline ternary alloys up to 973 K, but the anomalous second stage is extended up to 1073 K. The ﬂow stress at the peak temperature is 530 MPa. Above the peak, the ﬂow stress decreases rapidly. The single-crystal 2Ta also shows almost equivalent ﬂow stress to the polycrystalline 2Ta up to 1173 K, but the decrease in the ﬂow stress above 1173 K is slower. The ﬂow stress at 1243 K is over 500 MPa. Up to the peak temper- ature, the 0.2% ﬂow stress of the single-crystal 2Ta is 50–80 MPa higher than that of 11W, but at the highest temperature, the diﬀerence is widened to over 200 MPa. The higher strength observed in the single-crystal 11W above 973 K and 2Ta above 1173 K compared to the poly- crystalline alloys can be attributed to the elimination of grain boundaries. The polycrystalline specimens exhibit formation of severe cracks along grain boundaries after the tests at elevated temperatures. Therefore, the true anomalous ﬂow stress peak of 11W is located at 1073 K, the same temperature as the 2Ta alloy, not 973 K as is observed in the polycrystalline 11W. This result indicates

# 3.3. Dislocation substructures of polycrystalline 2Ta alloy  

The inverse dependence of the ﬂow stress on tempera- ture and the appearance of the peak in the ﬂow stress at ele- vated temperatures are phenomena observed in L1 2  and other intermetallic compounds  [8–16]  and also in  c / c 0   Ni- base superalloys  [7,8,16–19] . It is generally understood that the anomalous strengthening in L1 2  compounds is caused by pinning of cross-slipped screw segments of superdisloca- tions from the octahedral {111} plane to the cube {100} plane, which is driven by elastic anisotropy and/or lower anti-phase boundary (APB) energy on {001} planes [8,13–16,20–22] . In order to understand the origin of the ﬂow stress anomalies and strengthening mechanisms of the Co–Al–W-base alloys, dislocation substructures were examined in detail in the polycrystalline 2Ta alloy speci- mens after compression tests.  

Fig. 4  shows general features of the dislocation substruc- tures observed in 2Ta after deformation in compression at 873, 1073 and 1163 K, which correspond to temperatures below, near-peak and above the peak anomalous ﬂow stress. At 873 K ( Fig. 4 a) and 1073 K ( Fig. 4 b), disloca- tions are present preferentially in the  c  matrix channels that contain hyperﬁne  c 0   precipitates. Additionally, some dislo- cations are trapped within the  c 0   precipitates. Stacking faults extending across the precipitates are occasionally observed, and they are more frequent at 1073 K than at 873 K.  Fig. 4 c is taken from the specimen deformed at 1163 K. The observation from the [101] direction reveals a high density of planar faults on two {111} planes. These faults are stacking faults that extend across the entire diam- eter of the  c 0   precipitates. Thus, stacking faults become the dominant defects in the precipitates with increasing tem- perature from 1073 to 1163 K.  

Fig. 5 shows weak-beam dark ﬁeld images of paired dis-locations shearing a  c 0   precipitate at 873 K. Observation from the [100] direction ( Fig. 5 a) revealed a pair of dislo- cations, but the pair cannot be distinguished when observed from [101] ( Fig. 5 b), indicating that the pair of dislocations are located on the  ð 111 Þ  plane. The pairs of 1/2 h 110 i dislocations are observed on both {111} and {001} planes at the peak ﬂow temperature. In L1 2  single-phase alloys [13–16,20–22] , such as Ni 3 Al, it is generally understood that the primary deformation mode below the anomalous peak temperature is  h 110 i {111} slip. With increasing tem- perature, thermally activated cross-slip of segments of the super dislocations from the octahedral plane to the cube plane becomes frequent, resulting in the anomalous hard- ening. The observation of 1/2 h 110 i  dislocations on both octahedral and cube planes within the precipitate phase suggests operation of dislocation processes similar to the Ni-based L1 2  phases, and activation of multiple slip modes including  h 110 i {111},  h 110 i {001} and  h 112 i {111} is associated with the observed ﬂow anomalies in the exam- ined  c / c 0   two-phase Co-base alloys.

# 3.4. Dislocation substructures of single-crystal specimens

At 873, 1073 and 1173 K ( Fig. 8 a–c), dislocation substructures are similar to the ones observed in polycrystalline 2Ta ( Fig. 4 ). At 873 and 1073 K, dislocations are preferentially gliding in the  c  matrix channel. Fig. 10  shows the increment of the ﬂow stress,  D r , from 0.05% to 0.2% strain and from 0.2% and 1% strain during compression tests of the single-crystal 11W and 2Ta. The D r  from 0.05% to 0.2% shows the same trend in 11W and 2Ta. It increases from 873 K up to 1073 K (peak tem- perature), and then drops to a level close to that observed at 873 K. The increase can be attributed to work harden- ing, due to interactions of ordinary dislocations in the  c channels along with some hardening due to the pinning of cross-slipped dislocation segments in the precipitates. On the other hand,  D r  from 0.2% to 1% shows a very sig- niﬁcant increase above 973 K in 2Ta compared with 11W. This result is due to the diﬀerent deformation mechanisms in 11W and 2Ta above the peak temperature. In 11W, the contribution of the  c 0   precipitates to the strength becomes small above the peak temperature, as is clear from the dis- locations by-passing the precipitates. On the other hand, in 2Ta, the faulting mechanism within the  c 0   precipitates is sustained above the peak temperature, resulting in a greater degree of work hardening. The enhanced degree of stacking fault hardening with temperature in the 2Ta alloy indicates that either the formation of the 1/3 h 112 i dislocations at the interface or their propagation across the precipitates is thermally activated.  

# 4. Discussion  

Nickel-base alloys exhibit a unique combination of high- temperature deformation mechanisms that to date have not been duplicated in any higher melting point materials that might displace superalloys from technologically important systems such as aircraft engines and power-generation tur- bines. Nickel-base alloys have a high resistance to deforma- tion at high fractions of their melting temperature due to the diﬃculty of shearing the ordered L1 2  precipitates at moderate stresses and strain rates  [17] . However, since the precipitates may eventually be sheared by superdisloca- tions, as the stresses rise to high levels at crack tips or under high rate (foreign object damage) conditions, this class of materials maintains a reasonably high level of ductility and toughness, preventing catastrophic failure. Higher- temperature refractory alloys and intermetallic com- pounds, with generally high strengths and accompanying high resistances to dislocation motion, do not generally dis- play these characteristics and thus have not experienced widespread engineering application.  

The cobalt-based materials examined here, in many important respects, possess similar high-temperature defor- mation attributes compared to nickel-base superalloys. Deformation is initiated by glide of 1/2 h 110 i  dislocations in the fcc  c  matrix channels. As the stresses rise, the precip- itates begin to deform by glide of  h 110 i  super dislocations on octahedral and cube planes or by motion of 1/3 h 112 i dislocations across the precipitates, with accompanying stacking-fault formation. However, important diﬀerences in the deformation modes exist in the Ta-containing ver- sions of these Co-base materials at temperatures above 1073 K. Stacking fault formation in the L1 2  precipitates above the peak temperature results in very high work hard- ening rates, giving these Co-base materials potentially higher strengths compared to Ni-base materials at the high- est end of the temperature spectrum. A more detailed understanding of the partitioning of the Ta between the matrix and precipitate phases is needed to further exploit this unusual deformation characteristic. Nevertheless, this present study suggests that further alloying of these mate- rials may result in Co-base alloys with properties superior to Ni-base alloys above 1073 K. For this reason, it is worth considering several issues important to alloying for further improvements in the properties of these materials.  

Compared with the Ni-base superalloys, the Co–Al–W alloys have higher liquidus temperatures by more than 50 K  [6]  as is shown in  Table 2 . This suggests that there is a possibility for greater temperature capability compared to Ni-base alloys. However, the  c 0   solvus temperatures of the Co–Al–W-base alloys are lower by more than 100 K. The thermal stability of the  c 0   phase is key to improving the high-temperature strength of  c / c 0   alloys. In addition, the shearing mechanisms of the  c 0   phase at elevated temper- ature were important for sustaining the strength above the anomalous peak temperature. Based on the comparison of the dislocation substructures of single-crystal 2Ta and 11W ( Figs. 8 and 9 ), an addition of Ta seems to reduce the stak- ing fault energy of the  c 0   phase. The stacking-fault energy is closely related to the diﬀerence in bulk free energy of face- centered cubic (fcc) and hexagonal close packed (hcp) phases and the magnetic surface energy  [28,29] . Therefore, additions of elements that can increase the  c 0   solvus temperature and reduce the stacking fault energy of the  c 0 phase at elevated temperatures are required to improve the high-temperature strength. An improved understanding of the alloying eﬀect on APB energy would be also important to enhance the anomalous hardening. Availability of a thermodynamic database would provide useful guidance in identifying the key alloying elements. Finally, studies on the deformation characteristics of these alloys under creep and fatigue conditions are needed. Given the unusual shearing behavior of the precipitates at high temperatures, good high-temperature fatigue resistance might be expected.  

The grain boundary brittleness is detrimental, as is observed during compression tests of polycrystalline mate- rial ( Fig. 3 ). Additions of B and/or Hf are commonly uti- lized to improve the ductility in Ni-base superalloys  [1] , and these elements are expected to be also beneﬁcial in the Co–Al–W-base alloys for polycrystalline forms of material.  

One of the interesting aspects of this alloy system is the wide  c  single-phase region due to the relatively high liqui- dus and low  c 0   solvus temperatures. This wide window could permit high-temperature processing operations, sug- gesting that  c / c 0   Co-base materials could be developed as wrought or sheet materials. The 0.2% ﬂow stresses of the Co–Al–W ternary alloys are superior to the conventional Ni-base sheet alloy (IN617) and Co-base wrought alloy (Haynes 188), as shown in  Fig. 3 a. Thus,  c 0   strengthening may be taken advantage of in sheet, wrought as well as a cast materials. Considering that the Ni-base superalloys initially evolved from the Ni–Al–Cr system, the Co–Al– W-base alloys are still in their early stages if development and have high potential for improvement.  

# 5. Summary  

1. An addition of Ta to Co–Al–W alloys is eﬀective for sta- bilization of the  c 0   phase, while an addition of Cr decreases the  c 0   solvus temperature. An addition of Re does not aﬀect the transformation temperatures.

 2. The Co–Al–W-base  c / c 0   two-phase alloys exhibit the ﬂow stress anomalies above 873 K due to activation of paired 1/2 h 110 i  dislocation slip on both octahedral and cube planes in the  c 0   precipitates.

 3. The anomalous peak temperature of the ternary and Ta- containing alloys is at 1073 K, although the polycrystal- line ternary alloy showed a peak at 973 K due to the grain boundary brittleness.  

4. The Ta-containing alloy sustains its strength above the peak temperature due to the activation of 1/3 h 112 i  par- tial dislocations that shear the  c 0   precipitates and form a high density of stacking faults in the precipitates. On the other hand, the ternary alloy shows a rapid decrease in the ﬂow stress above the peak temperature, due to 1/2 110  dislocations by-passing the precipitates.
