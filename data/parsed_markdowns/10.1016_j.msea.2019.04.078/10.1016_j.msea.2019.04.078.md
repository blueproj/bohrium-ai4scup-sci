# Abstract  

In the present work, we study the effect of HIP rejuvenation treatments on the creep behavior  MANUSCRIPT and residual life of a pre-crept single crystal Ni-base superalloy of type CMSX-4. The present  work combines miniature creep experiments of precisely oriented [001] tensile creep speci- mens with HIP treatments and quantitative analysis of scanning and transmission electron  micrographs. A HIP-rejuvenation treatment after 5.0 % creep pre-strain significantly improves  creep strength. However, the microstructural damage induced by the creep pre-deformation  could not be fully removed. In a series of sequential creep/HIP/creep-experiments, increasing  levels of pre-deformation result in increasing levels of creep rates even after identical HIP- rejuvenation treatments. The memory effect, which causes this phenomenon, is related to an  accumulation of permanent microstructural damage, which is not associated with rafting or  cavitation. The mechanical results obtained in the present work are interpreted based on  γ -channel widths and  γʼ -size), on the pore  population (number density of pores, pore size distributions and pore area fractions) and  dislocation substructures which have formed during creep. # Introduction  

Single crystal Ni-based superalloys (SXs) are used for first stage blades in gas turbines,  which operate in aero engines and power plants [1-4]. They have to withstand mechanical  loads at temperatures well above half of the melting point in Kelvin, where creep [5-8] limits  their service life. During creep, the microstructures of SXs evolve. Dislocation densities in- MANUSCRIPT crease [9-12], dislocation networks form around cuboidal  into the ordered  γ ’-phase [20-30], cuboidal  then undergo a morphological change known as topological inversion [31-54]. Moreover,  small creep micro pores form, which cannot be easily distinguished from micro cast pores  [55-63]. During creep these elementary processes result in microstructural changes, which  lead to an increase in creep rate and initiate final rupture. In the present work, we investigate  whether the microstructural processes which degrade creep performance can be reversed by  rejuvenation treatments. This topic has received attention in the last decade [64-80]. It has  been shown that HIP rejuvenation procedures can improve microstructural and mechanical  properties of crept material states. The current investigation explores how SX microstruc- tures, which evolve during different degrees of creep predeformation, react to HIP rejuvena- tion heat treatments and how well initial microstructural and mechanical properties can be re- established. The present work builds on the findings of a previous investigation where the  effect of HIP rejuvenation on creep was studied for two levels of creep exposure (0.6 and 2.0  ACCEPTED % strain) [80]. In the present study we consider a wider pre-creep strain interval. The Ni- based SX of type ERBO/1 (CMSX 4 type) [61, 81] is pre-crept to strains ranging from  0.4 to 5.0 %. The evolution of microstructure during this pre-deformation is monitored. Then  the pre-crept material states are subjected to HIP rejuvenation treatments. After characteriz- ing the rejuvenated microstructures, the material states are again subjected to creep. The  results of the present work show, that rejuvenation is always beneficial. However, higher de- grees of pre-deformation make it more difficult to fully restore the original creep properties.

# 4. Discussion  

General aspects : Rejuvenation processes of polycrystalline and single crystal Ni-base sup- eralloys have been in the focus of interest of material scientists for more than three decades  [64-80]. Within the framework of the present study it is not possible to give fair credit to all  contributions to the field. It must suffice to say that it is well known that rejuvenation treat- ments are beneficial, e.g. Figure 16. A number of procedural rejuvenation aspects are today  generally accepted. Thus, there is general agreement that rejuvenation treatments should be  performed at temperatures between the  γʼ -solvus and the solidus temperatures of the alloy.  

Rejuvenation procedures should aim at dissolving all phases and re-precipitate the cuboidal  γ ’-particles to establish  γ / γ ’-microstructures with volume fractions, sizes and shapes which  are as close as possible to that of the initial state. If TCP-phase particles have formed during  service, these should be dissolved and their re-precipitation should be avoided. It was recog- nized early on that high temperature reactions during rejuvenation must be well controlled  and that it can be difficult to avoid the formation of carbides. The application of hydrostatic  MANUSCRIPT pressure in a HIP process helps to reduce porosity and ideally, the multiple step heat treat- ment which is required to establish an appropriate  the HIP rejuvenation treatment. All these findings could be reproduced in the present study,  which uses precisely oriented [001] miniature tensile creep specimens and a HIP unit, which  allows for fast cooling. This has helped to reduce scatter related to processing related miso- rientations from ideal [001] directions and poorly defined temperature time profiles. Efforts  were made to address recrystallization processing during rejuvenation treatments. This is  important but is outside of the scope of the present work, where measures were taken for  recrystallization not to occur (see experimental section).  

On remaining damage, which downgrades creep performance after rejuvenation rejuvenation of SX Ni-base superalloys is concerned, most studies have investigated the  behavior of overaged tensile and creep specimens [e.g. 71-76]. Only limited work has been  ACCEPTED done considering pre-creep, rejuvenation and a second creep period to identify whether the  rejuvenation treatment can fully wipe out all microstructural damage processes which oc- curred during pre-creep [70,79,80]. It was claimed that microstructural damage associated  with small pre-strains of the order of 0.6 % (before the creep rate minimum) can be fully reju- venated. In contrast, it was claimed that pre-deformations in excess of 2.0 % (after the mini- mum) produce damage, which cannot be fully removed by a rejuvenation treatment [80]. The  results obtained in the present work show that there is a memory effect, where larger pre- creep deformations result in lower creep strengths even after identical rejuvenation treat- ments. The creep performance of a pre-crept specimen is governed by the intensity of its  pre-creep deformation. A rejuvenation is always beneficial. However, it is not clear whether  rejuvenation can fully re-establish the creep properties of the initial state.  

Evolution of porosity and formation of carbides : It is well known that HIP rejuvenation heat  treatments reduce porosity. The hydrostatic pressure provides a driving force for the shrink- age of pores. In the present work, the initial state material was subjected to an integrated HIP  heat treatment and therefore has a low initial porosity of only 6 pores/mm 2 , Figures 5 and 6a  (area fraction: 0,0009 %). During creep pre-deformation to 0,4 and 5.0 % the area number  densities increases to 12 and 15 pores/mm 2 , and the pore area fractions increase to 0.0014  and 0.0025 %, Figure 13a. During creep rejuvenation the number of pores, their area fraction  and their average size decreases, Figure 13b. A second creep period results in a new in- crease of these three microstructural parameters, Figure 13b. The evolution of porosity dur- ing high temperature creep of single crystal superalloys is fully in line with the recent analysis  of Buck et al. [63]. The HIP process provides a driving force for pore closure [86]. Figures 14  and 15 provide clear experimental evidence for the fact that carbides form at the inner sur- MANUSCRIPT faces of pores, even though the initial state of the alloy investigated in the present work, prior  to any heat treatment, only contained trace levels of Carbon. It was also found (not shown  here) that C levels increase towards the surface of the material. The presence of C after high  temperature HIP treatments in the present work is due to the heat treatment in a graphite  furnace, where the graphite heating elements act as C sources. While this finding was not  intended, it allows to draw an interesting conclusion: There are certain elements, like C,  which under HIP conditions diffuse faster towards pores than others. In the present case the  C from the graphite heating elements diffuses into the alloy and forms carbides at the pore  surfaces. As can be seen from Table 3, the formation of carbides is associated with a deple- tion of carbide forming elements in the matrix. This type of observation has been previously  made by Pal at al. [76] for René N5, a single crystal superalloy similar to ERBO/1 or CMSX-

 4. In agreement with the findings of the present work, they also showed that particles of type 

 (Ta, Hf)C formed. It is possible that the stable carbide phase which forms during HIP rejuve- ACCEPTED nation delays the growth of remaining pores during the second creep period. This would ra- tionalize the small differences in the parameters of the pore population after rejuvenation and  after the subsequent second creep period (Figures 13b and c). Further work is required to  investigate the transfer of C from the graphite heating elements into the alloy. In our research  group we presently no longer use graphite heating elements. Instead, Mo heating elements  are used and carbide formation is no longer observed. The pores detected in the present  work are probably too small to affect creep rates. However, in the later stages of creep,  pores can coalesce and help in the nucleation of micro cracks which can initiate final creep  rupture. Therefore, it is important to study these elementary damage processes in more de- tail.  

Microstructural reasons for memorizing pre-creep events : From a materials science point of  view, it is interesting to clarify the reason for the pre-strain memory effect identified in the  present work. The detailed quantitative metallographic analysis, shown in Figure 9, reveals  that identical HIP heat treatments do not establish the same  γ / γʼ -microstructure. Instead, it  can be seen that after larger pre-creep deformations rejuvenated specimens show larger  γʼ - particle sizes and wider  γ -channels. One can exclude that this is related to the lower homog- enization temperatures 1280 and 1300°C of the second integrated HIP treatment as com- pared to 1290 and 1315°C during the initial HIP treatment, Figures 1 and 3. After all, the raft- ed microstructures shown in Figure 7 have disappeared and after rejuvenation new  γ / γ ’- microstructures with cuboidal  γ ’-microstructures have formed. This suggests that the direc- tionally coarsened  γ ’-particles were dissolved during rejuvenation in the homogenization  phase. However, the TEM micrographs in Figure 17 provide some evidence for a scenario,  which was recently suggested by Ruttert et al. [80]. They claimed that dislocations networks  MANUSCRIPT can stabilize  γʼ -particles and this type of stabilization may well be more effective after higher  degrees of creep pre-deformation. Some  Pre-existing  γʼ -particles can grow to larger sizes during the integrated precipitation heat  treatment of the HIP-rejuvenation process. At a constant volume fraction, larger  -particle  sizes are associated with wider  γ creep deformations result in faster creep strain accumulation after rejuvenation. Further work  is required to conform this scenario. Most importantly, microstructures need to be analyzed in  the TEM after different amounts of pre-creep deformations and rejuvenation.  

# 5. Summary and Conclusions  

In the present work we investigate the effect of a HIP-rejuvenation treatment (100 MPa,  1300°C) on the high temperature strength of a pre-crept (350 MPa, 950°C) single crystal  superalloy ERBO/1. A series of experiments were performed, which consisted of a first creep  exposure followed either by a direct continuation of the test under the same conditions of  stress and temperature or by an intermediate HIP-rejuvenation treatment before the test was  continued. From the results performed in the present work the following conclusions can be  drawn:  

(1) The micro porosity which characterizes the as cast single crystal superalloy ERBO/1 can  be reduced by an initial HIP treatment at a pressure of 100 MPa and a maximum HIP tem- perature of 1315°C. After [001] tensile creep at 950°C and 350 MPa one observes rafting  and a slight increase of the number density and the area fraction of pores on SEM cross sec- tions.  

(2) A HIP-rejuvenation heat treatment with integrated solution and precipitation steps re- establishes a regular equiaxed  γ / γʼ -microstructure after a first period of [001] tensile creep  exposure (950°C, 350 MPa) had established a rafted microstructure. The rejuvenation of  specimens with different levels of creep pre-strain resulted in slightly coarser  γ / γʼ - microstructures for higher amounts of pre-deformation. Some evidence has been presented  that suggests that this is associated with a better stabilization of  γʼ -particles by dislocation  networks for higher amounts of pre-creep deformation.  

(3) While the present study has shown that distinct levels of damage remain after pre-creep  deformation, it has also clearly demonstrated that HIP-rejuvenation has a beneficial effect on  creep life. It was shown that a HIP-rejuvenation treatment after 5.0 % creep pre-strain results  in a material state, which deforms significantly slower than the same material state without  an intermediate HIP-rejuvenation treatment.  

(4) Two microstructural scenarios have been suggested in the discussion of the experimental  results, which both require further investigation. First, during HIP-rejuvenation carbide phas- es form in/at pores.  These may react as diffusion barriers and delay pore growth during  creep re-exposure. Second, higher levels of creep pre-strain may lead to the formation of  more stable dislocation networks. It needs to be proven whether this can result in a stronger  stabilization of the  γ ’-phase during homogenization annealing.
