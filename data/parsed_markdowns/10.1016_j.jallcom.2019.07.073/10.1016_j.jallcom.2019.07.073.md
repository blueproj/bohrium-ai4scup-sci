# Abstract  

The heat resistance of copper alloys is desirable to improve while it services at high  temperature as electrically and thermally conductive components. In present paper, the  strengthening pattern of nickel-based superalloys is expected to be applied in copper alloys. Here  ACCEPTED the Cu 50 Ni 37.5 =Al, Cr, Mo) alloys were prepared by vacuum arc melting and heat-treated in  the corresponding conditions to obtain three different kinds of typical microstructure. The room  temperature (RT) properties (hardness and resistivity) and high temperature properties (melting  point, softening temperature and variable temperature resistivity) of the three alloys are contrasted  and analyzed in detail. The Cu alloy strengthened through the  γ ' phase coherent  precipitation shows the best conductivity and highest hardness (5.47 %IACS   (International  Annealed Copper Standard) and 310.1 HV). The splitting of the  γ ' phase is contributing to  hardening at high aging temperature. The resistivity-temperature behavior of the three alloys shows  that the resistivity with rising temperature consists of the ideal lattice resistivity and the resistivity  increment due to the defects. The Cu 50 Ni 37.5 Al 12.5  alloy has the smallest room temperature resistivity  

ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT and its resistivity maintains the lowest during the whole heating process (RT-1077K). Therefore, the  coherent precipitated  γ ' phase is expected to improve the heat resistance of the copper alloys while  maintaining its electrical conductivity.  

Keyword:  copper alloy; microstructure; resistivity;   heat resistance

4.2.3 Reason for the evolution of the microstructure with temperature  

With the temperature increasing, the morphology of the precipitated-phase in Cu 50 Ni 37.5 M 12.5  

 ( M =Al, Cr) alloys presents two different evolution trends, one is the  γ ' phase splits, the other is the  γ 2  phase merges and coarsens, both of the changes are aimed at reducing the total energy ( E total ).  The  γ ' and  γ  phases as well as the  γ 1  and  γ 2  phases, both belong to the single coherent-precipitation  system. The  E total  can be expressed by the following equation  [31]:  

# E total  =  E str  +  E surf  +  E int  

where  E str  is the elastic-strain energy between the precipitated-phase and the matrix,  E surf  is the  surface energy of the precipitated-phase, and  E int  is the elastic interaction-energy between the  precipitates (always negative) [32]. For the small precipitated-phases, the coarsening process is  MANUSCRIPT “interface-controlled”. When the precipitated-phases become larger, the  E surf  and the coarsening process gradually transformed into “strain-controlled”. During the  coarsening process, the  δ   larger, the distribution of precipitated phase would be in a thermoelastic “quasi-equilibrium” in  which the precipitate size coarsens very slowly. Continually coarsening, it could divide into two  distinct stages: further coarsening but a loss of coherency or reconfiguration of the precipitates  distribution (the large particles resist further growth and splits into smaller similar-sized particles)  [33].  

In the Cu ACCEPTED γ′  phase, and the distance (\~200 nm)  between the   phases are large at RT. With aging temperature rising, the size of  γ′  phase already  reach appreciable size, would opposes further growth and splits to smaller one, which could greatly  reduce the  E  increases through splitting.
