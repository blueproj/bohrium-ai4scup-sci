# 1. Introduction  

Novel  γ ′ -strengthened Co – Al – W-base superalloys (Hereinafter re- ferred to as the novel Co-base superalloys) not only have the high so- lidus/liquidus temperatures as well as low solidi ﬁ cation segregation, but also show a comparable high-temperature performance with Ni- base superalloys [ 1 – 3 ], and the novel Co-base superalloys become one of the most promising new generation of high-temperature structural materials. Compared with traditional Ni-base superalloys, the higher solidus temperature and lower  γ ′  solvus temperature of novel Co-base superalloys bene ﬁ t for obtaining larger processing window, which makes it possible to develop the high-performance wrought alloys for turbine disks.  

Increasing the contents and categories of the alloying elements is an important method to further improve mechanical properties of the novel wrought Co-base superalloys. Nevertheless, the increase of  γ ′ solvus temperature as well as the decrease of solidus temperature re- sulting from high-order alloying additions will sharply reduce the processing window and deteriorate hot workability of the alloys.  

Up to now, researches on novel Co-base superalloys predominantly focused on the e ﬀ ects of alloying elements on the microstructure and mechanical properties in the ternary to hexahydric alloys [ 4 – 10 ], and studies focusing on  γ ′ -strengthened multi-component wrought novel Co-base superalloys (Hereinafter referred to as the novel wrought Co- base superalloys) are still limited. Therefore, in order to develop the practical high-performance novel wrought Co-base superalloys, it is crucial to further improve the me- chanical properties of the alloys on the premise of maintaining a large processing window and good hot deformation property.  

Based on our previous research [ 14 ], this study aims at improving the mechanical properties of novel wrought Co-base superalloys on the premise of maintaining a large processing window and good hot workability, and investigates the e ﬀ ects of Al and Ti on the hot work- ability, partitioning behavior of alloying elements in  γ / γ ′  two phases, microstructure and mechanical properties for novel wrought Co-base superalloys. The results can provide guidance for the development of high-performance novel wrought Co-base superalloys.  

# 2. Materials and methods  

Previously reported alloy Co – 30Ni – 6Al – 2W-3.5Ti – 1Ta – 1Mo – 12Cr- 0.1Hf (at%) possesses low mass density and good microstructure sta- bility [ 14 ]. Based on our previous research, in order to keep similar processing window of the alloys, three novel wrought Co-base super- alloys were designed by using the concept of replacing 1.5 at% Al with

 0.7 at% Ti. The three alloys were named as 7Al-3.5Ti, 5.5Al-4.2Ti, 4Al-

 4.9Ti according to the content of Al and Ti, respectively. The nominal and actual compositions of the four experimental alloys (6Al-3.5Ti, 7Al- 3.5Ti, 5.5Al-4.2Ti, 4Al-4.9Ti) are listed in  Table 1 . Button ingots of 50 g were prepared by arc melting through eight cycles in an argon atmo- sphere to ensure homogeneity. The purity of all raw elements was 99.99% by mass or higher. The master ingots were then drop-casted into a copper mold with a dimension of  ϕ 10 mm × 100 mm. The C as well as Hf, B, Zr content of the master alloys were determined by high- frequency combustion infrared absorption method and inductively coupled plasma-atomic emission spectrometer (ICP-AES), respectively. Additionally, glow discharge optical emission spectroscopy (GDOES) was utilized to con ﬁ rm the Si content of the master alloys. Since the total mass loss of the master alloys during arc melting was less than 0.1%, it can be considered that the actual content of other main al- loying elements was the same as the nominal content.  

Alloy samples were ground and wrapped in Ta foils, and then en- capsulated in quartz tubes which were back- ﬁ lled with argon, following a two-step solution heat treatment of 1110 °C at 6 h + 1190 °C at 10 h. Density of the alloys was measured by Archimedes ’  principle, and  ﬁ ve measured values for each alloy were used to obtain the mean value. Di ﬀ erential thermal analysis (DTA) experiments were performed using a NETZSCH STA 449F3 instrument under high purity Ar  ﬂ ow to obtain the  γ ′  solvus, solidus and liquidus temperatures. Cylindrical samples extracted from the heat-treated ingots for DTA experiments with the mass from 70 to 100 mg were utilized, and the heating rate was 10 K (°C) min − 1   in the temperature range from 700 to 1400 °C. The  γ / γ ′ two-phase compositions were measured by a JXA8230 electron probe microanalyzer (EPMA) with 20 KV accelerating voltage and 10 nA beam current after a solution heat treatment and aging heat treatment of 1000 °C at 600 h.  

The samples were prepared by grounding and polishing, and etched electrolytic ally in a solution of 42 ml H 3 PO 4 +32 ml H 2 SO 4 +24 ml H 2 O. The  γ / γ ′  microstructure was examined with a Zeiss GEMINISEM 500  ﬁ eld-emission scanning electron microscope (SEM) in secondary electron imaging mode, and the equipped X-Max Extreme energy-dis- persive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) (Oxford Instruments) was used to determine the phase compositions. The  γ ′  volume fraction was analyzed by the standard point count method, and the size of  γ ′  precipitates (de ﬁ ned as the average edge length of the cuboidal precipitates) was measured by the Image-Pro plus 6.0 software. For each alloy, no less than three images were used to obtain meaningful statistics. Room temperature Vickers hardness measurements were conducted at a HXD- 1000TM tester with an applied load of 300 g and a dwell time of 15s and performed at ten points across multiple grains. To assess the pro- cessability above the  γ ′  solvus temperature, compression tests at a strain rate of 1 × 10 − 2 s − 1   were performed using a thermo-mechanical si- mulator Gleeble 3800 with cylindrical homogenized samples ( ϕ 9 × 12 mm) at 1150 °C. After the hot deformation, all the samples were held at 1150 °C for 1 h to complete the static recrystallization. Compression tests at a strain rate of 1 × 10 − 4 s − 1   were performed on a SANS equipment at ambient temperature and 750 °C, with cylindrical compression samples in size of ϕ 4mm × 5 mm. A JEM 2010 Transmission Electron Microscope (TEM) was utilized for dislocation analysis, and the foils obtained from the deformed samples were pre- pared by the GL-696 F ion-milled instrument.

# 3.2. Elemental partitioning between  γ  and  γ ′  phases  

Mechanical properties of the superalloys largely depend on the compositions of  γ  and  γ ′  phases. Therefore, researchers always show great interest in the partitioning behavior of alloying elements between γ  and  γ ′  phases [ 18 – 23 ]. Referring to the method utilized in Ref. [ 19 ], the size of some  γ ′  phases in the experimental specimens that were annealed at 1000 °C for 600 h is larger than 1  μ m, which is available for quantitative analysis of the  γ ′ / γ  two phases by using EPMA. The phase compositions and partitioning coe ﬃ cients K γ '/ γ  of each main element at 1000 °C are listed in  Table 3 , where the K γ '/ γ  is de ﬁ ned as the con- tent of an alloying element in the  γ ′  phase (at%) divided by its content in the  γ  phase (at%). It can be seen that Al, W, Ti, Ta, Ni are  γ ′ -formers which preferentially partition to  γ ′  phase (K > 1); and Co, Cr, Mo are γ -formers which preferentially partition to  γ -phase (K < 1). In the novel Co-base superalloys, the partitioning behavior of Mo between  γ ′ / γ  two-phase always arouses researchers ’  attention. Mo generally pre- ferentially partitions to  γ  phase in Ni-base superalloys, which results in an important e ﬀ ect on solution strengthening [ 20 ]. Nevertheless, Mo generally preferentially partitions to  γ ′  phase in the novel Co-base su- peralloys [ 21 – 23 ]. In the present study, Mo preferentially partitions to the  γ  phase in the four experimental alloys. Analyzing the main reason for the partitioning behavior of Mo in the present study: Ni can decrease the partitioning extent of Mo to  γ ′  [ 23 ]; in addition, results from ab initio calculations show that the Cr atoms prefer to replace Mo atoms in the sublattice sites of the L1 2  unite cell, which can promote Mo to preferentially partition to  γ  phase [ 24 ]. Therefore, the composition characteristics of the four experimental alloys which mean high Ni (30 at%) and Cr (12 at%) content as well as low W (2 at%) content eventually result in the partitioning behavior of Mo with K < 1.  

Fig. 1  shows the lever rule plots derived from the  γ ′ / γ  two-phase compositions for the experimental alloys at 1000 °C. It is noteworthy that since the  γ ′ / γ  two phases have the nearly equal atomic densities, the mole fractions of both phases can be considered as equal to their volume fractions. As is shown in  Fig. 1 , the di ﬀ erence of the con- centration of an alloying element in the nominal alloys composition and γ  matrix (C -C ) is plotted versus the di ﬀ erence of the concentration of m γ an alloying element in the  γ ′  and  γ  phases (C γ ′ -C γ ). The  γ ′  volume fraction f is the slope of a linear  ﬁ t to these points according to the lever rule condition: C  = fC +(1-f)C  Comparing the slope of the  ﬁ tted m γ ′ γ . curves, it can be seen that increasing 1 at% Al can e ﬀ ectively increase  γ ′ volume fraction, but the replacement of 1.5 at% Al with 0.7 at% Ti results in a decrease of  γ ′  volume fraction on the premise of maintaining the almost unchanged  γ ′  solvus temperature.

# 4. Discussion  

# 4.1. E ﬀ ects of Al and Ti content on the mechanical property  

The main strengthening methods for the mechanical properties of polycrystalline superalloys are the following: (i) grain boundary strengthening, a ﬀ ected by grain boundary carbides, borides and grain size, (ii)  γ ′ -precipitate hardening, determined by  γ ′  volume fraction, precipitate size, and intrinsic strength of the  γ ′  precipitate, (iii) solid- solution strengthening, which is associated with the type and content of elements in the  γ  matrix. As can be seen from  Figs. 3  and  6 , the com- pression yield strength of di ﬀ erent experimental alloys under the same heat treatment conditions is less a ﬀ ected by the size of  γ ′  precipitates. Likewise, based on the results in Section  3.3.1 , the relative content of Al and Ti may has a negligible impact on the solid solution strengthening of the experimental alloys. Also, all the test samples of the alloys pos- sess similar coarse grains (\~200  μ m), so the yield strength derived from the Hall-Petch relationship may be similar. Therefore, the variation of the yield stress for the experimental alloys is mainly associated with the volume fraction and intrinsic strength of the  γ ′  precipitates. Alloy 7Al- 3.5Ti has the highest  γ ′  volume fraction (63.9%), but its room-tem- perature yield strength (915 MPa) is signi ﬁ cantly lower than alloy 4Al- 4.9Ti (1025 MPa), even for alloy 6Al-3.5Ti (960 MPa). However, the alloy 7Al-3.5Ti shows a higher yield strength (764 MPa) at 800 °C than that of 6Al-3.5Ti alloy (656 MPa). Hence, this variation of yield strength may be related to the intrinsic strength of the  γ ′  precipitates,

As for 7Al-3.5Ti alloy at room temperature ( Fig. 8 (c)), apart from the concentration of the dislocations in the  γ  matrix, there exist some stacking faults observed along a near [110] axis which are oriented edge on and continuously cut through numerous  γ ′  phases. The dislocation structure for 6Al-3.5Ti alloy de- formed at 800 °C in  Fig. 8 (b) shows that the interaction between lots of dislocations and the  γ ′  phases makes the  γ ′  phases undistinguishable, but some microtwins which are oriented edge on (marked by the red arrows) can still be found by careful observation. The extra di ﬀ raction spots in the selected area electron di ﬀ raction patterns (SADP) due to the twinned regions are marked by the yellow arrows in the top right corner of  Fig. 8 (b), and the very thin edge-on twins result in the very slight streaking. It is noteworthy that a large number of micotwins appear in  Fig. 8 (d) for 7Al-3.5Ti alloy deformed at 800 °C. Some edge- on twins intersecting with each other (marked by the red solid line) are about 114° apart, which indicates the occurrence of two di ﬀ erent slip systems including di ﬀ erent 1/6 < 112 > {111} partial dislocations accompanied by complex stacking fault(CSF) formation, and the extra di ﬀ raction spots in the SADP due to the twinned regions are marked by the yellow arrows in the top right corner of  Fig. 8 (d).  

Previous studies by  ﬁ rst-principles calculations showed that the increase of Al content in the Co 3 (Al,W) system can obviously reduce its stacking fault energy [ 30 ], and the SISF energy of the Co 3 (Al,W) is higher than that of Ni 3 Al [ 31 ].  

Comparing the dislocation structure of 6Al-3.5Ti and 7Al-3.5Ti al- loys at room temperature, the increase of 1 at% Al may reduce the stacking fault energy of  γ ′  phases for 7Al-3.5Ti alloy and make them easier to be cut by partial dislocations to form the stacking faults. This also means that the increase of Al in  γ ′  phases may decrease the yield strength of  γ ′  phase (easier to be cut), which can eventually result in the decrease of room temperature yield stress of 7Al-3.5Ti alloy. Comparing the dislocation structure of 6Al-3.5Ti and 7Al-3.5Ti alloys at 800 °C, the increase of 1 at% Al may decrease the CSF energy of the  γ ′  phase at 800 °C, which will promote the formation of a large number of micro- twins in 7Al-3.5Ti alloy. Study in Ni-base superalloys shows that the formation of a large number of microtwins can seriously hinder the movement of dislocations, and improve the high-temperature me- chanical properties of the alloys [ 32 ]. In this study, the addition of 1 at % Al can signi ﬁ cantly increase the yield strength of 6Al-3.5Ti alloy at 800 °C, which may be attributed to the formation of a large number of microtwins in 7Al-3.5Ti alloy.  

In Ni-base superalloys, the formation mechanism of microtwins during high temperature tension and creep has been studied extensively [ 33 , 34 ]. Recently, Freund et al. [ 35 ] reported the formation mechanism of microtwins in the creep process of a novel Co-base superalloy at 750 °C with a load of 530 MPa, which refers that with the segregation of alloying elements at the twin boundary, microtwins grow by the slip of single 1/6 < 112 > {111} partial dislocations along the twin boundary. Up to now, there are no reports on the formation of microtwin in Co- base superalloys under the process of tensile or compression and the formation mechanism for the microtwins in the experimental alloys may be similar to the above-mentioned research. More detailed de- formation mechanisms for novel wrought Co-base superalloys under tensile process with small plastic deformation is currently under way within our research group.
