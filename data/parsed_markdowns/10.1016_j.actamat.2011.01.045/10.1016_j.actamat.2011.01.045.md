# Abstract  

Nickel-based superalloys are predominantly used as structural materials in high-temperature applications due to their exceptional high-temperature strength based on precipitation hardening by the coherent  c 0 -phase. In modern superalloys, the  c 0 -phase fraction can amount to 75%, at which  c 0 -precipitates align as cubes parallel to the  h 0 0 1 i  directions of the crystal lattice. At high temperatures and under a mechanical load, e.g. during service in gas turbines, the  c 0 -cubes coalesce to  c 0 -rafts, generating an inter penetrating micro- structure of  c  and  c 0 . By extracting one phase of this interpenetrated network, nanoporous superalloy membranes containing channel-like interconnected pores are produced. So far, this can only be achieved by applying simultaneously thermal and mechanical loads during tensile creep deformation. Here, a new production process is presented. Due to internal stresses, load-free aging of single-crystalline superalloys, e.g. CMSX-4, also generates an inter penetrating microstructure of  c  and  c 0 . This can be utilized to manufacture nanoporous superalloy membranes in the absence of an external mechanical load. The advantage of this process is its simplicity and the potential to fabricate larger membranes than possible by the costly tensile creep deformation process currently used.

# 1. Introduction  

Open-celled metals are required in many applications due to their unique functional properties  [1–5] . Based on the production processes, e.g. sintering of metal ﬁbers  [1] , powder processing using a foaming agent  [6] , coating of polymer foams  [7]  or melt inﬁltration techniques  [8] , the characteristic dimension of the porosity is usually in the range of 0.1–10 mm. However, ﬁner porosity would be highly beneﬁcial for many applications. Therefore, a pro- cessing route, capable of producing open-celled materials with nanoscale porosity, was recently developed using selective phase extraction of one phase from a two-phase alloy  [3,9,10] . This was demonstrated on nickel-based superalloys consisting of nickel solid-solution matrix ( c -phase) and precipitates of the coherent  c 0 -phase, which can amount to a phase fraction of up to 75%  [3,9–11] . The  c 0 -precipitates are cubic and align parallel to the h 0 0 1 i  directions of the crystal lattice. At high tempera- tures and under a mechanical load, e.g. during tensile creep deformation, the  c 0 -particles coarsen directionally, generat- ing an interpenetrating network of  c  and  c 0 . The  c 0 -coarsen- ing is consequently preferential in a plane perpendicular to the external load as long as the load is tensile and the superalloy has a negative misﬁt, as found in CMSX-4 [12] . To produce nanoporous membranes with channel-like interconnected pores, which are some hundred nanometers wide, either the  c  or the  c 0 -phase must then be extracted selectively, e.g. by an electrochemical process  [9] .  

Inter penetrating networks of  c  and  c 0   also occur during load-free aging of nickel-based superalloys  [13–16] . This process is not driven by an external load, but by internal stresses caused by diﬀerent thermal expansion coeﬃcients of  c  and  c 0   in conjunction with concentration gradients generated by segregation during solidiﬁcation  [17,18] .  

While refractory metals, such as tungsten and rhenium, are enriched in the dendrite cores, aluminum, titanium and tantalum segregate inter dendritic ally. Since the latter ele- ments are  c 0 -builders, the  c 0 -phase fraction is higher in interdendritic regions than in dendrites, causing internal stress ﬁelds  [19–25] . This so-called dendritic stress is the driving force for the observed directional coarsening  [18] . This work attempts to use the dendritic stress and the asso- ciated directional coarsening to generate an interpenetrat- ing network of  c  and  c 0   not only locally but globally throughout the specimen. If such a network can be formed, nanoporous membranes can be obtained simply by load- free aging and subsequent extraction of either the  c - or c 0 -phase. This would not only simplify the production pro- cess compared to the current procedure, using tensile creep deformation, but would also allow the fabrication of larger nanoporous membranes. To explore this possibility, the microstructure evolution of the nickel-based superalloy CMSX-4 upon load-free aging in the temperature range 1305–1405 K is analyzed in Section  3 . Tensile test samples are investigated in Section  4  after selective extraction of the c -phase to investigate the mechanical integrity of the remaining  c 0 -network. The results are discussed in Section 5 , where it will be demonstrated that remarkably strong nanoporous membranes can in fact be produced within cer- tain bounds of the annealing duration and temperature.  

# 2. Experimental procedure  

The objective of this investigation is to analyze the process of load-free coarsening for the production of nano- porous superalloy membranes. In this section, sample prep- aration and image analysis will be discussed in detail. The material used is a single-crystalline plate of the nickel-based superalloy CMSX-4  [12] , which was cast using the Bridg- man process. Its chemical composition is given in  Table 1 . As a result of the solidiﬁcation process, the microstruc- ture is dendritic. The orientation of the primary dendrites is along the [0 0 1] direction, whereas secondary dendrites are oriented along the [1 0 0] and [0 1 0] directions (see Fig. 1 ). To homogenize the material and generate a  c / c 0 - microstructure containing about 65% of cubic  c 0 -particles, the single-crystalline plate was homogenized and precipita- tion heat-treated as follows: 1550 K/2 h, 1561 K/3 h, 1569 K/3 h, 1577 K/2 h, 1586 K/2 h, 1589 K/2 h, 1591 K/ 2 h, 1594 K/2 h, 1353 K/4 h, 1143 K/16 h. Note, however, that complete homogenization is not possible due to the large amount of refractory elements, especially rhenium. Therefore, the dendritic microstructure is still apparent. The heat-treated CMSX-4 plate was spark eroded into 600  l m thick samples with the primary dendrites oriented parallel to the long edges of the samples (see  Fig. 1 ).  

To generate load-free coarsening, the samples were annealed at temperatures of 1305, 1355 and 1405 K. They were removed from the furnace at 100 h intervals. The maximum annealing duration was 1000 h. To obtain an activated surface for the subsequent extraction, the oxide layer had to be removed. Therefore, the samples were ground on both sides to a thickness of about 300  l m. Finally, the  c -phase was selectively extracted by electrolysis in a potentiostatic cell using a potential of 1.3 V and an aqueous electrolyte containing 1% citric acid and 1% ammonium sulfate solution  [26,27] .  

The microstructure evolution of the extracted samples was investigated by scanning electron microscopy image analysis. Due to the dendritic solidiﬁcation, the microstruc- ture contains four diﬀerent regions: primary dendrite roots (PDRs), primary dendrite nodes (PDNs), secondary den- drites (SDs) and interdendritic regions (IRs) (see  Fig. 2 ). A precondition for the analysis of the microstructure evolution is that all images show the same region. Here, primary dendrite roots are analyzed because the micro- structure consists mostly of PDRs. In addition, during load-free coarsening, the  c 0 -rafting in PDRs is along the (0 0 1) plane which is perpendicular to the long edge of the samples, and therefore perpendicular to the direction of load of the following tensile tests. Consequently, PDRs are critical for failure behavior and fracture strength as dis- cussed in more detail in Section  5 . All images were taken at a magniﬁcation of 10,000 and a resolution of 3072   2048 pixels, and edited and analyzed by a line-segmenting technique  [10] . The superimposing lines are rotated from 0   in 1   steps to an angle of 180  . The arithmetic average of the segment lengths in both phases is calculated for every angle. The average segment lengths are then plotted on a polar coor- dinate system, forming an ellipse for each phase. Due to symmetry, a half ellipse contains all information. # 3. Microstructure evolution  

The evolution of the microstructure is analyzed by examination of microstructure images and microstructure ellipses. In both cases, it is essential to compare similar regions. Due to the shape of the samples, all images were taken parallel to the (0 1 0) plane (see  Fig. 1 ). Before aging, the microstructure is still dendritic due to the solidiﬁcation process. The  c 0 -phase fraction is about 65%, though slightly higher in interdendritic regions than in dendrites. The c 0 -precipitates are cubes with an edge length of around 300 nm aligned parallel to the  h 0 0 1 i  directions of the crys- tal lattice.  

After load-free aging, the main coarsening direction of c 0   is diﬀerent in PDNs, PDRs, SDs and IRs (e.g.  Figs. 4 and 5 ). The ellipses of the ligaments are drawn on the left-hand side and the ellipses of the pores on the right- hand side. Due to the directional microstructure of super- alloy membranes, the ﬁgures displaying the average pore and ligament size are ellipses with a short axis  a  and a long axis  c . Therefore, the long axis of the ellipses is a measure of the average  c 0 -raft length, and the short axis gives the average width. The length/width ( c / a ) ratio indicates the anisotropy of the coarsening process. In IRs, the  c / a  ratio is small due to coarsening along several {0 0 1} planes, causing a low anisotropy. Furthermore, the microstructure is coarse, which is reﬂected in the size of the microstructure ellipses. IRs are also inhomogeneous, containing breakouts due to incomplete interconnection between  c 0 -ligaments, resulting in varying microstructure ellipses depending on the local microstructure. At 1355 K, the analysis was conducted on the basis of the samples displayed in  Fig. 7 . The microstructure ellipses of ligaments are larger than the ellipses of pores, resulting in a  c 0 -phase fraction of around 56%. As before, the c 0 -phase fraction does not change much over the annealing duration. At 1405 K, the samples, shown in  Fig. 8 , were analyzed. In general, the microstructure ellipses of ligaments and pores have the same size. The  c 0 -volume fraction drops to just over 50% due to the higher annealing temperature. Inspection of  Fig. 8 a reveal an important diﬀerence com- pared to the microstructure evolution at 1305 K (and to a lesser extend at 1355 K). At 1305 K, initial  c 0 -growth occurred almost exclusively along (0 0 1) planes, and only later on is cross-linking due to growth along other {0 0 1} planes signiﬁcant. In  Fig. 8 a, growth of the  c 0 -phase is still at an early state as indicated by still discernible indi- vidual  c 0 -particles and the often  “ wavy ”  shape of the c 0 -rafts. However, growth already occurs along all {0 0 1} planes. Furthermore, inspection of  Fig. 8 d shows a trend towards spheroidization of the  c 0 -ligaments towards the longest annealing duration. This becomes more apparent later on (compare with  Fig. 14 ) and indicates loss of coher- ency as a result of extensive  c 0 -growth.  

In brief, increasing temperatures not only accelerate coarsening of the microstructure, but also reduce the c 0 -phase fraction and lead to an increasing trend for growth along all {0 0 1} planes rather than only along (0 0 1).  

# 4. Mechanical behavior  

To describe the mechanical behavior, tensile tests were conducted on the nanoporous membranes produced. The direction of load was applied parallel to the [0 0 1] direc- tion, which is parallel to the primary dendrite arms (see Fig. 1 ). Macroscopically, all samples show linear elastic behavior until fracture without remaining plastic strain (see  Figs. 9–11 ). All measured values such as the ultimate tensile strength  R  m , fracture strain  A    at  r  ¼  R  m   and the elastic stiﬀness  E    are given in  Table 3 . However, micro- scopic analyses of the fracture surfaces show that ligaments close to the fracture path are bent and sheared oﬀ(see Fig. 13 ). Consequently, there is microplastic deformation of the intrinsically ductile  c 0 -phase which does not show macroscopically due to its conﬁnement to the fracture zone, as pointed out in Ref.  [10] .  

Stress–strain diagrams generated from the samples annealed at 1305 K are shown in  Fig. 9 . The curves can be divided into two groups. The ﬁrst group is characterized by high fracture strain, but low strength (below 5 MPa). These curves belong to samples which were annealed for 600 h or less. The second group exhibits signiﬁcantly higher strength values of around 20 MPa. These curves are gener- ated from samples annealed for 700 h or longer. The high- est fracture stress of 25 MPa is measured on the sample with the longest annealing duration of 1000 h.  

Stress–strain diagrams based on samples annealed at 1355 K are given in  Fig. 10 . The curves have similar slopes, and therefore the stiﬀness is similar. The ultimate tensile strength is around 60 MPa. The sample with the longest annealing duration of 1000 h also has the highest strength of 65 MPa. The trend is that longer annealing durations result in higher strength.  

Stress–strain diagrams of the samples annealed at 1405 K are displayed in  Fig. 11 . The majority of these curves have similar slopes. Signiﬁcant strength values are already reached after short annealing durations. An annealing duration of as little as 100 h generates a strength of about 20 MPa. The maximum strength of 32.5 MPa is reached after 200 h; longer annealings lead to decreasing strength values. After the longest annealing duration of 1000 h, the strength is decreased to less than 5 MPa.

# 5. Discussion  

The experiments demonstrated that it is possible to produce nanoporous membranes by load-free aging of nickel-based superalloys. Surprisingly, the membranes have properties similar properties to those of membranes manu- factured by tensile creep deformation  [10]  if optimal settings for annealing duration and temperature are selected. How- ever, the microstructure of membranes produced by load- free aging is not arranged as regularly due to the diﬀerent coarsening orientations in the diﬀerent (inter)dendritic regions (see  Fig. 4 ). In general, the orientation of  c 0 -rafting depends on the stress orientation and the lattice misﬁt between  c  and  c 0 . Due to the negative misﬁt of CMSX-4, c 0 -coarsening is oriented perpendicular to a tensile stress [17] . Upon load-free coarsening, no superimposing external load is applied to orientate the coarsening process of the  c 0 -

Refractory metals, such as tungsten and rhenium, enrich preferentially in the matrix phase and segregate dendritically causing an elevated c -phase fraction in dendrites. Elements such as aluminum, tantalum and titanium are  c 0 -builders and segregate inter- dendritically, causing an elevated  c 0 -phase fraction in interdendritic regions. The observed behavior becomes understandable in light of the tensile test results after heat treatment at 1305 K. At short annealing durations, the fracture surface of the nanoporous membranes is characterized by large voids ( Fig. 13 a). They are located in IRs and are caused by incom- plete coalescence of the  c 0 -particles to an interconnected network as discussed above. This leads to voids when the c  -phase is extracted during membrane fabrication and the strength of the membrane material is poor. The samples with annealing durations up to 600 h fall into this category. At longer annealing durations, the strength increases signif- icantly, apparently because a rigid  c 0 -network has now been established throughout the material volume. Large voids no longer occur and most of the fracture surfaces now contain c 0 -ligaments with preferred orientation along (0 0 1) (see Fig. 13 b). This means that PDRs are now preferential frac- ture sites, i.e. the weakest links in the microstructure. As dis- cussed above, this is to be expected because the  c 0 -ligaments are oriented perpendicular to the applied stress in these regions and cross-linking is relatively sparse. Since cross- linking is increasing with annealing duration (see  Fig. 6 ), it is also understandable why the highest strength of

 25.6 MPa is achieved after the longest annealing duration

 (see  Fig. 9 ). It is likely that even higher strength levels would result if the annealing duration were to be extended further. In summary, it is noted that the material strength is at ﬁrst limited by the IRs (due to slow  c 0 -network formation) and then by the PDRs (due to ligament orientation).  

If the temperature is increased from 1305 to 1355 K, the dendritic stresses decrease because (i) the strain misﬁt between dendrites and interdendritic regions diminishes; becoming zero at the  c 0 -solvus temperature  [18] ; (ii) the elastic constants decrease; and (iii) creep relaxation is accel- erated. Therefore,  c 0 -coarsening in the PDRs is less direc- tional along (0 0 1) and cross-links become more frequent (e.g.  Fig. 7 a). This improves the mechanical stability of the  c 0 -network in the [0 0 1] loading direction and explains why the ultimate tensile strength  R    of up to 62.3 MPa is m  

considerably higher at 1355 K as compared to 1305 K. Interdendritic voids do not occur at 1355 K for the investi- gated range of annealing durations. This can be attributed to faster diﬀusion, accelerating network formation in IRs accordingly. Consequently, membrane strength is no longer controlled by interdendritic regions but by the pri- mary dendrite roots.  

From the arguments discussed above, one might expect even higher values of  R    at 1405 K. However,  c 0 -dissolu- m tion is signiﬁcant at this temperature. This not only leads to a lower cross-sectional area of the load-bearing  c 0 -phase, but also makes interconnection of the initially isolated c 0 -particles more diﬃcult. As a result,  R    diminishes drasti- m cally from about 50–60 MPa at 1355 K to about 20– 30 MPa for annealing durations of up to 600 h. The fracture surface is, once again, controlled by PDRs, dem- onstrating that network formation in the interdendritic regions is fast at this temperature. The results show clear directions for an optimum annealing procedure. Firstly,  c 0 -network formation in the IRs and suﬃcient cross-linking of the  c 0 -ligaments in the PDRs has to be ensured. This can be achieved the more eﬃciently the higher the temperature is since (i) diﬀusion is accelerated and (ii) dendritic stresses are diminished, leading to more cross-linking. # 6. Conclusion  

It has been demonstrated that nanoporous superalloy membranes can be successfully produced by load-free aging. The basic principle is to generate an inter penetrating  c / c 0 - network by directional coarsening from which either the  c - or  c 0 -phase is subsequently extracted. In this process, the coarsening direction is controlled by internal stresses. These stresses are caused by chemical gradients and are often referred to as dendritic stresses. Investigating CMSX-4- based membranes with a  c 0 -backbone, the following key ﬁndings were made regarding the relation between anneal- ing parameters, microstructure and mechanical properties:  

(i) Annealing duration and temperature must be suﬃ- cient to ensure complete formation of an intercon- nected  c 0 -network throughout the material volume. This happens last in the interdendritic regions, requir- ing several hundred hours at 1305 K.

 (ii) The lower the temperature, the higher the dendritic stresses, causing strongly oriented rafts, but little cross-linking between them. The result is a low mate- rial strength. However, as the dendritic stresses relax with duration, cross-linking and material strength increase with increasing annealing duration.

 (iii) If the temperature is too high, the  c 0 -phase fraction decreases signiﬁcantly, causing weakening due to loss of load-bearing cross-section. Furthermore, high temperatures in combination with long annealing durations lead ultimately to Kirkendall porosity and loss of  c 0 -coherency, which severely aﬀect the strength of the membrane material.  

As a result of these dependencies, an optimum annealing temperature exists, which is high enough to allow for suﬃcient cross-linking but low enough to maintain a high c 0 -content. This optimum was reached here at around 1355 K, leading to a tensile strength of up to 60 MPa. The result demonstrates that load-free aging of single-crystalline superalloys followed by selective phase extraction is a pow- erful method to produce high-strength metallic membrane materials with nanoscale porosity.
