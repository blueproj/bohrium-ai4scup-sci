It is shown that cracking from oxygen-induced dynamic embrittlement in bicrystals of the nickel-base superalloy IN718 having symmetrical  ˙  = 5 (013) tilt boundaries is considerably more rapid in the fast- diffusion direction parallel to the tilt axis than in the direction perpendicular to this axis. This lends support to the model that envisions the process as caused by diffusive penetration of grain boundaries ahead of a sharp crack by a surface-adsorbed embrittling element.

The Cu–Sn alloy study involved the matrix element, tin, which is known to be surface active  [4]  and is an embrittling element in steels  [6] , for example. It has also been shown to segregate to grain boundaries in the Cu–Sn alloy during elevated-temperature aging  [7] . In contrast, the oxygen-induced cracking of nickel-base alloys involves an element from the vapor phase extraneous to the alloy that is not to be found  ab initio  in the grain boundaries. The present study was carried out to determine what kind of cracking anisotropy would be found in the commonly used alloy IN718 in experiments similar to the previous ones on the Cu–Sn alloy.  

# 1. Introduction  

Dynamic embrittlement is the diffusion-controlled intergran- ular cracking that can occur in a high-strength alloy that has a cohesion-lowering element adsorbed on a free surface. It was ﬁrst characterized in studies of oxygen-induced cracking of a Ni-Al inter- metallic  [1]  and of sulfur-induced cracking of a pressure-vessel steel  [2] . One characteristic that is generally noted in polycrystals is the disparity in the susceptibility to cracking among various grain boundaries. This was noted particularly in a study involving the nickel-base alloy IN718  [3] .  

This ﬁrst part of a two-part paper serves as the introduction to both and as the basis for understanding the anisotropy of the cracking process in polycrystalline material, which is the subject of Part II. The same specimen preparation for the bicrystals is used in Part II, and thus Part I also serves to explain that.  

This behavior was also noted in the highly susceptible Cu–8%Sn alloy  [4] . A later study of cracking of bicrystals of this alloy having symmetrical  ˙  = 5 (013) tilt boundaries showed that it occurred much more rapidly along the tilt axis (i.e., the fast-diffusion direc- tion) than perpendicular to the tilt axis  [5] . This supported the proposition that the phenomenon involves diffusive penetration by the surface element into the core of a sharp crack and that the atomic conﬁguration along the grain boundary would play a decisive role.  

The  ˙  = 5 (013) tilt boundary was again selected for this pur- pose because of the great disparity in self-diffusivity in the parallel and perpendicular directions found previously in this boundary in another FCC material, silver. The calculated  [8]  atomic struc- ture of this boundary in an FCC lattice is shown in  Fig. 1 (a), and the diffusion results of Mishin and Herzig  [9]  are shown in Fig. 1 (b). The presence of “diffusion pipes” along the tilt axis is readily apparent, and the diffusivity along these “pipes” is about double that in the perpendicular direction for self-diffusion in silver.

# 2. Experimental procedure  

Single-crystal bars of IN718 one inch in diameter were obtained from Howmet Castings Co. The longitudinal growth direction was found by orientation-imaging microscopy to be within two degrees of [001]. The chemical composition is given in  Table 1 .  

The material was solution treated in air at 1241 K and water quenched, aged for 8 h at 991 K, furnace cooled to 894 K and aged another 8 h, then air cooled. Sections of the single-crystal bar were held in a goniometer, oriented, and cut such that the cut faces con- stituted the opposing planes of a   5(031)[100] symmetrical-tilt boundary. The bicrystal halves were mounted in a phenolic resin and polished metal lo graphically to a 0.1   m diamond ﬁnish. The specimens were then removed from the resin and attached with carbon tape to steel disks.  

Because a polished surface can oxidize upon being exposed to air, and this can interfere with the bonding process, ﬁnal polishing and preparation for diffusion bonding was conducted in an inert environment using a glove box with ﬂowing argon and an argon- hydrogen getter/drier apparatus. Polishing was conducted for 1 h with a vibratory polisher with a 0.05   m alumina suspension. The bicrystal halves were cleaned with acetone and ethyl alcohol.  

The clean bicrystal halves were then brought together and wrapped in tantalum foil and placed in a steel jig that was also lined with tantalum. The lining prevented any bonding to the steel sur- faces and acted as an oxygen getter during diffusion bonding. This adequately sealed the interior of the jig from the ambient atmo- sphere during the brief period in which the loaded jig was moved from the glove box to the hot press.  

The loaded jig was placed in a hot press for diffusion bond- ing. A bonding load of 100 pounds was applied, and the furnace

The completed bicrystals were cut to 10 mm  ×  10 mm  ×  10 mm specimens. Extensions were made from 30 mm-long pieces of poly- crystalline IN718 and were brazed onto each side of the bicrystals using AMS 4777 foil brazing alloy. The brazing was done in the hot press that was evacuated to a pressure below 1.3  ×  10 − 3   Pa at 1323 K for 10 min.  

Side grooves were machined in the four-point-bend specimens along the boundary plane using EDM. The specimens were oriented such that the crack direction was either parallel or perpendicular to the tilt axis.  

The test frame used for the tests in lab air utilized infra-red lamps as heaters, as described in previous work  [3] , and the loading arrangement and single-edge-notch, four-point-bend specimens were also the same as in that work. The grain boundary was notched by EDM, and then the notch was ﬁnished with a 0.1 mm diameter molybdenum wire coated with 0.3   m diamond paste drawn back and forth for a cut of 200   m, to give a damage-free tip. The width of the specimen inside the side grooves along the boundary was 8 mm, and the length ahead of the notch was 7 mm.  

Specimens were placed in the load frame and given a 100 lb (445 N) pre-load, and the specimen was heated to 923 K. Initial loads ranging from 350 to 1100 lbs (1557–4893 N) were applied, after which the displacement was ﬁxed. The load dropped as a crack initiated and propagated through the specimen until it reached zero.

The intergranular fracture surface of the specimen cracked in the perpendicular direction exhibited a substantial number of slip bands, whereas the parallel-cracked specimen exhibited far fewer slip bands, and in some areas none at all. This is not surprising given the disparity in the loads and times involved in the cracking process.  

The present results are essentially identical to those found pre- viously for bicrystals of a Cu–Sn alloy  [5] , in which the embrittling element was the surface-active indigenous element Sn. Here, the embrittling element oxygen came from the vapor phase and was therefore exogenous to the alloy. A previous study  [3]  has shown that polycrystalline specimens of this material do not suffer inter- granular brittle cracking in the absence of an oxygen atmosphere, so there is no apparent role of indigenous impurities in the present phenomenon. The role of grain-boundary structure is addressed futher in Part II of this paper.  

# 4. Summary  

For the case of oxygen-induced intergranular cracking in symmetrical-tilt bicrystals of IN718 at 923 K, it is shown here that cracking along the tilt axis requires a relatively low stress and short time, compared with cracking in the perpendicular direction, which requires a much higher stress and far longer time. This result is consistent with the previously proposed model  [10]  that dynamic embrittlement involves diffusive penetration of grain boundaries by the embrittling element, as opposed to mere wetting of the surface at a crack tip.
