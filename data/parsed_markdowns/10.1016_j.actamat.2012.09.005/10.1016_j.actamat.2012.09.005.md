# Abstract  

Deformation mechanisms under tensile loading at room temperature have been studied in a polycrystalline nickel-based superalloy containing close to 50 vol.%  c 0 . In order to identify the eﬀect of  c 0   particle size on deformation mechanisms, model microstructures with unimodal  c 0   size distributions were developed. The investigations were carried out by combining in situ loading experiments using neu- tron diﬀraction and two-site elasto-plastic self-consistent plasticity modelling with detailed post-mortem electron microscopy. The microscopy work also includes results for samples strained at 500   C. During early plastic deformation, the diﬀraction data demonstrate that  c  and  c 0   display the same elastic strain response, indicating that at this stage  c 0   is cut by dislocations regardless of the  c 0   particle size. Scanning electron microscopy studies showed an abundance of shearing processes in all three microstructures, hence supporting the con- clusions drawn from the diﬀraction experiment. As the material is further deformed, elastic load transfer from  c  to  c 0   was observed in the medium (130 nm) and coarse (230 nm)  c 0   microstructures but not in the ﬁne (90 nm)  c 0   microstructure. The load transfer can be explained by assuming that Orowan looping becomes an additional operative deformation mode. Transmission electron microscopy conﬁrmed that in the ﬁne  c 0   microstructure deformation takes place by strongly coupled dislocations cutting the  c 0 , while the medium and coarse  c 0 microstructures showed additional signs of Orowan looping.

# 1. Introduction  

Nickel-based superalloys are an important class of mate- rial extensively used in gas turbine engines, power plants and the petro-chemical industry due to their excellent high temperature strength and corrosion resistance  [1,2] . The development of nickel-based superalloys ﬁrst began in the 1930s driven by the need for materials that could withstand the temperatures in aero-engines. A key limiting factor for aero-engine eﬃciency is the turbine entry temperature, which is limited by the temperature capability of the materials used in this part of the aero-engine  [3] . Consequently, there has been an eﬀort to develop disc and blade nickel-based super- alloys that can operate at higher engine temperatures. The high temperature strength in nickel-based superalloys origi- nates from the presence of the ordered  c 0   precipitate structure (Ni 3 Al, L1 2  structure). This has led to the development of nickel-based superalloys with high volume fractions of  c 0 . The most advanced polycrystalline superalloys now display c 0   volume fractions of about 50%  [4] .  

Nickel-based superalloys consist mainly of a  c  matrix (face-centred cubic, fcc), which contains  c 0   precipitates. The two phases are very similar in crystal structure as well as lattice parameter. The major diﬀerence between them is that  c 0   is an ordered phase (L1 2 ), while  c  is a solid- solution-strengthened cubic (fcc) phase. This means that intragranular  c 0   precipitates are often coherent with  c  hav- ing a cube–cube orientation relationship, although  c  and  c 0 have slightly diﬀerent lattice parameters. Resulting from this is a misﬁt between the phases. Typically, polycrystal- line nickel-based superalloys for disc applications have a bi- or trimodal  c 0   size distribution, depending on whether the material has undergone a sub- or supersolvus heat treatment  [5] . The intergranular primary  c 0   is typically around 1–3  l m and pins the grains during subsolvus heat treatments, but is dissolved in supersolvus heat treatments. Intragranular precipitates with a diameter of about 50– 250 nm are called secondary  c 0   and, ﬁnally, the tertiary  c 0 precipitates reside between secondary  c 0   with a particle size of around 5–50 nm  [6] .  

There has been a signiﬁcant amount of research on the interaction of dislocations with precipitates and on estimat- ing the threshold stresses for dislocations to overcome the obstacle presented by the particle  [7–10] . It is generally accepted that dislocations either shear (or cut) coherent precipitates or bow around them, leaving a dislocation loop behind (the Orowan mechanism). At elevated temper- atures, dislocations can also climb or cross-slip onto other glide planes in order to avoid  c 0   particles. With respect to the cutting mechanism, it is important to note that a dislo- cation in the  c  matrix will create an anti-phase boundary in the  c 0   precipitate, as the ordered structure is destroyed in its wake. The perfect structure of the precipitate is only re- established once a second dislocation follows on the same glide plane and annihilates the anti-phase boundary. As a result, dislocations are found to travel in pairs in the  c – c 0 microstructure, coupled by the energy of the anti-phase boundary  [10] . The dislocations can either be weakly or strongly coupled, depending on factors such as the  c 0   pre- cipitate size, morphology and volume fraction, as well as the anti-phase boundary (APB) energy. For weakly cou- pled dislocations, the cutting stress increases with increas- ing particle size, whereas it decreases in the case of strongly coupled dislocations  [10] . The maximum cutting stress and hence the optimum strengthening contribution is found for the  c 0   size at which the deformation mechanism changes from cutting by weakly coupled dislocations to strongly coupled ones  [10] . The particle size associated with the optimum strengthening contribution is representative of the peak-aged stage. For even larger  c 0   precipitates, the deformation mechanism may change from cutting to the Orowan process, where the dislocations bow around the precipitates leaving a dislocation loop behind. Jackson and Reed calculated the peak-aged particle size for U720Li, an alloy similar to RR1000. From their work it can be estimated that, in the case of a fully intragranular c 0   strengthened superalloy with about 45 vol.%  c 0 , the critical particle size would be around 45 nm. They also sug- gested that Orowan looping is an unlikely deformation mode in these alloys at the onset of plastic deformation, especially at low deformation temperatures  [11] .  

The majority of the deformation studies to date have relied on transmission electron microscopy (TEM)  

observations. Although this is a powerful technique, it only enables one to take a snapshot of the behaviour without a time resolved description of changes in deformation mech- anisms as a function of plasticity. Furthermore, TEM investigations focus on very small material volumes and, since the grains in polycrystalline materials behave very anisotropically, the exact deformation history is unknown for the grains studied. With the development of more materials engineering orientated beamlines at neutron and synchrotron facilities, there has been a wave of in situ studies aimed at addressing the issues described above  [12–15] . Such studies generally involve measuring the intergranular strains in the material that develop during mechanical loading at diﬀerent temperatures and using the acquired data to constrain ﬁtting parameters in plasticity models. In dual-phase alloys, and provided that the two phases are distinguishable by their diﬀraction patterns, the interphase strain data can indicate a change in defor- mation mechanism even without the beneﬁt of plasticity models. This makes this technique especially powerful to study the deformation behaviour of nickel superalloys, in situ and at elevated temperatures.  

Polycrystalline, crystal plasticity deformation models allow the modelling of a larger number of grains by consid- ering their elastic and plastic anisotropy, which is inﬂuenced by their crystal structure, their crystal orientation and the interactions between grains and the rest of the polycrystal. The modelling approaches most commonly used to inter- pret in situ straining diﬀraction studies are the elasto-plastic self-consistent (EPSC) model  [16,17]  and the crystal plastic- ity ﬁnite element model  [18] . In this context, the aim of these models is to determine the yield and hardening criteria of an eﬀective single crystal that, when combined in a statistically representative number to form a representative polycrystal, best ﬁts both the ﬂow curve of the material and the inter- phase and intergranular diﬀraction elastic strain responses measured during deformation.  

The EPSC model is based on the Eshelby–Hill formula- tion. A large number of grains are modelled, represented as elliptical inclusions inside a homogeneous medium. Each grain is attributed with appropriate anisotropic elastic and plastic properties. The stresses and strains are assumed to be homogeneous within the grain. The properties of the homogeneous medium are given by the average properties of all the grains. Consequently, its deformation behaviour is solved by iteration. This model is a simple description that considers interactions between grains only indirectly via the eﬀect of the grains on the surrounding medium, but beneﬁts from very short run times, in the range of sec- onds. A recent development of this approach is the use of so-called two-site self-consistent models, where grain pairs are considered within the medium at each calculation step, and the interactions between the two grains and between the grains with the homogeneous medium are solved.  

Daymond et al.  [19]  applied the methodology of comb- ing loading and diﬀraction experiments with plasticity modelling to study the deformation behaviour of the

It was suggested that, as the test temperature exceeds 650   C, the slip plane in  c 0   changes from {111} to {100}. However, since the work was carried out on material with a complex trimodal  c 0   size distribution, it was impossible to draw conclusions on the eﬀect of the  c 0   particle size on the active deformation mechanisms. The present work investigates the eﬀect of  c 0   size on the deformation behaviour in a high  c 0   volume fraction nickel- based superalloy. Three model microstructures, each with a unimodal distribution of diﬀerent  c 0   sizes, were generated. Post-mor- tem analysis of the deformed samples using electron microscopy is presented in order to provide validation of the way the neutron diﬀraction data are interpreted in the deformation model. The results from the post-mortem analysis are discussed for material deformed at room tem- perature as well as at 500   C. The high temperature TEM observations are included because neutron diﬀraction experiments indicated that basically the same deformation mechanisms operate at both test temperatures. Since sam- ples tested at 500   C were strained by a lower degree of plastic strain than the room temperature samples, the high temperature samples allowed a more in depth analysis of the dislocation structures. The neutron diﬀraction results recorded at 500   C are discussed in Ref.  [20] , which focuses on the dynamic strain ageing observed at this temperature.  

# 2. Methodology  

# 2.1. Model microstructures  

The material used in the present study is RR1000, which is a nickel-based superalloy for disc application developed by Rolls Royce plc  [21] . The composition of RR1000 can be found in  Table 1 . The alloy was produced via the pow- der metallurgical route, followed by forging. Typically, this alloy has a trimodal  c 0   size distribution in its ﬁnal condi- tion. In order to simplify the microstructure and enable a study that identiﬁes the eﬀect of the  c 0   particle size on the deformation mechanisms, a two-stage heat treatment was 3   devised. At ﬁrst, blanks measuring 13    13    90 mm were subjected to a 1 h supersolvus heat treatment at 1180   C followed by oil-quenching to minimize precipitation and growth on cooling, shown schematically in  Fig. 1 . After this heat treatment, the  c 0   distribution was bimodal with precipitate sizes of around 60 and 10 nm. Subsequently, the material was aged/partially solution heat treated for 1 h at 800, 925 or 1050   C in order to grow the small intra- granular precipitates to three diﬀerent sizes, then cooled   –1     slowly at a rate of 1 C min from 1050 to 925 C and then   –1     at 0.1 C min from 925 to 500 C. The slow cooling rates were chosen to prevent nucleation of new  c 0   precipitates in- between the existing particles and to minimize chemical dif- ferences between the three model microstructures  [22] . The three resulting model microstructures had mean  c 0   sizes of 90, 130 and 230 nm, respectively. In the following, the microstructures are called ﬁne, medium and coarse  c 0 , according to their respective particle sizes. As can be seen in  Fig. 1 c, the ﬁne microstructure resulting from the 800   C ageing heat treatment still contained both tertiary and secondary  c 0   precipitates and was hence not strictly unimodal. This is probably a consequence of the low age- ing temperature, which was not suﬃcient to dissolve the tertiary  c 0   precipitates that had formed upon cooling. Microscopic observations conﬁrmed that all three model microstructures had eﬀectively the same grain size (30  l m) and  c 0   volume fraction (45%). It is important to note that  c  grain coarsening during supersolvus heat treat- ments is probably restricted in RR1000 due to a ﬁne distri- bution of carbides which pin the grain boundaries. The heat-treated and aged blanks were subsequently machined into standard tensile samples with a 6 mm diameter, a 60 mm gauge length and M12 threads at either end.  

# 2.2. In situ tensile loading using neutron diﬀraction  

The tensile test samples produced in this way were loaded in situ on the ENGIN-X beamline located at the ISIS pulsed neutron source, Rutherford Appleton Labora- tory, Chilton, UK  [23] . There are two main reasons for using neutron rather than X-ray diﬀraction when studying c 0 -strengthened nickel superalloys. First, the superlattice reﬂections by which the  c  and  c 0   phases can be distin- guished are signiﬁcantly more intense when using neutrons compared to X-rays. This is a consequence of the fact that X-rays are scattered by the electron cloud of an atom and neutrons by the atomic nucleus. This means that the scat- tering length for X-rays increases with atomic number, whereas the scattering lengths of neutrons can vary greatly for elements close to each other in the periodic table. It can even lead to negative scattering lengths, such as in the case of titanium. The superlattice reﬂections are extinct for the  c matrix due to the random distribution of the alloying ele- ments in a solid solution. In the  c 0   phase, neighbouring lat- tice planes diﬀer in composition, resulting in diﬀerent

scattering properties for adjacent planes. This diﬀerence in scattering properties creates the intensity of the superlattice reﬂections. Titanium is a  c 0   stabilizer, and its negative scat- tering length means that the diﬀerence in the scattering properties of neighbouring planes is large for the particles. This results in the comparatively strong superlattice reﬂec- tions. Secondly, a larger gauge volume can be used for neu- trons compared to synchrotron X-rays. In the present case, a diﬀracting gauge volume of 4 mm    4 mm    8 mm was used, which allowed the application of a standard tensile sample geometry. The samples were loaded mechanically using a 100 kN Instron rig that employs universal joints ensuring an uniaxial stress state. Since statistically sound measurements of intergranular strain require a suﬃcient number of grains contributing to the individual reﬂections, a large diﬀracting gauge volume is highly desirable. In the case of the present neutron diﬀraction experiment it was estimated that approximately 6000–10,000 grains were within the diﬀracting gauge volume.  

ENGIN-X uses two detectors at ±90   to the incident beam. The beneﬁt of having a double detector system lies in the ability to derive the intergranular strain data in both the loading and transverse directions simultaneously dur- ing an in situ loading experiment  [12] .  

The main drawback of using neutrons is the long count- ing time needed to obtain suﬃcient single peak strain resolution. For the samples stated, counting times of 25 min were necessary to allow the acquisition of diﬀrac- tion patterns with a satisfactory signal to noise ratio for the superlattice reﬂections. The samples were loaded in a stepwise manner under load control to ensure that there was no signiﬁcant change in stress and plastic strain during the acquisition of the diﬀraction spectra. The frequency of measurement points was increased around the yield point, as the onset of plasticity is a key area of interest in under- standing deformation mechanisms.  

For the mechanical loading experiments undertaken at 500   C, heating was carried out using an optical furnace, which is described in Ref.  [24] . The temperature was mon- itored by a K-type thermocouple spot-welded to the sample.  

# 2.3. Fitting of diﬀraction spectra  

In the case of  c / c 0   nickel-based superalloys, the funda- mental peaks have a contribution from both phases. The small  c / c 0   misﬁt of the order of 0.0002 in RR1000 means that the individual response of  c  and  c 0   cannot be derived from the fundamental peaks alone. In contrast, the super- lattice reﬂections originate solely from the  c 0   phase. There- fore, a critical aspect for determining the intergranular strains of  c  and  c 0   during mechanical loading is the accu- rate measurement of the superlattice reﬂections. The infor- mation obtained from the (100) and (110) superlattice reﬂections of the  c 0   phase can then be used to deconvolute the  c  and  c 0   responses in the corresponding (200) and (220) fundamental reﬂections  [25] . The ﬁtting was under- taken using the commercial software package TOPAS. In order to deconvolute the (200)  c / c 0   and (220)  c / c 0   reﬂec- tions, the position and width of the fundamental  c 0   reﬂec- tions were calculated from the corresponding superlattice peaks. These were subsequently used to constrain the con- tribution of  c 0   to the fundamental peaks whilst the contri- bution of the  c  phase to the fundamental reﬂections was free to be ﬁtted.  

# 2.4. Modelling  

An EPSC model  [14]  was employed to model the defor- mation behaviour and the evolution of diﬀraction elastic strains during tensile loading. Since the aim was to model a  c 0   strengthened superalloy, the extended two-site EPSC model was used, which enables one to model the deforma- tion behaviour of a two-phase material  [19]  and enables the cube–cube orientation relationship between  c  and  c 0   to be considered. The EPSC code identiﬁes the possible slip sys- tems for each grain orientation and allows shear on those systems for which slip is easiest, based on the critical resolved shear stress (CRSS) and the amount of hardening. An extended Voce hardening law, given by Eq.  (1)  is used to calculate the hardening on each group of slip systems and for each grain. Here,  s  is the resolved shear stress,  s 0 in the initial resolved shear stress  s 1  +  s 0  is where the asymptote of the ﬁnal hardening rate intersects the resolved shear stress axis,  h 0  is the initial hardening rate and  h 1  is the ﬁnal hardening rate.  

In recent years, this modelling approach has been used extensively on a range of quasi-single phase engineering alloys to identify possible slip modes  [12,26,27] .  

Elastic lattice strain data recorded by neutron diﬀrac- tion provide additional information that can be used to constrain the choice of parameters used in the plasticity model. In other words, the input parameters from Eq. (1)  are chosen not only to best ﬁt a ﬂow curve, but also to enable accurate predictions of the elastic lattice strain evolution measured in the longitudinal and transverse direction by neutron diﬀraction  [28] . The ﬁtting parame- ters used in this case are the stiﬀness values for the elastic region, the slip systems and the coeﬃcients of Voce’s hard- ening law for the plastic behaviour. It is important, partic- ularly with a dual-phase material, that a large enough number of grains are modelled to gain satisfying statistical results for the lattice strain of individual grain families. Here it was suﬃcient to consider 1000 grains in the load- ing direction, while for the (100) reﬂection in the trans- verse direction approximately 5000 grains and for the (110) reﬂection 50,000 grains were required; the physical origin of the poorer quality statistical data observed in reﬂections transverse to the applied load is discussed in Ref.  [15] .  

# 2.5. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM)  

Post-mortem analysis of the tested specimens was car- ried out using advanced SEM techniques. Samples were cut from the neutron diﬀraction gauge volume of the tensile specimens parallel to the loading axis. Subsequently, they were ground and polished using standard polishing proce- dures. In the ﬁnal step, the surface was polished with colloi- dal silica solution for 3–4 h to obtain the required surface ﬁnish. For high-resolution imaging of the precipitates, the c 0   phase was removed by using the two-part etchant tech- nique described in Ref.  [6] . This enabled the  c 0   precipitate size and morphology to be determined in the as-heat-trea- ted samples and particle shearing to be detected in the deformed microstructures. An FEI Sirion ﬁeld-emission gun scanning electron microscope was used for all the SEM studies in the present work.  

Electron channelling contrast imaging (ECCI) is an SEM-based technique, which has been established to study deformation patterns  [29,30] . In the present work, large 2 areas (800    600  l m ) were imaged using this technique after polishing and prior to etching in order to detect shear lines. No tilting of the samples was carried out during ECCI.  

# 2.6. Transmission electron microscopy  

The dislocation structures of the deformed specimens were investigated using TEM. Foils were cut from the tested specimens parallel to the loading direction and thinned by grinding, before 3 mm discs were punched out of the material. The discs were ground subsequently to a thickness of 100–150  l m and electropolished using 8% per- chloric acid in acetic acid at 10   C and 40 V in a twin-jet electro polishing machine. A PHILIPS CM200 transmis- sion electron microscope with a double-tilt sample holder was used throughout the work. The samples deformed at room temperature failed at a signiﬁcantly larger strain than the ones deformed at 500   C. The high levels of plastic strain in the room temperature samples make conclusive observations involving TEM very diﬃcult. As a conse- quence, TEM investigations focused on the material tested at 500   C. The limited studies on room temperature sam- ples indicated no signiﬁcant diﬀerences to the samples tested at 500   C.

# 3.3. Intergranular strain evolution  

Figs. 6–8  show the intergranular strain evolution at room temperature as a function of applied stress for the coarse, medium and ﬁne  c 0   microstructures, respectively. They display the data recorded during the neutron diﬀrac- tion experiment (markers linked by grey dotted line) com- pared with those from a best ﬁt of an EPSC modelling simulation (solid line) for each microstructure. The data to the right of the vertical axis correspond to elastic strains along the loading direction while the data to the left corre- spond to elastic strains along the transverse direction to the loading axis. It should be noted that the best ﬁt here is meant in regard to both the intergranular strains and the ﬂow curve. Therefore, by ﬁtting the diﬀerent curves simultaneously, the choice of parameters used in the EPSC model is dramatically reduced. For the coarse microstruc- ture, elastic deformation occurs at applied stresses below about 800 MPa. Initially, there is a linear separation of the (200) and (220) responses as a result of the elastic anisotropy of the grains. Above about 800–900 MPa, these two grain families separate more quickly because the (220) grain family starts to deform plastically (it then takes on less elastic strain) while the (200) grain family does not (takes on more elastic strain). Although the maximum Schmid factors of the (200) and (220) grains are the same, the elastic anisotropy eﬀectively makes the (200) grains yield later. This is caused by the low compliancy of the (200) grain family, which allows for a larger elastic strain to be accumulated before yield than in the less compliant (220) grain family. Since the grains are constrained geometrically in a polycrystalline material, the stresses in the grains belonging to the (200) family will be lower than those in the (220) grain family at a given applied load, dur- ing elastic deformation. Therefore, the onset of plastic deformation leads to a non-linearity in the stress– diﬀraction elastic strain curve.  

For the coarse microstructure, the yield stress derived from the stress–strain curve is about 910 MPa.  Fig. 6  shows that at the onset of deformation the two phases ﬁrst deform together, as both take up the same amount of elastic strain. Load partitioning between  c  and  c 0   is ﬁrst visible in the lat- tice strain data at 1040 MPa. However, a separation in the peak broadening behaviour is not observed until 1160 MPa, which occurs shortly after the minimum in the hardening rate ( Fig. 4 ). At the point of failure, there is sig- niﬁcant load partitioning between the two phases. Note that it is signiﬁcantly easier to detect the onset of plastic deformation from the elastic lattice strain data than the ﬂow curve.  

The behaviour of the medium  c 0   microstructure ( Fig. 7 ) is very similar to the one of the coarse  c 0   microstructure, but the estimated yield stress is higher at 1020 MPa and the start of  c  to  c 0   load partitioning is only detectable from about 1170 MPa. The overall pattern of the intergranular strain evolution is very similar to the coarse  c 0   microstruc- ture, with the same load partitioning between  c  and  c 0   in the (200) grain family. However, in the (220) grain family the diﬀerence in diﬀraction elastic strains between the two phases is about half of that observed for the coarse  c 0 microstructure.  

In the ﬁne  c 0   microstructure, the yield stress was esti- mated to be 1150 MPa. Here,  c  and  c 0   display very similar intergranular strain development, with only a small amount of load partitioning between the two phases when the stresses exceed 1320 MPa ( Fig. 8 ). As mentioned ear- lier, this is coupled with little work hardening and only a slight separation between the (220)  c  and (220)  c 0   peak widths with increasing plasticity.

# 3.5. Dislocation structure  

TEM micrographs of the model microstructures deformed at 500   C are presented in  Fig. 11 . All TEM stud- ies conﬁrmed the presence of dislocations on the {111} plane in both phases. No other slip mode was detected. The ﬁne  c 0   microstructure ( Fig. 11 a) features closely spaced dislocation pairs, which suggests that the dislocations are strongly coupled. In the medium c 0 microstructure ( Fig. 11 b) dislocations are seen to bow strongly around the  c 0   precipitates, which is not found in the ﬁne  c 0   micro- structure. Only ﬁne slip lines are present in the coarse  c 0 microstructure ( Fig. 11 d), which are often interrupted or deviated near the precipitates. As for the medium  c 0   micro- structure, dislocations bow strongly in the vicinity of the precipitates in the coarse  c 0   microstructure ( Fig. 11 c). # 4. Discussion  

The most noticeable aspect when comparing the three ﬂow curves recorded at room temperature is the signiﬁcant diﬀerence in hardening behaviour. While the ﬁne  c 0   micro- structure shows very little strain hardening, the rate of hardening goes up with increasing  c 0   particle size. This alludes to the fundamental diﬀerences in the deformation behaviour of the three microstructures when the material exceeds 1–2% plastic strain. It is also noticeable that the minimum hardening rate is observed at increasing levels of plastic strain from coarse to ﬁne  c 0 . Strain hardening is often a result of dislocation interaction and therefore depends on the activation of multiple slip within the grains. Multiple slip is unavoidable in polycrystalline deformation and could explain the observed increase. An increase in work hardening can also occur as a result of slip-induced grain rotation, but this eﬀect is only signiﬁcant after large plastic deformations. Another possible strain hardening mechanism is twinning, where twin formation either leads to grain reﬁnement or to a twin volume rotating from a

“ soft ”  to a  “ hard ”  orientation  [31] . The changes in peak broadening shown in  Fig. 5  suggest that the increased level of work hardening in the medium and particularly coarse  c 0 microstructures is mainly related to the  c  phase; while the peak width evolution of the  c 0   phase is very similar for all three microstructures, peak broadening of the  c  phase is more signiﬁcant in the medium and coarse c 0 microstructure.  

The next important observation of the in situ loading experiments is the load transfer from  c  to  c 0 , which occurs after a certain level of plastic deformation, depending on the microstructure of the material ( Figs. 6–8 ). This load transfer is most signiﬁcant in the coarse  c 0   microstructure followed by the medium  c 0   microstructure, while a load transfer between the two phases is hardly noticeable in the ﬁne  c 0   microstructure. In the coarse  c 0   microstructure, the load transfer starts at the lowest levels of stress (1040 MPa) and plastic strain (1.2%) out of the three microstructures. At about 3.5% plastic strain, signiﬁcant c  peak broadening becomes visible. This suggests that the onset of  c  to  c 0   load transfer may be linked with accelerated c  peak broadening. The absence of load partitioning between  c  and  c 0   in the ﬁne  c 0   microstructure indicates that the  c  phase can only deform plastically if the same level of plastic strain is achieved in the  c 0   phase. This means that the two phases must undergo plastic deformation jointly. A joint plastic deformation of  c  and  c 0   would be expected if the precipitates were sheared by strongly coupled disloca- tions. This is conﬁrmed by the post-mortem electron microscopy analysis, where shearing of the precipitates is detected as the main deformation mechanism in the ﬁne c 0   at both 20 and 500   C when imaging the etched samples by FEG–SEM ( Fig. 9 a and b). TEM imaging of the ﬁne  c 0 microstructure after loading at 500   C revealed that the precipitates are cut by strongly coupled dislocations, with no evidence of any additional deformation mechanisms involving single dislocations like Orowan looping ( Fig. 11 a).  

Evidence of precipitate shearing is also abundant in the medium and coarse  c 0   microstructures after testing at 20 and 500   C, proving that this deformation mechanism is still active in these microstructures, at least during the early stages of deformation. This correlates well with the obser- vation that  c  and  c 0   show the same elastic lattice strain response at the onset of plastic deformation in the neutron diﬀraction results.  

However, there are diﬀerences in the FEG–SEM images of the three model microstructures. In particular, the degree of strain localization appears to increase with decreasing particle size. This can be seen in both the images of etched  c 0   precipitates as well as in the ECC images ( Figs. 9 and 10 ). This is already an indication that deformation of the  c 0   precipitates becomes more diﬃcult as the particle size increases, which is also evident in the increased load transfer observed in the neutron diﬀraction results. When it is more diﬃcult for dislocations to penetrate the  c 0   precip- itates, they bow around the precipitates (eventually leading to Orowan looping, for example). Shearing of precipitates is generally believed to cause highly localized deformation patterns as the cutting of the ﬁrst dislocation pair through the particles reduces its eﬀective diameter  [32] , supposedly making it easier for a second dislocation pair to follow on the same plane than on parallel glide planes. Continu- ous  c 0   shearing on localized slip traces is therefore expected to provide only a limited degree of strain hardening during deformation, as observed for the ﬁne  c 0   and in the early stages of deformation for the medium and coarse  c 0 . On the other hand, if the precipitates are circumvented by the Orowan mechanism, a dislocation loop is left behind around the precipitates, which makes it more diﬃcult for a second dislocation to follow on the same glide plane. This causes slip activity to be less localized, resulting in defor- mation patterns being more diﬀuse  [32] . In that case, the c  matrix still deforms plastically while the  c 0   phase only deforms elastically. As a consequence, a load transfer from the  c  to the  c 0   phase would be expected. This deformation mode would not result in any signiﬁcant peak broadening of the  c 0   reﬂections, while the build-up of dislocation frag- ments (loops) or cross-slip of dislocations in the  c  phase to overcome the obstacles could result in increased dislocation interaction in the matrix and therefore accelerated peak broadening. A similar eﬀect might also be expected if dislo- cations only become sessile temporarily as they bow and pile up in front of a  c 0   particle before they can eventually overcome the precipitate by shearing.  

The TEM images of the medium and coarse  c 0   micro- structure show strong bowing of the dislocations around the precipitates ( Fig. 11 b and c). This proves that  c 0   precip- itates impede glissile dislocations if they are suﬃciently coarse. This is also evident from the pile-up of dislocations around the precipitates’ boundary seen in the medium and coarse  c 0   microstructures ( Fig. 11 b–d). The increased inter- particle spacing and lack of tertiary  c 0   precipitates in comparison to the ﬁne  c 0   microstructure allow the  c  matrix (after some initial plastic deformation according to the neutron diﬀraction data) to deform plastically, while the  c 0   precipitates do not deform to the same extent. In the neutron diﬀraction data this is manifested in the load transfer observed in the medium and coarse microstruc- tures. These TEM images show clear indications that dislo- cation bowing and potentially the Orowan mechanism have become a dominant or at least signiﬁcant deformation mode in the medium and particularly the coarse  c 0   micro- structures. The observations of dislocation pile-ups on the boundaries of precipitates suggest that, once the applied stresses overcome the back stresses, dislocations might still be forced to shear the  c 0   particles. One has to bear in mind that the high plastic strain in the  c  matrix will cause stress accumulations in the precipitates. It is hence unlikely that the  c 0   precipitates only deform elastically in the course of plastic deformation of the matrix.  

When dislocation looping becomes an active deforma- tion mode, the dislocation networks developing at the  c / c 0   interface will make subsequent shearing of the  c 0   precip- itates more diﬃcult. Even though  c  to  c 0   load transfer is not observed at the onset of plastic deformation in any of the three microstructures, a limited amount of dislocation bowing and looping might still be operative, depending on the local arrangement of the precipitates. The disloca- tion networks arising from these initial bowing and looping processes might impede  c 0   shearing in the course of plastic deformation, and dislocation bowing and looping could then become increasingly dominant as deformation pro- gresses. Furthermore, as the number of looping processes increases, the dislocation pairs might no longer travel through the material together, as the APB that couples them is only created when the precipitates are sheared. This would further impede precipitate cutting. In the TEM images of the medium  c 0   sample stacking faults can be seen in the precipitates, which are created by single dislocations cutting through the precipitates, hence supporting this argument. This deformation pattern is often observed after creep tests in nickel-based superalloys. As loading was car- ried out in a stepwise manner here, the tensile tests at 500   C would resemble creep tests and might allow ther- mally activated deformation mechanisms to take place.  

The hypothesis of shearing and bowing of dislocations operating in a material at the same time is particularly plausible if the CRSSs for each deformation mode are sim- ilar. Especially considering that the distribution and size of the precipitates diﬀers locally within the grains, it is easy to comprehend how both mechanisms might be active depending on the local arrangement of the precipitates. It should also be remembered at this point that the stress state of grains in a polycrystalline material is far more complex than in a single crystal. Therefore, it is easily conceivable that several deformation modes might be active even if they have signiﬁcantly diﬀerent CRSS. Multiple slip is expected in all grains, but it is possible that, whereas some slip sys- tems might be able to shear the particles, others might not, leading to a combination of particle cutting and dislocation bowing. This leads to a complex evolution of the hardening behaviour with strain. This is analogous to deformation in materials like titanium, where, although the CRSS for h c + a i  slip is at least three times higher than prismatic h a i  slip, it nevertheless occurs readily in polycrystals  [33] . The tendency for bowing or looping of dislocations will increase with larger interparticle spacing. Therefore, this is consistent with the lack of signiﬁcant load transfer from c  to  c 0   in the ﬁne  c 0   microstructure. This trend is already demonstrated by the medium  c 0   microstructure, which requires larger plastic strains for  c / c 0   load partitioning.

The level of plastic strain at which the data start to deviate decreases with increasing  c 0   particle size. As demonstrated earlier, Orowan looping becomes an increasingly active deformation mode in these microstructures as the level of plastic deformation increases. In this case, the EPSC model would require the incorporation of a more complex hardening behaviour for both phases. Speciﬁcally, it would be required to couple the deformation of the two phases in terms of the strains evolving from the active slip system, rather than simply in terms of co-deformation as done at present. Our results suggest that a coupled latent hardening model could be required, where the hardening between diﬀerent (111) slip systems in the particles is linked to the work hardening of the diﬀerent (111) slip systems in the matrix.  

# 5. Summary and conclusions  

The deformation mechanisms in the advanced polycrys- talline nickel-based superalloy RR1000 were studied at room temperature (and 500   C) by means of in situ loading experiments using neutron diﬀraction and EPSC model- ling, as well as detailed electron microscopy. In order to study the eﬀect of the  c 0   particle size, model microstruc- tures with unimodal  c 0   size distributions were generated. The main ﬁndings can be summarized as follows:  

  It was found that the strain hardening is greatly aﬀected by the  c 0   particle size, with the largest level of strain hardening found for the coarsest  c 0   microstructure.

   Peak broadening and intergranular strain data recorded by neutron diﬀraction suggest that in the case of signif- icant strain hardening a change in deformation mode takes place from strongly coupled dislocations shearing c 0   to signiﬁcant dislocation bowing and potentially Oro- wan looping.

   This change in deformation mode was further conﬁrmed by post-mortem electron microscopy observations. In the medium and coarse  c 0   microstructures, the deforma- tion patterns are more diﬀuse, indicating less localized slip deformation. The dislocations were also seen to bow strongly around  c 0   precipitates in the medium and coarse  c 0   microstructures, which conﬁrms that Orowan looping is active as an additional deformation mecha- nism, making continued c 0 shearing increasingly diﬃcult.  

It was found that there is excellent agreement between the intergranular strain and peak broadening data recorded during the in situ loading experiments using neutron dif- fraction and the post-mortem analysis by electron micros- copy. By combining a macroscopic in situ technique with detailed investigations at the nanoscale, it has been possible to identify deformation mechanisms much more conclu- sively. While neutron diﬀraction is only an indirect mea- surement of deformation mechanisms, the work has demonstrated that it enables one to identify changes of deformation mechanisms as a function of plastic strain. In contrast, electron microscopy provides more direct observations of deformation mechanisms and therefore is highly complementary to the in situ studies.
