ABSTRACT:  Fms-like tyrosine receptor kinase 3 (FLT3) proteolysis-targeting chimeras (PROTACs) represent a promising approach to eliminate the resistance of FLT3 inhibitors. However, due to the poor druggability of PROTACs, the development of orally bioavailable FLT3-PROTACs faces great challenges. Herein, a novel orally bioavailable FLT3-ITD degrader  A20  with excellent pharmacokinetic properties was discovered through reasonable design.  A20  selectively inhibited the proliferation of FLT3-ITD mutant acute myeloid leukemia (AML) cells and potently induced FLT3-ITD degradation through the ubiquitin − proteasome system. Notably, oral administration of  A20  resulted in complete tumor

# INTRODUCTION  

Acute myeloid leukemia (AML) is considered to be among the most deadly hematologic cancer types worldwide.   Although traditional radiotherapy and chemotherapy can provide effective relief for patients, their lasting efficacy is limited, and they are often poorly tolerated. There is an urgent need to develop more precise targeted therapies. FLT3 mutations, which account for about 30% of genetic mutations in AML patients, represent the most prevalent genetic alterations in AML. These changes mainly include internal tandem duplication (ITD) mutation (25%) in the juxtamembrane domain and point mutations (5%) in the tyrosine kinase domain.   Among these, FLT3-ITD mutation is the predominant FLT3 mutation closely linked to the progression and poor prognosis of AML.   Therefore, therapy targeting FLT3-ITD mutation has become one of the most promising strategies for the treatment of AML. ,  

In the past decade, numerous prominent FLT3-ITD inhibitors such as midostaurin,   lestaurtinib,   sunitinib, sorafenib,   quizartinib,   gilteritinib,   and crenolanib 12   are clinically evaluated as targeted therapies for FLT3-ITD-positive AML. Among these, midostaurin, gilteritinib, and quizartinib have been approved by the FDA for the combination therapy or monotherapy of FLT3-ITD mutant relapsed or refractory AML patients. Despite considerable progress in treating AML with FLT3 inhibitors, most patients develop drug resistance soon after initial remission. ,   The secondary point mutation of the FLT3 gene is one of the main causes of acquired drug resistance; 15   for instance, relapsed FLT3-ITD mutant individ- uals with AML who were treated with quizartinib typically experienced secondary point mutations in the activation loop residue D835 (D835Y/V/H/E/N) 16   and the gate-keeper residue F691 (F691L).   Therefore, it is difficult to achieve lasting efficacy with conventional FLT3 inhibitors, and it is urgent to develop a class of anti-AML drugs with new mechanisms to fundamentally solve the issue of drug resistance.  

Recently, proteolysis-targeting chimera (PROTAC), a novel protein degradation technology, has come to the forefront as a promising drug discovery paradigm. ,   PROTAC is a bifunc- tional molecule comprising a target protein ligand, an E3 ubiquitin ligase ligand, and a chemical linker. It hijacks the target protein and E3 ligase to form a ternary complex, resulting in the poly ubiquitin ation of the target protein. This further leads to the subsequent target protein degradation by the proteasome.   In contrast to conventional inhibitors work in an occupancy-driven

# RESULTS AND DISCUSSION  

Design and Anti pro life rat ive Effects Evaluation of FLT3-PROTACs.  Stimulated by the studies of PROTACs and ongoing research efforts on anti-AML drugs,  PXA13 , a potent FLT3 inhibitor discovered in our previous work,   was selected as the FLT3 ligand to construct FLT3-PROTACs. According to the docking modeling of  PXA13  with FLT3, the pyrrolidine group points toward the solvent, indicating a favorable site for PROTAC linkage. For the E3 ubiquitin ligases, many ligases, such as VHL, CRBN, inhibitors of apoptosis protein (IAP), and murine double minute 2 (MDM2), have been discovered and utilized for PROTAC design. However, only CRBN and VHL are the two most commonly employed ligases. Notably, several orally bioavailable CRBN-recruited bifunctional degraders have entered clinical trials. − 40   Therefore, several CRBN- and VHL- recruited degraders were first synthesized by converting the pyrrolidine group of  PXA13  to piperazine to attach the linker and E3 ligase ligand ( Figure 2 ). In the first round of design, it was found that CRBN-recruited degraders showed stronger anti pro life rat ive activities against FLT3-ITD-positive AML cells MV4-11 and MOLM-13 ( Table 1 ). Thus, CRBN was selected as the E3 ubiquitin ligase in the subsequent design of FLT3- PROTACs. The synthetic routes for preparing compounds  A1 − A5  and  B1 − B5  are shown in  Scheme 1 .

Identification of A20 as a Potent Degrader of FLT3- ITD.  To evaluate the protein degradation activity of these synthesized FLT3-PROTACs, the changes in FLT3-ITD protein levels in MV4-11 and MOLM-13 cells were examined by Western blot analysis after the treatment of FLT3- PROTACs. As shown in  Figure 3 A − D, most compounds could significantly degrade FLT3-ITD protein in these two cell lines at a concentration of 100 nM. Of these,  A20  showed the strongest FLT3-ITD degradation activity with DC 50  values of 7.4 and 20.1 nM, respectively ( Figure 3 E,F). Considering the membrane localization of the FLT3 protein, the study further examined the  A20 -induced degradation of FLT3-ITD on plasma membranes by immuno fluorescence experiments. The immuno- fluorescent images demonstrated a substantial reduction in FLT3-ITD protein levels on the membrane after 24 h of  A20 treatment. Conversely, the protein was normally visible in vehicle-treated cells ( Figure 3 G). These results demonstrated that  A20  was a highly potent FLT3-ITD degrader worthy of further biological evaluation.

A20 Blocked AML Cell Cycle in G1 Phase and Induced Apoptosis.  To explore the mechanism underlying the anti pro life rat ive activity of  A20 , a further examination of its effects on AML cell cycle and cell apoptosis was conducted. As depicted in  Figure 6 ,  A20  dose-dependently induced MV4-11 cell cycle arrest in the G1 phase. In detail, the proportion of cells in the G1 phase gradually increased to 82.0, 85.5, and 89.2% after the treatment with  A20  at 1, 5, and 50 nM, respectively. Consistent with the results in MV4-11 cells,  A20  also dose- dependently induced MOLM-13 cell cycle arrest in the G1 phase. Additionally,  A20  also dose-dependently induced the apoptosis of MV4-11 and MOLM-13 cells ( Figure 7 ). In contrast, no significant apoptosis was observed after negative control  A25  treatment at the same concentration of 50 nM.  

Pharmacokinetic Properties of A20 In Vivo.  Subse- quently, to investigate whether  A20  is suitable for in vivo pharmacodynamic study, its pharmacokinetic (PK) properties were determined in rats. As depicted in  Figure 8  and  Table 5 , A20  displayed excellent PK properties with high plasma exposure, reasonable half-life time ( T 1/2  = 3.8 h), high oral bioavailability ( F  = 53%), and low clearance rate. This finding indicated that  A20  was suitable for oral administration in the subsequent in vivo anti-AML efficacy study.

In Vivo Anti-AML Efficacy of A20 on Systemic Xenograft Models.  Encouraged by the excellent in vivo anti- AML efficacy of compound  A20  on subcutaneous xenograft models, the study further evaluated its anti-AML effect on systemic xenograft models. As shown in  Figure 10 A − C, compared with the vehicle group,  A20 -treated mice exhibited significantly lower leukemic burdens and longer survival times. On average, the mice treated with vehicle died on day 49, whereas those treated with  A20  at a dose of 2.5, 5, or 10 mg/kg showed prolonged survival periods to days 58, 66, or 77, respectively. Furthermore,  A20  treatment significantly reduced the proportion of CD45 + CD33 +   human leukemic cells in murine peripheral blood, bone marrow, and spleen ( Figure 10 D). Remarkably, in mice treated with  A20  at 10 mg/kg, the levels of physiological indicators nearly returned to normal. This was evidenced by the lowest tumor burden, longest survival period, and least percentage of human CD45 + CD33 +   cells. Moreover, no obvious adverse responses were observed in different treatment groups throughout the experiment.  

Subacute Toxicity Study of A20 on Subcutaneous Xenograft Models.  To further evaluate the safety of compound  A20 , a subacute toxicity study of  A20  was performed on subcutaneous xenograft models of AML. As shown in  Figure 11 , after continuous oral administration of  A20  at the high dose of 120 mg/kg for 15 days, the weight of the mice did not change significantly. Moreover, tumor regression was observed on the third day, suggesting that  A20  exhibits high anti-AML efficiency and low toxicity.  

Proteomic Analysis of A20-Mediated Protein Degra- dation in MV4-11 Cells.  Intrigued by  A20 -mediated protein degradation, tandem mass tag (TMT)-based global proteomic profiling was conducted to identify differential proteins between the vehicle-treated and  A20 -treated groups in MV4-11 cells. Proteins extracted from MV4-11 cells were processed in parallel and analyzed by a liquid chromatography-tandem system ( Figure 12 A). Based on the result depicted in  Figure 12 B, 84 proteins were downregulated across all the identified 5830 proteins after the  A20  treatment. Among these downregulated proteins, FLT3 was selectively degraded by  A20  ( P  = 0.012, fold change >1.4). Furthermore, the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathway analysis of differentially expressed proteins indicated that  A20  exhibited a significant influence on the biological processes of cancer development, especially the PI3K-Akt signaling pathway downstream of FLT3 ( Figure 12 C). These results demonstrated that  A20  could selectively degrade the FLT3 protein in MV4-11 cells.

# CONCLUSIONS  

In conclusion, this study aimed to develop more effective treatment schemes for relapsed or refractory AML. This objective was pursued by designing, synthesizing, and system- atically assessing a series of novel FLT3-PROTACs for their anti-AML efficacy. Through a step-by-step discovery strategy, the study presented, for the first time, the discovery of a highly effective orally bioavailable FLT3-ITD degrader  A20 .  A20 exhibited selective and significant inhibition of FLT3-ITD mutant AML cell proliferation, along with potent induction of FLT3-ITD protein degradation in both in vitro and in vivo settings. Mechanistic investigations highlighted that  A20 - induced FLT3-ITD degradation was dependent on the ubiquitin − proteasome system with a long-lasting effect, and it could dose-dependently induce AML cell cycle arrest in the G1 phase and induced apoptosis. Remarkably,  A20  exhibited excellent pharmacokinetic properties with a remarkable oral

Cell Culture.  MV-4-11 ( # CRL-9591, ATCC) was grown in IMDM ( # 30 − 2005, ATCC). MOLM-13, HL-60, THP-1, and U937 were kindly gifts from Dr. Xuejun Zhu (Jiangsu Provincial Hospital, Nanjing, China) and grown in RPMI-1640 ( # 30 − 2001, ATCC). All cells were cultured in a complete medium comprising 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) ( # 1009148, Gibco) and 1% penicillin/streptomycin ( # 15140122, Gibco) at 37  ° C in a humidified air with 5% CO 2 .  

CCK8 Assay.  Logarithmic phase cells were seeded into 96- well plates at a density of (5 − 9)  ×  10 4   cells and subjected to incubation with different compounds at various concentrations for 48 h. Subsequently, the CCK-8 kit ( # A311, Vazyme) was used to examine the anti pro life rat ive activities of target compounds. The IC 50  value was calculated using GraphPad Prism 8.0.  

Cell Cycle Arrest Assay.  Cells in 6-well plates were exposed to compound  A20  at indicated concentrations. After the treatment for 24 h, the cells were collected by centrifugation at 1200 rpm for 3 min and subsequently rinsed several times with 1  ×  PBS. After overnight fixation with 75% cold ethanol, cells were rinsed and resuspended in PI/RNase Staining Buffer ( # 550825, BD Pharmingen) according to the protocol. Flow cytometry was then conducted to assess the cell cycle using CytoFLEX (BECKMAN COULTER).  

Cell Apoptosis Assay.  Cell apoptosis was detected by Annexin V/PI staining assay. The cells were seeded into 6-well plates at a density of 2  ×  10 6   cells, and incubated with  A20  at the specified concentration for 24 h. The collected cells were suspended in a binding buffer after twice rinses with cold 1  × PBS. The samples were subsequently subjected to staining with Annexin V-FITC/PI kit ( # A211, Vazyme) and incubated at room temperature for 10 − 15 min in the dark. Cell apoptosis was assessed utilizing a flow cytometer.  

Western Blot Assay.  A total of 2  ×  10 6   cells were seeded into 6-well plates and incubated with different compounds. After incubation, cells were subjected to lysis in RIPA buffer

 ( # P0013B, Beyotime) comprising protease inhibitor cocktail

 ( # 4693132001, Roche). Samples containing 10 − 30  μ g of proteins were loaded into each lane of SDS-PAGE gels. The primary antibodies employed were as follows: rabbit anti-FLT3

 ( # 3462), rabbit antiphospho-FLT3 ( # 3464), rabbit anti-Stat5

 ( # 94205), rabbit anti-Phospho-Stat5 ( # 4322), rabbit anti-p70 S6 Kinase ( # 9202), rabbit anti-Phospho-p70 S6 Kinase ( # 9204), rabbit anti-Erk1/2 ( # 4695), rabbit antiphospho- Erk1/2 ( # 9101), and mouse antibeta-actin ( # 3700). All antibodies were provided by CST. The secondary antibodies used in the Western blot assay were Peroxidase AffiniPure goat antirabbit IgG (H+L) and antimouse IgG (H+L) (Jackson ImmunoResearch).  

Animals and Xenograft Experiments.  All mice were purchased from GemPharmatech (Nanjing, China) and kept in a specific pathogen-free facility. For the subcutaneous AML xenograft models, four week old male nu/nu mice received a subcutaneous injection of MV4-11 cells with an inoculum of 5  × 10 6   cells/mouse. Once the tumors attained a size of 100 − 300 mm 3 , the mice were randomly classified into different groups and administered the indicated drug daily. Daily measurements were taken for body weight and tumor volume. The tumor volumes of the mice were calculated utilizing the formula below:  

tumor volume (mm )  

# = length (mm) × [ width (mm) ] × 0.5  

For the systemic AML models, NSG mice underwent inoculation of MV-4-11-luc cells with an inoculum of 5  ×  10 6 cells/mouse in their tail vein. After successfully establishing AML systemic xenograft models, the mice were picked out and randomly classified into four groups and intra gastric ally administrated with the indicated drug daily. Finally, bone marrow cells, spleen cells, and peripheral blood cells were collected. Flow cytometry was utilized to measure the proportion of viable human CD45/CD33-positive cells following staining with antihuman CD45 and CD33 antibodies. All animal experimental procedures were subjected to approval by the ethics committee of Nanjing University of Chinese Medicine (No. 202208A005, 202206A046).  

Plasma and Intestinal Fluid Stability Studies.  A certain amount of compound was dissolved in DMSO to prepare a 50 μ g/mL test solution. Subsequently, 2  μ L of the test solution was introduced into 198  μ L of plasma/intestinal fluid. The resulting samples were then incubated on a constant temperature oscillator. After incubation periods of 0, 2, 4, 6, and 8 h, the medium was collected, and the reaction was terminated by introducing 600  μ L methanol (tolbutamide concentration is 100 ng/mL). Subsequently, the mixture was centrifuged, and the supernatants obtained were subjected to analysis using LC-MS/ MS (Thermo TSQ Vantage).  

Pharmacokinetic Studies.  Male SD rats weighing 180 − 200g were purchased from Qinglong Mountain, Nanjing and randomly divided into two groups of three rats each. They were fed a standard laboratory diet throughout their rearing. Prior to the experiment, the rats underwent a 12 h fasting period and were provided unrestricted access to water. Compound  A20  was homogeneously dissolved in 5% DMSO and 5% cremophor EL, followed by 90% saline. Three rats were administrated intragastric at a dose of 10 mg/kg, and the other three rats were administrated intravenously through the tail vein at a dose of 1 mg/kg. Blood samples were obtained from the suborbital vein utilizing heparinized tubes at 0, 0.083, 0.17, 0.25, 0.5, 1, 2, 4, 6, 8, and 24 h following administration. The blood samples were subjected to centrifugation at 8000 rpm at 2 − 8  ° C for 5 min and then stored at  − 80  ° C. The plasma samples were examined using LC-MS/MS (Thermo TSQ Vantage) equipped with an ESI ion resource. The PK parameters were calculated by employing a non compartment al model in the Phoenix WinNonlin program.  

H&E and IHC Staining.  Paraffin-embedded tumor samples were first deparaffinized with xylene, followed by rehydration with ethanol several times. For H&E staining, the tissue sections were sequentially stained with hematoxylin ( # G1004, Service- bio) and eosin solution ( # G1001, Servicebio), following the procedure described in previous papers. The antigen retrieval process was performed in 10 mM sodium citrate buffer ( # G1202, Servicebio) before IHC staining. Subsequently, the slides were stained with Ki67 primary antibodies ( # GB111499, Servicebio) and cleaved-Caspase-3 ( # GB111532, Servicebio) at 4  ° C overnight. After incubation with a secondary antibody, the slides were washed, mounted, and photographed.  

Protein Extraction, Quantification, and Digestion. MV4-11 cells were subjected to treatment with the vehicle ( n = 3) or 100 nM  A20  ( n  = 3) for 8 h. Then, the cells were rinsed twice with cold PBS to eliminate fetal bovine serum, centrifuged, and subjected to lysis in fresh SDT lysis buffer comprising 0.2% SDS, 100 mM DTT ( # M109 − 5G, Amresco), and 100 mM Tris (pH = 7.6). The protein supernatants were collected and placed into a fresh tube following sonication and centrifugation. Bicinchoninic acid assay was performed to quantify protein concentration. An appropriate amount of protein was taken, reduced with a final concentration of 5 mM DTT at 37  ° C for 1 h and then returned to room temperature. A final concentration of 10 mM iodoacetamide ( # M216 − 30G, Amresco) was intro- duced into the sample and incubated away from light at room temperature for 45 min. The samples were then diluted by 25 mM ammonium bicarbonate ( # A6141 − 500G, Sigma-Aldrich) and lysed with trypsin ( # V5280, Promega) at a 1:50 enzyme to a substrate ratio, followed by incubation overnight at 37  ° C. The next day, the digestion was quenched by adding formic acid to adjust the pH to less than 3. A C18 Peptide Separation Technology (PST) column ( # 186003581, Waters) was used to desalt the peptides, which was then aliquoted for TMT labeling. TMT Labeling.  Each sample (100  μ g) was labeled by TMT 16-plex ( # A44520, Thermofisher) at room temperature for 1 h.  

Following incubation, the reaction was quenched by adding aqueous ammonia ( # 013 − 23355, Wako Pure Chemical Industries Ltd.) to the sample. The mixed TMT-labeled samples were vortexed and centrifuged to the bottom of the tube and then dried down using a SpeedVac Vacuum.  

Liquid Chromatography (LC) Coupled to Tandem Mass Spectrometry (MS) Analysis.  Proteomic fractions were separated by a Waters BEH 5  μ M C18 column on a RIGOL L-3000 HPLC operating at 0.7 mL/min. The mobile phase A was 0.1% (v/v) formic acid aqueous solution, and the mobile phase B was 0.1% (v/v) formic acid acetonitrile solution. The system was coupled to Thermo Scientific Orbitrap Exploris 480 for MS/MS analysis. The mass spectrometer worked in a data- dependent acquisition mode. Using standard AGC, MS1 survey scans were performed in the range of 350 − 1500  m / z  at 60,000 resolution (200  m / z ). A “top speed” mode was used for the collection of MS2 spectra at 30,000 resolution (200  m / z ) with 100% AGC and maximum injection time (auto). The collision energy for peptide fragmentation was set to 36%.  

Bioinformatics Analysis.  Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was conducted to visualize the data sets with biological variables. Student  t -test was conducted to identify proteins exhibiting significantly different expression profiles. Fisher’s exact test was utilized to identify enriched differentially expressed proteins in Gene Ontology (GO) and KEGG enrichment analyses.  

Statistical Analysis.  All statistical data were assessed utilizing GraphPad Prism 8.0 software. Student’s  t -test was employed to determine statistically significant differences. All results in this study were expressed as the means  ±  SEM from at least three independent experiments. The Kaplan − Meier method was utilized to estimate survival curves, and these curves were statistically compared using the log-rank test.
