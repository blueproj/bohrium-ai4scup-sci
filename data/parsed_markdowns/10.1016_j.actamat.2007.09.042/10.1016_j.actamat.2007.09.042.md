# Abstract  

The eﬀect of adding 2 at.% W to a model Ni–Al–Cr superalloy on the morphological evolution, spatial correlations and temporal evolution of  c 0 (L1 2 )-precipitates at 1073 K is studied with scanning electron microscopy and atomic force microscopy. Adding W yields a larger microhardness, earlier onset of spheroidal-to-cuboidal precipitate morphological transition, larger volume fraction (from   20% to 30%), reduction in coarsening kinetics by one-third and a larger number density ( N v ) of smaller mean radii ( Æ R æ ) precipitates. The kinetics of  Æ R æ  and interfacial area per unit volume obey  t 1/3   and  t  1/3   relationships, respectively, which is consistent with coarsening driven by interfacial energy reduction. The  N v  power-law dependencies deviate, however, from model predictions, indicating that a sta- tionary state is not achieved. Quantitative analyses with precipitate size distributions, pair correlation functions and edge-to-edge inter- precipitate distance distributions give insight into two-dimensional microstructural evolution, including the elastically driven transition from a uniform  c 0 -distribution to one-dimensional  Æ 001 æ -strings to eventually clustered packs of  c 0 -precipitates in the less densely packed Ni–Al–Cr alloy.

# 1. Introduction  

The widespread use of  c 0 (L1 2 )-precipitate-strengthened Ni-base superalloys as a turbine blade material in high- temperature commercial and military jet engines and land-based gas turbines warrants extensive study of the microstructural evolution and  c 0 -coarsening behavior in model  c (fcc)/ c 0   alloys, such as Ni-rich Ni–Al, Ni–Al–Cr and Ni–Al–Mo alloys  [1–13] . In these alloys, elastic aniso- tropy leads to coherent precipitates evolving from spheres- to-cubes to arrays of cubes or to doublets or octets of smaller precipitates  [1,8,14–17] , as well as face-to-face cube alignment along the elastically soft  Æ 100 æ -crystallographic directions  [1,2] . In all cases, the morphology is dictated by the minimization of the interfacial free energy and elas- tic free energy arising from lattice parameter misﬁt, while the spatial correlations are controlled by elastic and diﬀu- sional interactions among neighboring precipitates. To improve its high-temperature performance, modern com- mercial Ni-base superalloys contain as many as 12 or 13 microalloying elements  [18,19] . High-melting-point refrac- tory element additions, such as Nb, Re, Ru, W and Mo, are particularly eﬀective in improving creep resistance,

The chemi- cal and microstructural  c / c 0   decomposition that controls these properties is strongly dependent on the complex interplay between elastic interactions of misﬁtting  c 0 -pre- cipitates and rate-controlling diﬀusion of these refractory elemental additions with small diﬀusivities. The research reported herein is part of a systematic study  [21–24]  to understand the roles of small additions of refractory ele- ments on the  c / c 0   decomposition when a model, nominally Ni–10 Al–8.5 Cr (at.%), superalloy is aged at 1073 K. Herein, the inﬂuence of adding 2 at.% W is considered. Of all possible candidates for solid-solution strengthening, W has the highest melting temperature and is among the slowest diﬀusers in Ni  [20] ; however, relative to other refractory additions it has the largest molar volume com- pared to Ni – 36.93% greater than Ni  [25]  – which can sig- niﬁcantly impact the  c / c 0   lattice parameter mismatch that drives the elastic interactions.  

We reported previously  [21]  on the elemental partitioning behavior, evolving phase chemical composition and  c / c 0 heterophase interfacial chemistry at 1073 K as measured with conventional atom-probe tomography (APT) for the Ni–Al–Cr and Ni–Al–Cr–W alloys analyzed further here. In addition to details on the evolving chemical composition, the sample volumes analyzed in three dimensions (3D) with  ·  · 3 conventional APT ( 15  15  100 nm ) yield an under- standing of the  c 0 -precipitates’ morphology, dimensions and growth rate. Post-mortem analyses with conventional APT reveals it is not possible to suppress  c 0 -precipitation that occurs during a water quench after the alloys are homogenized, which is a result of the large solute supersat- urations in these two alloys. The as-quenched alloys contain c 0 -precipitates, 5–15 nm in 3D radius, that do not exhibit a signiﬁcant degree of coagulation and coalescence. This result is in contrast to a high degree of  c 0   coagulation and coalescence observed for an as-quenched quaternary alloy with 2 at.% Re  [23,24,26] . In addition to reducing the  c 0 -pre- cipitate growth and coarsening rate, the W additions parti- tion preferentially to the  c 0 -precipitates, with no evidence of non-monotonic (conﬁned) segregation of W at the  c / c 0 -het- erophase interface. Adding 2 at.% W also leads to a stronger partitioning of Al to  c 0 -precipitates and of Cr to the  c -matrix than measured for the Ni–Al–Cr alloy. At the shortest aging times (<1 h), the  c 0 -precipitates exhibit concentration gradi- ents in W in which their cores contain a smaller W concen- tration (  2.0 at.%) than the concentration within the regions immediately adjacent to the  c / c 0 -heterophase inter- face (  2.5 at.%). We note that the two alloys exhibit only small changes in chemical compositions after 4 h. This is when the alloys have reached a quasi-stationary-state coars- ening regime, i.e. when change in matrix supersaturation with time is approximately zero.  

Our present microstructural evaluation with scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and atomic force microscopy (AFM) focuses on the evolution of precipitate morpholo- gies and precipitate spatial correlations in the quasi- stationary-state coarsening regime ( t  > 4 h), where the ele- mental partitioning between the  c - and  c 0 -phases is nearly constant at precipitate dimensions that are reasonably well-resolved by these microscopic techniques, thus permit- ting the coarsening behavior to be modeled accurately with predictions for multi-component alloys  [27,28] .  

# 2. Experimental procedures  

To produce master ingots, high-purity constituent ele- ments (99.97 wt.% Ni, 99.98 wt.% Al, 99.99 wt.% Cr and 99.98 wt.% W) were induction melted under an Ar atmo- sphere to minimize oxidation and then chill cast in a cylin- drical copper mold, 19 mm in diameter by 86 mm long, with a conical hot top section to accommodate shrinkage within the casting. The cast ingots were homogenized at 1573 K for 20 h and the Ni–Al–Cr and Ni–Al–Cr–W alloys were further solutionized for 0.5 h at 1273 and 1363 K, respectively, in the single-phase region (  90 K above sol- vus  [21] ) to minimize  c 0 -precipitate formation during the subsequent water quenching. As-quenched sections were aged at 1073 K for times ranging from essentially zero (the as-quenched state) to 264 h, followed again by a rapid water-quench from the aging temperature.  

Bulk chemical analysis of the two alloys was performed using an Applied Research Laboratories Model 3560 inductively coupled plasma (ICP) atomic-emission vacuum spectrometer. ICP atomic-emission spectroscopy was also used to determine the composition of  c - and  c 0 -phases directly from specimen sections aged for 264 h; the two phases were separated electrolytic ally by standard phase- extraction techniques. The results of these chemical analyses are presented in  Table 1 . Phase extraction was accomplished by anodic dissolution of the  c -matrix phase with a 1:1 aqueous solution of citric acid and ammonium nitrate at constant current density. By comparing the weight of the extracted  c 0 -precipitates with the overall sec- tion weight, the volume fraction of the  c 0 -precipitates,  / ( t  = 264 h), was measured to be 0.189 and 0.308 for the Ni–Al–Cr and Ni–Al–Cr–W alloys, respectively. Microhardness measures indirectly the dynamics of the precipitation sequence through hardness changes. It is a function of the spatial distribution of precipitates, their dimensions, as well as their  N v  value, where peak microh- ardness corresponds to the optimal balance between the mean  c 0 -precipitate  Æ R ( t ) æ  and  N v ( t ) with aging. For the Ni–Al–Cr alloy, the microhardness for  t  = 0–264 h varies over a narrow range: 2.15–2.5 GPa at 1073 K ( Fig. 2 ). Peak microhardness is achieved at 4 h, after which the microh- ardness decreases steadily. For the Ni–Al–Cr–W alloy, the 1073 K values are 40–50% greater than the Ni–Al–Cr measurements, which likely results from the denser packing of the  c 0 -precipitates and added solid-solution strengthen- ing with the W addition. Similar to the Ni–Al–Cr alloy, the peak hardness (3.5 GPa) is achieved after 4 h; however, the Ni–Al–Cr–W alloy retains this peak up to 64 h of aging. For the ﬁnal aging time of 264 h, the microhardness decreases to 3.27 ± 0.10 GPa after the plateau.

# 4.1. Morphological evolution  

At early aging times, the morphology of  c 0 -precipitates is spheroidal. With further aging time and increasing  Æ R æ , the  c 0 -precipitates become less rounded and {100}-type facets form as a cuboidal morphology develops. The Æ R æ  value at which the microstructure becomes cuboidal is estimated to be   88 nm ( t  = 64 h) for the Ni–Al–Cr alloy and   50 nm ( t    32 h) for the Ni–Al–Cr–W alloy, based on the micrographs in  Fig. 3 . # 4.2. Coarsening kinetics  

Recent work on  c 0 -coarsening at 1173 K  [56]  in Ni–Al alloys shows that  Æ S v æ  follows a  t  1/3   law, even at longer aging times (  40 h) when the microstructure consists of rod-shaped precipitates and the elastic energy contribu- tion to the total energy of system is signiﬁcant; a non-  Æ 3 æ linearity in R  is, however, observed and attributed to non-equiaxed precipitate morphologies. The microstruc- tures in the systems under investigation here are equi- axed and not as coarse. Since the magnitude of the 3 elastic energy eﬀects scale as  R , these eﬀects are most likely not as large as for the Ni–Al alloys presented in Ref.  [56] . Therefore, the Ni–Al–Cr and Ni–Al–Cr–W alloys we study are expected to be governed by the reduction in interfacial free energy.  Figs. 8 and 9  demon- strate that the coarsening kinetics of  Æ R æ  and  Æ S v æ  obey t 1/3 and t  1/3 , respectively. The measured temporal power-law dependencies of  N v  from the stereological analysis of the 2D structure are 0.84 ± 0.04 for the Ni–Al–Cr alloy and   0.88 ± 0.07 for the Ni–Al–Cr–W alloy ( Fig. 10 ), in good agreement with one another, but deviating from the stationary-state prediction of  1; therefore, the alloys have not truly achieved a sta- tionary state. The PSD PS s ( Figs. 11 and 12 ) for both alloys evolve continuously with time, verifying that they have not yet achieved a stationary state; however, at the longest aging time, 264 h, the PSD PS s for both alloys are similar to but not exactly the stationary-state predic- tion  [27] .  

The coarsening kinetics is signiﬁcantly aﬀected by the addition of W. Although the precipitated  /  value is smal- ler, the Ni–Al–Cr alloy under investigation coarsens at a rate ( K ) that is 3.3 times faster than the Ni–Al–Cr–W alloy. This behavior suggests that chemical composition plays a critical role in the coarsening behavior of these systems. For the Kuehmann–Voorhees coarsening model of a ternary alloy in stress-free systems  [28] , the coarsening rate constant is a function of: (i) the magnitude of the par- titioning between the phases ( p i  ¼  C i c 0 ; eq   C c i ; eq Þ ; (ii) the relative ratios of curvatures of the Gibbs free-energy sur- faces for the matrix phase ( G k ; ij Þ ; and (iii) the solute diﬀu- sivities ( D ii ) of component  i , where the ratio  p i / D ii  is critical in determining  G k   contributions. Multicomponent ; ij coarsening models assume that the oﬀ-diagonal terms of the diﬀusivity matrix ( ergo  these terms are zero in the sus- ceptibility and Onsager matrices) and the vacancy chemi- cal potential are everywhere zero, which are strong assumptions, as it has been shown recently that the details of the diﬀusion mechanism in an Ni–Al–Cr alloy aged at 873 K strongly aﬀect the kinetic pathway of precipitation [57] . As found by APT measurements  [21] , the addition of W inﬂuences the elemental partitioning in the following manner: (i) Cr partitions more strongly to the  c -matrix than in the Ni–Al–Cr alloy, by a factor of 1.80 ± 0.09 (as determined from equilibrium partitioning ratios, C i c 0 ; eq = C c i ; eq , that we measured for both alloys); (ii) Al par- titions more strongly to  c 0 -precipitates, by a factor of 1.31 ± 0.05; and (iii) there are approximately twice as many W atoms per unit volume in the  c 0 -precipitates as in the  c -matrix. The stronger phase partitioning of Al and Cr for the Ni–Al–Cr–W alloy is also reﬂected in the p Al  and  p Cr  values calculated from the ICP measurements

 ( Table 1 ), where their absolute values increase by 1.64 and

 3.69 at.% for Al and Cr, respectively. For the Ni–Al–Cr– W alloy, the value of  p W  of 1.46 ± 0.03 is an order of magnitude smaller than the  p Al  of 10.62 ± 0.18, so the inﬂuence of  p W  on the coarsening constant may be small. Relative to its combined inﬂuence on elemental partition- ing ( p i ), the eﬀect of W on the solute diﬀusivities is minor, only reducing the Al and Cr tracer diﬀusivities in the  c - phase by 2–3% ( Table 1 )  [32,33] . The tracer diﬀusivity of W in the  c -matrix at 1073 K, 4.93  ·  10  18   m 2   s  1   [32,33] , is approximately one order of magnitude smaller that of Cr, the next slowest diﬀusing species, and counters the eﬀect of a small value of  p W . Given that the Al and Cr tra- cer diﬀusivities are similar for the alloys studied, it is likely the inﬂuence of W on the partitioning behavior of the ele- mental species, as well as its small mobility, causes the coarsening rate in the Ni–Al–Cr–W alloy to be signiﬁ- cantly slower than in the Ni–Al–Cr alloy. A similar coars- ening behavior has been established for Ni–Al–Mo alloys [12,13] , where the diﬀusivity of Mo in Ni is smaller than that of Al in Mo.  

# 5. Summary and conclusions  

The grain structure, hardness, precipitate morphology, precipitate spatial correlations and coarsening kinetics are explored in detail for two  c / c 0   alloys, Ni–9.8 Al–8.3 Cr at.% and Ni–9.7 Al–8.5 Cr–2.0 W at.%, isothermally aged at 1073 K from 4 to 264 h, where the unconstrained lattice parameter misﬁts are estimated to be 0.0022 ± 0.0007 and 0.0029 ± 0.0009, respectively, leading to the following results:  

  The Ni–Al–Cr alloy exhibits hardness values that vary between 2.15 and 2.5 GPa ( Fig. 2 ). Its grain structure is fairly coarse, equiaxed and twinned with diﬀerent crystallographic orientations ( Fig. 1 ). By adding 2 at.% W, the microhardness increases by 40–50% ( Fig. 2 ), the grains are less coarse, elongated radially and exhibit a  Æ 110 æ -texture ( Fig. 1 ).

   Employing stereological corrections for a section with a ﬁnite thickness (Eqs.  (2) and (4) ), the volume fraction ( / ), mean precipitate radii ( Æ R æ ), interfacial area per unit volume ( S v ) and number density per unit volume ( N v ) of the  c 0 (L1 2 )-precipitates protruding from a chemically etched  c (fcc)-matrix are precisely characterized with SEM and AFM measurements of the polish-relief depth ( Fig. 4 and Table 2 ). For the entire aging sequence, the value of  /  is constant and is at its equilibrium value ( Fig. 7 ). The coarsening kinetics of the quantities  Æ R æ and  S v  obey  t 1/3   and  t  1/3   laws ( Figs. 8 and 9 ), establish- ing that coarsening is dominated by a reduction in inter- facial energy. The N v power-law dependencies of  0.84 ± 0.04 and   0.88 ± 0.07 for the Ni–Al–Cr and Ni–Al–Cr–W alloys, respectively ( Fig. 10 ), deviate from the model stationary-state prediction of –1, suggesting that the systems are still undergoing quasi-stationary- state coarsening. That is, they have not achieved a sta- tionary state.

   Initially, the nanostructure in the Ni–Al–Cr alloy evolves temporally from isolated spheroidal  c 0 -precipi- tates uniformly distributed, to strings of cuboidal pre- cipitates aligned along one  Æ 100 æ -type direction and, ﬁnally, to highly faceted cuboids aligned in the orthogo- nal  Æ 100 æ -type directions into clustered groups ( Figs. 3a–d and 5 ). Despite this, the 2D pair correlation func- tions are time-invariant ( Fig. 13 a). However, the 2D interprecipitate distance distributions (IDD PS s) of edge-to-edge distances ( Fig. 14 ) evolve temporally. With string alignment, these IDD PS s narrow, become more intense and shift towards smaller normalized distances ( Fig. 14 b). As the  c 0 -precipitates cluster in groups, a broader and ﬂatter IDD PS  that shifts toward larger dis- tances results.  

  Due to a stronger nucleation current from a larger matrix supersaturation, coupled with slower coarsening kinetics, the Ni–Al–Cr–W alloy exhibits a larger value of N v  of smaller  c 0 -precipitates that are more densely packed than in the Ni–Al–Cr alloy ( Fig. 3 ). For the shorter aging times (4 and 16 h), the  c 0 -precipitates are spheroidal and some coagulation and coalescence among the  c 0 -precipitates leads to irregular morpholo- gies. With further aging, the  c 0 -precipitates become cuboidal and   5% are non-equiaxed ( Fig. 6 ). Through the morphological transition (  32 h) up to 64 h of aging, a sharp peak in the 2D pair-correlation functions ( Fig. 13 b) is time-invariant and attributed to this dense packing. For the ﬁnal aging state, a less dense distribu- tion with larger long-range interactions allows  c 0 -precip- itates to align elastically along the soft  Æ 100 æ -type directions into groups and results in a ﬂatter pair-corre- lation function. The edge-to-edge IDD PS s are approxi- mately but not exactly time-invariant ( Fig. 15 ).  

  Compared to stationary-state predictions for stress-free coarsening at non-negligible volume fractions  [27] , the 2D precipitate size distributions ( Figs. 11 and 12 ) are initially skewed towards smaller radii. With increasing aging time, these PSD PS s approach the predicted distri- bution and are in near agreement for the ﬁnal aging time (264 h).

   The addition of 2 at.% W to the Ni–Al–Cr alloy reduces the coarsening rate constant by more than one-third. Tungsten also signiﬁcantly inﬂuences the elemental par- titioning of the other constituents to the  c - and  c 0 -phases ( Table 1 ). It is likely that tungsten’s inﬂuence on the partitioning, as well as its inherently small diﬀusivity in Ni–Al–Cr, are responsible for the observed decelera- tion of the coarsening kinetics.
