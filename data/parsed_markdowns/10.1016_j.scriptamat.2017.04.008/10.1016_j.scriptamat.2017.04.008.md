# Formation of nanograins in Ni-Co based superalloys compressed quasistatically at high temperature

In this study, the performance of hot compressive deformation was investigated in two newly developed Ni-Co based superalloys having different stacking fault energy (SFE). It is interesting to discover that nanograins (NGs) can be produced in superalloy deformed at high temperature and relatively low strain rate. With the decrease of SFE and strain rate, the formation of NGs will be promoted obviously, leading to the negative strain rate sensitiv- ity of compressive stress in superalloys. The NGs, generated through the fragmentation of subgrains by microtwins, may become a candidate choice for dynamically strengthening superalloys at high temperature.

In mate- rials with highly twinned structure characterized by thin T/M lamellae, shear bands may develop to accommodate strain, which results in the transformation of the T/M lamellar structure to NGs  [9] . It is interesting to  ﬁ nd that when deformed at extremely high strain rate and low tem- perature, Inconel 600 superalloy can also be re ﬁ ned to nanoscale with the interaction of mechanical microtwins (MTs) and dislocations  [10] . However, practical application of this NG in the mechanical property may be restricted by its instability and coarsening at high temperature. It is meaningful if we can  ﬁ nd the way producing NGs that is thermally stable and available for strengthening superalloys at high temperatures. This study will give an introduction for the formation of thermally stable NGs and its effect on compressive stress in newly developed superalloys.  

Ni-base superalloys are widely used in the turbine blades and discs of aircraft engines and industrial gas turbines for their superior combi- nation of mechanical properties, such as good high-temperature strength, excellent fatigue properties, high oxidation resistance at ser- vice temperatures  [1] . Grain size plays an important role in achieving the desirable mechanical properties and optimizing hot-working pro- cess in superalloys. It has been well documented that dislocation-relat- ed grain re ﬁ ning down to nanoscale could take place in pure Fe and Ni with high SFE when processed at extremely high strain rate  [2,3] . For the medium-SFE Cu and Al, the grain size can be re ﬁ ned to submicron- scale at low strain rate  [4,5] . Generally, grain re ﬁ nement via dislocation activities arises from the strain induced evolution of dislocation con ﬁ gu- ration, from dense dislocation tangles, walls or cells to the subboundaries, and then to highly misoriented grain boundaries (GBs)  [2] .  

Two Ni-Co based disc superalloys, named as 13Co and 20Co, were chosen as the experimental materials, the nominal compositions are listed in  Table 1 . In comparison with 13Co alloy, the contents of Co and Ti elements in 20Co alloy increase by 7.1% and 1.2%, respectively, and the content of Cr element decreases by 1.4%. Other alloying ele- ments, such as Mo, Al, Fe, Zr, and C, have relatively little variation. Ac- cording to the previous studies, additions of Co and Cr are supposed to reduce the SFE, while Ti addition will increase the SFE  [11] . Considering the small discrepancy of Cr and Ti contents and their counteracting ef- fects on SFE, 20Co alloy possesses lower SFE than 13Co alloy because adding Co can effectively increase the content of electron holes, which is responsible for the decrease of SFE in Ni-Co based disc superalloys [12] . The variation of true stress on true strain of two superalloys during uniform compressive deformation is displayed in  Fig. 1 . It is clear that the  ﬂ ow stress of two alloys show distinct response to strain rate. For in- stance, at the strain of 0.3, deformation at higher strain rate leads to compressive stress increasing by 49 MPa in 13Co alloy, while it will make the compressive stress decrease by 40 MPa in 20Co alloy. When subjected to deformation at 10 s − 1 , the  ﬂ ow stress of 13Co alloy at the strain of 0.3 is about 39 MPa higher than 20Co alloy while  ﬂ ow stress in 13Co alloy is 50 MPa lower than 20Co alloy when the strain rate de- creases to 10 − 2   s − 1 . One remarkable feature can be found that the  ﬂ ow stress only increases by little or even decreases with the increase of strain rate, which is distinct from the previous results that compressive stress will be improved obviously with increasing strain rate in superal- loys  [13,14] . The particular changes of stress with strain rate and chem- ical compositions may be related to the underlying deformation micromechanisms of two superalloys. The heat-treated microstructures in two superalloys are shown in the inset. It can be found that primary γ ′  ( γ p ′ ) and secondary  γ ′  ( γ s ′ ) precipitates are coherently embedded in the  γ  matrix of two superalloys. With the increase of Co content, the shape of primary  γ ′  gradually evolves into a sphere from the original cube. Previous studies proved that the total volume of  γ ′  precipitates calculated by Thermo-Calc software nearly remains unchanged as the increase of Co content in Ni-Co based superalloys  [15] .

In 20Co alloy deformed at 10 s − 1 , apart from the well-developed DTs and DCs in  γ  channel, SFs in the same {111} plane are prevalent within the  γ p ′  precipitates ( Fig. 3 a). These SFs nucleated at one side of the  γ p ′ interface, propagated forward to the other side. High resolution TEM ob- servation demonstrates that the intrinsic nature of these SFs is superlattice extrinsic SFs ( Fig. 3 b). The difference in the site of forming SF between 13Co and 20Co alloys can be rationalized that decreasing the SFE of alloy will enhance the cross slip, climb of matrix dislocations [16] , so dislocations in 13Co alloy can bypass  γ p ′  phase without great re- sistance, while they are prone to get trapped by the  γ p ′  in 20Co alloy due to their low  ﬂ exibility overcoming barriers. With the accumulation of dislocations, the leading dislocation of the pile-up may dissociate into partial dislocations, these partials can shear across the  γ p ′  in 20Co alloy, leaving isolated SFs in the  γ p ′ . When the strain rate was reduced to 10 − 2   s − 1 , the formation of NGs are greatly enhanced, NGs distribute all over the observed region, rather than only restricted to the shear band zone as 13Co alloy ( Fig. 3 c). The magni ﬁ ed image in  Fig. 3 d showed the polygonal shape of NGs, revealing their mature feature as a NG.  

In order to  ﬁ gure out the underlying formation mechanism of NGs, lots of TEM observations were carried out to capture the evolution pro- cedure of NGs. An original subgrain containing several nano-sized blocks is shown in  Fig. 4 a, in which four blocks with small misorienta- tion (named as a – d) and two blocks with high misorientation (named as e, f) can be clearly distinguished. Further observation reveals that these blocks can be produced by the intersection and encirclement of SFs and MTs ( Fig. 4 b). As the plastic strain increases, these blocks may gradually transform into nanoscale subgrains or NGs because twin boundaries can transfer into the GBs of the block by remnant disloca- tions from the de-twinning of thin MTs as reported by Cao et al.  [17] . The polycrystalline diffraction pattern is presented in the inset, along with some imperfect diffraction spots of MTs due to the strain induced lattice distortion. These grains border upon each other with polygonal boundaries, meanwhile the MT or SF shears across the whole grain ( Fig. 4 c). For a NG, the intersection of MT or SF can further re ﬁ ne the grain size, meanwhile the generation of numerous MT/SF also plays a complementary role in accommodating plastic strain ( Fig. 4 d).  

Considering the above results, it can be inferred that the deformation mechanisms of two superalloys are dependent on the strain rate and SFE. Decreasing SFE and strain rate can both promote the formation of MT and NG since the variation of SFE will greatly affect the dissociation of dislocations in  γ  matrix. It is convinced that decreasing the SFE of two superalloys by adding Co element can hinder the cross-slip of disloca- tions, promote the decorrelation of a/2 〈 110 〉 matrix dislocations into Shockley partials, and facilitate the initiation of SFs and MTs in the matrix  [18] . For instance, in superalloys with relatively low SFE, abundant MTs can be introduced under the plastic deformation at high temperature and low strain rate  [19,20] . It has been veri ﬁ ed that deformation twinning in the precipitation strengthening superalloys occurs through a diffusion-controlled reordering within the  γ ′  precipi- tates after the pairwise passage of identical a/6 〈 112 〉 Shockley partial dislocation shearing across both  γ  and  γ ′  on adjacent {111} planes  [21, 22] . Pseudo twins will form after the overlapping of four consecutive complex SF (CSF) on {111} planes and transform into a true MT after the atom reordering. Therefore, under the strain rate of 10 s − 1 , the diffu- sion-controlled atom reordering will be suppressed both in 13Co and 20Co alloys, only leaving the SFs in  γ  matrix or  γ ′  particles. In contrast, when the strain rate is reduced to 10 − 2   s − 1 , atom reordering is then ac- tivated within the overlapped CSFs, leading to the incessant evolution of CSFs into MTs.  

The important role played by the MT in the re ﬁ nement of subgrain into NG re ﬂ ects that the grain re ﬁ nement is controlled by the formation of MT, which can be facilitated by decreasing the SFE of tested alloys. It can be inferred that the formation of NG depends on whether MT exist in deformed microstructure, meanwhile the proportion of NGs is also correlated to the content of MT in two alloys. For instance, NGs can only be observed after compression at low strain rate forming MTs, moreover the quantity of NGs increases with twinnability induced by decreasing SFE. Dislocation-mediated slip is also important for NG for- mation because it serves as the precursor for nucleating the subgrains that re ﬁ ne original coarse grains and supply Shockley partials for twin- ning  [23] . The dynamic introduction of NGs during compressive defor- mation probably acts as strong obstacles for dislocation motion, resulting in dispersion strengthening in materials. For 13Co alloy with higher SFE, only small amount of NGs is introduced at 10 − 2   s − 1 , the strengthening effect is mostly offset by the drawback of strain rate. When deformed at 10 s − 1 , the increased dislocation content and SF for- mation can both contribute to the strain hardening, leading to the slight increase of  ﬂ ow stress in 13Co alloy  [24] . By contrast, the nucleated SFs in 20Co are all con ﬁ ned to  γ ′  phase having weak impact on stress. When deforming 20Co alloy at 10 − 2   s − 1 , much more NGs are produced rela- tive to 13Co alloy, the  ﬂ ow stress is increased as well. It is revealed that compressive stress at relatively low strain rate can be enhanced ap- parently through decreasing the SFE, introducing certain quantity of NGs to produce dynamic dispersion strengthening in the Ni-Co based superalloys. So, SFE imposes an indirect effect on the formation of NGs, with the decrease of SFE, the twinnability of subgrains can be en- hanced, which will facilitate the subdivision and fragmentation of subgrains into NGs correspondingly. Decreasing SFE to introduce the dynamical NG strengthening may be suitable to be expanded to other superalloys having controllable SFE and certain plastic deformation ca- pacity, which needs further study in the future.  

In summary, the present results lead us to propose a formation mechanism of NGs that operates through the re ﬁ nement of subgrains by MTs. It is interesting to  ﬁ nd that abundant NGs can be introduced in the low-SFE superalloys by compressive deformation at high temper- ature and low strain rate, which will increase the  ﬂ ow stress in superal- loys. NGs are produced following the sequence of 1) formation of subgrains from dislocation tangles and cells, 2) fragmentation of subgrains down to nanoscale blocks by MTs, 3) development of these blocks into NGs. The formation of NGs is related to the formation of MTs, which is an atom diffusion controlled thermal activation process. Decreasing the SFE and strain rate can promote the formation of MTs, which therefore leads to the increase of the proportion of NGs  ﬁ nally.
