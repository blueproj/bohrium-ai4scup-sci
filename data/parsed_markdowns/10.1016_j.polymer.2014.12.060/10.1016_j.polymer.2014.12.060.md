# ABSTRACT  

We report the synthesis, optoelectronic properties, and photovoltaic properties of a donor-acceptor  low-band-gap polymer, poly(nap h th obis thi a diaz ole-triphenylamine) [poly(NTD-TPA)], which  consists of TPA as a donor unit and NTD as a stronger acceptor than b enzo thi a diaz ole. This polymer  ACCEPTED had an absorption edge at long wavelengths up to 610 nm. The optimized bulk-heterojunction (BHJ)  organic photovoltaic (OPV) cell using poly(NTD-TPA) and [6,6]-phenyl C71 butyric acid methyl  ester (PC 70 −2 , an open-circuit voltage  V oc  of 0.79  V, a ﬁll factor of 0.40, and a power conversion efficiency of 2.15%. The validities of these  photovoltaic properties are discussed using the incident-photon-to-current conversion efficiency, film  morphology, field-effect hole mobility, and electroluminescence (EL) spectrum from the charge  transfer states between the highest occupied molecular orbital of poly(NTD-TPA) and the lowest  BM. In particular, the  V oc  estimated from the current  density−voltage curve agreed well with that estimated from the peak energy of the EL spectra.  

# 1. Introduction  

In recent years, solution-processable organic photovoltaic (OPV) cells have received much  attention because of their many advantages such as low cost, light weight, ease of large-scale  fabrication, roll-to-roll printing techniques, and feasibility of flexible devices [1,2]. The most widely  used active layer for OPVs, the so-called bulk heterojunction (BHJ), is based on blending of  electron-accepting and electron-donating materials [3–5]. The BHJ significantly increased the  performance of OPVs based on polymer donors and fullerene acceptors if the morphology of BHJ  layer was optimized [6–8], and BHJ OPVs have exhibited a power conversion efficiency (PCE) of  more than 9% under standard illumination conditions (AM 1.5G) [9–11]. The development of novel  π -conjugated polymers as donors for BHJ OPV cells has contributed to significant improvements in  the PCE. For example, low-band-gap polymers are obtained with an alternating electronic  donor-acceptor (D-A) structure along the backbone[12,13]. The presence of D-A units on one chain  creates a push-pull effect that can be excited by lower-energy photons, leading to low-band-gap  photovoltaic materials [14–19]. Recent years have seen the emergence of an approach to designing  D-A polymers based on the use of triphenylamine (TPA) or triarylamine as donor units [20–29].  These TPA-based polymers present a number of speciﬁc advantages owing to their excellent thermal  and electrochemical stability, electron-donating ability, hole transport properties, and high absorption  coefﬁcients [30].  

Along these lines, we have developed the amorphous D-A polymer poly(b enzo thi a diaz ole-TPA)  MANUSCRIPT [poly(BTD-TPA)], which consists of TPA as a donor unit and BTD as an acceptor, for BHJ OPV  cells [24,25]. However, its optical band gap (absorption edge) in the film state was 2.21 eV (561 nm).   Thus, in this study, we developed the amorphous TPA-based polymer with a more red-shifted onset  of absorption by introducing the acceptor unit nap h th obis thi a diaz ole (NTD) [31,32] as shown in Fig.  1. Compared with BTD, NTD has an enlarged planar aromatic structure (bulky  -conjugated group)  containing two BTDs and a strong electron-withdrawing nature [33–37], which can lower the band  gap of poly(BTD-TPA). As we expected, poly(NTD-TPA), had an absorption edge at longer  wavelengths, up to 610 nm (an optical band gap of 2.03 eV), than poly(BTD-TPA). The effect of  bulky  π -conjugated groups between TPA units in TPA-based copolymers on optoelectronic properties  has also been reported in previous papers [30]. In view of this effect, poly(chrysene-TPA)  [poly(CR-TPA)], which consists of TPA and CR (similar in shape to NTD), was also used as the  

In this paper, we report the synthesis, optical properties, and photovoltaic properties of  ACCEPTED poly(NTD-TPA) with a comparative study of the related polymers mentioned above. Highly  crystalline polymers using NTD units for fabricating BHJ OPVs have already been reported in recent  papers [33–37], but there is no report on an amorphous polymer using NTD units for fabricating BHJ  OPVs. Generally, absorbance in a thin film of a crystalline polymer much changes depending on the  degree of crystallization [5]. On the other hand, by using amorphous polymers, we can simply  discuss optical band gaps in terms of highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and lowest  unoccupied   orbital (LUMO) energy levels of the materials.

2.4. Fabrication and characterization of organic field-effect transistors (OFETs)  

To estimate the hole mobilities of the TPA-based polymers, we fabricated and characterized  OFETs with a top-contact geometry. A glass/Au gate electrode/Parylene-C insulator substrate was  MANUSCRIPT prepared according to previously reported methods [39]. The Parylene-C layer was deposited with  thicknesses ranging from 1173 to 1669 nm. The dielectric capacitance of the insulating layer per unit  area ranged from 2.41 to 1.70 nF cm −2 . The  o -dichlorobenzene ( o -DCB) solution onto the Parylene-C layer. The coated substrate was then  transferred to a N 2 -filled glove box where it was dried for 10 min at 110 electrodes were thermally evaporated onto the substrates through shadow masks. The channel length  and width were fixed at 75  µ using a Keithley 2636A System. The OFET parameters were calculated as the average of the  measured results for four OFETs. Although the OFET measuring provides information on carrier  mobility in the direction parallel to thin films, these measurements are helpful for estimating  mobilities perpendicular to the substrate and interpreting the behavior of OPVs, in the case of the  amorphous films of poly(CR-TPA) or poly(NTD-TPA) in which the main chains are three  dimensionally structure extended at the central amine and isotropic carrier transports are realized. In  fact, Veres et al. reported the field-effect hole mobility of amorphous organic semiconductors such as  poly tri arya mines (PTAA) was very close to the bulk time-of-flight mobility [40].

The OPV cells were fabricated in the following configuration: indium tin oxide  ACCEPTED (ITO)/PEDOT:PSS/BHJ layer/LiF/Al. The patterned ITO (conductivity: 10  Ω /square) glass was  precleaned in an ultrasonic bath of acetone and ethanol and then treated in an ultraviolet-ozone  chamber. A thin layer (40 nm) of PEDOT:PSS was spin-coated onto the ITO at 3000 rpm and  air-dried at 110 C for 10 min on a hot plate. The substrate was then transferred to a N 2 -filled glove  box where it was re-dried at 110 C for 10 min on a hot plate. An  o -DCB solution of the TPA-based  polymers and PC BM blended in 1:4 weight ratios was subsequently spin-coated onto the  PEDOT:PSS surface to form the BHJ layer. The substrates with the BHJ layers were dried for 10 min  at 110 ° C for the spin-cast film. LiF (1 nm) and Al (80 nm) were then deposited onto the active layer  by conventional thermal evaporation at a chamber pressure lower than 5 × 10 −4  Pa, which yielded  devices with an active area of 2 × 2 mm 2 . The thicknesses of the BHJ and PEDOT:PSS layers were  measured using an automatic microfigure measuring instrument (SURFCORDER ET200, Kosaka  Laboratory, Ltd.). The current density–voltage ( J – V ) curves were measured using an ADCMT 6244  DC voltage current source/monitor under AM 1.5G solar-simulated light irradiation of 100 mW cm −2   (OTENTO-SUN III, Bunkoh-Keiki Co., Ltd.). The OPV parameters were calculated as the average  of the measured results for four OPVs. The incident-photon-to-current conversion efficiency (IPCE)  was measured using an SM-250 system (Bunkoh-Keiki Co., Ltd.).  

2.6. Fabrication and characterization of organic light-emitting diodes (OLEDs)  

The OLEDs were fabricated in the following configuration: ITO/PEDOT:PSS/polymer or  PC 70 BM or polymer:PC 70 BM layer/LiF/Al. The OLEDs were fabricated by the same method as the  BHJ OPVs. By applying a positive bias voltage to the ITO electrode with respect to the Al, both  electrons from the Al and holes from the ITO can be injected into the OLEDs (BHJ OPVs). The  resultant electroluminescence (EL) spectra from the OLEDs (BHJ OPVs) were measured using an  array spectrometer (MCPD-9800-311C, Otsuka Electronics Co., Ltd.). The spectrometer was  calibrated in wavelength and intensity.

3.2. Optical properties and field-effect hole mobilities  

The optical properties of poly(NTD-TPA) were compared with those of poly(CR-TPA) to  investigate the effect of the strong acceptor nature of NTD. Unlike poly(CR-TPA), poly(NTD-TPA)  exhibits broad absorption at long wavelengths ( λ max  = 553 nm) due to intramolecular charge transfer  (CT) from TPA donors to NTD acceptors [Fig. 2(a)]. The absorbance of the poly:PC 70 BM (1:4) blend  film is also larger between 450 and 700 nm [Fig. 2(b)]. The absorption edges in the neat films were  at 610 and 420 nm, which correspond to optical band gaps of 2.03 eV and 2.95 eV for  poly(NTD-TPA) and poly(CR-TPA), respectively. The HOMO energy levels of poly(NTD-TPA) and  poly(CR-TPA) were determined to be −5.40 and −5.55 eV, respectively. The absorption edge in the  film state was at 561 nm (a band gap of 2.21 eV) for poly(BTD-TPA). Therefore, we successfully  developed TPA-based polymers with a more red-shifted onset of absorption by introducing the strong  acceptor unit NTD instead of the typical acceptor BTD.

# 3.3. Photovoltaic properties  

Figure 4(a) shows the   curves for the optimized BHJ OPVs using poly(NTD-TPA) or  poly(CR-TPA), and Table 2 lists the average device characteristics of four devices, including the  short-circuit current density  J sc , open-circuit voltage  V oc , ﬁll factor (FF), and PCE under illumination  with 100 mW cm −2  of AM 1.5G. BHJ OPVs using poly(NTD-TPA) always showed higher PCEs than  these using poly(CR-TPA). The PCEs of the BHJ OPVs using poly(NTD-TPA) and poly(CR-TPA)  were both decreased by making the BHJ layer thicker. The decrease was caused mainly by a decrease  in the FF, which is ascribed to relatively low hole mobilities in amorphous polymers compared to  crystalline polymers or increased photocurrent recombination in the thicker BHJ layer [25,42,43].  The optimized BHJ OPV cell using poly(NTD-TPA) had higher IPCEs than the cell using  poly(CR-TPA), as shown in Fig. 4(b), because the absorbance of the poly(NTD-TPA):PC 70 BM at  wavelengths of 450–650 nm exceeded that of poly(CR-TPA):PC 70 BM, as shown in Fig. 2(b). To  accentuate the absorption peak at the wavelength of 553 nm due to intramolecular CT from TPA  donors to NTD acceptors in the poly(NTD-TPA) [Fig. 2(a)], BHJ OPVs with PC BM that has not  absorption at visible wavelengths were also fabricated, characterized, and summarized in Fig. S1 and  Table S1 in Supporting Information. The enhanced absorption of AM 1.5G solar-simulated light led  to the higher  J sc  in the BHJ OPVs based on poly(NTD-TPA) than in those based on poly(CR-TPA).

The  V oc  values of OPVs based on poly(CR-TPA) (HOMO of −5.55 eV) should be higher than  those of OPVs based on poly(NTD-TPA) (HOMO of −5.40 eV), because the  V oc  of BHJ cells using  PC 70 BM as an acceptor is determined mainly by the energy difference between the lowest  70 BM (−3.76 eV) [44] and the HOMO level of  V oc  in this study were almost the same. Thus,  to obtain detailed information on these  V oc  values, EL spectra from the CT states between the HOMO  level of the polymer and the LUMO level of PC 70 BM in the BHJ OPVs were measured. The peak  energy of CT EL spectra reportedly has an empirical relationship with  V oc  expressed as  V oc  (V) = CT  EL peak energy (eV) − 0.4 [48]. Figure 5 shows the EL spectra from (a) OLEDs with  single-component layers and (b) BHJ OPVs with polymer:PC 70 BM blended layers. Completely  different EL spectra are observed in Figs. 5(a) and (b). Thus, we can conclude that the EL spectra of  BHJ OPVs originate from the CT emission in the polymer/PC 70 BM interface.

# 4. Conclusions  

In this study, we designed the TPA-based amorphous polymer by introducing the acceptor unit  NTD, which is a stronger acceptor than BTD, and reported the synthesis, optoelectronic properties,  and photovoltaic properties of poly(NTD-TPA). For a comparative study of these properties,  poly(CR-TPA), which consists of TPA and CR (similar in shape to NTD), was used as the reference  material. Poly(NTD-TPA) had an absorption edge at longer wavelengths, up to 610 nm, than  poly(BTD-TPA) (561 nm) and poly(CR-TPA) (420 nm). The optimized BHJ OPVs using  poly(NTD-TPA):PC 70 BM [poly(CR-TPA):PC 70 BM] exhibited a  J sc  of 6.87 (6.02) mA cm −2 , a  V oc  of  0.79 (0.76) V, and a PCE of 2.15% (1.52%). The optimized BHJ OPV cell using poly(NTD-TPA)  exhibited higher IPCEs than the cell using poly(CR-TPA) because the absorbance of  poly(NTD-TPA):PC 70 BM at wavelengths of 450–650 nm exceeded that of poly(CR-TPA):PC 70 BM.
