The microstructure of a new Ni-base oxide dispersion strengthened superalloy powder was studied for high temperature gas turbine applications after the mechanical alloying process. In this study, an atomized powder with a composition similar to the CMSX-10 superalloy was mechanically alloyed with yttria and Hf powders. The mechanically alloyed powder included only the supersaturated solid solution  γ  phase without  γ ′  and yttria provided by severe plastic deformation, while after the 3-step aging, the  γ ′  phase was precipitated due to the partitioning of Al and Ta to the  γ ′  and Co, Cr, Re, W, and Mo to the  γ  phase. Mechanical alloying modi ﬁ ed the morphology of  γ ′  to the new coherent  γ – γ ′  nanoscale lamellar structure to minimize the elastic strain energy of the precipitation, which yielded a low lattice mis ﬁ t of 0.16% at high temperature. The  γ ′  lamellae aligned preferentially along the elastically soft [100] direction. Also, the precipitated oxide particles were re ﬁ ned in the  γ  phase by adding Hf from large incoherent YAlO 3  to  ﬁ ne semi-coherent Y 2 Hf 2 O 7  oxide particles with the average size of 7 nm and low interparticle spacing of 76 nm.

In order to reach a superior pinning effect, it is necessary to reduce the interparticle spacing through the distribution of  ﬁ ne oxide nanoparti- cles  [13,14] . Commercially available mechanically alloyed (MAed) ODS superalloys such as MA6000 consist of different incoherent Y-Al oxide particles including YA1 2 0 9 , Y 3 Al 5 O 12 , and YAlO 3  [15] . However, earlier studies in our group demonstrated that the addition of Hf is effective to re ﬁ ne oxide particles by changing the composition to Y 2 Hf 2 O 7  in Ni-base ODS superalloys  [16] .  

# 1. Introduction  

Ni-base oxide dispersion strengthened (ODS) superalloys are promising materials for gas turbine applications due to their excellent high temperature properties  [1 – 4] . Recent studies have investigated the effect of microstructure on high temperature properties of conven- tional ODS superalloys such as MA6000 and PM 3030  [5 – 8] . They achieve high temperature capability especially creep resistance by both ordered gamma-prime ( γ ′ ) precipitation strengthening at intermediate temperatures and oxide dispersion strengthening at high temperatures. The morphology and distribution of the  γ ′  precipitates inside the  γ  matrix have been found to in ﬂ uence the high temperature properties strongly  [9] . The development of  γ ′  morphology is governed by the interfacial and elastic strain energies  [10] . As the volume fraction of  γ ′  increases, the elastic strain energy plays a more dominant role over interfacial energy, thus stabilizing the morphology of the precipitates [11] . Ricks et al. have observed that the morphology of  γ ′  often evolves from spherical to cuboidal shape to arrays of cubes during aging time due to elastic strains associated with lattice mis ﬁ t  [12] . Besides, the control of oxide dispersion is an effective way to improve the high temperature strengthening through the pinning of moving dislocations.  

This study aims to characterize the microstructure of a newly developed MAed Ni-base ODS superalloy with a composition similar to CMSX-10 superalloy combined with oxide particles in view of the morphology of  γ ′  precipitation and oxide particle re ﬁ nement, which leads to superior high temperature mechanical properties.  

# 2. Materials and experiments  

An atomized superalloy powder with a composition similar to CMSX-10 was mixed with Hf and Y 2 O 3  powders. The powder mixture was mechanically alloyed in Ar atmosphere using an attrition type ball mill for 48 h. The rotation speed was 200 rpm with a ball to powder ratio of 10:1. The composition of the MAed powder is shown in Table 1 . Using this chemical composition, solvus and solidus tempera- tures of the new Ni-base ODS superalloy were calculated by means of Pandat code. To perform the annealing treatment, the MAed powder was capsuled in a silica tube and sealed in vacuum.  

![Table 1 Chemical composition of the Ni-base ODS superalloy (wt.%). ](images/01b8395bff8c8a5a1bd12fdfb84232f3344a3d175fbdc8d5cc19ffe8b8110a46.jpg)  

In order to get elemental mapping and point analyses, the cross- section of the powder and microstructures was observed by JEOL JXA-8530F  ﬁ eld emission scanning electron microscopy equipped with ﬁ eld emission electron probe micro-analyzer (FE-EPMA). In addition, X-ray diffraction was conducted by means of Cu-K α  radiation (0.154 nm) with a Philips X'Pert PRO to specify the phases and compo- sition of oxide particles. A JEOL JEM-2010 transmission electron micros- copy was used to study the microstructure in high magni ﬁ cation with an acceleration voltage of 200 kV. The samples for TEM observation were prepared by focused ion beam (FIB) (JEOL JIB-4600F) and then electropolished in a solution consisting of 80% C 2 H 5 OH + 20% H 2 SO 4 . The oxide particle size distributions were measured with MacView software based on the TEM micrographs. It is noted that the oxide particle size distributions were calculated from at least 500 particles to get a statistical analysis. In addition, after different annealing conditions, the Vickers hardness of the powders was measured by a HMV-Micro- Hardness Tester, Shimadzu, under a load of 980 mN and dwell time of 10 – 20 s. Ten indentations were performed for each powder condition and the average value was considered as the micro-hardness value.

# 3.3.  γ ′  morphology  

Fig. 8 (a) shows an elongated  γ  grain between some  γ ′  grains with a length of about 1.5  μ m (typical square indicated in  Fig. 6 ). It is clear that there are many spheres of  γ ′  precipitated along this grain. These spheres of  γ ′  are the same dark  ﬁ ne spots in  Fig. 6 , which have dispersed in all  γ grains. The  γ ′  spheres with the diameter of 50 – 80 nm in addition to the egg-like  γ ′  grain can be observed in  Fig. 8 (b) (typical square indicated in Fig. 6 ) with a distinct morphology of  lamellar structure . Also, the diffrac- tion pattern con ﬁ rms coherency in the  γ / γ ′  interfaces as expected from the low lattice mis ﬁ t.  Fig. 9 (a) (typical square indicated in  Fig. 8 (a)) illustrates the high magni ﬁ cation of the  γ – γ ′  lamellar structure in the γ ′  grains in the 3-step aged Ni-base ODS superalloy. The thickness of the dark  γ ′  lamella is about 5 nm with the  γ  interlamella up to 10 nm.

The SADP near [110] direction in  Fig. 9 (b) expresses that the alignment of  γ ′  lamellae is in [100] direction of the  γ  matrix. Since this direction is the elastically soft direction of the Ni solid solution phase  [23] , the preferential orientation of the coherent  γ ′  lamellae reduces the free energy change of the  γ ′  precipitation through a decrease in the elastic strain energy.  

The directional coarsening of  γ ′  with high volume fraction has been previously observed under the application of external stress in Ni-base single crystals  [24 – 28] . In this study, the contribution of strain energy introduced by severe plastic deformation during mechanical alloying is drastic to raise potential nucleation sites for the  γ ′  precipitation [29] . Also, according to our observations and previous studies  [26,30] , the amount of the  γ ′  precipitation in CMSX-10 superalloy is consider- able (70 – 80 vol.%). Therefore, the development of the  γ ′  morphology progresses as the nanoscale lamellar structure in the preferred elastically soft [100] direction in order to accommodate the elastic strains (i.e. the mechanical alloying process modi ﬁ es the  γ ′  morphology into the nanolamellar structure to minimize the elastic strain energy of the  γ ′ precipitation). On the other hand, the  γ  grains consist of lower volume fractions of the  γ ′  precipitates. Therefore, the minimum interfacial energy associated with spherical shape results in the formation of the lamellar structure of  γ ′  in separate spheres.  

# 3.4. Precipitation of oxide particles  

Fig. 10  shows the X-ray analyses of the MAed Ni-base ODS superalloy powders after annealing at 400 – 1300 °C for 1 h. There is no peak of oxide particles in the MAed condition suggesting the decomposition of yttria particles. However, it can be observed that the Y 2 Hf 2 O 7  oxide particles appear to precipitate after annealing around 1000 °C. In addition, as indicated in  Fig. 5 , the hardness of the annealed powder at 1000 °C for 1 h increased slightly due to the oxide precipitation.  Fig. 11  shows the X-ray analyses of oxide particles in the 3-step aged Ni-base ODS superalloy powder. The principle peaks are attributed to the formation of Y 2 Hf 2 O 7 , and another small peak may correspond to YAlO 3  particles.  

Y 2 O 3  has been reported to be unstable during the mechanical alloying process and it dissociates into Y and O  [14,31,32] . Also, atmospheric contamination during mechanical alloying has happened due to about 0.5 wt.% extra oxygen available in the powder composition (estimated from the total oxygen content minus the oxygen coupled with Y 2 O 3  [33] ). Then, during the heat treatment process, precipitation of new oxide particles such as Y 2 Hf 2 O 7  (or YAlO 3 ) can occur by adding Hf (or Al). However, Hf has a lower Gibbs free energy of oxide formation than that of Al at high temperatures  [1] . Thus,  ﬁ rst Y 2 Hf 2 O 7  forms and the remaining Y and O will react with Al to precipitate YAlO 3 .

Tang  [1]  has proved that the addition of Hf to Ni-base super- alloys re ﬁ nes the oxide particles by promoting the coherency with the matrix. Therefore, it can be supposed that  ﬁ ne particles have the Y – Hf oxide composition and that large particles are Y – Al oxide particles. As it was previously explained,  γ ′ precipitates by the partitioning of elements at lower temperatures (about 500 – 600 °C) compared to the precipitation temperatures of oxide particles (about 1000 °C). It has been reported that Y and Hf partition to the  γ  phase  [23] . Therefore, the  γ ′  precipitates are considered to become depleted in Y and Hf, and there is no Hf and Y in the  γ ′  phase to precipitate Y 2 Hf 2 O 7  oxide particles at high temperatures.  

# 4. Conclusions  

In this study, a new Ni-base ODS superalloy powder was manufactured by the mechanical alloying process. The results showed that the 3-step aged Ni-base ODS superalloy powder involved a new kind of very coherent nanoscale  γ – γ ′  lamellar structure morphology modi ﬁ ed by mechanical alloying with a low lattice mis ﬁ t of 0.16% at high temperature. The  γ ′  lamellae aligned in the elastically soft [100] direction to minimize the elastic strain energy of the  γ ′  precipitation. Also, the addition of Hf re ﬁ ned the oxide particles from large incoherent YAlO 3  with the average size of \~28 nm and the interparticle spacing of 252 nm to  ﬁ ne semi-coherent Y 2 Hf 2 O 7  with the average size of 7 nm and the interparticle spacing of 76 nm.
