# Microstructural evolution of the heat a ﬀ ected zone of a Co – Ti – W alloy upon laser cladding with a CoNiCrAlY coating  

Jaewook Han a , Boryung Yoo a , Hye Ji Im a , Chang-Seok Oh b , Pyuck-Pa Choi a ,  

a  Department of Materials Science and Engineering, Korea Advanced Institute of Science and Technology (KAIST), 291 Daehak-ro, Yuseong-gu, Daejeon, 34141, Republic of Korea b  Materials Modeling and Characterization Department, Korea Institute of Materials Science (KIMS), Changwon, 51508, Republic of Korea  

# A R T I C L E I N F O  

# A B S T R A C T  

We report on the microstructural evolution of the heat a ﬀ ected zone of a  γ / γ ′ -strengthened Co – Ti – W alloy upon laser cladding with a CoNiCrAlY coating. Using electron microscopy and atom probe tomography we observed two distinct heat a ﬀ ect zones, namely  γ  islands containing  γ ′  precipitate ( ∼ 45 nm in size) colonies and heat a ﬀ ected  γ / γ ′  regions outside the  γ  islands comprising  ﬁ ne  γ ′  precipitates ( ∼ 25 nm in size). Based on the ex- perimental data and thermodynamic calculations, we propose a model for the formation of these two heat a ﬀ ected zones and discuss the di ﬀ erences in the observed precipitate sizes.  

Keywords: Laser cladding Heat a ﬀ ected zone Superalloy Overlay coating Atom probe tomography

However, during laser cladding, the high-power laser beam can not only a ﬀ ect the powder deposit but also the base metal and lead to the formation of a heat a ﬀ ected zone (HAZ) and a partially melted zone (PMZ) [ 13 ]. Microstructural changes in HAZ and PMZ are often detri- mental to the high-temperature mechanical properties [ 14 , 15 ]. More- over, grain boundary liquation and cracking due to a eutectic reaction in the HAZ and PMZ was reported for welded Ni-base superalloys [ 16 ] as well as high-Mn steels [ 17 ].  

Recently, there has been a growing interest in Co-based alloys with a two-phase  γ / γ ′  microstructure (L1 2  ordered  γ ′  precipitates in a dis- ordered fcc  γ  matrix) owing to their excellent high-temperature strength and hot corrosion resistance [ 1 ]. Among the reported Co-based systems, Co – Ti is the only known binary alloy which exhibits a two- phase region comprising a thermodynamic ally stable  γ ′  and  γ  phase [ 2 , 3 ]. By adding W to a Co – Ti base alloy, Yoo et al. [ 4 ] obtained an increased solvus temperature (1144 °C) and a reduced  γ / γ ’  lattice mis ﬁ t (0.69%), making Co – Ti – W alloys promising for high-temperature ap- plications.  

Numerous reports exist on the phase formation and stability of  γ / γ ′ - strengthened Co-based superalloys [ 18 – 22 ]. However, to the best of our knowledge, no reports exist on the evolution of HAZ and PMZ of  γ / γ ′ - strengthened Co-based superalloys upon laser cladding or welding, though they may strongly a ﬀ ect the high-temperature properties of these alloys. To address this issue, we deposited an overlay coating on a Co – Ti – W alloy through laser cladding and investigated the resulting microstructure of the HAZ and PMZ in the Co – Ti – W base metal using advanced characterization techniques such as electron microscopy and atom probe tomography. The coating material was a CoNiCrAlY [ 5 , 23 ] (not to be confused with the high-entropy alloys [ 24 – 26 ] studied in- tensively in recent years) alloy, widely used for enhancing the oxidation resistance of superalloys.  

However, Co – Ti – W alloys are prone to oxidation and corrosion since they do not contain any protective oxide forming elements such as Cr or Al. Thus, an oxidation-resistant coating of superior quality is re- quired. MCrAlY (M = Co, Ni) overlay coatings deposited by thermal spraying are typically used to protect a superalloy from high-tempera- ture oxidation. However, thermal spraying is reported to lead to in- ternal oxidation and pore formation, which can deteriorate the per- formance of the coating [ 5 , 6 ]. Laser cladding, which is based on powder deposition and melting using a laser, is an attractive alternative method for deposition of MCrAlY coatings [ 7 – 10 ]. This method can produce dense layers with strong metallurgical bonding to the substrate [ 11 ]. Ansari et al. [ 12 ] reported outstanding oxidation resistance and good scale adhesion of the IN738 alloy laser cladded with MCrAlY even at 1100 °C, which was attributed to the superior quality of the MCrAlY  

# 2. Experimental  

A Co – 11Ti – 3W alloy (at.%) fabricated in a vacuum arc melting furnace and subjected to a homogenization treatment at 1170 °C for 24 h and an aging treatment at 900 °C for 24 h (according to Ref. [ 4 ]) was used as a substrate for laser cladding. Gas atomized CoNiCrAlY powder (Amdry 995C provided by Oerlikon Metco) with a nominal composition of 33.47 at.% Co, 28.14 at.% Ni, 20.21 at.% Cr, 17.96 at.% Al, 0.22 at.% Y and particle sizes ranging from 45 to 90  μ m was used for laser cladding. The laser cladding experiments were done using an In- ssTek MX-mini directed energy deposition machine equipped with a ytterbium  ﬁ ber laser. A laser power of 250 W, powder feed rate of 8 g/ min, a scan velocity of 14.16 mm/s, and a hatch distance of 0.3 mm  × were used as process parameters to deposit samples of 7  7 mm 2   in area. The as-cladded samples were cut perpendicularly to the laser scan direction, mounted in resin, and polished to 1  μ m  ﬁ nish. The micro- structures of the PMZ and HAZ were examined by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) (Hitachi SU-5000) in backscattered electron (BSE) imaging mode. Focused ion beam (FIB) (Helios NanoLab 450 F1) mil- ling was used to prepare transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and atom probe tomography (APT) specimens from the PMZ and HAZ. TEM images and di ﬀ raction patterns were recorded with a FEI Talos F200X instrument operated at 200 kV and pixel values of dark- ﬁ eld TEM images were acquired through Image J software to calculate the  γ ′ volume fraction. Energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy analyses were performed in scanning TEM (STEM) mode for elemental mapping. APT analyses were performed using a CAMECA LEAP ™ 4000X HR system, installed at the Pohang University of Science and Technology (POSTECH), in pulsed laser mode at a laser pulse frequency of 200 kHz and pulse energy of 60 pJ. The specimen base temperature was kept at about 70 K. Phase diagram and equilibrium phase compositions of  γ  and γ ’  were calculated for the Co – Ti – W system using the ThermoCalc soft- ware and TCNI9 database.  

# 3. Results  

Fig. 1 (a) shows a cross-sectional BSE image of the CoNiCrAlY cladded Co – Ti – W alloy. The microstructure of the as-cladded sample comprised four regions, namely the CoNiCrAlY cladding layer on top, the PMZ with a thickness of 25  μ m, the HAZ with a thickness of 10  μ m, and the una ﬀ ected CoTiW base metal. The PMZ and HAZ could be distinguished by their compositional (see  Table S1  in supplementary material) and microstructural di ﬀ erence. While the original micro- structure of the CoTiW base metal consisted of cuboidal  γ ′  precipitates of 200 – 250 nm in size and 45 nm thick  γ  channels, the  γ ′  precipitates in the PMZ were completely dissolved. Only a cellular dendritic micro- structure could be observed within the PMZ. While most of the PMZ consisted of the  γ  phase (dendrite cores), some  γ ’  nano-precipitates enriched with Ti were found at the periphery of the dendrites through TEM di ﬀ raction and EDS analyses (see  Fig. S1  in supplementary ma- terial).  

In the HAZ, only some of the  γ ′  precipitates were dissolved and lost their cuboidal morphology, as shown in the SEM image in  Fig. 1 (b). Some of the dissolved  γ ′  precipitates and their surrounding  γ  channels transformed into islands of 3 – 8  μ m in size.  Fig. 1 (c) shows a dark- ﬁ eld TEM image of such an island region and a selected area electron dif- fraction (SAED) pattern recorded along the [001] zone axis. The SAED pattern showed di ﬀ raction spots of both  γ  and  γ ′  phase. The dark- ﬁ eld TEM image was acquired using the (100) superlattice re ﬂ ection of the L1 2  ordered  γ ′  phase. Some of the original cuboidal  γ ′  precipitates and their surrounding  γ  matrix were found to be dissolved into an island of a few micrometer in size in agreement with  Fig. 1 (b). Within the  γ  is- land a colony of  γ ′  precipitates of 40 ∼ 50 nm in size was formed (see also  Fig. S2  in supplementary material). The STEM-EDS maps in Fig. 1 (d) revealed Ti partitioning to the  γ ’  colony and W partitioning to the neighboring  γ  region, whereas Co did not show any clear parti- tioning tendency.

# 4. Discussion  

The HAZ in the laser-cladded Co – Ti – W alloy essentially comprises two regions: i.) parts of the original  γ / γ ′  microstructure dissolved into  γ islands containing  γ ′  colonies; ii.) heat a ﬀ ected  γ / γ ′  regions outside the γ  islands in which Ti has di ﬀ used from the original cuboidal  γ ′  pre- cipitates to the  γ  channels and in which newly formed  γ ′  nanoprecipi- tates are observed ( Fig. 2 (a) and (b)). This linear equation was derived by calculating the bulk composition and equilibrium  γ / γ ’  compositions using the ThermoCalc software. Although the rapid heating and cooling processes during laser cladding represent non-equilibrium conditions, we use the calculated phase diagram in  Fig. 5 (a) for a qualitative dis- cussion of the microstructural evolution of the HAZ, as it provides plausible explanations for the observed microstructures.  

As heat absorption during laser cladding is a dynamic process, the temperature distribution is not considered to be uniform at the micro- meter-scale. If a speci ﬁ c part of the  γ / γ ′  microstructure is exposed to a temperature above the  γ ′  solvus temperature, T solvus , it is expected that γ ′  precipitates will dissolve into the  γ  phase ( Fig. 5 (b) - ⅰ ). However, due to the rapid heating during laser irradiation the temperature of this heat a ﬀ ected region may reach the phase transformation temperature T p  (see Fig. 5 (a)) even before the dissolution of the  γ ′  precipitates starts, sug- gesting that the  γ ′  phase may partially undergo a phase transformation γ ’  → γ ’  +  γ  + L following the compositional line of the former  γ ′  phase. Upon further heating in  Fig. 5 (a) the residual  γ ′  dissolves according to the reaction  γ ’  +  γ  + L  → γ  + L. The formed  γ  island may enclose an island-shaped liquid phase ( Fig. 5 (b)- ⅱ ). As the heat source moves away and the cooling process begins, the liquid island inside the  γ  island solidi ﬁ es into  γ  and  γ ’  (L  → γ  +  γ ′ ). Due to the rapid cooling and limited di ﬀ usion process,  γ ’  precipitates are only of a few nanometers in size and form a colony along the trace of the liquid ( Fig. 5 (b)- ⅲ ).  

The peak temperature outside the  γ  island is lower than T p  and higher than T solvus , and thus dissolution of the  γ ′  precipitates can only occur through di ﬀ usion of Ti and W ( Fig. 5 (b)- ⅰ ). However, W remains partitioned ( Figs. 2 (b) and  5 (b)- ⅱ ) because of its sluggish di ﬀ usion ki- netics, whereas Ti becomes homogeneously distributed. Some re- searchers reported that instantaneous exposure to a temperature above T solvus  in the HAZ of precipitation-strengthened alloys leads to partial dissolution and liquation at the interface of precipitates due to a che- mical gradient [ 16 , 27 , 28 ]. However, in this study, the original  γ ′  pre- cipitates could be completely dissolved into  γ  rather than partially due to their small size ( ∼ 250 nm). As the cooling process begins, pre- cipitation of the nano-sized  γ ′  particles, as observed by APT ( Fig. 3 (b)), occurs outside the  γ  island ( Fig. 5 (b)- ⅲ ).  γ ′  precipitation also occurs in the heat a ﬀ ected  γ  region, since the Ti concentration is high due to di ﬀ usion during the heating process ( Fig. 2 (b)). The dark- ﬁ eld TEM image in  Fig. 2 (a) shows that the ordering of  γ ′  nano precipitates pre- ferentially occurs at the heat-a ﬀ ected  γ ’  region possibly due to a high local W concentration. To the best of our knowledge, there are no re- ports about the e ﬀ ects of W on the L1 2  ordering kinetics in Co-based superalloys and further research is needed to address this issue.  

The newly formed  γ ′  precipitates in the HAZ show di ﬀ erent sizes and volume fraction depending on where the reaction occurred. Referring to image analysis result ( Table 1 ) and average ESD calcula- tion ( Table 4 ), the sizes and volume fraction of precipitates were higher in the  γ ′  colonies than outside the  γ  island. The larger size of the  γ ’ precipitates in the colonies can be ascribed to the precipitation reaction, which involves a liquid phase (see  Fig. 5 (b)). Outside the  γ  island, the liquid phase does not form during the heating process and the pre- cipitation reaction occurs within the solid phase, in which atoms have relatively slow di ﬀ usion rates as compared to the liquid phase.  

# 5. Conclusion  

In this work, we investigated the microstructural evolution of the HAZ of a  γ / γ ′  forming Co – Ti – W alloy upon laser cladding with CoNiCrAlY. Some of the  γ ′  cuboidal precipitates dissolved and formed a microstructure consisting of  γ  islands and  γ ′  precipitate colonies. In the HAZ outside the  γ  islands, Ti di ﬀ usion from the original  γ ′  precipitates to the  γ  channels was detected, whereas the W distribution remained inhomogeneous due to sluggish di ﬀ usion kinetics.  γ ′  precipitates were formed by the dissolution of the original cuboidal  γ ′  precipitates and the subsequent precipitation reaction within the  γ  phase upon cooling. Precipitates in the  γ ′  colony were larger than the precipitates in the heat a ﬀ ected  γ / γ ’  region due to a higher di ﬀ usion rate in the liquid than in the solid phase.
