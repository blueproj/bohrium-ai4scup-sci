# Abstract  

In powder metallurgy Ni-based superalloys, the mechanical properties such as strength, creep  and fatigue resistance at the elevated temperature closely rely on the solution cooling rate  during heat treatment. Understanding the precipitation mechanisms upon different quenching  media can offer a bridge across cooling rate to mechanical response. To date, extensive works  have been done to construct the relationship between cooling rate and mechanical  performance only using a limited quantity of quenching media such as water, oil and air,  resulting in the inadequacy of process-microstructure-property database. In this work, a  high-throughput approach based on the Jominy end quenching technique was developed to  produce a gradient distribution of γ’ precipitates within an individual specimen by introducing  a wide range of cooling rates from 35 °C/min to 6346 °C/min. A transformation from  unimodal to bimodal γ’ Particle Size Distribution (PSD) was observed along the end  quenched specimen. The average size of γ’ precipitates is inversely proportional with cooling  rate in logarithmic scale. The hardness measurements display a strong dependence on cooling  rate, which is explained by phase field simulation to provide deeper insight into the  interaction between dislocation and γ’ precipitates.  

Keywords:  High-throughput, solution   cooling rate, Ni-based superalloys, γ’ precipitates,  hardness, phase field simulation  

# 1. Introduction  

Powder metallurgy Ni-based superalloys are widely used for gas turbine discs of aircraft  engines in virtue of their tensile strength, creep and fatigue resistance at the elevated  temperature [1,2]. These attractive high temperature mechanical properties inherently depend  on the size, volume fraction, spatial distribution and morphology of γ’ precipitates [1,3-5]. In  the past decades, the effect of solution cooling rate on the thermodynamics of γ’ precipitates  [6-14] and hardness/tensile strength [15-17] has been intensively investigated. The goal of  previous works is to establish the dependence of γ’ precipitates [8-14] and mechanical  properties [3-5,15-17] on cooling rate using multiple samples. In these literatures the choice  of cooling media is quite limited such as oil, water, air, and thus leading to the available  cooling rates are accordingly discrete in the spectrum. To achieve a better understanding of  cooling rate in affecting γ’ precipitates and mechanical strength, we expect to introduce  and/or produce a series of cooling rates in the spectrum to construct their correspondences  using specimens as less as possible. In this regard, it seems like that gradient cooling process  becomes the only option if we want to realize these two goals. Once realized, how do we  build the framework from cooling rate to mechanical strength? Progress in finite element  simulation [18] enables us to obtain cooling rates in the interested position that  correspondingly connect to γ’ precipitates measured by microscopic technique. Prediction of  mechanical strength based on microstructural features can be realized using physics-based  models, which can be verified by Vickers hardness results [3]. This method represents a shift  of paradigm or research culture [19] via overcoming drawbacks in experiment-based  trial-and-error approach.  

Motivated by this idea, we developed an approach based on the Jominy end quenching  technique [20,21], in which the end of specimen (soaked at high temperature with certain  duration) was quenched by sprayed water, to produce a series of continous solution cooling  rates, and thus gain gradient distribution of γ’ precipitates with different sizes, morphologies  and volume fractions in a polycrystalline Ni-based superalloy specimen. In the following we  presented these data, i.e. cooling rate and hardness and the associated cooling  rate-microstructure-hardness relation. Our results confirm that this high-throughput approach  can be applied for accelerating optimization of heat treatment parameters in cooling  rate-dependent structural materials, and outperforms traditional trial-and-error method with  high efficiency.

# 3. Results and discussion  

3.1 Cooling profiles through the end-quenched specimen  

Fig. 2 illustrates the schematic of interested positions for cooling rate simulation and  microstructure/mechanical characterization along the end quench bar and the gradient cooling  rate within a single specimen. The cooling history along the column is calculated using  one-dimensional unsteady heat-conduction differential equation, and the cooling rates of six  typical positions (points 1 to 6) are shown in Fig. 2b. It is found that the fastest cooling rate is  6346 °C/min at point 1 and the slowest cooling rate is 35 °C/min at point 6, which reveals the  wide range of cooling rate is achieved in an individual specimen. It should be noted that the  cooling rate is calculated using the time t spent between temperature 1180 °C and 650 °C, i.e.,  cooling rate=1180-650 °C/(t 650 -0).  

# 3.2 Microstructure observation under different cooling rates  

Fig. 3 shows the microscopic observation of γ’ precipitates that are closely related to the  position corresponding to the different cooling rates. At the bottom side of specimen, such as  points 1 and 2, the cooling rate is quite high and a unimodal PSD of γ’ precipitates with  nearly spherical morphology (Fig. 3a and 3b) are obtained, corresponding to the cooling rates  of 6346 and 951 °C/min, respectively. For point 3 in Fig. 3c, the cooling rate decreases to  171 °C/min and a bimodal PSD consisting of the secondary and tertiary γ’ precipitates (the  insert in Fig. 3c) is observed. The generation of unimodal and bimodal γ’ precipitates is  controlled by the competition mechanism between undercooling and matrix supersaturation  [12,25]. In the case of bimodal PSD, the secondary γ’ precipitates nucleate first once the  driving force in matrix reaches the critical point at a higher temperature. As the temperature is  further decreased, the first nucleation brust of γ’ is shut-off until further undercooling reaches  then the tertiary γ’ precipitates reprecipitate and as a result a bimodal distribution is generated.  In contrast, when the cooling rate is high enough, the undercooling capability available for  precipitation invariably exists resulting in the formation of unimodal PSD. It has been  proposed [26] that the morphologies of γ’ precipitates are primarily controlled by the lattice  misfit between γ matrix and γ’ precipitates. In the case of high cooling rate, the diffusions of γ’  forming elements are largely suppressed, leading to a small lattice misfit and thus forming the  nearly spherical γ’ precipitates due to the dominated mechanism of isotropic interfacial  energy [11]. As the cooling rate is further decreased (Fig. 3d, 3e and 3f), the secondary γ’  precipitates tend to become cubical and butterfly-shaped owing to the diffusion of the γ’  forming elements, which leads to a larger lattice misfit [27]. Meanwhile, the average particle  size of tertiary γ’ precipitates gradually increases but their morphologies remain spherical.  

Through image pixel based measurements, the average particle size and volume fraction of γ’  precipitates from points 1 to 6 are shown in table 2, where  d s  and  d t  are the average diameter  ( it is should be note that for points 4, 5 and 6, a critical size of 40 nm is applied for  distinguishing the secondary or tertiary γ’ phase ) for the secondary and tertiary γ’  precipitates,  V  is the total volume fraction of γ’ precipitates, respectively. The average grain  sizes from point 1 to point 6 are approximately equivalent, indicating that no obvious grain  growth occurs during the solution cooling process on basis of the EBSD measurement (the  average grain size is also shown in table 2).

# 3.4 Hardness measurement  

Vickers hardness provides a rapid and effective means to investigate the influence of cooling  rate on the mechanical strength [3, 15]. A nonlinear dependence of Vickers hardness along  the end quench bar is found from points 1 to 6, which also presents in cooling rate related plot 

 (see Fig. 5). From point 1 to 3, the value of hardness increases with increasing distance 

 (decreasing cooling rate) away from the water quenched end, while the maximum hardness  value emerges at the point 3 with a cooling rate of 171 °C/min. After that point, the hardness  drops with longer distance upon the quenched end or lower cooling rate.  

In powder metallurgy Ni-based superalloys, the mechanical strength is governed by grain size,  precipiptate and solid solution effect [3,30-33]. In this work, we can consider the contribution  of grain size effect on the strength to be almost equivalent owing to the similar grain size for  point 1 to 6. Although there exists element partition between γ/γ’ under different cooling rates  [6,11], the effect of atomic-scale element segregation on the mechanical properties can be  ignored when the indentation mark is in the range of micrometer size. In other words, the  differene of hardness response from point 1 to 6 can be tracked to γ’ precipitates distribution.  It has been proved that the strengthening effect of γ’ precipitates primarily depends on their  sizes and volume fractions [30,31]. And the underlying mechanisms for the evolution of  γ’   precipitates related hardness will be discussed in the following section.  

# 3.5 Phase filed simulation  

Phase field simulation provides a unique tool to capture the individual influence of PSD and  volume fraction of γ’ precipitates on tailoring the hardness simultaneously to understand the  detailed dislocation interaction mechanisms with secondary and tertiary γ’ precipitates and is  thus employed to explore the mechanism between a full dislocation on {111} slip plane and  different microstructures.  

The results of phase field simulation are depicted in Fig. 6. For faster cooling rate, such as  points 1 and 2, the APB shearing smaller γ’ precipitates (the red area indicates the dislocation  shears γ’ precipitates) is the dominant mechanism accompanied with some Orowan looping  larger γ’ precipitates (the blue area means Orowan looping is occuring), as shown in Fig. 6a  and 6b. In the case of slower cooling rate, like point 3, a bimodal PSD is presented, the  dislocation interacts with all secondary γ’ precipitates and some tertiary (with relative larger  size than fine tertiary γ’ precipitates) in the means of Orowan looping and APB shearing is  still working between it and the fine enough tertiary γ’ precipitates (Fig. 6c). It should be note  that the symmetry appeared in Fig. 6c is due to artificially mirroring the microstructure with  the aim to generate the same amount of simulation size as well as capture the small tertiary γ’  precipitates. As cooling rate further decreases (points 4 to 6), both volume fraction and size of  secondary γ’ precipitates increases and Orowan looping becomes the dominant mechanism  between dislocation and γ’ precipitates, see Fig. 6d, 6e and 6f. Due to the highest gamma  prime area fraction is \~35.6%, 60% area being sheared is determined as the finishing criteria  for CRSS comparison and the results are in good agreement with those in hardness test, as  ploted in Fig. 7. The highest CRSS is achieved at point 3 which can be attributed to APB  shearing the high number density fine tertiary γ’ precipitates. Relative larger size (there is a  critical size to generate the maximum CRSS in APB shearing [1]) and lower number density  are deduced the main reason of lower CRSS observed in Points 1 and 2, higher CRSS at point  2 is formed by its higher volume fraction of γ’ precipitates than that of point 1. For points 4 to  6, the transformation in dominat interaction mechanism form APB shearing to Orowan  looping between dislocation and γ’ precipitates is responsenable for the drop of CRSS. And  increasing size of secondary γ’ precipitates causing lower number density thus larger γ  channel spacing leading the continously decrease due to the stress required for dislocation to  loop between γ’ precipitates is inversely proportional to the γ channel spacing [23,32].  

# 4. Conclusions  

A high throughput approach is employed to investigate the influence of solution cooling rate  on the microstructure and Vickers hardness in a powder metallurgy Ni-based superalloy.  

(1)   Upon end quenching, a wide range of cooling rates, i.e., 35-6346  °C /min, can be obtained  and gradient distribution of γ’ precipitaes is accordingly produced within an individual  specimen.  

(2)   The average size of secondary γ’ precipitaes changes linearly with solution cooling rate is  observed when plotted in a double-logarithmic axes, which is rationalized through the  mechanism of diffusion controlled growth for γ’ precipitates. 

 (3)   The Vickers hardness displays a nonlinear character relative to cooling rate, and a range  of optimum cooling rate exists to maximize the mechanical strength.  

 (4)   Phase field simulation results reveal that the underlying physics of interaction between  dislocation and γ’ precipitaes is related to the PSD of γ’ precipitaes.
