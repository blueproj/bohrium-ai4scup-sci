Strain-controlled low cycle fatigue (LCF) and creep-fatigue testing of Ni-based alloy 230 were carried out at 850  1 C. The material creep-fatigue life decreased compared with its low cycle fatigue life at the same total strain range. Longer hold time at peak tensile strain further reduced the material creep-fatigue life. Based on the electron backscatter diffraction, a novel material deformation characterization method was applied, which revealed that in low cycle fatigue testing as the total strain range increased, the deformation was segregated to grain boundaries since the test temperature was higher than the material equicohesive temperature and grain boundaries became weaker regions compared with grains. Creep- fatigue tests enhanced the localized deformation, resulting in material interior intergranular cracking, and accelerated material damage. Precipitation in alloy 230 helped slip dispersion, favorable for fatigue property, but grain boundary cellular precipitates formed after material exposure to the elevated temperature had a deleterious effect on the material low cycle fatigue and creep-fatigue property.

# 1. Introduction  

The Very High Temperature Reactor (VHTR) is one of the Gen-IV reactor design concepts which embody a common goal of providing safe, longer lasting, proliferation-resistant, and economically viable nuclear energy  [1] . One of the biggest challenges in the research and development of Gen-IV reactor systems is the performance and reliability issues involving structural materials for both in-core and out-of-core applications. For instance, to maintain its economic advantage over early generation reactor systems, the VHTR has a goal of using helium at temperatures higher than 900  1 C and pressures up to 7 MPa for a design life of 60 years  [1 , 2 ]. The operation conditions of the VHTR impose tremendous challenges to materials used in this reactor system. To be suitable for the VHTR applications, Murty et al.  [3]  identiﬁed that structural materials should possess the following characteristics: (1) dimensional stabi- lity against creep damage due to elevated temperatures and irradiation; (2) satisfactory mechanical properties at elevated tem- peratures; (3) favorable resistance to neutron radiation damage and helium embrittlement; and (4) compatibility between the structural materials and the coolant.  

To better understand and address these issues, low cycle fatigue (LCF) and creep-fatigue tests of alloy 230 were conducted in this study. In addition, various microanalysis techniques, including scanning electron microscopy (SEM), energy-dispersive X-ray spec- troscopy (EDS), and electron backscatter diffraction (EBSD), were used to identify the underlying damage mechanism of alloy 230 under LCF and creep-fatigue tests.  

Currently, one of the metallic candidate materials for the intermediate heat exchanger in the VHTR is Ni-based alloy 230

# 2.2. Mechanical testing  

Cylindrical specimens and a servohydraulic test frame were used for testing. The long axis of the specimen was parallel to the rolling direction of the raw material. The detailed descriptions about the specimen design, the test frame setup, and the test matrix can be found in Refs.  [7 , 8 ].  Table 2  summarizes the test conditions. For LCF tests, triangular test waveforms with a strain rate of 2.5    10  4 /s were applied, whereas in creep-fatigue tests, additional hold time between 3 and 30 min on peak tensile strain were incorporated into the fatigue testing ( Fig. 1 ). All the tests were conducted in air at 850  1 C. The heating rate was 10  1 C per min and after reaching the target temperature specimens were soaked in that temperature for 45 min before the start of the test to stabilize the temperature. The end-of-test criterion was the attainment of 40% decrease in the cyclic maximum tensile stress.  

# 2.3. Microstructure characterization  

Microstructures of the as-received material, the aged material (aging condition: 850  1 C for 45 min), and post-test specimens were studied. The thermal aging mimicked the high temperature baking period during which specimens were subject to before the start of a test. The reason to investigate the aged material was to study possible microstructural changes during the thermal aging period.

GB cellular type precipitates were experienced in the aged material and the cellular precipitate was mainly Cr-rich M 23 C 6 ( Fig. 6 b). The effect of GB cellular precipitates on mechanical property of alloy 230 will be addressed further in later sessions. Due to precipitation reactions, the material microstructure right before the start of mechanical testing was different from that of the as-received material.  

# 3.3. Cracking mode under LCF and creep-fatigue tests

For LCF tests, alloy 230 exhibited classic transgranular crack initiation and propagation ( Fig. 7 a). However, cracking mode could be determined easily from inverse pole ﬁgure as the crystal orientation on both sides of any point along the crack path was same in transgranular cracking, whereas the crystal orientation on both sides of any point along the crack path was different in intergranular cracking. # 3.4. Deformation mechanism  

The distribution and quantity of low angle GBs derived from EBSD scans on the tested materials were investigated to reveal the deformation behavior of the material under LCF and creep-fatigue tests. In the wake of plastic deformation, large numbers of disloca- tions are created and they tend to reorganize themselves into dislocation cells which eventually coalesce to form sub-GBs  [27] . As the deformation continues, the misorientation angle of sub-GBs gradually increases and when the misorientation angle is larger than 2–5 1 , sub-GBs become low angle GBs. To accommodate the defor- mation, more dislocations must be introduced resulting in a growing wall of dislocations. Therefore low angle GBs can be considered essentially as an array of dislocations and are representative of the material deformation behavior  [28] .  Fig. 8 a–f illustrate the low angle GB distribution in alloy 230 specimens after different test condi- tions. Starting from the aged specimen,  Fig. 8 a shows few low angle GBs existing in the material because of lack of deformation. After the LCF test with 0.5% or 1.0% total strain range, numerous spots enriched with low angle GBs spreaded homogeneously within the material ( Fig. 8 b). As the total strain range increased to 1.5% in the LCF test, low angle GBs started to concentrate near original random high angle GBs ( Fig. 8 c). Comparing the difference in low angle GBs from specimens after creep-fatigue tests with 3 min strain hold time specimens after LCF tests at the same total strain range ( Fig. 8 c and e) reveals that creep deformation during the strain hold period led to more low angle GBs formation. Moreover the morphology of low angle GB distribution changed when 3 min strain hold time was incorporated into the 1.0% total strain range LCF test ( Fig. 8 b and d). The low angle GBs no longer distributed homogeneously as spots spread within the material, but were segregated to random high angle GBs of the material. For creep-fatigue tests at 1.0% total strain range, the quantity of low angle GBs increased with the strain hold time ( Fig. 8 d and f) since the creep deformation probably increased with strain hold time.  

The differences of the low angle GB distribution in LCF tested alloy 230 specimens can be explained in the following fashion. In the as-received material, numerous undissolved precipitates existed ( Fig. 4 ). In addition, the W alloying element might cause relatively lower stacking fault energy (SFE) in alloy 230 since the electron vacancy number of W is high and the SFE of FCC alloys would decrease with increasing electron vacancy number of alloying elements  [5 , 29 ]. Owning to the large fraction of carbide precipitates and reduced dislocation mobility in conjunction with low SFE, for LCF tests with smaller total strain ranges, i.e. 0.5% and 1.0%, intragranular precipitates of alloy 230 pinned down a large number of dislocations and hence lots of low angle GBs existed as numerous small spots throughout the specimen ( Fig. 8 b). In the LCF test with 1.5% total strain range, higher stress level assisted dislocations bypass of the precipitates and therefore less low angle GB spots were observed ( Fig. 8 c). Moreover, the emergence of low angle GBs near original random high angle GBs ( Fig. 8 c) indicated localized deformation near random high angle GBs since the test temperature was above the material equicohesive temperature and the strength of GB is lower than grain itself  [30 , 31 ].  

In creep-fatigue tests, due to the peak tensile strain hold period additional creep damage was introduced to the material. To inves- tigate the creep deformation mechanism during the strain hold period, the deformation map most relevant to alloy 230 was identiﬁed from Ref.  [32] . The deformation map was derived from a Ni–Cr–TiO 2  alloy which is Ni-based alloy combining solution and dispersion hardening with a grain size of 200  m m. Alloy 230 was also solid-solution strengthened with additional precipitation strength- ening. However precipitates in alloy 230 were coarse and hence behaved like dispersions  [32] . Therefore the deformation map of Ni–Cr–TiO 2  should be relevant to the deformation behavior in alloy 230. Based on the range of normalized shear stress alloy 230 subjected to (from the mid-life stress relaxation data of each creep-fatigue test) and the test temperature ( T / T M E 0.7), the possi- ble deformation mechanisms can be derived from the deformation map. During the ﬁrst few seconds of the strain hold period, the stress level was high and dislocation glide-controlled ﬂow was the main mechanism for the material deformation. As the stress

Owning to the high test temperature, the diffusive motion of vacancies to or from the climbing dislocation through the lattice controlled the dislocation climb at an atomic level  [32] . The dislocation climb- controlled creep process encouraged dislocation bypass of precipi- tate obstacles which explained why precipitates in creep-fatigue tested specimens pinned down fewer low angle GBs than precipi- tates in LCF tested specimens ( Fig. 8 b and d).  

GB sliding, generally thought to occur by GB dislocation motion, is another important creep deformation mechanism. Although in a typical deformation map, no region corresponding to GB sliding exists because of uncertainties regarding the suitable constitutive equation for this mechanism. However, recent studies have shown that the dislocation creep ﬁeld in a deformation map can be subdivided with a GB sliding contribution existing at the lower stress levels  [30] . Since the stress level was relatively low during the most part of strain hold time, GB sliding should be operational. Indeed, abundant low angle GBs were created near original random high angle GBs in creep-fatigue tested specimens ( Fig. 8 d–f). In addition, GB sliding process needs to be accommodated by other deformation modes to maintain grain contiguity. Diffusional creep at high temperatures and dislocation creep at lower temperatures and elevated stresses  [32]  are the two major deformation processes normally afﬁliated with GB sliding. If neither deformation processes are operable efﬁciently, GB decohesion would develop by the formation of a planar array of GB cavities which eventually coalesce to form GB cracks. This explains the occurrence of interior inter- granular cracks in alloy 230 after creep-fatigue tests ( Fig. 9 ). The intergranular cracks were usually aligned at 45 1  or perpendicular to the axial stress. GBs at 45 1  to the axis stress are on planes of maximum shear stress where GB sliding would tend to open cracks. GB oriented perpendicular to the tensile axis experienced the greatest normal force during tests, which also tend to open cracks [33] . The interior intergranular cracks demonstrated additional creep damage during creep-fatigue testing.  

Low angle GB derived from EBSD analysis was proved to be an innovative tool for monitoring material deformation behavior in this study. It was capable of resolving tiny amount of deformation in a micrometer scale. It also ﬁlls the gap of the existing literatures on the explanation of reduced creep-fatigue life of alloy 230. Current literatures  [25 , 34 ] attribute the reduced creep- fatigue life of alloy 230 to additional creep damage incurred during the strain hold period without direct microstructure proof. Instead, ﬁndings in this study clearly revealed localized deforma- tion, in the form of low angle GBs, near material original random high angle GBs due to creep damage mechanism. In that regard, the results of this work also support the existence of a mechani- cally induced interface energy term, proposed by Aifantis et al. [35]  and Zhang et al.  [36] , which predicts the ability of GBs to deform and its inﬂuence on the material mechanical behavior. Further studies are needed to correlate the experimental ﬁndings in this study with the model developed in Refs.  [35 , 36 ].

Slip dispersal can improve fatigue properties when fracture is entirely transgranular  [21] . Since fracture modes of alloy 230 in LCF and creep-fatigue tests were mostly transgranular, the high precipitation volume fraction and coarse carbide precipitates in alloy 230 should improve the material LCF and creep-fatigue property.  

GBs containing cellular precipitates in alloy 230 were studied with EBSD.  Fig. 10 a shows that a random high angle GB appeared on the precipitate front. The GB cellular precipitates form by a mechanism called discontinuous precipitation reaction in which a cell composed of solute-depleted phase (matrix) and lamellar precipitate forms behind a GB advancing into a supersaturated matrix  [37] . Hence the reaction is a combination of precipitation and GB migration with the supersaturation in the matrix as the driving force for the reaction  [38 , 39 ]. During the discontinuous precipitation reaction, lamellar precipitate is coherent with the grain away from which it is growing and is incoherent with the cell growth front  [40 – 43 ] which explains the existence of a random high angle GB on the precipitate front in  Fig. 10 a.  

Cellular precipitates formed by discontinuous precipitation reaction are of interest because they usually have a deleterious effect on the mechanical, physical, and chemical properties of many commercial alloys, i.e. signiﬁcant decrease in high- temperature ductility, rapid initiation of GB cracks, and creep cavitation  [37] . Indeed, for the 1.5% total strain range LCF test in some cases the crack path could follow GB (the red arrow in Fig. 10 b) indicating potential GB embrittlement due to cellular precipitates. Under creep-fatigue test conditions, interior intergra- nular cracks were only found on random high angle GBs with cellular precipitates but not on random high angle GBs without cellular precipitates ( Fig. 9 ). This conﬁrmed the deleterious effect of GB cellular precipitates on mechanical properties of the material. Fig. 10 c illustrates one example of an intergranular crack asso- ciated with the GB cellular precipitate. On occasion, random high angle GBs could migrate away from the cellular precipitate and became denuded of precipitates in creep-fatigue tested materials. The GBs of this conﬁguration were especially susceptible for intergranular cracking in creep-fatigue testing ( Fig. 10 d) because of lack of GB precipitates which are known to impede GB sliding [44 , 45 ]. For the development of GB denuded of precipitates in alloy 230 two conditions need to be satisﬁed: (1) the material has been subject to creep-fatigue testing; and (2) cellular precipitates have precipitated on the GB. The ﬁrst condition highlights the additional driving force available for GB migration during creep-fatigue testing. Namely in creep-fatigue testing, deformation focused on GB area and plenty of dislocations were created near GBs which eventually led to strain energy gradient across the GB. Therefore, the creep-fatigue test induced strain energy gradient became the additional driving for GB movement  [46] . Secondly, cellular M 23 C 6 carbides on GBs might not possess the same ability as general GB precipitates in limiting GB migration  [47]  and hence only GBs with cellular precipitates were able to migrate away from cellular precipitates. Nevertheless the GB cellular precipitation can be avoided by appropriate heat treatment and chemistry control to further improve the mechanical properties of alloy 230  [5] .  

By employing EBSD analyses, the inﬂuence of precipitation on LCF and creep-fatigue property of alloy 230 was identiﬁed successfully. For the ﬁrst time, the cellular precipitation in alloy 230 was conﬁrmed to be formed by the discontinuous precipita- tion reaction which was a combination of precipitation and GB migration reaction. In addition, the detrimental effect of cellular precipitates on mechanical property of alloy 230 was observed.  

# 4. Conclusions  

To investigate the major damage mechanism for materials used as the intermediate heat exchanger in the VHTR, LCF and creep-fatigue tests of Ni-based alloy 230 were conducted. Various microanalysis techniques, e.g. SEM, EDS, and EBSD, were applied to study the material deformation mechanism. The main conclu- sions drawn from this study are:  

1. The material creep-fatigue life decreased compared with its LCF life at the same total strain range. Longer hold time at peak tensile strain further reduced the material creep-fatigue life.

 2. A novel material deformation characterization method based on low angle GBs revealed that in LCF testing as the total strain range increased, the deformation was segregated to GBs since the test temperature was higher than the material equicohe- sive temperature and GBs became weaker regions compared with grains.

 3. Creep-fatigue tests enhanced the localized deformation, result- ing in material interior intergranular cracking, and accelerated material damage.

 4. Although precipitates in alloy 230 helped slip dispersion, favorable for fatigue property, grain boundary cellular precipitates formed after material exposure to the elevated temperature had a deleterious effect on the material LCF and creep-fatigue property.
