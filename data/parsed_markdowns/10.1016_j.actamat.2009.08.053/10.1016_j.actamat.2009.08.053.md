# Abstract  

Focused ion beam serial sectioning was used to study the three-dimensional morphology of secondary  c 0   precipitates in two nickel- based superalloys: Rene ´-88 DT, a turbine disk alloy, and UMF-20, an experimental alloy. The precipitate morphology was reconstructed using a multi-step image-processing approach, including registration, intensity corrections, segmentation and segmentation cleanup. A total of 260 dendritic precipitates were obtained for the Rene ´-88 DT alloy, and 477 cuboidal precipitates in the UMF-20 alloy. The shapes of these precipitates were analyzed by means of moment invariants. The cuboidal precipitates in UMF-20 are well described by an analytical superellipsoid model. The morphological evolution of the dendritic precipitates in Rene ´-88 DT is analyzed by combining the aﬃne moment invariant with the dimensionless shape quotient. This approach is promising as an objective tool for the description of complex precipitate shapes.

# 1. Introduction  

Recent years have seen an increase in the application and use of three-dimensional (3-D) microstructural charac- terization methods. This increase, which has been docu- mented in recent issues of Scripta Materialia  [1]  and the Journal of Metals  [2] , is due in part to the partial or com- plete automation of experimental data acquisition. Readily available desktop computational power has also contrib- uted to a reduction in the time needed to acquire experi- mental data. While the data acquisition side has made signiﬁcant progress, the subsequent analysis of the data continues to be an area of intense research.  

Certain morphological properties of a microstructure, such as the connectivity of precipitates, and the true precip- itate shape, can only be determined by 3-D characteriza- tion techniques. Other characteristics, such as precipitate size distribution and the number of precipitates, can be measured in two dimensions, but require geometric assumptions for stereological estimates  [3] . Therefore, experimental techniques that can be used to investigate the 3-D nature of microstructures are necessary to provide more complete microstructural characterization. This is not a new insight and researchers have applied 3-D charac- terization techniques throughout the development of the ﬁelds of metallurgy and materials science. For example, in 1918, Forsman used serial sectioning to examine the 3-D morphology of pearlite, and constructed a solid model of cementite lamellae  [4] . Although the manual collection of serial sectioning data is a laborious task and represents a signiﬁcant barrier to widespread usage of 3-D data, there are other issues related to the subsequent handling of the data that also require development. Prior to the availability of computational tools for visualization, 3-D data was visualized using, for example, hand sketches of the 3-D microstructure, or the construction of physical models (e.g.  [5] ). Recent serial sectioning developments include ultramilling and optical microscopy  [6] , robot-controlled polishing and optical microscopy (RoboMet.3D)  [7] , and dual beam focused ion beam (FIB) scanning electron microscopy (SEM)  [8–11] . Generally, the spatial resolution of the reconstructed 3-D microstructure is controlled by the distance between each section, and by the image resolu- tion of the individual sections.  

In this paper, we present a serial sectioning study by means of FIB/SEM of the structure of secondary  c 0   precip- itates in two diﬀerent Ni-based superalloys, UMF-20 and Rene ´-88 DT. This approach, described in detail in Ref. [9] , allows for the automated acquisition of large numbers of consecutive sections with section thicknesses in the range of 10–50 nm. The two alloys considered in this study diﬀer substantially in the shape of their secondary  c 0   precipitates. In the UMF-20 alloy, the precipitates have a typical cuboi- dal shape, whereas in the Rene ´-88 DT alloy, the heat treat- ment produced complex dendritic precipitate shapes. The experimental work, described in Sections  2–4 , is followed by an analysis of the precipitate shapes using a novel math- ematical and numerical approach: 3-D moment invariants.  

Moment invariants are combinations of second- (or higher) order moments of inertia of an object that are invariant with respect to classes of coordinate transforma- tions. They were introduced in 1962 by Hu for use in auto- mated character recognition  [12] . Since then, moment invariants have been used primarily in pattern recognition and machine learning applications. The majority of the lit- erature has focused on 2-D moment invariants, although there has been some work on 3-D (e.g.  [13–15] ) and  n -D moment invariants (e.g.  [13] ). More recently, moment invariants have been used in such diverse ﬁelds as solid mechanics ﬁnite-element models  [16] , image watermarking [17] , font recognition  [18]  and 2-D ﬂow ﬁelds  [19] . The work presented in the present paper is based on the 3-D extension in Ref.  [13] , and applies the moment-invariant approach to precipitates in Ni-base superalloys.  

The primary goal of the moment-invariant approach as employed in this paper is to move beyond the standard qualitative description of object shapes. For instance, when the results of numerical microstructure simulations of mul- tiphase alloys are compared with experimental images, the comparison rarely goes beyond the qualitative (and some- what subjective) statement  “ they look the same ” . While the human visual system is rather good at recognizing and comparing shapes, there is an obvious advantage to having an objective standard for shape comparison; ideally, one should be able to completely remove the human assessment from the experimental sequence. The moment-invariant approach, as employed in this paper, provides a 3-D repre- sentation of particle morphology, and places microstruc- ture modeling on a more ﬁrm footing by providing a quantitative metric for the comparison of morphologies. A secondary goal of the research reported in this paper is to create a shape comparison tool that requires minimal user intervention and that can be automated.

# 2.2. Experimental techniques  

The Rene ´-88 DT sample was supplied as a TEM thin foil (a 3 mm disk which had undergone mechanical polish- ing and electro polishing). Serial sectioning was completed using cross-section milling and ion imaging, from a region of the foil which was 10 – 20  l m thick, using a cantilevered sample geometry. A speciﬁc grain was selected for exami- nation such that the sectioning plane was nearly perpendic- ular to a  h 111 i  direction. This orientation provided good image contrast between  c 0   precipitates and the  c  matrix when obtaining ISE images using the secondary electron detector. The serial sectioning experiment was completed at the Air Force Research Lab (AFRL) at Wright Patter- son Air Force Base (Dayton, OH) using an FEI Strata DB-235 dual beam FIB. Data was collected over a contin- uous period of 86 h, resulting in 187 serial sections; of these, 154 sections were used to generate the 3-D data set. The section thickness was approximately 25 nm. For an overview of FIB-based serial sectioning we refer the reader to Ref.  [25] .  

The UMF-20 sample was prepared for FIB-based serial sectioning using standard mechanical grinding and polish- ing techniques to produce a foil of approximately 20  l m in thickness. Serial sectioning was performed at Carnegie Mellon University using an FEI Nova 600 nano-lab dual beam FIB. The sample was placed on a 45   pre-tilt sample holder, and a cantilevered sample geometry was milled from the sample surface. Automated serial sectioning was then completed using cross-section milling and ion imag- ing. The serial-sectioning experiment produced 288 slices over a continuous period of 60 h, with approximately 20 nm section thickness; of these sections, 223 were used to generate the 3-D data set. For each slice, several ISE images were collected, to facilitate the segmentation pro- cess, as described in Section  3.3.2 .  

# 3. 3-D microstructure reconstruction  

Before the  c 0   precipitate shapes can be analyzed quanti- tatively, the experimental 2-D sections must be converted to 3-D volume data sets. This conversion consists of four steps: (1) image registration; (2) correction for intensity shifts and ﬂuctuations; (3) image segmentation; and (4) post-segmentation processing. In the following subsections, each of these steps is described in more detail.  

# 3.1. Image registration  

The sample stage inside a FIB microscope is not suﬃ- ciently accurate to provide perfect registration of consecu- tive slices; small stage position variations are unavoidable, and must be removed by a numerical image registration step. Manual image registration, while relatively straight- forward, is subjective and lacks the (sub-pixel) accuracy needed to align a complete stack. In this paper, image cross-correlation was used to align each of the data sets. Alternatively, one could use a technique based on mutual information and image entropy to perform the registration step  [26] .  

Prior to registration, images were smoothed and multi- plied by a rectangular 2-D Gaussian mask, to keep the image edge from interfering with the alignment process. The image intensity of each section image was normalized by subtracting the mean and dividing by the standard devi- ation. An approximate alignment (to within one pixel) was determined by computing the cross-correlation between the images and taking the location of the correlation maximum to represent the coarse translation vector between the two images. Sub-pixel alignment was subsequently achieved by shifting the images with respect to each other using cubic-convolution interpolation  [27]  while maximizing the value of the cross-correlation using Powell’s method  [28] .  

This procedure takes a few hours for each data set and results in a registered 3-D data stack.  

# 3.2. Correction for intensity shifts and ﬂuctuations  

Despite the use of automated scripts and high-quality ion optical columns, the long duration of a typical serial sectioning experiment almost always leads to small inten- sity variations between individual sections, and, possibly, an overall intensity gradient from the ﬁrst to the last sec- tion image. In addition, each individual image may also contain an intensity gradient due to nonuniform illumina- tion conditions. All of these intensity variations must be corrected before the image segmentation step can be car- ried out.  

Intensity gradients can be removed by determining the average intensity in a rectangular box, such as the one shown in  Fig. 1a , as a function of the vertical position of the box. If the average intensity varies systematically across the image (in  Fig. 1a  the average intensity varies approxi- mately linearly along the vertical direction), then the aver- age intensity curve can be used to create a correction mask which is subtracted from the original image to obtain an intensity-corrected image. This procedure is repeated for each section and can also be repeated across the entire stack (from the ﬁrst to the last section) to remove intensity gradients caused by a drift of the beam intensity. The data stacks for both Rene ´-88 DT and UMF-20 alloys were pro- cessed in this fashion.  

# 3.3. Image segmentation  

Segmentation involves partitioning the image into two or more classes in order to diﬀerentiate between the  c 0   pre- cipitates and the  c  matrix. There are many segmentation methods available in the literature, including manual seg- mentation, or automated methods, such as those based on histogram thresholding, edge detection, region growing, watershed transform and so on; for an overview of com- mon thresholding methods see Ref.  [29–31] . Due to the rea- sonably good image contrast between the  c 0   precipitates and the matrix for the alloys studied in this paper, we have elected to perform segmentation based on image histogram thresholding. Since the procedures followed for the two alloys were somewhat diﬀerent, we will discuss each alloy separately.  

# 3.3.1. Segmentation of Rene ´-88 DT images  

In this speciﬁc data set, segmentation was performed on an image-by-image basis, due to variations in imaging con- trast and diﬀerences in the number of histogram peaks, caused by the presence of twins and other grains in the microstructure. Global (3-D) thresholds following a histo- gram normalization have been applied to Ni-base superal- loys (e.g.  [8] ), but do not work in this case. The presence of twins causes the appearance of multiple peaks in the inten- sity histogram and requires the selection of multiple thresh- old points for each image. Since the number of histogram peaks, in particular those corresponding to the  c 0   precipi- tates, varied from one section to the next, a semi-auto- mated approach was used for segmentation. The histogram peaks and threshold points were determined automatically, by smoothing the image histogram to remove local minima and maxima, and subsequently com- puting both ﬁrst and second derivatives to determine the location of all intensity extrema. Minimal operator interac- tion was required to correctly select the appropriate histo- gram peaks to use for segmentation. # 3.4. Post-segmentation processing  

The segmented binary stack must be prepared for visu- alization and quantitative analysis. During segmentation, particles may acquire internal voids resulting from the thresholding operation; these voids are caused by noise that is not removed during the morphological opening operation. While internal voids will not be visible in the ﬁnal rendered images, they should be closed in order to decrease the time required to render the precipitates. Voids caused by the segmentation process should always be closed when numerical parameters such as the Minkowski functionals  [33]  are to be computed.  

Due to the voxelated nature of binary data, particles less than a minimum size retain insuﬃcient information regard- ing their true shape and are consequently removed from the stack. Similarly, those precipitates which intersect an edge of the experimental sample volume are also eliminated. Finally, there is typically a diﬀerence between the in-section resolution of each image and the distance between consec- utive sections. Therefore, the 3-D segmented stack is scaled anisotropically, so that the voxel dimensions become equal in all directions; scaling is accomplished using a cubic con- volution interpolation method  [27] .

# 8. Conclusions and summary  

Serial sectioning by means of FIB-SEM was employed to study the 3-D morphology of secondary  c 0   precipitates in two Ni-based superalloys: Rene ´-88 DT, a damage-toler- ant alloy used in turbine disks, and UMF-20, a Re-contain- ing experimental alloy. These alloys were selected because their  c 0   morphology is very diﬀerent. In the UMF-20 case, most secondary  c 0   precipitates have a cuboidal shape, whereas in the Rene ´-88 DT alloy, the thermal processing conditions were such that the precipitates were formed by solid-state dendritic growth.  

We have described the serial section data-acquisition process as well as the image-processing steps that need to be carried out to convert the 2-D images into a 3-D binary data stack, with voxels that belong to a secondary  c 0   precip- itate set to the value 1, and the disordered matrix set to the value 0. The image-processing steps include registration, correction of intensity shifts, segmentation (performed by automated single or joint histogram thresholding) and post-segmentation cleanup. The 3-D stack thus obtained can be used for further microstructural modeling.  

The results presented in this paper show that it is possi- ble to describe the shapes of secondary  c 0   precipitates in Ni-based superalloys in a quantitative way, by making use of the concept of moment invariants. This approach is quite general, easy to implement and is not restricted to a particular class of shapes; indeed, our earlier theoret- ical paper on 3-D moment invariants  [39]  shows that the approach is valid for all 3-D shapes.  

As stated earlier, it is not possible to reconstruct a 3-D shape when only its second-order moments are known; an arbitrary shape can be reconstructed exactly only if all its moments are known. Therefore, the second-order moment-invariant approach is the lowest-order approach possible (the ﬁrst-order moments do not contain any useful shape information other than the center-of-mass). Given how simple it is to compute the moment invariants, it is somewhat surprising that they have not been used before in the study of precipitate shapes. Particle aspect ratios have been used in the past to describe certain aspects of microstructures, but our theoretical work in this area clearly shows that there is an additional quantity that must be considered at the same level as the aspect ratios of a shape, i.e. the aﬃne invariant,  X 3 , which is a descriptor of shape classes. Therefore, if one desires to construct a description of a shape based on its aspect ratios, then one must also include the aﬃne invariant, since it is also a sec- ond-order parameter; indeed, it is the only other second- order parameter that is invariant with respect to aﬃne coordinate transformations. The correlations between  X 3 and  Q , shown in  Figs. 12 and 14 b show a diﬀerent average slope for the two shape classes, indicating that the shape quotient provides additional shape information, not cap- tured by the moment invariants.  

We anticipate that this observation will have an impact in the area of digitally synthesized microstructures, in par- ticular the quantitative comparison of experimental and simulated microstructures. The moment-invariant approach removes the subjective comparison of 3-D visualizations of experimental and modeled microstructures and replaces it with an objective and quantitative comparison. If a numerical model of a microstructure is to be a correct rep- resentation of a real microstructure, then, at the very least, the distribution of the microstructural components in the normalized moment invariant space should be the same for both microstructures. In other words, if a set of  c 0   pre- cipitates, determined from an experimental 3-D reconstruc- tion, occupies a certain region in the moment-invariant space, then the precipitate shapes obtained from, for instance, a phase-ﬁeld simulation of this microstructure, should occupy the same region of the moment invariant space. If the two sets of points do not overlap, then the sim- ulation parameters may need to be ﬁne-tuned. Therefore, a moment-invariant analysis can become an important tool to match simulation parameters to experimental micro- structures. An example of such an application will be described in a forthcoming paper, dealing with the analysis of how well 3-D synthetic microstructures compare with the original experimental microstructures they are sup- posed to represent.  

From a numerical point of view, it is straightforward to compute higher-order moments  l pqr ð p  þ  q  þ  r  >  2 Þ , and there is some literature on third-order invariants. It is easy to show that the third-order moments all vanish for a shape with inversion symmetry (most of the shapes considered in this study have approximate inversion symmetry, so that such a third-order analysis would not be very meaningful in this case). The number of invariants increases rapidly with increasing moment order, so that it becomes more dif- ﬁcult to represent them in a graphical way, similar to the normalized moment invariant space used in this paper. Despite these diﬃculties, there may be some beneﬁt in con- sidering the inclusion of third- and fourth-order moment invariants in the description of shapes. For statistical distri- butions, the moments correspond to the following quanti- ties: variance (second-order), skewness (third-order), and kurtosis (fourth-order). In addition, kurtosis and variance are often combined in the kurtosis excess. All of these parameters are important to the description of the shape of a statistical distribution, and we anticipate that they will also be important (in invariant form) for the description of actual 3-D shapes. In addition, there may be some beneﬁt in combining the moment-invariant approach with an anal- ysis of the Minkowski functionals  [33]  of a given shape, using the dimensionless approach of the Blaschke diagram.
