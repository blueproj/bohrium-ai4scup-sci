ABSTRACT:  Peroxiredoxin (PRDX1) is a tumor-overexpressed antioxidant enzyme for eliminating excessive reactive oxygen species (ROS) to protect tumor cells from oxidative damage. Herein, a series of celastrol urea derivatives were developed based on its cocrystal structure with PRDX1, with the aim of pursuing a PRDX1-specific inhibitor. Among them, derivative  15  displayed potent anti-PRDX1 activity (IC 50  = 0.35  μ M) and anti pro life rat ive potency against colon cancer cells. It covalently bound to Cys- 173 of PRDX1 ( K D  = 0.37  μ M), which was secured by the cocrystal structure of PRDX1 with an analogue of  15  while exhibiting weak inhibitory effects on PRDX2 − PRDX6 (IC 50  > 50  μ M), indicating excellent PRDX1 selectivity. Treatment with  15  dose- dependently decreased the mitochondria membrane potential of SW620 cells, probably due to ROS induced by PRDX1 inhibition, leading to cell apoptosis. In colorectal cancer cell xenograft model, it displayed potent antitumor efficacy with superior safety to celastrol. Collectively,  15  represents a promising PRDX1 selective inhibitor for the development of anticolorectal cancer agents.  

# INTRODUCTION  

Reactive oxygen species (ROS) are crucial for cell survival by regulating normal physiological functions and signal trans- duction of cells.   However, the excessive production of ROS beyond the cellular tolerability threshold induces oxidative stress damage to proteins, nucleic acids, and even organelles, which sequentially lead to path o physiological changes such as cell apoptosis and necrosis. ,   Aberrant redox status is a common feature of many cancer cells with increased levels of intracellular ROS, ,   thus rendering these cells reliant on specific antioxidant enzymes to eliminate various ROS for survival. Several groups of antioxidant enzymes, such as glutathione peroxidases (GPXs), peroxiredoxins (PRDXs), thioredoxins (Trxs), and glutaredoxins (Grxs), have been characterized as a powerful antioxidant defense to protect cells from oxidative damage and maintain the cellular redox homeostasis by the reversible formation of disulfides  via  the oxidation of active-site cysteine thiols. − 10   Thus, the disruption of redox homeostasis through inhibiting antioxidant enzyme- induced ROS clearance has emerged as a rational strategy for cancer therapy.  

PRDXs are a superfamily of non s elen oper oxidase s that catalyze peroxide reduction to remove cellular excessive hydrogen peroxide (H 2 O 2 ) by using the TRX/Trx reduc- tase/NADPH system as a reducing equivalent. − 13   According to the number and position of conserved cysteine residues as well as catalytic mechanism, PRDXs are divided into three categories including typical 2-Cys (PRDX1 − 4), atypical 2-Cys (PRDX5), and 1-Cys PRDXs (PRDX6),   which are involved in the regulation of various physiological processes, such as cell growth, differentiation, immune response, and metabolism. Recently, we and co-workers identified celastrol as a non- selective PRDX1 inhibitor covalently linked to the Cys-173 residue of PRDX1  via  a Michael-like addition reaction.   Based on our solved high-resolution crystal structure of PRDX1 in complex with celastrol, we tentatively synthesized the C-20 urea derivatives with terminal methyl ester, which more selectively inhibited the peroxidase activity of PRDX1 among the human PRDX protein family when compared to celastrol. Biology.  Structure − Activity Relationship (SAR) Study against PRDX1 and Colorectal Cancer Cells.  All of the synthesized urea derivatives were assessed for their inhibitory effects on the peroxidase activity of PRDX1 and cellular anti proliferation activity by using celastrol as a positive control. The peroxiredoxin activity was determined by the recombinant PRDX1 protein in a TRX − TrxR − NADPH coupling assay. PRDXs were assayed by coupling its activity to the oxidation of NADPH  via  TrxR. Considering that PRDX1 is closely involved in the development of human colorectal cancer by scavenging ROS, ,   two representative colorectal cancer cell lines SW620 and HCT116 were employed for assessing the anti proliferation efficacy of new derivatives in the CellTiter-Glo luminescent cell viability assay. As shown in  Table 1 , most of the tested compounds displayed more potent enzymatic and cellular activities than celastrol (IC 50  = 0.50  μ M against PRDX1, 0.67 μ M against SW620, 1.4  μ M against HCT116) and compound 3  (IC 50  = 0.49  μ M against PRDX1, 0.33  μ M against SW620, 0.80  μ M against HCT116). Replacement of the terminal methyl ester in  3  by the terminal amide led to weaker PRDX1 inhibitory activity ( 4 , IC 50  = 0.54  μ M) and anti pro life rat ive activity against SW620 ( 4 , IC 50  = 1.4  μ M) and HCT116 ( 4 , IC  = 1.8  μ M). Aryl derivatives, such as 12  with terminal L -tyrosinate and  13  with terminal L - tryptophanate, displayed more potent suppressing effects on both PRDX1 peroxidase and cancer cell viability than  3  and celastrol.

Derivative  15  Is a Selective and Covalent PRDX1 Inhibitor.  PRDX has six isoforms (PRDX1 − 6) with a high degree of sequence similarity, which are involved in a variety of physiological processes. We previously disclosed that celastrol is a pan-PRDX inhibitor (except PRDX4) with undesirable side effects, probably due to its poor PRDX subtype selectivity. Therefore, it is a critical challenge to develop a selective PRDX1 inhibitor. We selected compound  15  for further evaluation of PRDX1 selectivity. The peroxiredoxin inhibitory activity of  15  was determined in a TRX − TrxR − NADPH coupling assay by using recombinant PRDX2, PRDX3, PRDX4, PRDX5, and PRDX6 proteins, respectively. As shown in  Figure 4 , compound  15  displayed negligible inhibitory effects against the peroxidase activity of PRDX2 − PRDX6 with IC 50  values greater than 50  μ M ( Figure 4 B − F), indicating that it is a highly selective PRDX1 inhibitor among PRDXs. Our previous study has confirmed that celastrol is a covalent inhibitor of PRDX1 with potent binding affinity, and the click labeling assay suggested that celastrol bound to PRDX1  via  addition reaction to the Cys-173 residue of PRDX1. To evaluate whether  15  could covalently modify PRDX1, we used mass spectrometry to assess the covalent bonding between  15  and PRDX1. As a result, derivative  15 showed apparent covalent labeling of PRDX1 as indicated by a 577 Da molecular weight increase ( Figure 4 I). Moreover, the binding affinity of  15  with PRDX1 protein measured by the surface plasmon resonance (SPR) method is 0.37  μ M ( Figure 4 G). Notably, further protein thermal shift (PTS) experiments showed that the interactions between  15  and PRDX1 protein resulted in a significant thermal transition with the  Δ T m value of  − 7.5  ° C at a molar ratio of 1:10 ( Figure 4 H). These results demonstrated that  15  covalently binds to PRDX1.

Compound  15  Elevates ROS Accumulation in SW620 Colorectal Cancer Cells.  PRDX1 is a vital antioxidant enzyme responsive for regulating oxidative stress by scavenging ROS. To determine whether compound  15  impacts the cellular redox state  via  targeting PRDX1, ROS levels were measured by flow cytometry analysis of SW620 cells with dichlorodihydro- fluorescein diacetate (DCFH-DA) probe staining. As shown in Figure 6 , the cellular level of ROS was elevated in a dose- dependent manner after treatment of SW620 colorectal cancer cells with  15  or celastrol at 0.5 − 2  μ M for 48 h. The ROS- positive rates in SW620 cells were sharply accumulated to 19.5% from 6.3% when the concentration of  15  was increased from 0 to 1  μ M and further accumulated to 40.6% at 2  μ M. These results showed that the inhibition of PRDX1 induced by 15  could significantly elevate the intracellular ROS level.

# CONCLUSIONS  

Disruption of cellular redox homeostasis by the inhibition of antioxidant enzymes that clear intracellular ROS in cancer cells has emerged as a novel and promising strategy for cancer therapy. PRDX1 overexpressed in the tumor is an antioxidant enzyme responsible for eliminating cellular excessive H 2 O 2  to protect tumor cell from oxidative damage. Thus, this enzyme has become a promising target for the development of anticancer agents. Recently, we identified the natural product celastrol as the covalent inhibitor of PRDX1. However, it also showed notable inhibition against the other isoforms of the PRDX family, which might result in undesirable side effects. Accordingly, a series of the C-20 urea derivatives of celastrol were designed and synthesized based on the cocrystal structure of PRDX1 and celastrol with the aim to develop a safer PRDX1 inhibitor. Among them, derivative  15  displayed the most potency against the peroxidase activity of PRDX1 with an IC 50 value of 0.35  μ M together with comparable potency against the proliferation of colon cancer cells. In contrast, it exhibited weak inhibitory effects on PRDX2 − PRDX6 with IC 50  values greater than 50  μ M, indicating excellent PRDX1 selectivity. Furthermore,  15  covalently bound to the Cys-173 residue of PRDX1 with a binding affinity of 0.37  μ M to block the peroxidase activity, which was further secured by the crystal structural analysis of PRDX1 in complex with  16 , an analogue of  15 . The orientational discrepancy of  15  in complex with PRDX1 as compared to celastrol might contribute to its improved selectivity. Treatment with  15  dose-dependently induced the accumulation of cellular ROS by blocking the peroxidase activity of PRDX1 and further decreased the mitochondrial membrane potential of SW620 cells to induce mitochondrial dysfunction in a dose-dependent manner, thus significantly leading to the cell apoptosis together with cell cycle arrest. More importantly, as selective PRDX1 inhibitor, 15  at 20 mg/kg displayed a much higher surviving rate without notable path o his to logical change of the major organ tissue sections than celastrol, demonstrating superior  in vivo  safety. In the colorectal cancer xenograft model, it displayed moderate antitumor efficacy at 2 mg/kg (TGI = 45.4%) with acceptable tolerance. Taken together, the urea modification at the C-20 position of natural product celastrol could significantly enhance the subtype selectivity toward PRDX1, and the resulting derivative  15  could serve as a promising PRDX1 selective inhibitor for the development of anticolorectal cancer agents with excellent safety and efficacy. Nevertheless,  in vivo antitumor potency and PK properties of  15  remain to be further improved for clinical translation through structural optimization. In addition, we will perform on-target engage- ment assay in cells or animal models to evaluate potential off- target risk. All of these results will be reported in due course.

Cell Culture.  Human colorectal cancer cell lines HCT116 and SW620 as well as normal cell lines including 293T, LX2, and L929 were obtained from the Type Culture Collection of the Chinese Academy of Sciences (Shanghai, China) and were cultured in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium (DMEM, Gibco) with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS), 1% penicillin/streptomycin (PS, Invitrogen) in a humidified incubator (37  ° C, 5% CO ).  

Cell Viability Assay.  To determine the inhibitory effects of celastrol and derivatives on cell viability, the cells were seeded in 96-well plates at a concentration of 2000 cells per well and cultivated overnight. The culture medium was replaced with medium containing 9 or 10 concentration gradients of compounds or 0.1% DMSO and further cultured for 72 h. Measuring the cell proliferation after 72 h can provide relatively accurate data, as by this time, cells have usually completed at least one growth cycle and reached a relatively stable proliferation state. The CellTiter-Glo luminescent cell viability assay proceeded in an environment avoiding light. Fifteen  μ L of CellTiter- Glo reagent (Promega, G7572) was added to each well and mixed gently for 15 min; then, 60  μ L of cell lysates was transferred to a 96- well optiplate. Fluorescence signals were generated by a luminescence protocol using an Envision microplate reader (PerkinElmer), and data were processed and plotted using GraphPad Prism (version 8.0).  

TRX − TrxR − NADPH Coupling Peroxiredoxin Activity Assay.  The peroxiredoxin activity was determined by recombinant PRDX1 protein in a TRX − TrxR − NADPH coupling assay since TRX is the most common reductant of PRDXs. PRDXs were assayed by coupling their activity to the oxidation of NADPH  via  TrxR. The assay was conducted in 96-well plates as previously described.   Celastrol and derivative compounds were incubated with PRDX1 − PRDX6 proteins for 0.5 h at room temperature in each assay well. Then, the mixture solution containing 0.3  μ M yeast TRX, 0.15  μ M yeast TrxR, and 200 μ M NADPH was added to assay wells. The reaction was initiated with 100  μ M hydrogen peroxide (H 2 O 2 ) and detected with Thermo Multiskan FC at 340 nm. For PRDX1 − 6, the work concentration is 0.6, 10, 5, 20, 0.5, and 50  μ M, respectively. The initial reaction’s slope of assay wells containing a mixture solution and H 2 O 2  without PRDXs and compounds ( S BLANK ) was considered as 100% inhibition of PRDXs’ peroxidase activity. The initial reaction’s slope of assay wells containing mixture solution, H 2 O 2 , and PRDXs without compounds ( S DMSO ) was considered as 0% inhibition of PRDXs’ peroxidase activity. The inhibition rate at different doses of celastrol and derivative compounds ( S compound ) was calculated from the slope of initial reaction. The inhibition rate (%) was calculated from the following equations: ( S compound  − S DMSO )/( S BLANK  − S DMSO )  × 100. The inhibition rates of celastrol and derivative compounds were calculated from three independent experiments. Data were analyzed and plotted using GraphPad Prism (version 8.0).  

Protein Thermal Shift Assay.  Protein thermal shift assay was performed in a QuantStudio 6 Flex Real-Time PCR system (Applied Biosystems) with a buffer containing 20 mM HEPES (pH 7.5) and 150 mM NaCl. The final system was 20  μ L, which contained 10  μ M PRDX1 protein, 1:500 dilution of SYPRO Orange dye (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 5000 ×  stock in DMSO), and DMSO or  15  (final DMSO concentration was 5%). The mixtures were heated from 25 to 95  ° C (2.5  ° C/min), and the protein melting temperature ( Δ T m ) was calculated with fluorescence raw data by the Protein Thermal Shift software v.1.3 (Life Technologies). Data were plotted using GraphPad Prism (version 8.0).  

Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR) Binding Assay.  The SPR binding assay was performed on a Biacore T200 (GE Healthcare) using commercially available CM5 sensor chips at 25  ° C according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The running buffer contained 20 mM HEPES (pH 7.0), 150 mM NaCl, and 1% DMSO. Then, compound 15  was injected at a flow rate of 30  μ L/min to bind with the recombinant PRDX1 protein for 120 s and dissociated for 180 s each cycle. The dissociation constant value of  15  against PRDX1 protein was finally calculated by the Biacore T200 software Version 1.0 to evaluate the binding affinity. Data were plotted using GraphPad Prism (version 8.0).  

Mass Spectrometry Assay.  For intact mass analysis, PRDX1 protein samples (2 mg/mL) were incubated with an equivalent molar ratio of  15  in buffer containing 20 mM HEPES (pH 7.0), 100 mM NaCl overnight before mass spectral procedures. For mass analysis, protein samples were diluted to a concentration of 1 mg/mL with 0.1% formic acid. Two mg of each protein sample was used for the LC/MS run. Regular sets of LC/MS instruments and analysis of protein mass were employed as previously described.  

Crystallization and Structure Determination.  Since the mutation of Cys 52 and Cys 83 significantly improved the quality of the PRDX1 protein crystal, we used mutant PRDX1 at Cys 52 and Cys 83 for crystallization. The purified mutant PRDX1 protein (PRDX1 − 175aa ) was concentrated to 6 mg/mL and then mixed with a crystallization solution that contains 20% PEG3350 (v/ v) and 8% Tacsimate pH 8.0 (v/v) in a 1:1 volume ratio. After 2 weeks at 18  ° C, diffraction-quality crystals were obtained using the sitting-drop vapor-diffusion method and transferred to a buffer containing 20% PEG3350 (v/v) and 8% Tacsimate pH 8.0 (v/v); 100 μ M compounds  16  and  6  and incubated for at least 15 days at 18  ° C. Before data collection, the crystals were transferred into a cryo- protectant solution (containing 20% PEG3350 (v/v), 8% Tacsimate pH 8.0 (v/v), 18% glycerol) and flash-frozen into liquid nitrogen. X- ray diffraction data were collected at the BL19U1 beamline of Shanghai Synchrotron Radiation Facility (SSRF), integrated using the XDS package, and scaled using the Scala module of the CCP4 software. The structure was solved using molecular replacement using the Phaser module of the Phenix software, with the dimer of PRDX1 (PDB code: 7WET) as the search template. Then, the initial model after molecular replacement was fixed using the ARP/wARP module of CCP4 and Coot. The atomic coordinates of  16  and  6  were determined according to the electron density omit map, using the LigandFit module of Phenix. The final model was adjusted in Coot and finished after cycles of refinement in Phenix. The statistics for crystal data collection and refinement are summarized in  Supporting Table S1 . The atomic coordinates and structure factor files have been deposited to the Protein Data Bank (PDB) with accession numbers 8 ×  71 and 8  ×  73 for  16  and compound  6 , respectively.  

Cell Apoptosis Assay.  SW620 cells (5  ×  10 5   cells) were seeded in 6-well plates overnight, and then DMSO/Celastrol/ 15  was added to the cells with different concentrations. After another incubation of 48 h, both adherent and floating cells were collected and washed once with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS). Resuspended cells were incubated with 100  μ L of binding buffer containing 1  μ L of PI and 1  μ L of Annexin V at room temperature for 15 min in the dark (BD Pharmingen). Apoptosis was measured immediately using the flow cytometry (Beckman cyteflex).  

Cell Cycle Arrest Assay.  SW620 cells (5  ×  10 5   cells) were seeded in 6-well plates overnight, and then DMSO/Celastrol/ 15  was added to the dishes with different concentrations. After incubation for 24 h, the cells were harvested and washed with cold PBS. Then, the cells were fixed in ethanol (75%) at  − 20  ° C for at least 12 h. The cells were centrifuged, and the ethanol was discarded. The resuspended cells were incubated with 500  μ L PI/RNase and cultured for 15 min. The cells were analyzed by flow cytometry (BD Pharmingen).  

Measurement of ROS.  SW620 cells (5  ×  10 5   cells) were seeded in 6-well plates overnight and treated with celastrol or  15  for 48 h, respectively. The cells were harvested and washed with serum-free medium, followed by incubation with DCFH-DA (1:1000000) for 10 min at 37  ° C in the dark. DCFH-DA was deacetylated by intracellular esterase to a nonfluorescent compound, which was later oxidized by intracellular ROS to the fluorescent DCF. DCF fluorescence was detected using a flow cytometer (BD Pharmingen).  

Measurement of Mitochondrial Membrane Potential by Flow Cytometry.  SW620 cells (5  ×  10 5   cells) were seeded in 6-well plates overnight and treated with celastrol or  15  for 48 h. The cells were harvested, which was followed by incubation with 0.5 mL JC-1 (Beyotime Cat No. C2006) for 20 min at 37  ° C in the dark. After incubation, the mixture was centrifuged at 1200 rpm for 5 min, followed by discarding the supernatant and washed with JC-1 dyeing buffer twice. Finally, the cells were suspended with 300  μ L JC-1 staining buffer, and the fluorescence intensity was detected by flow cytometry.  

Measurement of Mitochondrial Membrane Potential by Fluorescence Imaging.  SW620 cells (1  ×  10 4   cells) were seeded in 24-well plates overnight and treated with  15  (0.5 and 1  μ M) for 48 h. The cells were harvested, which was followed by incubation with 0.5 mL JC-1 (Beyotime Cat No. C2006) for 20 min at 37  ° C in the dark. After incubation, the samples were collected and imaged using a laser scanning confocal fluorescence microscopy (Leica TCSSP8). The excitation wavelengths of JC-1 monomer and aggregate were 488 and 568 nm, respectively. The pictures were analyzed by ImageJ software.  

Western Blot Analysis.  SW620 cells (5  ×  10 5   cells) were seeded in 6-well plates overnight and treated with DMSO or  15  for 48 h. Then, the total proteins were extracted with RIPA lysate (Beyotime) and determined by a BCA protein assay kit (Beyotime). All of the protein samples were analyzed by SDS-PAGE (10% gel). Then, the proteins were detected by the conventional method. Since  γ H2AX is the maker of DNA damage and Cyt C, Bcl-2, and Bcl-xL are marker proteins of cell apoptosis related to mitochondrial dysfunction, we used this assay to examine their expression.  

P harm a co kinetics Study of Compound  15 .  Three mice were used to conduct this experiment. Compound  15  was administered by tail vein injection at a dose of 2 mg/kg. Blood samples were collected 0.05 mL each time from the cheeks at 5, 15, 30 min and 1, 2, 4, 6, 8, 24 h after the administration. The concentration of compound  15  in mouse plasma samples was determined by liquid chromatography- tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS), and the pharmacokinetic parameters were calculated by WinNolin software.  

Acute Toxicity Assay.  The animal experiments were approved by the Animal Research Committee of Shanghai Jiao Tong University and performed following established guidelines. For acute toxicity test, female C57 mice were randomly divided into three groups (6 per group). Each group of mice was intra peritoneal ly injected with vehicle (2% DMSO/5% PEG-400/5% Tween-80/88% ddH 2 O), 20 mg/kg celastrol, or 20 mg/kg  15 . After one dose, the weight and survival of the mice were recorded daily. Two weeks later, all mice were sacrificed and livers, lungs, hearts, spleens, and kidneys were separated, followed by a fixation process with 4% formaldehyde overnight. Then, tissues were dehydrated and embedded in paraffin. The slices were then stained with hematoxylin and observed under a microscope and photographed.  

In Vivo Antitumor Activity.  Since compound  15  exhibited the most potent cellular activity against SW620 cells, we used these cancer cells to establish the xenograft mouse model. SW620 cells (10 6   /mice) were subcutaneously injected into BALB/c female nude mice. When most of the tumors reached approximately 75 mm 3 , the mice were randomly divided into 3 groups. Each group of mice was intra peritoneal ly injected with vehicle (2% DMSO, 5% PEG-400, 5% Tween-80), 2 mg/kg celastrol, or 2 mg/kg  15  for once a day according to  in vivo  PK parameters of  15 . The tumor size was measured using a caliper, and the tumor volume was calculated with the formula  V  = 1/2 (length  ×  width 2 ). After treatment for 16 days, the mice were sacrificed and the tumors were collected and photographed. Tumor tissues were embedded in paraffin wax, followed by immuno his to chemistry analysis.  

Immuno his to chemistry (IHC) Assay.  Xenograft tumors from nude mice were fixed in 4% para formaldehyde for 24 h and embedded in paraffin. Sections were made using paraffin microtomy and baked at 65  ° C overnight. After rehydration in a series of graded alcohols, the sections were treated with citrate buffer (pH = 6.0) and hydrogen peroxide. After blocking in 5% BSA for 1 h, the sections were incubated with the corresponding primary antibodies ( K i -67, TUNEL) at 4  ° C overnight, followed by incubation with a specific HRP-conjugated secondary antibody. Detection was accomplished using a 3,3 ′ -diaminobenzidine (DAB) substrate kit (Solarbio).
