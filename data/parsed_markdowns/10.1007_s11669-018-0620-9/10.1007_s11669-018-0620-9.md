# Interdiffusion in the Face-Centered Cubic Phase of the Co-Al-W-Ta System Between 1090 and 1240   C  

A. Epishin 1  •   A. Chyrkin  •   G. Nolze 4  •   J. Midtlyng  •   H. M. Mayer 1  • N. Petrushin 5  •   W. Reimers 1  

Submitted: 3 November 2017 / in revised form: 2 February 2018   ASM International 2018  

Abstract  Interdiffusion of Al, W, Ta and Co in a Co-base alloy at temperatures between 1090 and 1240   C has been investigated. The interdiffusion coefﬁcients were found to be close to those reported for these elements in Ni-base alloys. Combining the diffusion simulation software DIC- TRA with the Ni-base diffusion databases TCNi 5  and MobNi 3 , the interdiffusion proﬁles of Co, Al W, and Ta were modeled for Co9Al8W2Ta/Co diffusion couples annealed at different temperatures and for different times. The results show that interdiffusion in the Co-Al-W-Ta alloys can be modeled reasonably well using the available commercial databases for thermodynamics and kinetics of Ni-base systems.  

Keywords  alloys    interdiffusion    modeling  

# 1 Introduction  

Co-base superalloys are a new class of high-temperature materials still under development, which have  c / c 0 -mi- 1 ]   crostructures similar to those of Ni-base superalloys. One of the promising alloy subsystems within this group is Co- Al-W-Ta, where Co is the base, Al is a  c 0 -forming element, W is a solid-solution strengthener of the  c -matrix and a  c 0 -   stabilizer, and Ta increases the  c 0 solvus temperature. It is expected that  c 0 -strengthened Co-base alloys might sub- stitute Ni-base superalloys in selected high-temperature applications. Therefore, many research groups intensively develop and investigate alloys of this system, e.g. Ref  2 - 15 .  

Modeling of processes controlled by diffusion, such as creep deformation, oxidation, homogenization during heat treatment, and evolution of the microstructures at high temperatures, requires reliable diffusion data for the system under study. Databases for this purpose are therefore desired, but currently the number of reports contributing such data for Co-base alloys is comparatively small, e.g. Ref  8 ,  9 ,  14 . Occasionally, research groups use diffusion data for Ni-base alloys instead, available from the well- developed diffusion databases for Ni-base alloys, like in Ref  15 . However, the applicability of Ni-base data to the modeling of Co-base alloys lacks any veriﬁcation.  

The aim of the present work is to investigate the dif- fusion mobility of alloying elements in the Co-rich part of the Co-Al-W-Ta system. The interdiffusion coefﬁcients obtained experimentally are presented in comparison with those reported in literature for Co- and Ni-base alloys. The measured concentration proﬁles are compared to proﬁles modeled with the diffusion simulation software DICTRA in combination with the commercial databases TCNi 5  and MobNi 3 . The validity of such simulations, using diffusion data for Ni in place of that for Co, is discussed.  

# 2 Experimental  

The objects of this investigation were diffusion couples made of alloy Co9Al8W2Ta (at.%) welded together with pure cobalt. A 20 g specimen of this alloy was cast at the Helmholtz–Zentrum Berlin under vacuum in a levitation melting induction furnace. In order to provide good mixing of the elements, the casting was remelted several times. After casting, the specimen was homogenized at 1240   C for 192 h under vacuum. The alloy composition was checked by electron probe microanalysis (EPMA). Aver- aging the EPMA results measured at 20 spot positions yielded the composition given in Table  1 .  

Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) showed that at room temperature, the alloy has a cuboidal  c / c 0 -mi- crostructure similar to that of Ni-base superalloys. No   phases different from  c  and  c 0 were observed.  

Furthermore, the homogenized alloy was investigated by differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) in a DSC instru- ment, Linseis HDSC PT 1750. The heating and cooling DSC curves, Fig.  1 , show only one phase transformation, c \$ c 0 , from which follows that the alloy’s crystal structure remains cubic within the complete scanned temperature interval; namely A1 for  c  and L1 2  for  c 0 . The interpretation of the DSC curves gave the critical temperatures 1085   C ( c 0 -solvus), 1379   C (solidus), and 1448   C (liquidus).  

One diffusion couple was assembled from disks of the Co9Ak8W2Ta alloy and pure cobalt. The disks were 4 mm thick and had a diameter of 12 mm. They were ground with abrasive papers and polished on one side using diamond powder pastes, ﬁnishing with a particle size of 1  l m. The preparation was performed using special ring holders in order to ensure perfectly parallel planes, a prerequisite to obtaining continuous contact of the joining surfaces during diffusion welding. To protect the joining surfaces against oxidation or any kind of contamination, the polished disks were stored in high-purity acetone.  

The diffusion welding was performed under a vacuum - 5   of about 10 mbar in a thermo mechanical simulator, 16 ]   Gleeble 3800, using direct resistant heating. To ensure continuous contact of the joining surfaces, the disks were strongly compressed at room temperature, at a stress of -  100 MPa. Diffusion experiments of different durations were performed at temperatures above the  c 0 -solvus of the Co- alloy (1085   C), namely at 1090   C for 1524 h, at 1140   C for 762 h, at 1190   C for 384 h, and at 1240   C for 192 h. Pure cobalt undergoes an allotropic   17 ] transformation hcp  ?  fcc at temperatures 400-450 C, hence it is also fcc at these temperatures. Thus, in our experiments, diffusion occurred in the fcc solid solution of Co. After the annealing, the specimens were quenched: The ampoules were rapidly removed from the furnace and broken on a cold, thick steel plate. The cooling rate was about 100   C/s.  

For investigation by light microscope and EPMA, the annealed specimens were grinded and then polished with diamond pastes, ﬁnishing with a particle size of 1  l m. For further investigation by electron backscatter diffraction (EBSD), the specimens were additionally polished with colloidal silica (0.25  l m particle size) in order to remove the remaining deformed layer.  

The EPMA measurements were performed in a micro- probe, JEOL JXA-8900RL. The concentration proﬁles were measured in the direction perpendicular to the weld interface, with a step size of 5  l m. To reduce the scatter caused by local concentration variations, the electron beam was defocused. The effective spot diameter was in the range of 5  l m.  

EBSD measurements were carried out in a scanning electron microscope, LEO 1530 VP (Carl Zeiss Meditec, Oberkochen, Germany), which is equipped with an EBSD - HR detector, e Flash , controlled by ESPRIT 1.94 software (Bruker, Berlin, Germany).

# 3 Numerical and Analytical Modeling  

The numerical modeling of interdiffusion was carried out 24 ]   using the diffusion simulation software DICTRA. The calculations were performed assuming that a diffusion couple represents two plates that are inﬁnite along the interface plane but have ﬁnite thickness of 4 mm, like in the experiment. The interdiffusion proﬁles were calculated using the homogenization model implemented in 25 ]   DICTRA. The chemical potentials were calculated using the commercial database for Ni-base superalloys 26 ]   TCNi 5 . The atomic mobilities were retrieved from the 27 ]   kinetic database MobNi 3 . The concentrations deter- mined by EPMA (see Table  1 ) were used as input for the alloy composition.

The reason for this porosity is that the ﬂux of atoms from the Co-alloy into pure cobalt, speciﬁcally that of aluminium atoms, is higher than the ﬂux of Co-atoms in the opposite direction. While mutual ﬂuxes sum to zero, the net ﬂow of atoms causes a net ﬂow of vacancies into the alloy’s part of the diffusion couple. This results in their accumulation and in pore formation. # 5 Summary and Conclusions  

1. Interdiffusion coefﬁcients were measured in Co9Al8W2Ta/Co diffusion couples annealed at tem- peratures between 1090 and 1240   C. At these tem- peratures, both parts of the diffusion couples have an fcc crystal structure. The interdiffusion coefﬁcients measured for diffusion of Al, W and Ta in this Co-base alloy system were found to be close to those reported for diffusion in Ni-base alloys. That is reasonable, because of the similarities between Co and Ni. These elements occupy the neighboring positions in the periodic table, respectively 27 and 28, and belong to the same group VIIIB.

 2. Modeling of interdiffusion in the Co9Al8W2Ta/Co diffusion couples using the diffusion simulation soft- ware DICTRA with the commercial Ni-base databases TCNi 5  and MobNi 3  predicted the concentration pro- ﬁles in good agreement with experiment. Also, the observed Kirkendall porosity was approximately cor- rectly forecasted.

 3. We conclude from 1 and 2 that modeling of diffusion- ally controlled processes in the Co-base alloys using the Ni-base diffusion databases is reasonable for engineering approximations. In contrast to the Boltzmann solution for the problem of cross-diffusion between two semi-inﬁnite plates (Eq  1 ), which could be used for estimation of the diffusion length  L , modeling with DICTRA permits to predict special diffusional effects like the formation of kinks in the concentration proﬁles at the weld interface.  

4. Alloying of Co by Al, W and Ta with concentrations of about 5.5, 1.1 and 0.6% (atomic fractions in percent) stabilizes the fcc lattice, while areas of the Co solid solution with lower concentrations of Al, W and Ta undergo a sequential phase transformation hcp  ? fcc  ?  hcp during the heating  ?  holding  ?  cooling cycle 20   C  ?  1240   C/192 h  ?  20   C. During this transformation, a parent Co hcp-crystallite divides into plenty of hcp-crystallites of seven different orientations.
