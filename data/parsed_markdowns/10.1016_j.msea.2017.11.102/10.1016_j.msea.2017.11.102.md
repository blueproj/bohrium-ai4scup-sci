# A B S T R A C T  

# A R T I C L E I N F O  

The present work introduces a novel circular notched miniature tensile creep (CNMTC) specimen which is used to study the in ﬂ uence of notches on creep and of multiaxial stress states on microstructural evolution in Ni-based single crystal (SX) superalloys. It is brie ﬂ y discussed how mild circular notches a ﬀ ect the stress state in the notch root of a tensile bar during elastic loading. Then the stress redistribution under creep conditions is calculated using the  ﬁ nite element method (FEM), assuming isotropic material behavior. Two series of interrupted creep experiments with the Ni-based single crystal superalloy ERBO1 (CMSX-4 type) were then performed at 950 °C, with  ﬂ at uniaxial miniature tensile creep (FUMTC) and CNMTC < 100 > specimens, respectively. The evolution of cavities and microcracks in both types of specimens was carefully analyzed after 81, 169, and 306 h. In the uniaxial experiments, a growth of cast pores and the formation of new creep cavities were observed. These degradation processes were much less pronounced in the circular notched specimens. The results of the present work are discussed in the light of previous  ﬁ ndings on the in ﬂ uence of multiaxial stress states on creep in single crystal superalloys.  

Keywords: Ni-based superalloy single crystals Creep Circular notched tensile specimens Stress state Cavity Microcrack  

# 2. Material, experiments and methods  

# 1. Introduction  

Single crystal Ni-base superalloys (SXs) are used for gas turbines in aero engines and in fossil  ﬁ red power plants which operate in the creep regime  [1] . The creep behavior of SXs under uniaxial tensile loading has been extensively studied. Most components, however, operate under multiaxial stress states and little information is available on the in ﬂ uence of stress multiaxiality on creep. The presence of notches causes multiaxial stress states and produces stress concentrations  [2 – 4] . Sharp notches in tensile specimens can induce high stress concentra- tions which lead to notch root cracking and early rupture. Mild notches, on the other hand, may alter the stress state in the whole cross section of the specimen without leading to crack initiation  [5 – 9] . The present work focuses on mild circular notches as shown in  Fig. 1 .  

# 2.1. Material  

In the present study we investigate miniature creep specimens with and without notches which were machined from laboratory slabs of the single crystal superalloy ERBO1 (CMSX-4 type). The average chemical composition of this material is given in  Table 1 . All details describing the heat treatment and the microstructure of this superalloy have been documented in the literature  [13 – 15] .  

The microstructure of the material investigated in the present work is shown in the scanning electron microscopy (SEM) micrograph in Fig. 2 . The material shows a  γ / γ ’ -microstructure which is characteristic for Ni-base SX  [1] . The SEM micrograph shown in  Fig. 2  was obtained using a SEM from Carl Zeiss AG, type Leo Gemini 1530 VP. All details describing SEM specimen preparation and details of SEM operation are published elsewhere  [16 – 19] .  

The objective of the present work is to compare the SX creep be- havior of circular notched miniature tensile creep (CNMTC) specimens with the creep behavior of  ﬂ at uniaxial miniature tensile creep (FUMTC) specimens  [10 – 12] , with a special focus on strain accumu- lation and on the nucleation and growth of creep cavities. Preliminary ﬁ nite element creep stress calculations are performed to study the multiaxial stress states in CNMTC specimens and to  ﬁ nd out how stresses redistribute during creep.

# 2.3. Creep testing  

The 950 °C creep tests were performed following the procedure re- cently described by Wollgramm et al.  [12] . The specimens were mounted in the creep machine and heated up to the test temperature in a furnace with three independently controlled heating zones. The test temperatures were measured at the upper and lower ends of the gage sections of the specimens with two Pt10Rh/Pt thermocouples. Ceramic rod in tube extensometers were used to transfer creep displacements to two linear vertical transducers outside the furnace. The displacement signal was obtained as the average of these two measurements. The specimens were heated under a small preload and the test temperature of 950 °C was reached after about 2 h. Once the test temperature was reached, the full load was applied in a few seconds.  

A uniaxial stress and a nominal net section stress of 300 MPa were applied on FUMTC and CNMTC specimens. For each creep test with a CNMTC specimen, this net section stress was applied by adjusting the loading with respect to the true net section diameter calculated from the dimensions listed in  Table 2 . Three uniaxial < 100 > tensile tests (numbered 1 – 3 in  Table 2 ) were performed using FUMTC specimens. Another three 4 – 6 in  Table 2 ) were performed with CNMTC specimens. For each specimen geometry, three creep tests were interrupted after 81, 169 and 306 h.  Fig. 5 a and b show SEM micrographs of the CNMTC specimen 4 before and after creep. It can be seen that the overall de- formation in the region of the notch is small. High temperature oxi- dation has altered the appearance of the specimen surface but the overall e ﬀ ect of oxidation on the notch geometry is small. After creep testing, the longitudinal cross sections of all the crept specimens were prepared. The specimens were then carefully investigated to  ﬁ nd out whether crack initiation occurred in the notch roots, and how much cavitation damage had accumulated.

# 3.1. Stress distribution in CNMTC specimens  

It is well known that stresses in a notch root cross section redis- tribute during creep  [23 – 25] .  Fig. 6 a indicates Neuber's prediction for the elastic longitudinal stress in the notch root as a full square symbol. The elastic FEM solution is shown as a dashed line, which coincides with Neuber's analytical solution in the notch root.  Fig. 6 b shows results from FEM creep calculations for the targeted specimen geometry. Here x  represents the distance from the notch root and  d  the notch root diameter. Calculated stress pro ﬁ les  σ L (x/d) which were established after creep periods of 1, 9, 25, 100 and 1000 h are shown. The type of results shown in  Fig. 6 b have been discussed in detail in the literature [23 – 25] . In view of the scienti ﬁ c objectives of the present work it is important to highlight two facts. First, for the creep conditions which are considered in the present work, the high notch root stresses in loading direction relax from over 400 MPa to about 300 MPa within the ﬁ rst 25 h of creep. Second, by comparing the results which are obtained for 100 and 1000 h creep exposure one can conclude that after 100 h a large part of the stress redistribution (from the elastic solution shown in Fig. 6 a to the 1000 h creep solution in  Fig. 6 b) has already taken place.  

For the objective of this work it is important to  ﬁ nd out how geo- metric inaccuracies associated with specimen machining a ﬀ ect the stress distribution. For this purpose additional elastic and creep FEM calculations were performed for specimens 4, 5, and 6 from  Table 2 . The results are presented in  Fig. 7 , where  Fig. 7 a shows the elastic solutions of specimens 4, 5, 6, and the results for the target geometry for reference. The larger symbols on the y-axis of  Fig. 7 a (notch root,  x / d  = 0) indicate the results which were obtained using Neuber's for- mula. The curves represent the elastic FEM results. The same holds for the FEM creep stress results obtained for 100 h of creep shown in  Fig. 7 b. # 3.3. Cavities and absence of notch root cracking  

Fig. 11 a and b show SEM micrographs of cavities in FUMTC speci- mens, which were interrupted after 81 and 306 h creep. The central part of the 306 h specimen (blue rectangle) is shown at a higher mag- ni ﬁ cation in  Fig. 11 c. After 81 h, most of the cavities represent micro cast pores, as reported by Buck et al. # 4. Discussion  

# 4.1. Circular notched miniature creep specimens  

In the present work we show that FUMTC specimens can be used as blanks from which cylindrical CNMTC specimens can be machined with reasonable accuracy ( Figs. 3 – 5  and  Table 2 ). In case of the CNMTC specimens subjected to a net section stress of 300 MPa, signi ﬁ cant amount of creep strain accumulation occurs in the region of the notch. Therefore scatter in notch geometry results in scatter in creep dis- placements, as shown in  Fig. 10 c. This scatter mainly occurs in the early stages of creep. Eventually all CNMTC specimens creep at a similar rate. This rate is in agreement with the FEM results, which are based on a Norton law which cannot account for primary and tertiary creep e ﬀ ects, Fig. 10 c. The early scatter in creep displacements is probably also re- lated to the fact, that the CNMTC specimens only contain a few (e.g. 3) dendrites and that the volume of the notch region is not large enough to provide a good average for the heterogeneous cast microstructure of SX Ni-base superalloys with its dendritic and interdendritic regions  [13] .  

# 4.2. FEM stress/strain calculation  

The creep FEM results obtained in the present work provide some important information. First they show that the analytical value for the axial stress in the notch root is in good agreement with the elastic FEM results ( Figs. 6 and 7 ). Second they show that in agreement with what

Further work is required to reveal the true nature of the stress distribution under anisotropic creep conditions and to account for the primary and tertiary creep.  

4.3. Notch root cracking and cavitation  

Yue et al.  [8]  investigated a similar SX superalloy with a lower Re content, which probably made this alloy less creep resistant than the material investigated in the present work. The authors also considered a sharp notch, as opposed to the mild circular notch considered in the present work. In their creep experiments with sharper notches, they observed the nucleation of cracks in the notch root. In contrast, the CNMTC specimens with mild notches studied in the present work did not show notch root cracking. This is most probably due to the fact, that the fast stress redistribution in the early stages of creep reduces the high axial stress in the notch root quickly. Our CNMTC specimens produce a triaxial tensile stress state which does not promote notch root cracking within the creep exposure times considered in this work. Therefore it seems attractive to perform further experiments with this specimen geometry to investigate the in ﬂ uence of stress states on the directional coarsening of the  γ / γ ’  microstructure. The comparison of  Fig. 11  and Fig. 12  shows that uniaxial testing (FUMTC specimens) causes sig- ni ﬁ cantly more creep cavitation than the testing of CNMTC specimens. This shows that the triaxial type of tensile stress state which char- acterizes the CNMTC specimens does not govern cavity formation and growth. Instead, high temperature plasticity (i.e. accumulated creep strain) seems to be a critical parameter. After 306 h of uniaxial creep exposure, the FUMTC specimen has already developed small micro cracks, which seem to have the potential to initiate  ﬁ nal rupture, Fig. 11 . Such micro cracks were however not observed in CNMTC specimens under the same creep conditions. This suggests that cavity nucleation and growth in Ni-base SX cannot proceed without plasticity. Under the creep conditions considered in the present work, the hy- drostatic tensile stress state alone is not su ﬃ cient to promote cavity nucleation and growth. It would be interesting to  ﬁ nd out whether this also holds at higher temperatures.  

# 5. Summary and conclusions  

The present work compares the creep behavior of uniaxial (FUMTC) and circular notched (CNMTC) specimens of the Ni-based single crystal superalloy ERBO1 at a net section stress of 300 MPa and a temperature of 950 °C. Creep tests were interrupted after 81, 169, and 306 h. Isotropic FEM creep calculations were performed in parallel to study the stress and strain states in CNMTC specimens. From the results ob- tained in the present study the following conclusions can be drawn:  

(1) Creep tests performed at 300 MPa and 950 °C show that the CNMTC specimens accumulate much smaller displacements than FUMTC specimens, when the uniaxial stress in the FUMTC specimens equals the net section stress in the CNMTC specimens.

 (2) It can be concluded from the FEM stress and strain calculations that (i) stresses redistribute fast under creep conditions, (ii) creep results in a more homogeneous stress state over the notch cross section than elastic loading and (iii) in the case of CNMTC specimens most of the creep displacement accumulated in the notched region of the specimen.

 (3) FEM results show that unavoidable inaccuracies associated with the machining of miniature circular notched specimens can be toler- ated, because they only have a small e ﬀ ect ( ≈ 5%) on the stress parameters which characterize the multiaxial stress states.
