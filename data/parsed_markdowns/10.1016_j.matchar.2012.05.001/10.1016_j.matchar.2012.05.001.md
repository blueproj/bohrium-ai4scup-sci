# A B S T R A C T  

Article history: Received 23 January 2012 Received in revised form 2 May 2012 Accepted 5 May 2012  

Fourteen nickel-base superalloy single crystals containing a range of chromium (Cr), cobalt (Co), molybdenum (Mo), and rhenium (Re) levels, and fixed amounts of aluminum (Al) and tantalum (Ta), were examined to determine the effect of bulk composition on basic microstructural parameters, including  γ ′  solvus,  γ ′  volume fraction, topologically close-packed (TCP) phases,  γ and  γ ′  phase chemistries, and  γ – γ ′  lattice mismatch. Regression models describing the influence of bulk alloy composition on each of the microstructural parameters were developed and compared to predictions by a commercially-available software tool that used computational thermodynamics. Co produced the largest change in  γ ′  solvus over the wide compositional range explored and Mo produced the biggest effect on the  γ  lattice parameter over its range, although Re had a very potent influence on all microstructural parameters investigated. Changing the Cr, Co, Mo, and Re contents in the bulk alloy had an impact on their concentrations in the  γ  matrix and to a smaller extent in the  γ ′  phase. The software tool under-predicted  γ ′  solvus temperatures and  γ ′  volume fractions, and over-predicted TCP phase volume fractions at 982 °C. However, the statistical regression models provided excellent estimations of the microstructural parameters and demonstrated the usefulness of such formulas.

# 1. Introduction

Co has also been reported to improve alloy stability  [4 – 7] . However, the key alloying strategy employed in this work involved replacing some of the strategic and high density Re, and all of the high density tungsten (W), with Mo for solid solution strengthening. Mo is known to be a potent solid solution strengthener, but has a density almost half that of either Re or W.  

A series of nickel (Ni)-base superalloy single crystals has been developed recently  [1 – 3]  in a new compositional design space for potential turbine blade applications. This alloy series, termed LDS (low density single crystal), was explored in an effort to produce single crystal alloys with low densities, while concur- rently achieving high temperature creep resistance, microstruc- tural stability, and excellent oxidation resistance. The Ni-base alloy contained fixed contents of aluminum (Al) and tantalum (Ta) for  γ ′  precipitation, as well as variations in the levels of chromium (Cr), cobalt (Co), molybdenum (Mo), and rhenium (Re). Cr was added to the alloy for improved oxidation resistance and was explored  [1 – 3]  at relatively low levels to determine minimum Cr concentrations required for sufficient oxidation  

The alloys in the present paper represent the compositional design space described above, which is considered novel because of the relatively high Mo content combined with a low Cr level in single crystal form. Superalloy single crystals with high Mo contents were studied earlier  [8 – 10] , but these were cast without Cr, Co, and Re, in order to investigate systematically the effects of lattice mismatch on  γ ′  coarsening and creep behavior in simple model alloys. Other investigations examined the use of higher Mo contents  [11 – 13] , as well as a range of Co  [4,5] , but these alloys did not contain Re and tended to have higher Cr levels. More recent studies  [6,7,14]  have examined the effect of Re on various microstructural features. However, these alloys did not contain high Mo contents and included W, ruthenium (Ru), and titanium (Ti) in their compositions along with a number of other significant alloying differences.  

The purpose of this paper is to characterize more fully the key microstructural parameters that are known to influence the mechanical properties of nickel-base superalloy single crystals. This paper focuses on the effects of alloy composition on microstructural parameters, including  γ ′  solvus,  γ ′  volume fraction, topologically close-packed (TCP) phase content,  γ  and γ ′  phase chemistries, and  γ – γ ′  lattice mismatch. In the present study, quantitative assessments of these microstructural fea- tures are provided, and regression models are developed to predict these microstructural parameters from bulk alloy com- position. Ultimately, these microstructural parameters will be used in the development of a creep model that will be described in a future paper. It is expected that the regression models presented herein, coupled with a creep model, will enable the alloy composition to be fine tuned to obtain microstructural features that deliver targeted mechanical properties. Additional- ly, the use of computational thermodynamic modeling tools for application to multi-component Ni-base superalloys has be- come increasingly common. Thus, a commercially-available software package JMatPro 6.0®, an acronym for Java-based Materials Properties software  [15,16] , was employed to predict the microstructural parameters observed experimentally. It is hoped that the experimental data and its interpretation provided in this paper will also enable improvements in the predictive capability of JMatPro and other computational tools.  

# 2. Materials and Experimental Procedures  

A design of experiments approach  [2,3]  was employed that minimized the number of alloys to be cast. Based on this approach, a total of fourteen LDS alloys ( Table 1 ) were cast into single crystal slabs at a commercial casting vendor, PCC Airfoils LLC, Minerva, OH. The aim baseline alloy selected for this design strategy contained 6.1 wt.% Al, 6.2 wt.% Ta, 50 – 100 ppm wt. yttrium (Y), and a balance of Ni. Alloying elements were varied as follows: 0 – 5 wt.% Cr; 0 – 11 wt.% Co; 6 – 12 wt.% Mo; and 0 – 4 wt.% Re. These element ranges were chosen  [2,3]  to allow for good predictive capabilities within the compositional design space, while enabling attractive alloys for potential applications to be explored. Small quantities of carbon (C) and boron (B) were added as grain boundary strengthening elements, which has been done more recently in third and fourth generation alloys to reduce the impact of undesirable grain boundary defects  [2,3] that may be present in turbine blades and to improve superalloy cleanliness  [17] . Hafnium (Hf) was added to selected alloys at a nominal level of 0.2 wt.% for oxidation resistance. The slab castings measured approximately 15 cm long in the single crystal growth direction, 5 cm wide, and 0.6 cm thick. The castings were solution treated at a commercial heat treatment vendor; the solution temperature was reached by a series of step increments to avoid incipient melting and overshooting. Single crystal castings without Hf additions were solution treated for 6 h at 1315 °C, and castings with Hf were solution treated for 6 h at 1305 °C. Because of its lower solidus, LDS-1110 was given a step heat treatment with a solution temperature of 1288 °C for 18 h at Glenn Research Center. All castings were fan quenched in argon from the solution temperature to tempera- tures below 1093 °C at a rate of 43 °C/min.  

Bulk chemical analyses were performed by inductively coupled plasma emission spectroscopy (ICP) at the top and bottom sections of at least one casting of each composition. In addition, trace impurity levels of sulfur (S) were analyzed in selected alloys by Glow-Discharge Mass Spectroscopy (GDMS) at SHIVA Technologies, Syracuse, New York. Pins, nominally 1.6 mm square by 19 mm long, were supplied for this latter analysis.  

Differential Thermal Analysis (DTA) was conducted on LDS alloys in a helium atmosphere using a Netzsch STA 409C instrument. Selected alloys were examined in the as-cast condition, and all alloys were analyzed in the as-solutioned condition. γ ′  area fractions for each alloy were determined using gage length sections of tested creep rupture specimens. Longitudinal sections were polished and etched with 33% acetic acid, 33% water, 33% nitric acid, and 1% hydrofluoric acid. Scanning electron microscopy was used in the secondary electron imaging mode to obtain a minimum of five areas at 5000×. Fovea Pro 4.0 imaging software was used to quantify the area fractions of  γ ′ .  

To determine the volume fraction of third phase precipitates in the LDS alloys, transverse cross-sections from the uniform gage length of tested creep rupture specimens were polished metal lo graphically and examined in the unetched condition using back-scattered electron (BSE) microscopy. BSE images were obtained from a minimum of five locations at 100×, 250× and 500×, and quantitative image analysis software was employed to determine the area fraction of the third phase precipitates. Enriched with heavier elements, third phases appeared bright with BSE microscopy and were easily distin- guishable from the darker background containing the  γ  and  γ ′ phases. Both carbides and TCP phases showed similar bright BSE contrast. As will be described in the results section, in some cases the amount of carbides could be measured separately from the TCP phase content, whereas in other cases, the volume fraction of total third phases represents a sum of both carbide and TCP content.  

Electrolytic phase extraction was performed on the LDS alloys using standard procedures  [4,18] , in order to separate the γ ′  precipitates, as well as the carbides and TCP phases, from the γ  matrix. The carbides and TCP phases were extracted using an electrolyte of 1 wt.% tartaric acid in a solution of 10 vol.% HCl and 90 vol.% methanol. X-ray diffraction of the extracted carbides and TCP phases was used to determine their crystal structures. The  γ ′  precipitates were extracted in three separate runs with an electrolyte of 1 wt.% citric acid and 1 wt.% ammonium sulfate in water. After each extraction, the  γ ′ residue was analyzed by ICP as was the residual liquor from extraction, which was assumed to represent the  γ  matrix.  

γ – γ ′  lattice mismatch measurements were performed in- situ using a Phillips HR-2 X-ray diffractometer on single crystals that had been aged under the following conditions: 1149 °C for 24 h+1079 °C for 8 h+871 °C for 12 h, followed by an argon gas quench to room temperature. The diffractometer had a three- axis goniometer and an incident beam monochromator that passed only the Cu K α 1  wavelength. Each sample was aligned after mounting on the diffractometer by iteratively optimizing the omega, 2-theta, and tilt axes until the difference between the intensity peak and actual position for each axis was less than 0.05°. Data was gathered from the (400) γ ,  γ ′  peaks, and peak positions were obtained with analytical fitting functions including the Split Pearson VII and the Pearson VII functions.  

Regression models for the prediction of the microstructural parameters were developed using JMP® 7.0 by comparing stepwise forward and reverse selection of significant terms, with at least a 90% probability of significance necessary for inclusion of a term. This method allowed for the determination of the influence of specific elements, as well as their interaction terms, on various microstructural parameters. Comparisons to the predictions of JMatPro-Ni 6.0® were also made. Within this material property simulation program, the upper and lower heat treatment limits of 1371 and 871 °C, respectively, were selected for allowing phase equilibrium; the phase distributions were frozen below 871 °C. Unless otherwise noted, all phases were taken into account by the simulation program and were allowed to form. The nickel-base superalloy database and the single crystal database, both available with JMatPro, were found to provide equivalent predictions.  

# 3. Experimental Results  

# 3.1. Chemical Analyses  

The analyzed chemistries of the LDS single crystal slab castings are shown in  Table 1 . The compositions shown are averages of the ICP measurements obtained from the top and bottom sections of the 15 cm long slabs. The variation in the analyzed contents of the main elements from top to bottom of the castings was typically less than 0.1 wt.%, with no obvious trends in the main elements as a function of location within the slab. The mean analyzed chemistries were within 0.3 wt.% of the aims for each main element, but more typically within 0.1 wt.% of the aims. Thus, there was no particular difficulty in obtaining the aim chemistry targets for this alloy composition space.  

The analyzed levels of the interstitial elements, B, C, nitrogen (N), and oxygen (O), were also all within acceptable limits. B and C were intentionally added at very low levels. B has been observed to combine with Cr or Mo to form borides  [19] , and C may combine with Ta or Mo to form carbides. Borides and carbides are known grain boundary strengtheners that can precipitate on undesirable grain boundaries that sometimes form in single crystal castings, and adding these interstitial elements in very small quantities has become a fairly standard practice in ad- vanced single crystal alloys.  

Reactive Y was intentionally added to the alloy design because of its well-known effect of gettering the S impurity through sulfide formation, thus providing improved oxidation resistance. S was typically measured to be 3 ppm wt. in these castings,  Table 1 . Considerable effort has been expended by superalloy manufacturers to reduce the S impurity in superalloy melts to levels below 10 – 20 ppm wt.  [19] , and it should be noted that the LDS alloys had measured S contents nearly an order of magnitude lower than this limit. However, the retained Y content was observed to vary significantly from top to bottom in many of the single crystal castings. The reason for this variation is that Y can react with the common shell molds used to cast superalloys, so obtaining the targeted level of retained Y can be a challenge. It is evident in  Table 1  that the top sections of the single crystal castings consistently possessed Y levels in a desirable range between 0.0040 and 0.0080 wt.%. However, the bottom sections of the slabs were often enriched in Y at levels significantly above the desired maximum of 0.01 wt.%. The enrichment in Y was sometimes observed to have caused a small amount (<0.6 vol.%) of incipient melting in the bottom casting sections during solutioning, as evidenced by spheroidal, Y-rich precipitates and associated pores present in low quantities in the bottom sections. Less than 1 vol.% of incipient melting is generally considered acceptable by industry standards. Never- theless in this study, only the top halves of each slab were used, in an effort to restrict the retained Y levels to an acceptable range and to eliminate incipient melting.  

# 3.2. Differential Thermal Analysis  

Differential Thermal Analysis (DTA) was performed on selected alloys in the as-cast condition and on all alloys in the as- solutioned condition. The thermograms from DTA of the as-cast samples exhibited more gradual transitions through trans- formations, particularly through the  γ ′  solvus reaction, than the as-solutioned samples. This difference between the as-cast and as-solutioned materials is likely the result of dendritic segregation effects. Other investigators  [20]  have used heat treated samples to enhance the detection of the  γ ′  reaction during DTA. In the present study, as-cast samples exhibited  γ ′ solvus temperatures that were up to 5 °C lower and solidus and liquidus temperatures that were up to 12 °C higher than as- solutioned material. This difference was consistent with work previously reported  [21] . The DTA results described below will focus primarily on that obtained from as-solutioned samples, because it is in this more homogeneous condition that the material will be used.  

In the present study, single crystal samples were heated through the full melting range to a maximum temperature of 1500 °C, and cooling curves were subsequently obtained. Because the samples were brought through melting, the material on cooling had an as-cast structure. Thus,  γ ′  solvus, solidus, and liquidus temperatures were obtained from the heating curves only. Duplicate, as-solutioned samples showed consistent results and exhibited solvus, solidus, and liquidus temperatures within 2 °C. Co is well known to have a strong effect on reducing  γ ′  solvus [4 – 6,14] , and the figure demonstrates that the  γ ′  solvus decreased by nearly 50 °C as the Co level was increased from 0 to 11 wt.%. # 3.3. γ ′  Volume Fraction  

The volume fraction of the  γ ′  phase was obtained from longitudinal sections of creep rupture tested specimens. During creep rupture testing, all LDS alloys developed lamellar  γ – γ ′ microstructures, also called  γ ′  rafting  [8,9,22 – 25] , which formed perpendicular to the applied stress direction. This  γ – γ ′  mor- phology was very amenable to quantitative assessment. How- ever, since the creep samples were slowly furnace cooled after rupture, the  γ ′  volume fraction in the post-tested specimens was not representative of that at the test temperature. As a result, small sections of the specimen gage lengths were re- heated and held at the testing temperature for 0.5 h and then were water quenched to freeze in the microstructure. Area fractions of the  γ ′  phase were then determined for each alloy at each creep testing temperature.  Fig. 3 a and b displays the  γ – γ ′ microstructures that were representative of testing at 982 and 1093 °C, respectively, for LDS-4583. The area fraction of  γ ′  can be considered equivalent to the volume fraction in this case, since the extent of  γ ′  through the depth can be reasonably assumed to be equal to that across the field of view used for measurement. It was evident by comparing  Fig. 3 a and b that the  γ ′  volume fraction was higher at 982 °C.  

The measured volume fraction of  γ ′  is shown as a function of temperature from 760 to 1310 °C for a subset of the LDS alloys in  Fig. 4 . The alloys displayed in the figure represent the extremes in the LDS compositional space, and the volume fractions presented in the figure bracket the range observed at 982 and 1093 °C. Although the majority of data was obtained from specimens creep tested at 982 and 1093 °C, additional measurements were acquired from selected alloys creep tested at 760, 816, 871, and 1038 °C. The  γ ′  solvus temperatures are represented at 0 vol.% in  Fig. 4  and were obtained from the previously described DTA measurements; the alloys chosen for the figure also bracket the range in solvus temperatures observed. It may be seen in  Fig. 4  and  Table 2  that the volume fraction of  γ ′  was above 60 vol.% for all the alloys at 982 °C but decreased steadily as the temperature increased from 1000 °C to the solvus temperatures.

Aging for short times of 5 h at 982 or 1093 °C tended to produce some M 6 C as well, although the total vol.% of carbides remained low. It is possible that M 6 C was also present in the same amount in these alloys in the as- solutioned condition but such small quantities were not easily detectible.

# 3.6. Lattice Parameters of  γ  and  γ ′  Phases  

Lattice parameters of the  γ  and  γ ′  phases were measured  in-situ on single crystal samples at room temperature by X-ray diffraction. The alloys were solutioned and then aged in a multi-step heat treatment of 1149 °C/24 h, 1079 °C/8 h, and 871 °C/12 h, followed by an argon gas quench to room temper- ature. This aging treatment was performed in order to overage the material and lose coherency between the  γ  and  γ ′  phases, so that unconstrained lattice parameters and unconstrained  γ – γ ′ lattice mismatch could be obtained  [9,35,36] . In LDS-0101, the  γ ′  precipitates remained mostly cuboidal and discrete, and thus the precipitates in that alloy were still primarily coherent with the  γ  matrix. Little, if any, TCP phase precipitation was observed in the LDS alloys after the multi-step aging treatment.

# 4. Discussion  

# 4.1. Element Potency Effects on Microstructural Parameters  

The statistical regression models generated in this study provided excellent predictions of the microstructural parame- ters based on bulk alloy composition and demonstrate the usefulness of such formulas. By standardizing the regression coefficients over the element's compositional range, the  relative effects  of each significant term in the equations were able to be compared  over the entire compositional range varied for that element . Clearly, the impact of Co and Cr as  γ ′  solvus depressants was in agreement with previous investigations  [4 – 6,14] . Re and Mo were found to increase the  γ ′  solvus. The effects of composition on  γ ′  volume fraction were similar to the effects of composition on  γ ′  solvus, which was not surprising since empirical mea- surements of  γ ′  solvus and high temperature  γ ′  volume fraction tend to correlate  [4,5,14,18] .  

An alternative way to examine compositional effects is to consider an element's potency, which is defined on a per at.% basis by dividing the change of a microstructural parameter by the bulk compositional range of an element in at.% over which that change occurred. The potency of each element was calculated for each microstructural parameter, because some elements may have exhibited a large effect but may have been varied over a wide content range. For example, Co was shown to decrease the  γ ′  solvus temperature by a very significant  − 46.2 °C over its wide 11.1 at.% range, yet this corresponded to a change of only  – 4.1 °C/at.% Co. Similarly, Cr had an equivalent potency at  − 4.1 °C/at.% Cr, and Mo displayed a weak potency on  γ ′  solvus at +2.5 °C/at.% Mo. However, Re increased the  γ ′  solvus by +14.3 °C/at.% Re. Thus, whereas Co produced the largest change in  γ ′  solvus over its wide compositional range, on a per at.% basis, Re was the most potent at producing a change in the  γ ′  solvus.  

Re was also found to be the most potent element affecting the γ ′ volume fraction at 982 °C, by increasing this parameter +2.3 vol.%/at.% Re. Mo had a high potency as well, by increasing this parameter +1.9 vol.%/at.% Mo. In contrast, Cr and Co decreased the 982 °C  γ ′  volume fraction at  − 0.26 vol.%/ at.% Cr and  − 0.19 vol.%/at.% Co, respectively. In contrast, Re did not have a statistically significant influence on the  γ ′  volume fraction at 1093 °C. This was particularly surprising since Re had such a potent effect on this parameter at 982 °C. At 1093 °C, Mo produced the most potent effect on the  γ ′  volume fraction at +0.99 vol.%/at.% Mo. Cr was slightly less potent at  − 0.89 vol.%/ at.% Cr, and Co was less effective at  – 0.34 vol.%/at.% Co.  

Re once again was shown to be a very potent alloying element with respect to its effect on the  γ  lattice parameter, even with the inclusion of a negative MoRe interaction term. The interaction term produced non-parallel output curves for the  γ  lattice parameter for Re levels as a function of Mo, and thus the potency of Re was influenced by the amount of Mo in the alloy and vice versa. The potency of Re on the  γ  lattice parameter ranged from a low of 0.0101 Å/at.% Re at the highest bulk Mo content to a high of 0.0141 Å/at.% Re at the lowest Mo content in the bulk. Mo's highest potency level was found to be 0.0089 Å/at.% Mo in the absence of Re and was 0.0075 Å/at.% Mo at the highest level of Re. Thus, both Re and Mo were more potent when the other element was either not present or present at lower levels. In contrast, Cr was not nearly as potent of an alloying element on the  γ  lattice parameter at +0.0023 Å/ at.% Cr, and Co was even less effective at  − 0.0004 Å/at.% Co. The same ranking of the potency of these elements was observed for γ – γ ′  lattice mismatch, which would be expected since the  γ ′ lattice parameters did not change much and the  γ  lattice parameters dominated the compositional dependence. Re was found to be the most potent alloying element as it changed mismatch by  − 0.336%  δ /at.% Re; the potency of Mo ranked second at  − 0.222%  δ /at.% Mo. In contrast, Cr at  − 0.059%  δ /at.% Cr and Co at +0.011%  δ /at.% Co were less effective. The influences of these elements on the  γ  lattice parameter may be explained by their atomic size as well as by their influence on the resulting γ  phase chemistry, which will be explained below.  

# 4.2. γ  and  γ ′  Phase Chemistries  

The  γ  and  γ ′  phase chemistries exhibited some interesting temperature dependencies that were strongly influenced by the temperature dependency of the  γ ′  volume fraction .  More γ ′  (and therefore less  γ ) was present at 871 °C than at 1093 °C, and thus the  γ  formers were more heavily concentrated in the γ  phase at the lower aging temperatures. As the temperature increased, the elements that were tied up in the  γ ′  phase dissolved into the  γ  matrix, and this resultant effect was seen in the measured  γ  phase chemistries. The Cr, Co, Mo, and Re concentrations in the  γ ′  phase were much more constant as a function of temperature, although small but consistent changes were observed ( Fig. 10 ). The concentration of  γ  formers in the  γ ′ phase increased slightly as the temperature increased and the γ ′  volume fraction decreased. However, the Mo level in  γ ′  was an exception to this trend, since its concentration decreased with increasing temperature, as seen in  Fig. 10 c. This decrease may have been the result of limited solubility of Mo in the  γ ′ phase with increasing temperature  [11] , particularly in the presence of a strong  γ ′  former such as Ta. Loomis et al.  [11] reported similar trends and small changes in magnitude of the Cr, Al, and Mo concentrations in the  γ ′  phase of Ni – Al – Mo – Cr alloys as aging temperature increased, and noted that Ti sharply reduced Mo solubility in the  γ ′  phase. It seems quite possible that Ta behaves similarly to Ti in reducing the solubility of Mo in  γ ' with increasing temperature, and the Ta, Al, and Ni concentrations in  γ  and  γ ′  will be described in detail in another study  [34] .  

One may rationalize the relative magnitudes of the co- efficients in the  γ  lattice parameter regression equation based on atomic radii. The elements with an atomic radius closer to that of Ni would be expected to have a smaller effect on the  γ  lattice parameter and smaller regression coefficients in Eq.  (6) . Clearly Co and Cr have smaller atomic sizes  [39]  and smaller regression coefficients relative to Mo and Re. In addition, the regressions showing the effects of bulk chemistry changes on the  γ  phase chemistries help to further explain the  γ  lattice parameters, since changes in bulk chemistry of one  γ  former often affected the concentrations of other elements in the  γ  phase. The phase chemistries at 871 °C were pertinent to this discussion as this was the last aging step employed before lattice parameter measurements were obtained at room temperature. For exam- ple, from Eq.  (4a) and (4c) , as Cr was increased in the bulk from 2.8 to 5.7 at.% with the other  γ  formers held constant at their mid-range, the concentrations of Cr and Mo in the  γ  phase increased significantly from 5.6 to 14.1 at.% and from 7.4 to 8.9 at.%, respectively. These effects of Cr would both serve to increase the  γ  lattice parameter and drive the  γ – γ ′  lattice mismatch in the negative direction. Similarly, as Mo in the bulk was increased, the concentrations of both Mo and Cr increased in the  γ  phase. These effects of Mo would also serve to amplify the increase in  γ  lattice parameter and drive the lattice mismatch in the negative direction.  

Although Mo has a slightly bigger atomic radius than Re [39] , increasing Re in the bulk alloy increased the concentrations of Re, Cr, and Co in the  γ  phase. This overall effect of Re would cause an increase in the  γ  lattice parameter, and would also drive the lattice mismatch in the negative direction. Co was the only alloying element in this study that partitioned to the  γ phase and, based on Eq.  (6) , decreased the  γ  lattice parameter, which would drive the mismatch in a slightly positive direction. This effect of Co has also been observed in first generation single crystal alloys  [4,5] . In contrast, Caron  [14]  indicated that Co increased the  γ  lattice parameter in superalloys, based on calculated  γ  lattice parameters that used the lattice parameters of pure Ni and Ni – Co binaries, as well as calculated  γ compositions. In the present study, adding Co to the bulk alloy significantly increased the amount of Co in the  γ  and  γ ′  phases and had no effect on the concentration of any other  γ  former at 871 °C. Cobalt's negative effect on  γ  lattice parameter cannot be rationalized based on atomic size or Ni – Co binary data. It can only be ventured that the Co effect is quite small and is the consequence of subtle changes in phase chemistries.  

# 4.3. Topologically Close Packed Phases  

The qualitative trends in TCP phase formation as a function of composition were not unexpected as it is well known that increasing the refractory metal content increases the propen- sity for TCP formation. This is consistent with the EDS analysis performed in this study on extracted TCP precipitates, which showed that the TCP laths were rich in Mo, Ni, Re, and to a lesser extent, Cr. One key to successful alloy development is to know with very high accuracy the critical concentration limits for the strengthening elements and for the critical combination of elements. PHACOMP  [27,28]  and Md  [29]  results did not provide a clear limit for the concentration of refractories in LDS composition space, and another study  [40]  agrees that they are not robust indicators of TCP formation. Nv3 equations may be updated and improved for a set of alloys with knowledge of the γ  phase chemistry as a function of temperature. However, within this alloy design space, the total refractory metal (Cr +Mo+Re) content in the bulk alloy provided a useful tool for predicting the influence of alloying on third phase precipi- tation, since this approach revealed a critical refractory metal content of 15.3 wt.%, above which excessive TCP phase precipitation was induced.  

Any additional conclusions based on compositional effects on TCP formation are provisional in nature because most of the LDS alloys were stable. Only two alloys exhibited TCP volume fractions significantly greater than 1 vol.%, and more unstable alloys would need to be examined to capture with more certainty the compositional effects on this microstructural parameter. Co is of particular interest, since it was intentionally varied in this design space because of its reported benefit in reducing TCP content  [4 – 6,30,32] . Comparison of LDS-1101 to LDS-1501 is consistent with that trend, although the TCP contents in both of these alloys were low (<1 vol.%). Similarly, LDS-4183 and LDS-4583 showed a small beneficial effect of Co at 1093 °C, although at 982 °C, their mean third phase volume fractions were not statistically different.  Table 3  demonstrates that JMatPro predicted Co to have a detrimental effect on TCP precipitation at 982 °C, although it predicted a beneficial effect at 1093 °C. PHACOMP and Md also did not predict the beneficial effect of Co, but instead predicted the opposite.  

The exact mechanism for the potential beneficial effect of Co in reducing TCP content is still open for discussion. One possible mechanism is that Co may increase the solubility of refractory metals in the  γ  and  γ ′  phases  [5] , thereby increasing the saturation limits and reducing supersaturation of the refrac- tories in the  γ  phase. Another mechanism postulated  [6,32]  is that Co may change the partitioning ratio of alloying elements by forcing higher quantities of the  γ  formers into the  γ ′  phase, thereby diluting the refractories in the  γ  phase. This idea was not supported in the present study by the regressions of the phase chemistries at 871 °C. Another plausible mechanism is that Co may reduce the supersaturation of refractory metals in the  γ  matrix directly by decreasing the  γ ′  volume fraction and increasing the  γ  volume fraction, thereby diluting the refrac- tories in the  γ  phase. Other factors believed to be important in affecting stability, include the individual character and potency of each alloying element, plus the presence or absence of other elements.  

It should be noted that prior work  [2,3]  indicated higher TCP volume fractions for LDS-5051 were formed after 500 h of unstressed aging, as compared to that in the present paper after creep exposures at the same temperatures. This was particu- larly evident at 982 °C where 6.1 vol.% of third phase was measured after aging  [2,3] , compared to 0.3 vol.% of third phase obtained after creep exposures. At 1093 °C, 4.1 vol.% of third phase was measured in LDS-5051 after aging  [2,3] , and 2.4 vol.% of third phase was observed after creep exposures. The other LDS alloys without the Hf addition showed no significant differences in the third phase content between stressed and unstressed exposures, with quantities all being  ≤ 1 vol.%. However, the Hf-bearing alloys seemed more prone to measur- able differences between types of exposures, although these differences were not nearly as dramatic as those observed for LDS-5051. The Hf-bearing alloys exhibited on average 1 vol.% more third phase after 982 °C aging, compared to that after 982 °C creep, and exhibited on average 0.7 vol.% more third phase after 1093 °C aging than that after 1093 °C creep. Some studies  [26,41]  have concluded that the application of stress decreases the amount of TCP phase precipitation in single crystals during high temperature exposures; this is worthy of further investigation since screening for microstructural stability is usually done via unstressed aging, despite service conditions being under applied stress at elevated temperatures.  

# 4.4. Comparisons to Computational Tool Predictions  

Clearly, software tools that use computational thermodynam- ics are of growing importance and are commonly used to predict various microstructural parameters in nickel-base superalloys. However, major discrepancies were observed between computational tool predictions and the experimental data generated in this study. JMatPro significantly under- predicted the  γ ′  solvus by an average of 10 °C and the solidus by an average of 17 °C. If these predictions are used in practice and in the absence of experimental data, heat treatment temperatures could be far from optimum, resulting in unsolu- tioned primary  γ ′ . Furthermore with JMatPro, the combination of the lower solvus temperature predictions and even lower solidus temperature predictions serves to predict unrealistically small heat treatment windows, which could eliminate viable alloys from adequate consideration. JMatPro also under- predicted the quantity of  γ ′  by an average of 10 vol.% at 1093 °C. Since  γ ′  is the strengthening precipitate in Ni-base superalloys, use of such predictions in alloy development could cause an unnecessary re-design of composition space. There- fore, improvement of such predictions should be a high priority.  

JMatPro predicted the LDS alloys to have positive  γ – γ ′ lattice mismatches so large in magnitude at room temperature that if these predictions were accurate, many of the alloys in this study would be expected to have positive mismatch also at creep testing temperatures. The  γ ′  phase typically has a smaller thermal expansion coefficient than that of the  γ  matrix  [35]  in Ni-base superalloys, and thus as temperature is increased, the lattice parameter of the  γ  phase increases more than that of the γ ′  phase. However, empirical results from high temperature X-ray diffraction show that the magnitude of the lattice mismatch in Ni-base superalloys changes only by a small amount from the room temperature measurements. For example, a change in the magnitude of lattice mismatch of no more than 0.1% was measured from room temperature to 1000 °C in Re-free alloys  [35] , and alloys with higher refractory metal content were noted to exhibit lower thermal expansion coefficients for both the  γ  and  γ ′  phases. Other studies  [42] showed a change in the magnitude of lattice mismatch of up to 0.2% in Re-free alloys and up to 0.12% in Re-bearing alloys from room temperature to 1000 °C. Alloys which have positive lattice mismatch at elevated temperatures produce  γ ′  rafts that align parallel to the applied stress direction during creep exposures [35,43] . However, all LDS alloys in this study developed rafts perpendicular to the applied stress direction, which is expected for alloys with negative mismatch at elevated temperatures. Thus, most of the JMatPro predictions for lattice mismatch were not only of the wrong sign at room temperature, but many were too large in magnitude to become negative at creep testing temperatures. Thus, neither the empirically deter- mined mismatch values nor the microstructural evidence for the sign of lattice mismatch obtained in this study support the JMatPro predictions for  γ – γ ′  mismatch.  

User selections within the JMatPro program greatly affected the predictions of lattice mismatch, and users clearly need to be cognizant of such options. Since JMatPro over-predicted the amount of TCP observed experimentally in this study, it seemed logical to try to suppress TCP formation within the software and examine its effects on predicted lattice parameters. This approach dramatically improved the software's predictions for γ  lattice parameter at room temperature, although it resulted in a slight, concurrent worsening of  γ ′  lattice parameter pre- dictions. Additionally, the selection of a heat treatment temper- ature range within the software also greatly impacted the predictions.  

JMatPro predicted the carbide types that were identified experimentally by X-ray diffraction in the present alloys. Other investigators  [44]  have also concluded that the thermo- dynamic calculations usually predict the correct carbides. However, JMatPro did a less than satisfactory job in predicting the presence and amount of third phases in LDS composition space, and this was consistent with other observations [40,44,45] . Specifically in this study, it was found that JMatPro significantly over-predicted the amount of third phase pre- cipitates observed at 982 °C, and predicted ten out of fourteen alloys to be unstable when they were in fact stable ( Table 3 ). Alternatively, at 1093 °C, JMatPro predicted all LDS alloys to be stable, including the two LDS alloys that were unstable. One reason given for such discrepancies is that some of the TCP phases are poorly described within the thermodynamic database since experimental data are often scarce  [40] . Furthermore, a criticism of validation testing is that the experimental results do not represent equilibrium conditions that the thermodynamic calculations are predicting  [44]  in software packages such as JMatPro. Since the amount of third phase precipitates did not change significantly after 200 h of creep exposures, it is believed that the experimental results in this study represented near-equilibrium conditions for the third phases, and thus the observed discrepancy between experimental results and JMatPro predictions cannot be explained by the absence of equilibrium conditions. It is hoped that the experimental results described herein can be used to improve these predictions.  

# 5. Conclusions  

1. Re, Cr, Mo, and Co preferentially partitioned to the  γ  phase, although they also exhibited some solubility in the  γ ′ phase. Partitioning ratios and phase chemistries were not constant but were a function of temperature and bulk alloy composition.  

2. Phase chemistry changes were affected by the decrease in γ ′  volume fraction with increasing temperature. The  γ phase exhibited large compositional changes with temper- ature; the  γ ′  chemistry remained relatively constant, although some very small but consistent changes were observed.  

3. Co decreased  γ ′  solvus and had the biggest impact on this parameter over its 0 to 11 wt.% range. Mo had the biggest influence on increasing the  γ  lattice parameter and the magnitude of  γ – γ ′  lattice mismatch over its 6 to 12 wt.% range. Co and Cr decreased  γ ′  volume fraction but Mo and Re increased  γ ′  volume fraction. Re was the most potent alloying element investigated on an at.% basis for the microstructural parameters examined.  

4. Refractory metal concentration limits that avoid excessive TCP phase formation were best described by the total Cr +Mo +Re content in the bulk alloy. PHACOMP and Md were somewhat less effective discriminators.  

5. JMatPro 6.0® correctly identified the observed carbide types in the LDS alloys and provided reasonably close estimations of the  γ ′  lattice parameters. However, JMatPro underestimated the  γ ′  volume fraction and the  γ ′  solvus, the latter of which could lead to the use of inadequate heat treatment temperatures that do not fully solution the primary  γ ′  phase, thereby negatively impacting mechani- cal properties.  

6. JMatPro 6.0® tended to grossly over-predict the amount of TCP phase that actually formed at 982 °C. However, its predictions for  γ  lattice parameters and  γ – γ ′  lattice mis- match was improved after TCP precipitation was suspended in the software program.  

7. Regression models developed in the present study fit the  γ ′ solvus,  γ ′  volume fraction,  γ  lattice parameter and  γ – γ ′  lattice mismatch extremely well and demonstrated the useful- ness of such methods for estimating bulk compositional effects.
