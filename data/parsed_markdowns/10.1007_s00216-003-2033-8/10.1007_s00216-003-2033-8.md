# Electrospray ionization mass spectrometry  of ionic liquids and determination of their solubility in water  

Received: 11 December 2002 / Revised: 3 February 2003 / Accepted: 12 May 2003 / Published online: 24 June 2003 © Springer-Verlag 2003  

Abstract  Electrospray ionization mass spectrometry is used to detect both the cations (C + ) and the anions (A – ) of ionic liquids (CA). In this study, the ionic liquids are di- luted with aqueous methanol before injection. In addition to the main peaks of the parent ions, fragmentation prod- ucts are observed upon increasing the cone voltage, whereas aggregates of the parent ion with one or more ionic liquid molecules (e.g., C(CA) + , A(CA) – ) are ob- n n served upon decreasing the cone voltage. The ions of sev- eral ionic liquids in a mixture are also detected and the ra- tios of their concentrations estimated. A method is devel- oped to determine quantitatively the concentration of an ionic liquid in solution by using the cation and anion of another ionic liquid as internal standards. By using this method, the solubilities in water at room temperature (22±1 °C) of three typical hydrophobic ionic liquids have been determined: 0.70±0.08 g L –1   for methyl tri but yl ammo- nium  bis(tri flu oro methyl s ulf on yl)imide (MeBu 3 NNTf 2 ), 6.0±0.5 g L –1   for but yl methyl pyr ro lid in i um bis(trifluoro- methylsulfonyl)imide  (BuMePyrNTf ), and 18.6±0.7 g L –1 2 for 1-butyl-3-methyl i mid a zo liu m hex a flu oro phosphate (BMIPF 6 ).

# Introduction  

Ionic liquids are salts composed of large cations and an- ions that cannot easily form an ordered crystal and thus remain liquid at or near room temperature. The physical properties of ionic liquids can be modified over a wide range by changes in the detailed structure of the cation or by the identity of the anion. They can serve as solvents for a large number of chemical processes and, because their vapor pressures are negligible, they are considered good candidates for greener processing. Various classes of ionic liquids are miscible with water whereas others are consid- ered immiscible. The identity of the anion seems to greatly influence the miscibility of the ionic liquid with water and the extent to which water is soluble in the ionic liquid [1]. For example, a number of ionic liquids con- taining the anions PF –   or (CF SO ) N –   are generally con- 6 3 2 2 sidered insoluble in water whereas others containing, for example, Cl – , BF –   or CH SO –   are completely miscible. 4 3 3 The extent of solubility of an ionic liquid in water is a par- ticularly important parameter for several reasons. Since these substances are non-volatile, water solubility is likely to be the method for transport through the environment. Further, ionic liquids that are considered insoluble are routinely washed with water to purify them of other ions. This could serve as a significant source of environmental contamination, depending upon their actual solubility. Moreover, the loss of ionic liquids into the aqueous phase may be an important factor in estimating the cost of the ionic liquid used and the cost of water treatment. The pre- sent study was undertaken to develop a simple quantita- tive method for measurement of ionic liquid concentration in solution and to determine the solubility in water of rep- resentative hydrophobic ionic liquids.  

Because they are composed of ions, most ionic liquids absorb a significant amount of water and are difficult to dry, even those that are considered hydrophobic. The sol- ubility of water in ionic liquids and of ionic liquids in wa- ter has been reported for several cases [2, 3]. The solubil- ity of an ionic liquid in water is easily determined from the optical absorption of the solution if the ionic liquid has a characteristic absorption peak and significant absorbance at that peak. This is the case for ionic liquids with hetero- cyclic cations, such as imidazolium or pyridinium. If, however, the ionic liquid is aliphatic and has no peak in the UV region, and generally only a very weak absor- bance, optical measurement is not likely to provide reli- able results. For this reason we chose to study the mass spectra of ionic liquids by electrospray ionization mass spectrometry (ESMS) and attempted to determine the sol- ubility using this method. ESMS appears to be suitable for this purpose because it does not require prior separation of the ionic liquid and because the mass spectra are fairly simple and provide positive identification of the ions. Since ionic liquids are composed totally of ions, they may be detected by mass spectrometry without undergoing fur- ther ionization, only removal of the solvent and separation of the ions in the gas phase are required. Ionic liquids have been studied by various mass spectrometric methods [4] to determine their molecular mass, but were not quan- titatively analyzed by such techniques. Detection of or- ganic cations and anions by the early version of ESMS has been described [5, 6]. ESMS has been used for quan- titative determination of non-volatile compounds, mainly compounds of pharmaceutical or toxicological interest [7, 8, 9, 10, 11], and it is expected to be applicable for deter- mination of ionic liquids as well.  

The designation of these new solvents as “green sol- vents” ultimately will require that they be easily recycled and reused. Routine disposal or destruction would defeat a major reason for their development. In order to be recy- cled back into the process or into a new process, the purity of the ionic liquid will have to be certified. This will re- quire the development of appropriate and easily used ana- lytical tools. The approach described here, ESMS after dissolving the ionic liquid sample in an appropriate sol- vent, may prove useful for this purpose, particularly with the very clean spectra expected.  

# Experimental  

Several ionic liquids were used in this study: 1-butyl-3- methyl i mid a zo liu m hex a flu oro phosphate (BMIPF 6 ) and tetra flu oro borat e (BMIBF 4 ),  N -butylpyridinium tetrafluoro - borate (BuPyBF 4 ),  N -butyl-4-methyl pyr id in i um hexafluoro - phosphate (BuPicPF 6 ), methyl tri but yl ammonium bis(tri- flu oro methyl s ulf on yl)imide (MeBu 3 NNTf 2 ), butylmethyl- pyrrolidinium bis(tri flu oro methyl s ulf on yl)imide (BuMe PyrNTf 2 ), and hex yl tri but yl ammonium methane s ulf on ate (HxBu 3 NMs). The ionic liquids were prepared and puri- fied as described previously [12, 13, 14] and were dried in vacuum at 80 °C. Salts of PF –   and Tf N –   are generally 6 2 hydrophobic whereas the other salts are highly soluble or miscible with water. The ionic liquids were dissolved in methanol and then diluted with methanol and water to reach a final solvent composition of 1:1 (v/v) methanol/ water. To measure the solubility of ionic liquids in water, excess amounts of the ionic liquids were added to pure water and vigorously stirred for 24 h at room temperature. After phase separation, an aliquot of the aqueous phase was removed by pipette and diluted by a factor of 5–50 (to reach a concentration range where the ESMS response is nearly linear, see below).  

Mass spectra were measured with a Micromass Quattro Micro triple-quadrupole mass spectrometer (MS) equipped with an electrospray ionization source [15]. The samples were introduced into the MS by a syringe pump to yield a constant flow of solution (5  µ L/min). The source temper- ature was 80 °C and the desolvation temperature was 150 °C. Three scans were averaged. Optical absorption spectra were measured with a Cary 3 spectrophotometer in 10-mm or 2-mm cuvettes. The values of the solubilities and the slopes of the linear response plots are reported with their estimated overall standard uncertainties, which include the standard deviation of the experimental measurements and estimated uncertainties in the weights and volumes used.  

# Results  

Electrospray ionization mass spectra of the ionic-liquid solutions were measured under various conditions to as- certain that the original cations and anions were observed. In addition to the parent ions, some fragmentation prod- ucts and cluster ions were observed. The amounts of frag- mentation products increased and of cluster ions de- creased with increasing cone voltage. For example, with a cone voltage of 45 V considerable fragmentation was ob- served and there were very few cluster ions. On the other hand, with a cone voltage of 25 V there was very little fragmentation but more of the cluster ions (Fig. 1). The cluster ions were composed of the parent cation (C + ) or the anion (A – ) with one or more ionic-liquid molecules (e.g., C(CA) + , A(CA) – ; clusters with n=1–3 were readily n n observed). The fragmentation under high cone voltage is probably due to the collisions of the accelerated ions un- der such voltage. Because the separation in  m/z  values be- tween the parent ions and their cluster ions is larger than that between the parent ions and their fragments, it is ad- vantageous to run the experiments with a cone voltage of 25 V. Reducing this voltage further reduces the already small extent of fragmentation but it also reduces the total current of the parent ion so that 25 V appeared to be the best choice for these measurements.  

The identity of the ions was checked by measuring the product ions after collision of the accelerated original ions with Ar atom gas targets (collision-induced dissociation), making use of the tandem mass spectrometer (MS-MS) fea- ture of the instrument. At higher concentrations the signal intensity is sub-linear due to the limited number of ions that can be produced. The ion cur- rent vs. concentration curves can be described by the equa- tion I –1 =a+bC –1   or I=a(1 -e bC ). Typical examples are shown in Fig. 2 for the cation (circles) and anion (triangles) of MeBu 3 NNTf 2 . To compensate for this saturation effect and for any instability in the instrument operation, we measured the ratio of the signals of the analyte to that of an internal standard. In LC-MS measurements the internal standard is often the same compound as the analyte, to be eluted at the same time, but isotopically labeled to sepa- rate the  m/z  signals [7, 8, 9]. Because isotopically labeled ionic liquids are not available and because chromato- graphic separation is not an issue in the present measure- ments, we used a different ion as internal standard. # Solubility in water  

The solubilities of BMIPF 6 , MeBu 3 NNTf 2 , and MeBuPyrNTf 2 in water were determined by electrospray ionization mass spectrometry. An aliquot of the aqueous phase saturated with the ionic liquid was diluted by a factor of 5–50 to make a final solution containing 43 mg L –1   of BuPyBF  as 4 internal standard and aqueous methanol (1:1) as solvent. From the cation signals for BMIPF 6  [I( m/z  139)/I( m/z 136)] we calculate a concentration of (374±12) mg L –1   and from the anion signals [I( m/z  145)/I( m/z  87)] we calculate (370±7) mg L –1 , based on a calibration curve in the range 250–460 mg L –1   BMIPF  in a similar solvent. After consid- 6 ering the dilution by a factor of 50, the solubility of BMIPF 6 in water at room temperature is thus (18.6±0.7) g L –1 [(6.5±0.3) × 10 –2 mol L –1 ] (Table 4). This is a rather large solubility for a substance considered to be hydrophobic. In this case it was important to check the solubility by measuring both the cation and the anion to confirm that the high solubility does not result from hydrolysis of PF – 6 to form the more soluble BMIF. Hydrolysis of PF –   is 6 known to occur in acidic solutions and it is important to ascertain that hydrolysis did not take place in neutral wa- ter over many hours. Since it was found that the concen- trations of BMI +   and of PF –   are identical, it is concluded 6 that no hydrolysis occurred over 24 h at room tempera- ture.  

In the case of MeBu 3 NNTf 2  a considerably lower solu- bility was measured. The cation signals lead to a solubil- ity of (0.72±0.06) g L –1   while the anion signals lead to a solubility of (0.69±0.05) g L –1 . Thus the solubility of MeBu NNTf  in water is (0.70±0.08) g L –1   [(1.46±0.16) × 3 2 10 –3 mol L –1 ]. The solubility of MeBuPyrNTf  was inter- 2 mediate between the above values. The cation signals lead to a solubility of (6.0±0.3) g L –1   while the anion signals lead to a solubility of (6.0±0.4) g L –1 . Thus the solubility of MeBuPyrNTf  in water is (6.0±0.5) g L –1   [(1.42±0.12) × 2 10 –2 mol L –1 ]  (Table 4).  The  higher  solubility  of MeBuPyrNTf 2  as compared with MeBu 3 NNTf 2  is proba- bly due to the smaller cation in the former case, i.e., C 9  vs C 13 .  

BMIPF 6  has significant optical absorption in the UV with  λ max  at 212 nm (we have determined  ε 212 =(4.03± 0.05) × 10 3 L mol -1 cm -1 ). Therefore, one can measure its sol- ubility  in water by comparing the absorbance of the satu- rated solution with the absorbance of solutions of known concentration. Using this method we measured a solubil- ity of (18.3±0.8) g L -1 . This value is in good agreement with that measured by mass spectrometry. The optical measurement, however, is based solely on the absorption of the cation and does not provide evidence against hy- drolysis of the anion as discussed above. An earlier mea- surement of the solubility of BMIPF 6  in water by optical absorption gave a value of (20±3) g L -1   [3], in reasonable agreement with our value. In contrast, the solubilities of MeBu 3 NNTf 2  and MeBuPyrNTf 2  in water cannot be de- termined by optical absorption because these ionic liquids have very low absorbances. No peak is observed above 200 nm and the absorbance around 200 nm is very weak (for MeBu NNTf ,  ε =24 L mol -1 cm -1 ,  ε =14 L mol -1 3 2 200 205 cm -1 ,  ε =7 L mol -1 cm -1 ). The low absorbance, combined 210 with the low solubility and the high sensitivity of this wavelength region to contaminant interference, make it difficult to measure the solubility of MeBu 3 NNTf 2  in wa- ter with an acceptable accuracy. This is expected to be a general problem with aliphatic ionic liquids. For these cases, the mass spectrometric method provides both specificity and high sensitivity for measuring the concentration of the ionic liquid in water and for determining the solubility of these substances.
