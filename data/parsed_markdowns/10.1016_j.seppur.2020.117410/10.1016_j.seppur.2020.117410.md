# A R T I C L E I N F O  

# A B S T R A C T  

Lithium-rich continental brines are highly saline solutions with total dissolved solids in the order 300 g L − 1 , but Li +   concentrations around 1% only. A drastic increase in the ratio of Li +   to other cations ’  concentration is a must for an e ﬃ cient lithium recovery. Here we present results on the use of membrane electrolysis coupled to crystallization as a new methodology to separate a large share of Na +   present in a real brine sample. In a 3- compartment reactor, cations selectively migrate from the middle compartment through a cation exchange membrane to the cathodic compartment, where water electrolysis produces alkalinisation, and through CO 2 spurging sodium bicarbonate precipitation is triggered. By following the real time concentrations in middle and cathodic compartments, we were able to prove the proposed methodology, and recover a solid with a purity of 99.5%. A coulombic e ﬃ ciency of (95 ± 5)% was calculated, and the separation coe ﬃ cients showed a pre- ferential migration in the order K +   > Na +   > Li + . The methodology produces fresh water as a by-product, with total salinity 2 orders of magnitude lower than the feed. 3 di ﬀ erent applied current density values were tested. At 10 A m − 2 , 331.3 kWh are needed to treat 1 m 3   of brine.

# 1. Introduction  

Lithium is a fundamental raw material in the production of re- chargeable batteries, ceramics, glass, lubricating greases, casting and alloy industries, air conditioning, and aluminium, amongst others  [1] . The battery industry is currently the largest user. It has expanded at an amazing speed in recent years and is still driving the need for increasing lithium carbonate production from 167 ′ 700 Tons in 2015 to 410 ′ 000 Tons in 2019  [2] . According to the European Union, lithium exceeds the threshold for economic importance and is very close to the threshold of supply risk  [3] , while it is listed as a critical element in the USA  [4] . Lithium can be extracted from either hard rock minerals and brines: geothermal, oil ﬁ eld and continental brines. The latter are highly saline solutions, with total dissolved solids (TDS) in the range 170 – 330 g L − 1 , containing typically large quantities of Na + , and lower concentrations of Li + , K + , Mg 2+ , and Ca 2+ . Anions are principally Cl − with minor amounts of SO 4 2 − , HCO 3 − , and di ﬀ erent borates  [5] . The largest lithium ’ s worldwide resources are found in continental brines, or salt lakes, the two terms been used almost indistinctly. Technically,  

Three important points are at the core of any attempt of lithium recovery from continental brines. Firstly, Li +   is very diluted in brines: 1.5% in the best scenario, going down until 0.3 – 0.5%. Secondly, Li +   is dissolved with much larger amounts of other ions, notably around 8 – 13% Na +   [5] . Finally, the chemistry of the  ﬁ rst three alkaline ele- ments (Li + , Na + , and K + ) is quite similar, and most salts of these elements are highly water soluble  [7] . Thus, most attempts to directly precipitate Li +   salts from brine will also precipitate large amounts of Na +   and K +   salts. The currently applied industrial methodology for lithium recovery from brines is known as the  evaporitic technology . Brie ﬂ y, it involves the concentration of brines by evaporation for 12 – 24 months in large and shallow open-air evaporation ponds. Wind

In the ponds the brine gets concentrated and a large share of NaCl and KCl precipitates as halite (NaCl) and sylvinite (NaCl.KCl). When Li +   reaches a concentration of about 6000 ppm, recovery of Li 2 CO 3  takes place by carbonation with soda ash  [3,5,8] . Other processing to reach battery grade lithium carbonate involves the removal of Mg 2+ , Ca 2+ , and boron compounds. Over 90% of the water originally contained in the brine is evaporated and lost. This is not a minor issue, since evaporation takes place in highly desertic regions [3,9] . On average, for every ton of Li 2 CO 3 , 0.4 million litres of brine are evaporated and 115 tons of waste are left to be land ﬁ lled  [3] . From an economic perspective, the technology is relatively ine ﬃ cient, requiring over a year for brine concentration in the ponds  [3,5] .  

To replace solar evaporation, various prospective technologies have been reported for lithium recovery from brines. These include absorp- tion  [10 – 12] , solvent extraction  [12 – 15] , liquid membrane  [16,17] , nano ﬁ ltration  [18,19] , electrochemical lithium insertion  [20 – 24] , and membrane related technologies  [25] . Adsorption methods rely on ex- tremely large eluent volumes, usually fresh water, which is extremely scarce around mining facilities  [3,26,27] . Similarly, the high-volume usage of organic solvents as extractants not only corrodes the process equipment but also may cause damages to the local environment  [25] . Leakage of organic solvents and the necessity to use chemical reagents for desorption are the limitation of liquid membranes  [25,28] . Elec- trochemical lithium insertion shows recovery capacities in the order of 7 g Li/150 g insertion electrode. Thus, extremely large area electrodes would be required to process large brine volumes  [29 – 31] .  

Membrane processes, mostly nano ﬁ ltration and electricity driven, have shown great capabilities for the selective separation of mono- valent from divalent ions from brines  [32 – 34] . However, to the best of our knowledge, there are still no proposals as to how to achieve the selective recovery of Li +   ions from a solution containing larger amounts of both K +   and Na + . Promising work has shown some degree of se- paration. For example, 9.14% separation e ﬃ ciency between Na + /Li + has been reported  [34] . The same authors managed to achieve 15.91% separation e ﬃ ciency between K + /Li + . A selectivity ratio of 1.26 for Li + /Na +   was reported for another membrane  [35] . Alternatively, the successful precipitation of lithium carbonate has been achieved de- parting from arti ﬁ cial brines with Li + /Na +   concentrations ’  ratio that are unrealistic if compared to real brines. For example, Jiang et al. propose an electrodialysis-related technology, but there is neither Na + , nor K +   in their departing solutions  [36] . Yuan et al. studied the com- petitive migration of Li + , Na + , K + , Ca 2+ , and Mg 2+   across monovalent cation exchange membranes, although no lithium salts were crystal- lized  [37 – 40] . In our view, amongst the few membrane-processes that successfully separate Li +   from other monovalent ions, are those em- ploying Li +   selective electrodes, with the above mentioned limitations [22,41] . Very recently, Lee et al .  have proposed the use of membrane distillation for the successful concentration, and thus increase in Li + / Na +   ratio, of an arti ﬁ cial Li +   rich brine  [42] .  

In the perspective of a circular economy we believe that lithium-rich continental brines should be considered as a potential source of several raw materials. Indeed, even before the lithium boom, continental brines have been exploited for decades as a source of potassium, sodium, calcium salts, and borates, amongst other industrial minerals  [5,43,44] . However, usually only one industrial mineral, or two of them at the most were jointly recovered at each deposit. NaCl is the most con- centrated salt that could be recovered. Na 2 CO 3  is used in the manu- facture of soda-lime glass,  ﬁ berglass, pulp and paper, soaps and de- tergents, for metal re ﬁ ning,  ﬂ ue gas de sulphur iz ation, synthesis of a large list of chemicals and for water treatment  [45] . In 2019, the total global production of Na 2 CO 3  was estimated to be about 58 million metric tons [46].  

Electro-membrane processes have been known for decades and have been applied to diverse separation processes in the chemical industry. The extraction via membrane electrolysis is an interesting alternative among the di ﬀ erent technologies available and this method would be potentially easy to scale up. We propose an overall membrane electrolysis concept as a new potential methodology for the joint re- covery of at least 5 brine components, including fresh water. The pro- posed methodology consists of 3 independent stages (see  Fig. 1 ). In the ﬁ rst stage, Mg 2+   and Ca 2+   are removed as Mg(OH) 2  and Ca(OH) 2  [47] . In the second stage, that is the focus of work presented here, brine deprived from Mg 2+   and Ca 2+   is fed into a three-compartment water electrolyzer with an attached side-crystallizer, and Na +   is recovered as NaHCO 3 . Lithium carbonate is recovered in the last stage of the process [48] . Here we show at proof-of-concept level that NaHCO 3 , which is later transformed into Na 2 CO 3 , can be precipitated with high purity from a real brine, and thus no valuable Li +   ions are lost in the process. In this way, a selective depletion in Na +   concentration can be achieved. Thus a solution with a much increased Li + /Na +   concentration ratio is obtained, which is much better suited for Li 2 CO 3  recovery in a suc- cessive stage. Water with a reduced salinity is obtained as a by-product. The  ﬁ nal total salinity content depends on the extent of the electrolysis, and the consequent energy investment. The di ﬀ erential ion migration is studied, and the separation coe ﬃ cients, ion transport numbers and migration rates are evaluated. The new method is faster, independent from weather conditions, and potentially much easier to adapt to brines of di ﬀ erent compositions.  

# 2. Materials and methods  

# 2.1. Electrochemical cells  

Three-compartments electrochemical cells were designed and made in house based on a previously described design  [47,49] . Three acrylic frames were used as anode, middle and cathode compartments (internal dimensions: 5 × 20 × 2 cm). Anode and middle compartments were separated by an anion exchange membrane (AEM) (AMI-7001CR, Membrane International Inc., USA). Middle and cathode compartments were separated by a cation exchange membrane (CEM) (CMI-7000S, Membrane International Inc., USA). Membrane speci ﬁ cations are given in  Table S1 . The frames were bolted together between two rectangular acrylic plates. The total volume for each compartment was 200 mL. The anode used was titanium (Ti) mesh electrode coated with an iridium- based mixed metal oxide (IrO 2 /TiO 2 ; 65/35%), with a centrally at- tached, perpendicular current collector (dimensions: 4.8 × 19.8 cm; 1 mm thickness, Magneto Special Anodes, The Netherlands). The cathode was a stainless steel wire mesh with a stainless steel current collector. The distance between electrodes was 23 mm. Between the surface of the electrodes and the membranes, two plastic meshes are placed to avoid direct contact between the electrodes and the mem- brane. Experiments were run in constant current mode using a DC/CC regulated power supply (V&A instrument, China). Di ﬀ erent batch runs were carried out at varying current density values (in each run the current density was kept constant). The current densities are reported with respect to the projected surface area of the membrane in contact with the solutions (100 cm 2 ). The cell voltage was monitored directly from the digital screen in the power supply.  

All electrochemical experiments were run at least in triplicate. In all ﬁ gures and numerical results, the average of at least three replicates is always reported. In between replicates, results were never beyond 10% of each other (cell potential vs. circulated charge at constant current density, and concentrations vs. circulated charge). Experiments were run in batch mode at room temperature (22 ± 3 °C). The anodic compartment was connected to a 2 L Schott-bottle, the middle com- partment was connected to either a 0.5 or 1 L Schott-bottle, while the cathodic compartment was connected to a 0.5 L Schott-bottle, that acted as a crystallizer. Considering electrochemical cell + connected vessel, the total volume for the anolyte, middle and catholyte solutions, were 2 L, 0.5 L (or 1 L), and 0.5 L, respectively. These volumes were constantly recirculated through the cell compartments using a peri- staltic pump ( ﬂ ow rate = 27 L h − 1 , Oem Bm 127, Argentina). Recirculation also assured stirring of the solutions.  

Real brine without Mg 2+   and Ca 2+   was added to the middle com- partment (see Section 2.3). In the anodic compartment, 1 M carbonate/ bicarbonate (industrial grade) bu ﬀ er solution, pH = 10, was added. In these proof-of-concept experiments, we are focused in the transfer of cations from the middle to the cathodic compartment. Thus, we pur- posely chose to keep a pH around 10 in the anodic compartment to avoid chlorine gas formation. At pH above 7, Cl 2  disproportionates into Cl − and ClO − [7] . In the cathodic compartment, 400 mg L − 1   NaHCO 3 (p.a, Cicarelli) solution was used. All solutions were prepared with de- ionized water. CO 2  was bubbled in the Schott-bottle connected to the cathodic compartment, working as a crystallizer. In order to keep 7 < pH < 9 an electro valve (Intor, Argentina) and a pH-meter were connected to an Arduino UNO R3 card. The Arduino controlled the CO 2 bubbling,  i.e.  opening and closing the electro valve if pH is > 9 or < 7, respectively. After each batch run, the reactor and the membrane were rinsed with de-ionized water. When not in use, the membranes were kept in NaCl 5% solution. While CO 2  bubbling in the crystallizer pro- duced that most of the solid was formed outside of the cathodic com- partment, a minor amount of solid was still formed in that compartment in this non-optimized reactor setup, although this happened mostly towards the end of the batch run. At the end of the batch runs the precipitates recovered were vacuum  ﬁ ltered at 0 °C (to minimize re- dissolution), washed with de-ionized water, also at 0 °C, and dried in a laboratory oven at 110 °C for 24 h.

The applied current and the need to maintain electro neutrality in all three compartments require a net transport of anions from the middle to the anodic compartment, and of cations from the middle to the cathodic compartment, respectively. The migration of other species in opposite directions cannot be fully avoided, although the calculated current e ﬃ ciency, Section 3.4, shows that this does not happen to a major extent. Interestingly, we observe that in  Figs. 2 and 3 a – f the cations ’  con- centrations, conductivity and density in the middle compartment for experiments at 10 A m − 2   decrease much faster than for the other current density values. The answer seems to be in the volume variation of the solutions in the middle and cathodic compartments, which are plotted in  Fig. 4 .  

The volume variation for the solution in the anodic compartment (see  Fig. S1 ) has a similar behaviour than that of the cathodic com- partment. The volume of the solutions in the middle compartment de- creases considerably, while the volume of the solution in the cathodic and anodic compartments increases for experiments at 50 and 300 A m − 2 , with larger variations for the latter. However, the behaviour is exactly the opposite for the experiment at 10 A m − 2 . Water molecules can be transported through the ion-exchange membranes by osmosis and by electro-osmosis  [51 – 53] . Osmotic water transport occurs due to concentration di ﬀ erences, and it occurs from the more diluted towards the more concentrated solution ( i.e.  from anodic and cathodic com- partments, to the middle compartment, throughout most of the ex- periment). Osmosis water transport depends on the time of operation, thus it increases when a low current density is used  [52] . Electro-os- mosis refers to the water transported in the hydration shell of the ions migrating through the membranes due to electrical potential gradient [51] ,  i.e.  each ion is surrounded by n molecules of H 2 O. The co- ordination number of ions depends mostly on the relative size of the ion, the hydration energy, how the solvent molecules are arranged around the ion  [54] , and the total salts concentration  [51] . Moreover, electro-osmosis is proportional to the applied current density and in- dependent of the concentration gradient across the membrane  [52] . Thus, in our experiments, osmosis and electro-osmosis e ﬀ ects are ex- actly the opposite. Our results show that for the faster experiments, performed at higher current density values, electro-osmosis is the pre- dominant phenomena, and its e ﬀ ect is more marked at 300 A m − 2 , than at 50 A m − 2 . Conversely, for the experiment at 10 A m − 2   the osmotic water transport exceeds electroosmotic e ﬀ ects. While K +   transfers faster than Na + , for each K +   cation that can migrate there are 12 Na +   cations that can migrate. Statistically, there are many more Na +   cations that can transfer charge than any other species. The cation migration rate is also dependent of current density. Membrane transport numbers of cations at 300 A m − 2   were 0.831, 0.116 and 0.025 for Na + , K +   and Li + , respectively.  

# 3.2. Solid formation  

The amount of solid precipitate obtained depends on the volume ratio between middle and cathodic compartments. Our strategy is to precipitate as much Na +   as NaHCO 3  in order to achieve a  ﬁ nal solution where the Li + /Na +   ratio has been largely increased with respect to that of the feed solution. Unfortunately, NaHCO 3 , the same as most Na + salts, has a relatively large solubility in aqueous solutions. Thus, a de- crease in the total volume of the solution in the cathodic compartment, vs. the total initial volume is desirable. Experiments were the initial solution volume in the cathodic compartment was half of that of the middle compartment yielded larger solid masses than those where the initial volumes were equal. In addition, experiments performed at a current density of 10 A m − 2 , where osmosis reduced the solution vo- lume in the cathodic compartment also yielded larger solid masses. While a detail engineering of a potential industrial process is beyond the scope of work presented here, we can advance that the larger the di ﬀ erence in volumes between the middle and cathodic compartments, the better. A volume ratio much larger than used here (limited in a lab- scale experimental setup) would be ideal. Because of the relatively large solubility of NaHCO 3 , it would be best to concentrate ions in the cathodic compartment as much as possible.  

At temperatures above 80 °C, sodium bicarbonate gradually de- composes into sodium carbonate, water, and carbon dioxide (Eq.  (11) ). The decomposition of NaHCO 3 becomes quite rapid at about 120 – 130 °C  [56] . At temperature > 109 °C sodium anhydrous carbo- nate is obtained and at T < 109 °C sodium carbonate monohydrate can be obtained  [57] . In order to obtain a single solid, we decided to dry the precipitate, beyond 109 °C. This will convert all bicarbonate into car- bonate, which would also be desirable in an industrial process, since it would allow us to recycle a large part of the CO 2  back into the process.

A careful reader might wonder why we did not attempt to pre- cipitate Na 2 CO 3  directly, instead of NaHCO 3 . The answer is in the re- lative solubilities of the compounds that could potentially precipitate (see  Table 3 ). Neither potassium, nor lithium precipitate as bicarbo- nates. Hence, if the predominant species in solution is bicarbonate, contamination of the solid with potassium, and most importantly the loss of Li +   ions are avoided. In turn, the three alkaline elements do precipitate as carbonates, and Na 2 CO 3  is 16 times more soluble than Li 2 CO 3 . Also, Na 2 CO 3  is more soluble than NaHCO 3 . Thus, our choice to deplete the large Na +   concentration by precipitating NaHCO 3 . # 3.3. Side-production of low salinity water  

Table 4  compares data for the concentrated brine that was fed to the middle compartment and the solution coming out of that same com- partment when the electrolysis is pursued for di ﬀ erent time intervals/ total circulated charge. We can observe a dramatic decrease in the amount of total dissolved solids, conductivity, and solution density when comparing the original feed, and any of the possible outcomes. Because the  ﬁ nal aim of the overall concept (full  Fig. 1 ) is the recovery of Li 2 CO 3 , it is in our interest to transport the largest proportion of cations from the original feed to the cathodic compartment. However, as in any desalination process, a larger proportion of ions transferred across the membranes will come at a higher energy input. As the so- lution in the middle compartment becomes more diluted, particularly towards the last 5% of the electrolysis, the conductivity in the middle compartment becomes lower ( Fig. 2 a), and as a consequence there is an important increase in the cell voltage, with an increase in power con- sumption per amount of cations recovered in the cathodic compartment (see  Fig. 8 b). In addition, we should note that the amounts of water potentially recoverable through this process are huge. These amounts are certainly much larger than the industrial water volumes that are needed in the whole process. Thus, we could envision that the excess water could be provided to parallel activities, such as irrigation of the desertic land with agricultural purposes, requiring a certain amount of TDS.  

# 3.4. Preliminary assessment of techno-economic aspects  

The highest operational costs associated to electro-membrane pro- cesses are membrane replacement and electricity. A detailed membrane ageing study was not performed. However, all experiments presented in this work, including replicates, were performed with the same AEM and CEM membranes, which were used for over 3000 h. In the last batch run, the cell potential was reproducible with respect to the  ﬁ rst ex- periment with the new membrane and at equal current density. This is a strong suggestion of the absence of serious membrane fouling. One of the possible reasons could be CO 2  is bubbled in the decanter, promoting most of the precipitation away from the membrane. Moreover, the membrane is very close to the electrode. Thus, the pH in the vicinity of the membrane is higher than in the decanter and most of the carbon species are present as carbonate anions, instead of bicarbonate. We have to admit, however, that our experiments were performed in a non- optimized reactor setup. In order to proceed with a pilot-scale im- plementation, an optimized reactor should be designed with, amongst other considerations, full avoidance of solid formation within the cathodic compartment.  

The calculated current e ﬃ ciency to obtain low salinity water was (95 ± 5) %,  i.e.  ions seem to be transferring almost exclusively due to the applied electric  ﬁ eld.  Fig. 3 g – i had shown that there were no ap- parent e ﬀ ects of the applied current density on the net transfer of ions. Titration of chloride showed the continuous decrease of Cl − in the middle compartment ( Fig. S2 ), while we could not detect the presence of chloride in neither the catholyte, nor the precipitated solid. Thus, it is reasonable to exclude ionic transfer in either direction of the CEM due to other phenomena.  

In all our experiments, the applied current density was kept con- stant. The arising cell voltage, E source , was maintained mostly constant (within 3% variation), except for the  ﬁ rst and last part of the electro- lysis. Higher values at the beginning of the batch run were attributed to the lower conductivity in the cathodic compartment (initial con- centration 0.4 g L − 1   NaHCO 3 ). As cations traversed the CEM, and oxydriles and bicarbonate anions were generated, the conductivity in- creased, and hence the cell voltage decreased. The sharp increase in voltage towards the end of the run was attributed to a sharp decrease in the conductivity of the middle compartment ( Fig. 2 a).  Fig. 8 a is a plot of E source  (constant value excluding start and  ﬁ nal values) vs. the applied current density, showing a linear relationship. This linearity and the high voltage values (above the thermodynamic voltage for water splitting of 1.23 V) imply that ohmic drops due to electrolytes and the resistance of the two membranes are the main reasons for the voltage evolution with current. The value of the E/I slope provides an internal resistance of 1.95  Ω .  

While increasing the current density accelerates the process, this also carries out an increase in the power consumption, and hence costs. This is shown in  Fig. 8 b. Because the process is based on ionic transport, the electricity cost will be proportional to the total cationic composi- tion, and hence, will change according to brine composition. For a brine with composition as shown in  Table 1 , 1122.7, 458.9 and 331.3 kWh m 3   for 300 A m − 2 , 50 A m − 2   and 10 A m − 2   respectively, are needed to transfer over 99.9% of the ions present in 1 m 3   of brine, and to obtain low salinity water ( Table 4 ). Those values have been obtained by in- tegrating the E source  values obtained in the non-optimized cell con ﬁ g- uration that was used to run the experiments reported here. An opti- mized cell should be designed in order to limit the ohmic drop across the electrodes,  i.e.  using less resistive exchange membranes, and, most importantly, decreasing the distance between electrodes (currently at 23 mm). Evidently, decreasing the applied current density, while slowing down the process, has an enormous advantage in terms of costs.

The current evaporitic process takes at least a year to concentrate the brine. Therefore, the proposed methodology will be considerably faster than evaporation, even if working at lower current densities than ap- plied here. Those energy requirements correspond only to the elec- tricity consumption needed to drive the electro-membrane process. In a real system, the cost of CO 2 , solution pumping, drying of the pre- cipitate, etc. should also be taken into account.  

The proposed system is certainly energy intensive, and the fact that most of the electric charge is used to transfer K +   and Na +   ions across the membrane could be considered an ine ﬃ ciency. Moreover, the stage of the process considered here is by far the most energy consuming, if compared to 62 KWh m − 3   and 70.6 KWh m − 3 , respectively for the  ﬁ rst and third stages of the overall process proposed in  Fig. 1 [47,48] . While the costs might seem excessive, we should recall that the regions where lithium-rich continental brine deposits are located are on average at 3500 m above sea level, and are thus exposed to extremely high sun irradiation. The total annual radiation levels are in the order of 2200 kWh m − 2   [59 – 61] , which makes these ideal locations for solar energy harvesting. Overall, only a full techno-economic assessment, including a life cycle analysis, which is beyond the scope of work presented here, will indicate whether the energy costs are justi ﬁ able in terms of overall sustainability and technical e ﬃ ciency. To the best of our knowledge, only processes involving at some point evaporation in open air ponds with associated large water losses, have been shown fully successful to concentrate real brines and/or radically reduce Na +   concentration to an extent where Li 2 CO 3  could be attempted. In this light, new propo- sals, even if energy intensive, should be analysed.  

This new methodology is proposed as part of an integral membrane- electrolysis concept which includes a previous step to deplete divalent cations, and a subsequent step to recover lithium carbonate. However, we could also envision that the process described here could be coupled to other strategies for those tasks, such as addition of lime for magne- sium precipitation  [5] . Once the concentrations of Na +   and Li +   have reached the same order of magnitude, precipitation of Li 2 CO 3  could be produced by either classical addition of soda ash, or one of many pro- spective technologies  [36,39] . Membrane electrolysis as proposed here would be used to avoid the use of solar evaporation ponds, saving precious time. Finally, because this methodology is totally independent of weather conditions and land cost, we could also envision that it could be applied to other aqueous lithium sources, such as wastewater from the oil industry, or geothermal brines.  

# 4. Conclusions  

We propose a membrane-electrolysis strategy for increasing the Li + /Na +   ratio in lithium rich brines. That change is needed in order to recover Li 2 CO 3  at a later stage. Under the experimental conditions applied here, the ionic migration rate across the CEM decreases in the order Na +   > K +   > Li + . Sodium bicarbonate is produced by the reaction of gaseous CO 2  in alkaline medium, thus the method has the potential of being considered a CO 2  sequestration strategy. After heating and conversion of bicarbonate to carbonate, solid samples with purities of 99.5% of Na 2 CO 3  were obtained departing from real brine samples.  

With our proposed methodology fresh water can be recovered. This could cause a huge impact on local communities in the vicinity of li- thium mining facilities. The  ﬁ nal composition of the fresh water thus recovered is acceptable to be used for intensive irrigation. While the proposed methodology is energy intensive and thus costly, prices for renewable energy are steadily going down, and the price of Li 2 CO 3  is maintained at around 12,000 US\$ ton − 1 . With the raw numbers available from these proof-of-concept experiments, producing Li 2 CO 3 , and additional industrial minerals through this methodology would still be economically pro ﬁ table. Finally, other variables should be taken into account, such as potentially much lower environmental impact, side water production, a much faster process, and the possibility to rapidly adjust production to sudden changes in demand.
