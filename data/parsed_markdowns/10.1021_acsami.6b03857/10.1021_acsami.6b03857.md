ABSTRACT:  Lithium ( ﬂ uorosulfonyl)(n-nona ﬂ uorobutanesulfonyl)imide [Li[(FSO 2 )(n-C 4 F 9 SO 2 )N] (LiFNFSI)] is inves- tigated as a conducting salt, which can form a relatively stable solid-electrolyte-interphase  ﬁ lm in concentrated ether electrolyte to achieve favorable protection for lithium metal anodes. Li | Cu and Li | Li cells with concentrated LiFNFSI-based electrolyte have been demonstrated to display high average Coulombic e ﬃ ciency ( ≈ 97%) and excellent cycling stability (over 1,000 h) of metallic lithium anodes, compared to concentrated lithium bis(tri ﬂ u oro methane s ulf on yl)imide [Li[N(SO 2 CF 3 ) 2 ] (LiTFSI)]-based electrolyte. The morphologies and compositions of the lithium − metal anode surface are also comparatively analyzed by scanning electron microscopy and X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy, respectively. Moreover, superior electrochemical performance in the concentrated LiFNFSI-based electrolyte for Li | LiFePO 4  cells is also presented herein. These results indicate that concentrated LiFNFSI-based electrolyte is a promising candidate for metallic lithium rechargeable batteries.

# 1. INTRODUCTION  

Lithium (Li) metal is widely considered as an attractive and promising anode material for rechargeable batteries because of ﬁ − 1  

in comparison to graphite as well as the lowest density (0.59 g cm − 3 ) and negative electrochemical potential ( − 3.04 V versus the standard hydrogen electrode) observed over the past ,   several decades. Investigations of next-generation energy storage battery systems with Li metal as the anode, which emerged post-Li-ion batteries, have recently been renewed. For instance, lithium − sulfur (Li − S) battery possessing a high theoretical capacity of 1672 mA h g − 1   and a speci ﬁ c energy of 2,600 W h kg − 1   as well as lithium − air (Li − O 2 ) batteries with − 9   even higher theoretical values have been studied. Unfortu- nately, highly reactive Li metal can easily react with organic solvents, which leads to limited Coulombic E ﬃ ciency (CE) of metallic Li and uncontrollable growth of Li dendrites during continuous cycling processes. These problems have seriously hindered the practical applications of rechargeable Li ,   batteries. Thus, the protection of Li metal is a crucial factor in solving the aforementioned bottleneck problem as a matter of course.  

Selecting a valid electrolyte system is one of the most critical approaches that has been proposed to protect Li metal anodes. In general, Li metal is thermodynamic ally unstable toward nearly all organic solvents; however, a dynamically stabilized Li electrode surface could be obtained via forming a solid-  ﬁ − 14   electrolyte-interphase (SEI) lm. The relatively stable SEI ﬁ lm plays a crucial role in protecting the Li metal anode, which e ﬀ ectively inhibit the further side reactions between the electrolytes and the Li metal. As a result, the cycling e ﬃ ciency and cycling stability of the Li metal anode can be improved. Carbonate-based electrolytes with Li salts, as appropriate universal electrolytes for Li − ion batteries, have been extensively applied because of their wide electrochemical stability windows as well as good compatibility with conventional positive and negative electrodes. Nevertheless, the CE of Li metal in carbonate electrolytes, such as ethylene carbonate (EC) and propylene carbonate (PC), is <80% and Li dendrites growth is observed in the deposition process.   Some functional additives, such as vinylene carbonate (VC) and  ﬂ uoroethylene carbonate (FEC) have been used to provide interfacial protection by promoting the formation of SEI  ﬁ lms on the ,    ﬁ surface of Li metal. Unfortunately, low CE in the rst cycle is a fundamentally di ﬃ cult problem to overcome in the above- mentioned methods.  

As indicated by Aurbach,   ethers exhibt better compatibility with Li metal electrode than carbonates. In particular, the cyclic ether solvents, 1,3-dioxolane (DOL), can decompose and regenerate polymer passivated  ﬁ lms with high elasticity and stability on Li anode surfaces to e ﬀ ectively inhibit Li dendrite growth.   According to the present research on ether-based electrolytes for metallic Li secondary batteries, other than LiTFSI, lithium bis( ﬂ uorosulfonyl)imide [Li[N(SO 2 F) 2 ] (LiFSI)] has been the focus of alternative conducting salts in electrolytes as a result of its advantageous properties over carbonate-based electrolytes. In particular, among the many investigations, concentrated electrolytes were found to exhibit the advantage of e ﬀ ectively stabilizing the surface of Li metal ,   during cycling. Recently, we found that a new class of highly concentrated LiTFSI-DOL/DME electrolyte can play an inhibitory e ﬀ ect on Li dendritic growth with a value of Li +   ion transference number as high as 0.68, which is much higher than that of traditional liquid electrolytes. In addition, the CE of metallic Li plating/stripping on copper substrates was veri ﬁ ed to be relatively high.   More recently, Zhang et al. reported a concentrated electrolyte-based on LiFSI as a conducting salt in the DME solvent, which can achieve a relatively high average CE of 98% even at high rates.   Note that in cyclic ether solvents (e.g., DOL), LiFSI as a single conducting salt or the impurities in the LiFSI are prone to induce ring-opening ,   polymerization of DOL. In addition, novel dual-salt electrolytes based on LiTFSI and LiFSI, as well as lithium ( ﬂ uorosulfonyl) (tri ﬂ uoromethanesulfonyl)imide [Li[(FSO 2 )- N(SO 2 CF 3 )] (LiFTFSI)] and LiFSI, have been explored to cope with the low CE and Li dendrite growth simulta- ,   neously. These results indicate that concentrated electro- lytes play a pivotal role in improving the CE of Li metal and inhibiting Li metal dendritic growth.  

Interestingly, LiFNFSI as a conducting salt dissolved in carbonate electrolyte was investigated and found to exhibit good performance at both room-temperature and high- ,   temperature for Li-ion batteries. Herein, inspired by the aforementioned work, a novel salt-concentrated ether electro- − lyte with LiFNFSI, whose anion (FNFSI ,  Scheme 1 ) structure may combine the advantages of facilitating SEI-formation with stability in the ether solvent system, was designed and investigated. Thus, this electrolyte may not only exhibit excellent cycling stability of Li-metal anode, but also prevent 24   the polymerization of DOL as a result of the high , − 30   delocalization of the electrolyte. Moreover, the phys- icochemical properties and other characterization results of the LiFNFSI-based electrolyte were also reported.

# 2. EXPERIMENTAL SECTION  

2.1. Preparation of Electrolyte Solutions, Electrodes, And Cells.  All the experiments for the preparation of nonaqueous electrolyte solutions, cell assembly and disassembly, and electro- chemical measurements were performed in an argon- ﬁ lled glovebox (M.Braun Inertgas Systems Co., Ltd., H 2 O and O 2  < 0.1 ppm). Lithium( ﬂ uorosulfonyl) (nona ﬂ uorobutanesulfonyl)imide (LiFNFSI,

 99.5%, Suzhou Fluolyte Co., Ltd., China) and lithium bis-

 (tri ﬂ u oro methane s ulf on yl)imide (LiTFSI, TCI, Japan) were both mixed with anhydrous 1,3-dioxolane (DOL)/dime th oxy methane (DME) (v/v, 1:1, BASF Co., Ltd.) in a plastic bottle in a ratio of mole number to volume for preparing the concentrated LiFNFSI- based and LiTFSI-based electrolytes (i.e., 3 mol LiX per L of solvent, where X = FNFSI or TFSI, corresponding to a mole ratio of salt to solvent of 1:4, henceforth abbreviated as 3 M LiX). The Li foil used as the anode (14 mm in diameter and 600  μ m in thickness) was obtained from China Energy Lithium Co., Ltd. The standard sheet of LiFePO 4 cathode (14 mm in diameter and 9.2 mg cm − 2 , obtained from Suzhou Phylion battery, China) was prepared by mixing a slurry of LiFePO 4 powder, polyvinylidene  ﬂ uoride (PVDF), and conductive super P (94:3:3) with Al foil as a current collector and dried at 120  ° C for 12 h in a high vacuum oven. The coin cells (Li | Cu, Li | Li, andLi | LiFePO 4 ) using CR2032 coin cells were assembled in an argon- ﬁ lled glovebox with pure lithium foil as the counter electrode and Celgard 2400 polyethylene membrane as a separator.  

2.2. Characterization and Electrochemical Measurements. The DSC measurement was performed using a NETZSCH 200F3 DSC instrument. A trace amount was sampled and hermetically sealed in an aluminum pan in an argon- ﬁ lled glovebox. The test temperature ranged from  − 150 to 70  ° C at a rate of 10  ° C min − 1   under nitrogen ﬂ ow. The ionic conductivities ( σ ) of the electrolyte solutions were measured by an alternating-current (AC) impedance method in a platinum-black electrode conductivity cell. The AC impedance spectra were recorded on an Autolab PGSTAT302N impedance analyzer (Eco Chemie, Netherlands) and the temperature was accurately controlled from 0 to 60  ° C using an oil bath. The viscosity ( η ) of the electrolyte was determined on a programmable viscometer (Brook ﬁ eld, DV-iii+) at 25  ° C in a cylindrical dry chamber, and the temperature was accurately controlled from 0 to 60  ° C using a Brook ﬁ eld TC-502 oil bath. For the measurement of Li-ion transference numbers ( t Li + ), AC impedance and direct-current (DC) polarization techniques for a symmetric Li | Li cell were combined at 25  ° C and polarized with a potential of 10 mV.  

The morphologies of Li metal electrodes were observed by a scanning electron microscope (Hitachi S-4800) equipped with a vacuum chamber. The cycled cells were disassembled in the glovebox to remove the Li metal electrodes which were loaded into a vacuum transfer box after repeated washing with anhydrous DME and thorough drying. Surface analysis of the cycled lithium metal electrodes was performed by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS, ESCALAB 250) with a monochromatized Al K α  source. Rinsed metallic Li electrodes were sealed on the XPS sample stage for analysis.  

The Li | Cu cells were also tested on a Land CT2001A battery test system (Wuhan LANHE Electronics, China) at 25  ° C. First, during galvanostatic discharging, 1 mA h cm − 2   metallic Li was deposited onto the Cu substrate at the stated current density (0.5 mA cm − 2 ). Then, the Li metal was stripped by charging with the same current density until the potential reached 1 V (vs Li + /Li). The symmetric Li | Li cells were assembled with Li foil for both positive and negative cycling at a current density of 0.5 mA cm − 2   and the plating/stripping coulomb quantity of metallic Li for each cycle was 1 mA h cm − 2 . 3.3. Electrochemical Performance and Compatibility with LiFePO 4  Cathode.  In addition to the stability and reversibility of the Li anode, the electrochemical performance and compatibility of rechargeable Li batteries using our salt- concentrated electrolyte are signi ﬁ cant characteristics for evaluating novel electrolyte systems, e.g., with LiFePO 4  cathode material.  Figure 6 a, c displays the charge/discharge pro ﬁ les of Li | LiFePO 4  cells in the  ﬁ rst 5 cycles at a rate of 0.2  C , utilizing the concentrated LiFNFSI-based and LiTFSI-based electro- lytes, respectively. The consistency of overlapped curve trends for the cell with concentrated LiFNFSI-based electrolyte is quite good, which indicates that a prominent electrochemical reversibility can be obtained. According to  Figure 6 b, d, the speci ﬁ c capacities and CEs of the Li | LiFePO 4  cell in two di ﬀ erent electrolytes at a rate of 0.2  C  with increasing cycle number are presented. The speci ﬁ c charge capacities are 134.8 and 143.4 mA h g − 1   and the speci ﬁ c discharge capacities are 118.0 and 125.1 mA h g − 1   for the  ﬁ rst cycle in LiFNFSI and LiTFSI-based electrolytes, respectively. The value of CE in the ﬁ rst cycle is 87.5% and then gradually increases to nearly 100% (average CE  ≈ 99.8%) in LiFNFSI-based electrolyte. After 200 cycles, the discharge capacity retention is 98.5% in LiFNFSI- based electrolyte, which is slightly higher than that in LiTFSI- based electrolyte (97.6%). These results indicate that relatively stable interphases are formed not only between the electrolyte and LiFePO 4  cathode but also between the electrolyte and metallic Li anode, indicating outstanding compatibility.  

# 4. CONCLUSIONS  

In summary, a novel concentrated LiFNFSI-based electrolyte is proposed that could not only exhibits a high average CE ( ≈ 97%) (Li | Cu cells) but also displays excellent cycling stability of the Li metal anode (Li | Li cells) compared to the concentrated LiTFSI-based electrolyte. This  ﬁ nding is attributed to the unique SEI  ﬁ lms formed on the Li-metal anode in the concentrated LiFNFSI-based electrolyte. The decomposition of LiFNFSI salt is assumed to participate in the formation of stable SEI  ﬁ lms. Moreover, superior cycling performance in the concentrated LiFNFSI-based electrolyte for Li | LiFePO 4  cells has been obtained. All of the above results demonstrate that LiFNFSI shows great potential for application as a conducting salt and this novel single-salt-concentrated electrolyte provides a promising alternative for high-energy metallic Li rechargeable batteries.
