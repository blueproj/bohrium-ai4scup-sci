#   Creep behavior in a  c 0 strengthened Co–Al–W–Ta–Ti single-crystal alloy at 1000   C  

b   a , , ⇑ F. Xue, H.J. Zhou, Q.Y. Shi, X.H. Chen, H. Chang, M.L. Wang and Q. Feng  

a State Key Laboratory for Advanced Metals and Materials, University of Science and Technology Beijing, Beijing 100083, People’s Republic of China b National Center for Materials Service Safety, University of Science and Technology Beijing, Beijing 100083, People’s Republic of China  

Received 25 August 2014; revised 7 October 2014; accepted 9 October 2014 Available online 3 November 2014  

The tensile creep behavior and microstructural stability of a Co–Al–W–Ta–Ti single-crystal alloy were investigated at 1000   C. Long-term aging at 1000   C for 1000 h revealed stable  c – c 0   structure with 75%  c 0   volume fraction. The measured positive lattice misﬁt resulted in directional coarsening parallel to the applied stress during creep. The  c – c 0   interfacial dislocation networks and shearing of  c 0   precipitates with stacking faults and antiphase boundaries appeared to contribute to the creep resistance with 1.0% creep stain.  

2014 Acta Materialia Inc. Published by Elsevier Ltd. All rights reserved. Keywords:  Co-base alloys; Superalloys; Lattice misﬁt; Creep; Dislocation  

Nickel-base superalloys have been utilized in gas turbine applications for decades due to their superior creep resistance strengthened by L1 2 -type  c 0   precipitates at high temperature  [1,2] . The recently reported Co–Al–W-base alloys exhibit similar  c – c 0   two-phase microstructure and comparable creep resistance to ﬁrst-generation Ni-base single-crystal superalloy at 900   C, and are promising candidates for high-temperature applications  [3–5] .  

In an attempt to increase the  c 0   strengthening eﬀect, which usually depends on a high  c 0   volume fraction in c – c 0   two-phase alloys, the alloying eﬀects on  c 0   solvus tem- perature have been investigated in Co–Al–W-base alloys [6–11] . In general, the  c 0   solvus temperature and volume fraction were signiﬁcantly increased by Ta, Ti, Nb and V additions, whereas secondary phases were observed among certain alloys due to the very narrow region of  c – c 0   two- phase  [4,6–8] . Creep research revealed a pronounced increase of high-temperature strength with Ta addition, whereas quaternary alloys containing Ti yielded the best creep resistance, which was ascribed to the high  c 0   solvus temperature and microstructural stability  [4,9] . However, in contrast to the extensive microstructural studies, rela- tively limited work has been conducted to investigate high-temperature mechanical properties, in particular for creep properties of Co–Al–W-base single-crystal alloys as potential turbine blade or vane materials  [4–6,12] . More- over, these investigations have been mainly conducted in ternary and quaternary alloys at temperatures lower than 1000   C due to relatively low  c 0   solvus temperature, and limited attempts have been made to understand the creep deformation mechanism  [4,12,13] . Although  c 0   directional coarsening was observed in Co-base alloys and was gener- ally diﬀerent from commercial Ni-base single-crystal super- alloys with negative misﬁt, its role in creep behavior and strengthening remains unclear, especially at temperatures above 950   C  [4,5,13] .  

In our previous research, a Co–Al–W–Ta–Ti single- crystal alloy was designed and investigated based on fundamental studies on ternary and quaternary alloys [8,10,14–16] . This alloy exhibited a high  c 0   solvus tempera- ture of 1131   C, a  c 0   volume fraction of   63% and the absence of secondary phase as well as  c 0   directional coarsen- ing after aging at 1050   C for 1000 h, indicating better microstructural stability than quaternary alloys containing either Ta or Ti  [14] . Its rupture life exceeded those of com- mercial ﬁrst-generation Ni-base single crystal superalloys at 982   C and 248 MPa  [15,17] . In this study, its high-tem- perature and relatively low-stress creep behavior was inves- tigated at 1000   C and 137 MPa. The associated long-term microstructural stability, e.g. the absence of secondary phase and the low directional coarsening rate of  c 0   precipitates, and the lattice misﬁt were also studied. The observation of microstructural evolution and dislocation substructure was performed to understand the deformation mechanism.

The microstructure and dislocation conﬁguration of Alloy TaTi after 1.0% creep strain was examined in order to elucidate the deformation mechanism.  Figure 4 a shows the typical three-dimensional microstructures of Alloy TaTi. The directionally coarsened  c 0   precipitates were well deﬁned on the (100) and (010) vertical sections and paral- lel to the applied tensile stress. The rafts mainly exhibited [100] and [010] normals, which were consistent with the regular inter penetrating lamellar structure on the horizon- tal section ( Fig. 4 a).  

Observation of dislocation substructure in Alloy TaTi identiﬁed  c – c 0   interfacial dislocation networks, dislocations coupled with stacking faults (SFs) and antiphase bound- aries (APBs) within the  c 0   precipitates as the general defects.  Figure 4 b is a TEM image taken from the foil nor- mal to the [010] orientation, showing typical dislocation networks at the  c – c 0   interface. The interfacial networks were often observed in (010) planes rather than (001) planes, suggesting that they preferentially existed in the ver- tical section of Alloy TaTi. A high density of dislocations with SFs was formed within a single raft and frequently intersected each other ( Fig. 4 b), which implied the shearing of  c 0   precipitates by activating multiple <112> slips  [5,6] . Figure 4 c shows that some SFs appeared in an edge-on view (marked by arrows) in the [011] beam direction, which is parallel to two types of {111} planes, again indicating that multiple <112> slip systems were equally stressed on diﬀerent {111} planes. In addition to the SFs, it is interest- ing to note that the APBs frequently coexisted with SFs in c 0   precipitates. The APBs were observed by using superlat- tice reﬂections because of their phase shift of | p |  [19] .  Figure 4 d is a centered dark-ﬁeld micrograph under g  ¼  0 11 in the same [011] beam direction as  Figure 4 c, demonstrating the APBs with the dark contrast (marked by arrows) adjacent to SFs, whereas APBs possessed a residual contrast under g  ¼  02 2 ( Fig. 4 c).  

In comparison with other Co–Al–W-base alloys at 1000   C and 137 MPa, Alloy TaTi exhibited more creep resistance than 2Ta alloy, which produced the longest creep rupture life of 187.9 h in a previous report  [5] . Although their initial  c 0   volume fractions were similar, long-term aging at 1000   C for 1000 h revealed  c – c 0   microstructure with 75% c 0   volume fraction in Alloy TaTi ( Fig. 1 b). In contrast, the c 0   phase was <10% and signiﬁcant amounts of secondary phases precipitated in 2Ta alloy after recrystallization heat treatment at 1000   C for 168 h following cold rolling with a reduction ratio of 50%  [5] . The improved creep resistance of Alloy TaTi appears to be associated with its better micro- structural stability in terms of the higher  c 0   volume fraction compared to 2Ta alloy. This is analogous to previous inves- tigations, which suggested that Co–Al–W-base alloys with a high  c 0   volume fraction of   75% exhibited better creep strength than alloy with   40%  c 0   volume fraction at 900   C [4,20] . The high  c 0   volume fraction in Alloy TaTi may be ascribed to its higher  c 0   solvus temperature (33   C higher than that of 2Ta alloy  [5,14] ) and modiﬁed phase equilibria promoted by the combined addition of Ta and Ti. The rela- tionship between the  c 0   volume fraction and creep property in Ni-base single-crystal superalloys has been well investi- gated  [21] , while more work is required on this type of rela- tionship in the new class of Co-base single-crystal superalloys.  

It is known that the directional coarsening behavior of  c 0 precipitates during high-temperature creep results from the superposition of the  c – c 0   lattice misﬁt and applied stresses in Ni-base superalloys  [22] . The investigation of Fa ¨hrmann and coworkers indicated that the initial cuboidal  c 0   precip- itates evolved into plates parallel to the tensile axis or perpendicular to the compression axis in Ni–Al–Mo alloy with positive misﬁt at elevated temperature  [23] . The Co– Al–W-base alloys, which were reported to possess positive misﬁt, exhibited analogous rafting behavior to Ni-base superalloys under either compressive or tensile stresses [4,9,13,20] . In the current study, Alloy TaTi demonstrated positive misﬁt at 1000   C ( Fig. 2 ), and rafts parallel to the direction of the applied tensile stress were developed during creep. Both results are consistent, again indicating that the rafting behavior in Co–Al–W-base alloys was asso- ciated with the positive misﬁt and the applied tensile stress during the creep process.  

Alloy TaTi presented interfacial dislocation networks at 1.0% creep strain ( Fig. 4 b), indicating the build-up of dislocation networks relatively early in the creep stage at 1000   C. The early formed dislocation networks in the pres- ent study may be beneﬁcial since they act as barriers that pre- vent dislocations in the  c  matrix from cutting the  c 0   phase [24] . The shearing of  c 0   precipitates by a high density of SFs indicated the occurrence of multiple <112> slips, which were predominantly within a single raft with obviously large size rather than extending over the matrix and neighboring c 0   precipitates. The intersections between numerous multiple <112> slips seem to provide signiﬁcant creep resistance of Alloy TaTi at high temperature and low stress. It is thus sug- gested that the matrix no longer provides a continuous path for dislocation motion when rafting is complete, leading to the shearing of  c 0   precipitates by SFs. The shearing of  c 0 precipitates by a high density of SFs was observed under the same creep condition in Co–9Al–8W–2Ta alloy, which has a lower W content than 2Ta alloy  [5] . Additionally, the shearing of  c 0   precipitates by APBs also occurred in Alloy TaTi and could be another reason for the improved creep resistance with 1% creep strain ( Fig. 4 b–d), whereas this deformation mode has not been reported in Co–Al– W-base alloys at 1000   C. The presence of APBs next to SFs may be a variation of shearing by <112> slip with an intermediate step, e.g. the entire ½ <110> dislocations entered into the precipitates and left APBs in their wake which were then removed by the Shockley loops  [25] . Never- theless, the APBs might have been induced by appropriate deformation during creep after the formation of SFs within the  c 0   precipitates. The deformation mechanism with SFs and APBs in Alloy TaTi at 1000   C was diﬀerent from the shearing mechanism by paired dislocations with APBs in Ni-base superalloys under similar creep conditions  [26] , indi- cating that SFs were easily formed in Co-base alloys possibly due to their SF energy being lower than those of Ni-base superalloys  [27] . However, ﬁrst-principles study suggested that the superlattice intrinsic stacking fault energy of  c 0 - Co 3 (Al,W) and Co 3 (Al,W,Ta) was higher than that of  c 0 - Ni 3 Al using the axial next nearest neighbor Ising model [28] . More work is required to clarify the creep behavior and deformation modes in Co-base alloys with combined Ta and Ti additions through changing SF energy or/and APB energy. Detailed dislocation analyses are underway and will be published elsewhere.  

In summary, the lattice misﬁt of a Co–Al–W–Ta–Ti alloy was determined to be +0.44% by using synchrotron radia- tion at 1000   C, where the prolonged heat treatment for 1000 h revealed a stable  c – c 0   two-phase microstructure with a  c 0   volume fraction of 75%. Directional coarsening of  c 0   pre- cipitates occurred during creep and promoted the formation of  c 0   rafts parallel to the tensile stress axis, again suggesting a good agreement with its positive misﬁt. The creep life with 1.0% strain in the experimental single-crystal alloy signiﬁ- cantly exceeded the previously reported longest creep rup- ture life in Co–Al–W-base alloy at 1000   C and 137 MPa. The improved microstructural stability in terms of high  c 0 volume fraction, the formation of  c 0   rafts,  c – c 0   interfacial dislocation networks as well as the shearing of  c 0   precipitates by <112> multiple slips with SFs and APBs are expected to contribute to the improved creep resistance. For future alloy development, it will be necessary to ﬁnd why the combined addition of Ta and Ti improves the stability.
