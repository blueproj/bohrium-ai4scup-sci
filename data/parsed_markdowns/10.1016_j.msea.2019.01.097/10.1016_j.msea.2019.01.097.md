# A B S T R A C T  

# A R T I C L E I N F O  

With the aim of achieving an enhanced high-temperature strength compared to the Co-Ni-Al-W-Ti-Ta alloy from our previous study, a new single crystalline CoNi-base superalloy has been developed based on thermodynamic calculations. The  γ ′  solvus temperature and the  γ ′  volume fraction were experimentally determined as 1247 °C and 85.2%. Thermodynamic calculations correctly predict the tendency of how the alloying elements in ﬂ uence the thermophysical properties of investigated alloys. In comparison with the original Co-Ni-Al-W-Ti-Ta alloy, the newly developed CoNi-base alloy exhibited stronger compressive strength ranging from the room temperature to 1100 °C. The deformation behaviors at di ﬀ erent temperature stages were investigated in detail. Interestingly, superlattice intrinsic stacking faults (SISFs) appear in the specimen deformed at the anomalous peak tempera- ture, whereas the formation of SISF in the original Co-Ni-Al-W-Ti-Ta alloy occurs as the compression tem- perature increases beyond the anomalous peak temperature. The relationship between the occurrence of planar defects and the alloying e ﬀ ects was discussed.

# 1. Introduction  

Conventional Co-base superalloys strengthened by metal carbides exhibit insu ﬃ cient high-temperature strength for structural applica- tions as the temperature increases beyond 800 °C  [1] . In contrast, Ni- base superalloys exhibit outstanding high-temperature mechanical properties resulted from the thermodynamic ally stable microstructure composed of L1 2  precipitates ( γ ′ ) and face-centered cubic A1 matrix ( γ ). Hence Ni-base superalloys always play a critical role withstanding the hottest section of turbine engines. To enhance the performance of tur- bine engines, increasing the thermal e ﬃ ciency should be the priority task but which is currently limited by the unchangeable solidus tem- perature of Ni-base superalloys. Due to the higher melting point of cobalt, Co-base alloys have been considered as a promising alloy system which could outperform Ni-base superalloys in high-temperature ap- plication areas. From decades ago researchers began seeking for Co- base systems that strengthened by L1 2  precipitates ( γ ′ ) and enable to exhibit the positive temperature dependence of the yield stress, such as Co-Ti  [2 – 5]  and Co-Ta  [6,7] . However, numerous disadvantages hinder the development of these alloys including insu ﬃ cient thermal stability of the microstructure, low  γ ′  solvus temperatures, and undesirable high magnitudes of the lattice mis ﬁ t between  γ  and  γ ′  phases. Until recently, Sato et al.  [8]  discovered a new class of Co-base superalloy, Co-Al-W  

There are however two major drawbacks present in Co-Al-W su- peralloys, inferior high-temperature strength and lower  γ ′  solvus tem- perature relative to advanced Ni-base superalloys. In literature it has been reported that additions of Ni, Ti, and Ta prefer to partition to the γ ′  phase and which leads to improved high-temperature strength and higher  γ ′  solvus temperature for Co-Al-W superalloys  [9 – 16] . Metal carbides have recently been introduced into  γ ′ -strengthening Co-Al-W alloys to improve the mechanical properties  [17,18] . Even though the yield stress has been increased by formations of carbides, the creep resistance might be greatly reduced due to the large amount of in- coherent interphase between carbides and the matrix phase. In addition to observations on measured properties, attention should also be paid on exploring the deformation behaviors that fundamentally in ﬂ uence the mechanical properties of metallic materials. Previous studies [11,19,20]  have illustrated the deformation behaviors of ternary Co-Al- W superalloys that most dislocations tend to shear in the  γ  matrix, and some paired 1/2 < 110 > -type super dislocations shear in  γ ′  pre- cipitates at temperatures below the peak temperature. As the tem- perature increases beyond the peak temperature, dislocations begin to bypass the  γ ′  precipitates. Furthermore, additions of Ta in Co-Al-W superalloys cause the shearing of 1/3 < 112 > -type superpartial dis- locations in  γ ′  precipitates with formation of stacking faults in high- temperature as-deformed specimens  [11,20,21] . The deformation me- chanism is therefore strongly in ﬂ uenced by various elemental addi- tions. To optimize the alloy design of Co-Al-W superalloys,  ﬁ guring out the relationship between alloying elements and deformation me- chanism should be crucial.  

Recently, we have investigated on a TaTi-containing CoNi-base su- peralloy, CoNi-2, which possesses a comparable high-temperature strength with respect to 1st generation Ni-base superalloys  [21] . However, it contains an ultrahigh  γ ′  volume fraction (92%), which has been proved to degenerate high-temperature mechanical properties [22,23] . In the present study, a new Co-Ni-Al-W-Ta-Ti alloy, CoNi-5, with improved mechanical properties has been designed by thermo- dynamic calculations. This paper focuses on discussing the variations of deformation behaviors between the investigated alloys and how these deformation behaviors a ﬀ ect the mechanical properties.  

# 2. Experimental procedures  

The chemical composition of investigated CoNi-base superalloys, CoNi-1, CoNi-2, and CoNi-5, were measured by the X-ray  ﬂ uorescence (XRF) technique and the results are given in  Table 1 . Single crystalline specimens were produced by using a Bridgeman furnace with a with- drawal rate of 6 mm/min. The specimens were grown along the [001] crystal orientation and the deviation was determined to be less than 10° utilizing the transmission electron microscopy (TEM). Investigated al- loys were homogenized at 1250 °C for 24 h, subsequently aged at 900 °C for 168 h.  

The CALPHAD method  [24,25]  was used to calculate the  γ ′  volume fraction and the  γ ′  solvus temperature of investigated alloys utilizing software Thermo-Calc TCNI8 database (version 8.1)  [26]  which con- tains the newest thermodynamic data of Co-base and Ni-base super- alloys.  

The scanning electron microscopy (SEM) characterization was car- ried out using a Zeiss GeminiSEM500 and the specimens were me- chanically grinded and polished following standard metallography procedures. As for the determination of the  γ ′  volume fraction, ImageJ software was used to measure the area fraction of the  γ ′  phase on SEM images of investigated alloys. To investigate the deformation behaviors of as-deformed specimens, the transmission electron microscope (TEM) characterization was conducted using a Jeol Jem2100 TEM. For the TEM specimen preparation, thin foils were transversely cut from the single crystalline alloys and grinded to the thickness of around 50 μ m. The  ﬁ nal thinning utilized the twin-jet electro polishing system operated at 233 K in a 92.5% ethanol  –  7.5% perchloric acid electrolyte.  

To experimentally determine the  γ ′  solvus temperature of CoNi-5, the di ﬀ erential thermal analysis (DTA) was conducted on a specimen with mass around 250 mg using a NETZSCH STA 449F3. The in- vestigated specimens were heated from the room temperature to 1400 °C and the  γ ′  solvus temperature was determined under a heating rate of 5 °C/min. The 0.2%  ﬂ ow stresses of investigated alloys at various temperatures were obtained by compression tests utilizing a thermo- mechanical simulator Gleeble 1500D. The compressive specimens were cylindrical with 5 mm in diameter and 10 mm in length. The deformation direction was along the [001] crystal orientation with a strain rate of 10 − 4   s − 1 .  

# 3. Results  

# 3.1. Microstructure and  γ ′  solvus temperature  

Fig. 1 (a)-(f) show the as-treated microstructures of the investigated alloys, CoNi-1, CoNi-2, and CoNi-5, via secondary electron SEM and bright  ﬁ eld TEM techniques. All alloys exhibited a  ﬁ ne microstructural stability after the heat treatment since the as-treated microstructures were observed to possess a typical  γ / γ ′  two-phase microstructure without any additional intermetallic precipitates such as B2-, Laves, and D0 19 -phases. Nevertheless, the micrographs reveal a change of the γ ′  phase in the morphology and the volume fraction between in- vestigated alloys. The edges of  γ ′  phases in CoNi-2 and CoNi-5 are sharper than that of the  γ ′  phase in CoNi-1. It can be inferred from the literature that additions of Ta and Ti in CoNi-2 and CoNi-5 lead to larger values of the lattice mis ﬁ t and a slight loss of coherency com- pared to CoNi-1  [27] . The  γ ′  volume fraction of CoNi-1 was measured to be 67.3%, whereas the additions of strong  γ ′  formers, Ta and Ti, in- creased the values of CoNi-2 and CoNi-5 to 92.1% and 85.2%  [21] . In comparison with CoNi-2, CoNi-5 replaces 3 at% of W with 1 at% of Co, 1 at% of Ta, and 1 at% of Ti and which has decreased the  γ ′  volume fraction to 85.2%. It is resulted from the 1 at% increase of the  γ  phase former element, Co, and the 1 at% decrease of the  γ ′  phase former element  [14,28 – 32] .  

Fig. 2 (a) and (b) reveal the measured  γ ′  solvus temperature of CoNi- 5 and a comparison between CoNi-5 and alloys from ref.  [10,21,33] . CoNi-5 exhibits a higher  γ ′  solvus temperature around 11 °C relative to CoNi-2. It can be said that additions of Ta and Ti can improve the thermal stability of the  γ ′  phase more e ﬀ ectively compared to the ad- dition of W, which is consistent with the theoretical thermodynamic calculation by Ooshima et al.  [34] . Furthermore, the alloying e ﬀ ects of Ni, Ti, and Ta have enabled the  γ ′  solvus temperature of the ternary Co- 10Al-10W to increase over 200 °C and the lack behind SRR-99 alloy, a 1st generation single-crystal Ni-base superalloy, has been reduced to less than 30 °C.

3.3. Temperature dependence of the 0.2%  ﬂ ow stress  

To measure the 0.2% ﬂ ow stress of investigated alloys, a thermo mechanical simulator was used to conduct compression tests at various temperatures. The curves representing the temperature de- pendence of the 0.2%  ﬂ ow stress of investigated alloys are shown in Fig. 4 . Apparently that CoNi-2 and CoNi-5 exhibited a similar tem- perature dependence of the 0.2%  ﬂ ow stress, the yield stress anomaly arose as the compression temperature increased beyond 500 °C and reached the peak  ﬂ ow stress at around 900 °C. Nevertheless, CoNi-5 performed superior 0.2%  ﬂ ow stresses at every tested temperatures compared to CoNi-2, which possesses a stronger high-temperature strength at 900 °C relative to the 1st generation Ni-base superalloy, 713LC  [21] . For the room-temperature strength, CoNi-1 was very much stronger than CoNi-2,5 due to the relatively large amount of the  γ  phase that allowed more hyper ﬁ ne  γ ′  precipitates to form during the cooling stage of the aging process. It has been known from the literature that dislocations would preferentially shear in the  γ  phase at low tempera- tures and hence hyper ﬁ ne  γ ′  precipitates could e ﬀ ectively hinder the movements of dislocations  [39,40] .

# 4. Discussion  

The outstanding high-temperature mechanical properties given by the stable and strong  γ ′ -Ni 3 Al precipitates enable Ni-base superalloys to be an irreplaceable structural material in gas turbine engines  [23] . In this research, we aimed to enhance the high-temperature strength of the CoNi-2 alloy through modifying the alloy design. In comparison with the CoNi-2 alloy, the design concepts of the CoNi-5 alloy have suc- cessfully increase the thermal stability of the  γ ′  phase and decrease the unacceptably high  γ ′  volume fraction. It should be noticed that the thermodynamic calculations using the database TCNi8 correctly predict the in ﬂ uence of alloying e ﬀ ects on the  γ ′  solvus temperature and the  γ ′ volume fraction prior to the experimental measurements. However, the calculated values signi ﬁ cantly deviate from the experimental values. Interestingly, a recent study published by Volz et al.  [45]  has reported a deviation, 30% less in  γ ′  volume fraction and 100 °C lower in  γ ′  solvus temperature compared to experimental values, which is nearly identical to the result we obtained in the present study, as listed in  Table 2 . The transitions of deformation mechanisms at di ﬀ erent temperature stages, i.e. below, at, and above the peak temperature, in the CoNi-5 alloy could be interpreted by energies of faults. At the  ﬁ rst stage, below the peak temperature, the presence of super dislocations separated by APB on {111} planes rather than the formation of SISF indicates that the {111}APB energy is lower than the SISF energy. As the compression temperature increases to 900 °C, at the peak temperature, APB and SISF can be found in the microstructure simultaneously. It could be deduced that the magnitude of APB energy and that of SISF energy are similar to each other and therefore the presence of APB or SISF might be decided by the  ﬂ uctuated local chemical composition. This result fairly agrees with the literature which claimed that based on the  ﬁ rst-principles calculation, the SISF energy in Co-Al-W superalloys is largely in ﬂ uenced by the  ﬂ uctuations of the local chemical composition  [47,48] . The driving force for the chemical  ﬂ uctuations stems from the tendency of forming stable intermetallic D0 19  phases  [47] . Besides, the existence of cross-slip segments in long super dislocations represents that the ther- modynamic driving force is su ﬃ cient for cross-slips from octahedral planes to cube planes, where the energy of APB is relatively low. At the last stage, above the peak temperature, the deformation mechanism is dominated by the dissociation of < 110 > super dislocations into Shockley superpartial dislocations separated by SISF on {111} planes and the shearing of unpaired super dislocations in  γ ′  precipitates. Ac- cording to the previous studies, the APB energy is weakly dependent on the chemical composition and the deformation temperature  [19,49] . On the contrary, the SISF energy can be locally reduced by the solute segregation  [48] . It can therefore be inferred that the relatively large extent of the solute segregation caused by the elevated temperature could lead to a great amount of SISF as the compression temperature is above the peak temperature. However, the occurrence of solute segre- gation in the vicinity of SISF in the as-compressed CoNi-5 alloy at elevated temperatures should be experimentally con ﬁ rmed in the fu- ture.  

It is interesting to noted that there is a di ﬀ erence of the deformation behavior between CoNi-2 and CoNi-5 at 900 °C which is the peak temperature for both investigated alloys. The SISF is totally absent in CoNi-2 deformed at 900 °C, whereas a large amount of SISF can be observed in CoNi-5 deformed at 900 °C. It is likely that the reduction of 3 at% tungsten in CoNi-5 compared with CoNi-2 has increased the in- terdi ﬀ usion coe ﬃ cients of alloying elements and decreased the energy barrier for the solute segregation. Consequently, the formation of SISF occurs at a lower temperature in CoNi-5 compared to CoNi-2. Furthermore, the appearance of SISF, to some extent, have inhibited the motion of dislocations during the compression test. Hence the stacking fault strengthening could also be a reason leading to the superior peak ﬂ ow stress of CoNi-5 relative to CoNi-2. Indeed, the competitive re- lationship of energies of faults is the deciding factor in ﬂ uencing the deformation behaviors of CoNi-base superalloys. To quantitatively de- termine the energies of faults using experimental and simulation methods is currently a main research direction in our group.  

# 5. Conclusions  

An advanced  γ ′ -strengthening CoNi-base superalloy, CoNi-5, has been designed using the thermodynamic calculation. The thermo- physical property, high-temperature strength, and deformation beha- viors of the CoNi-5 alloy were investigated. The conclusions from this research are drawn as follows:  

1. Thermodynamic calculations utilizing TCNI8 (version 8.1) database provide a correct prediction about how alloying e ﬀ ects a ﬀ ect the  γ ′ solvus temperature and the  γ ′  volume fraction. However, the cal- culated values are considerably lower than the experimental values.

 2. The CoNi-5 alloy exhibits superior  ﬂ ow stresses at all tested tem- peratures relative to the CoNi-2 alloy. Aside from the better solid solution strengthening e ﬀ ects of Ta and Ti compared to W, the re- duced  γ ′  volume fraction and the stacking fault strengthening suc- cessfully enhance the  ﬂ ow stress of CoNi-5 at low temperatures and high temperatures, respectively.  

3. The deformation mechanisms of CoNi-2 and CoNi-5 at various temperature stages are similar, whereas the SISF appearing tem- perature of CoNi-5 is lower than that of CoNi-2 owing to the re- duction of the slow-di ﬀ using element, W, in CoNi-5.
