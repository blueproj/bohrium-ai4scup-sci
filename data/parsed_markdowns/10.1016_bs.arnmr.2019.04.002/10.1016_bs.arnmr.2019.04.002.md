# Abstract  

In electrochemistry, the important physical constants are ionic conductivity, viscosity and ion diffusion constants. Although the ionic conductivity ( σ ) and viscosity ( η ) have been widely obtained, measurements of diffusion constant ( D ) became recently possi- ble by the pulsed field gradient (PFG) NMR method. The relations between  D  and  η  and D  and  σ  are described by the Stokes-Einstein and Nernst-Einstein equations, respec- tively. The research and development of the Li-ion battery (LIB) is one of the most pop- ular subjects and the observation of ion diffusion constants by the PFG-NMR methods  

became a state-of-the-art technique. In this chapter, PFG-NMR measurements of the + diffusion constants of Li , and the related anions and solvents of solution electrolytes + +   and Li , cations and anions in ionic liquids, as well as Li diffusion phenomena in inorganic solid electrolytes will be described.  

Keywords:  PFG-NMR, Ion diffusion, Electrolyte for Li-ion battery, Organic solution electrolyte, Ionic liquid, Inorganic solid electrolyte  

# 1. Introduction  

The first commercial Li-ion batteries (LIBs) were produced in 1991. Their main composition was LiCoO 2  (cathode), graphite (anode), and organic solvents including Li salt (electrolyte). To date, there has been much progress in industrial and basic research in LIBs and this is likely to continue. Electro- chemical ionic conductivity ( σ ) is one of the most important parameters in LIBs and a higher  σ  in materials is always desirable to produce better LIBs. The parameter  σ  is defined as the velocity of ions multiplied by the numbers of charge-carrying ions. In liquid electrolytes, cations and anions both contrib- ute to  σ . The well-known Nernst-Einstein (NE) equation describes the rela- tion between  σ  and the cation and the anion diffusion constants ( D + + D ).  

The diffusion constant ( D ) can be measured by NMR with application of field-gradient (FG) in addition to a static magnetic field. The application of a static field-gradient (SFG) method was proposed in the early stages of NMR history and ultra-high SFG-NMR techniques were described  [1] . Pulse field-gradient (PFG) sets of a diffusion probe equipped with PFG coils and an amplifier were gradually produced as commercially available equip- ment. Meanwhile, to suppress solvent signals, the attachment of a modest PFG unit to usual high-resolution liquid-state NMR spectrometers became popular. The PFG technique was found to be useful for automatic quick shimming to obtain a homogeneous magnetic field. In addition, to separate spectra of mixtures of various molecular weights having different diffusion constants, diffusion-ordered NMR spectroscopy (DOSY) became impor- tant, where PFG coils must be wound in an NMR probe head. At present, almost all the new and standard high-resolution NMR spectrometers are equipped with a PFG unit of up to about 100 gauss cm  1   (  1 Tm  1 ). The  D  values of molecules and ions in non-viscous liquids can be measured by popular NMR machines.  

For measurements of slow diffusion, a larger PFG strength is needed. Commercial PFG diffusion probes equipped with solid PFG coils (up to  15 Tm  1 ) have been improved for the measurement of multinuclear dif- fusion phenomena under reasonable temperature ranges, which are suitable for observing ion diffusion in electrolytes of LIBs. The principles and general applications of the PFG-NMR techniques were described by Stilbs in 1987 [2]  and NMR microscopy techniques were described by Callaghan in 1991  [3] . In the early stages, the targets using commercially available PFG units were mainly neutral materials by   1 H NMR  [4 – 8] . In this chapter, the targets of +   7 the multinuclear NMR measurements by the PFG method are Li ions ( Li), 19 11 1 anions ( F and   B) and solvents ( H) that are included in Li solution elec- +   trolytes and ionic liquids. Also, Li diffusion in inorganic solid electrolytes for next-generation all-solid Li batteries will be discussed. Using a commercially available multinuclear PFG-NMR instrument, the diffusion constant data have been collected for ions in electrolytes used in LIBs. The range of the diffusion constants enabled by the PFG-NMR methods is between 10  9 and 10  13   m 2   s  1 . The Li diffusion within electrodes are much slower (10  14  0  17   m 2   s  1 ) and PFG-NMR cannot measure such slow Li diffusion.  

In homogeneous liquid electrolytes, an individual ion has a unique  D value at a certain temperature and a physical constant comparable with  η and  σ . The  D  values depend on the temperature and the salt concentration. At first, we will describe briefly how to measure accurate  D  values for a liq- uid electrolyte. As the Li diffusion phenomena in solid inorganic electrolytes intended for use in all-solid-state Li batteries are very complicated, the mea- surement details will be described in  Section 5 .  

Diffusion measurements by PFG-NMR methods have been widely developed and many books and review papers have been published on the topic  [2 – 8] . In this chapter, we do not intend to explain the general principles of PFG-NMR. Our main purpose is to present practical descrip- tions of diffusion measurements and experimental results for the ions in liquid and solid Li electrolytes. We will start by showing how to measure 7 19 11 1 the  D  of Li ion ( Li),  D  ( F and   B), and  D  ( H) in homoge- Li anion solvent neous liquid electrolytes. To obtain accurate and reliable  D  values, we need to know the calibration methods and the instrumental problems. To mea- sure  D  values, we always observe echo signals. At present, pulsed-gradient spin-echo NMR (PGSE-NMR) and pulse field-gradient (PFG-NMR) are used for the same meanings. Since PFG-NMR has been used in “Annual Reports on NMR Spectroscopy”, we will use PFG-NMR in this chapter.

# 2.1 PFG coil size and sample volume  

The basic concept of PFG-NMR is that the first PFG marks target nuclei in the initial position and after a time interval  Δ , the encoded nuclei are detected by the second PFG. In a PFG probe head, a Maxwell pair coil (a pair of circular winding coils in the opposite symmetrical direction) is located around the sam- ple’s centre. The shield coils are wound outside of the coils to prevent eddy current effects. As the linear portion (e.g.   5%) of the PFG for a 5 mm sample tube is limited (up to   10 mm vertical length, on an ordinal specification of a standard PFG probe), the whole sample volume must be limited within the linear PFG volume applied. For variable temperature control conducted by the flow method, it is desirable that the sample size is small to prevent convec- tion effects in liquid samples or temperature inhomogeneity.  

# 2.2 Calibration of PFG strength  g  

To obtain an accurate value of  D , the calibration of  g  is extremely important. Usually the  D  value of water ( D H2O ) is used as the primary reference to calibrate g (DH2O¼2.30109 m2 s1 at 25°C) [9]. The temperature-dependent numerical values of  D H2O  between 15  ° C and 56  ° C were given by Holz et al.  [10]  between 1.77 and 4.44  10  9   m 2   s  1 . # 2.3 Calibration of temperature  

The  D  values in solutions are very sensitive to temperature and the temper- ature calibration is also important. Using methanol (CH 3 OH) and ethylene 1 glycol (HO-(CH ) -OH), the temperature can be calibrated by the   H shift 2 2 differences  [13] .  

# 2.4 Convection effects  

When the variable temperature experiments are conducted by the flow method, a temperature gradient occurs within a sample volume, in which the temperature inhomogeneity of the sample is affected by each temperature-variable probe-design  [14] . With the diffusion measurements, the convection effects are additive to the self-diffusion constant, notably the effects appear in non-viscous liquids. Generally, to prevent convection effects, the use of a small sample volume is preferable. Additionally, a longer Δ  enhances the convection effects as shown in  D  of diglyme (CAS Number: 111-96-6, viscosity: 1.00 mPas at 25  ° C). A specially designed sample-tube was proposed to reduce the convection effects  [15] . If the apparent  D  values of a non-viscous liquid sample increase with an increase of  Δ , it is necessary to doubt the convection effects.  

# 2.5 PFG shape  

The shape of PFG is one of the important factors in determining the accu- racy of diffusion constants. Technical improvements in commercial PFG probes make it possible to produce a rectangular PFG with short rising and falling times to minimize eddy current effects. When a rectangular PFG is used, the  D  value should be the same for measurements with a constant  g  with varying  δ  or a constant  δ  with varying  g . # 4. Ionic liquids including lithium salts  

In the 1990s, researches on room-temperature ionic liquids (RTIL, IL) composed of an organic cation and an anion became very popular. The PFG-NMR diffusion constants ( D ) of ions in ILs have been reported for the first time on non-chloroaluminate ILs, together with physicochemical constants such as viscosity and ionic conductivity  [54] . Successively, impor- tant papers for physicochemical properties of ILs have been published from Watanabe group  [55] . NMR studies of molten salts and RTILs were described and  D  values of cations and anions of neat ILs at 298 K were sum- marized by Rollet and Bessada  [56] . NMR studies on translational and rotational motions for ILs were described by Hayamizu through Internet [57] . Generally, ILs are stable, negligible vapour pressure, low flammability and high ionic conductivity. As electrolyte materials, ILs are attractive to use electrochemical devices, particularly Li ion batteries (LIBs). In this chapter, +   the subjects are limited on the Li diffusion and related phenomena of ILs which included Li salts.

For particles in homogeneous space, a unique diffusion constant can be obtained and assigned as a physical constant at a certain temperature. In addi- tion to homogenous diffusion, diffusion behaviours of particles in various environments have been widely studied. Related to Li diffusion in solid inorganic electrolytes, non-homogeneous diffusion behaviours are known: (1)  Δ -dependent diffusion for particles in porous or highly viscous materials such as zeolites, membranes, and polymer electrolytes, called “anomalous dif- fusion”. The apparent diffusion constant becomes faster for shorter  Δ [122 – 124] . (2) Diffusive diffraction patterns in echo attenuation plots for par- ticles confined in a restricted space, called “restricted diffusion”  [8,125 – 128] . In the inorganic solid electrolytes, atomic-level Li pathways are narrow and curved through standing anions without wide free space, suggested by the XRD analysis and computational methods  [102,103,129] . The micrometre Li diffusion measured by the PFG-NMR must be related to the collision +   and diffraction through narrow Li channels. (3) It is not certain that all +   the migrating Li ions behave in a uniform manner within a limited space.

In the present stage, an important question remains, i.e., what is a proper D Li  value to compare with  σ . In inorganic solid electrolytes, many values for  D Li  are obtained depending on  Δ  and  g . As the  Δ  becomes longer, the reduction of  g -dependence occurs. Then, the values of  D Li  obtained with long  Δ  are used to relate with  σ  in  Figs. 34 and 35 . The diffusion con- stant obtained by the PFG-NMR is a measure of transfer for species marked by the first PFG and detected for the encoded species by the second PFG after  Δ . During the period  Δ , the processes of Li +   diffusion are unknown. On the other hand,  σ  is defined as ion velocity multiplied by carrier number. It is not certain that the velocity is a constant value from an electrode to the opposite one in solid electrolytes and also the same ions move between the electrodes  [139] .  

7 NMR people observed the   Li and anion-related spectra of solid inor- ganic electrolytes and measured the atomic-level information derived from +   relaxation times. Micrometre-space Li diffusion data are not many. Also, solid materials differ in morphology owing to the preparation methods and the treatments after the preparation. Some samples change in morphol- ogy depending on time which were detectable by diffusion measurements +   with puzzling questions. At present, PFG-NMR Li diffusion data are not enough and the concept will be improved with the increase of data and also numbers of research groups having various research backgrounds. Very recently, Hayamizu et al. reported the Li-diffusion in single crystal and crushed samples of LLZO-Ta  [140]  and found the collision-diffraction pat- terns are related to grain-boundaries, because of the disappearance for the diffraction patterns in a fine single crystal. The understanding of anomalous Li diffusion in inorganic solid electrolytes will be enhanced by further studies.  

# 6. Conclusion  

In this chapter, ion diffusion was shown to be observed by two PFG pulse sequences. Actually, numerous diffusion pulse sequences have been proposed with the aims of obtaining better results, mainly to compensate for the lack of perfectness of the hardware in the PFG probe and its current amplifier; for example, the prolonged eddy current effects, residual background DC bias current, distorted PFG shape, convection effects. When we measure PFG-NMR diffusion, we must determine the proper pulse sequence and measuring conditions for the sample to be measured. To date, ion diffusion data in electrolytes were collected to understand diffusion mechanisms related to electrolyte structures of organic solution electrolytes, ionic liquids and inorganic solid electrolytes. In the liquid elec- trolytes, the SE, and NE relations are important for understanding the mutual relationships of  D ,  η  and  σ . For the concentrated viscous liquid elec- trolytes, further studies to introduce new concepts are necessary. Anomalous +   Li diffusion phenomena have been observed for inorganic solid electrolytes such as  Δ - and  g -dependences and collision-diffraction in narrow Li chan- nels. Although these experimental findings are not fully understood at pre- sent, they are valuable for explaining fast ionic conductivity in solid materials. In the future, a new class of functional materials will be introduced to the scientific world. If the target materials include NMR-applicable and friendly nuclei, the PFG-NMR will become one of the non-trivial tech- niques for investigating the migration of ions and molecules and will meet with curious diffusion phenomena as was found  g -dependent diffusion in inorganic solid electrolytes, which may be fully new. The PFG-NMR will increase in importance in the field of material science.
