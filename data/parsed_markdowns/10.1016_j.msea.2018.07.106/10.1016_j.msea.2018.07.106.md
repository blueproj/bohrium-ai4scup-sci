# The temperature dependence of high-temperature strength and  deformation mechanism in a single crystal CoNi-base superalloy  

Zhongding Fan a , Chenchong Wang b , Chi Zhang a\* , Yunhe Yu a , Hao Chen a , Zhigang Yang a Key Laboratory of Advanced Materials of Ministry of Education, School of Materials Science and  Engineering, Tsinghua University, Beijing 100084, China   b State key laboratory of rolling and automation, School of Materials Science and Engineering,  Northeastern University, Shenyang 110819, China  \* Corresponding author. chizhang@tsinghua.edu.cn  

# Abstract  

The thermal stability and high-temperature strength of single crystal CoNi-base  superalloys, Co-30Ni-10Al-7W (at.%) and Co-30Ni-10Al-7W-2Ta-3Ti (at.%),  containing  𝜸 ’(L1 2 ) phase have been investigated. The investigated CoNi-base  superalloy with alloying additions of Ti and Ta exhibits excellent  𝜸 ’ solvus temperature,  1236 ℃ , and high-temperature strength, 622MPa at 900 ℃ , compared to reported  𝜸 ’-strengthening Co-base superalloys. Detailed analyses of the deformation  mechanisms for the investigated alloys were carried out by using the transmission  electron microscopy (TEM) characterization. Two different transitions of deformation  mechanisms for the  𝜸 ’ phase, from superpartial dislocation shearing to dislocation  bypassing and to stacking fault shear, occurred in the investigated alloys as the  deformation temperature increased above the peak temperatures. Meanwhile, the  high-temperature strength of the investigated alloys largely decreased as the transitions  of deformation mechanism took place. The influence of alloying effects on energies of  faults and the relationship between high-temperature strength and deformation  mechanisms were discussed.  

Keywords:  Cobalt-base superalloy; Transmission electron microscopy; Compression  test; Dislocation; Stacking fault  

# 1.   Introduction  

For the past decades,  i ncreasing the thermal efficiency of gas turbine engines was  always a primary issue for aerospace and power generation industries. The  development however was limited by the inalterable solidus temperature of Ni-base  superalloys, widely used in the hottest part of a turbine engine. It is thus essential to  develop a new class of structural materials able to withstand higher operating  temperatures than Ni-base superalloys are. Recently, Sato et al.[1] have discovered a  new Co-base superalloy strengthened by a stable ternary  𝜸 ’-Co 3 (Al,W) phase (L1 2   structure) which was embedded coherently in  𝜸 -Co solid-solution matrix. This  Co-Al-W superalloy possesses a  𝜸  / 𝜸 ’ two-phase microstructure which is  morphologically similar to that of Ni-base superalloys. Besides the superior resistance  to hot corrosion, oxidation, and wear of Co-base superalloys, cobalt inherently  possesses a higher melting point than Ni does[2, 3]. Therefore, the Co-Al-W superalloy  has been considered as a promising high-temperature material which might outperform  Ni-base superalloys.  

However, the inferior high-temperature strength disabled the Co-Al-W  superalloys from being put into practical applications. It is partly owing to the poor  high-temperature stability of the  𝜸 ’-Co 3 (Al,W) phase, which tends to decompose into  

other thermodynamic ally stable phases such as  𝟀- Co 3 W (D0 19 )[4-6]. In order to  optimize the high-temperature stability of the  𝜸 ’-Co 3 (Al,W) phase, different alloying  effects on high-temperature mechanical properties of Co-Al-W superalloys have been  investigated[7-12]. Shinagawa et al.[7] found that adding Ni could enlarge the  𝜸 / 𝜸 ’  two-phase region in the quaternary Co-Ni-Al-W phase diagram at 900 ℃  and increase  the  𝜸 ’ solvus temperature. Hence a suitable amount of Ni additions in Co-Al-W  superalloys would be beneficial to the high-temperature stability of the  𝜸 ’-Co 3 (Al,W)  phase and the high-temperature strength of CoNi-base superalloys would also be  enhanced compared to the ternary Co-Al-W superalloys.  

In addition, Ta has been reported as an effective alloying element which enhances  the  𝜸 ’ solvus temperature and the high-temperature mechanical properties of Co-Al-W  superalloys[13-15]. It is noted that Ta addition increases the superlattice intrinsic  stacking fault (SISF) energy of the  𝜸 ’ phase and which improves the resistance of the  𝜸 ’  phase to initial shearing of a/3<112> dislocations[16]. Furthermore, experimental  results from Xue et al. [9] showed that simultaneously alloying Ta and Ti with the  ternary Co-Al-W superalloy would considerably increase  𝜸 ’ solvus temperature to  1157 ℃  and the microstructural stability could be maintained throughout 1000 hours  aging at 900 ℃ .  

Although research related to influences of alloying elements on high-temperature  mechanical properties of Co-Al-W superalloys is abundant [8, 13, 14, 17-22], relatively  limited research has discussed about the complicated deformation mechanisms [14, 23,  24]. In this study, the authors chose to stabilize the  𝜸 ’ phase by alloying Ni, Ta, and Ti  into a ternary Co-Al-W superalloy and focused on exploring the high-temperature  strength and the high-temperature deformation mechanisms of the investigated  CoNi-base superalloys.  

# 2.   Experimental methods  

The measured composition of investigated alloys are listed in  Table 1 . The X-ray  fluorescence (XRF) was used to measure the chemical composition. Sample  preparations began with ingot casting by a vacuum induction melting (VIM) system.  For the single-crystal preparation, as-cast ingots were directionally solidified into  cylindrical bars (16mm in diameter and 220 mm in length) in a Bridgeman furnace. A  withdrawal rate of 6mm/min was utilized and the withdrawal direction was parallel to  [001] crystal orientation. Deviations were within 10 °  which was determined by the  TEM. The investigated alloys were homogenized at 1250 ℃  for 24 hours and aged at  900 ℃  for 168 hours in air, as listed in Table 2.  

In order to determine the  𝜸 ’ solvus temperature, the differential thermal analysis  (DTA) was conducted on cylindrical specimens with mass between 200 to 250mg using  a NETZSCH STA 449F3 and the transformation temperatures were determined under a  heating rate of 5K/min. Argon gas was used as the experimental atmosphere to prevent  the oxidation of specimens. The  𝜸’  volume fraction was quantitatively determined by  measuring the area fraction of the  𝜸 ’ phase on the SEM images using ImageJ  software.  

The microstructural characterization of as-aged specimens were carried out by the  scanning electron microscope (SEM) using a Zeiss GeminiSEM500. For the SEM  characterization, specimens were transversely cut from single-crystal bars and were  grinded and polished using standard metallography methods. Nitric acid  

hydrochloride was used as the etching agent.  

A Jeol Jem2100 transmission electron microscope (TEM) was used to observe and  identify crystal defects in as-deformed specimens. For the TEM characterization, thin  foils were cut from the transverse section of specimens. The final thinning was carried  out by a twin-jet electro polishing system operated at around 233K using a voltage of  17-20V and a current of 25-30mA in a 92.5% ethanol – 7.5% perchloric acid  

electrolyte.  

For high-temperature compression tests, a thermal-mechanical simulator Gleeble  1500D was utilized. Compressive specimens with geometries measuring 5mm in  diameter and 10mm in length. The compressive direction was along [001] crystal  

orientation and with a strain rate equal to 10 -4  s -1  

# 3.   Results and discussion  

# 3.1 As-aged microstructural characterization  

Fig. 1  shows the as-aged microstructures of investigated alloys, CoNi-1 and  CoNi-2. From the observation of electron microscopy images,  𝜸 / 𝜸 ’ two-phase  microstructure of both CoNi-1 and CoNi-2 maintained stable throughout the 168  hours aging and both alloys turned out to possess near-cuboidal  𝜸 ’ precipitates. The  diffraction patterns in  Fig. 1 (c) and (d) confirm the superlattice structure of the L1 2   𝜸 ’  phase to be present in the microstructure. The  𝜸 ’ volume fractions of CoNi-1 and  CoNi-2 were measured by ImageJ software to be 67% and 92%, respectively. Since the  binding energy of the  𝜸 ’-Co 3 (Al,W) phase in the Ta - substituted system   was much  lower than that of the ternary Co-Al-W system, the addition of Ta can effectively  stabilize the  𝜸 ’phase[25, 26]. Ni and Ti were also reported to be able to stabilize the  𝜸 ’phase in Co-Al-W alloys and increase the  𝜸 ’ volume fraction[7, 27, 28]. Hence a  large amount of the  𝜸 ’ phase was observed to form in the microstructure of the CoNi-2  alloy.  

# 3.2  𝜸 ’ solvus temperature  

𝜸 ’ solvus temperature is a crucial factor utilized to evaluate the high-temperature  

mechanical properties of  𝜸 ’-strengthening superalloys.  Fig. 2 (a) reveals the DTA  results of the investigated alloys in the present study. The  𝜸 ’ solvus temperatures for  CoNi-1 and CoNi-2 were measured to be 1049 ℃  and 1236 ℃ , respectively.   A  comparison between the results from the present study and the previous studies[7, 10,  17] was demonstrated in  Fig. 2 (b). Observed from the  𝜸 ’ solvus temperatures of the  ternary 9Al-9W alloy[7], the 7Al-7W-4Ti-2Ta[17] alloy, CoNi-1, and CoNi-2, the  addition of Ni can certainly increase the thermal stability of the  𝜸 ’-Co 3 (Al,W) phase  in Co-Al-W superalloys. It is strongly probable that the face-centered atomic position  of a  𝜸 ’(L1 2 ) unit cell thermodynamic ally prefers to be occupied by a Ni atom rather  than a Co atom. The 9Al-8W alloy with 2at.% additions of Ta, Ti, and Nb exhibits  higher  𝜸 ’ solvus temperatures than that of CoNi-1 and thus that Ta, Ti, and Nb are  strong  𝜸 ’ stabilizers compared to Ni. Furthermore, the CoNi-2 alloy possesses a  relatively high  𝜸 ’ solvus temperature compared to other  𝜸 ’-containing Co-base alloys  from the previous studies[7, 10, 17]. That is to say, Ni, Ti, and Ta can independently  stabilize the  𝜸 ’-Co 3 (Al,W) phase without deleteriously interactive effects.  

# 3.3  Temperature dependence of 0.2% flow stress  

High-temperature compressive tests were conducted on the investigated alloys  with a strain rate 10 -4  s -1 . The measured results of 0.2% flow stresses were plotted in Fig.  3 as a function of temperature. The 0.2% flow stress of CoNi-1 alloy, as shown in Fig. 3,  decreases as the increasing temperature within the range between 25 ℃  to 300 ℃  and  become stable between 300 ℃  to 600 ℃ . As the deformation temperature exceeds 600 ℃ ,  the yield stress anomaly (YSA) begins to occur until reaching the peak temperature  around 700 ℃ . The 0.2% flow stress subsequently drops when the temperature  increases above the peak temperature. On the other hand, the 0.2% flow stress of the  CoNi-2 alloy maintains stable as the increasing temperature from 25 ℃  to 500 ℃  and the  YSA starts to occur from 500 ℃  to the peak temperature, 900 ℃ . The peak 0.2% flow  stresses of CoNi-1 and CoNi-2 are 485MPa and 622MPa at 700 ℃  and 900 ℃  (peak  temperatures), respectively.  

Fig. 3 also shows 0.2% flow stresses of a traditional Co-base superalloy FSX-414  [3], a 1st generation Ni-base superalloy 713LC[3], a quaternary Co-Al-W-Ta alloy  2Ta[14] for comparison. The investigated alloys, CoNi-1 and CoNi-2, exhibits superior  high-temperature strength relative to FSX-414 from 500 ℃  their peak temperatures and  which could be mainly owing to the YSA of the  𝜸 ’ phase. However, the effect of YSA,  resulting from thermally-activated cross-slips, decreases as the decreasing  temperature that CoNi-2 exhibits lower 0.2% flow stress than FSX-414 at  temperatures between 25 ℃  and 500 ℃.  It is noted that  𝜸 ’ precipitates with fine particle  sizes (\~7.5nm in diameter) have been considered as the main strengthening agent at low  temperatures in superalloys[29]. Due to the high  𝜸 ’ volume fraction of CoNi-2, the  

amount of  𝜸  channel was insufficient for secondary  𝜸 ’ precipitates with fine sizes to  form during the cooling stage of the aging treatment. On the other hand, the CoNi-1  alloy possesses not only a larger amount of secondary  𝜸 ’ precipitates but primary  𝜸 ’  precipitates with finer particle sizes compared to the CoNi-2 alloy. The 0.2% flow  stress of CoNi-1 is therefore higher than that of CoNi-2 at temperatures lower than  500 ℃ .  

Fig. 3 indicates that the 2Ta alloy exhibits greater 0.2% flow stresses than CoNi-1  does at all temperatures, whereas CoNi-2 exhibits an even higher peak 0.2% flow stress  at 900 ℃  compared to 2Ta and CoNi-1 .  It is likely that among the additions of Ni, Ta,  and Ti, Ta is the most effective alloying element to improve the strength of Co-Al-W  alloys. Nevertheless, for CoNi-2, alloying additions of Ni, Ta, and Ti contribute a  superior thermal stability and solid-solution strengthening to the  𝜸 ’ phase. As the result,  the high-temperature strength and the peak temperature have been further increased.  

It seems reasonable to consider CoNi-2 as a  𝜸 ’-(Co,Ni) 3 (Al,W) single crystal  containing Ta and Ti. Fig. 3 reveals a comparison of temperature dependence of 0.2%  flow stress between CoNi-2, the L1 2  compound Ni 3 (Al,W) and the L1 2  compound  Co 3 (Al,W). The onset temperature of yield stress anomaly for CoNi-2 is around 200 ℃ lower than that of Co 3 (Al,W), which greatly enhances the effect of YSA and the peak  0.2% flow stress. The high onset temperature of YSA for Co 3 (Al,W) is owing to the  

low complex stacking fault (CSF) energy which makes the driving force for cube  cross-slips become higher [30]. It is strongly probable that additions of Ni, Ti, and Ta  increase the CSF energy of the  𝜸 ’-Co 3 (Al,W) phase. Combining the low onset  temperature of YSA with the high  𝜸 ’ solvus temperature in CoNi-2, the peak 0.2% flow  stress is improved to a value comparable to the L1 2  compound Ni 3 (Al,W).  

# 3.4  Transmission electron microscopy analysis  

TEM characterizations were conducted on as-deformed specimens, CoNi-1 and  CoNi-2, to investigate the substructure of dislocations and planar defects by using  various two-beam conditions.  

# 3.4.1 Deformation mechanism for CoNi-1  

TEM microstructural observations on the as-deformed CoNi-1 specimens are  shown in  Fig. 4 . It can be seem from the lower part of  Fig. 4 (a) that a great amount of  dislocations were shearing in  𝜸  matrix at 700 ℃ , which was near the peak temperature.  Most of the dislocations were curved in 90°, pointed by an arrow in  Fig. 4 (a), and  exhibited dislocation cross-slips in  𝜸  matrix. In addition, a fault loop, pointed by a  double arrow in  Fig. 4 (a), was observed in the microstructure. Based on the TEM  observations, dislocations preferred to shear in  𝜸  matrix when the compressive  

temperature was below the peak temperature. Hence most of  𝜸 ’ precipitates were  dislocation-free after 700 ℃  compression test. Deformed  𝜸 ’ precipitates nevertheless  could still be observed in the microstructure after 700 ℃  compression. A pair of  superpartial dislocations was shearing inside a  𝜸 ’ precipitate, as shown in Fig. 4(b). To confirm the Burgers vector of superpartial dislocations shearing in the  𝜸 ’ phase, the  

𝒈∙𝐛   analysis was carried out by using various two-beam conditions on a conventional  TEM, as shown in  Fig. 5 . The investigated alloy was the as-deformed CoNi-1 and the  deformation temperature was 700 ℃.  It can be seem that the contrast of the enclosed  dislocations diminishes when the TEM observations were under  𝒈= (02 ̅0)  and  

𝒈= (13 ̅1)  reflections. On the contrary, the enclosed dislocations can be clearly  observed when the  𝒈= (200)  and  𝒈= (131 ̅)  reflections were used. Utilizing the  𝒈∙𝐛= 𝟎   invisibility criteria, the Burgers vectors of the enclosed dislocations can be  determined to be  𝑎/2[101 ̅] . Furthermore, it can also be deduced that the APB  separated by the enclosed dislocations is lying on {111} slip planes.  

As the deformation temperature raised to 900 ℃ , which was higher than the peak  temperature,  𝜸’  precipitates instead of the  𝜸  matrix were full of dislocations after  

compression, as shown in  Fig. 4 (c) and (d). Based on the observations of shape and  size on microscopy images, the morphology of  𝜸’  precipitates maintained unchanged  throughout the deformation. It could be determined that  𝜸’  precipitates were deformed  by dislocation bypassing during the compression tests at 900 ℃ . That is to say, the  deformation mechanism of the CoNi-1 alloy changes from dislocation shearing in  𝜸   channels to dislocation bypassing surrounding  𝜸’  precipitates when the compressive  temperature increases above the peak temperature.   This deformation mechanism is  consistent with the ternary Co-Al-W alloy from the previous study[14]. Furthermore,  comparing the observed deformation mechanism to the results of compression tests in  

Fig. 3 , a/2<110> unpaired dislocations bypassing decreases the flow strength of the  CoNi-1 alloy. The decreasing strength could be owing to the lack of thermally-activated  cross-slips and thus the pinning effect of superpartial dislocations on {100} planes was  diminished.  

# 3.4.2 Deformation mechanism for CoNi-2  

Fig. 6  reveals TEM microstructural characterizations of the as-deformed CoNi-2  alloys at different temperatures. Observed from  Fig. 6 (a), dislocations with preferred  orientations were slightly curved on their {111} slip planes. In addition, several pairs of  superpartial dislocations were present in an as-deformed CoNi-2 specimen at 900 ℃  

which was near the peak temperature. The Burgers vectors of dislocations were  determined to be a/2<110> by the  g∙ b  analysis. It can be said   that   the deformation of  CoNi-2 occurs by superpartial dislocation shearing in  𝜸 ’ phases and then results in the  formation of APB between paired a/2<110> superpartial dislocations. Based on the  microstructural and diffraction characteristics on TEM images taken under different  reflections, the dislocation segments pointed by the double arrow were observed to  cross-slip from an octahedral plane onto a cube plane and therefore formed a  

Kear-Wilsdorf (KW) lock in the microstructure, as shown in  Fig. 6 (b). Accordingly,  KW locks immobilized several superpartial dislocations during the compression tests,  which maintained the effect of yield stress anomaly even as the deformation  temperature increased up to 900 ℃ . It can be concluded that the main deformation  mechanism of the CoNi-2 alloy at temperatures below the peak temperature is  superpartial dislocation shearing in  𝜸 ’ phases.  

A large amount of stacking faults began to form in  𝜸 ’ phases, as shown in  Fig. 6 (c),  when   the deformation temperature increased to 1000 ℃ . The dominant deformation  mechanism of the  𝜸 ’ phase in CoNi-2 changed from a/2<110> superpartial dislocation  shearing to a/3<112> partial dislocations shearing[32]. In comparison with the as-aged  microstructure of CoNi-2, the volume fraction and the morphology of the  𝜸 ’ phase in  the as-deformed CoNi-2 alloy maintained unchanged throughout the 1000 ℃  

compression and thus the  𝜸 ’ phase could be considered to possess a fine thermal  stability. Comparing the above TEM microstructural observations to the compressive  results of CoNi-2 alloys shown in  Fig. 3 , it can be said that the formation of stacking  faults greatly reduces the effect of YSA and thus results in decreased 0.2% flow stress.  It seems likely that the stacking fault energy decreases as the increasing temperature.  However, no stacking fault could be found in the microstructure of CoNi-1 even as the  deformation temperature increased to 900 ℃  which was around 200 ℃  higher than its  peak temperature. The influential factors cannot just include temperature but also  alloying elements. Based on a previous high-resolution scanning transmission electron  microscopy (HRSTEM) observation from Titus et al.[33] on as-crept specimens of Co-  and CoNi-base alloys, an elemental segregation of Ta atoms near superlattice intrinsic  stacking faults (SISF) was present. Besides, a creep-induced SISF was observed to  possess a local D0 19  structure. It is thus that a local phase transformation from L1 2 -  𝜸 ’  phase to D0 19  phase occurs when the creep-induced SISF forms in the microstructure.  As a matter of fact, a first principle calculation has proved that the Ta-containing D0 19   phase exhibits a significantly lower SISF energy compared to the  𝜸 ’ phase[33].  Assuming this discovery could explain the appearance of stacking fault in CoNi-2 at  elevated temperatures in the present study, it can be said that elevated temperatures give  alloying elements higher diffusivities and enable them to diffuse to their preferred  

atomic positions. Consequently, the required diffusivity of Ta for stacking fault  formations is obtained as the temperature increases to 1000 ℃.  That is to say, the  diffusivity of Ta at 1000 ℃  is high enough to complete the segregation and the local  phase transformation within the period of compressive deformation under the strain  rate, 10 -4  s -1 .  This hypothesis should be further confirmed by experimentally measuring  the local chemical composition near stacking faults in CoNi-2.  

Fig. 7  schematically reveals the major deformation mechanism of  𝜸 ’ phases in  CoNi-1 and CoNi-2 under different deformation temperatures. At temperatures below  peak temperatures, paired a/2<110> superpartial dislocations were observed to shear in  𝜸 ’ phases and the 0.2 flow stress of investigated alloys, meanwhile, increases as the  increasing temperature. As the deformation temperature increased above the peak  temperatures, unpaired a/2<110> dislocation bypassing and a/3<112> partial  dislocation shearing in  𝜸 ’ phase began to dominate the deformation mechanisms of  CoNi-1 and CoNi-2, respectively. The above transitions of deformation mechanisms  inhibit the yield stress anomaly and decrease the 0.2% flow stress of investigated  alloys. It is worth mentioning that the deformation mechanism of CoNi-2 is consistent  with that of a Co-Al-W-Ta alloy from the previous study[14]. Hence the alloying  additions of 2at.%Ti and 30at.%Ni are proved not to influence the competitive  relationship between the anti-phase boundary energy (APBE) and the stacking fault  

energy (SFE) in Co-Al-W superalloys. In order to optimized the alloy design of  Co-Al-W superalloy, the interactive relationships between the deformation  mechanisms, elemental partitioning behaviors in the  𝜸/𝜸’  microstructure and the  energies of faults should be further clarified.  

# Conclusions  

The  𝜸 ’ thermal stability, high-temperature strength, and the deformation  mechanisms of CoNi-1 and CoNi-2 have been investigated. The relationships between  the above factors were analyzed. The following conclusions are drawn from this study: 

 1.   Additions of Ti and Ta to a quaternary Co-Ni-Al-W alloy effectively improve the  thermal stability of the  𝜸’-( Co,Ni) 3 (Al,W)   phase and the high-temperature strength,  which shows similar alloying effects known from other Co-Al-W superalloys.  

 2.   The onset temperature of YSA for the CoNi-2 alloy, 500 ℃,  was measured to be  much lower than that for the  𝜸’- Co 3 (Al,W) alloy, 750 ℃ . The CSF energy of  CoNi - 2 was likely to be increased by additions of Ni, Ta, and Ti, which leads to  the superior flow strength at 900 ℃ .  

3.   Transitions of deformation mechanism occurred in both investigated alloys as the  deformation temperature increased above the peak temperatures. Unpaired  

a/2<110> dislocation bypassing and a/3<112> partial dislocation shear diminish the  formation of thermally-activated dislocation cross-slips and thus result in a drop of  flow strength.
