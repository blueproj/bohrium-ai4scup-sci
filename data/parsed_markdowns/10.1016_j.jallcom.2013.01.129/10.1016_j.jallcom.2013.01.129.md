The effects of heat treatment for recovering microstructure of Ni-based single crystal superalloy with added C and Hf after thermal exposure have been evaluated. The optimum heat treatment conditions were drawn from the microstructure analysis, after thermal exposure at 982   C for 3000 h and rejuvena- tion treatment. The solvus and solidus temperatures of this alloy were 1305 and 1315   C respectively, and most of Ta and Ti-rich carbide dissolved after performing  c 0   solid solution treatment at 1305–1310   C. The fraction of microporosity and eutectic phase showed a tendency of increase at solvus temperature. Also, an uphill diffusion of Al, Cr, Ta and Re was observed in eutectic phase at the solvus temperature, and the change of the composition of Ta was relatively higher than other elements. The gradient of heating curves in DSC analysis showed its sharp change after  c 0   solid solution treatment at the temperature below solvus temperature of 1305   C because of the existence of un-dissolved coarse  c 0   in the interdendritic region.

# 1. Introduction  

CMSX-4 is a representative of commercialized single crystal superalloy and is being used as hot section components of gas tur- bine blades and vanes  [6] . SX MK4HC has been also applied to gas turbines of power plants, and the small angle grain boundary rein- forcement elements such as C, B and Hf have been added to CMSX- 4  [7] .  

The development of Ni-based superalloy has led to the single crystal superalloy as its process went through from equiaxed crys- tal structure to columnar crystal structure. Single crystal superal- loy has been especially known to have excellent oxidation and corrosion resistance as well as superb creep and fatigue character- istic. Because of such features, the signal crystal superalloy has been applied to hot section components such as blades and vanes in gas turbines for improving their efﬁciency.  

It is extremely challenging to produce defect-free gas turbine blades for power plants because it is relative bigger and more com- plex than the ones used in aircrafts. For CMSX-4 without grain boundary reinforcement element, the low cycle fatigue lifetime in vertical  h 001 i  direction is superior. However, if small angle grain boundaries with an angle of 7    8   are existed, the low cycle fati- gue lifetime in horizontal  h 010 i  direction decreases sharply com- pared to the vertical direction  [7] . To resolve such issue, grain boundary reinforcement elements have been added to SX MK4HC so that the low cycle fatigue lifetime in the horizontal direction is improved by forming carbide at small angle grain boundaries.  

The single crystal superalloy has evolved continuously since 1980s. For the ﬁrst generation of single crystal superalloy such as PWA1480, Rene N4 and SRR99, the contents of Al, Ti, and Ta which consist of  c 0   phase increased, while intergranular reinforcing ele- ments C and B, which have been added to the existing columnar crystal structure, were removed. The second generation single crystal superalloys such as PWA1484, CMSX-4 and ReneN5 is char- acterized by most of them containing 3 wt.% of Re, while that of the third generation such as CMSX-10 and Rene N6 contains Re up to 6 wt.%  [1–3] . General trend of the development of the single crystal superalloy has been decreasing the contents of Cr and increasing the contents of Al and Re while maintaining the contents of Ti and Mo at the same level compared to the existing alloys. The fourth generation alloy such as MC-NG, EPM-102 and TMS-162 containing Ru has been under development since 2000  [4,5] .  

After using hot section components for a certain period of oper- ating, a decision regarding their recycle is made based on inspec- tions. To reclaim mechanical characteristics, rejuvenation heat treatment is performed through the repair. Rejuvenation heat treatment generally consists of four stages; stress relief treatment, c 0   solid solution treatment, precipitation treatment, and aging heat treatment. Here, because of an existence of segregation generated during solidiﬁcation process, it is required to set proper solution treatment temperature region. It is also required to prevent recrys- tallization on the surface of the components because the recrystal- lization causes severe degradation to mechanical characteristics and corrosion resistance of hot section components  [7] . Therefore, the main purpose of the rejuvenation heat treatment can be de- ﬁned as recovering the mechanical characteristics as close to their original forms as possible without incurring the recrystallization.  

This research acquired an optimum rejuvenation treatment condition for the single crystal superalloy, which is currently being applied to hot section components of gas turbines, through an observation of changes in microstructure during thermal exposure as well as in various  c 0   solid solution treatment conditions.  

# 2. Experimental procedure  

The single crystal superalloy used in this research was a modiﬁed CMSX-4 by addition of C and Hf. Its chemical composition in  Table 1  was obtained from hot sec- tion components of gas turbines for power plants. Thermal exposure was performed at 982   C for 3000 h using heat treatment furnace (UAF 15/27, Lenton Thermal De- signs), and vacuum heat treatment furnace (Super VIII, Centorr Vacuum Industries, Inc.) was used for rejuvenation treatment, and during the heat treatment, the vac- uum level was maintained below 1.0    10  4   torr. The rejuvenation treatment pro- cess consists of three heat treatment conditions;  c 0   solid solution treatment,  c 0 precipitation treatment, and aging heat treatment. The  c 0   solid solution treatment was performed for 7 h at 8 different temperatures of 1290, 1295, 1300, 1305, 1310, 1315, 1320 and 1335   C in this research. Afterwards,  c 0   precipitation heat treatment and aging heat treatment were performed for 4 h at 1140   C and for 16 h at 870   C respectively. After each heat treatment, argon gas cooling was applied.  

For microstructural analysis, optical microscope (DM15000M, Leica) and scan- ning electron microscope (JSM-7001F, JEOL) were used to examine the surface per- pendicular to the single crystal growth direction [001]. Microporosity and eutectic phase was measured by using Image Analyzer (Leica MW). Also, contents of Al, Ti, Cr, Co, Ni, Ta, W and Re in the areas of dendrite core, interdendritic region and eu- tectic phase were analyzed by using EDS (Energy Dispersive Spectroscopy) of scan- ning electron microscope. The chemical composition of eutectic phase was determined from an area of 100  l m 2   at the center region of eutectic phase. For mea- surement of liquidus, solidus and solvus temperatures, Differential Scanning Calo- rimetry (DSC404C, Netzsch) was used by increasing the temperature from 1000 to 1450   C with a rate of 10   C per minute. The sample used in this study was a thin plate with a dimension of 3 mm  3 mm  0.5 mm.

It was also conﬁrmed that rod-like phases with high con- tents of Cr, Co, W and Re were formed in the primary and second- ary dendrite arms, as shown in  Fig. 1  (c). Various solution heat treatments were performed for 7 h at eight different temperatures in a range within 1290–1335   C followed by precipitation and aging treat- ments. As shown in  Fig. 3 a, the  c 0   precipitates dissolved in the den- drite core and secondary dendrite arm region when solid solution treatment was performed at 1290   C, but it was not happened in the interdendritic region. Therefore, 1305   C becomes the solvus temperature of this alloy ( Fig. 3 d).  c 0   precipitates dis- solved in all regions for the specimen treated at 1310   C as well, and it was conﬁrmed that most of the carbide also dissolved at only 1305 and 1310   C ( Fig. 4 b–d). It was observed that incipient melt- ing in the interdendritic region occurred when the solid solution treatment was performed at 1315   C. Therefore, 1315   C is the sol- idus temperature of this alloy ( Fig. 3 f). In the case of  c 0   solid solu- tion treatment temperature higher than 1315   C, the amount of microporosity along with eutectic phase increases with tempera- ture. This is because of formation of microporosity caused by dif- ferent density between liquid phase and solid phase as a result of the re-solidiﬁcation of the previously-melted region  [10] . Fig. 4 e shows re-solidiﬁcation region and microporosity sur- rounded with eutectic phase by incipient melting. The fraction of microporosity and eutectic phase caused by melting increased sig- niﬁcantly as the  c 0   solid solution treatment temperature increased to 1320 and 1335   C ( Fig. 3 g and h). Since the mechanical charac- teristics degrade because of microporosity and eutectic phase, the occurrence of incipient melting should be avoided during the solu- tion heat treatment.  

Fig. 5  shows the area fraction of eutectic phase and microporos- ity with respect to various temperatures of solution heat treat- ment. The fraction of eutectic phase decreased as the  c 0   solid solution treatment increased to 1315   C except 1305   C, and then increased substantially at temperatures higher than 1320   C as shown in  Fig. 5 a. The continuous decrease of eutectic phase came from that the diffusion rate of alloying elements increased along with the increase of solution treatment temperature and the in- crease of eutectic phase at 1320   C is caused by the formation of eutectic phase by incipient melting. As shown in the  Fig. 5 b, the area fraction of microporosity also decreased as the  c 0   solid solu- tion treatment increased to 1315   C except 1305 and 1310   C, and then increased sharply at 1320   C because of the incipient melting. The changes of area fraction in eutectic phase and micro- porosity corresponding to the  c 0   solid solution treatment tempera- ture were similar, especially the increase of the eutectic phase and microporosity at the solvus temperature of 1305   C. Based on the microstructural analysis that was previously conducted, such change is caused by the carbide dissolution at the solvus tempera- ture. As the solid solution treatment temperature increased from 1305 to 1320   C the contents of Ta and Al in the eutectic phase de- creased gradually and the contents of Re and Cr increased again. The increased segregation at the solvus temperature of 1305   C can be considered as the uphill diffusion effect. The chemical potential of each element is affected by the afﬁnity acting between elements, and uphill diffusion has been observed in single crystal superalloy that contains multiple elements  [21–24] . # 4. Conclusion  

1. The rafting of  c / c 0   and TCP formation were observed when Ni- based single crystal superalloy was thermally exposed at 982   C for 3000 h. The coarsening directions of  c / c 0   rafts in the secondary dendrite arm was perpendicular to the growth direc- tion of each secondary dendrite arm, while  l  phase with high contents of Cr, Co, W and Re elements was formed in the den- drite arm.  

2. The solvus temperature of the Ni-based single crystal superal- loy with added C and Hf was 1305   C, and most of Ta and Ti-rich carbides dissolved at this temperature. The solidus temperature of this alloy was 1315   C and eutectic phase was formed in the interdendritic region due to the incipient melting.

 3. The fraction of eutectic phase and microporosity decreased as the  c 0   solid solution treatment temperature increased and increased at 1305   C. This is due to the effects of changes in con- centration of Ta and Ti and density in the interdendritic region caused by carbide dissolution.

 4. The gradient of DSC heating curve changed sharply when the  c 0 solid solution treatment was performed at a temperature lower than solvus temperature. This is because the  c 0   in the interden- dritic region was not completely dissolved and the sizes of  c 0   in this region are coarse.

 5. The uphill diffusion of Al, Cr, Ta and Re in the eutectic phase was observed during the  c 0   solid solution treatment at the solvus temperature of 1305   C, and the changes of Ta concentration were bigger than other elements. This is due to the dissolution of Ta-rich carbide at the solvus temperature.
