# A B S T R A C T  

Ni-rich nickel-cobalt-manganese layered oxides have been widely used as advanced cathode materials; however,  they exhibit poor thermal stability at elevated temperatures. Therefore, in this study, we synthesized lithium  tetrafluoro(fluoromalonato)phosphate (LFMP) as a dual-functional electrolyte additive to improve the thermal  stability of LiNi 0.8 Co 0.1 Mn 0.1 O 2  (NCM811) cathode and graphite anode materials. The LFMP additive imparts  excellent cyclability, storage performance, and mitigated gas evolution of NCM811/graphite cells at 60 ◦ C  compared to its boron analog, lithium difluoro(fluoromalonato)borate (LFMB). The vast advantages of the LFMP  additive can be attributed to the following two-fold origin: (i) On the NCM811 cathode, LFMP derives a cathode  electrolyte interphase (CEI) that suppresses electrolyte decomposition and gas evolution more effectively than  LFMB; (ii) On the graphite anode, LFMP induces a LiF-rich solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) that is more resistant  to attack by phosphorus pentafluoride (PF 5 ) than an LFMB-induced organic-rich SEI. Our first-principles cal­ culations corroborate that the CEI and SEI layers derived by LFMP are superior to those of LFMB owing to the  strong binding with a superoxide radical (O ▪ 2 − ) and weak binding with PF 5  and favorable LiF formation,  respectively. Considering its advantages, LFMP is an outstanding electrolyte additive for thermally stable  NCM811/graphite batteries. This study opens a new avenue for using malo nato phosphates as a new class of  electrolyte additives for enhanced surface stability of Ni-rich cathodes and graphite anodes.

# 1. Introduction  

Li-ion batteries (LIBs) have been widely used in diverse applications,  including electric vehicles and energy storage systems. Because the en­ ergy density of LIBs is highly dependent on cathode materials, Ni-rich  cathode materials such as LiNi 0.8 Co 0.1 Mn 0.1 O 2  (NCM811) have attrac­ ted considerable attention owing to their high specific capacities and  energy densities  [1] . However, the structural degradation of Ni-rich  NCM cathodes at elevated temperatures remains a critical drawback  that needs to be urgently addressed [ 2 , 3 ]. The insufficient thermal sta­ bility of Ni-rich NCM materials is manifested by unwanted NiO phase  formation, which causes mechanical fracture of the bulk crystal struc­ ture, and release of reactive lattice oxygens and superoxide radicals  (O ▪ 2 − ), which accelerate electrolyte decomposition and concomitant gas  evolution  [4 – 6] . # 2. Results and discussion  

# 2.1. SEI and CEI layer formation on NCM811 cathodes and graphite  anodes  

LFMP was synthesized according to the scheme shown in  Fig. 2 a which is similar to a previously reported LMFB synthesis protocol  [24] First, the reduction behavior of LFMP and LFMB additives on the  graphite anode was investigated by examining the differential capacity  curves (d Q /d V  vs.  V ) of the graphite/Li cells ( Fig. 2 b ). The reduction  peak of the base electrolyte without any additive was observed at 0.55 V  (vs. Li/Li + ), which can be assigned to the reduction of EC [ 25 – 27 ]. With  the addition of LFMP and LFMB, the reduction peak of EC is significantly  suppressed and negatively shifted to 0.43 and 0.48 V, respectively, while  a new small peak assignable to the reduction of LFMP or LFMB appears  at 0.86 or 0.83 V ( inset in  Fig. 2 b ). This suggests that LFMP, LFMB, and  EC participated in SEI formation on the graphite anode. In addition, the  trend of the reduction potential (LFMP  >  LFMB  >  EC) was consistent  with the predictions of the DFT calculations ( Fig. 2 c  and  S1 ). The charge  (lithiation)/discharge (delithiation) capacities and Coulombic effi­ ciencies in the first cycle of the graphite/Li cells are summarized in  Table S1 . The cell with the LFMP electrolyte displayed a higher  discharge capacity (338 mAh g − 1 ) and Coulombic efficiency (84%) than  the cells with the base (325 mAh g − 1 , 75%) and LFMB (315 mAh g − 1 76%) electrolytes, suggesting the superior properties of the SEI derived  by LFMP compared to those of EC and LFMB. The reversibility of the  electrolyte additives during the operation of graphite/Li cells was also  confirmed through cyclic voltammetry measurements ( Fig. S2) . Typical  redox peaks pertaining to the charge/discharge reaction of the graphite  anode were observed, manifesting reversible Li-ion insertion/deinser­ tion reaction with the electrolyte additives. In addition, the order of the  Coulombic efficiency during the first cycle was the same as that obtained  from the differential capacity measurements (base  <  LFMB  <  LFMP).  

The oxidation behavior of the additives was examined using linear  sweep voltammetry (LSV) with a Pt working electrode ( Fig. 2 d ). In  general, CEI additives are oxidized prior to other electrolyte compo­ nents, which form a protective CEI on the cathode surface, suppressing  further electrolyte decomposition. In the first anodic scan, the LFMP and  LMFB electrolytes showed higher oxidation currents than that of the  base electrolyte, but the trend was reversed in the subsequent scan  ( inset in  Fig. 2 d ). This indicates that the oxidation of LFMP and LFMB  formed a passivating layer on the Pt surface, suggesting that LFMP and  LFMB are likely to act as CEI additives. In addition, the order of the  experimental oxidation potentials (base  >  LFMP  >  LFMB) matched well  with the DFT prediction ( Fig. 2 e and S1 ).  Fig. S3  shows the cyclic  voltammograms of NCM811/Li cells with the additives at 25 ◦ C, sup­ porting the reversibility of the electrolyte additives during the operation  of the NCM cathode.

Next, we examined the influence of additives on the thermal stability  of the NCM811/graphite cells.  Fig. 3 a  compares the cyclabilities of the  NCM811/graphite cells at 60 ◦ C. The cell with the LFMB electrolyte  exhibited a higher capacity than the cell with the base electrolyte up to  the 125th cycle; however, it suffered from severe capacity fading af­ terward, resulting in only 13% capacity retention after 250 cycles, even  lower than that of the base electrolyte (30%). The trend in the AIC ( Fig. 3 b ) and Coulombic efficiency  ( Fig. S7 ) matches well with that of capacity retention. Furthermore, we  examined the self-discharge behavior of fully charged NCM811/  graphite cells during storage at 60 ◦ C ( Fig. 3 c ). The time for the OCV to  reach 3.0 V decreased in the following order: LFMP (87 days)  >  LFMB  (76 days)  >  base (23 days). Notably, the steady-state  pressure decreased in the following order: base (25 kPa)  >  LFMB (23  kPa)  >  LFMP (20 kPa). The dominant gas species were CO 2  and CO,  derived from the oxidative decomposition at the cathode, rather than  hydrocarbons (CH 4 , C 2 H 4 , and C 2 H 6 ), derived from the solvent reduc­ tion reactions ( Fig. S8 ) [ 1 , 24 ]. These results clearly suggest the  outstanding ability of LFMP to suppress side reactions during cycling  and storage tests at 60 ◦ C.

Symmetric cell tests were performed to examine the influence of  LFMP on the NCM cathode and graphite anode separately. During the  60 ◦ C cycling tests ( Fig. 3 e and f ), the cathode symmetric cell with LFMP  displayed much higher capacity retention (60% after 200 cycles) than  those with LFMB or without any additive (29% for both cases).  Furthermore, the LFMP electrolyte remarkably enhanced the 60 ◦ C  cyclability of the graphite symmetric cell; the capacity retention after  200 cycles decreased in the order LFMP (58%)  >  LFMB (31%)  >  base  (16%). This suggests that LFMP improves the thermal stability of both  the NCM811 cathode and graphite anode, which jointly contributes to  the superior cyclability of the NCM811/graphite full cells ( Fig. 3 a ). In  contrast, LFMB adversely influenced cathode cyclability while it  improved the cycle performance of the anode, which seems to be  responsible for the poor thermal stability of the full cell.  

To further assess the thermal stability of the CEI and SEI derived from  the additives, a differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) analysis was  performed.  Fig. S9a  exhibits the DSC profiles of the delithiated NCM811  cathodes for the base, LFMB, and LFMP electrolytes. The samples were  cycled three times from 25 ◦ C to 350 ◦ C and back. Importantly, the onset  temperature increased in the order base (197 ◦ C)  <  LFMB (202 ◦ C)  ≈ LFMP (204 ◦ C), and the total heat generation decreased in the order base  (1536 J g − 1 )  >  LFMB (1414 J g − 1 )  >  LFMP (1005 J g − 1 ), suggesting the  enhanced thermal stability imparted by the additives.  Fig. S9b  com­ pares the DSC responses of the delithiated graphite anodes with the base  and malonate electrolytes. Since thermal reactions are absent due to the  Li intercalation, the exothermic peaks over 70 – 130 ◦ C can be attributed  to the thermal decomposition of the SEI itself [ 30 – 33 ]. Notably, the  LFMP-derived SEI displayed a higher onset temperature (83 ◦ C) and less  heat generated (48 J g − 1 ) than those by the base (74 ◦ C and 61 J g − 1 ) and  LFMB (63 ◦ C and 45 J g − 1 ) electrolytes, supporting that LFMP-induced  SEI is more resistant to thermal decomposition.  

In addition, we examined the dissolution of metal ions from fully  charged NCM811 cathodes by using inductively coupled plasma optical  emission spectroscopy (ICP-OES). As presented in  Fig. S10,  the amounts  of dissolved Ni, Co, and Mn ions decreased in the order LFMP  <  LFMB  < Base. This supports that the CEI derived from LFMP can effectively  suppress the metal dissolution of the NCM cathodes. This fact is in line  with the beneficial effects of LFMP on the thermal stability of the NCM  cathode.

# 2.4. Surface analysis of graphite anodes  

Furthermore, the beneficial effect of LFMP on graphite anodes was  confirmed.  Fig. 6 a ‒ c  displays TEM images of the graphite anode  collected from the NCM811/graphite cells cycled three times at 25 ◦ C,  indicating that a thin, uniform SEI layer covered the graphite anode.  However, after 100 cycles at 60 ◦ C ( Fig. 6 d ‒ f ), the SEI layers derived  from LFMB and the base electrolytes were significantly damaged,  exhibiting a rough and porous structure. In contrast, the LFMP-derived  SEI layer remained intact even after cycling at 60 ◦ C. These results  indicate that the LFMP additive leads to a more stable SEI layer on the  graphite anode compared to the LFMB and base electrolytes.  

In addition, the electrode morphology of the graphite anode was  examined using SEM analysis ( Fig. S13 ). The SEM images of the  graphite anode subjected to three cycles at 25 ◦ C displayed no significant  difference ( Fig. S13a ‒ c ); however, after 100 cycles at 60 ◦ C ( Fig. S13d  and e ), the graphite electrodes cycled in the base and LFMB electrolytes  were severely damaged by the large void formation. In contrast, the  electrode cycled in the LFMP electrolyte retained its physical integrity  without notable deformation ( Fig. S13f ).

# 2.5. Theoretical study on the roles of LFMB and LFMP additives  

We performed DFT calculations to understand the different molec­ ular behaviors of the LFMB and LFMP additives ( Fig. 8  and  9 ). The  thermal degradation of the NCM cathode is associated with the  generation of superoxide radicals, which lead to electrophilic attacks on  the electrolyte solvents, causing CO and CO 2  evolution  [35 – 37] . The  previous sections corroborate that the LFMP-derived CEI layer is stable  at elevated temperatures, whereas the LFMB-derived CEI layer is  vulnerable to thermal decomposition, as shown in  Fig. 8 a . Therefore, we  suppose that the phosphorus moiety in the LFMP-derived CEI is more  efficient in trapping the superoxide radical than the boron counterpart  in the LFMB-derived CEI. To verify this, we calculated the Gibbs free  energy ( Δ G ) for the reactions of superoxide radicals with the oxidative  decomposition products of LFMB and LFMP ( Fig. 8 b and c ). The  oxidative decomposition mechanisms of LFMB  [24]  and LFMP are  shown in  Fig. S16 . Notably, the  Δ G  value of the LFMP oxidation product  ( − 20.9 kcal mol − 1 ) is much lower than that of the LFMB oxidation  product ( − 12.5 kcal mol − 1 ), indicating that the LFMP-derived CEI  would capture the superoxide radical more readily than the  LFMB-derived CEI. The different affinities toward superoxide radicals  can be ascribed to the fact that the partial charge at the P atom is almost  double that of the B atom of the boron analog ( 2.610 vs.  1.375,  + + Fig. 8 b and c ).  

The effect of the LFMP additive on the graphite anode, in addition to  its beneficial role in stabilizing the cathode interface, is shown in  Fig. 9 a Lithium fluoride (LiF) in the SEI layers prevents electrolyte degradation  on graphite anodes [ 38 , 39 ]. The LiF content in the LFMP-derived SEI  layer on the graphite anode was discernibly higher than that in the  LFMB-derived SEI layer, which is consistent with the superior thermal  stability of the LFMP-derived SEI ( Fig. 7 c and d ). Our DFT calculations  demonstrated that the generation of LiF upon reduction at the anode  surface is thermodynamic ally favored in the case of LFMP over that of  LFMB ( Fig. 9 b) . The F atom attached to the  α -carbon of the two malo­ nates has a high reactivity; therefore, they readily form LiF. However,  compared with the B F bonds in LFMB, the P F bonds in LFMP are  − − much more readily broken to form LiF. The difference between borate  and phosphate was also confirmed for lithium salts other than malo­ nates, suggesting that LiF formation is generally more favored in phos­ phate compounds than in borate counterparts ( Fig. S17 ). Moreover, PF 5  generated by the hydrolysis of LiPF 6  is known to be a major cause of SEI  degradation  [40 – 42] . Hence, we calculated the binding energies (BEs) of  PF 5  with the reductive decomposition products LFMB and LFMP, as  shown in  Fig. S16.  As displayed in  Fig. 9 c , the BE value of the LFMP  reduction product (10.1 kcal mol − 1 ) is significantly lower than that of  the LFMB one (63.3 kcal mol − 1 ), suggesting that the LFMP-derived SEI is  less vulnerable to PF 5  attacks than the LFMB-derived SEI.  

# 3. Conclusions  

In summary, we have reported LFMP as a dual-functional additive  that improves both the thermal stability of the NCM811 cathode and the  graphite anode. Compared to LFMB, LFMP exhibited excellent cycling,  storage performance, and suppression of gas evolution in an NCM811/  graphite full cell at 60 ◦ C. Furthermore, the LFMP additive alleviated the  cell impedance and thus showed a superior rate capability to that of the  LFMB additive. With the help of in-depth experimental analysis and DFT  calculations, the origin of the beneficial effects of the LFMP additive can  be explained as follows: (i) on the NCM811 cathode, LFMP forms a stable  CEI layer that suppresses electrolyte decomposition and gas evolution  more effectively than the LFMB-derived CEI; (ii) on the graphite anode,  LFMP forms a LiF-rich SEI layer that is less vulnerable to PF 5  attack. This  work highlights the need for further studies on malo nato phosphates as  versatile electrolyte additives for thermally robust LIBs and promotes  their utilization in advanced battery systems.
