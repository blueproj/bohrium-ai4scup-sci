# THERMAL CYCLE SIMULATION OF ANi-BASE SUPERALLOY  

N.E. Glover and C.L. Davis Department of Materials Science and Metallurgy University of Cambridge, Cambridge, CB2 3QZ, United Kingdom  

(Received April 19, 1995)  

Thermal cycle simulation techniques have been investigated as a method of studying the effect of cooling rate, from sub-solvus solution heat treatment temperatures, on the microstructure and fatigue properties of Ni-based superalloy specimens.A Smitweld thermal cycle simulator (TCS) and a Gleeble 1500 TCS were used during the investigation. Grain growth was observed, predominantly in the sample centre, in a number of specimens simulated using the Smitweld TCS as a result of severe temperature gradients set up across the specimen. It is important both to quantify and minimise this temperature gradient, to allow accurate heat treatment at the desired temperature. The discrepancy between surface and core temperatures, under different simulation conditions, was determined using three different specimen geometries for internal thermocouple placement. The optimum conditions to minimise the temperature overheat were found to be a highly polished specimen surface tested under high vacuum using a small (0.15mm) diameter K-type thermocouple for temperature control. The radial temperature distribution in the simulated zone was further investigated by microstructural examination of the supersolvus grain growth region in a series of samples simulated to a range of peak temperatures.  

# 1.INTRODUCTION  

The fatigue crack propagation resistance of Ni-based superalloys is related to the microstructural features of the material namely the grain size and ' size and volume fraction (1). One factor controlling the ' size is the cooling rate after solution heat treatment of the alloy. Thermal cycle simulation allows close control of the cooling rate, especially at the crucial temperatures close to the ' solvus temperature, to be achieved in test bars. Hence the effect of cooling rate on ' size and fatigue properties can be studied.  

Thermal cyc:le simulators (TCS) employ resistance and/or induction heating of the specimen. Cooling is achieved using water cooled grips, direct water cooling of specimen ends and/or water or gas quenching of the sample surface. During this investigation two simulators, a Smitweld TCS and a Gleeble 1500 TCS, were used to heat treat a Ni-based superalloy. A non uniform temperature profile across the specimen thickness was observed during initial trials. Tests were performed to investigate and minimise the occurrence of such temperature gradients.

Initial testswere performed on a Smitweld TCS using specimens measuring 10.5 x 10.5 x 75 mm with 4 mm diameter holes of 15 mm depth machined into each end of the specimen. This system utilises electrical resistance heating and both indirect water cooling, through the grips, and direct water cooling, into the ends of the specimen. The specimen temperature is monitored using a K-type thermocouple spot welded onto the surface of the sample. The overall specimen configuration is shown schematically in Figure 3. A problem occurred with temperature overheat in the centre of the specimen relative to the surface during these tests.  

Subsequent trials were performed on a Glee ble 15 o oTC S which also uses resistance heating and water cooling,in this case through thegripsonly.The Glee bl eTC S specimens were 10 x 10 x 75mmwithno direct cooling holes.The advantages of the Glee ble system are that testing can be carried out under vacuum and closer control of the specimen temperature can be achieved during long heat treatments. The magnitude of thie temperature difference between the surface and centre of the specimen was measured using external and internal thermocouples, the surface temperature measurement being fed back to the computer for control of the thermal cycle. External and internal temperatures were measured at 100 °C intervals during the heating and cooling stages of thesimulation thermal cycle and at 30 secondintervals during the hold period. The temperature overshoot (AT = T intermal - T extermat) was then calculated.  

Three specimen geometries for internal thermocouple placement were investigated, Figure 4. In the first two geometries 1mm diameter holes were spark eroded radially and axially to the centre of the specimen respectively. For the third geometry a 0.3 mm slit was machined along the entire length of the specimen to half depth. In each case a 0.15 mm diameter K-type thermocouple was inserted to the centre of the specimen where it was cemented in place using a small drop of Autostick FC8 high temperature adhesive.  

The radial and axial hole geometries result in a disruption of the current flow through the specimen and therefore of the temperature profile around the hole (2). A further difficulty with these designs is the placement of the thermocouple wires to ensure good thermal contact is achieved at the centre of the specimen, i.e. at the end of the hole. Lagging, that remained intact to high temperatures (=1150 °C), was required to prevent a short circuit occurring along the length of the thermocouple. The high temperature adhesive was used to paint an insulating layer on the thermocouple wires before positioning them in the specimen. In general a good, reproducible thermal contact at the base of the hole was difficult to achieve.

The third specimen geometry, having a 0.3 mm wide machined slot along the length of the specimen, satisfies the requirement for maintaining a uniform cross sectional area for the current and thermal flow, thereby minimising any temperature disruption effects. The depth to width ratio of 15:1 for the slot also ensures an oven like environment is created for the thermocouple, resulting in a more accurate temperature reading, as radiative heat losses are reduced. There is still some difficulty in accurately positioning the internal thermocouple and ensuring good thermal contact, although considerably less than for the radial and axial hole geometries. Therefore this specimen geometry was adopted for all subsequent trials. Although this technique allows the influence of simulation parameters on thedegreeof overheattobe investigated, the absolute level of overheat cannot be determined, by this method, as it will be reduced by the presence of the internal thermocouple.  

# I. RESULTS  

# Smitweld TCS Trials  

Initial trials on the Smitweld TCS were carried out in air with a computer hold temperature, for the thermal cycle, of 1150 °C. Microstructural examination revealed grain growth in the simulated zone of some specimens even though 1150 \*C is sub solvus for this alloy. The grain growth occurred in the central and lower portion of the simulated zone (away from the surface with thermocouple) and is indicative of a large overheat in temperature for this region compared to the measured surface temperature.  

The slow heating rate, 2 °C/sec, used during simulation meant that the overshoot in measured temperature at the surface of the specimen with respect to the computer set point value, due to insufficient heating control, was minimised. With the Smitweld TCS a maximum overshoot of \~ 10 °C, with respect to the computer hold temperature, can occur. This is insufficient to account for the grain growth observed. A large thermal gradient must therefore exist between the surface and the centre of the specimen.  

Several problems with simulation using the Smitweld TCS were identified during the trials. Firstly the limited power capability, and the low thermal conductivity of Ni, mean that close control of the thermal cycle cannot generally be achieved even with the slow heating rate. This can result in an overshoot in measured surface temperature compared to the computer hold temperature. A second problem arises from the design of the simulator. The Smitweld TCS was primarily designed for thermal cycle simulation of welding, which does not involve prolonged heat treatment at high temperatures. During these tests a hold time of 5 minutes at about 1150°C was required. The control system relies on water cooling to the grips and specimen through plastic pipes, coupled with resistance heating. These plastic pipes became hot during simulation and had a tendency to burst. Whilst this equipment problem is easy to overcome by changing the water pipes the general lack of close control, versatility and robustness led further trials to be performed on the Glee ble 15 o 0 TC S.

It can be seen that the use of a fine diameter thermocouple helps to minimise thermal overheat. Reports (3) have suggested that K-type thermocouples are unsuitable for high temperature simulations of steel specimens, as chemical diffusion between the thermocouple and specimen result in inaccuracies in temperature measurement. K-type thermocouples are comprised of Ni-Al and Ni-Cr wires; the alloy being simulated in these trials is a primarily Ni, Cr, Al containing alloy hence little diffusion is expected. Tests were carried out to confirm the suitability of K-type thermocouples by simultaneous measurement using K-type and R-type thermocouples spot welded on a specimen heated in a furnace. R-type thermocouples are Pt/Pt-Rh and have good high temperature stability. No difference in temperature reading was observed, after exposure at temperatures up to 1io0 °C over a two hour period, between the two thermocouples suggesting thar chemical diffusion is not a problem for K-type thermocouples with these samples. c)Radiativelosses:  

Heat is lost from the specimen surface by radiation, the amount of heat loss being controlled by the emissivity of the material surface.Emissivity values depend critically on the sample surface condition, increasing with roughness of a clean surface and also with the cover of oxide film. A series of tests were performed to investigate this effect on the degree of overheat using specimens with 0.15 mm K-type surface thermocouples. The results are summarised in Table 3.  

The results show that a polished surface under high vacuum (1o - Torr) results in the lowest degree of overheating. A polished surface under low vacuum (10 - Torr) initially shows a similar reduction in overheat, however during the hold period at solutioning temperature some oxidation of the specimen surface occurs, resulting in an increase in emissivity, and a corresponding increase in overheat. The rough surface has a higher emissivity, and hence shows a greater overheat.  

A small amount of oxidation was seen even under high vacuum. It was decided to back fill the chamber with argon, after evacuation, and then to re-evacuate to eliminate all oxidation thereby further reducing the degree of overheat. The chamber was evacuated to 10 5 Torr, backfilled with argon to atmospheric pressure and re-evacuated to 10 - Torr. This was repeated a second time prior to running the test. However, it was found that the level of oxidation and hence overheat was not reduced, this was believed tooccurfrom small leaks inthevacuum and argonsystem.  

An attempt to reduce radiative losses was also made by insulating the specimen. Two techniques were used, the first involved lagging the simulated zone using kaolite wool soaked in high temperature cement and securely wrapped around the specimen.The conductivity of thelagging material isvery low thereby setting up a large thermal gradient across the lagging. Emissivity losses from the surface of the lagging will be small since the surface temperature is low. Ideally the low conductivity of the lagging material means that there is minimal loss in heat from the specimen to the lagging. This should result in negligible losses from the specimen surface through radiative or conductive losses. In practice it was found that the overheat in core temperature was slightly higher for the lagged specimen than that of the highly polished sample at high vacuum.  

The second method tested used a fibreglass sleeve wrapped around the simulated zone. This technique proved to be unsatisfactory as the sleeve deteriorated at high temperatures. It was therefore decided to only use ahighlypolished specimen insubsequent tests.  

It was concluded that the optimum thermal cycle conditions for Ni based superalloys were a highly polished surface, simulated under high vacuum, with temperature measurement using a fine diameter K. type thermocouple spot welded on to the sample surface.  

# Quantitative Temperature Gradient Determination:  

It was desired to quantitatively determine the actual overheat and temperature distribution. This was done through knowledge of the grain growth temperature determined from furnace heat treatment trials.  

Samples measuring 10x10x10 mm were heat treated for 5 minutes in a vacuum furnace at a range of temperatures spanning the expected solvus temperature of the alloy. The sample temperature was measured using a K-type thermocouple spot welded onto the surface. Microstructural evaluation was performed to determine the grain growth temperature. Grain growth is expected when the solvus temperature is approached as a large volume fraction of primary grain boundary Y' goes into solution and no longer pins the grain boundaries.  

Simulations were performed under optimum conditions (determined to be a highly polished surface, high vacuum and 0.15mm K-type thermocouple) to a series of solutioning temperatures. The samples were subsequently sectioned through the simulated zone,polished,etched andexamined to determine the extent of the grain growth region. These results are shown schematically in Figure 6. From these results it was possible to generate an approximate radial temperature profie through the specimen, Figure 7. The results revealed an asymmetrical temperature distribution with the temperature decreasing towards the specimen surface with the thermocouple attached. A gradient of 15-20 °C exists across the specimen with a further \~25°Cerrorbetween thermocouple and the specimensurface.  

The aim of the work is to simulate samples that are then used in fatigue tests. From the radial temperature profile it is possible to determine a computer hold temperature which ensures that the critical portion of the sample is solutioned to a known temperature e.g. just sub solvus thereby eliminating any graingrowth.  

# IV. CONCLUSIONS  

Thermal simulation techniques have been used to perform accurate heat treatment cycles on Ni based superalloy specimens. Tests were performed to investigate how the temperature overheat in the specimen core relative to the surface temperature is affected by simulation parameters.Themain conclusionsare asfollows:  

Grain growth was observed during preliminary trials using the Smitweld TCS due to overheat in the centre of the simulated zone. Difficulties in close thermal control and overall versatility of the Smitweld TCS led subsequent trials to be performed on the Gleeble 1550 TCS.
