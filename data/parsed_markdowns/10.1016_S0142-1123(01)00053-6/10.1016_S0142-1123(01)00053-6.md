# Abstract  

The fatigue crack growth rate and fracture behaviour of a nickel–base superalloy UDIMET 720 Li was investigated at 700 ° C in vacuum and air environments using corner crack specimens. The effects of load ratio at a frequency of 0.25 Hz were examined while the effects of loading frequency from 5 Hz to 0.008 Hz were also examined for a constant load ratio. The mode of fracture was intergranular at all load ratios at a frequency of 0.25 Hz in an air environment. Two-parameter models were proposed to describe separately the effects of load ratio and frequency. The model prediction was combined with data from vacuum tests to form a fracture mechanism map showing limited contribution of creep, while oxidation controls the fatigue crack growth rate as the frequency decreases.   2001 Elsevier Science Ltd. # 1. Introduction  

Nickel base superalloys have been manufactured in a variety of forms such as high temperature creep resistant single crystals, directionally solidiﬁed columnar grain structure, wrought polycrystalline and powder metallur- gical prepared alloys such as UDIMET 720 Li, that is being investigated in the present work. The chemistry of these alloys has been considerably modiﬁed and made more complex over the years [1]. The Ni base superal- loys are used largely as materials for manufacturing tur- bine discs and turbine blades for aero-engines, and these components are subjected to cyclic loading during ser- vice. Therefore, a main factor limiting the lives of the components is cyclic fatigue loading which often arises due to centrifugal forces, thermal stresses or temperature gradients [2]. The failure of the material under cyclic fatigue loading consists of propagation of sub-critical cracks that may originate from pre-existing ﬂaws or may be formed at regions of stress concentration. The lives of the components can be easily predicted if the crack growth rate is determined in a controlled atmosphere and at low stresses. However, the increasing demand for high efﬁciency of the engine in turn implies higher operating temperatures, so many other factors must be taken into consideration in the life prediction. At high tempera- tures, creep deformation becomes signiﬁcant in addition to cyclic deformation and at the same time oxidation effects cannot be avoided. Under these harsh conditions and with application of both static and cyclic loading, it is necessary to determine the allowable ﬂaw size that can be used in the prediction of the residual life of the alloy. Also safe life prediction and improvement in alloy design can be better achieved if the mechanisms of frac- ture of this alloy are well understood. Recent work on a similar alloy has shown that the fracture surface during crack growth shows a typical feature termed “tear drop” and that this tear drop phenomenon is not entirely due to the transition from plane stress to plane strain con- ditions [3,4]. The present work reports on the fatigue crack propagation rate of UDIMET 720 Li under con- ditions of cyclic deformation and when interaction between cyclic deformation and creep deformation occurs. Finally a two-parameter model is proposed to evaluate the relative contributions of cycle dependent and time dependent deformations on the fatigue crack growth rates. Similarly the effect of loading frequency  

was described by a modiﬁed Paris power law and then combined with fractographic results to form a fracture mechanism map.

Fatigue crack propagation rate (FCPR) tests were car- ried out in load control conditions at a temperature of 700 ° C in laboratory air and in vacuum ( P O2 = 10 − 3   Pa) with an  R  ratio ( = K min / K max ) of 0.05. In order to study the inﬂuence of time dependent damage mechanism, a trapezoidal wave shape with hold time at maximum and minimum loads has been employed. The wave shape has the gen- eral trapezoidal form shown in Fig. 1(b) and is character- ised by four numbers that indicate the time, in seconds, spent during the loading ( t 1 ), the hold at maximum load ( t 2  t 1 ), the unloading ( t 3  t 2 ) and the hold at minimum load ( t 4  t 3 ) stages respectively. Corner crack specimens (CC) with a square gauge cross-section of 7 × 7 mm 2   and a 0.2 mm deep starter notch were used (Fig. 1(c)). Before the high temperature fatigue crack growth test, the specimens were fatigue pre-cracked for about 0.1 mm at room temperature at a frequency of 10 Hz, and at a load level well below that used for the subsequent high temperature test. The crack length was measured by the electric potential drop tech- nique. The fracture surfaces were examined by scanning electron microscopy (SEM).

# 3.2. Inﬂuence of load ratio  

The inﬂuence of load ratio ( R ) on fatigue crack propa- gation rate for a 1–1–1–1 wave shape is presented in Fig. 3. The crack growth rate increases generally with increasing load ratio since the magnitude of the mean tensile stress acting on the crack increases with increas- ing load ratio. Although it is not clear why the crack growth curve for  R = 0.2 should slightly rise above that of  R = 0.4 at certain   K  values, it may not imply any peculiar effect. It can also be observed that at d a /d N above 2 × 10 − 7   m/cycle all the curves show a linear dependence between the logarithm of crack growth rate and the logarithm of the stress intensity factor range,  K , indicating a Paris regime. The data were therefore ﬁtted to a Paris growth law of the form: d a /d N = C  K   n and the values of the constants  C  and  n  for different values of  R  are presented in Table 2. The exponent  n  of the Paris equation ranges between 2 and 4 for the differ- ent load ratios and these values are consistent with those usually reported for polycrystalline materials [6]. The transition from Paris regime to high   K  regime where fast fracture occurs is not well deﬁned and it is therefore difﬁcult to estimate a   K c  that leads to ﬁnal unstable crack growth.  

The fracture surfaces were examined by SEM in order to establish the mechanisms of crack growth. It has been reported that the mode of fracture may depend on the level of   K  and the value of the  R  ratio so that a fracture mechanism map can be constructed [7]. For this reason we have particularly examined the fracture mode at dif- ferent  R -values and at different stages of crack growth. Typical fracture surfaces for different  R -values are shown in Fig. 4. At a low  R  value of 0.2, in air environ- ment the fracture mode is mixed transgranualar and intergranular with the latter dominating (Fig. 4(a)). Simi- larly at other load ratios the fracture mode is completely intergranular as shown in Fig. 4(b), (c) and (d) corre- sponding to  R = 0.4, 0.6 and 0.8 respectively. There is no evidence of microcavities on the fracture surfaces that could be associated with creep deformation.  

# 3.3. Effects of loading waveform  

# 3.3.1. In vacuum  

Fig. 5 shows the inﬂuence of loading waveform as well as of the environment on the fatigue crack propa- gation rate with load ratio of approximately zero at a temperature of 700 ° C. Under vacuum conditions d a /d N  vs   K  curves for 0.1–0–0.1–0 (loading frequency of 5 Hz) and 1–1–1–1 (i.e. loading frequency of 0.25 Hz) waveforms do not result in any appreciable differ- ence. Even for the longer dwell time of 120 s (loading frequency of 0.008 Hz) a slight increase in crack growth rate was observed and this increase may have contri- butions from the quality of the vacuum. This shows that the frequency of loading has only a marginal effect on the crack growth rate of the alloy in vacuum conditions and for the load ratio investigated in this study.  

The fracture surfaces were also examined by SEM for the different loading frequencies since the mode of frac- ture depends not only on the magnitude of   K  and K max , but also on the loading frequency. At high fre- quency the degree of slip homogeneity is obviously high and transgranular mode of fracture is often predominant, while at low frequency the degree of slip homogeneity is reduced and intervention of time dependent defor- mation may take place [8]. The fracture surfaces, as observed for the different loading frequencies in vacuum and for  R = 0.05, are presented in Fig. 6. For the fre- quencies of 5 Hz (Fig. 6(a)) and 0.25 Hz (Fig. 6(b)) the fracture mode is entirely transgranular and traces of fatigue striations can be seen on the fracture surfaces. This suggests that the deformation is mainly cycle- dependent. However, at the lower frequency of 0.008 Hz (Fig. 6(c)) the mode of fracture is predominantly inter- granular.

# 5. Conclusion  

In vacuum environments the fatigue crack growth rate of UDIMET 720 Li superalloy remains practically unchanged for hold time up to 1 s, while a small increase is observed when the hold time is increased up to 120 s. The lack of inﬂuence of hold time on FCPR is mainly because it is controlled by an oxidation dominated mech- anism.  

In air environments the fatigue crack growth rate increases with increasing dwell time and it is consider- ably higher than those obtained in vacuum, indicating the adverse effect of oxidation.  

A two-parameter Paris law model was used to describe the effect of  R -ratio at a frequency of 0.25 Hz. The model shows the strong contribution of  K max  to the crack growth rate and correlates well with the inter- granular fracture that was observed at all load ratios.  

Another model in the form of two-parameter Paris law was also proposed to assess the effect of the loading frequency, and a fracture mechanism map was plotted using crack growth rate and frequency as co-ordinates. The mode of fracture changes at high frequency (5 Hz) from transgranular to intergranular as the frequency decreases showing that the FCP rate was controlled by oxidation mechanism.
