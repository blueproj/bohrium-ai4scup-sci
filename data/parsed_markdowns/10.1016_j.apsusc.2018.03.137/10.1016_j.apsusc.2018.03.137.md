# ABSTRACT  

The paper presents results of microstructural investigations of TBCs on Pt- and PdPt- aluminide bondcoatings with focus on the interfacial phenomena that take place during the  pre-oxidation treatment as well as further thermal cycling at 1100 °C. The Pt and Pd coatings  were produced using a Physical Vapor Deposition (PVD) method while aluminizing was  performed using a high activity Vapor Phase Aluminizing (VPA). The ceramic yttria  stabilized zirconia (YSZ) top coating was deposited using Electron Beam Physical Vapor  Deposition (EB-PVD) method. Prior to the EB-PVD process the bondcoatings were pre- oxidized at 1140 °C for 2 hours in air atmosphere in order to form a stable and adherent α- Al 2 O 3  TGO with minimum transient oxidation. Special effort has been done in order to  investigate the microstructure of the Thermally Grown Oxide (TGO) formed during pre- oxidation treatment prior to YSZ deposition and the phenomena occurring at the interface  between the YSZ, TGO, and the bondcoatings. Microstructure evolution of the TGOs is  described and related to the conditions of the pre-oxidation treatment and the chemistry of the  bondcoatings. During thermal cycling at 1100 °C the TGO grows by inward oxygen diffusion  forming distinctive columnar grains which grow deeper at triple grain junctions with the  bondcoating. Therefore the TGO / bondcoating interface evolves from flat to a convoluted  during high temperature oxidation. Several segregation phenomena can be found in the TBCs  that include i.a. the Reactive Element Effect involving the presence of Hf and Y on α-alumina  grain boundary disconnections in the TGO. It was found that compared to bare René N5     superalloy the Hf segregation is hindered in the presence of modified aluminide bondcoatings.  

# 1.   Introduction  

Thermal Barrier Coatings (TBC) are complex coating systems deposited on turbine  components working under the harshest environments. They provide surface temperature  reduction up to 150 °C prolonging the component lifetimes, increasing turbine efficiency as  well as lowering the emissions. TBCs consist of three main components: the outer ceramic  coating usually made of yttria stabilized zirconia (YSZ) deposited by plasma spraying or  Electron Beam Physical Vapor Deposition (EB-PVD), oxidation resistant bondcoating (BC)  and the thermally grown oxide (TGO) which evolves during high temperature exposure  between the BC and the YSZ. The YSZ due to its low thermal conductivity provides thermal  insulation which in addition to internal cooling passages leads to temperature reduction of the  coated components. The role of the BC is to enable the formation of an adherent and slow  growing TGO made of α-Al 2 O 3  at the interface between it and the YSZ [1-3]. The most  common bondcoatings applied in TBCs are i.a. plasma sprayed NiCoCrAlY coatings,  aluminide coatings modified by noble elements such as Pt or Pd produced using Chemical  Vapor Deposition (CVD) or recently developed Pt-diffused ɣ/ɣ’ bondcoatings. The vast  diversity of available TBC systems along with their resistance to oxidation have been recently  summarized in an extensive study by Smialek based on a broad literature base [4].  

During service at high temperatures the degradation of TBCs occurs due to the spallation  of the ceramic top coat, the YSZ, leading to the exhibition of the metal surface to high  temperature. Among many possible degradation mechanisms of EB-PVD TBCs the most  crucial are the phenomena taking place at the interface between the BC and the TGO, TGO  and YSZ [5] as well as within each of these components [6]. These include the growth of the  TGO during thermal exposure, void formation between the TGO and the BC as well as within     them, and also phase and microstructural transformations occurring within the YSZ, TGO and  BC [7-9].  

The simple and modified diffusion aluminide bondcoatings are known to suffer from the  rumpling (surface roughening) phenomenon [10, 11]  as well as β-NiAl to ɣ’-Ni 3 Al [12] and  martensitic transformations [13, 14]  during high temperature exposure. It is generally  agreed that the growth of the TGO layer during high temperature exposure has a direct impact  on the durability of TBCs. It is related to the critical thickness of the oxide scale  h crit   upon  which spallation occurs. It is believed to be around 6-7 µm in the case of diffusion aluminide  bondcoatings [15].  

Rybicki [16], Smialek [17], Brumm and Grabke [18] performed systematic studies on  oxidation of alloys from the Ni-Al system and showed that during initial stages of oxidation  transient alumina polymorphs are formed. These include i.a. the highly defected cubic ɣ- Al 2 O 3  (a = 0.7949 nm) and monoclinic θ-Al 2 O 3  (a=0.5620, b=0.2906, c=1.1790, β=103°20’) 

 [19] that grow in the form of plate- or needle-like grains by outward cationic lattice diffusion 

 [20]. The outward diffusion of Al 3+  leads to the vacancy injection at the metal-scale interface  which causes the formation of interfacial voids compromising the adhesion of the scale [21,  22]. The transformation of these transient polymorphs to the stable α-Al 2 O 3  follows the path  ɣ-Al 2 O 3  -> θ-Al 2 O 3  -> α-Al 2 O 3   [16]  and involves unit cell volume change of 8% giving rise to  tensile stresses within the TGO  [23] .  

The deposition of YSZ top coat using EB-PVD method is typically performed at  temperatures around 1000°C in an oxygen containing atmosphere thus the growth of TGO is  also initiated. A typical TGO after the deposition of the YSZ consists of a mixture of alumina  and zirconia (termed as a “mixed zone”) [8,  24 ]. Its formation is attributed to either the  nucleation of the YSZ between the TGO needles in its transient growth stage  [25]  or  dissolution of zirconia in the transient alumina and its subsequent precipitation upon θ-Al 2 O 3  -    > α-Al 2 O 3  transformation  [26].  There is no generally agreed correlation between the formation  of the mixed zone and the performance of TBCs  [27] , but there have been reports that cracks  and buckle-type delaminations can form parallel to the interface between it and the columnar  TGO  [28] .  

Numerous research works concerned the improvement of TBC lifetimes by application of  various surface modification techniques, including shot peening [29], irradiation by  high-current pulsed electron beam [30] or   pre-oxidation [31]. The last one is  aimed at the  formation of a thin, stable and slow growing α-Al 2 O 3  TGO prior to the deposition of the YSZ.  With this approach, time, temperature, and oxygen partial pressure for the pre-oxidation  treatment are chosen independently of the conditions of the subsequent EB-PVD cycle. It is  assumed that the rate of transformation from the transient polymorphs determines the  nucleation rate of the steady-state α-alumina and also its grain size  [31, 32] . Since the growth  of α-alumina proceeds by grain boundary diffusion of oxygen anions the formation of a  coarse-grained scale should allow to control its subsequent thickening rate due to fewer  transport pathways  [33] .  

Indeed, experimental data shows that the controlled formation of TGO prior to deposition  of YSZ provides measurable improvement in TBC lifetime. These include the application of  annealing in vacuum  [34, 35] , Ar-H atmosphere  [35] , low pO 2   [31, 36-38]  or air  [39] . Nijdam  et al.  [37, 38]  showed that the application of pre-oxidation under low pO 2  can in fact lower the  growth rate of TGO. Moreover, application of a low pO 2  allows inhibiting the formation of  undesired spinel phases such as NiAl 2 O 4  [37] . It should also be noted that the when performed  under a too low pO 2  the pre-oxidation process may not provide complete coverage with stable  oxide scale. It is therefore crucial to study the interfaces between the TGO and the  bondcoatings as well as the ceramic top coating in order to fully understand the phenomena  that occur during high temperature exposure of TBCs. Therefore, the goal of this paper is to     describe the interfacial phenomena taking place in TBCs on Pt- and PdPt-aluminide  bondcoatings during pre-oxidation in air atmosphere at 1140 °C and subsequent cyclic  oxidation test at 1140 °C.  Moreover, the paper presents the results of research conducted  on partial replacement of Pt with Pd indicating that modification of aluminide coatings  with Pd or both these elements provides very good oxidation resistance along with  significant cost reduction, due to the fact that the density of Pd is almost two lower than  that of Pt.  

# 2.   Experimental procedure  

The substrate material was a single crystal René N5 superalloy with the following  chemical composition (in wt. %): Al-6.14, C-0.06, Co-7.3, Cr-7.3, Hf-0.15, Mo-1.5, Re-2.88,  Ta-6.5, W-4.91, Zr-0.01, Y-<0.02, S-0.2 ppm, Ni-balance. The samples of the René N5  superalloy (cylinder shaped, 10mm diameter, 80mm height) were put under heat treatment  that consisted of solutioning and aging in order to obtain the γ-γ’ microstructure. The  bondcoatings were produced by depositing Pt and Pd+Pt coatings using the PVD (Physical  Vapor Deposition) method followed by vacuum annealing at 1140°C for 2h and high activity   Vapor Phase Aluminizing (VPA) in CrAl alloy granules at 1050°C for 5h in argon  atmosphere. The PVD process was performed at the Fraunhofer-IST, Braunschweig,  Germany, the Vapor Phase Aluminizing (VPA) process was conducted at the Silesian  University of Technology in Poland and the 7%wt. YSZ TBC was produced using EB-PVD at  

DLR, Germany. Prior to YSZ deposition the as-deposited bondcoatings were pre-oxidized at  1140 °C for 2h in laboratory air atmosphere.  

Cyclic oxidation test were performed at 1100°C with the dwell time of 1:15 h in  laboratory air environment followed by 15 minutes of cooling. After the deposition of the  YSZ top coat and during the cyclic oxidation test the samples were measured using a 3D  optical scanning nondestructive method in order to monitor the changes occurring on their     surface. After 1100 cycles at 1100 °C (total of 1375 hours at high temperature) the cyclic  oxidation test was stopped as the 3D scanning method revealed that TBCs on both  bondcoatings exhibit measurable deviations indicating onset of YSZ top coat spallation. The  samples were cut, mounted in a resin, ground and polished in order to examine their cross- section microstructure using scanning electron microscope FEI Inspect F equipped with FEG.  High resolution STEM analyses of interfacial phenomena between the YSZ / TGO / Pt- and  PdPt-aluminide bondcoatings were conducted using FEI TITAN 80-300 equipped with X- FEG. The samples for STEM investigations were prepared using Focused Ion Beam method  where Pt layer was applied to protect the sample during the ion (Ga + ) thinning process.

3.1.2.   Formation of the Thermally Grown Oxides (TGOs) during pre-oxidation treatment  

Prior to the deposition of the outer ceramic top coat, i.e. YSZ, the pre-oxidation  treatment was performed in order to form a thin and slow-growing TGO scale on the surface  of the Pt- and PdPt-aluminide bondcoatings. For a detailed study both HAADF and ADF  detectors were applied each one of them providing important information concerning the  chemistry (HAADF) and grain structure (ADF) of the TGO scales. The HAADF images ( Fig.     2a,c ) of the TGOs formed on both bondcoatings indicate that the TGO scale is 425±47 nm  thick on the Pt-aluminide and 375±54 nm thick on PdPt-aluminide and both are characterized  by uniform chemical composition with no variations in the \~Z 2  sensitive contrast. Only minor  nanometric particles are visible in  Fig. 2a  which contain Ni or Cr rejected from the transient  oxides during their transformation to the α-Al 2 O 3 . In fact, the α-Al 2 O 3  phase was identified  for both studied TGO scales and no other phases, such as e.g. NiAl 2 O 4 , were found.

The crystallographic orientation contrast sensitive imaging using the ADF detector  ( Fig. 2b,d ) allowed for a characterization of the grain size and structure of the YSZ / TGO /  bondcoating interfaces. It has been found that the applied pre-oxidation treatment at 1140 °C  for 2h led to the formation of adherent α-Al 2 O 3  ( Fig. 2g,h ) scales with continuous interfaces  with both Pt- and PdPt-aluminide bondcoatings. The TGO on the Pt-aluminide bondcoating  has significantly narrower α-Al 2 O 3  grains with width of around 240 nm ( Fig. 2b ) compared to  around 770 nm on the PdPt-aluminide ( Fig. 2d ). It is noteworthy that no interfacial voids  were found on either of the bondcoatings indicating that the transient oxidation stage was  limited to a minimum during the pre-oxidation treatment possibly due to the relatively high  temperature – 1140 °C. However, the initial formation of either θ or ɣ-alumina cannot be  excluded on both bondcoatings since the formed TGOs contain very fine internal voids that  probably originate from the volumetric changes accompanying their phase transformation to  the stable α-Al 2 O 3  ( Fig. 2a,c ). The delamination at the TGO / bondcoating interface visible in  Fig. 2c,d  originates most probably from FIB milling procedure. The interface between the  TGO and the YSZ is clearly visible and no mixed zone was formed during the EB-PVD     process of YSZ deposition in the case of both bondcoatings. It is noteworthy that the YSZ /  TGO interface on the Pt-aluminide bondcoating contains numerous ridges, i.e. convex regions  that formed due to outward diffusion of Al 3+  through α-Al 2 O 3  grain boundaries ( Fig. 2b ).  Conversely, on the PdPt-aluminide bondcoating this interface is smoother most probably due  to a lower width of the α-Al 2 O 3  grains thus a smaller amount of grain boundaries in the  observed region ( Fig. 2d ). As noted previously the PdPt-aluminide bondcoating is free from  any other phases than the β phase ( Fig. 2i ), both within the coating and at the interface with  the TGO. The interface between the Pt-aluminide bondcoating and the TGO contains  nanometric PtAl 2  precipitates ( Fig. 2b,e ) on the grain boundaries of the β phase ( Fig. 2f ).  Interestingly, the TGO formed on the PtAl 2  grains is very similar to that which formed on the  β phase in the Pt-aluminide bondcoating ( Fig. 2b ). Moreover, some nanometric inclusions of  Al 2 O 3  can be found in the subsurface layer of the Pt-aluminide bondcoating ( Fig. 2b ). Some  authors suggested that such particles originate from the grit blasting procedure performed  prior to coating deposition, however the samples analyzed in this study were not grit blasted.  It is thus suggested that such small particles are alumina nuclei that form during the annealing  treatment of the Pt and possibly also PdPt layers.  

3.1.3.   Cyclic oxidation test of Thermal Barrier Coatings on on Pt- and PdPt-aluminide  bondcoatings  

The cyclic oxidation test of the TBCs on the Pt- and PdPt-aluminide bondcoatings  studied in this paper was stopped after 1100 cycles at 1100 °C based on nondestructive  evaluation which indicated onset of the YSZ top coat delamination.  The cross-sectional  microstructures of the Pt- and PdPt-aluminide bondcoatings studied in this paper after     1100 cycles at 1100 °C are presented in Fig. 3a and b, respectively . The SEM images of  the YSZ / TGO / bondcoating interfaces are presented in  Fig. 3c and d  for the Pt- and PdPt- aluminide bondcoatings, respectively. The STEM-EDS elemental mapping from the grain  boundaries of the martensitic β’ phase in the Pt-aluminide bondcoating is presented in  Fig.  3e,f . The comparison of the chemical composition of the β’ and ɣ’-Ni 3 Al phases in the studied  bondcoatings  is presented in Tab. 2.  

After the cyclic oxidation test the TBC systems on both bondcoatings exhibhit a very  similar cross-sectional microstructure ( Fig. 3a,b ) and are composed of the YSZ top coat, the  TGO and the bondcoating. In both cases delamination has been observed at the interface  between the YSZ and the TGO which did not propagate far enough for the catastrophic  spallation to occur thus macroscopically no signs of delamination were observed in either  TBC system. The microstructure of both PdPt- and Pt-aluminide bondcoatings evolved during  thermal cycling i.a. due to diffusion processes including the outward diffusion of Al to the  growing TGOs as well as inward diffusion of Al to the substrate alloy. In etched  microstructures of the bondcoatings presented in  Fig. 3a,b  it is evident that majority of the  outer zones, which are around 115 µm, consists of the martensitic β’ grains with lamellar  structure and ɣ’-Ni 3 Al precipitates.

Underneath the outer zones both bondcoatings contain the internal zone where the  main consituent is the ɣ’-Ni 3 Al phase along with β’ phase and refractory element rich  precipitates ( Fig. 3a,b ). At the interface between the substrate René N5 and the bondcoatings  a so-called Substrate Diffusion Zone (SDZ) can be observed  [40] . The SEM analysis of the  interfaces between the PdPt- and Pt-aluminide bondcoatings and the corresponding TGOs  showed that the ɣ’-Ni 3 Al precipitated on the grain boundaries of the β’ phase. Moreover,  several types of TCP phases were observed both within the β’ grains as well as on the β’/β’  and β’/ɣ’-Ni 3 Al grain boundaries ( Fig. 3c,d ). A STEM-EDS elemental mapping ( Fig. 3e,f )  performed on one of the grain boundaries within the Pt-aluminide bondcoating revealed the  presence of regions containing segregated Cr, W, Co, Re, Mo, Ta and Hf. Such regions can  serve as a nucleation site of the ɣ’-Ni 3 Al in the Al-depleted bondcoatings. Moreover, the  mapping of Cr also revealed the presence of nanometric α-Cr precipitates that are known to  form within the martensitic β’ phase  [41] .  

The SEM investigation of the YSZ / TGO interfaces in the PdPt-aluminide and Pt- aluminide bondcoatings revealed that they are relatively flat similarly to the as-deposited  condition. Interestingly though the interface between the TGOs and the bondcoatings evolved  during the cyclic oxidation test from flat to a significantly convoluted one. The level of     convolution is somewhat higher in case of the Pt-aluminide where the TGO grows inwards  the bondcoating forming a type of anchorage. Little difference of the TGO morphology was  observed in the regions containing β’ and ɣ’-Ni 3 Al phases. Such features are present to a  smaller extent in case of the PdPt-aluminide bondcoating, however it is evident that the TGO  also grows inwards it in certain regions. Moreover, these differences in microstructures affect  also the thickness of the TGOs – 10 µm in the case of Pt-aluminide bondcoating and 8 µm on  the PdPt-aluminide.  

Chemical composition analysis of the PdPt- and Pt-aluminide bondcoatings after the  cyclic oxidation test showed that the β’ and ɣ’ phases present within them are very similar.  The differences between the β’ and ɣ’ phases are significant though. The content of Al in the  β’ phases in both bondcoatings is around 35 at. % which is typical for the L1 0  martensitic  phase  [42]  and it still provides a reservoir of Al enough to sustain the growth of protective  alumina. In contrast, the Al content in the ɣ’-Ni 3 Al is lower – around 23 at. %. The combined  content of Pd and Pt (around 3,5 at. %) in the β’ phase of the PdPt-aluminide is comparable to  the Pt content in that of the Pt-aluminide and indicates a significant loss of these elements  from the bondcoatings due to interdiffusion with the superalloy. A similar observation can be  made in case of the ɣ’-Ni 3 Al phase where the summarized content of Pd and Pt is around 1,4  at. %, in the case of the PdPt-aluminide, while the content of Pt in the Pt-aluminide is around  1,8 at. %. Another measurable difference between the β’ and ɣ’-Ni 3 Al phases is the lower  amount of Ta in the former - around 0,5 – 1,5 at. % compared to 3 at. % in the latter.

3.1.4.   Interfacial evolution in Thermal Barrier Coatings on Pt- and PdPt-aluminide  bondcoatings during the cyclic oxidation test: growth of the TGO  

In order to study the phenomena at the YSZ / TGO / bondcoating interface in detail cross- sectional STEM characterizations were performed in the areas marked in  Fig. 3c and d  for  TBC systems on Pt-aluminide and PdPt-aluminide bondcoatings, respectively. The STEM- HAADF image of the TGO formed on the PdPt-aluminide bondcoating is presented in  Fig. 4a   along with  the results of EDS elemental mapping of Ta, Y and Hf from the α-Al 2 O 3  grain  boundary ( Fig. 4b ) as well as its HRTEM image ( Fig. 4c ). The STEM-HAADF image of the  TGO formed on the Pt-aluminide bondcoating is presented in  Fig. 5a  along with elemental  mappings of Ta, Y, Hf and Zr on the α-Al 2 O 3  grain boundaries in the outer ( Fig. 5b)  and inner  part of the scale ( Fig. 5c ).  

As denoted in the previous section the thickness of the TGO after 1100 cycles at 1100  °C in the TBC system on the PdPt-aluminide bondcoating is around 8 μm while that on the Pt- aluminide is around 10 μm. The oxide scales are composed of typical columnar α-Al 2 O 3   grains that nucleated by oxidation reaction at the interface between the pre-oxidized scales  and the bondcoatings. Further, the TGOs grew by inward grain boundary diffusion of oxygen  ions thus no interfacial voids were found between it and the bondcoatings. During the long- term high temperature exposure no other phases were formed at the YSZ / TGO interfaces  which was the goal of the performed pre-oxidation treatment prior to deposition of the YSZ.  

The inward diffusion of O 2-  through the α-Al 2 O 3  grain boundaries in the TGOs can be  proven based on the STEM-HAADF microstructural images where the grain boundaries are  visible ( Fig. 4a and 5a ). It is evident that the thickness of the TGO depends whether it  measured within a single α-Al 2 O 3  grain or it is the grain triple junction where α-Al 2 O 3  grain  boundaries meet the interface of the bondcoating. The phenomenon of interface convolution  between the TGO and the bondcoating during the cyclic oxidation test observed using SEM  ( Fig. 3c,d ) for both bondcoatings can also be explained based on this observation. Since the  grain boundary diffusion of oxygen governs the growth of the TGO it is reasonable to  conclude the oxidation reaction occurs faster at those triple junctions compared to the regions  where single α-Al 2 O 3  grains meet the bondcoating. The higher level of convolution of the  TGO / Pt-aluminide interface observed in the SEM images ( Fig. 3c,d ) can be explained based  on the STEM investigations where the α-Al 2 O 3  on Pt-aluminide were narrower in the pre- oxidized state thus during further high temperature exposure more grain boundaries were  present, as evidenced in  Fig. 5a . The inward growth of the TGOs is also proven by the ɣ’  particles embedded within it both in the case of the PdPt-aluminide and the Pd-aluminide  bondcoatings. During further high temperature exposure the convex region of the  bondcoatings surrounded by the α-Al 2 O 3  grain boundaries would most probably also be  embedded in the TGO. Also, in the case of the PdPt-aluminide bondcoating the presence of  NiTa 2 O 6  was observed within the TGO indicating that its formation took place most probably  at the interface between the growing TGO and the bondcoating and was then embedded on the  α-Al 2 O 3  grain boundaries. As noted by Smialek et al.  [43]  the presence of this particular oxide  does not compromise the oxidation resistance of the TBC system and assists in tying up Ni in  dispersed, slowly growing fine particles.  

The Z 2  sensitive imaging performed using the STEM-HAADF detector allowed for the  detection of the reactive elements at the grain boundaries of the TGOs formed on both  bondcoatings. As a matter of fact, these grain boundaries are visible as brighter than the grains  themselves indicating the presence of elements with higher atomic number than Al and O  forming the scales. The STEM-EDS elemental mapping performed on the α-Al 2 O 3  grain  boundaries on both bondcoatings ( Fig. 4b and 5b,c ) revealed the presence of Y, Hf, Zr and  Ta. In the case of both bondcoatings the evidently higher intensity of Y on the grain  boundaries of the internal part of the scale can indicate a higher content of this element  compared to Hf and Ta. The signal of Ta on the grain boundaries of TGOs on both PdPt- and  Pt-aluminide is very low but discernible. An additional mapping performed on the grain  boundaries of the external (equiaxed) grain boundaries of the TGO formed on Pt-aluminide  revealed also the presence of Zr. However, as shown in  Fig. 5b , it most probably diffuses  from the YSZ particles incorporated in the TGO during the EB-PVD process. This result  proves the presence of ions on the grain boundaries or the nuclei of e.g. oxides containing Y,  Hf or Ta. In order to answer this issue the high resolution TEM (HRTEM) imaging was  performed in the region of the grain boundary analyzed in the case of the PdPt-aluminide,  presented in  Fig. 4c , where the (104) α  and (110) α  planes visible and marked. No additional  atomic planes were observed in the studied region thus it was concluded that the detected Y,  Hf and Ta are present in the form of ionic segregants. Moreover, grain boundary  disconnections were observed similar to those being currently the topic of research conducted  by Heuer and others [19].

4.   Discussion  

The application of palladium in the modification of aluminide coatings has not  yet been explored so well as in the case of platinum, however some research data is  available that allows summarizing its effect on the coatings high temperature behavior.  

Alperine et al. [44, 45] highlighted the need for adequate selection of processing  routes in the production of palladium coatings. Significant incorporation of hydrogen  into the coating can result from the application of hydrogen containing atmospheres or  NH 4 Cl activators in the aluminizing process. This leads to the formation of pores and  blisters compromising the oxidation and hot corrosion resistance of the coatings. Such  effect was not observed in the coatings studied in this paper as the deposition of Pd (as  well as Pt) was performed using a clean physical vapor deposition process without the  application of H 2  while the vapor phase aluminizing utilized argon atmosphere and an  aluminum fluoride activator instead of NH 4 Cl.  

The application of Pd instead of Pt involves an improvement in the control of the  aluminide coating’s microstructure. Palladium is believed [12, 46] to stabilize the β-NiAl  phase due to its high solubility in it [47]. Platinum compared to Pd has a lower solubility  in the β-NiAl phase and higher in the ɣ’-Ni 3 Al phase (42 at. % and 32 % at.,  respectively) [48] thus it appears to also increase the ɣ’ phase stability [49] more than  Pd. Furthermore, Pd does not form brittle phases with Al in aluminide coatings, such as  PtAl 2  in the case of Pt [10, 44] that can deteriorate the fatigue resistance of the coated  superalloy [44, 45, 50]. The results of this study as well as a previous one [41] showed  that simultaneous application of both Pt and Pd in different amounts can result in a  double-phase β-NiAl + PtAl 2  or a single-phase β-NiAl structure.  

Literature data is consistent that the modification of aluminide coatings with Pd  provides them with superior properties in terms of hot corrosion [45, 51] and cyclic  oxidation resistance [10]. While the oxidation kinetics of the (Ni,Pt)Al coatings were  found to be twice that of the NiAl coatings [52] there have been reports that Pd-modified  aluminide coatings exhibit similar mass gains as their Pt-modified counterparts [49].  The application of both Pt and Pd was found to provide superior cyclic oxidation     resistance over aluminide coatings modified only with Pt [10]. Interestingly, while the  effect of Pd on the scale adhesion was found to be poorer than that of Pt a synergistic  effect was reported for both these elements when combined with Hf [46]. A fundamental  difference between the effect of Pt and Pd on the oxidation mechanism of aluminide  coatings can be found based on the reports stating that the former delays [53] while the  latter facilitates [54] the θ-Al 2 O 3  to α-Al 2 O 3  transformation.  

As mentioned earlier the main issue defining the durability of thermal barrier coatings is  the adhesion of the ceramic top coat to the underlying substrate. It is influenced by many  factors including the microstructure at the interface between bondcoating and the top coat,  chemical and phase composition of the bondcoating as well as the growth mechanism of the  TGO. The critical factor is the ability of the bondcoating to form an adherent α-Al 2 O 3  scale  with negligible formation of transient oxides in the initial oxidation stage. The schematic  representation of the TGOs formed during pre-oxidation treatment at 1140 °C for 2h on the  Pt- and PdPt-aluminide bondcoatings is presented in  Fig. 6 .  

The matrix of the studied Pt- and PdPt-aluminide bondcoatings in the as-deposited  condition is the super stoic hio metric β-NiAl phase with PtAl 2  precipitates in the former type of  a bondcoating with a relatively high Al content of around 53 at. %. The schematic  representation of the TGO formed during the pre-oxidation treatment at 1140 °C for 2h is best  fit for the Pt-aluminide but the scale structure corresponds also to PdPt-aluminide  bondcoating. It was proven by STEM investigations that the microstructure of the TGO  formed on the PtAl 2  phase is identical to that on the β-NiAl phase owing to the similar Al  content.

Fig. 6.  Schematic representation of the Thermally Grown Oxide (TGO) formed during pre- oxidation treatment at 1140 °C for 2h on Pt- and PdPt-aluminide bondcoatings.  

The high Al content (about 52 at. %) in both studied bondcoatings, i.e. Pt- and PdPt- aluminide, as well as relatively high pre-oxidation temperature – 1140 °C made it possible to  obtain a continuous, compact and thin TGO layer prior to the deposition of the YSZ top coat.  The dominant constituent of the TGOs observed to form in both investigated TBC systems on  Pt- and PdPt-aluminide bondcoatings is the α polymorph of the Al 2 O 3  oxide. It is believed to  be the most desired phase due to its low growth rate governed by inward grain boundary  diffusion of oxygen  [32] . It has a beneficial influence on the bonding between the ceramic  topcoat and the TGO since the growth of the latter does not take place at that interface which  could lead to YSZ spallation. It is also noteworthy that no transient   - or ɣ-Al 2 O 3  polymorphs  were found in the as-deposited TBCs. It is in agreement with the results obtained by  Alvarado-Orozco et al.   [55]  who showed that at 1150 °C and under pO 2  = 0.21 atm the   - Al 2 O 3  is not stable and nucleation of α-Al 2 O 3  occurs from the very early stages of oxidation.  However, the formation of transient alumina polymorphs cannot be excluded since  nanometric porosities grains were found within the α-Al 2 O 3  originating most probably from     the volumetric changes occurring during the transformation to α-Al 2 O 3   [22, 23] . Most  importantly, no voids were found between the TGOs and the bondcoatings thus the interface  in the as-deposited state was continuous and free from defects. The strong influence of the  pre-oxidation temperature on the microstructure of the TGO can be seen when comparing  these results with the observations from  [41]  where the PdPt-aluminide bondcoating formed a  porous TGO with interfacial voids during pre-oxidation at a lower temperature of 1100 °C. It  is indeed the case since many papers showed that at higher temperatures the transformation  from transient   - to steady state α-Al 2 O 3  takes place faster up to 1200 °C where no    is  believed to form  [20, 23] . In other studies that applied lower pre-oxidation temperatures, e.g.  1100 °C, the obtained TGO contained equiaxed α-Al 2 O 3  with considerable porosity and Cr- rich precipitates indicating a prolonged presence of the transient polymorphs and their  subsequent transformation  [41, 56] . Experimental work performed by Spitsberg et al.  [31]   indicated that the grain structure of the TGO on Pt-aluminide bondcoatings can be also altered  by varying partial pressure of oxygen during pre-oxidation treatment above 1050 °C. They  have obtained very thin (0.1 µm) α-Al 2 O 3  grains with width ranging from 0.05 to 0.5 µm  depending on the pO 2  [31] . Another important factor is of course the chemistry of the  bondcoating – the Al content in particular. It was shown in  [57]  that low-Al Pt-modified ɣ +  ɣ’ (containing 8 and 18 at. % of Al, respectively) bondcoatings form a mixture of porous α- Al 2 O 3  and NiAl 2 O 4  when pre-oxidized under the same conditions as the Pt- and PdPt-  

aluminide bondcoatings studied in this paper (1140 °C for 2h). During thermal cycling the  subsequent growth of the spinel phase was found to contribute in TBC failure. Matsumoto et  al.  [36]  showed that in case of other low-Al bondcoating, namely CoNiCrAlY, the pre- oxidation under low pO 2  limited the formation of spinel phases, i.a. (Co,Ni)(Al,Cr) 2 O 4 , which  are detrimental for the durability of TBCs.  

The obtained results prove that the pre-oxidation at 1140 °C applied in this study is  high enough to provide α-Al 2 O 3  coverage on both Pt- and PdPt-aluminide bondcoatings while  simultaneously hindering the growth of transient polymorphs to a minimum. Moreover, at the  interface between the ceramic top coat and the TGO there was a clear boundary with no other  phases besides α-Al 2 O 3 . Other authors reported the formation of the so-called “mixed zone”  forming during the deposition process of the ceramic top coat between the growing alumina  oxides and the deposited ZrO 2  by one of the mechanisms described i.a. in  [58, 59] . Stiger et  al.  [28]  indicated that such a zone can be a weak link within the TGO layer where cracks can  initiate parallel to the YSZ / TGO interface. The results of the investigations performed within  this paper are in agreement with those obtained by Tolpygo et al.  [39]  who performed pre- oxidation treatment at 1150 °C for 5h on a Pt-aluminide bondcoating. These authors reported  the formation of a thin (0.4 µm) TGO with a characteristic ridged structure with no mixed- zone or interfacial void formation. The thicknesses of the TGOs obtained on both Pt- and  PdPt-aluminide bondcoatings studied in this paper were similar however their grain structure  differed. The application of the crystallographic orientation contrast imaging using the  STEM-DF detector allowed to observe that the grains of the α-Al 2 O 3  in the initial pre- oxidized TGO on the Pt-aluminide bondcoating were considerably narrower (over 3 times)  compared to that on the PdPt-aluminide. There is little empirical data on the influence of the  alumina grain structure on its growth kinetics but based on the transport mechanism of α- Al 2 O 3  scales it is reasonable to assume that the higher amount of short circuit diffusion paths  in the case of the narrower grains can lead to higher growth rates  [31, 37, 38, 60] . The reason  for this pronounced difference between the two bondcoatings may lie in their structure  especially at the interface with the oxidizing atmosphere. Based on the cross-sectional STEM- A DF images ( Fig. 2b,d ) it is evident that the Pt-aluminide is characterized by a finer structure  due to the presence of the PtAl 2  precipitates compared to the PdPt-aluminide where only the  β-NiAl phase was found in the subsurface region ( Fig. 2d ). The finer structure of the Pt-    aluminide bondcoating provides more grain boundaries for the outward diffusion of Al to the  oxidizing atmosphere thus more alumina nuclei can form on the surface during the pre- oxidation treatment.  

The detailed microstructural characterization of the TBCs on Pt- and PdPt-aluminide  bondcoatings after the cyclic oxidation test at 1100 °C allowed for the preparation of a  schematic representation of the TGO growth at the interface between the ceramic top coat and  the bondcoating presented in  Fig. 7 .  

![](images/33d020501ed7d2bdd65dcb0899c657a8b7a649eafe9807a8bdf81a433046a0e2.jpg)  
Fig. 7 . Schematic representation of the interfacial evolution in TBCs on Pt- and PdPt-  

aluminide bondcoatings during 1100 cycles at 1100 °C.  

During the cyclic oxidation test of the TBCs studied in this work the evolution of the  TGOs takes place at the interface between the bondcoatings and the oxide scale formed  during the pre-oxidation treatment. The growth mechanism of the TGOs is directly related to  the inward diffusion of oxygen along the grain boundaries of the α-Al 2 O 3  scale. Although the  outward diffusion of Al 3+  was proven to occur during the oxidation of similar bondcoatings  [61]  the STEM imaging results indicate that no microstructural evolution occurs at the  interface between the TGO and the YSZ top coat. Since the substrate alloy - René N5 contains  Hf, Y and Zr this may be related to the Reactive Element (RE) effect in the studied system  which will be addressed in the following part of this paper. During the initial stages of  oxidation of the TBCs the oxidation reaction at the interface between the pre-oxidized scale  and the bondcoatings leads to the homogeneous nucleation of new α-Al 2 O 3  grains. Further  inward of oxygen to the metal-scale interface allows for the newly nucleated grains to form  their columnar structure similar to those observed by Unocic et al. in modified aluminide  bondcoatings as well as Pt-ɣ/ɣ’ bondcoating  [62, 63] . Again, such a structure is also related to  the RE effect as indicated by Pint  [64] . Based on the comparison between the TGOs formed  on the Pt- and PdPt-aluminide bondcoatings it can be deduced that the coarser grains of the α-    Al 2 O 3  TGO in the initial condition of the TBC system on the PdPt-aluminide lead to the  formation of wider columnar grains during further oxidation. Since the thickness of the TGO  on the PdPt-aluminide was lower compared to that on the Pt-aluminide after 1100 hours of  oxidation it is reasonable to assume that the wider α-Al 2 O 3  grains provide less preferred  diffusion paths in the form of grain boundaries and thus a lower growth rate of the TGO.  Similar effect was observed for MCrAlY bondcoatings [65, 66]. Moreover, it was  reported [67] that the bigger the grain size of alumina in in-plane direction and the  thinner the oxide scale the easier it breaks.  The effect of the initial TGO microstructure on  its further development is also visible when comparing the TGO / bondcoating interface for  both Pt- and PdPt-aluminide. As described in the Results section the TGO / Pt-aluminide  interface is considerably more developed which is attributed to a higher amount of α-Al 2 O 3   grain boundaries. For example, a Pt-aluminide bondcoating oxidized for 900 1h cycles as well  as Pt-ɣ/ɣ’ oxidized for 1500 1h cycles at at 1150 °C  [62]  maintained a flat interface with the  TGO. Similarly, the PdPt-modified bondcoating pre-oxidized at 1100 °C and then oxidized at  the same temperature for 430 1h cycles did not exhibit any convolution at that interface. This  indicates the strong influence of the pre-oxidation treatment parameters on the microstructure  of the TGO during the whole TBC life. These parameters are not completely decisive though.  It was shown that Al-lean bondcoatings such as Pt-ɣ/ɣ’ pre-oxidized at 1140 °C for 4h form  porous initial TGO  [57]  and evolve forming a flat TGO / bondcoating interface.  

The behavior of the Reactive Elements   [68]  during high temperature oxidation was  studied using STEM-EDS elemental mappings of the grain boundaries in the TGOs formed in  TBCs on Pt- and PdPt-aluminide bondcoatings. The comparative analysis of the results  obtained in this study and previous studies performed on bare  [69]  as well as Pt-diffused René  N5 superalloy  [57]  showed a distinct difference in the segregation processes of Hf, Y and Zr.  The TGOs that formed during the cyclic oxidation test at 1100 °C in the studied TBCs on     both Pt- and PdPt-aluminide bondcoatings were characterized by a considerably lower EDS  signal from Hf as well as lack of Zr signal. These results are different from those obtained for  uncoated René N5 superalloy after oxidation at 1100 °C, where Hf segregation was clearly  visible in the STEM-EDS images showing its distribution along α-Al 2 O 3  grain boundaries  [69,  70] . Based on this observation it is suggested that the aluminizing process hinders the outward  supply of Hf from the superalloy during the oxidation process and has no measureable effect  on Y. It was shown that both Pt and PdPt modified aluminide bondcoatings exhibit similar  effect on RE. Moreover, a clearly visible segregation of Hf was observed in TGO formed on a  Pt-ɣ/ɣ’ bondcoatings where no aluminizing was applied thus the presence of Pt does not affect  the outward diffusion of RE  [57, 62] .  

In the case of the TGO formed on the Pt-modified aluminide bondcoating the  segregation of Hf, Y and Zr was observed on the grain boundaries of the equiaxed α-Al 2 O 3  grains in the outer zone of the scale – near the interface with the YSZ top coat. It is in contrast  to the results obtained from the columnar grains where no Zr was found. The source of Hf is  admittedly the substrate René N5 superalloy where its content is 0.15 wt. %. In the case of Y  previous studies have indicated that its inward diffusion from the ceramic top coat to the TGO  is not energetically favorable  [64] . Also the STEM characterizations of the substrate René N5  superalloy oxidized in the range from 1050 to 1150 °C indicated that even at low Y contents  (2<ppm) this element diffuses along the alumina grain boundaries during high temperature  oxidation  [69, 70] . No segregation of Zr was found in the columnar grains of the TGOs  formed on either Pt- or PdPt-aluminide coatings while its content in the superalloy is around  200 ppm. The only sign of Zr segregation was found in the equiaxed grains of the TGO on the  Pt-aluminide bondcoating where particles of zirconia were embedded in the initial TGO  during the EB-PVD process and served as sources for its outward diffusion in the oxygen  potential gradient which is in agreement with the results of others  [41, 70] . In fact, the     substrate alloy René N5 used in this study was found to form alumina scale with Zr  segregation at 1150 °C  [69] .  

# 5.   Summary and concluding remarks  

The results presented in this work concerned the interfacial phenomena that occur  during the pre-oxidation treatment at 1140 °C for 4h in air atmosphere and further cyclic  oxidation at 1100 °C of TBCs on Pt- and PdPt-aluminide bondcoatings up to 1100 cycles.  

It was shown that during pre-oxidation at 1140 °C for 2h in air atmosphere the Pt- and  PdPt-aluminide bondcoatings form TGOs that are 425±47 and 375±54 nm thick, respectively,  and consist of dense α-Al 2 O 3  grains with minor internal porosity thus its further growth  occurrs only by the grain boundaries. The applied pre-oxidation treatment limited the  formation of transient alumina polymorphs therefore the interface at the TGO / bondcoating is  continuous and free from voids that could compromise the whole TBC system. Moreover, no  mixed zone was formed at the YSZ / TGO interface which is attributed to the formation of α- Al 2 O 3  scale prior to YSZ deposition. It was found that the TGO formed on the PdPt-aluminide  was characterized by measurably wider grains (770 nm) compared to the Pt-aluminide  (240nm). Such a structure of the pre-formed scales influenced the evolution of the TGOs  during further cyclic oxidation test. During the cyclic oxidation test the TGO grew in the form  of columnar α-Al 2 O 3  grains that nucleated by oxidation reaction at the interface between the  pre-formed TGOs and the bondcoatings. The TGOs on both bondcoatings were found to grow  deeper at triple junctions at the interface with the bondcoatings which is attributed to inward  diffusion of oxygen and such a convoluted interface is believed to provide a type of  mechanical keying of the TGO to the substrate. Moreover, the TGO on the PdPt-aluminide  was found to be somewhat thinner as a result of a smaller amount of grain boundaries due to  wider α-Al 2 O 3  grains.  

The analysis of α-Al 2 O 3  grain boundaries in TGOs formed on both bondcoatings  revealed the segregation of Y and Hf that occurs during the cyclic oxidation test. Based on  high resolution investigations it was suggested that the RE are present in the form of ionic  segregants on grain boundary disconnections. Moreover, it was found that in the presence of  the aluminide bondcoating (whether it is Pt or PdPt modified one) the Hf segregation is  hindered compared to bare René N5 superalloy. It was concluded that this effect is due to the  presence of the aluminide coating and not the modification itself. The segregation of Zr was  found in the upper part of the TGO on Pt-aluminide bondcoating however its presence is  attributed to local incorporation of the YSZ particles within the TGO which served as an  additional source of Zr.

# Highlights  

Microstructural characterization of interfaces in EB-PVD TBCs using STEM is presented  Pre-oxidation in air at 1140 °C forms α-Al 2 O 3  TGO on modified aluminide bondcoatings  PdPt-aluminide bondcoatings form thinner TGO with wider α-Al 2 O 3  grains than Pt-aluminide  Segregation of Y and Hf to α-Al 2 O 3  grain boundaries in TGO is evidenced using STEM  Aluminide bondcoatings hinder the segregation of Hf to α-Al 2 O 3  grain boundaries
