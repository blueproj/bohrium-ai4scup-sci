# A B S T R A C T  

# A R T I C L E I N F O  

This paper presents results from a research initiative aimed at the evolution of secondary  γ ′  and its e ﬀ ects on hot deformation behavior by using compression tests of a supersolvus-treated superalloy at 1080 – 1200 °C and 0.001 – 1 s − 1 . Secondary  γ ′  in deformed regions and in dynamic recrystallization (DRX) regions have di ﬀ erent evolution behaviors during hot working. In deformed regions precipitates have a noteworthy coarsening ten- dency depending on strain rates. At high strain rates, e.g. at 1 s − 1 ,  γ ′  is elongated along strain direction by plastic deformation. Whereas, directional coarsening takes place at low strain rates, e.g. 0.001 s − 1 , owing to the severe plastic deformation and the high concentration level of  γ ′ -forming element induced by supersolvus treatment. And thus secondary  γ ′  evolution in deformed regions can be attributed to two competing factors: (a) plastic  ﬂ ow and (b) directional coarsening induced by di ﬀ usion. In contrast,  γ ′  in DRX regions always dissolves following grain boundary migration and almost re-precipitates at boundaries. Only a small number of re-precipitated  γ ′  can be found in the interior of new grains. Abnormal phenomenon of sharp stress decline appears at low strain rates and low deformation temperatures, which is related to DRX,  γ ′  directional coarsening and  γ ′  dissolution.

# 1. Introduction  

Precipitation strengthened nickel-base superalloys, a typical multi- phase alloys endowed with excellent mechanical properties and cor- rosion resistances at elevated temperature, have been widely used in power generation and aerospace industries [ 1 – 3 ]. These alloys have a microstructure consisting of numerous ordered  γ ′ -Ni 3 Al precipitates coherently embedded in face-centered cubic  γ  matrix [ 4 ]. Among of them, advanced superalloys with more than 40 vol% of  γ ′  are being developed and applied for improvements in engine e ﬃ ciency and ser- vice temperature [ 5 ].  

It is well known that grain boundary migration can be restricted by particles through a pinning pressure referred as Zener drag [ 12 , 13 ]. For the incoherent  γ ′  precipitate so-called as primary  γ ′ , it may either in- hibit grain growth or be bypassed by the grain boundary, according to the driving force of boundary motion [ 8 ]. However, coherently pre- cipitated  γ ′  has a lower  γ ′ / γ  interface energy and exists a higher Zener pinning force for a moving grain boundary [ 14 ]. If a coherent pre- cipitate interacts with a high angle grain boundary, the particle inter- face would transform into an incoherent interface when the grain boundary crossed [ 13 ]. These particles are unstable and may be ac- companied by various evolutions, including rotation, dissolution, and re-precipitation, during the passage of the grain boundary [ 6 – 8 ].  

In order to obtain excellent comprehensive performances for a speci ﬁ c application,  γ ′  characteristics, including volume fraction, size distribution and morphology, are usually controlled and optimized by a scheduled heat treatment. Beyond that,  γ ′  precipitates are considered crucial as well in determining the hot deformation behavior of super- alloys. Microstructure evolution of superalloys operated at  γ  +  γ ′  du- plex phase  ﬁ eld is strongly in ﬂ uenced by  γ ′  [ 6 , 7 ]. Hot forming opera- tions are therefore carefully developed upon the  γ ′  solvus temperature to optimize the grain size of  γ  matrix as well as to attain the target  

Clearly,  γ ′  undergoes signi ﬁ cant and complex transformation as encountering with a moving boundary. Yet, the evolution behavior of  γ ′ in deformed and un re crystallized regions is rarely reported and remains unaccounted for. Therefore, the purpose of this work is to characterize the  γ ′  evolution in a turbine disc superalloy EP741NP during hot de- formation, and the evolution mechanism of  γ ′  in deformed regions is emphatically investigated. In addition, the in ﬂ uence of  γ ′  evolution on the hot deformation behavior is discussed.  

# 2. Experimental procedures  

Nickel-base superalloy EP741NP powder fabricated through plasma rotating electrode process (PREP) was utilized in this study. The che- mical composition (wt. %) is as follows: 8.9 Cr, 15.8 Co, 5.7 W, 4.0 Mo, 5.0 Al, 1.8 Ti, 2.6 Nb, 0.3 Hf, 0.014 Zr, 0.01 B and Ni balance. Complete densi ﬁ ed compact was obtained through hot isostatic pressing (HIP) at 1200 °C for 2 h under a pressure of 140 MPa, followed by furnace cooling. Because the  γ ′  solvus temperature of EP741NP is between 1180 °C and 1190 °C [ 15 ], HIP sintered compact was then supersolvus- treated at 1200 °C for 2 h followed by air cooling to room temperature, to dissolve large  γ ′  precipitates formed during furnace cooling after HIP.  

Cylindrical specimens prepared for compression, with 8 mm in diameter and 12 mm in height, were machined from the heat-treated bulk. Isothermal uni-axial compression tests were performed by a Gleeble-3800 thermal/mechanical simulator at temperatures of 1080, 1120, 1160 and 1200 °C and strain rates of 0.001, 0.01, 0.1 and 1 s − 1 , respectively. Before compression tests all specimens were heated to pre- set deformation temperatures at a heating rate of 10 °C/s and were held for 3 min to gain the uniform temperature. All specimens were sub- jected to 40% height reduction and subsequently cooled to room tem- perature within 10 s by forced-air cooling. Additionally, heat treatment at 1120 °C/3 min was carried out for comparison.  

For metallographic observation, specimens were polished to 0.5  μ m diamond  ﬁ nish and chemically etched by Kalling's etchant or electro- chemically etched by the solution of 170 ml phosphoric acid +10 ml vitriolic acid +15 g chromium trioxide at room temperature. Thin foils for transmission electron microscope (TEM) were prepared by double jet electro-polishing using a solution of 8 ml perchloric acid + 92 ml alcohol at - 30 °C. Microstructure examinations were obtained by op- tical microscope (OM), scanning electron microscope (SEM), electron backscatter di ﬀ raction (EBSD) and TEM. Step sizes used for EBSD col- lection of deformed specimens were 1  μ m and 0.2  μ m, respectively, for di ﬀ erent observation scales. All of microstructures were examined from the center of deformed/heat-treated samples.  

# 3. Results  

# 3.1. Microstructure before compression  

Fig. 1  shows the microstructure of as-HIPed, supersolvus-treated and subsolvus-treated specimens, respectively. As a kind of superalloy containing 43 wt % alloying element and \~60 vol% of  γ ′  [ 16 ], grain boundary migration of superalloy EP741NP is inhibited by solute drag and/or  γ ′  Zener pinning. The grain size merely increases from 43  μ m to 47  μ m during supersolvus treated at 1200 °C for 2 h and makes no dif- ference after 1120 °C/3 min subsolvus treatment.  

Compared to the relatively stable  γ  matrix,  γ ′  precipitates vary with the thermal history. Both large square and octet shaped  γ ′  particles (in the inset of  Fig. 1 a) are formed during the furnace cooling process following HIP. These furnace cooling formed  γ ′  can be completely dissolved after heat treated at 1200 °C for 2 h, as shown in the inset of Fig. 1 b. Nevertheless, because of the high content of  γ ′  forming ele- ments including Al and Ti [ 2 ], numerous small granular  γ ′  with \~80 nm precipitate from the  γ  matrix during cooling from 1200 °C to room temperature, and these small granular particles are de ﬁ ned as cooling γ ′ .  

Fig. 1 c gives the microstructure obtained from subsolvus treatment at 1120 °C/3 min, which is corresponding to the microstructure before compression at 1120 °C (below  γ ′  solvus). In comparison with small granular precipitates in  Fig. 1 b, there are lots of square particles with \~200 nm in the inset of  Fig. 1 c, which re ﬂ ects that  γ ′  rapidly pre- cipitates and grows up during the heating process operated below  γ ′ solvus line. In order to di ﬀ er from cooling  γ ′ , these square ones are classi ﬁ ed as secondary  γ ′ . In fact, cooling  γ ′  does not exist at de- formation temperatures for its special forming mechanism and thus has no in ﬂ uence on hot deformation behavior. Thereby, our study is focus on the secondary  γ ′ .  

# 3.2. Flow curves  

Fig. 2  illustrates true strain-true stress curves at di ﬀ erent compres- sion conditions. All curves exhibit a typical dynamic recrystallization (DRX) type  ﬂ ow stress with a peak stress followed by a gradual fall. Usually, working hardening is mainly induced by the dislocation in- teraction and multiplication and the generation of poorly developed subgrain boundaries [ 17 ]. Dislocation accumulation can be enhanced at higher strain rates and lower temperatures for a suppressed DRX soft- ening process. The  ﬂ ow stress level therefore increases with the in- creasing strain rates and decreases with the rise of deformation tem- peratures.  

Fig. 3  is the plot of peak stress ( σ p ) varying with strain rates ( ε ), which shows a notable di ﬀ erence in slope in the relationship with the  γ ′ solvus. At temperature above  γ ′  solvus, such as at 1200 °C, work hardening depends on dislocations and subgrains formed in single  γ phase. While hot working operated at the temperature lower than  γ / γ ′ solvus line, secondary  γ ′  would dispersedly precipitate from the matrix during the operation process for studied alloy. These second-phase particles inhibit dislocation motion [ 14 ]. Dislocation accumulation is therefore strongly enhanced in duplex  γ  +  γ ′  ﬁ eld, resulting in a dif- ferent mechanism on working hardening.  

It is interesting that  ﬂ ow curves obtained at subsolvus temperatures and low strain rates, such as at 1120 °C/0.001 s − 1   and 1080 °C/0.001 s − 1 , decline sharply following peak stress.

# 4. Discussion  

# 4.1. Evolution mechanism of secondary  γ ′  in deformed regions  

γ ′  directional coarsening or rafting frequently appears in single- crystal superalloys su ﬀ ering from creep deformation [ 23 – 25 ]. Owing to the applied loading, chemical potentials of atoms present subtle dif- ference in directions perpendicular to and parallel to the external stress, which act as driving forces for di ﬀ usion  ﬁ nally leading to  γ ′  raft formed as type P (parallel) coarsening or type N (normal) coarsening [ 26 , 27 ]. The morphology and kinetics of rafting are generally ruled by two key factors: (a)  γ / γ ′  mis ﬁ t and (b) applied stress [ 28 , 29 ]. This rafting pro- cess is commonly accomplished after creeping for hours or more.  

Chemical potential gradients in di ﬀ erent directions can be ampli ﬁ ed by a higher applied load, which seems to be related to a higher rafting

According to the  “ elastic-plastic ”  model, plastic  ﬂ ow and disloca- tions on the  γ / γ ′  interfaces are considered crucial in determining the kinetic of directional coarsening [ 31 ], and di ﬀ erences between elastic constants of  γ  and  γ ′  phases are considered as a second order e ﬀ ect [ 29 ]. Dislocation contents exist variations in di ﬀ erent types of  γ  chan- nels [ 32 ]. Similarly, during hot deformation  γ  channels parallel to the compression direction may undergo more plastic deformation as dis- locations prefer the horizontal channels, which enhances the accumu- lation of dislocations in the  γ / γ ′  interface that verticals to compression direction (in  Fig. 9 a). This di ﬀ erence of interface dislocations provides the driving force to promote the migration of solute atoms from the horizontal to the vertical channel. Moreover, dislocations and disloca- tion walls in  γ  matrix (in  Fig. 9 b) that induces by heavy plastic de- formation o ﬀ er additional di ﬀ usion paths for solutes.  

Notably, the studied alloy is supersolvus treated before hot de- formation, which always relates to a high  γ ′ -forming solute con- centration level and results in a rapid precipitation behavior of  γ ′ , as shown in  Figs. 1c and 5 . This supersaturated  γ  matrix is of importance on the directional coarsening process as well, due to the dependence of directional coarsening rate on the solute concentration [ 33 ]. As a consequence,  γ ′  directional coarsening is encouraged at some strain rates for a few minutes (e.g. at 0.001 s − 1   for \~15 min in this work), under the combined in in ﬂ uence of plastic deformation and super- saturated matrix. This kind of directional coarsening parallel to the compression axis is classi ﬁ ed as type P coarsening [ 27 ].  

In conclusion, as illustrated in  Fig. 10 , during hot working the morphology of  γ ′  precipitate is mainly controlled by two strain rate governing factors: (1) plastic  ﬂ ow and (2) directional coarsening.  

For hot working carried out at high strain rate, such as at 1 s − 1 , di ﬀ usion controlled directional  γ ′  coarsening is suppressed for a short deformation time. Secondary precipitates mainly su ﬀ er from disloca- tion shearing during hot deformation, as shown in  Fig. 11  γ ′  mor- phology is determined by the heavy plastic  ﬂ ow of  γ  +  γ ′  at high strain rate, leading to the precipitate elongated along strain direction.  

Conversely, plastic  ﬂ ow e ﬀ ect can be o ﬀ set by the directional coarsening at low strain rate. As shown in  Figs. 9 and 11 , the major deformation mechanism transforms from  γ ′  shearing to Orowan looping, as the strain rate decreasing to 0.001 s − 1 . Dislocations are accumulated at  γ ′ / γ  interface. These  γ -channel dislocations together with the supersaturated  γ  matrix signi ﬁ cantly reduce the time for rafting [ 32 ]. On the other hand, a lower strain rate provides a longer time for  γ ′ -forming solute di ﬀ usion from the vertical  γ -channel to the horizontal  γ -channel, resulting in an encouraged directional coarsening process. Type P coarsening is therefore developed at low strain rate as shown in  Fig. 5 b and d.

# 4.2. The interaction of secondary  γ ′  and DRX  

Dispersed  γ ′  particles are of great importance on the microstructure evolution of superalloys operated at  γ  +  γ ′  two-phase region for Zener pinning. The magnitude of this pinning e ﬀ ect is related to the size, shape, volume fraction and spacing of  γ ′  [ 14 ].  γ ′  particles generated from the liquid and those precipitated from the matrix have di ﬀ erent coherent relationships with  γ  matrix, which a ﬀ ect the interaction of  γ ′ and grain boundary migration as well. Primary  γ ′  particles, mainly with 1 – 5  μ m in diameter and incoherent with the  γ  matrix, exert a retarding force on moving boundaries and always exist at triple junctions or within grains [ 2 , 8 , 34 ]. However, secondary  γ ′  coherently precipitated from  γ  matrix possess a lower  γ ′ / γ  interface energy and therefore exist a higher Zener pinning force than incoherent particles [ 14 ]. Secondary  γ ′ in grain boundaries in  Fig. 12  con ﬁ rms that these particles restrict the growth of new grains. So both incoherent and coherent  γ ′  make sense to optimize the grain size [ 35 ], which results in hot forming operations of superalloys commonly performed below  γ ′  solvus temperature [ 8 ].  

DRX and grain growth, in turn, have an impact on the evolution of  γ ′ as well. Coherent  γ ′  precipitates in the original matrix will lose coher- ency after the passage of new grain boundaries [ 14 ]. These incoherent particles are less stable, and thus they may dissolve and re-precipitate during grain boundary migration. Whereas, plenty of secondary  γ ′  appear and su ﬀ er signi ﬁ cant evolution in  γ  +  γ ′  ﬁ eld at 1080 °C and 1120 °C. At low strain rates  γ ′  directional coarsening in deformed regions and  γ ′  dissolution in DRX regions concurrently occur, which contribute to deteriorate the  ﬂ ow stress as well. Consequently, secondary  γ ′  evolution enhances the softening process in combination with DRX, resulting in a sharp stress decline following peak stress. In contrast, at high strain rates both di ﬀ usion induced directional coar- sening and DRX are inhibited, leading to a normal gradient stress de- cline.  

# 5. Conclusions  

In this work, dynamic revolution of  γ ′  phase and its e ﬀ ect on hot deformation behavior of a high  γ ′  volume fraction superalloy were in- vestigated by hot compression at 1080 – 1200 °C and 0.001 – 1 s − 1 . The results could be summarized as follows:  

1. The evolution of secondary  γ ′  is mainly controlled by two competing factors: (a) plastic  ﬂ ow and (b) directional coarsening.

 2. Supersolvus treatment provides a supersaturated  γ  matrix with high solute concentration level, and therefore assists  γ ′  in deformed re- gions to manifest directional coarsening in a few minutes together with  γ -channel dislocations.

 3. Secondary  γ ′  inhibits the growth of DRX and would dissolve after encountering new grain boundaries.  γ ′  re-precipitation prefers to discontinuously occur at grain boundaries and only tiny of that generates in the inner of new grains.

 4. Strengthening mechanism of secondary  γ ′  transforms from shearing to looping, as strain rates decreasing from 0.1 s − 1   to 0.001 s   − 1 .

 5. Hot deformation performed at low strain rates and low temperatures exert sharp stress decline following peak stress, under the combined in ﬂ uence of DRX,  γ ′  directional coarsening in deformed regions and γ ′  dissolution in DRX regions.
