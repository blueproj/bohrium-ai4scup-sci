# Character is ation of cerivastatin as a P-glycoprotein substrate: studies in P-glycoprotein-expressing cell monolayers and mdr1a/b knock-out mice  

Received: 11 April 2004 / Accepted: 26 May 2004 / Published online: 30 July 2004 #  Springer-Verlag 2004  

Abstract  The aim of this study was to characterise the role of the efflux transporter P-glycoprotein in the disposition of cerivastatin. We investigated directional 14 transport characteristics of [ C]cerivastatin across cell monolayers expressing P-glycoprotein (Caco-2 and L- MDR1) and disposition of cerivastatin in mice with disrupted  mdr1a  and  mdr1b  genes. The mice were given orally 1 mg/kg cerivastatin and plasma and tissue samples for analysis of cerivastatin were obtained 10, 20, or 30 min after drug administration. Four knock-out mice and four wild-type mice were studied at each time point. In addition, the hypothesis that gemfibrozil-mediated inhibi- tion of P-glycoprotein contributes to the interaction between gemfibrozil and cerivastatin was tested in Caco- 2 cells. The apparent permeability coefficient ( P app ) value for the basal-to-apical transport of cerivastatin in Caco-2 and L-MDR1 cell monolayers was 2.4 times ( P <0.001) and 3.8 times ( P <0.001) as high as the apical-to-basal  P app value respectively. The P-glycoprotein inhibitor PSC-833 (1  μ M) inhibited the net basal-to-apical transport of cerivastatin in Caco-2 monolayers by 35% ( P <0.01) and the MRP inhibitor MK-571 (10  μ M) by 50% ( P <0.01). At concentrations up to 250  μ M, gemfibrozil showed no significant effects on the net transport of cerivastatin in Caco-2 cells. The concentration of cerivastatin in the brain at 30 min was 3.1 times higher in the knock-out mice than in the wild-type mice ( P <0.05). The brain-to-plasma cerivastatin concentration ratio at 20 min and 30 min was 2.1 ( P <0.05) and 3.6 times ( P <0.05) higher respectively in the knock-out animals compared with the wild-type animals. Collectively, these results indicate that cerivastatin is a P-glycoprotein substrate, although other transporters probably contribute to cerivastatin transport in humans. As several statins are P-glycoprotein substrates, beneficial as well as adverse effects of the statins might be affected by interindividual differences in P-glycoprotein expression or function caused by, e.g., the  MDR1  poly- morphism.

# Introduction  

Cerivastatin was withdrawn from the market in 2001 because its use had been associated with a relatively high number of cases of severe myopathy and rhabdomyolysis (Staffa et al.  2002 ). This adverse reaction is a class effect of the statins and can be induced by any statin (Omar et al. 2001 ; Evans and Rees  2002 ). Although a clinically significant myopathy is a rare event under statin therapy (Gaist et al.  2001 ), this reaction is of considerable importance in light of the wide and increasing use of the statins. The mechanisms of statin-induced myopathy are yet to be clarified (Thompson et al.  2003 ), but certain drugs are known to increase the risk of myopathy by increasing plasma statin concentrations. For example, CYP3A4 inhibitors greatly elevate concentrations of statins that undergo significant CYP3A4-mediated first- pass metabolism (Neuvonen et al.  1998 ). In addition, high statin doses are associated with an increased frequency of musculoskeletal adverse events (Evans and Rees  2002 ).  

There are no published studies regarding the role of the efflux transporter P-glycoprotein in the transport of cerivastatin. P-glycoprotein, which is expressed in the enterocytes, liver, kidney as well as in the capillary endothelial cells of the blood – brain barrier, plays an important role in the disposition of a large number of drugs (Fromm  2002 ). In addition, polymorphisms of the gene encoding P-glycoprotein,  MDR1 , have been recently shown to contribute to interindividual variability in the p harm a co kinetics of P-glycoprotein substrates (Schwab et al.  2003 ; Marzolini et al.  2004 ). Co-administration of gemfibrozil is known to increase the risk of statin-induced myopathy (Omar et al.  2001 ; Shek and Ferrill  2001 ). Fibrates such as gemfibrozil also possess myotoxic effects (Gaist et al.  2001 ) and the increased risk of myopathy during co-administration of gemfibrozil and a statin may be partly explained by additive toxic effects on skeletal muscle. However, recent studies indicate that gemfibrozil is capable of increasing the plasma concentrations of several statins, including cerivastatin (Backman et al. 2000 ,  2002 ; Kyrklund et al.  2001 ). The inhibitory effects of gemfibrozil on enzymes involved in the biotransforma- tion of cerivastatin, CYP2C8 (Prueksaritanont et al. 2002a , 2002b ; Wang et al.  2002 ) and UDP-glucuronosyl- transferases (UGTs) (Prueksaritanont et al.  2002a , 2002b ), have been suggested to contribute to this interaction. However, the possibility that gemfibrozil might affect the p harm a co kinetics of cerivastatin and other statins by modulating the function of drug transporters such as P- glycoprotein and OATP1B1 (OATP2/OATP-C; Shitara et al.  2003 ) remains to be investigated.  

The aim of this study was to characterise the role of P- glycoprotein in the disposition of cerivastatin, using P- glycoprotein-expressing Caco-2 and L-MDR1 cell mono- layers and the  mdr1a/b  knock-out mouse model, which has to our knowledge not been previously applied to any statin. One major objective was to examine whether cerivastatin concentrations are higher in the skeletal muscle of  mdr1a/b  knock-out mice compared with wild- type mice. In addition, the hypothesis that gemfibrozil- mediated inhibition of P-glycoprotein contributes to the pharmacokinetic interaction between gemfibrozil and cerivastatin was investigated in Caco-2 monolayers.  

# Materials and methods  

3 Materials  [ H]Digoxin (19 Ci/mmol) was supplied by 14 NEN Life Science Products (Boston, MA, USA). [ C] Cerivastatin (sodium salt of cerivastatin acid) (55 Ci/mol) and unlabeled cerivastatin sodium were generous gifts from Bayer (Wuppertal, Germany). Gemfibrozil was obtained from Sigma-Aldrich Corporation (St Louis, MO, USA) and MK-571 sodium from Alexis Biochem- icals (Grünberg, Germany). PSC-833 (valspodar) was a kind gift from Novartis (Basel, Switzerland).  

Drug transport in cultured Caco-2, L-MDR1, and LLC- PK1 cells  Caco-2 cells are a human colon carcinoma cell line and constitutively express P-glycoprotein. LLC-PK1 is a porcine kidney-derived cell line and L-MDR1 cells are LLC-PK1 cells stably transfected with human  MDR1 cDNA and thus expressing human P-glycoprotein. L- MDR1 and LLC-PK1 cells were kindly provided by Dr A. H. Schinkel (Netherlands Cancer Institute, Amsterdam, the Netherlands) and Caco-2 cells were obtained from the American Type Culture Collection (Rockville, MD, USA). Caco-2, L-MDR1, and LLC-PK1 cells become polarised when grown as a monolayer on semiporous filters, and Caco-2 and L-MDR1 cells express P-glycoprotein on their apical surface. Cells were plated onto Transwell filters (Costar; Cambridge, MA, USA) and grown as previously described (Pauli-Magnus et al.  2000 ). Transport experi- ments were performed on day 7 after plating. About 1 h before the start of a transport experiment, the medium in each of the two compartments of a well was replaced by serum-free medium (OPTIMEM 1; Invitrogen Corpora- tion, Paisley, UK). OPTIMEM 1 is a modification of Eagle ’ s minimal essential medium, buffered with HEPES and sodium bicarbonate, and supplemented with hypo- xanthine, thymidine, sodium pyruvate,  L -glutamine, trace elements, growth factors, insulin and transferrin. In addition, transepithelial resistance was measured in each well; only wells registering a resistance of 200  Ω  or greater, after correcting for the resistance obtained in control blank wells, were used in transport experiments. The transport of cerivastatin and digoxin was measured after replacing the medium in one compartment with 800  μ l of OPTIMEM 1 containing [ 14 C]cerivastatin (0.9  μ M, representing an estimated concentration at the 3 enterocytes after oral administration to humans) or [ H] digoxin (5  μ M). These drug concentrations represent theoretical (calculated) initial concentrations. Experiments performed to validate the drug transport model showed that adsorption of cerivastatin or digoxin onto the device was not a problem. When medium containing the drug was applied on the basal side of the monolayer, drug-free medium was applied to the apical side, and vice versa. Each drug application constituted one experiment. The amount of drug (radioactivity) appearing in the opposite compartment of a well (basal or apical side of the monolayer) after 1, 2, 3, and 4 h was measured in aliquots of 25  μ l after the addition of 5 ml of Aquasafe 300 Plus scintillation fluid (Zinsser Analytic, Frankfurt, Germany). The measured radioactivity was expressed as the fraction (in %) of total radioactivity added to the donor compart- ment at the beginning of the experiment. Inhibition of drug transport was determined in a similar manner after the addition of gemfibrozil (10 – 250  μ M, representing a range of clinically relevant concentrations), the P-glycoprotein inhibitor PSC-833 (1  μ M), or the MRP (multidrug resistance protein) inhibitor MK-571 (10  μ M) to both the basal and apical side of the monolayer before addition of the radiolabelled substrate drug. These concentrations of PSC-833 and MK-571 represent estab- lished concentrations to study inhibition of P-glycoprotein and MRP, respectively. Digoxin, a prototypical P-glyco- protein substrate, was used as a positive control for P- glycoprotein-mediated transport. Net basal-to-apical drug transport was calculated at 4 h by subtracting the apical-to- basal transport rate from the basal-to-apical transport rate. To study the possible effects of dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO, used to dissolve gemfibrozil) on drug transport, the transport of cerivastatin and digoxin was examined in the presence of 2.5% DMSO (the final DMSO concentra- tion in experiments with the highest gemfibrozil concen- tration). DMSO did not influence the transport of cerivastatin and digoxin (data not shown). With PSC- 833 and MK-571, final concentrations of organic solvents were <0.1%.  

Determination of tissue distribution of cerivastatin in mdr1a/b ( − / − ) and mdr1a/b (+/+) mice  Male  mdr1a/b  ( − / − ) mice, 12 weeks of age, and genetically matched male mdr1a/b  (+/+) mice of the same age in an FVB background were obtained from Taconic Farms (German- town, NY, USA). In mice, two genes,  mdr1a  and  mdr1b , encode drug-transporting P-glycoproteins and together appear to fulfil the same function as  MDR1  in humans (Schinkel et al.  1997 ). These genes have been disrupted in mdr1a/b  ( − / − ) mice. The disposition of cerivastatin in mdr1a/b  ( − / − ) and  mdr1a/b  (+/+) mice was investigated 10, 20, and 30 min after oral administration of cerivastatin. Four knock-out mice and four wild-type mice were studied at each time point. After an overnight fast, each mouse was given 1 mg/kg cerivastatin sodium dissolved in sterile water. The drug solution (5  μ l/g of body weight) was administered by direct injection into the stomach using a blunt-ended needle inserted via the esophagus. After 10, 20, or 30 min, the mice were anaesthetised using diethyl ether and a blood sample was obtained by orbital bleeding. The mice were then killed under anaesthesia and the harvested tissue samples (brain, skeletal muscle obtained from the thigh, liver, kidney) were blotted on filter paper, weighed and homogenised. The animal research protocol was in accordance with the current version of the German Law on the Protection of Animals and was approved by the appropriate local authority (Regierungspräsidium Stuttgart).  

Determination of plasma and tissue cerivastatin concen- trations  Cerivastatin concentrations were determined by gas chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (GC – MS – MS) using lovastatin as the internal standard. Plasma samples (40 – 100  μ l) were spiked with 10 pmol of the internal standard and 0.5 ml of phosphate buffer (0.1 M, pH 7.4). After extraction with 5 ml of diethyl ether/2- propanol (9:1 v/v), the organic layer was evaporated to dryness in a stream of nitrogen. Penta flu oro benz yl (PFB) derivatives were prepared by treating the residue with 25  μ l of PFB bromide (10% in acetonitrile; Sigma-Aldrich Corporation) and 25  μ l of diisopropyl ethyl amine (10% in acetonitrile; Sigma-Aldrich Corporation) for 20 min at room temperature. After evaporation to dryness, 50  μ l of N , O -bis(trimethylsilyl)tri flu oro acetamide (BSTFA; Sigma- Aldrich Corporation) was added and the samples were kept at room temperature for 10 min. Subsequently, the samples were centrifuged and 25  μ l of the supernatant was transferred to autosampler vials for GC – MS – MS analysis. Tissue samples (20 – 100 mg) were homogenised with phosphate buffer (pH 7.4) and the internal standard in a FastPrep FP120 homogeniser (Thermo Savant, Holbrook, NY, USA) at speed 6.0 using Lysing Matrix D (Qbiogene, Heidelberg, Germany). Further handling of the samples was as described for plasma.  

For GC – MS – MS, a TSQ 700 mass spectrometer (Finnigan MAT, Bremen, Germany) coupled to a 5890 II gas chromatograph (Hewlett Packard, Waldbronn, Ger- many) was used. Gas chromatography was performed on a Rtx-5MS column (30 m, 0.25 mm i.d., dimethyl poly silo x- ane with 5% phenyl groups, 0.25  μ m film thickness; Restek, Bad Homburg, Germany) in the splitless mode, with helium as carrier gas at an inlet pressure of 100 kPa. The initial oven temperature of 120°C was held for 1 min, increased by 15°C/min to 240°C and then by 30°C/min to 300°C. The final temperature was held for 7 min. Mass spectrometry was performed in the negative ion chemical ionisation (NICI) mode, using the following conditions: source temperature, 150°C; CI gas (methane) pressure, 75 Pa; electron energy, 120 eV; emission current, 200  μ A; argon collision cell pressure, 133 mPa; collision energy, 15 eV. Precursor and product ions were  m/z  602.3 and  m/z 422 for cerivastatin and  m/z  565.3 and  m/z  385 for lovastatin.  

The method was linear from 0.01 pmol to 2 pmol for brain samples, from 0.1 pmol to 50 pmol for plasma, muscle and kidney and from 10 pmol to 500 pmol for liver samples. Quality control samples at the limit of quantifica- tion (0.01 pmol) could be determined with a coefficient of variation (CV) of 12.4% and a bias of 1.6%. At higher concentrations (0.5 pmol and 2 pmol for brain; 0.1, 1.0 and 10 pmol for plasma, muscle and kidney; 10, 100 and 400 pmol for liver) the CV ranged from 1.7% to 8.1% and the bias was between  − 9.2% and 9.1%.  

Statistical analysis  Data are presented as mean ± SD. Effects of putative inhibitors on drug transport were analysed with one-way ANOVA, using the Tukey test for post hoc comparisons, as appropriate.  P app  values, plasma and tissue concentrations of cerivastatin, and tissue-to- plasma cerivastatin concentration ratios (calculated to take into account any differences in plasma concentrations within each group) were analysed using the unpaired  t -test (two-tailed).  P  values < 0.05 were considered statistically significant.  

# Results  

Drug transport  In both Caco-2 and L-MDR1 cell monolayers, the basal-to-apical transport of cerivastatin (0.9  μ M) was markedly greater than the apical-to-basal transport (Fig.  1 ). The  P app  value for the basal-to-apical transport of cerivastatin in Caco-2 and L-MDR1 mono- layers was 2.4 times ( P <0.001) and 3.8 times ( P <0.001) as high as the apical-to-basal P app value respectively (Table  1 ). In the LLC-PK1 cell monolayers, no difference between the basal-to-apical and apical-to-basal transport of cerivastatin was observed (Fig.  1 , Table  1 ). The P- glycoprotein substrate digoxin (5  μ M) exhibited, as expected, a good directional transport in Caco-2 mono- layers. The respective  P app  values (basal-to-apical, 13.1 − 6   − 6   ±2.45×10 cm/s; apical-to-basal, 4.09±1.22×10 cm/s; P <0.001) are in good agreement with previously reported values (Pauli-Magnus et al.  2001 ).  

Inhibition of drug transport  At concentrations up to 250  μ M, gemfibrozil showed no significant effects on the net basal-to-apical transport of cerivastatin in Caco-2 cell monolayers (Fig.  2 ). The net transport of digoxin also remained unaffected by gemfibrozil. The P-glycoprotein inhibitor PSC-833 (1  μ M) inhibited the net basal-to-apical transport of cerivastatin by 35% ( P <0.01) and the MRP inhibitor MK-571 (10  μ M) by 50% ( P <0.01; Fig.  2 ). The addition of PSC-833 to Caco-2 monolayers abolished the directional difference in digoxin transport. In addition, MK-571 reduced the net basal-to-apical transport of digoxin in Caco-2 monolayers by about 20% ( P =0.05).  

Disposition of cerivastatin in mdr1a/b ( − / − ) and mdr1a/b (+/+) mice  The  mdr1a/b  ( − / − ) mice showed a 3.1-times higher concentration of cerivastatin in the brain 30 min after cerivastatin administration than the  mdr1a/b  (+/+) mice ( P <0.05; Table  2 ). Further, the brain-to-plasma cerivastatin concentration ratio at 20 min and 30 min was 2.1 times ( P <0.05) and 3.6 times ( P <0.05) higher respectively in the knock-out mice compared with the wild-type mice (Fig.  3 , Table  2 ). # Discussion  

In both Caco-2 and L-MDR1 cell monolayers, the basal- to-apical transport of cerivastatin was significantly greater than the apical-to-basal transport. In contrast, a preferential basal-to-apical versus apical-to-basal transport was not evident in the LLC-PK1 monolayers. The L-MDR1 cells differ from the parent LLC-PK1 cells by the stable overexpression of the human  MDR1  gene. Thus, cerivas- tatin behaved as a P-glycoprotein substrate in these experiments. Although most substrates of P-glycoprotein are organic cations and lipophilic compounds, atorvastatin, an organic anion and monocarboxylic acid like cerivastatin, has been previously shown to be a P-glycoprotein substrate (Wu et al.  2000 ). The lack of a difference in plasma cerivastatin concentrations between the  mdr1a/b ( − / − ) and  mdr1a/b  (+/+) mice might be partly explained by the rapid and extensive bio transformation of cerivas- tatin in mice (Boberg et al.  1998 ), which may also have contributed to the considerable between-animal variability in exposure to cerivastatin observed in both mice groups. Bischoff and Heller ( 1998 ) investigated the disposition of orally administered cerivastatin in rats and found tissue- to-plasma AUC ratios for liver and muscle that were similar to the respective tissue-to-plasma concentration ratios observed in the wild-type mice in the present study. However, the brain-to-plasma ratio was about tenfold higher in the rats compared with the mice. Potential explanations for this observation include a better passive diffusion of cerivastatin into the brain in the rat and/or a more efficient P-glycoprotein function in the blood – brain barrier of the mouse.  

A well-established inhibitor of P-glycoprotein function, PSC-833, showed only a moderate inhibitory effect on the net basal-to-apical transport of cerivastatin in Caco-2 monolayers, indicating that other transporters contributed to the polarised transport of cerivastatin across the monolayers. Indeed, both Caco-2 cells and normal human jejunum have been shown to contain in addition to P-glycoprotein many other drug efflux proteins of the ATP-binding cassette (ABC) transporter family, including MRP2 (Hirohashi et al.  2000 ; Taipalensuu et al.  2001 ). MRP2 is located in the apical membrane of intestinal enterocytes and biliary and renal epithelial cells (König et al.  1999 ; Fromm et al.  2000 ; Kusuhara and Sugiyama 2002 ). As P-glycoprotein, MRP2 plays an important role in the disposition of many drugs (Gerk and Vore  2002 ). The MRP inhibitor MK-571 inhibited the net basal-to- apical transport of cerivastatin more than PSC-833, by about 50%. The fact that MK-571 showed a slight but almost significant inhibitory effect on the net transport of digoxin, a P-glycoprotein substrate, appears to suggest that the effect of MK-571 on cerivastatin was partly mediated through inhibition of P-glycoprotein. However, in a recent study (Lowes et al.  2003 ), the transport of digoxin by Caco-2 epithelia was significantly inhibited by MK-571, although MK-571 did not inhibit digoxin transport in MDR1 -transfected Madin-Darby canine kidney (MDCKII) cell monolayers. Further experiments suggested a negli- gible contribution of MRP2 to digoxin transport by Caco-2 cells, and the authors concluded that Caco-2 cells contain an additional MK-571-sensitive secretory pathway for digoxin (Lowes et al.  2003 ). Thus, the effect of MK-571 on the directional transport of cerivastatin was probably not mediated by inhibition of P-glycoprotein, but may reflect inhibition of MRP2 or some other drug efflux protein.  

Gemfibrozil had no effect on the transport of cerivas- tatin in Caco-2 cell monolayers. The lack of effect of gemfibrozil on digoxin transport confirmed that gemfi- brozil is not a P-glycoprotein inhibitor. Recent studies indicate that several mechanisms, including inhibition of CYP2C8 (Prueksaritanont et al.  2002a , 2002b ; Wang et al. 2002 ) and UGTs (Prueksaritanont et al.  2002a , b ), may contribute to the marked effect of gemfibrozil on cerivastatin p harm a co kinetics (Backman et al.  2002 ). In addition, there is the possibility that gemfibrozil inhibits the OATP1B1 (OATP2/OATP-C)-mediated uptake of cerivastatin into the liver (Shitara et al.  2003 ), resulting in accumulation of cerivastatin in the circulation.  

Recent epidemiological studies suggest that statins may be protective against Alzheimer ’ s disease and for other types of dementia (Hajjar et al.  2002 ; Rockwood and Darvesh  2003 ). Brain cholesterol metabolism seems to be central to the pathophysiology of Alzheimer ’ s disease (Burns and Duff  2003 ; Poirier  2003 ) and statins have been shown to reduce cholesterol turnover in the human brain (Locatelli et al.  2002 ). As several statins are P-glycopro- tein substrates (Kim et al.  1999 ; Wu et al.  2000 ; Wang et al.  2001 ), the effects of statins on Alzheimer ’ s disease might be affected by genetic polymorphisms of the P- glycoprotein-encoding  MDR1  gene (Schwab et al.  2003 ; Marzolini et al.  2004 ) or concomitant treatment with P- glycoprotein inhibitors. Further, a number of case reports have raised the possibility that statins may be associated, in rare cases, with cognitive impairment such as memory loss (Wagstaff et al.  2003 ). Possible associations between the P-glycoprotein polymorphism and beneficial or harmful effects of statins on the central nervous system warrant further study.  

The muscle-to-plasma concentration ratios for cerivas- tatin were higher in the  mdr1a/b  ( − / − ) mice than in the mdr1a/b  (+/+) mice, although statistical significance was reached at only one of the three studied time points. Studies on the expression of P-glycoprotein in human skeletal muscle have reported equivocal results (Thiebaut et al.  1989 ; Cordon-Cardo et al.  1990 ). However, strong expression of P-glycoprotein has been found in endothelial cells of capillary blood vessels in several organs (Cordon- Cardo et al.  1990 ; Meissner et al.  2002 ). Subjects with impaired P-glycoprotein-mediated statin efflux at the endothelial cell lining of the capillary blood vessels of skeletal muscle tissue might show higher and possibly toxic statin concentrations in the muscle than subjects with a normal P-glycoprotein function. Given that most statins are substrates of the P-glycoprotein (Kim et al.  1999 ; Wu et al.  2000 ; Wang et al.  2001 ), further studies are required to address the question whether there is an association between the P-glycoprotein polymorphism and statin- induced myopathy.  

In conclusion, this study has provided evidence that cerivastatin is a substrate of P-glycoprotein, although other transporters probably contribute to cerivastatin transport in humans. P-glycoprotein is involved in the disposition of several statins, and beneficial as well as harmful effects of the statins might be affected by interindividual differences in P-glycoprotein expression or function caused by, e.g., the  MDR .
