# Rotational Motions of Solvents and Anions  

To analyze the rotational motion of the solvent and anion in the sulfolane   (SL)-based electrolytes,  NMR spin-lattice relaxation time ( T 1 ) measurements were performed.   For spin 1/2 nuclei such as  1 H and  19 F, the spin-lattice relaxation is mainly caused by the fluctuation of the local magnetic field provided by  the rapid reorientation of the dipolar interaction arising from the tumbling motion of molecules.  When  the average rate of the molecular motion in the liquid is close to the Larmor frequency of the nuclear  spin, the most effective relaxation occurs, resulting in the  T 1  minimum. As the molecular motion becomes  either slower or faster than the Larmor frequency, the relaxation becomes less efficient;   that is,  T 1   becomes longer.   Therefore, the temperature dependence of  T 1  for molecules in the liquid often exhibits  a downward convex parabolic behavior. Note that both TFSA −  and FSA −  anions adopt  C 1 (cis) and  C 2   (trans) conformers.  The conformational changes of the anions also affects the  T 1  relaxation of their  nuclei. Here we investigate the  T 1  relaxation of LiBF 4 /SL mixtures, where the BF 4 −  anion has no freedom  of conformational change.  Figure S4  shows the temperature dependence of  T 1  for the  1 H nuclei of SL in  the LiBF 4 /SL electrolytes. Here,  T 1  of the  1 H nuclei in the two different positions of the SL molecule  showed a similar temperature-dependent behavior ( Figure S5 ). Hereafter, the rotational motion of SL is  discussed based on the  T 1  of the  1 H signal of the methylene chain at position 3. The  T 1  value for  [LiBF 4 ]/[SL] = 1/10 increased linearly with temperature and showed no minimum value, indicating that  the motion of SL in the liquid became faster at higher temperatures. In the case of highly concentrated  electrolytes, the Arrhenius plots of  T 1  exhibit minima near room temperature. The  T 1  minimum shifted to  a higher temperature with increasing mole fraction of LiBF 4  in the electrolyte, suggesting that the  mobility of SL molecules decreased owing to the increase in viscosity.

Notably, the  1 H nuclear spin can be relaxed via translational, rotational, and intramolecular motions  of the SL. The translational motion generally has less effect on the spin–lattice relaxation than the other  molecular motions because the intermolecular dipolar interactions are very small owing to the long spin– spin distance.  In addition, the effect of local intramolecular motion of SL, such as conformational  change, can be neglected because the conformations of the methylene groups of SL molecules are limited  owing to the cyclic structure. We obtained  K  from the  T 1  minimum value and, then, estimated  𝜏𝜏 c   of SL at various  temperatures. The estimated  𝜏𝜏 c  values of the SL in the LiBF 4 /SL electrolytes are shown in  Figure S4 .  Each electrolyte exhibited Arrhenius-type temperature dependence of  𝜏𝜏 c . The  𝜏𝜏 c   value increases with  increasing Li salt concentration, which indicates that the molecular motion is slowed owing to the  increase in viscosity. The molecular structure of a solvent also affects its rotational motion. Another cyclic  sulfone solvent, 3-methylsulfolane (MSL), can also dissolve LiBF 4  at high concentrations.  For  comparison, we performed  T 1  measurements for the [LiBF 4 ]/[MSL] = 1/2 electrolyte ( Figure S6 ). The  𝜏𝜏 c  value of MSL is longer than that of SL because of the higher viscosity of the MSL-based electrolyte  and the steric hindrance of the methyl group of MSL.  The activation energies of  𝜏𝜏 c  in the LiBF 4 /SL  electrolytes are shown in  Table S2 . It is noteworthy that the activation energy ( E a ) of  𝜏𝜏 c  is lower than  that of fluidity and diffusivity in highly concentrated electrolytes. For the  19 F nuclei of BF 4 −  anion, the  T 1  values were also measured at various temperatures ( Figure S7 ). The  T 1  minimum of the  19 F nuclei  was not observed in each electrolyte, even at low temperatures, implying that another specific relaxation  is dominant other than dipole–dipole relaxation at low temperatures. The origin of the short  T 1  value at  low temperatures remains unclear and requires further investigation. For the highly concentrated solution  of [LiBF 4 ]/[SL] = 1/2,  𝜏𝜏 c  of BF 4 −  is comparable to that of SL ( Figure S7 ); however,   the  E a  of  𝜏𝜏 c  of  BF 4 −  is lower than that of SL. The facile rotational motion of BF 4 −  may be attributed to its small ionic  size and spherical structure.
