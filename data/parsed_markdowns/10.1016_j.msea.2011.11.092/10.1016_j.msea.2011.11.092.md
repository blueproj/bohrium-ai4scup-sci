# 1. Introduction  

Nickel-base superalloys are generally used in the high tem- perature parts of aircraft engines where very good mechanical properties are required. The alloy of this study is the Nimonic 263 which is generally used in the medium temperature range (around 500   ◦ C) for structural application requiring reasonable fatigue prop- erties with high oxidation resistance. Metallurgical and mechanical investigations have been extensively conducted in the past but only for standard processing routes such as forging of cast ingots [2,3,4] .  

Selective Laser Melting process (SLM) has recently emerged and is based upon the stereo lithography technology which allows to produce prototypes by building it layer after layer in a pool of photo-sensible resin. In the case of SLM, ﬁne metallic powder layers (25–50  m)    are spread out on the building platform. According to the initial CAD (Computer aided Design) ﬁle, the laser beam scans each layer in order to locally melt the powder particles and form a melting pool which is instantaneously solidiﬁed as the laser beam moves forwards. Due to the small laser beam diameter, the proto- types can now contain speciﬁc features such as cooling channels or very thin details ( ≃ 200  m in thickness) in very short times because the process does not involve the use of expensive tools. Obviously, the powder not affected by the heat coming from the laser can mostly be recycled for further use.  

The objective of this paper is to present the as-processed microstructure of the Nimonic 263 alloy processed by SLM. Because the interaction time between the laser beam and the powder is very short (a few millisecond), the resulting microstructures are out-of-equilibrium and require heat treatments, on one hand, to homogenize the microstructure and develop the strengthening precipitates, or on the other hand, to relieve the residual stresses present in the SLM manufactured parts. These heat treatments are developed from the initial as-processed microstructure. Finally, tensile tests at room temperature are carried out in order to com- pare the mechanical properties of the various SLM microstructures to standard material obtained from conventional processing route.

The various microstructures are observed by optical microscopy (OM), scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission elec- tron microscopy (TEM). In the case of OM and SEM, the samples are mechanically polished using 1  m diamond paste. Thin foils are mechanically thinned down to 150  m before being jet electro- polished using a perchloric and acetic acid solution at − 5   ◦ C and 15 mA.  

Transmission electron microscopy has been performed with two different microscopes: a Philips EM340 T with a LaB 6  electron gun has been used for selected area diffraction analysis (SAD) and con- ventional diffraction contrast imaging, and a FEZ Tecnai F20ST with a ﬁeld emission gun (FEG) has been used for imaging and X-ray energy dispersive spectrometry (EDS).  

All the heat treatments are carried out under argon with a fur- nace heating rate of 10   ◦ C/min. The various quenches used are water quench (WQ) and air cooling (AC). Tensile tests are conducted at room temperature at ˙ ε  = 10 − 3   s − 1 . They are carried out on two building directions in order to assess the effect of the orientation of the layers on the tensile properties. The TD (transverse direc- tion) samples have the loading direction perpendicular to the layers whereas the LD (longitudinal direction) samples have the loading direction parallel to the layers.  

# 3. The as-fabricated microstructure  

The resulting as-processed microstructure of the Nimonic 263 samples is illustrated in Fig. 1 where it is possible to observe the wide columnar grains crossing multiple layers. This is true what- ever the building orientation of the sample on the substrate. This typical grain morphology has been found by other authors work- ing on additive processes [5,6] . This columnar grain growth in the ⟨ 0 0 1 ⟩ direction can be explained as follows. Early in the fabrica- tion, most of the heat induced by the laser beam is transmitted through the dense substrate, resulting in an important thermal gra- dient along the building direction. Indeed, even though the part is embedded in the powder bed, the thermal conductivity of this powder bed is very low because the porosity content is around 50% in volume. Moreover, for each layer, the melting pool com- prises the molten powder particles of the freshly deposited layer as well as a fraction of the dense material previously solidiﬁed. This characteristic is particularly important in order to guaran- tee high bonding between layers. Actually, this has an important effect on the microstructure because this permits to extend the

Careful electron diffraction analysis in TEM show that the pre- cipitation of the  ′ -Ni 3 (Al,Ti) phase is totally inhibited by the very fast cooling rate following the SLM processing of the powder. However, macrohardness measurements reach 300HV30, which is a relatively high value for a nickel-base solid solution without any precipitates distribution ensuring its hardening. TEM analysis ( Fig. 3 ) reveals the presence of a very high dislocation density in the whole as-processed sample. What can be identiﬁed as the dis- location microstructure – mainly cells and traces of slip planes – are characteristic of plastic deformation. This plastic deformation is the result of the differential contraction affecting the contin- uously adjacent areas submitted to the steep thermal gradients, following the laser beam trajectories. This heavy dislocation den- sity acts as strain hardening of the precipitate free as-processed alloy and is responsible for the relatively high hardness value of 300HV30.  

Because the alloy was designed to work in the medium tem- perature range, it is necessary to precipitate the strengthening phases. To do so, in one side, the standard two stages heat treat- ment (1150   ◦ C/2 h WQ    + 800   ◦ C/8 h AC) is carried out to solutionize the thermal behavior of the process and precipitate the strengthen- ing phases. In the other side, the direct aging treatment at medium temperature is carried out in order to guaranty high dimensional stability of the SLM manufactured parts.

# 5. Stress relieving treatment, direct aging treatment  

In order to relieve thermal residual stresses which arise dur- ing the very fast cooling of the heat affected zone and the melting pool as the laser moves forwards and the liqueﬁed powder solid- iﬁes, the effect of direct aging treatments at various temperatures on the microstructure, applied to the SLM as-processed samples, have been studied. In fact, it may be important to skip the high temperature heating of the standard treatment in order to avoid grain growth and geometrical distortion of the SLM process parts, as the SLM process was initially intended to produce mechanically unloaded parts or prototypes.  

SLM processed samples have been annealing for 8 h at temper- atures between 400   ◦ C and 1000   ◦ C. It can be observed in Fig. 5 that hardness measurements are constant for annealing temperatures between 400   ◦ C and 600   ◦ C and increase from 600   ◦ C to 700   ◦ C. At 800   ◦ C the hardness starts to decrease in comparison to its value at 700   ◦ C and keeps on decreasing at higher temperatures, reaching only 230HV30 at 1000   ◦ C.  

Microstructural analysis of samples annealed at temperatures below 600   ◦ C does not show any modiﬁcation of the as-processed microstructure but electron diffraction pattern from the sample annealed at 600   ◦ C for 8 h reveals the presence of  ′ -Ni 3 (Al,Ti) phase. TEM dark ﬁeld diffraction contrast using speciﬁc diffracted beam of this phase does not allow to image the precipitate dis- tribution but the existence of these precipitates is proven by the diffraction patterns. Actually, the  matrix of this sample still shows the same high dislocation density microstructure as the as-processed sample, though the 8 h treatment at 600   ◦ C should have mostly achieved its restoration. Then the  ′ -Ni 3 (Al,Ti) nucle- ation has probably occurred on dislocation core and the very ﬁne  ′ precipitates have pinned the dislocation and inhibited restoration. These precipitates remain too small after 8 h at 600   ◦ C to be imaged by dark-ﬁeld imaging in the distorted  matrix crystal lattice and their diameter is therefore supposed to be smaller than 10 nm. Hardness measurement gives a value close to the hardness of the as- processed sample as the microstructure is mainly unchanged and the strengthening still results from the large dislocation density.  

Hardness after 8 h at 700   ◦ C reaches 370HV30 which is the high- est value we have measured as it is only 330HV30 after the standard heat treatment 1150   ◦ C/2 h WQ    + 800   ◦ C/8 h AC. TEM shows that the dislocation density is still the same, but around 10 nm diameter

This extremely high hardness for such alloy (370HV30) probably results from the combination between strain hardening produced by the high dislocation density and precipita- tion hardening from the  ′ -Ni 3 (Al,Ti) particles.  

The annealing treatment leading to the  ′ -Ni 3 (Al,Ti) precipita- tion should rather be considered as a Direct Aging (DA) treatment as it strengthens the alloy without the usual high temperature solution treatment. Such Direct Aging treatment is, for instance, proposed to the IN718 type alloys in order to pin the dislocation microstructure resulting from the forming process, by nucleat- ing the  ′′ -Ni 3 (Nb) hardening precipitates in the dislocation core, resulting in an increase of some mechanical properties.  

Higher annealing temperatures (>700   ◦ C) result in lower val- ues of the hardness. In fact, such temperatures are high enough to modify the distribution of the  ′ -Ni 3 (Al,Ti) precipitates. Both their diameter and their distance from each other increase by the Oswald ripening. The distance between the precipitates becomes too large to pin the dislocations. Dislocation climb and slip are made easier and the dislocation microstructure can restore. Strain hard- ening weakens meanwhile precipitation hardening is less and less effective as the annealing temperature increases. Annealing at tem- perature close to or higher than the  ′   solvus temperature (960   ◦ C for the Nimonic 263) leads to immediate restoration of the dislo- cation microstructure and to hardness measurements much lower than the 300HV30 of the SLM as-processed material.  

In order to relieve the thermal stresses arising during manufac- turing, the 600   ◦ C/8 h AC annealing treatment is chosen because it permits to maintain the high dislocation density of the as- manufactured sample as well as reasonable hardness (300HV30). It is worth mentioning that this heat treatment does not induce distortion of the parts during treatment which is a very impor- tant point because the SLM parts must respect the dimensional tolerances. The effect of this annealing treatment on the ther- mal stresses is assessed on a 120 mm    × 20 mm × 25 mm Nimonic 263 sample attached to the 30 mm thick substrate. # 6. Mechanical characterization

The samples are ﬁrst heat treated and then machined to the correct geometry given by the French Aeronauti- cal standard CEAT TP5. The tensile strength, yield strength, ductility and elastic modulus are discussed and compared to the standard properties found in the literature [1] .

# 7. Conclusion  

This article intends to present the as-processed microstructure of the Nimonic 263 superalloy elaborated by selective laser melting for which the interaction time between the laser beam and the pow- der bed is very short. Due to the very high cooling rate of the melting pool (10 5 ◦ C/s), the resulting microstructure is quite homogeneous but remains out-of-equilibrium. It consists of wide columnar grains because of the particular thermal regime. Close to the substrate, the heat induced by the laser beam generates a thermal gradient along the building direction because the thermal conductivity of the substrate is much higher than the one of the powder. Far from the substrate/sample interface, the top of these columnar grains are remelted at each layer because the melting pool comprises a part of fresh powder particles as well as previously solidiﬁed material. The fact to remelt few layers underneath the freshly deposited powder bed permits to guarantee high bonding between the multiple layers and give high mechanical properties.  

Also because of the high cooling rate of the melting pool, the as-processed microstructure of the Nimonic 263 alloy shows some dendrites with a ﬁne interdendritic zone (50 nm). Some small MC    carbides (200  m) are observed in this solute rich zone. TEM observations conﬁrm that the high hardness of the as-processed microstructure is due to the high dislocation density attributed to different coefﬁcients of thermal expansion between the liquid and the solid. No strengthening precipitates such as  ′ -Ni 3 (Al,Ti) are observed.  

Standard heat treatment (1150   ◦ C/2 h WQ + 800   ◦ C/8 h AC) enables to solutionize the as-processed microstructure and then to precipitate the strengthening phases of the alloy. TEM observa- tions conﬁrm that the  ′ -Ni 3 (Al,Ti) precipitates are about 30 nm in diameter whereas the volume fraction is around 10–12%. It is also shown that a ﬁne distribution of M 23 C 6  carbides is present along the grain boundaries.  

In contrast, the direct aging treatment enables to maintain the high dislocation microstructure present in the as-processed sam- ples although X-rays residual stresses measurements conﬁrm that the residual stresses are relieved after 600   ◦ C/8 h AC heat treat- ment. It is also noted a ﬁne precipitation of  ′ -Ni 3 (Al,Ti) precipitates (less than 10 nm in diameter) on the dislocation cores. However, no chromium rich carbides are found close to the grain boundaries.  

The tensile properties at room temperature of the as-fabricated samples exhibit high yield strength and high ultimate strength even though they are only strengthened by solid solution and strain hardening. The TD samples present higher ductility than the LD samples because of the columnar grain growth, very typical of the additive processes.  

The standard heat treatment still shows an important anisotropy between the two building directions but permits to increase the yield strength and the ultimate strength. In this case, these tensile properties are slightly higher than the standard heat treated properties.  

The direct aging treatment presents tensile properties close to the SLM as-processed properties. It is noted a slight dispersion of the ductility which is attributed to the presence of manufacturing defects on the fracture surfaces.  

The perspective of this work is to characterize the mechanical properties of the Nimonic 263 alloys processed by SLM through high temperature tensile tests, high temperature fatigue tests and creep tests. These types of mechanical tests should be more sensi- ble to the manufacturing defects present in the SLM as-processed material.
