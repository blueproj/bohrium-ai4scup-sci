# 15  Abstract  

Different solidification rates may cause a significant difference in microstructure  and creep property in different locations of turbine blades in aircraft engines. In this  study, the effect of solidification rate on microstructural stability and creep property  was revealed in a polycrystalline nickel-based superalloy K465. The K465 alloy was  cast as a turbine blade, solid bars and hollow tubes. A larger cross section size caused  a slower solidification rate in the blade shank and bar than that of the blade airfoil and  tube. Microstructural characteristics and corresponding stress rupture properties under  975 °C/225 MPa were investigated after thermal exposure at 900 °C for 300 h to 1000  h. Plate-like  µ  phase formed only in the interdendritic regions of the blade shank and  the bar, but not in the blade airfoil and the tube after thermal exposure. The  precipitation of  µ  phase was mainly responsible for the much worse stress rupture  property of the bar in comparison with the tube. The micro segregation degree,  chemical composition of  γ  matrix and precipitates including  γ ’  phase and various  carbides, and dislocation configurations were examined. Compared to the tube, a  slower solidification rate caused a higher degree of micro segregation, coarser  γ ’   precipitates and carbides, as well as a much higher dislocation density in the bar after  standard solution treatment. The formation of  µ  phase was stress-induced and  attributed to the remaining dislocations in the interdendritic regions. A longer solution  treatment was suggested to effectively suppress the formation of  µ  phase in the bar  and blade shank for practical applications. These results provide a guidance for the  manufacturing and evaluation of microstructural degradation of turbine blades made  from conventionally cast polycrystalline nickel-based superalloys.  Keywords : Turbine blade; Micro segregation; Microstructure;  µ  phase; Dislocations  

# 1. Introduction  

Polycrystalline cast nickel-based superalloys are widely used for aero-engines  turbine blades [1, 2], which are subjected to complex elevated temperature and stress  couplings during service [1, 3]. At present, blades are generally designed with  complicated geometrical configurations to achieve the requirements for sustaining  such high loads and temperatures. This inevitably results in the variation of  cross-section size at different locations, such as the shank and airfoil tip. The size  difference can result in a variation of thermal mass, which have an influence on  casting parameters such as solidification rate, and ultimately affect the resulting  microstructure. Zheng and Cai [4] reported that the  γ ’  solvus temperature in the  thickest area was nearly 30 °C higher than that in the thinnest area for Udimet 500  cast turbine blade. Pal et al. [5] showed that topologically close-packed (TCP) phases  were prone to precipitate only in some specific locations of a serviced René N5  turbine blade. Such studies show that the casting section size can significantly affect  the local microstructural stability of a blade after casting [1, 6], solution-treatment [7],  during service and even after rejuvenation [5].  

Generally, solid bars are used to evaluate the microstructure and mechanical  properties of a given alloy. However, thin-wall specimens such as hollow tubes are  considered to be more suitable for mimicking the creep behavior of the hollow airfoil  of turbine blades [6, 8-10]. K465 superalloy (namely M963 superalloy or  ЖС 6 У   superalloy) is a conventionally cast nickel-based superalloy that has been widely used  in turbine blades and vanes of aircraft engines. Until now, most creep property studies  of K465 superalloy utilize solid bar samples, which may not be the representative of  the blade airfoil. Such studies generally report microstructural degradation, such as  coarsening and dissolution of  γ ’  precipitates, decomposition of MC carbides, as well  as the formation of M 6 C and M 23 C 6  carbides occurring during thermal exposure in the  850-1050 °C temperature range [11-14]. Additionally, plate-like  µ  phase precipitation,  which causes significant creep resistance degradation of K465 alloy, has been  reported during thermal exposure at 900 °C of bulk samples [11, 12]. However, no  µ   phase has been observed and reported by microstructural evolution studies, utilizing  the airfoils of serviced K465 turbine blades [15].  

The addition of refractory elements, such as W and Mo, promotes the  precipitation of TCP phases such as  µ  phase in nickel-based superalloys [2]. TCP  phases are always detrimental to the creep durability of superalloys due to their brittle  nature, and alloy design typically aims at suppressing/limiting their formation [16].  Generally,  µ  phase precipitates from the supersaturated matrix during solidification  [17] or under elevated temperature and/or stress [18, 19], and thus most studies on the  precipitation behavior of  µ  phase focus on the effect of alloy composition [17, 19-22].  However, limited studies have explored the precipitation behavior of  µ  phase in  different locations of the same turbine blade.  

The objective of this study is to explain the solidification rate related  microstructural differences produced in different locations of the same blade during  casting, and explore the effect of such difference on the microstructural evolution and  creep behavior of a K465 superalloy blade. Additionally, it aims at elucidating the  reason for the observation of  µ  phase in solid bars and its absence in airfoils produced  from the same material after thermal exposure at 900 °C. This study will be beneficial  for the optimization of solidification conditions and thermo mechanical processing  schemes of aero-engine components.  

# 2. Experimental  

The stock material was vacuum induction melted (VIM) and cast by Beijing  Institute of Aeronautical Materials into turbine blades, solid bar (representing the  blade shank) or hollow tube (representing the blade airfoil) shapes using the same  investment casting technology. The cross-section width of the blade shank was \~12  mm, while the airfoil thickness was \~2 mm. The cylindrical solid bars were 100 mm  long with a corresponding diameter of 14 mm. The hollow tubes (cast to size, rather  than machined) were also 100 mm long with an intermediate gauge length of 36 mm,  11 mm in outer diameter and 2 mm in wall thickness. The 2 mm thickness was  purposely selected to match the thickness of the airfoil. Subsequently, the cast blades,  bars and tubes were subjected to the standard solution heat treatment at 1210 °C for 4  h followed by air cooling [23]. Fig. 1(a) shows the sketch of a K465 turbine blade,  and the blade samples were sectioned at the shank and leading edge in the middle of  airfoil. The blade, bars and tubes were then thermally exposed at 900 °C for 300-1000  h followed by air cooling. This temperature reasonably represents the normal service  temperature of the airfoil. To evaluate the effect of microstructural evolution on creep  resistance after thermal exposure, the stress rupture lives of the bar and tube were  compared under 975 °C/225 MPa in air. The stress rupture tests were only conducted  on the tubes under 975 °C/225 MPa, considering that the stress rupture properties of  the bars have been reported in Ref. [12]. The sketch of the stress rupture specimens of  the tube is shown in Fig. 1(b). Two to four specimens were tested for each heat  treatment condition for statistical significance.

The compositions of carbides in the bar and tube after the standard solution  treatment and after thermal exposure at 900 °C for 1000 h were measured through  physicochemical phase analysis, and are shown in Table 4. The results show that the  same type of carbides in the bar and tube showed no obvious difference in chemical  composition. MC carbides were rich in Ti and Nb, while M 23 C 6  carbides were  composed of Cr, and W. # 417  4. Discussion  

418  The complexity and intricate design of turbine blades can lead to location specific  419  microstructure during the casting and the subsequent heat treatment, making it  420  difficult to predict the microstructural evolution and creep property of the turbine  421  blade as a whole. In the present study, the effect of the solidification rate on the  422  microstructural evolution and creep durability of a K465 superalloy turbine blade was  423  simulated and evaluated using solid bar and hollow tube samples. A solid bar and  424  hollow tube can be used to simulate the microstructure of the blade shank and airfoil,  425  respectively, based on the present study (Figs. 2, 3 and 4). The following discussion  426  will mainly focus on the solid bar and hollow tube for simplicity.  

# 427  4.1 Effect of solidification rate on microstructure after the standard  428  solution treatment  

429  During the casting of conventional nickel-based superalloys,  γ  dendrites form and  430  grow from the liquid, followed by MC carbides, sometimes M 6 C carbides and  γ / γ ’ 431  eutectic solidify at the last [27]. Due to the dendritic solidification, segregation of  432  alloying elements is inevitable because of the difference in weight and elemental  diffusion rates in Ni. Increasing the solidification rate typically decrease the size of  γ   dendrites and precipitates, as well as the degree of micro segregation [28], due to the  increased solid nucleation rate compared to dendrite growth kinetics [29]. During the  final stage of the casting process, shrinkage is also unavoidable due to the volumetric  contraction associated with the liquid to solid transition and further cooling. A higher  degree of micro segregation could cause a larger mismatch of thermal expansion  coefficients between dendrite core and interdendritic regions due to chemistry  difference and result in a higher stress concentration at dendrite core / interdendritic  region interfaces. Dislocations thus have to be generated to alleviate these stresses. As  the dendrite core regions are typically rich in the refractory alloying elements, which  strengthen the matrix, the interdendritic regions could be deformed relatively easily  and therefore dislocations typically form in this region during solidification and  cooling [30, 31]. Additionally, precipitates have significantly different compositions  and thermal expansion coefficients compared with the surrounding area, therefore  dislocations also form at their interface to accommodate the interfacial stresses. The  subsequent solution treatment aims at homogenizing the bulk composition and  annihilating the dislocations. However, dislocations sometimes cannot be completely  annihilated after solution treatment, especially in the interdendritic regions, as  reported by Nörtershäuser et al. in Ref. [30].  

In the present study, Figs. 3(a) and (b) show that the  λ 2  value in the solid bar was  2 times that of the hollow tube. Typically, the dendrite size is described by the  secondary dendrite arm spacing (SDAS or  λ 2 ), which satisfies the following equation  [29]:

This indicates that the  solidification rate of the tube was about 8 times that of the bar during solidification.  Compared with the tube, the much slower solidification rate resulted in a larger  dendrite size in the bar (Figs. 3(a) and (b)), along with a higher degree of  micro segregation (Fig. 4), coarser  γ ’  precipitates and carbides (Figs. 3(c)-(f)). It can  be inferred that more dislocations existed in the bar than in the tube after the casting.  The standard solution treatment for K465 alloy (1210 °C/4h, air cooling) is only a  semi-solution treatment in order to decrease the cost and production times. During  this solution treatment, the  γ ’  precipitates in the dendrite core regions were completely  dissolved, however, some  γ ’  precipitates and carbides still remained in the  interdendritic regions due to higher microstructural stability. This resulted in  remaining dislocations in the interdendritic regions of the bar and tube after the  standard solution treatment (Figs. 6(c)-(f)). Due to the smaller degree of  micro segregation (Fig. 4), finer  γ ’  precipitates and carbides (Figs. 3(c)-(f)), the  interdendritic region in the tube had a much lower dislocation density than that in the  bar (Figs. 6(c)-(f)).  

# 4.2 Effect of solidification rate on microstructural evolution  

# 4.2.1 Microstructural evolution of  γ ’  precipitates, carbides and  µ  phase  

The  γ ’   phase is the primary strengthening phase in Ni-based superalloys, hence  good  γ ’   phase stability is highly desired. During thermal exposure, local chemical  heterogeneity of alloying elements and minimization of the surface area cause the  coarsening and coalescence of  γ ’  precipitates [32]. This process is exacerbated by  internal chemical gradients and misfit strain [33]. The volume fraction of  γ ’  phase  plays a significant role on the mechanical properties of superalloys during thermal  exposure [34], and in general, a higher  γ ’  volume fraction typically means a better  stress rupture property [35].   In this study, Fig. 5 and Table 2 show that the composition of  γ ’  precipitates and  area fractions of  γ ’  precipitates in the dendrite core regions were similar in the bar and  tube after the standard solution treatment. During thermal exposure at 900 °C, the  γ ’  precipitates in the bar and tube coarsened and started coalesce, but kept a similar  morphology and area fraction (Fig. 7 and Table 2). Compared to the dendrite core  regions, the  γ ’  phase in the interdendritic regions had a higher area fraction due to the  enrichment of  γ ’  forming elements (Table 3), and the precipitates exhibited more  irregular morphology. Nevertheless, no obvious difference was identified between the  bar and tube, suggesting that the solidification rate had a negligible effect on the  formation and microstructural degradation of  γ ’  precipitates in the dendrite core and  interdendritic regions of K465 superalloy.  

The main microstructural differences were observed for the carbide degradation  and  µ  phase formation in the interdendritic regions of the samples with different  geometries, as indicated in Figs. 8 and 9. The degradation of carbides during thermal  exposure is easy to understand, as MC carbides decompose to form M 6 C and/or  M 23 C 6  carbides by releasing C into the  γ  matrix, where it reacts with Cr to form  M 23 C 6  carbides, or with W and Mo to form M 6 C carbides [36]. As the diffusion rate of  Cr is higher than those of W, Co, and Mo, more M 23 C 6  carbides precipitated compared  to M 6 C carbides at 900 °C (Fig. 10) [11, 37].  

The formation of  µ  phase in the bar at 900 °C has also been previously reported  by Yuan et al. [11, 12]. It is generally accepted that the precipitation of  µ  phase in the  γ  matrix can be attributed to two factors: the enrichment of  µ  phase formers (W, Mo  and Cr) [17, 38] and stress-induced formation [19, 20]. In the present study, it is  surprising that  µ  phase precipitated only in the bar, but not in the tube during thermal  exposure at 900 °C (Figs. 8 and 9). The forming elements of  µ  phase typically  segregated to the dendrite core regions (Fig. 4), hence it is expected that this phase  would form in these regions first, prior to the precipitation in the interdendritic  regions, but this was not the case in this study (Fig. 8). Due to the higher fraction of  γ ’ forming elements in the interdendritic regions, the  γ  forming elements in the matrix at  this location might be concentrated, and then drive the precipitation of  µ  phase.  However, this was ruled out by the measurement of the local precipitate and matrix  chemistries (Fig. 5), where no such enrichment was observed. This implies that the  precipitation of  µ  phase in this case may be stress-induced.  

# 4.2.2 Formation mechanism of  µ  phase  

Kontis et al. [39] reported that Co and Cr segregated to the dislocations during  high temperature deformation, and Wu et al. [25] found that the dislocations were  generally rich in  γ  formers and lack in  γ ’   formers. This may imply that the dislocations  in the interdendritic regions of the bar were rich in Co and Cr, and some W and Mo ( γ   and  µ  phase formers). The enrichment would result in a high driving force for the  µ   phase nucleation [19], while the general chemistry of the alloy, high W and Mo  contents, would provide the driving force for the growth during the thermal exposure.  Interestingly, some  µ  precipitates were observed to connect to the MC or M 6 C  carbides (Figs. 8(b) and (d)). As discussed previously and shown in Fig. 6(e), there is  an accumulation of dislocations between carbides and the surrounding area, and since  MC carbides are rich in Ti and Nb, this could result in local enrichment of W, Co, Cr,  and Mo in the vicinity of MC carbides [22, 40]. This further supports the nucleation  of  µ  phase driven by dislocation segregation. With the transformation of MC to M 6 C  carbides during thermal exposure, the  µ  phase can also nucleate on M 6 C carbides  because of the high similarity in crystal structure and composition of  µ  phase and  M 6 C carbide (Fig. 8(f) and Table 4) [41, 42]. Overall, multiple dislocations in the  interdendritic regions contributed to the formation of  µ  particles, once  µ  precipitates  have formed in the interdendritic regions, the internal chemical gradients contribute to  µ  phase growth toward dendrite cores due to the micro segregation (Fig. 8(a)).   In the tube, the initial dislocation density was very low after the standard heat  treatment (Figs. 6(b) and (d)), which didn’t provide a strong enough driving force for  µ  phase nucleation. As the MC carbides were finer than those in the bar, the  dislocation accumulation at the interface of MC carbides and  γ  matrix was also lower  M 23 C 6  carbides over  µ  phase during thermal exposure at 900 °C (Fig. 9).   The stress-induced formation of  µ  phase is further confirmed by the stress rupture  specimens of the tube. As significant amount of dislocations accumulated during the  creep,  µ  phase can form during the testing of the thermally exposed samples (Figs.  12(b) and (c)). This is consistent with other studies observing accelerated TCP phase  formation with stress [20]. For the stress rupture test of the tube after the standard  solution treatment, no  µ  phase formed since the MC carbides had not started  

decomposing. These results indicate that although the formation of  µ  phase is  stress-induced, some degree of microstructural degeneration is still needed.   Our results reveal that the formation of  µ  phase was mainly controlled by the  amount of remaining dislocations and degree of micro segregation in the sample after  the standard solution treatment. As the homogenization during solution treatment is  elemental diffusion controlled, a larger  λ 2  value leads to longer elemental diffusion  distances, which require more time to eliminate the micro segregation [43]. Hence  increasing the solution treatment time could annihilate dislocations and reduce the  degree of micro segregation more effectively, and thus suppress the formation of  µ   phase. In order to validate this assumption, we solution treated the solid bar sample to  a much longer time for simulating the blade shank. Fig. 13 is a SEM-BSE image of  the typical microstructure of the interdendritic regions in the bar after solution  treatment at 1210 °C for additional 24 h and subsequently thermally exposed at 900 °C  for 500 h. It’s observed that no  µ  phase formed in the interdendritic region. This means  that prolonging the solution treatment time is one effective way to suppress the initial  formation of  µ  phase for practical engineering application, through annihilation of  dislocations and reducing the degree the micro segregation inherited from the  solidification of the alloy.

# 573  4.3 Effect of solidification rate on the stress rupture property  

574  Generally, the creep properties of hollow tubes are worse than those of solid bars  575  with a much higher diameter [6, 8, 44, 45], which has been attributed to  576  microstructural changes [9, 46], more pronounced effect of number of grains [6, 10]  577  and oxidation [47, 48]. In the present study, the stress rupture life of the bar was  578  significantly less than that of the tube after thermal exposure at 900 °C (Fig. 11),  579  which means that a thin-wall related debit of properties cannot be considered for this  580  creep rupture condition. Although the larger casting geometry size caused a coarser  581  microstructure in the bar, the grain number in the cross sections of the bar and tube  582  was close and was basically between 5-10 (Figs. 3(a) and (b)). The differences in the  583  original microstructure was negligible, considering the stress rupture lives of the bars  584  and tubes was close after the standard solution treatment. Furthermore, no serious  585  oxidation occurred during the creep rupture tests, as all tests lasted less than 65 h (Fig.  586  11). Therefore, the difference in creep property between the bar and tube was mainly  587  caused by the difference in microstructural degradation during thermal exposure.   588  During thermal exposure at 900 °C, the progressive degradation of  γ ’  precipitates  589  and carbides can explain the gradually decrease in stress rupture lives of the tube with  increasing thermal exposure time. Microstructural characterizations showed that the  degradation degree of  γ ’   phase and MC carbides were close in the tube and bar after  thermal exposure at 900 °C (Figs. 7 and 10, Table 2). This means that the additional  deterioration of the creep rupture lives of the bar could be attributed to the large  amount of  µ  phase that formed in the interdendritic regions of the bar after thermal  exposure at 900 °C. The  µ  phase was rich in W, Co and Mo (Table 4), and its  formation depleted these strengthening elements in the matrix. In addition, multiple  cracks initiated at the interface of  µ  phase and  γ  matrix, based on the fracture  morphology [12], leading to a reduction in the load bearing area and deterioration of  creep resistance. Although  µ  phase also precipitated in the tube during the stress  rupture tests (Figs. 12(b) and (c)), its quantity was siginifcantly lower than that of the  bar, and its damaging effect was less severe.  

In summary, this study used a solid bar and hollow tube to simulate the  microstructural evolution of a blade shank and airfoil, respectively. A full solution  treatment suppressed the formation of  µ  phase during thermal exposure. This study is  of high relevance to industry as it shows that the solidification rate during casting can  lead to striking differences in microstructural stability and mechanical properties, and  the optimization of the standard solution treatment need to be considered to ensure the  long-term safe service of K465 turbine blades.  

# 609  5. Conclusions  

The effect of solidification rate on microstructural stability and the stress rupture  property in K465 superalloy was revealed, combined with a systematic investigaton of  chemical  compositions,  microstructural  characterizations  and  dislocation  configurations of a hollow turbine blade, solid bars and hollow tubes made of K465  superalloy. The conclusions can be summarized as follows:   (1) For K465 superalloy turbine blade, the shank exhibited larger dendrites, a  higher degree of micro segregation and coarser carbides compared to the airfoil after  the standard solution treatment. A significant amount of  µ  phase formed only in the  shank, while much M 23 C 6  carbides precipitated in the airfoil after thermal exposure at  900 °C.  

(2) Considering the micro segregation behavior, standard solution-treated  microstructure and thermally-exposed microstructure at 900 °C, the blade shank and  bar were equivalent, while the blade airfoil and tube were similar. This suggests that  the bar and tube can simulate the microstructure of blade shank and airfoil,  respectively.  

(3) A slower solidification rate in the bar was primarily responsible for the larger  dendrites, higher degree of micro segregation and coarser carides in comparison with  the tube. Dislocations remained in the interdendriitc regions of the bar and tube after  the standard solution treatment, while the bar contained much denser dislocations.  

(4) The high dislocation density and significant micro segregation degree of the bar  were mainly responsible for the nucleation and growth of  µ  phase during thermal  exposure at 900 °C, respectively.  µ  phase could also nucleate on MC and M 6 C carbides  and grow towards the W and Mo rich dendrite cores. Meanwhile, the low dislocation  density in the tube suppressed the formation of  µ  phase and instead promoted the  formation of M 6 C and M 23 C 6  carbides.  

(5) The precipitation of  µ  phase caused a substantial decrease in stress rupture life  of the bar compared to the tube. A longer solution treatment successfully suppressed  the precipitation of  µ  phase in the bar during thermal exposure at 900 °C by  annihilating dislocations and reducing the degree of micro segregation more  effectively.
