# Two-Photon Photo degradation of E3 Ubiquitin Ligase Cereblon by a Ru(II) Complex: Inducing Ferroptosis in Cisplatin-Resistant Tumor Cells  

Xinlin Lin, Mengsi Zheng, Kai Xiong, Fa Wang, Yu Chen, \*  Liangnian Ji, and Hui Chao \*

ABSTRACT:  Using photodynamic therapy (PDT) to trigger nonconventional cell death pathways has provided a new scheme for highly efficient and non-side effects to drug-resistant cancer therapies. Nonetheless, the unclear targets of available photo- sensitizers leave the manner of PDT-induced tumor cell death relatively unpredictable. Herein, we developed a novel Ru(II)- based photosensitizer,  Ru-Poma . Possessing the E3 ubiquitin ligase CRBN-targeting moiety and high singlet oxygen yield of 0.96,  Ru- Poma  was demonstrated to specifically photodegrade endogenous CRBN, increase lipid peroxide, downregulate GPX4 and GAPDH expression, and consequently induce ferroptosis in cisplatin- resistant cancerous cells. # INTRODUCTION  

Cancer drug resistance is a challenge to cancer treatment. Conventional anticancer treatments, such as chemotherapy, radiotherapy, targeted therapy, and immunotherapy, mostly kill tumor cells by inducing apoptosis.   Cancers can develop resistance by dysregulation of apoptotic machinery, and the increasing prevalence of these drug-resistant cancers neces- sitates further research and treatment development.   In contrast to apoptosis, other programmed cell death, for example, ferroptosis, pyroptosis, and necroptosis, are different in terms of signaling pathways and critical proteins executing death, providing a theoretical basis for circumventing apoptosis resistance. ,   Recently, Gaiddon reported two Ru(II) com- plexes triggering nonapoptotic programmed cell death through endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress pathways and were more effective against apoptosis-resistant cells than the clinical drug oxaliplatin.   Su et al. reported an Ir(III) complex that induced mitophagy in cisplatin-resistant cancer cells.   Our group developed Ru(II) and Cu(II) complexes as necroptosis and oncosis triggers, respectively, and demonstrated excellent therapeutic efficiency against drug-resistant cancer cells in vivo. ,

Research on the targets of photo sensitize rs and their subsequent effects is still in its infancy. Sadler developed organoiridium photo sensitize rs and found them to oxidize specific residues of heat-shock protein-70 and aldose reductase by mass spectrometry.   Given the highly viscosity environ- ment of the cell and the limited range of action of singlet oxygen species, the target of PDT-generated ROS attack is highly dependent on photosensitizer localization. ,   There- fore, in contrast to the above-mentioned large-scale target identification approach, PDT’s site-specific damage and subsequent effects can be modulated by the introduction of targeting motifs on the photosensitizer. For example, Liu developed a photodegradant of glutathione peroxidase 4, PDTAC, by binding porphyrin photo sensitize rs to a GPX4- targeting peptide, which triggers ferroptosis.   Zhang demon- strated the targeted photolysis of estrogen receptor  α  by the organic photosensitizer combined with a protein of interest (POI) ligand, enabling an excellent anti proliferation perform- ance.   Hao incorporated a protein-targeting moiety into a Ru(II)-based photosensitize to degrade carbonic anhydrase IX and inhibit the related hypoxia pathway.   Aside from these few studies, this area remains underexplored, and their therapeutic effects in drug-resistant cancer cells need further evaluation.  

The ubiquitin-proteasome system (UPS) is crucial in regulating cellular homeostasis and metabolism, and as parts of which, there are E3 enzymes controlling the degradation of substrates and therefore emerging as promising drug targets. Cereblon (CRBN) is one of the most widely studied E3 ubiquitin ligases and is recognized as an essential target associated with a wide range of biological functions, including cancer development and drug resistance. ,   Various CRBN- based proteolysis targeting chimeras (PROTAC) molecules have been constructed to degrade disease-associated pro- teins, ,   but reports on CRBN inhibitors or the degradation of CRBN are rare.  

Given the background presented above, we synthesized and characterized a Ru(II)-based photosensitizer,  Ru-Poma ( Figure 1 A), incorporating the pomalidomide moiety acting as a CRBN warhead. Upon two-photon irradiation (810 nm), Ru-Poma  produced   1 O 2  and locally damaged CRBN. The antitumor activity of  Ru-Poma  was evaluated as the first metal- based CRBN photodegradable agent that triggers ferroptosis ( Scheme 1 ).  

# RESULTS AND DISCUSSION  

Synthesis and Characterization.  The synthetic routes are presented in  Scheme S1 . The pro-ligand 1,2-diphenyl-1H- imidazo[4,5- f ][1,10]phenanthroline (dpip), 4-(1-phenyl-1H- imidazo[4,5- f ][1,10] phenanthrolin-2-yl)phenol (dpip − OH), and the parent complex  Ru-dpip  without pomalidomide moiety were synthesized using a literature procedure.   The ligand dpip-poma was created by combining dpip − OH and pomalidomide with 1,4-dibromobutane ( Scheme S1 ).

Subsequently, the target product  Ru-Poma  was obtained from the precursor  cis -[Ru(dip) 2 Cl 2 ] · 2H 2 O (dip = 4,7- diphenyl-1,10-phenanthroline) with ligand dpip-poma. The complex was obtained in moderate yield (53%). The complex was characterized by ESI-MS spectrum, 1 H NMR and   13 C NMR spectroscopies, and elemental analysis ( Figures S1 − S3 ). The high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) trace was also obtained ( Figure S4 ). According to the elemental analysis and HPLC results, the purity of  Ru-Poma  was >95%.  

The octanol/water partition coefficients (log  P O/W ) assay showed that  Ru-Poma  is lipophilic with positive log  P O/W values of 1.22  ±  0.04, which is favorable for the application in biomedicine.  

Photophysical Properties.  The UV − vis spectrum and photo luminescence of  Ru-Poma  (1  ×  10 − 5   M) were recorded in phosphate buffer solution (PBS) containing 1% dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) at room temperature.  Ru-Poma  exhibits a broad absorption feature ranging from 350 to 520 nm with a peak at approximately 475 nm ( Figure 1 B), which is a characteristic metal−ligand charge transfer transition (MLCT)of Ru(II) complexes.   The emission spectrum exhibits a  λ em value of 625 nm ( Figure 1 C), with moderate quantum yields ( Φ ) of 0.042, with [Ru(bpy) 3 ] 2+   as the reference. Like other Ru(II)-based photo sensitize rs, ,   Ru-Poma  exhibits a sig- nificant Stokes shift, exceeding 150 nm.  

The FDA-approved photo sensitize rs PHOTOFRIN, Visu- dyne, and 5-ALA are single-photon excited. Shallow pene- tration depth (a few tens of  μ m) and uncontrolled excitation along the entire path of the light beam limit their application in tumor tissues growing in the depth of the body.   Developing two-photon excited photo sensitize rs is a feasible approach. The two-photon absorption cross section  σ 2  value was measured in methanol from 790 to 930 nm in 10 nm intervals by the reported protocol with rhodamine B as the reference. The result illustrated in  Figure 1 D indicated that the most considerable  σ 2  value of  Ru-Poma  appeared with an excitation wavelength at 810 nm. The  σ 2  value was 330 Goeppert-Mayer units (GM, 1 GM = 1  ×  10 50   cm · s 4 · photon − 1 · molecule − 1 ), which is much larger than that of tetraphenyl-porphyrin ( σ 2  = 2.2 GM). Importantly, this value is approximately twice that of the parent complex  Ru-dpip  ( σ 2  = 155 GM) without pomalidomide moiety,   indicating that introducing pomali- domide moiety improves the two-photon photophysical property.  

The two-photon induced emission spectra ( Figure S5 ) are similar to the single-photon excited emission spectrum. The inset result is the quadratic dependence of the two-photon- induced fluorescence intensity on the excitation power. The slopes of the logarithmic plots are approximately 2, confirming that  Ru-Poma  is two-photon-active.

Singlet Oxygen Generation.  Singlet oxygen is considered the main cytotoxic species in PDT processes.   The ability of Ru-Poma  to produce singlet oxygen was demonstrated by electron spin resonance (ESR) spectra in the presence of capture agent 2,2,6,6-tetramethyl-4-piperidone hydrochloride (TEMP). As shown in  Figure 2 A, a triple peak with an intensity of about 1:1:1, that is, the characteristic signal of TEMP − 1 O 2  adduct,   was observed between 3460 and 3560 G upon irradiation (450 nm, 6 J · cm − 2 , 4 min). To quantify the singlet oxygen generation quantum yield ( Φ Δ ) of  Ru-Poma , an indirect method was performed using 1,3-diphenyl iso benz of ur- an (DPBF) as a   1 O 2  scavenger ( Figure 2 B).   The  Φ Δ  values of Ru-Poma  in methanol were calculated to be 0.96 by the slope. The singlet oxygen yield is not only significantly higher than the FDA-approved photo sensitize rs PHOTOFRIN, Visudyne, and 5- ALA but also higher than  Ru-dpip  ( Φ Δ  = 0.77), , which indicates that  Ru-Poma  can generate 1 O 2  more efficiently than these compounds.  

Biological Studies.  Effect on Cell Viability.  The (photo)- cytotoxicity result of  Ru-Poma  against different cancerous cell lines is presented in  Table 1 .  Ru-Poma  was moderately cytotoxic (IC 50 , 8.92 − 18.46  μ M) in the dark toward cancer cell lines A549 and SW620 and their corresponding drug- resistant cell lines A549R and SW620/AD300. By contrast, Ru-Poma  showed significant photo sensitization activity against these cancerous cells with cytotoxic values ranging from 0.20 to 0.70  μ M (450 nm, 6 J · cm − 2 , 4 min). The photo cyto toxicity index (PI) values for A549, A549R, SW620, and SW620/ AD300 were 44.6, 49.9, 48.7, and 21.2, respectively. Since  Ru- Poma  exhibited the best PDT lesions in A549R cells (IC 50 , 18.46  ±  0.49  μ M in the dark and 0.37  ±  0.09  μ M upon irradiation), the following experiments were performed in A549R cells.  

Cellular Uptake and Distribution.  The cellular uptake of Ru-Poma  by A549R cells was qualitative, using confocal luminescence imaging. After 2 h of incubation, red emission from  Ru-Poma  was visible inside the cells. Its intensity increased with prolonged incubation time ( Figure S6 ). The cellular uptake was further quantitated by inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS). After incubation of  Ru- Poma  (5  μ M) for 3 h, the cellular Ru content was 18.36  ± 1.03 ng per 10 6   cells. The cellular uptake reached the plateau phase after 6 h of incubation with Ru contents of 45.74  ±  1.20 ng per 10 6   cells ( Figure S7 and Table S1 ). Notably, when  Ru- Poma -treated A549R cells were co-incubated with commercial organelle probes Hoechst 33342, ER-Tracker Green, Golgi- Tracker Green, MitoTracker Green, and LysoTracker Green, no significant fluorescence merge was observed ( Figure S8 ), indicating that  Ru-Poma  accumulated in the cytoplasm. This result differs from the parent complex  Ru-dpip , which is localized selectively in mitochondria.   Since the cellular CRBN proteins also distribute in the cytoplasm, we speculate that the cytoplasm targeting of  Ru-Poma  was attributed to the interaction of the pomalidomide moiety and CRBN proteins. Intracellular ROS Production and CRBN Degradation. The promising photocytotoxic properties of complex  Ru- Poma  encouraged us to investigate its cellular action further. A549R cells were treated with  Ru-Poma  (0.5  μ M) for 6 h and then maintained in the dark or exposed to 450 nm irradiation (6 J · cm − 2 , 4 min). ROS generation was examined using confocal microscopy with a 2 ′ ,7 ′ -dichlorodihydrofluorescein (DCFH) dye. As shown in  Figure 2 C, the evident green fluorescence of 2 ′ ,7 ′ -dich l oro fluoresce in (DCF), produced by the ROS-oxidation of DCFH within the cell, was observed. On the contrary, cells of control groups showed no evident fluorescence. The cellular ROS production was also determined by flow cytometry. Exposure of A549R cells to Ru-Poma  (0.5 and 1.0  μ M) in the dark did not modify the cellular ROS content. In contrast, the ROS levels increased by 7.4- and 6.6-fold upon irradiation ( Figure 2 D).

Cell Death Mechanism.  As mentioned above, apoptosis is the usual PDT-triggered mode of death. For example, the parent complex  Ru-dpip  without pomalidomide moiety was demonstrated as an apoptosis trigger.   Caspase 3 is the biochemical hallmark of both early- and late-stage apoptosis. As shown in  Figure 4 A, the PDT process of  Ru-Poma  (450 nm, 6 J · cm − 2 , 4 min) failed to activate caspase 3 in A549R cells, suggesting that apoptosis is not involved in  Ru-Poma - inducing cell death. In contrast, without a pomalidomide moiety, the  Ru-dpip -PDT resulted in an appropriate 6-fold enhancement in caspase 3/7 activity, confirming it is an apoptosis inducer. A cell death inhibition assay was carried out to understand the  Ru-Poma -PDT-induced cell death further. As shown in  Figure 4 B, after co-incubating with ferroptosis inhibitor ferrostatin-1 (Fer-1), the cell viability of  Ru-Poma - PDT increased from 36.13  ±  0.77% to 88.57  ±  15.40%. In contrast, the other cell death mode inhibitors, including apoptosis inhibitor Z-VAD-FMK (Z-V), lysosomal protease- mediated cell death inhibitor leupeptin (Leu), necrosis/ necroptosis inhibitor necrostain-1 (Nec-1), paraptosis inhibitor cycloheximide (Cyc), autophagic inhibitor 3-methyladenine (3-MA), and oncosis inhibitor DPQ, were ineffective. ,

Two-photon PDT in 3D Multicellular Tumor Spheroids (MCTSs).  MCTS provides a closer model of in vivo conditions of solid tumors and is the most widely exploited tissue model for the light penetration depth and PDT efficacy assessment. As expected, due to the location of the two-photon excitation wavelength in the therapeutic window for the tissue at 700 − 900 nm, Z-stack scanning showed a greater penetration depth of two-photon than single-photon excitation ( Figure 5 A). We then examined the two-photon-induced singlet oxygen generation in the model. The control group was photo- sensitizer-absent, and no fluorescence was detected before or after irradiation. A significant fluorescence enhancement was observed only by treating A549R MCTSs with  Ru-Poma followed by two-photon irradiation, indicating  Ru-Poma catalytically produces therapeutic species ( Figure 5 B).  

A long-term observation (8 days) was performed to further confirm the ability of  Ru-Poma -PDT to inhibit A549R MCTSs’ growth.  Figure S10  depicts representative optical images of MCTSs with different treatments. The volume change trend of MCTSs is summarized in  Figure 5 C. The volume of A549R MCTSs increases by about 2.5 times the initial volume in the absence of  Ru-Poma  or two-photon excitation. On the contrary, the volume of A549R MCTSs remained the same after two-photon PDT of  Ru-Poma . The cell viability of the A549R MCTSs was determined by calcein- AM staining. As shown in  Figure 5 D, for  Ru-Poma -treated MCTSs, a significant reduction of calcein-AM fluorescence was observed after irradiation, indicating cell death.  

# CONCLUSIONS  

In this study, we reported the first sample of a CRBN-targeting Ru(II) complex,  Ru-Poma , active in two-photon PDT, by introducing pomalidomide into previously reported Ru(II) photosensitizer  Ru-dpip .  Ru-Poma  exhibited a high singlet oxygen yield (up to 96%) and two-photon absorption cross section value (330 GM), superior to those of  Ru-dpip  (0.77 and 155 GM). Different from the mitochondria-targeting character of  Ru-dpip ,  Ru-Poma  distributed in the cytoplasm, consistent with the localization of endogenous CRBN. The CETSA results showed that the abundance of CRBN protein in the  Ru-Poma -free cells decreased with increasing temper- atures but remained stable in  Ru-Poma -treated cells, confirming the couple of  Ru-Poma  and CRBN protein.  Ru- Poma  was demonstrated to specifically photodegrade endogenous CRBN, increase lipid peroxide, downregulate GPX4 and GAPDH expression, and induce ferroptosis in cisplatin-resistant cancerous cells, significantly different from the apoptosis inducer  Ru-dpip . However, the relationship between CRBN degradation and ferroptosis remains unclear, and we are actively investigating this aspect. With the deep penetration of two-photon excitation,  Ru-Poma  achieved drug- resistant circumvention in a 3D tumor cell model. These results highlight that the presence of pomalidomide is crucial to achieving a marked change in their photophysical properties and PDT behavior, supporting the feasibility of our design strategy. We believe that this work opens a new avenue for developing promising photo sensitize rs for drug-resistant cancer treatment.

Synthesis.  The precursor [Ru(dip) 2 Cl 2 ] · 2H 2 O, the pro-ligand 1,2- diphenyl-1H-imidazo[4,5- f ][1,10]phenanthroline (dpip), 4-(1-phe- nyl-1H-imidazo[4,5- f  ][1,10] phenanthrolin-2-yl)phenol (dpip − OH), and the parent complex Ru-dpip without pomalidomide moiety were synthesized using a literature procedure.  

Synthesis of 2-(4-(4-Bromobutoxy)phenyl)-1-phenyl-1H- imidazo[4,5-f ][1,10]phenanthroline (dpip-Br).  A mixture of dpip − OH (0.20 g, 0.5 mmol), 1,4-dibromobutane (0.50 g, 2.5 mmol), and potassium carbonate (0.35 g, 2.5 mmol) was suspended in 25 mL of CH 3 CN and refluxed for 12 h to give a light-yellow solution. After the reaction, the solution was cooled and evaporated to dryness. The product was used directly in the next step without purification.  

Synthesis of the Ligand dpip-Poma.  A mixture of dpip-Br (0.10 g, 0.2 mmol), pomalidomide (0.03 g, 0.1 mmol), and potassium carbonate (0.07 g, 0.5 mmol) was stirred in 10 mL of DMF under an argon atmosphere at room temperature for 4 h. After the reaction was complete, the hot mixture was filtered to give an orange solution, in which DMF was removed by evaporation. The residue was purified by silica gel chromatography using CH 2 Cl 2 /CH 3 OH (10:1, v/v) as the eluent to afford a pale-yellow product (Yield = 47.3%).  

Synthesis of  Ru-Poma . (Photo)Cytotoxicity Assay.  The (photo)cytotoxicity of  Ru- Poma  toward cancerous cell lines A549, A549R, SW620, and SW620/ AD300 was investigated by the classic MTT assay. For the cytotoxicity assay, cells were seeded in 96-well plates at 5  ×  10 3 cells per well for 24 h and then treated with different concentrations of tested drugs for 48 h. MTT solution (20  μ L, 5 mg/mL) was added into each well for 4 h. Finally, the supernatant medium was removed and added to each well with 150  μ L of DMSO. The absorbance was measured after shaking for 180 s at 590 nm using a microplate reader (SpectraMax CMax Plus, Molecular Devices).  

For photo cyto toxicity assay under normoxic conditions, the cells were incubated for 24 h and then treated with different concentrations of tested drugs for 24 h. The medium was removed and replaced with 200  μ L of fresh medium. The cells were irradiated by LED (450 nm, 6 J · cm − 2 , 4 min). After they were incubated for another 24 h, the procedure was identical to cytotoxicity assay and was detected absorption at 595 nm by a microplate reader.  

Cellular Uptake.  The isotope of ruthenium tested in the experiment was chosen to be 101Ru. The calibration process was conducted by professional workers who were in charge of the ICP-MS machine. A549R cells were seeded in 10 cm 2   culture plates at 5  ×  10 5 cells per plate and incubated for 24 h to adherent. The cells were incubated with  Ru-Poma  (5  μ M) for 3, 6, 9, 12, and 24 h, respectively. The cells were collected carefully after being washed with cold PBS three times. Next, the cells were counted and completely digested with 0.5 mL of concentrated HNO 3  and 1.0 mL of H 2 O 2  at room temperature for 2 days. The samples were diluted with Milli-Q water to 10 mL with 3% of HNO 3 . The concentrations of Ru were determined by ICP-MS. Data were reported as the mean  ±  standard deviation ( n  = 3).  

Confocal Luminescence Imaging.  A549R cells were plated onto 35 mm confocal microscope specialized cell culture dishes and incubated for 24 h to allow adherence.  

Visualization of Cellular Uptake.  A549R cells were incubated with Ru-Poma  for 2, 4, and 6 h, respectively. The cells were washed with cold PBS three times and then imaged by a confocal microscope.

Determination of Ru-Poma Interaction with CRBN.  Cellular Thermal Shift Assay (CETSA).  The CETSA procedure was conducted as previously reported.   Briefly, the cells were treated at the indicated concentrations of  Ru-Poma  for 6 h. Then, the cells were collected and washed by ice-cold PBS. Subsequently, the cell pellet was resuspended in ice-cold PBS containing 1 ×  protease inhibitor cocktail and aliquoted into six tubes. The tubes were heated at the indicated temperature for 3 min and then immediately incubated at 25  ° C for another 3 min. The samples were then put through three free − thaw cycles to lyse the cells fully. Cell lysates were centrifuged (4  ° C, 21,000 g ) for 45 min to remove the insoluble fractions. The soluble content was administrated to the following Western Blot steps for detection.  

MALDI-TOF-MS Analysis.  CRBN (20  μ M) protein and  Ru-Poma (20  μ M) were mixed in Milli-Q H 2 O for 10 min, and then the mixture was irradiated with 450 nm LED (6 J · cm − 2 , 4 min). The molecular weight of the mixture was determined using a Bruker ultrafleXtreme matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization time-of-flight mass spec- trometry (MALDI-TOF-MS).  

Cell Death Mechanism.  Cell Death Inhibitors Assay.  A549R cells were seeded in 96-well plates at 5  ×  10 3   cells per well overnight. The cells were treated with  Ru-Poma  (0.5  μ M) for 12 h and then incubated with ferroptosis inhibitor ferrostatin-1 (Fer-1), apoptosis inhibitor Z-VAD-FMK (Z-V), lysosomal protease-mediated cell death inhibitor leupeptin (Leu), necrosis/necroptosis inhibitor necrostain-1 (Nec-1), paraptosis inhibitor cycloheximide (Cyc), autophagic inhibitor 3-methyladenine (3-MA), or oncosis inhibitor DPQ for 30 min. The culture medium was removed, and 200  μ M new mediums were added. The samples were exposed to 450 nm light (6 J · cm − 2 , 4 min) and then incubated in the dark for 10 h. MTT solution (20  μ L, 5 mg/mL) was added into each well for 2 h. Finally, the supernatant medium was removed and added to each well with 150  μ L of DMSO. The absorbance was measured after shaking for 180 s at 590 nm using a microplate reader (SpectraMax CMax Plus, Molecular Devices). The experiments were performed in triplicate ( n  = 3).  

Caspase 3/7 Activation Assay.  A549R cells were seeded in white 96-well plates overnight.  Ru-Poma  (0.5  μ M),  Ru-dpip  (1  μ M), or cisplatin (15  μ M) was added and treated for 12 h. Then, the cells were incubated for another 12 h after 450 nm area LED irradiation (6 J · cm − 2 , 4 min). The caspase 3/7 activation was determined by the commercial kit (Promega) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. The results were obtained by an Infinite M200 PRO (TECAN, Swiss). Values and standard deviations are derived from three independent experiments (mean  ±  SD,  n  = 3).  

Western Blotting.  A549R cells adhered to 100 mm culture dishes for 48 h. The old medium was replaced by a fresh medium containing Ru-Poma  (0.5  μ M) or  Ru-dpip  (1  μ M). After being cultured for 12 h, the cells were irradiated with 450 nm LED (6 J · cm − 2 ) for 4 min and continued to incubate for 6 h. Afterward, the cells were collected and lysed, and intracellular proteins were extracted by differential centrifugation (Cell lysis buffer, PMSF, 1 mM). The extracted protein was quantified with a BCA protein detection kit. Samples were electrophoresed in 10% polyacrylamide gel and transferred to a PVDF membrane. PVDF membranes were blocked with 5% skim milk and incubated with primary antibodies against CRBN (goat anti-rabbit), GPX4 (goat anti-rabbit), and GAPDH (goat anti-rabbit) overnight at 4  ° C. After washing with TBST 3 times, PVDF membranes were incubated with secondary antibody Goat Anti-Rabbit IgG H&L (HRP) for 2 h. Subsequent staining was performed with a DAB horseradish peroxidase chromogenic kit (Thermo Fisher), and chemiluminescence was detected with Omega Lum C.  

Two-Photon Performance Assay.  3D MCTSs Formation. A549R single-cell suspensions at a density of 1  ×  10 5   cells per mL were transferred to 1% agarose-coated transparent 96-well plates with a volume of 200  μ L per well. The cells were incubated in an incubator, and the culture medium was refreshed every 2 days. A549R MCTSs aggregates formed spontaneously in 3 days with a diameter of approximately 300 − 500  μ m.  

Z-Axis Scanning of 3D MCTSs.  3D MCTSs pretreated with 10  μ M Ru-Poma  for 48 h in the dark were carefully washed three times with PBS and subjected to confocal microscopy. The one-/two-photon excited luminescent images of sections along the  z -axis were captured and stacked in the z-stack mode to give a final one-/two-photon  z -axis stack imaging.  

Two-Photon Excited Singlet Oxygen Generation.  3D MCTSs were treated with 10  μ M  Ru-Poma  for 48 h in the dark. The culture medium was removed, and the cells were allowed to incubate for an additional 30 min with 2 mL of PBS containing 10  μ M DCFH-DA. The culture medium was refreshed with fresh PBS after this period and subjected to two-photon irradiation (100 mW, 80 MHz, 100 fs) at 810 nm for 3 min/section (section interval: 3  μ m) using a laser source equipped in an LSM 880 Carl Zeiss laser scanning confocal microscope, which captured fluorescent images ( λ ex = 488 nm,  λ em = 515  ±  10 nm).  

3D MCTSs Growth Inhibition and Viability Assay.  3D MCTSs were treated with 10  μ M  Ru-Poma  and incubated in the dark for 2 days. Then, the A549R MCTSs were subjected to two-photon irradiation (100 mW, 80 MHz, 100 fs) at 810 nm for 3 min/section (section interval: 3  μ m) using the same equipment noted above, and the samples were incubated in the dark for an additional 6 days. The culture media were refreshed every 2 days without altering the drug concentration. All of the procedures were conducted strictly in the dark. Images and diameter data of the 3D MCTSs were collected every 48 h. MCTSs volume changes were recorded over 8 days. 3D MCTSs were stained with Calcein-AM ( λ ex = 488 nm,  λ em = 515  ±  10 nm) solutions in fresh medium for 60 min. Images were obtained using an LSM 880 Carl Zeiss laser scanning confocal microscope.
