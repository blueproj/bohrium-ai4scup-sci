# The Effects of Deformation and Pre-Heat-Treatment on Abnormal Grain Growth in RENE ´ 88 Superalloy  

YOUNG KYU CHO, DUK YONG YOON, and MICHAEL F. HENRY  

When an extruded strain-free RENE ´ 88 Ni-base superalloy about 1 to 2  m m in grain diameter is heat- treated at 1150  8 C, abnormal grain growth (AGG) begins after 50 hours. When heat-treated at 1200 8 C, AGG occurs at 15 minutes. Some of the grain boundaries are faceted with hill-and-valley structures when observed in transmission electron microscopy (TEM), and the occurrence of AGG is consistent with the boundary step and dislocation mechanism for the migration of singular boundaries with faceted shapes, as observed and proposed in other pure metals and alloys. The dissolution of abundant g 8  precipitates (with a solvus temperature of 1107  8 C), which are coherent with the matrix and hence strongly pin the grain boundaries, does not cause AGG during early stages of heat-treatment at 1150 8 C. Small deformations drastically alter the AGG behavior. When deformed to 4 pct, AGG begins after heat-treating for 10 minutes at 1150  8 C, compared to the apparent incubation time of 50 hours for an undeformed specimen, and very large abnormal grains are produced. With increasing deformation to 6 and 9.2 pct, the abnormal grain size decreases. These results are qualitatively similar to those observed in Cu. This deformation effect on AGG is attributed to the absorption of lattice dislocations in the grain boundaries, which produces nonequilibrium structures that, in turn, can apparently cause rapid boundary migration. When heat-treated at 1200  8 C, the largest abnormal grains are found in the specimens deformed to 2 pct. When the initial grain size is increased to about 14  m m by heat- treating the extruded alloy at 1150  8 C for 30 minutes, similar low deformation effects on AGG are observed. When these specimens are deformed to 10, 13, and 15.2 pct, primary recrystallization occurs during the heat-treatment at 1150  8 C, and large abnormal grains are again produced because of the small recrystallized grain size. Therefore, there are two peaks in the grain size  vs  deformation curve after heat-treating at 1150  8 C for 1 hour. A pre-heat-treatment of this alloy at 1050  8 C below the solvus temperature of the  g 8  phase greatly reduces the size of the abnormal grains obtained in the specimen deformed to 4 pct after the heat-treatment at 1150  8 C, probably because some recovery of the dislocations takes place at grain boundaries during the pre-heat-treatment. The deformation effect on AGG observed in this alloy is qualitatively similar to that previously observed in Cu and appears to be consistent with the boundary step and dislocation mechanism for AGG.  

# I. INTRODUCTION  

I T  has been observed   in several pure metals and alloys that small deformations on the order of a few percent, below the levels required for recrystallization, can accelerate abnor- mal grain growth (AGG). Under certain conditions, the time required for AGG during the isothermal heat-treatments of undeformed specimens is so long that AGG may appear to be caused by the small deformations, if the heat-treatments are carried out only for relatively short periods. In practical heat-treatments, very large grains may appear in the small deformation region of a product in the early stages and spread to the surrounding regions of finer grains as the heat- treatments continue.  

In order to understand the effect of a small deformation on AGG, it is necessary to know the mechanism of AGG in the systems that have no deformation. In pure metals and single-phase alloys without any textures, it was recently observed   that AGG occurred at relatively low tempera- tures when most of the grain boundaries had faceted hill- and-valley structures. Because the faceted grain boundary segments are likely to be singular, corresponding to the cusps in the polar plots of the grain boundary energy against the grain boundary normal (or the inclination angle), they can move either by nucleating boundary steps or through the movement of grain boundary dislocations, as has been pro- posed by Gleiter,   Schober, Balluffi,   Rae, and Smith. Then, only a few large grains can grow at increasing rates to cause AGG.   At high temperatures close to the melting points, the grain boundaries become defaceted, with smoothly curved shapes, which indicate atomically rough structures. Then, atoms can readily move across the grain boundaries, causing continuous grain boundary motion and, hence, normal grain growth.  

If AGG is caused by the migration of singular grain bound- aries through the step mechanism, it can be strongly depend- ent on defects that can be produced at these grain boundaries. Extrinsic dislocations are produced at grain boundaries by deformation, and during annealing, the migrating grain boundaries encounter more dislocations in the grains. The absorption process of these dislocations at the grain bound- aries has been extensively studied by transmission electron microscopy (TEM)   in order to elucidate the recrystalli- zation mechanism. Recently, the effects of both small and large deformations on AGG in bulk Cu polycrystal have been investigated.   Small deformations of about 2 pct were observed to accelerate the AGG process in bulk Cu polycrys- talline specimens of initially large (about 168  m m in average size) grains during heat-treatments at 800  8 C and 600  8 C. With increasing deformation up to the level required for recrystallization (about 10 pct), the number of the abnor- mally growing large grains increased and, hence, the average size after their impingement decreased. The TEM observa- tions after the heat-treatments showed that the grain bound- aries were heavily distorted, apparently by the interaction with the extrinsic dislocations, and it was therefore proposed that the effect of a small deformation on AGG arose from the increased mobilities of these grain boundaries with metastable structures produced by extrinsic dislocations. Similar observations and suggestions have also been made by Valiev  et al.   for a magnesium alloy but without realizing that such an effect would arise only for singular boundaries that move by a step mechanism. If grain boundaries have a rough atomic structure (at temperatures close to the melting point), their mobilities and, hence, the grain growth behavior will not be much changed by the extrinsic dislocations. In such a case, there will only be a momentary, slight increase in the driving force for normal grain growth produced by small deformations.

The second goal of this work is to examine the possibility of suppressing or reducing the effect of small deformations by pre-heat-treating the deformed specimens at a temperature below 1107  8 C, where some recovery may occur at grain boundaries and in grains while they are pinned by the precipi- tates. # II. EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURES  

The nominal composition of the commercial Ni-base superalloy RENE ´ 88 used in this study was 12.87Co,

 15.74Cr, 4.09Mo, 4.16W, 2.06Al, 3.65Ti, 0.71Nb, 0.051C,

 0.015B, 0.045Zr, and balance Ni in wt pct.   The solvus temperature of the  g 8  phase of this alloy is 1107  8 C. A large plate of the alloy was made by the hot extrusion of gas- atomized, compacted powder. Cylindrical specimens, 10 mm in diameter and 12 mm in height, were cut out of this plate by the electric discharge process and were compressed at room temperature to various amounts between 2 and 12.5 pct (as measured by the height change) in a universal test machine at a strain rate of 3  3  10 2 3 /s. The compression axis was parallel to the extrusion direction. The compressed specimens were heat-treated at 1150  8 C and 1200  8 C in dry argon or in a sealed quartz tube under vacuum in a tube furnace. The specimens were rapidly pushed into the pre- heated furnace and pulled out rapidly after heat-treatments for quenching in water. The heat-treatment times were meas- ured from the time the specimen was placed at the center of the furnace tube to the time it was pulled out. From the color change of the specimens, they appeared to reach the set temperatures in about 1 minute. The heat-treatments were carried out for 1 hour for most of the specimens, but some were heat-treated for various times at these temperatures.  

Another series of specimens was prepared by first heat- treating the specimens of the as-extruded alloy at 1150  8 C for 30 minutes in order to increase the initial grain size. These specimens were then compressed and heat-treated identically, as were those in the first series with the as- received alloy specimens. In another experimental series, the specimens prepared by compressing the as-received alloy pieces were pre-heat-treated at 1050  8 C (below the  g 8 -solvus temperature) for 16 hours before heat-treating again at 1150 8 C for 1 hour.  

The heat-treated specimens were sectioned parallel to their circular faces through their centers and etched in Kalling solution (2.5 g CuCl 2 , 50 mL hydrochloric acid, and 50 mL ethanol) after polishing for optical microscopy. The maximum and the average sizes of about 300 grains were measured for each specimen with an image analyzer.  

# III. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

The grain boundaries and grains appeared to be relatively free of dislocations. In the main experimental series of this work, the as-received alloy piece was used as the starting material without any heat- treatment. Each specimen prepared from the as-received alloy will be designated by deformation/heat-treatment temperature/heat-treatment time. Thus, for example, 4 pct/ 1150  8 C/10 min will be the designation for the specimen deformed to 4 pct and heat-treated at 1150  8 C for 10 minutes. The specimen series heat-treated for various times at the same temperature will be designated by its deformation/ heat-treatment temperature; thus, for example, 4 pct/1150 8 C will be the designation for the series heat-treated for various times at 1150  8 C after deforming to 4 pct.

It is therefore likely that at least some grain boundaries in the undeformed specimens shown in Figures 2 and 3 also had faceted structures. It was shown earlier that AGG occurred when all or some of the grain boundaries were faceted in Ni,   Cu,   a model Ni-base superalloy,   and 316L stainless steel   at low tem- peratures and in alumina   and BaTiO 3  with additives. It has been proposed that AGG can occur with faceted grain boundaries, because the singular grain boundary segments move either by nucleating boundary steps   or on existing steps produced by dislocations.   Earlier, Gleiter observed that in an Al-Cu alloy, grain growth occurred by the move- ment of spirals of grain boundary steps;   he formulated a theory of grain boundary migration based on the atom attachment to and detachment from these grain boundary steps.   This mechanism, as pointed out by Gleiter, is similar to the spiral growth of screw dislocation steps on a crystal surface, and predicts a nonlinear variation of the boundary migration rate with the driving force.   Schober and Balluffi   observed extrinsic grain boundary disloca- tion spirals in high-angle, twist-grain boundaries in Au, and also proposed that the grain boundary migration could occur by the movement of these grain boundary dislocation spirals or by nucleating grain boundary steps. On the basis of  in-situ  TEM observations of the movement of extrinsic grain boundary dislocations and grain boundary steps, Rae and Smith   also proposed that these could contribute to the grain boundary migration. If the grain boundary steps are nucleated, the boundary migration rate will vary more strongly with the driving force than was predicted by Gleiter.   As for the growth of crystals from vapor or melt, the step growth mechanism can only operate when the grain boundaries consist of singular segments. If the grain boundaries are rough, with a large number of thermally induced steps, their migration rate will be limited only by the rate of net atom jump across the boundary.  

The driving force for the growth (or shrinkage) of a grain is determined by the size of that grain relative to the grains surrounding it, the average size of which may be assumed to be equal to the average of all grains. As proposed ear- lier,   in the early stages of heat-treatment with initially uniform grain sizes, all grains will grow slowly, limited by the low rate of step nucleation or growth, while maintaining a nearly uniform grain size distribution. It has been sug- gested that such a growth behavior be called stagnant growth,   in order to distinguish it from normal growth. During the heat-treatment at 1150  8 C, all grains appeared to undergo this stagnant growth for up to 10 hours, as shown in Figure 2(a) and (b). Even during AGG, fine matrix grains underwent stagnant growth, as shown in Figure 2(c) and (d). Similar stagnant growth behavior of the matrix grains before and after the onset of AGG was observed also in Cu.  

Even during the stagnant growth, some large grains will grow faster than the average because of the nonlinear varia- tion of the migration rate with the size difference. When the size difference between the large grain and the average grain becomes sufficiently large, the growth of the large grains will accelerate with the high step nucleation rate, initiating AGG, as shown in Figure 2(c). There thus appears to be an incubation time for AGG. Because the step nucle- ation and the atom transport will be faster at higher tempera- tures, AGG occurs earlier at higher temperatures, as observed at 1200  8 C (Figure 3).  

It was earlier proposed   that AGG in superalloys occurred when the precipitates, which had been pinning the grain boundaries, were dissolved upon heating above the solvus temperature. Valiev  et al.   proposed that the nonequilibrium grain boundaries had higher mobilities than did the grain bound- aries with equilibrium structures; this enhanced the rate of AGG. If the singular grain boundaries migrate by the movement of grain boundary steps and extrinsic grain boundary dislocations, as proposed by Gleiter,   Schober and Balliffi,   and Rae and Smith,   the effective boundary mobility will increase with increasing deformation because the density of the extrinsic grain boundary dislocations will increase. It is possible that the step nucleation rate will also increase. At low deformations, the nonequilibrium grain boundaries may vary in their degrees of deviation from the equilibrium structure and, hence, in their mobilities. Then, a few grains can probably grow faster than others at the early stages of heat-treatment, and thus shorten the incuba- tion time for AGG and produce only a few large grains, as observed in the specimens deformed to 4 pct.

It thus appeared that, at the critical deformations, the variations in the non- equilibrium structures of the grain boundaries produced by absorbing the lattice dislocations were the largest.

# IV. CONCLUSIONS  

The first important result of this study is that AGG in this alloy does not occur when the  g 8  precipitates, which strongly pin the grain boundaries, are dissolved during the heat-treatments at temperatures above the  g 8 -solvus temper- ature, as has been proposed for a general mechanism of AGG. Instead, it appears that AGG occurs because of the faceted grain boundaries in such pure metals as Ni   and

Cu.   Small deformations in the range between 1 and 4 pct were observed to shorten the incubation time for AGG drastically and to increase the size of the abnormal grains. This effect is attributed to the selective increase of the mobil- ities of some grain boundaries because of the dislocations produced by the deformation. The observed small deforma- tion effect is thus consistent with the step growth mechanism for the singular grain boundaries with faceted shapes.  

It should be noted that the variation of the grain size with deformation will depend on the heat-treatment time. In this report, the results obtained after heat-treating for 1 hour were primarily shown. If the heat-treatments were carried out for longer periods until AGG occurred also in the unde- formed specimens, the grain sizes at 0 deformation will be much larger than those obtained after heat-treating for 1 hour. The pre-heat-treatment at a temperature below the solvus temperature was shown to reduce the small deforma- tion effect on AGG in this alloy. Such a pre-heat-treatment effect can be general in other alloys.
