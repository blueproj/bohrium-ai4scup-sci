# Thermodynamic Analysis of Carbide Precipitation and Effect of Its  Configuration on Creep Properties of FGH95 Powder Nickel-based  Superalloy  

D. L. Shu 1 , S. G. Tian  , N. Tian 2 , J. Xie 1 , Y. Su 1  

1. Shenyang University of Technology, Shenyang 110870, China  2. Guizhou University of Engineering Science, Bijie, 551700, China  

Abstract : By means of heat treatment at various regimes, creep property measurement, microstructure  observation and thermodynamic analysis, the precipitation behaviors of carbides and effect of its configuration on  the creep properties of FGH95 alloy are investigated. Results show that after solution treated at 1155 ℃  and fully  heat treatment, the microstructure of alloy consists of fine γ' phase, γ matrix, (Nb, Ti)C and M 23 C 6  carbides. The  excellent creep life of alloy at 1034MPa/650 ℃  is attributed to some fine carbide particles precipitating along  boundaries to restrain dislocations and boundaries slipping. Moreover, the boundaries appear in the original PPB  regions of alloy after solution treated at 1165 ℃ , and the carbide films are continuously distributed along  boundaries to weaken the bonding strength between grains, which are thought to be the main reason of the alloy  displaying a weakest creep resistance. By means of thermodynamic calculation, the precipitation temperatures of  NbC and TiC carbides are determined to be 1080 ℃  and 807 ℃ , respectively. Compared to (Nb,Ti)C carbide, the  reason of no WC carbide precipitated in the alloy is attributed to its smaller driving force of nucleation.  

Key words : FGH95 alloy, solution temperature, creep properties, MC carbide, thermodynamic analysis  

# 1. Introduction  

The microstructure of powder nickel-based superalloys consists mainly of γ matrix, γ   phase and carbides,  and the sizes, configuration and distribution of γ   phase and carbides may be adjusted by heat treatment regimes  [1-4]. Especially, various carbides may be precipitated in the alloys, such as MC, M 6 C and M 23 C 6  carbides[5], and  their configuration has an obvious effect on the mechanical and creep properties of alloys[6, 7]. Therefore, the  influence of heat treatment regimes on the configurations and kinds of carbides is widely investigated[8].  

During heat treatment, the various carbides may be precipitated in the powder nickel-based superalloys, and  the sequence of the carbides stability is identified as MC    M 6 C   M 23 C 6 [9], in turn. Wherein, the carbides with  MC type in the alloys may stably exist in the temperature ranges of 760 \~ 1150 ℃ , their morphologies are related  to the solution temperature and cooling rate during heat treatment. After solution treated at the temperature being  lower than 1160 ℃  and cooled rapid to ambient temperature, the particle-like MC carbide is dispersedly  precipitated in alloy[10]. As the cooling rate decreases, the skeleton-like MC carbide is precipitated in the  inter-dendrite region[11].  

During solution treated at temperature being higher than 1160 ℃ , the MC carbide in alloys may be partly  dissolved, and then the carbides with M 6 C and M 23 C 6  structures may re-precipitate during aging[12]. Wherein, the  carbide with M 23 C 6  type along boundaries is usually attributed to the degenerating reactions of MC carbide, or the  precipitation of supersaturated carbon in the matrix of alloys. Some investigations show[13] that the Cr atoms are  mainly dissolved in the M 23 C 6  carbide, and the Cr atoms in the Cr 23 C 6  may be replaced by the Mo, W atoms with  bigger radius to form the Cr 21 (Mo,W) 2 C 6  carbide. When the mass fraction of Mo + W in the alloys is more than  6--8 wt%, the M 6 C carbide may precipitate prior along boundaries in the form of particle-like and film-like  configuration. Due to the composition and structure of M 6 C carbide being similar to the ones of TCP phase, the  

precipitate along boundaries, the pinning role of them may restrain the slipping of boundaries to improve the creep  resistance of alloys[15]. But the effect of the film-like carbides on the creep resistance of alloys is not clear.  

It is thought according to the analysis of inter-atomic affinity that the NbC, TiC, WC and M 23 C 6  carbides  may be precipitated in the nickel-based powder superalloys, but actually no WC carbide is detected in the alloys[1,  16]. Although the kinds, size, configuration and distribution of carbides may be adjusted by controlling some  thermodynamic and kinetic factors of carbides nucleating and growing[5, 17-19], the configuration and  distribution of carbides are sensitive to the thermodynamic and kinetic factors. In addition, the thermodynamic  conditions of precipitating carbides are still unclear.  

Hereby, by means of solution treatment at various temperatures, microstructure observations, the influence of  solution temperature on the precipitation behaviors of carbides in FHG95 powder alloy is investigated. Combined  with the analysis of thermodynamic on the precipitating temperatures and driving-forces of carbides, the  precipitating behaviors and affecting factors of carbides in the powder nickel-based superalloys are investigated.  

# 2. Experimental procedure  

The powder particles of FGH95 nickel-based superalloy with the size of about 150 meshes were put into a  stainless steel can, and then the can was hot isostatic pressing (HIP) treated for 4 h under the condition of 120  MPa at 1120  ℃  to preparing the ingot of FGH95 alloy. The chemical composition of the ingot is listed in Table 1.  In order to investigate the influence of solution temperatures on the kinds, size, configuration and distribution of  carbides, the ingot of FGH95 superalloy was cut into the billets with the size of 40 mm    15 mm    14 mm, and  the billets are solution treated for 30 min, respectively, at various temperatures, and immersed rapid in the “molten  salt” at 550 ℃  for keeping 20 min, and cooled in the air. And then the billets are kept for 1 h at 870 ℃  and kept for  24 h at 650 ℃ , for carrying out twice aging treatment. The error of the heat treatment furnace is about    2 ℃ . The  used heat treatment regimes of the alloy are shown in Table 2.  

![Table 1 Chemical composition of powder nickel based superalloy  ](images/96f33afc7dbe0ce504acbe15e2876d259d6de7d56a98464b4338ce13b88ebf7a.jpg)  

After heat treated at various regimes, the billets are cut into the creep specimens with the cross-section of 4.5  mm    2.5 mm and the gage length of 20 mm. After ground and polished, the specimens are placed in a creep  testing machine with GWT504 model, the creep properties of the specimens at 650 ℃ /1034MPa are measured, to  investigate the effect of solution temperature on the creep properties of the alloy. The microstructures of the  specimens are observed under SEM/TEM, and the chemical composition in micro-area and phases constitution of  the specimens are analyzed, under TEM/EDS and XRD, to investigate the effect of solution temperature on the  carbides configuration of FGH95 superalloy.  

![Table 2. Heat treatment regimes of FGH95 alloy  ](images/fe95ae8ad2333a4097d962477426caa24aecc9f8b987dfc8854e2c0afe759ecc.jpg)  

The thermodynamic conditions of precipitating carbides, such as the driving forces of phase transformation  and nucleation, must be met when the ones are precipitated from γ matrix. In the paper, the driving forces of the  phase transformation and nucleation of the precipitating carbides are calculated by means of calculating the excess  free energies of the precipitating carbides with various structures[20]. In the further, the precipitating temperatures  of some carbides are identified according to the dependence of Gibbs free energy of precipitating carbides on  temperature[21]. This may provide the theory base for choosing the heat treatment regimes.

4. Thermodynamic analysis of MC carbide precipitation  

4.1 Driving forces of precipitating MC carbides  

It is indicated from Fig. 6 that the creep properties of alloy are related to the temperature of solution treatment,  wherein, the alloy solution treated at 1155 ℃   displays the longest creep life of about 283 h at 650 ℃ / 1034 MPa.  The particle-like carbides distributing within grains and along boundaries may hinder the dislocations movement  and restrain the slipping of boundaries (as shown in Fig. 7 and Fig. 8), which is thought to be the main reason of  the alloy displaying an excellent creep properties. The reason of the alloy treated at 1165 ℃   displaying the shortest  creep life is attributed to the carbide films along boundaries to decrease the bonding strength between grains[23].  Therefore, it is concluded that the carbide configuration possesses an important effect on the creep properties of  alloy. Actually the precipitated configurations of carbides are related to the thermodynamic and dynamics  conditions during heat treatment, so the thermodynamic conditions and effect factors of carbides precipitation  need to be calculated, by means of thermodynamic method, to provide a basis for drafting the heat treatment  regime.  

In order to simplify the thermodynamic calculation, the multi-component alloy is simplified as several binary  and ternary solid solutions, the assumptions in the calculation are given as follows:  

1) During solution treatment at high temperature, the components Mo, Cr, Co, Al, W, Nb, Ti and C are  completely dissolved in the Ni matrix, which may form the Ni(  ) solid solution when the components alloy is  rapidly dipped into the “molten salt” at 550 ℃ .  

2) During cooling, the components alloy may form the Ni-M (M=Nb, Ti, W)-C solid solutions, respectively,  and the atoms Nb, Ti, W and C may be precipitated from the Ni-Nb-C, Ni-Ti-C and Ni-W-C solid solutions,  effect on the free energies of the precipitated carbides.  

3) The solutes Mo, Co and Al are completely dissolved in the solid solution because they are not the former  of carbides. And the thermodynamic calculation of precipitating Cr 23 C 6  phase is omitted since the atom Cr can  only form a few Cr 23 C 6  carbide in the alloy.  

4) The solutes M (M = Nb, Ti, W) dissolved in Ni matrix are regard as the Ni(M) solid solution, the solutes  Nb, Ti and W in the Ni(M) solid solution and the solute C can form the Ni-Nb-C, Ni-Ti-C and Ni-W-C ternary  alloys, respectively. In the thermodynamic calculation of precipitating MC carbides, the ternary alloys are thought  to be the regular solid solution, and only solutes M in the ternary alloys may react with the C atom to form the MC  carbides.

# 5. Discussion  

5.1 Theory analysis of carbide precipitation  

During heat treatment at the temperature being higher than    phase dissolution, the quantity of carbides  precipitated in the alloy increases with temperature. And the precipitating configuration of carbides transforms  from the complicated to regulation, or the one changes from granular to filmy. When the temperature of solution  treatment is lower than 1160 ℃ , the particle-like carbide is precipitated both along boundaries and within grains, as  shown in Fig. 3, 4. When the temperature of solution treatment is higher than 1160 ℃ , the film-like carbides are  precipitated along boundaries, as shown in Fig. 2(d) and Fig. 5(c), which may reduce the mechanical and creep  properties of alloy to a great extent. The analysis of XRD shows that no WC carbide is detected in the alloy, the  carbides precipitated in alloy include only a few Cr 23 C 6  and more (Nb, Ti)C carbides. And it is hard for the (Nb,  Ti)C carbide to decompose and eliminate once the one are precipitated in alloy during heat treatment. Therefore, it  is very important to control the configuration of carbides during preparation and heat treatment of alloys.  

Because the powder nickel-based alloy contains the trace C and more former of carbides, such as Ti, Nb, W,  the elements may dissolve in the matrix of alloy during solution treated, and the solute elements may be  precipitated in alloy to form the carbides during cooling. Although the element W is the strong former of carbide,  it is indicated according to thermodynamic calculation that the precipitation temperature of WC carbide in alloy is  in the alloy during heat treatment. The elements Nb and Ti are also the strong former of carbides, the ones may be  precipitated in alloy during isothermal cooling at 550 ℃   to form the NbC and TiC carbides. And the atoms Nb and  Ti in the NbC and TiC carbides may be replaced each other due to the same structures and close radius of  them[28]. Therefore, when the NbC and TiC carbides are precipitated during cooling, the (Nb, Ti)C linear carbide  may be formed in the alloy due to the replacement of atoms Nb and Ti each other.  

Compared with the elements Nb and Ti, the element Cr is a weak former of carbides, although much more  atoms Cr are dissolved in the alloy, only a few Cr 23 C 6  carbide and more (Nb, Ti)C carbide are precipitated in the  alloy during heat treatment due to the role of chemical avidity. Moreover, compared to Cr 23 C 6  carbide, the  precipitation of W 23 C 6  carbide needs a higher temperature, which is higher than the temperature of solution  treatment, therefore, the precipitation of no W 23 C 6  carbide is detected in the alloy.  

5.2 Effect factors of carbides morphology and distribution  

Some investigations on the morphologies and distribution of carbides indicate that the carbides with MC and  M 23 C 6  types may be precipitated in the nickel-based alloy[5, 29], and the precipitated carbide with MC type  displays the irregular configuration. The primary nucleus of precipitating carbide possess the octahedral  configuration, namely, the outside surfaces of the one are parallel to {111} plane, and the carbide with MC type in  the alloy possesses the particle-like, stripe-like or bulk-like configuration. And the atoms Nb and Ti are enriched  in the particle-like and stripe-like carbide with MC type, the atoms Cr, W and Mo are enriched in the bulk-like  carbide with MC type, while the atoms Cr are mainly enriched in the M 23 C 6  carbide which is easily precipitated in  the temperature range of 732\~982 ℃ [30]. Under the various service conditions, the decaying reaction of the  carbide with MC type may occur in the alloy[31, 32] according to the equation : MC +       M 23 C 6  +   . And the  crystallographic relationship between the fine M 23 C 6  carbide and    matrix is kept as follows [33]:

Although the carbon atom has a smaller radius, the thermodynamic  activity of the C atoms in the Ni-M-C solid solution may promote their precipitation to form the carbide during  isothermal cooling at 550 o C. Therefore, significant amount of fine carbides are precipitated along boundaries and  within grains. Moreover, some fine oxides are located in the regions of primary powder boundary (PPB), and the  growth up of the carbides relying on fine oxides as the nucleating core needs only lower energy[34, 35]. Therefore,  the carbon atoms in boundaries are easily precipitated to promote the growth of carbide relying on the fine oxides  core. This is thought to be the main reason of the alloy having only a few carbides precipitated along boundaries  because the diffusion rate of carbon atoms is slower when the alloy is isothermally cooled in the oil bath at 150 ℃   [8, 36, 37].  

When the solution temperature enhances to 1165 ℃  which is higher than the dissolving temperature of     phase, the boundaries appear in the original PPB regions due to the dissolution of the coarser   phase during  solution treatment. And much more Nb, Ti and C atoms diffuse easily to the boundary regions due to much more  defects and voids in the ones. Therefore, when the solid solution is isothermally cooled at 550 ℃ ,  more carbides  are rapidly formed to grow into the films along boundaries due to a bigger superheat degree and diffusing rate of  carbon atoms during cooled. And the mechanical and creep properties of the alloy decrease to a great extent once  the carbide films are formed along boundaries.

# 6. Conclusions  

(1) The distribution and configuration of carbides change with solution temperatures, some fine particle-like  carbides are distributed in the alloy after solution treated at 1155 ℃ , and the microstructure of alloy consists of  γ matrix, γ' phase, (Nb, Ti)C and M 23 C 6  carbides.  

(2) After solution treated at 1155 ℃ , the creep life of alloy at 1034MPa / 650 ℃  is measured to be 283 h, wherein,  some fine carbides precipitated along boundaries may restrain the slipping of boundary to improve the creep  resistance of alloy, which is thought to be the main reason of the alloy having a longest creep life.  

(3) The grain boundaries appear in the original PPB regions of alloy after solution treated at 1165 ℃ , the carbide  films precipitate along boundaries to decrease the bonding strength between grains, which is thought to be  the main reason of the alloy displaying a weakest creep resistance and a shortest creep life.  

(4) Compared with the precipitation of WC carbide, the bigger driving force of nucleation is thought to be the  main reason of NbC and TiC carbide precipitated in the alloy. Wherein the atoms Nb and Ti in the NbC and  TiC carbides may be replaced each other due to same structures and close radius of them, which is thought to  be the main reason of only (Nb, Ti)C carbide precipitated in the alloy.  

(5) By means of thermodynamic calculation, the precipitating temperatures of NbC and TiC carbides in the alloy  are determined to be 1080 ℃  and 816 ℃ , respectively. The reason of no WC carbide precipitated in the alloy is  attributed to its smaller driving force of nucleation.
