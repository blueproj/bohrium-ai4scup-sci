# Effect of various heat treatment conditions on microstructure of cast polycrystalline IN738LC alloy  

N. El-Bagoury   ∗ , M. Waly, A. Nofal  

Casting Technology Department, Central Metallurgical Research and Development Institute, P.O. Box 87, Helwan, Egypt Received 19 September 2007; received in revised form 1 October 2007; accepted 1 October 2007  

# Abstract  

The microstructure of cast polycrystalline Inconel 738LC (IN738LC) under different heat treatment conditions was investigated. The cast microstructure of this alloy consists of austenitic    matrix,   ′   precipitates, MC carbide and   /  ′   eutectic. The microstructure of the conventional solution treatment, 1120   ◦ C/2 h/accelerated air cooling (AAC), contains a bimodal   ′   precipitate. Solution treatment at several temperatures of 1120, 1180, and 1220   ◦ C for 1.5 h as well as 1180   ◦ C/1.5 h + 1220   ◦ C/2 h has been carried out under argon atmosphere. Accelerated air cooling (AAC) and water quenching (WQ) were applied after solution treatment. Moreover, aging treatment at 845   ◦ C was carried out for all solutioning conditions followed by WQ. Solution treatment at 1180   ◦ C/1.5 h + 1220   ◦ C/2 h gives the best homogeneity for different alloying elements and a uniform size for the ﬁne   ′   precipitates. AAC increases the volume fraction and tends to agglomerate   ′   precipitates compared to WQ.

# 1. Introduction  

Ni-based superalloys are widely used for high-performance applications such as disks and blades of either aircraft engines or land-based gas turbines  [1–6] .  

In order to function satisfactorily in such severe environ- ments, the alloys are required to possess at high temperatures, good corrosion resistance, optimal thermal properties, strength coupled with ductility, creep and fatigue resistance, as well as optimal impact and wear resistance  [7] .  

Gas turbine blades used for power generation are mostly made of nickel-based superalloys such as IN738LC alloy. This alloy is a cast polycrystalline Ni base superalloy, containing a large amount of refractory elements like tungsten, molybdenum, tantalum and niobium  [8] .  

IN738LC alloy is strengthened through the precipitation of   ′ phase, Ni 3 (Al,Ti), in a fcc   -Ni matrix  [9] . The precipitate pro- vides the alloy with good mechanical properties such as creep resistance especially at elevated temperatures. The size, shape and fraction of   ′   precipitates in the matrix and consequently the mechanical properties of the alloy are optimized via heat treat- ment  [10] . Many research programs have been focused on the effect of heat treatment on microstructures and creep properties [10] . On the other hand, some researchers investigated the effect of heat treatment on the tensile properties. Mao et al.  [11]  stud- ied more extensively the effect of cooling rate on   ′   precipitation kinetics and subsequent strengthening of Udimet 720Li.  

The standard solution treatment for IN738LC alloy, at 1120   ◦ C for 2 h  [12,13] , is not an adequate solutionizing pro- cedure to dissolve the primary   ′   phase in matrix to yield a single-phase solid solution, the solvus temperature of   ′   is 1235   ◦ C with 4 h holding time  [7] . Therefore, partial solution treatment at higher temperatures is required to dissolve the pri- mary   ′   phase and give a chance to increase the volume fraction of ﬁne   ′   precipitates during aging treatment. Jensen and Tien [14]  pointed out the importance of   ′   particle size and distri- bution on dislocation movement. They stated that dislocations loop around the precipitates or cut through them depending on their nature, which accordingly affects the mechanical proper- ties.  

High-temperature strength of Ni base superalloys depends mainly on the volume fraction and morphology of  ′   precipitates. Precipitation hardening heat treatment has a signiﬁcant effect on these properties  [15] .

# 3. Results and discussion  

# 3.1. Structure of as-cast specimens  

The microstructure of cast IN738LC consists of a Ni base austenitic  matrix,  ′   precipitates,MCcarbideand  /  ′   eutectic, as shown in  Fig. 1 . The average grain size of cast IN738LC was estimated to be about 100   m represented by No. 4 according to ASTM Designation E112,  Fig. 1 .  

![](images/af968966f673e5562460ad5896b554d453a3e343cd3d38682fd1b457daa9e419.jpg)  
Fig. 7. Microstructure of solution treatment at (a) 1120   ◦ C, (b) 1180   ◦ C, (c) 1220   ◦ C, and (d) 1180 + 1220   ◦ C with AAC.  

In the microstructure there are two types of carbides; the MC blocky type carbides which are located inside the    matrix as well as at the grain boundaries,asshownin Figs.2and3 .TheMC agglomerated carbides mainly consist of (Ta, Ti) C in addition to lower percentages of Nb and W,  Fig. 4 . The other type of carbides is the ﬁne M 23 C 6  carbides; with Cr as the basic element (Cr 23 C 6 ), which can be observed only at the grain boundaries, as shown in  Figs. 3 and 5 .  

Speciﬁc types of carbides formed under different cooling rates from the melt. The melting temperature of carbides is above 1525   ◦ C  [16] , which is much higher than the melting range (1232–1315   ◦ C) of IN738LC itself  [17] . Slow cooling rates pro- duce more of large blocky type TaC, but carbide reﬁnement occurs with fast cooling and after cooling from temperatures close to 1595   ◦ C  [18] , since at this high temperature the car- bides completely dissolve in the melt and during cooling they precipitate as very ﬁne ones  [17] .  

The   /  ′   eutectic is distributed at dendrite boundaries, often near micropores, showing that they are formed during the last stages of solidiﬁcation,  Fig. 6 . The eutectic size varied from 30 to 135   m and its volume percent was determined to be in a range of 0.15–6%, depending on the observed position. The   /  ′   eutectic is another set of main phases in a Ni base superalloy  [15] . A high volume fraction ( V f ) of the   /  ′   eutectic would degrade the mechanical properties of superalloy because of the production of   ′ -deﬁcient regions  [19] .  

Fig. 5  shows a SEM micrograph in which the microstruc- ture has duplex size precipitates of ﬁne and coarse   ′   phase. In dendritic region (A) there are ﬁne   ′   precipitates while in inter- dendritic region (B) coarse size   ′   precipitates exist, as it has been also observed by Kim et al.  [10] . The  V f  of   ′   precipitates in as-cast conditions is about 20–23% while after standard heat treatment it reached to 42–46%  [20,21] .  

Occurrence of microporosity mainly associated with some eutectic regions was observed at higher magniﬁcation as shown in  Fig. 6 . The average hardness of as-cast IN738LC alloy is about 393Hv 30 .

# 3.2.4. Effect of aging treatment on  γ ′   precipitates  

Aging at 845   ◦ C subsequent to ST at 1120   ◦ C for 1.5 h/AAC leads to the formation of a bimodal   ′   precipitate in the microstructure. The coarse primary   ′   precipitates in cuboidal morphology have a cube edge of about 0.45–0.55   m. The spheroidal secondary   ′   particles exist in microstructure with 0.05–0.1   mindiameter,asshownin Fig.10 (a).Theseresultsdo notcoincidewiththeﬁndingsofBalikcietal. [7] ashementioned that single aging at any lower temperature for 24 h subsequent to 1120   ◦ C/2 h ST does not produce any cuboidal precipitates.  

However, in  Fig. 10 (b) the microstructure after the same con- ditions of aging and solution treatments but under WQ cooling condition has spheroidal   ′   particles with duplex size.  

Solution-treated alloys at 1180   ◦ C/1.5 h followed by aging at 845   ◦ C for different time was also investigated.  Fig. 11  shows the effect of cooling conditions (WQ and AAC) after aging for 48 h on   ′   particles. Specimens cooled with WQ have ﬁne spheroidal   ′   particles while others cooled with AAC contain coarser spheroidal   ′   precipitates. The resulted coarse precip- itates originated from the longer time available for the solute atom diffusion in case of cooling by AAC than WQ.  

Duplex size of   ′   precipitates can be seen in  Fig. 11 (b) where in case of AAC there is a combination of ﬁne and coarse   ′ precipitates.  

Microstructures obtained after aging at the same conditions for 24 and 48 h for the subsequent solution-treated specimens at 1220   ◦ C/1.5 h are presented in  Fig. 12 . In case of WQ, aged  ′   precipitates in the microstructure are spheroidal precipitates. Primary   ′   precipitates are coarse and the secondary ones are very ﬁne and can barely be noticed at higher magniﬁcation, 50 000 , as shown in  Fig. 12 (a).  

The   ′   precipitates are larger in size for the AAC comparing to WQ after aging at the same time (24 h). The higher growth of both primary and secondary   ′   precipitates in case of AAC than WQ is due to the longer time for the diffusion of solute atoms. Duplex   ′   precipitates are presented in microstructures of WQ and more remarkable in case of AAC,  Fig. 12 (a) and (b). The higher  V f  of the   ′   precipitates obtained after AAC indicates that solute ejection from the matrix continues during cooling leading to further growth.  

By prolonging the aging time to 48 h for AAC, the  V f  of   ′ precipitates is larger than that of 24 h. Moreover, the secondary  ′   precipitates  V f  increases at the expense of the coarse pri- mary   ′   ones. To be summarized, as the aging time increases the coarse primary   ′   precipitates gradually disappear from the microstructure and the ﬁne secondary   ′   precipitates are the dominant ones in the microstructure. In addition, the morphol- ogyoflarger  ′   precipitates turned to be cuboid al with cubic edge of about 1–2   m for 48 h instead of the precipitates formed after 24 h.  

The ﬁrst part of double ST (i.e. 1180   ◦ C for 1.5 h) was aim- ing at reaching a high level of homogenization of all alloying elements particularly those contributing to the formation of  ′   precipitates such as Ti, Al and Ni as the   ′   phase is an intermetallic compound containing Ni with Al and/or Ti. The microstructure would therefore contain homogeneous and uni- formly distributed ﬁne   ′   particles. The second part of the double ST process (1220   ◦ C for 2 h) is thought to be responsible for the dissolution of most of coarse primary   ′   precipitates from the microstructure, a process where the ﬁrst part of the treatment could also play a supporting role as well.  

Aging at 845   ◦ C for different times varying from 6 to 48 h after the above conditions of the ST was carried out. As shown in  Fig. 13 (a), the microstructure of solution treated followed by WQ then aged for 6 h contains unimodal spherical coarse   ′ precipitates.  

A further coarsening for the primary   ′   precipitates was noticed by prolonging the time of aging to 48 h,  Fig. 13 (b). Additionally, the agglomeration and coalescence for the   ′   pre- cipitates are more obvious for 48 h than in 6 h. Moreover, the  V f of   ′   precipitates for 48 h is higher than the corresponding one for the 6 h.  

There is no any evidence for the precipitation of the ﬁne secondary   ′   precipitates, according to the capabilities of SEM, even at higher magniﬁcations for 6 h and for longer time, 48 h, as shown in  Fig. 13 (a) and (b).  

The resulted  V f  and size of the aged   ′   precipitates entirely changed using AAC instead of WQ after the  V f  process. The aged microstructure for 24 and 48 h are presented in  Fig. 14 (a) and (b).  

The  V f  of the coarse primary   ′   precipitate is signiﬁcantly reduced for AAC in comparison with WQ,  Fig. 14 . This means that the coarse   ′   precipitates increase in size and reach its equilibrium maximum size during aging before dissolving in   matrix. By increasing the holding time at 845   ◦ C from 24 to 48 h, the attained size is increased while the  V f  is decreased for the coarse primary   ′   precipitates in the microstructure, Fig. 14 (b).  

The produced secondary   ′   precipitates are ﬁne, well dis- tributed in the microstructure and almost have the same size. The size of these precipitates is larger for the 48 h case than for 24 h one and the  V f  is higher for 48 h in comparison with 24 h, Fig. 14 (b).  

It is concluded that after aging at 845   ◦ C the compound ST of 1180   ◦ C/1.5 followed by 1220   ◦ C/2 h gives the best distribution, size and morphology as well as the highest V f  for   ′   among the other conditions of solution pro- cess.  

# 4. Conclusions  

1. The  V f  of   /  ′   eutectic in microstructure of cast IN738LC decreases as the temperature of ST process increases, and the eutectic   /  ′   entirely vanishes from the microstructure with the double ST conditions of 1180   ◦ C/1.5 h + 1220   ◦ C/2 h.  

2. ST eliminates segregation and enhances the distribution and homogeneity of different alloying elements in the microstructure of IN738LC alloy. The best distribution and homogeneity for alloying elements are accompanied with 1180   ◦ C/1.5 h + 1220   ◦ C/2 h treatment.

 3. Conventional solution treatment, 1120   ◦ C/1.5 h, gives bimodal   ′   precipitates while other conditions of ST provide a unimodal   ′   particles.

 4. Cooling rate with AAC gives longer time for solute diffu- sion resulting in precipitation of secondary   ′   particles. In addition, the  V f  of   ′   precipitates is higher in case of AAC in comparison with WQ.

 5. Among the ST conditions, ST of 1180   ◦ C/1.5 h + 1220   ◦ C/2 h provides the optimum ﬁne spheroidal size of   ′   precipitates compared to conventional ST one.
