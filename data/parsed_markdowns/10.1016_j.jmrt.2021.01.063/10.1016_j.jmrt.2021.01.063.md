# a b s t r a c t  

In this work, a model of the degradation of the Mg e Zn e Mn e Ca alloy system in SBF (Simulated Body Fluid) is proposed by applying the isoconversion method. Alloy ingots with nominal compositions of Mg e 2Zn e 0.2Mn e 0.2Ca (wt.%) designed A.2 alloy and Mg e 4Zn e 0.2Mn e 0.2Ca (wt.%), A.4 alloy, was induction melted at 680    C. In a eudiometer, the alloys were immersed for 600 min in Kokubo solution at temperatures of 23.5    C, 36.5    C, 49.5    C, and the evolution of hydrogen was monitored. In the isoconversion method, the Arrhenius’ equations and the kinetic mechanism models of chemical and diffusion control were used to construct the model. The degradation rate in A.2 alloy varied from 6.66 mg of 2   2 2   Mg/cm to 9.40 mg of Mg/cm , and in A.4 alloy, from 5.48 mg to 6.70 mg of Mg/cm with a test temperature variation of 23.5 e 49.5    C. The control mechanism observed was the mixed one, being predominantly of chemical control. # 1. Introduction  

Every year, a large number of citizens suffer bone injuries caused by accidents or diseases. Usually, the treatments require intervention with exogenous materials, named as biomaterials, to achieve complete functional recovery [ 1 ]. In recent years, magnesium (Mg) alloys have been attracting in- terest among the new generation of metal alloys for biomed- ical applications due to their bio compatibility, low modulus of elasticity, and their ability to degrade in the biological envi- ronment after a certain period of functional use that, com- bined with an adequate host response, can lead to the development of residue-free implants [ 1 e 5 ]. The insertion of alloy elements such as Zn and Ca in this magnesium matrix has been studied because, in addition to the improvement of the initial mechanical properties, it combines elements that are essential to the human body, which will be absorbed during the controlled degradation of the metallic matrix [ 6 e 9 ]. In this context, one of the most important properties that must be put to the test when developing new biomedical al- loys to be used in implants is the controlled degradation inside environments that simulate the body ﬂuids and, conse- quently, the kinetics of degradation of the material under these speciﬁc conditions.  

The application of isoconversion is a practical analysis method that allows obtaining information on kinetics control mechanism behavior from thermo analytical measurements. Although its conception dates back to 1957 [ 10 ], it is still cur- rent and continues to be used by several authors in the ki- netics mechanism of control [ 11 e 15 ]. The approach of isoconversion at constant temperatures of degradation per- formed at various temperatures allows the kinetic character- ization to deﬁne the parameters of activation of conversion [ 16 , 17 ].  

This work aims to obtain a model that describes the degradation behavior in the ﬁrst 10 h of contact with the studied alloys with the Kokubo solution as a function of temperature. The degradation rates used for comparison are obtained at three constant temperatures. The isoconversion method comprises two independent functions: one that de- scribes the degradation and the other the behavior with the temperature variation. Two functions are evaluated, one that describes the chemical control (Cs) and the other the diffu- sional control (Ds), in order to ﬁnd an ideal model function in the representation of the degradation. Besides, to investigate the temperature dependence on degradation, the Arrhenius relationship was used. The methodology used allows us to predict magnesium alloys’ degradation rate at various tem- peratures precisely with fewer degradation experimental runs, enough to deﬁne the apparent activation energy and the pre-exponential factor in the model proposed. Thus, the model formulation and adequate determination of the model parameters permit the accurate evaluation of the degradation rate under simulated body ﬂuid and adequate treatment design.

Eqs.  (1) e (3)  describe the degradation of magnesium alloys generating Mg(OH) 2,  which at pH  <  11.5 becomes a soluble hydroxide. The degradation rate and hydrogen evolution are important parameters for evaluating the bio de grad ability and are obtained through tests performed in vitro [ 18 ]. The SBF (Simulated Body Fluid) is an aqueous solution composed of ions like body ﬂuids and controlled pH. The SBF is used in the immersion of the test body for the evaluation of its biode- gradability. Among the solutions used, the Kokubo solution stands out, which is the one that most resembles human blood plasma [ [ 18 e 20 ]].  

The high chemical afﬁnity of magnesium with oxygen re- quires processes with strict controls of the atmosphere to obtain the alloys based on magnesium. In this case, this study used inductive melting with a coating of alkaline salts, giving rise to the samples used in the raw state of melting. The improvement of corrosive and mechanical properties can be implemented through heat treatments [ 20 ]. The use of mag- nesium and zinc as a base for biocompatible alloy projects can have their corrosive and mechanical properties improved by introducing a second matrix compound through the powder laser fusion process (LPBF). This process exhibits high ﬂexi- bility and efﬁciency in the production of parts with complex structure and customized format. Yang et al. used reduced graphene oxide (RDO) to reinforce a zinc matrix in manufacturing a scaffold by the LPBF process, where it simultaneously increased the resistance and ductility [ 21 ]. Moreover, the cellular test conﬁrmed that RGO promoted cellular growth and differentiation [ 21 ]. Still using LPBF, Yang et al. also studied the effects of incorporation of bioactive ceramics, bioglass, in the magnesium matrix and obtained an increase in mechanical resistance, an improvement in apatite deposition that served as effective protection reducing the rate of degradation, besides a signiﬁcant improvement in cell growth [ 22 ].  

Recently, studies also explore the effects of degradation of MgZnMnCa alloys in Simulated Body Fluid with variations in zinc content in ranges up to 4% by weight [ 20 , 23 ]. However, no work aims to identify the proﬁle of the kinetic mechanism to control the interaction of the liquid medium with the Mg e Zn e Mn e Ca alloy, as well as an equation that determines the predictability of the converted fraction of alloy elements as a function of time was not developed in previous works. Thus, it is expected that the knowledge of kinetics under simulated conditions contributes to a better understanding of the phe- nomena of the interaction of the liquid body media with the Mg e Zn e Ca e Mn alloy and allows the precise design of the biomaterial implants.  

# 2. Experimental procedure  

# 2.1. Processing of magnesium alloys (A.2 and A.4)  

Magnesium ingots were induction melted at 680    C in SiC crucible under-protected alkali salts (MgCl 2 e KCl e BaCl 2- e CaF 2 ), using raw materials with high purity. The chemical compositions of alloys ingots A.2 (Mg e 2Zn-0.2Mn-0.2Ca) and A.4 (Mg e 4Zn-0.2Mn-0.2Ca), which can be observed in  Table 1 , were obtained by the inductively coupled plasma optical emission spectrometry - ICP OES. Details about the processing and properties of these alloys are presented in previous work [ 20 ].  

The alloy microstructures were characterized using scan- ning electron microscopy (SEM) (ZEISS EVO-10) with energy- dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS). The surfaces were etched after polishing in a 3% nitric acid solution and 97% ethanol for about 25 s.

# 4. Discussion  

Alloy A.4 showed higher resistance to degradation due to a higher percentage of zinc compared with A.2. This resistance to degradation is attributed to the formation of phosphates þ 2 þ 2 (HPO 4 ) at the surface, induced by the presence of Mg , Zn , þ   þ 2   Ca and Mn ions present in the test solution [ 19 , 20 ].  

The linearizations of the chemical control showed a good correlation of the square R for the two alloys at all tempera- tures: at A.2, 0.98 e 23.5    C, 0.98 e 36.5    C and 0.97 e 49.5    C, at A.4, a correlation coefﬁcient of 0.99 e 23.5    C, 0.98 e 36.5    C and 0.99 e 49.5    C, thus a strong tendency of a chemical degrada- tion control process. In diffusional they did not present a good correlation: in A.2, 0.92 e 23.5    C, 0.91 e 36.5    C, 0.95 e 49.5    C, in A.4, 0.93 e 23.5    C, 0.91 e 36.5    C and 0.97 e 49.5    C, thus indi- cating a weak but signiﬁcant tendency of a process of diffu- sional control. Thus, according to the kinetics analysis of the reaction of the degradation of alloys A.2 and A.4, we can classify it as a mixed control process, preferably of chemical control. This control may be the result of the microstructure, Fig. 7 , in which (a) is the alloy A.2 and (b), the alloy A.4, resulting from the fusion that presented a magnesium matrix with the presence of a second phase, reported [ 20 , 27 ] as Mg 3 Zn 7  preferably positioned in the grain boundaries. This second phase in the Mg matrix exposes the degradation process to galvanic corrosion in the matrix/second phase interface, causing the removal of microparticles and thus facilitating the diffusion of the solution inside the alloy, affecting it to highlight the mixed control in the minority portion, the diffusional [ 26 ]. Furthermore, the chemical con- trol we interpret as a front of reactions described with Eqs. (1) e (3 ) describes the direct conversion of the Kokubo solution and the alloy’s matrix.

- The chemical control model in applying the isoconversion methodology presented excellent correlation coefﬁcients for alloys A.2 and A.4 at test temperatures. It also presented activation energy of 3.24 kJ/mol for alloy A.2 and 3.45 kJ/ mol for alloy A.4, and a pre-exponential factor of
