# Abstract  

The cyclic deformation and lifetime behavior of a cast nickel-based superalloy M963 was investigated under both thermo mechanical fatigue (TMF) and isothermal fatigue (IF) conditions. Two types of TMF tests, i.e. in-phase (IP) and out-of-phase (OP), were performed in temperature range of 450–900   ◦ C, and IF tests were conducted at a temperature of 900   ◦ C. All tests were carried out under mechanical strain control at a given strain rate of 1.0  ×  10 − 4   s − 1 . Results revealed that isothermal fatigue exhibited a longer lifetime than both types of TMF at corresponding mechanical strain amplitude in spite of its higher fraction of plastic strain amplitude. Different cracking behavior occurred in IP and OP TMF. In IP loading, cracks tended to initiate and propagate intergranularly or inter dendritic ally, while totally transgranular cracking occurred in OP loading. Three life prediction models were selected and compared to evaluate the possibility of predicting TMF lifetime by using IF data.

# 1. Introduction  

During startup, load change and shutdown operations, high temperature components in aeroengines experience thermal induced strains and stresses. Repeated such operations cause thermo mechanical fatigue (TMF) damage  [1,2] . TMF can lead to a limited lifetime compared with isothermal fatigue (IF) due to additional deformation and damage mechanisms under vary- ing temperature conditions  [3,4] . Extensive studies were carried out on IF of superalloys in the past decades  [5–7] , however, thermo mechanical fatigue, due to its time-consuming, labor intensive and of course expensive features,has been less reported despite its great importance. Frequently, data from isothermal fatigue tests are used to predict the deformation behavior and the lifetime of components exposed to TMF  [8,9] . However, this method cannot work well if IF tests may not capture the important damage mechanisms under varying temperature TMF conditions  [10,11] . Further research work has to be conducted to understand the different mechanisms of TMF and IF, and discuss the feasibility of predicting TMF lifetime by using IF data.  

In the present study, in-phase and out-of-phase thermome- chanical fatigue tests of a nickel-based superalloy were carried out; the experimental results were analyzed and compared with isothermal fatigue tests. The damage and fracture mechanisms were investigated for both TMF and IF. Moreover, life predic- tion models were used to evaluate TMF lifetime by utilizing IF data  [12–14] .

# 2.2. Fatigue tests and characterization  

Fatigue tests were conducted on an MTS810 servo-hydraulic thermo mechanical fatigue machine with computer control. A high-frequency induction generator was used for heating. Cool- ing was mainly achieved by thermal conduction into the speci- men grips and could be forced by blowing compressed air. The temperature was measured and controlled with a thermocouple spot-welded in the middle of the gauge length. The total strain ( ε t ) was measured using a high temperature axial extensometer of 16 mm gage length with ceramic rods. A triangle waveform was used for both thermal cycling and mechanical cycling. IF test was conducted under mechanical strain control at a con- stant temperature of 900   ◦ C with strain amplitudes of 0.5%, 0.4%, 0.35%, respectively. Two kinds of TMF tests were consid- ered: in-phase where the maximum mechanical strain coincided with the maximum temperature and out-of-phase where the maximum mechanical strain was attained at the minimum tem- perature. Fractographic observation was performed on the fracture sur- faces of failed samples. The fracture source initiated at cast pores, carbides, interdendritic boundaries, grain boundaries, etc. In IP-TMF loading, as shown in  Fig. 5 a, a crack initiated at grain boundary and then propagated in a transgranular manner.  Fig. 5 b shows a typical fracture surface in OP-TMF tests, the crack initi- ated at carbides and propagated trans dendritic ally.  Fig. 5 c shows a propagation region at low mechanical amplitude in IF loading, the crack initiated at a subsurface pore. Note that the fracture mode for both TMF and IF tests is transgranular.  

Under high magniﬁcations, fatigue striations can be seen on the fracture surfaces in all the testing conditions ( Fig. 6 ). The fatigue striations are more evident for OP TMF and IF than for IP TMF, and only few striations can be observed in IP conditions.

# 4. Discussions  

# 4.1. Cyclic deformation and fatigue life  

InTMFtests,theYoung’s modulus of the specimen at the high temperature half cycles is lower than that at the low temperature half cycles, which leads to a higher  σ min  than  σ max  in IP TMF and a higher  σ max  than  σ min  in OP TMF, as shown in  Fig. 2 b. For this reason, compressive mean stress and tensile mean stress is obtained in IP and OP TMF, respectively. Compressive mean stress is beneﬁcial for fatigue lifetime because it retards crack nucleation and propagation under IP TMF conditions. Whereas tensile mean stress developed in OP TMF may promote crack initiation and crack growth, leading to a reduced lifetime.  

IF exhibited a longer fatigue life than TMF at the same mechanical strain amplitude in spite of its higher fraction of plas- tic strain amplitude ( Figs. 3 and 4 ), especially at low mechanical strain amplitudes. Varying temperature is considered to be the reason which limits TMF lifetime  [17,21] . Under TMF vary- ing temperature conditions, thermal stress appeared at some special positions due to thermal mismatch between different phases in superalloy, i.e. carbides, eutectics and matrix. This local thermal stress may cause local thermal fatigue damage during the repeated cycles  [22] . Typical creep and enhanced oxi- dation behavior  [23]  in TMF will also promote crack initiation and propagation, and thus lead to a much decreased lifetime in both types of TMF. This fast crack propagation was also proved in  Fig. 12 , where  α  is the driving force for crack propagation. In  Fig. 12 a, the driving force  α  in IP and OP TMF are higher than in IF at the same mechanical strain amplitudes. This well explain the reason why IF gives a longer life. Moreover, the driving force  α  was plotted against plastic strain amplitude, as shown in  Fig. 12 b. It can be seen that IP and OP TMF exhibit

Conclusions can be drawn that the ratio of IF lifetime to TMF lifetime increases as plastic strain amplitude decreases, which can well explain the increasing disparity between IF and TMF at low plastic strain amplitudes in Ostergren model ( Fig. 8 ).  

For thermo mechanical fatigue, in-phase and out-of-phase exhibit a crossover in the mechanical strain amplitude-life curves, as shown in  Fig. 3 . This crossover occurred at mechani- cal strain amplitude of about 0.4%, while the position could vary depend on materials  [17,18] . The crossover could be explained by combined contributions of creep, oxidation and fatigue dam- age. Since the fraction of plastic strain amplitude in mechanical strain amplitude coincides very well for IP and OP TMF ( Fig. 4 ), their fatigue damage would be expected to be similar. Therefore, the crossover may be mainly due to different levels of creep and oxidation. At high mechanical strain amplitudes, the time spent at elevated temperature is shorter because the fatigue life of the alloy is relatively low, and a relatively weak oxidation was obtained. However, creep damage in IP TMF at high mechan- ical strain amplitudes is signiﬁcant, which caused a decreased lifetime in IP than in OP. At low mechanical strain amplitudes, the corresponding plastic strain portion is very small and dam- age resulted from creep can be neglected  [24] . Thus, oxidation makes the difference in lifetime between IP and OP TMF. In OP TMF loading, the oxide is in tension at low temperature half cycles and may rupture readily, thus a much decreased OP TMF lifetime than IP TMF is obtained  [17] . Tensile mean stress devel- oped in OP TMF conditions may also reduce OP TMF lifetime by promoting crack initiation and growth  [10] .  

# 4.2. Fatigue crack and fracture  

Both IP and OP TMF, and IF showed a transgranular fractur- ing surface in spite of the creep damage in IP loading, as shown in  Fig. 5 . This is because the grain size of superalloy M963 is relatively large, which provides a good creep resistance and less possibility for crack to encounter grain boundary in the process of crack propagation.  

The clearer fatigue striations observed in OP TMF is because the crack surface is closed by compressive stress at maxi- mum temperature under OP conditions, so the fatigue striations formed are less oxidized. However, the disturbance of creep and oxidation in IP TMF may lead to the result that few fatigue striations were found in IP TMF  [25] .  

The observed interdendritic cracking and intergranular crack- ing in IP TMF were ascribed to creep and oxidation ( Fig. 7 a and b). Crack is open at elevated temperature and the crack tip is exposed to air under IP TMF conditions, while grain bound- aries and interdendritic regions which are rich in easily oxidized elements such as aluminium, titanium, chromium, etc., provide rapid propagation channels for the crack  [26] . Thus the crack tends to propagate along the relatively weak interdendritic and intergranular regions. Moreover, creep may cause great grain boundary damage at high mechanical strain amplitudes, which will certainly induce intergranular cracking ( Fig. 7 b). Similar oxidation mechanism occurred in IF conditions, which can well explain the alternate transdendritic and interdendritic cracking behavior  [19] , as shown in  Fig. 7 e and f. Creep did not take place in IF loading, so no obvious grain boundary damage was found. While in OP TMF conditions, the transdendritic cracking can be ascribed to great tensile stress at low temperature half cycles. The relatively small cracks in OP TMF are because of the high stress concentration of the main crack.  

# 4.3. Life prediction  

In the present three life prediction models, Ostergren model failed in evaluating TMF lifetime using IF data, while both Zam- rik model and Miller model gave good prediction of TMF life. The main difference between Ostergren model and the latter two models is whether the elastic strain amplitude is incorporated as part of damage parameter besides inelastic strain amplitude. For high-strength materials with limited ductility like nickel-base superalloys, the elastic strain amplitude becomes very signiﬁ- cant, and is certainly important for life prediction. On the other hand, the inelastic strain amplitude is so small that large errors arise when attempting to measure it or use it to predict life  [13] . Thus, a damage parameter including both elastic strain ampli- tude and inelastic strain amplitude is expected to give a good capability of life prediction. Zamrik model and Miller model are based on tensile hysteresis energy and microcrack propagation, respectively, both of them incorporate elastic strain amplitude and inelastic strain amplitude together as damage parameters, which leads to a similar prediction result ( Fig. 11 ) and a good capability of predicting TMF with IF data by these two models.  

# 5. Conclusions  

Based on the experimental results and discussion above, the following conclusions can be drawn:  

1. IPTMFshowedlongerfatiguelifetimethanOPTMFatlowerstrainamplitude,but tended to give a shorter lifetime at higher strain amplitude. While IF showed a longer lifetime than both types of TMF at corresponding strain amplitude in spite of its higher fraction of plastic strain amplitude.

 2. Cracks initiate and propagate intergranularly and interden- dritically in IP TMF, while totally transgranular cracking occurred in OP TMF. Under IF condition, a mixed way of interdendritic and transdendritic cracking was observed.

 3. The Zamrik model and Miller model which incorporated elastic strain and plastic strain together as damage param- eters can give satisfactory life prediction by using IF data.
