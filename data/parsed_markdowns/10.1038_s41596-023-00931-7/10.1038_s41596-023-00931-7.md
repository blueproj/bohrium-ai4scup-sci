# Reconstruction, simulation and analysis  of enzyme-constrained metabolic  models using GECKO Toolbox 3.0  

Yu Chen    , Johan Gustafsson    , Albert Tafur Rangel    , Mihail Anton    4 , Iván Domenzain    1 ,  Cheewin Kittikunapong 1 , Feiran Li    , Le Yuan    1 , Jens Nielsen  & Eduard J. Kerkhoven  

# Abstract  

Genome-scale metabolic models (GEMs) are computational representations  that enable mathematical exploration of metabolic behaviors within cellular  and environmental constraints. Despite their wide usage in biotechnology,  biomedicine and fundamental studies, there are many phenotypes that GEMs  are unable to correctly predict. GECKO is a method to improve the predictive  power of a GEM by incorporating enzymatic constraints using kinetic and  omics data. GECKO has enabled reconstruction of enzyme-constrained  metabolic models (ecModels) for diverse organisms, which show better  predictive performance than conventional GEMs. In this protocol, we describe  how to use the latest version GECKO 3.0; the procedure has five stages: 

 (1) expansion from a starting metabolic model to an ecModel structure, 

 (2) integration of enzyme turnover numbers into the ecModel structure, 

 (3) model tuning, (4) integration of proteomics data into the ecModel and 

 (5) simulation and analysis of ecModels. GECKO 3.0 incorporates deep  learning-predicted enzyme kinetics, paving the way for improved metabolic  models for virtually any organism and cell line in the absence of experimental  data. The time of running the whole protocol is organism dependent,  e.g., \~5 h for yeast.  

# Key points  

•  Genome-scale metabolic  models have the potential to  predict changes in phenotype  resulting from different  environmental conditions.  Their predictive power can be  improved by including more  information about the enzyme  kinetics.  

•  GECKO is a program that gives  users an automated and manual  mechanism to incorporate  relevant data. The protocol  also describes how to tune the  program to compensate for  incorrect or missing values.

# Protocol  

# Introduction  

The use of genome-scale models is a valuable approach to mathematically explore metabolism,  and has accelerated biotechnology, biomedicine and fundamental biology studies 1 – 5 .  A conventional genome-scale metabolic model (GEM) is reconstructed from all metabolic  reactions of the organism of interest and converted into a computational format (Fig.  1a ).  The model is based on a system of equations with variables being reaction rates, represented in  a stoichiometric matrix ( S -matrix) that contains stoichiometric coefficients of metabolites  in reactions (Fig.  1b ). The system can be solved by constraint-based methods 6 , which impose  constraints on reactions rates and thereby search for solutions within an allowable solution  space where all possible metabolic states reside. Therefore, integration of more constraints into  the model may shrink the solution space and further improve the predictive power of the model.  

While the most widely used constraints are based on condition-dependent measurements  such as uptake rates of nutrients, the addition of additional innate constraints on metabolic  reactions have recently gained a lot of attention. In particular, constraints on the catalytic capacity  of enzymes have been used to reduce the solution space, and several methods that integrate  such constraints into conventional metabolic models have emerged, e.g., metabolism and  expression-model 7 , resource balance analysis 8 , expression and thermodynamics flux 9 , pcModel 10 ,  flux balance analysis with molecular crowding 11 , MetabOlic Modeling with ENzyme kineTics  (MOMENT) 12 , GECKO 13 , short (s)MOMENT 14  and enzymatic constrained metabolic network  model (ECMpy) 15 . Among them, GECKO 13 , , a method that enhances a Genome-scale metabolic  model with Enzymatic Constraints using Kinetic and Omics data, has been widely adopted to  build enzyme-constrained metabolic models (ecModels) by expanding on conventional GEMs  for various organisms 17 , . Based on the two predecessors, this protocol presents the latest GECKO  version 3.0 and describes how to use it for reconstruction, simulation and analysis of ecModels.  Note that GECKO can in principle convert not only GEMs, which are at genome scale, but also  smaller-size metabolic models to ecModels. In this protocol, we focus on GEMs.  

# Development of the protocol  

GECKO enables direct conversion of a conventional GEM into an enzyme-constrained  version. The conversion is based on the equation  v  ≤  k cat ×  e , where  v  represents the rate of  the enzymatic reaction,  k cat  the turnover number of the enzyme that catalyzes the reaction  and  e  the concentration of the enzyme. This means that the minimum usage of the enzyme  of the reaction is the reaction rate  v  over  k cat  and thus mass usage is the rate  v  times the  molecular weight (MW) of the enzyme over  k cat . Accordingly, each enzyme is included as a  pseudo-substrate in the reaction that it catalyzes (Fig.  1c ), and the model captures both the  turnover number and MW of the enzyme ( k cat  and MW, Fig.  1d ).  

# Enzyme usage reactions  

In addition, enzyme usage reactions are added into the model. In the absence of any proteomic  data, all protein masses are drawn from a total protein pool. If quantitative data are available for  any of the enzymes, this knowledge can be used to explicitly constrain the reactions catalysed  by these enzymes, and the measured enzyme mass is subsequentially subtracted from the  protein pool (Fig.  1c,d ).  

In GECKO versions 1 and 2,  k cat  and MW were included as separate coefficients in metabolic  and enzyme usage reactions 13 , , while in version 3, they are combined to form the stoichiometric  coefficient: −MW/ k cat . This mathematical conversion reduces the chance that the program will  predict extremely low fluxes in enzyme usage reactions and thereby could avoid numerical  issues in some linear programming solvers, where the flux values in certain circumstances  could otherwise be lower than the numeric tolerance of the linear problem solver to separate  different flux values. This form links to the concept of protein cost that quantitatively reflects  the protein mass required per flux of an enzymatic reaction 19 . Compared with conventional  GEMs, the  S -matrix is expanded and the model can predict enzyme usage besides the reaction  rates (Fig.  1d ).

# GECKO light  

In addition, GECKO 3.0 offers an option to reconstruct a light ecModel 20 , with similar formalism  as other frameworks 14 , , i.e., the protein cost of the enzyme is included in the metabolic  reaction while enzyme usage reactions are excluded (Fig.  1e ). GECKO light has a much shorter  calculation time than the full version and is therefore more practical for large-scale model  reconstruction and analysis, e.g., for cell lines. While light ecModels are incapable of proteomics  integration and enzyme usage prediction, because they do not include enzyme usage reactions,  they give comparable results to full ecModels when only using a total proteome constraint.  

# DLKcat to provide predicted k cat  values  

Given the essential role of  k cat  values in ecModels, their availability and accuracy dominate  the model performance. While  k cat  values can be gathered from public databases such as the  BRaunschweig ENzyme DAtabase (BRENDA) 22 , the databases are far from complete, meaning  that not every enzymatic reaction in an ecModel can be assigned with a measured  k cat . The  coverage of measured  k cat  values is small in model organisms and even worse in lesser studied  organisms 23 , which can explain why ecModels have only been reconstructed for relatively few  organisms 18 . To address this, the deep learning-based model DLKcat has been demonstrated  to be effective in predicting  k cat  values for given enzyme–substrate pairs 24 . It was previously  shown how the use of predicted  k cat  values drastically improved the reconstruction of ecModels  for 343 yeast/fungi species 24 . Therefore, GECKO can run DLKcat to provide predicted  k cat  values  as an alternative source. With this step, one can theoretically assign  k cat  values to all enzymatic  reactions and thus makes it possible to turn any conventional GEM into an ecModel.  

# Applications of GECKO  

GECKO is mainly used to convert a conventional GEM to an ecModel by including enzyme  constraints, which can considerably reduce the solution space and thereby the flux variability  ranges in simulations 16 , , . Notably, this reduction of the solution space can effectively lead  to accurate predictions of phenotypes such as overflow metabolism 13  and batch growth of  microbes 13 , , which cannot be captured by conventional GEMs unless specific enzymatic,  

transport or exchange reactions are constrained, such as oxygen uptake. Additionally, the  reconstructed ecModels, attributed to the involved enzyme information, enable calculation  and comparison of parameters, e.g., pathway protein costs 28  and flux control coefficients 29 .  Lastly, the ecModels can be used to guide and interpret metabolic engineering strategies for  overproduction of metabolites 27 , – 33 , and quantitatively characterize phenotype responses of  microbes under industrially relevant conditions 13 , .  

In addition, any constraint-based method 34  that fits a conventional GEM can also be applied  to an ecModel as GECKO merely expands the  S -matrix with additional pseudo-metabolites  and pseudo-reactions while retaining linearity. The constraint-based methods do not only  refer to typical ones, e.g., flux balance analysis (FBA) 35  and flux variability analysis (FVA) 36 , but  also those applied in strain design and optimization such as flux scanning based on enforced  objective flux 37  as applied to guide heme overproduction 30 . Moreover, it has been demonstrated  that GECKO fits with task-driven integrative network inference for tissues (tINIT) 38 , one of the  transcriptomics-based constraint methods that enable context-specific model reconstruction 39 .  This has been shown by a sequential use of tINIT and GECKO with the GEM of  Homo sapiens 40 .  

# Comparison of GECKO 3.0 with other methods  

It should be noted that the enzyme constraints can be incorporated by alternative methods  such as sMOMENT 14  and ECMpy 15 . The applications of the resulting ecModels are similar to  the ecModels by GECKO, e.g., improved predictions of phenotypes 14 , – 43 . sMOMENT adds a  pseudo-metabolite, i.e., protein pool, into metabolic reactions, and an exchange reaction for  this protein pool, and the resulting model is therefore similar to the light ecModel by GECKO.  The difference is that sMOMENT is based on Python and unable to construct the full ecModel.  

Another Python-based tool, ECMpy, avoids adding enzymes into reactions but constrains  the protein pool outside the  S -matrix, therefore the resulting model has a smaller size compared  with the full and light ecModel by GECKO. A limitation of defining the protein pool outside  the  S -matrix is that the enzyme constraints cannot be directly considered in constraint-based  simulations that were developed for conventional GEMs.  

GECKO differs from the alternative methods in the capability of proteomics data prediction  and integration, and potential exploration of between metabolism and other processes, e.g.,  signaling 44 . In addition, the underlying model structures of ecModels as defined by GECKO are  similar to conventional GEMs, which makes it possible to apply virtually any type of analysis  or simulation that has previously been developed for conventional GEMs. Furthermore, by  incorporating deep learning-predicted enzyme kinetics, GECKO 3.0 is capable of considerably  improving the parameter coverage, which is currently the major obstacle using other methods.  

# Limitations  

While following the same overall structure, ecModels are much larger than the starting  conventional GEMs, which means that the computational cost in large-scale simulations is  substantially larger. GECKO 3.0 addresses this limitation by providing light ecModels, where  consideration of individual enzyme levels is disabled. There is, therefore, a trade-off between  integration of proteomics data, or prediction of individual enzyme levels, and computational  speed that needs to be considered.  

The reduction in solution space by enzyme constraints integration improves predictions  but may also lead to larger deviations from experimental observations due to uncertainties in  the applied constraints. One uncertainty comes from the  k cat  values, which are obtained from  either reported measurements or DLKcat predictions. Measurements and predictions might  sometimes be inaccurate, particularly for enzyme complexes 45 , and in addition, almost all the  sources reflect in vitro enzyme kinetics, which might differ from in vivo conditions. GECKO  provides multiple strategies to test the resulting ecModel with known phenotype data, and  suggests what constraints should be modified. Therefore, experimental data are necessary for  high-quality reconstruction of ecModels.

# Overview of the procedure  

In addition to a preparation stage, the procedure consists of five stages, where the first four are  associated with ecModel reconstruction (Fig.  2 ) and the last one is simulation and analysis of  ecModels.  

The starting material for Stage 1 is a conventional GEM that will be converted to the ecModel  format. At the end of Stage 1, most of the variable fields are empty and will be populated in Stage 2.  

Before Stage 2 the enzyme data need to be assembled and formatted. It is very important  that the enzyme information, including MWs, protein sequences and EC numbers, is well  organized. These data can normally be collated from organism-specific entries from the  UniProt 46  database. The Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) 47  database could  also be an alternative source for gathering EC numbers.  

In Stage 2, enzyme  k cat  values are integrated into the empty ecModel, resulting in a draft  ecModel, which thus introduces enzymatic constraints as can be seen in the mathematical  formulation of the model. In Stage 3, model parameters are tuned based on physiological data if  available, resulting in a functional ecModel. If there is proteomics data available, Stage 4 enables  proteomics integration, resulting in a proteome-constrained ecModel, which thus has more  constraints.  

All of these stages are also applicable to the reconstruction of light ecModels, except for  Stage 4 as proteomics integration does not fit light ecModels.  

# Stage 0: Steps 1–7, preparation of project file structure and data  

This stage is to generate files and prepare data and parameters for ecModel reconstruction,  simulation and analysis. The ideal starting material is a highly curated conventional  constraint-based metabolic model of the organism of interest. If such a model is not available,  one can reconstruct it from scratch or from template models following published protocols 48 ,   within public toolboxes 34 , , . In addition to the file of the starting GEM, each ecModel requires  a model adapter, which is a text file that is loaded as a MATLAB structure. The organism-specific  data and parameters can be collated and populated into the adapter file.  

Stage 1: Steps 8–14, expansion from a starting metabolic model to an ecModel structure The starting GEM is expanded so that enzyme constraints can be implemented in later stages. 1.	 Reversible reactions are split into forward and backward reactions as enzyme kinetics are  direction dependent.  

2.	 Reactions that are catalyzed by isozymes are split into multiple reactions by individual  isozymes due to differences in enzyme kinetics and MWs of isozymes (the light ecModel  skips this and instead uses the lowest protein cost among isozymes.)

 3.	 The protein pool is added as a pseudo-metabolite and an exchange reaction is added   for it.

 4.	 Enzymes are added as pseudo-metabolites, which draw protein resources from the protein  pool by enzyme usage reactions (this step is not performed in the light version, because the  protein pool is directly added into enzymatic reactions.) In this stage, an empty ecModel is obtained, in which ECs are not yet incorporated.  

# Stage 2: Steps 15–32, integration of  k cat  into the ecModel structure  

This stage is to integrate enzyme  k cat  values together with the enzyme information that has  been prepared into the empty ecModel. It is important to bear in mind that not every enzymatic  reaction has a reported measurement. GECKO can directly retrieve reported data from the  BRENDA database for the reaction if both the EC number and the substrate of the reaction of  the organism are matched with the  k cat  annotation. If there are multiple  k cat  values matched  for the reaction, then the maximum value is selected.  

If the perfect match of all organism, substrate and EC number is not found, GECKO allows  for  k cat  matches by querying the BRENDA database based on a set of hierarchical matching  criteria 16  as summarized here:  

•  If the organism is not matched, then the  k cat  value of the phylogenetically closest organism is  selected •  If the substrate is not matched, then the  k cat  value for any alternative substrate is selected •  If the EC number is not found, then a fuzzy EC number, i.e., by introducing wildcards into the  EC number, is used for matching •  Where no  k cat  value is available in the BRENDA database, specific activities that are typically  given in μmol per min per mg protein can be converted into  k cat  values, as an alternative In addition, the DLKcat package, which is independent of EC number, can complement  

the BRENDA-based hierarchical matches, particularly for less studied organisms, or even be  used as a sole source of  k cat  values, enabling considerable improvement of the  k cat  coverage of  ecModels 24 .  

While it is possible to use either of these two automated approaches to populate  k cat  for  all reactions, GECKO also allows convenient manual curation of the  k cat  values so that the  user can also assign  k cat  values for reactions of interest from other sources, such as in vivo  estimations 8 , – 56  and predictions by other approaches 57 .  

Once the  k cat  values are obtained, enzymes with their protein costs in the form of MW  over  k cat  are added into all enzymatic reactions in the model. The product of Stage 2 is a draft  ecModel. This model is not functional, because the protein pool is unlimited and the enzyme  constraint is, therefore, not active.  

# Stage 3: Steps 33–52, model tuning  

To make the ecModel functional, a maximum constraint should be imposed on the protein   pool. According to the original publication 13 , the value of the maximum constraint is calculated  by  σ  ×  f  ×  P tot , where sigma  σ  represents the average in vivo saturation of all enzymes involved  in the model,  f  the mass fraction occupied by metabolic enzymes in total proteome and  P tot   the total protein mass in the unit of gram per gram cell dry weight. In cases where the value  of one or more factors is unknown, a default value of 0.5 can be used for any of the factors.  Consequentially, the default maximum constraint without knowledge of any factors is  0.5 × 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.125. In addition, there will be some uncertainty regarding the  k cat  values,  which might lead to incorrect predictions. Therefore, in this stage the model can be tuned by  entering already known physiological data.  

In GECKO 3.0, the draft ecModel can be tuned by adjusting the protein pool and/or  k cat  of  specific enzymes. There are several ways in which this can be done:  

The easiest approach is to increase the maximum constraint on the protein pool.  If the protein pool is allowed to increase without constraint, the model will be able  

to reach a higher growth rate. While this is an effective way of increasing the growth rate,  it might result in an unrealistically high protein pool, mostly as a consequence of extremely  low and incorrect  k cat  values of some key enzymes (protein pool values higher than one signify  that at least one of the factors  σ ,  f  and  P tot  is greater than one, which is in theory impossible).  Another option is to replace extremely low  k cat  values with an arbitrary value (e.g., tenfold  of the original value), which is also easy to implement and can be combined with the first  option. In addition to these easier but less biologically meaningful options, GECKO 16  provides  the functionality to identify the enzymes that most limit growth and substitute their  k cat   with higher values. By tuning the model with the strategies above, a functional ecModel is  obtained, which is constrained by a protein pool that can be distributed between individual  enzyme usages.  

It is important to bear in mind that model tuning requires physiological data,  particularly growth rate data. If there is no such data, e.g., in the case of large-scale   model reconstruction for various cell lines, only draft ecModels can be obtained.  While the draft model may not be functional for simulating realistic metabolic fluxes  due to the undefined upper bound on the protein pool, it can serve as a useful tool to  compare cellular parameters, e.g., the comparison of protein costs 28 . Even if no experimental  data are available, functional ecModels could be obtained by assuming an arbitrary value  for the protein pool and increasing low  k cat  values to ensure that reasonable simulations are  achieved.  

# Stage 4: Steps 53–65, integration of proteomics data into the ecModel  

Enzyme usage reactions allow for the integration of proteomics data into the ecModels  reconstructed by GECKO, leading to proteome-constrained ecModels that are context specific.  Due to, e.g., measurement errors in proteomics data and uncertainties in enzyme kinetics, the  ecModels are most likely to be overconstrained by proteomics data, resulting in an infeasible  solution. This can be addressed by using a proteomics integration algorithm that loosens the  constraints on the enzymes with great control coefficients 16 , where the control coefficient of an  enzyme is calculated by the change in the growth rate over the change in the concentration of  the enzyme.  

# Stage 5: Steps 66–79, simulation and analysis of ecModels  

Due to the similar mathematical formulations between ecModels and conventional GEMs,  the constraint-based methods that are widely used in conventional GEMs are compatible  with ecModels, such as FBA 35  and FVA 36 . Notably, the estimated rates for enzyme usage  reactions represent the minimum enzyme levels that are required for maintaining the fluxes  through the corresponding metabolic reactions. If proteomics data are available, one could  investigate whether certain enzymes are limiting by comparing the simulated and measured  enzyme levels.

# Box 1  

# Extension of conventional GEM to empty ecModel  

To enable the constraining of metabolic reactions with enzyme  constraints, the conventional GEM is converted into an empty  ecModel through up to 12 steps, some of which are skipped for light  models. These steps are listed here to indicate how the structure of  the ecModel is different from the starting conventional GEM.

 1.	 Any potential gene associations are removed from  pseudo-reactions: as these are not assumed to be enzymes  that catalyze realistic metabolic reactions, they should not be  considered by enzyme constraints. Such reactions are identified  by either reaction name containing ‘pseudoreaction’, or reaction  identifier being specified in ‘data/pseudoRxns.tsv’.

 2.	 Any irreversible reactions that are only allowed to carry a negative  flux (lower bound < 0 and upper bound = 0) are inverted.

 3.	 An ‘ecModel.rev’ vector, which indicates whether reactions are  reversible or not, is defined from the vectors of lower and upper  bounds.

 4.	 Nearly all reversible reactions are split into forward and reverse  reactions, to yield an irreversible ecModel. The new (previously  reverse) reactions will have _REV appended to its reaction  identifier, while the reaction identifier of the forward reaction  will remain unchanged. The only exception are exchange  reactions, which completely retain their original form and are  not split into two reactions, therefore still being allowed to carry  a negative flux.

 5.	 (Skipped for light ecModels) Reactions catalyzed by isozymes  (indicated by or in the gene associations in ‘ecModel.grRules’)  are split, so that each reaction is catalyzed by one enzyme or  enzyme complex. The new reaction will have their reaction  identifier suffixed with _EXP_ followed by a sequential number.  Example: reaction r_0001 catalyzed by two isozymes will have  two reactions, r_0001_EXP_1 and r_0001_EXP_2. If a backward  reaction r_0001_REV exists, this will give two more reactions:  r 0001 REV EXP 1 and r 0001 REV EXP 2. As this step is skipped  for light ecModels, their reaction identifiers remain unchanged  from how they were defined in the starting GEM.  

6.	 An empty ‘ecModel.ec’ structure is constructed, which will  eventually be populated with information about, e.g., enzymes,  k cat  values and associate enzymes to the correct reactions (Table  3 ).

 7.	 Enzyme details such as MW and amino acid sequence are added  to the ‘ecModel.ec’ structure. This information is gathered from  UniProt, which is automatically obtained using the UniProt related  parameters in the model adapter.

 8.	 The ‘ecModel.ec.rxnEnzMat’ indicate which reactions are  catalyzed by which enzymes. Momentarily, this is indicated  by a 1, while at a later stage this can be modified to reflect  potential different numbers of subunits that make an enzyme  complex.

 9.	 (Skipped for light ecModels) The enzymes are added as  pseudo-metabolites to the ecModel, they will now appear also  in ‘ecModel.mets’ with the identifier prefix prot_ followed by  the protein identifier (as specified in ‘ecModel.ec.enzymes’).  For instance  S. cerevisiae  enolase gene YHR174W with (UniProt)  protein identifier  P00925  appears as pseudo-metabolite  prot_P00925.

 10.	As previously described in the summary of Stage 1, a protein pool  pseudo-metabolite is added to ‘ecModel.mets’.

 11.	(Skipped for light ecModels) Add usage reactions for the enzyme  pseudo-metabolites, which replenish enzyme pseudo-metabolites  that are used by catalyzing reactions. The identifiers of the usage  reactions are usage_ prefix followed by the metabolite identifier of  the enzyme e.g., usage prot P 00925.

 12.	An exchange reaction for the prot_pool pseudo-metabolite is added. ▲  Critical step   If Step 12 is omitted, or if information is missing for a particular enzyme  complex, the ecModel will assume that each subunit has a stoichiometry of one, which is  however not an unrealistic assumption for most complexes.  

13.	 Carefully inspect the content of ‘proposedComplex’ and consider whether manual curation  of the respective ecModel gene association might be appropriate. This may require  additional literature study.

 	 ▲  Critical step   The model derived from ‘apply Complex Data’ only integrates complex  data for a reaction if a full match (100%) is found between its ecModel gene association  and the data retrieved from Complex Portal. If no full match is found, ‘proposedComplex’  will suggest complexes that have a partial match. A complex from Complex Portal can  be suggested by either of two criteria: (i) at least 75% of the ecModel genes of a reaction  match with a complex in Complex Portal or (ii) a complex in Complex Portal contains more  subunits than the genes that are associated to an ecModel reaction.

 14.	 Save the ecModel to disk; this can be done at any point in the procedure. To retain all  ecModel content, including the ‘ecModel.ec fields’, store the ecModel in YAML format.  ‘saveEcModel’ automatically does this in the adapter folder, while the more generic  ‘writeYAMLmodel’ can also store the file in any desired folder. To load the model back into  MATLAB, use ‘loadEcModel’ or the generic ‘readYAMLmodel’.

# Stage 2: integration of  k cat  into the ecModel structure ●  Timing  1 h  

15.	 Decide which source(s) of  k cat  data you are going to use. GECKO can consider the four  sources of  k cat  data listed below. Any combination of source(s) can be used in any order, but  at least one of the  k cat  gathering approach should be conducted. •  Through fuzzy matching with the BRENDA database ( https://www.brenda-enzymes.org/ ) •  By deep learning prediction with DLKcat •  From a list of manually curated custom  k cat  values •  Assignment of a standard  k cat  value  

# Fuzzy matching with BRENDA database  

16.	 Gather reaction-specific  k cat  data from the BRENDA database, where the reactions are  matched by EC numbers and substrate names. As this approach is intended for flexible  matching with either wildcards in the EC number or  k cat  from other substrates, it is also  known as fuzzy matching.  

17.	 Assign EC numbers to reactions. This is done either by reading them from the ‘model. eccodes’ field in the conventional GEM, if such a field exists, or otherwise by gathering  them from annotations in the UniProt and KEGG databases. •  If the starting GEM has manually curated EC numbers, populate ‘ecModel.ec.eccodes’  with model-derived EC numbers using ‘getECfromGEM’. For reactions without  EC number annotation, query UniProt/KEGG with ‘get EC from Database’ to get the  highest coverage. As ‘get EC from Database’ will overwrite any entries in ‘ecModel. ec.eccodes’, it is necessary to first define which entries were not populated with  EC numbers after running ‘getECfromGEM’:  

ecModel = getECfromGEM(ecModel);  noEC = cellfun(@isempty, ecModel.ec.eccodes);  ecModel = get EC from Database(ecModel, noEC);  

•  If the starting GEM is not annotated with a ‘model.eccodes’ field; it does not contain  standard EC numbers (i.e., four groups of digits separated by a period); or the user has  low confidence in the accuracy of the ‘model.eccodes’ field as it is not a well-curated  model, only run ‘get EC from Database’:  

ecModel = get EC from Database(ecModel);  

•  If desired, the EC numbers derived from the database can be transferred to the model. eccodes fields with ‘copyECtoGEM’.  

18.	 After populating ‘ecModel.ec.eccodes’ with corresponding EC numbers, gather  k cat  from  BRENDA:  

kcatList_fuzzy = fuzzy Kc at Matching(ecModel);  

In this process, BRENDA is queried for  k cat  values by EC number, substrate and organism.

 19.	 Look at the results. The output from this function (and later functions) is a ‘kcatList’ structure.  This object documents which potential  k cat  could be assigned to the various reactions, together  with information on how precise the match is for each  k cat  (i.e., number of required EC number  wildcards, if matched by substrate and/or organism). It is common, even for model organisms  such as  S. cerevisiae , that no full match can be found in the BRENDA database. In those cases,  k cat  values from partial organism and/or substrate matches are returned. If no  k cat  is found for  a particular EC number, wildcards in the EC number are introduced: e.g., EC 2.4.2.3 (uridine  phosphorylase) becomes 2.4.2.- (pen to syl transfer as e).  

# Deep learning prediction with DLKcat  

20.	 Gather  k cat  through deep learning prediction with DLKcat, based on enzyme sequence  information and substrate structural information in simplified molecular-input line-entry  system (SMILES) format.

 21.	 As starting GEMs rarely come with SMILES annotation, gather these by querying PubChem  with ‘findMetSmiles’, thus creating ‘data/smilesDB.tsv’, or manually populating the file  ‘data/smilesDB.tsv’ in the adapter folder.  

[ecModel, noSMILES] = findMetSmiles(ecModel);  

▲  Critical step   The ‘findMetSmiles’ function reports which percentage of unique  metabolites could be assigned with SMILES annotation. If this percentage is below 100%, as is  almost always the case, it is worthwhile to inspect the ‘noSMILES’ list of metabolite names.  For example, in some models the metabolite name is suffixed by the metabolite formula  (even while SBML has a dedicated field for this information), which precludes matching with  PubChem. Manual curation of the ‘ecModel.metNames’ field is required to resolve such issues.

 22.	 Define what metabolites should be used by DLKcat. The DLKcat prediction does not run  natively in MATLAB but requires Python. However, GECKO can write the required input  file for DLKcat. As part of this function, in accordance to how DLKcat functions 24 , currency  metabolites that occur in pairs e.g., adenosine triphosphate (ATP) versus adenosine  diphosphate (ADP) and NADH versus NAD, and a selection of small molecules, e.g., Fe 2+  are  excluded when writing the DLKcat input file. Nonexhaustive lists of such metabolites  and molecules are provided under ‘GECKO/databases’ as ‘D LK cat Currency Mets.tsv’ and  ‘DLKcatIgnore.tsv’, while one can provide ecModel-specific metabolite names by having files  with those same names under data in their adapter folder. Note that currency metabolites  are only excluded if the reactions have other reactants (after small-molecule removal), such  as the case for ATP synthase.  

23.	 Write the DLKcat input file to ‘data/DLKcat.tsv’ under the adapter folder with ‘write D LK cat Input’,   which contains, e.g., amino acid sequences for proteins and SMILES for metabolites.  

write D LK cat Input(ecModel);  

▲  Critical step   Any ‘DLKcat.tsv’ file generated will be specific for either the full or the  light version of the ecModel, as the file contains the reaction identifiers from ‘ecModel. ec.rxns’ that will be required when loading the predicted  k cat  values back into MATLAB  (see below). Therefore, separate ‘DLKcat.tsv’ files need to be constructed for either of the  two ecModel versions.  

24.	 Run the DLKcat prediction pipeline with ‘runDLKcat’, which automatically download and  start a Docker image:  

runDLKcat();  

Note that no other input parameters are given, as the function assumes that the DLKcat  input file is written to ‘data/DLKcat.tsv’ in the adapter folder.  

25.	 To construct a ‘kcatList’ structure, load back the outcome from DLKcat into MATLAB: kcatList_DLKcat = read D LK cat Output(ecModel);  

# Merge DLKcat and BRENDA structures  

26.	 If  k cat  values are gathered from both DLKcat (Step 25) and BRENDA database (through fuzzy  matching, Step 18), merge the two ‘kcatList’ structures to increase the  k cat  enzyme coverage.  

kcatList_merged = mergeD LK cat And Fuzzy K cats(kcatList_DLKcat, kcatList_ fuzzy);  

During this process, single  k cat  values are assigned to each reaction, where priority is  given to those  k cat  that were obtained from BRENDA by full EC number match. By default,  mismatches on organism and/or substrate are allowed, but this can be modified by  providing additional input parameters. If no exact EC number match is available, the  k cat   derived from DLKcat is used. If DLKcat was not able to predict a  k cat  value for a reaction (for  example, if its substrate had no SMILES assigned), then EC number wildcard matches from  fuzzy matching are allowed, if available. For instance, instead of assigning a  k cat  to pyruvate  oxidase by querying EC 1.2.3.3, it will query EC 1.2.3.- that includes any oxidoreductase  acting on the aldehyde or oxo group of donors with oxygen as acceptor, irrespective of  reactant. From this group, the highest  k cat  value will be selected. A more detailed description  of the fuzzy matching algorithm is included in GECKO 2 (ref.  16 ).  

27.	 To integrate  k cat  data into the ecModel, run ‘selectKcatValue’ with a ‘kcatList’ as input, which  could be derived from any source in previous steps. Here, the merged ‘kcatList’ populates  ‘ecModel.ec.kcat’ with the relevant  k cat  values:  

ecModel = selectKcatValue(ecModel, kcatList_merged);  

Provide custom  k cat  values 28.	 Custom  k cat  values can be provided as alternative or sole source of  k cat  values. These  k cat  values can be based on manual curation, potentially based on results from later in  this procedure, or obtained through another analysis or prediction procedure, such as  in vivo apparent enzyme turnover numbers. Document the custom  k cat  values in ‘data/ customKcats.tsv’ in the adapter folder that are mapped to reactions and/or enzymes,  and add directly to ‘ecModel.ec.kcat’ with:  

[ecModel, rxnUpdated, notMatch] = apply Custom K cats(ecModel);  

This function overwrites any existing values in ‘ecModel.ec.kcat’ for matching reactions,  assuming that the custom  k cat  values are of higher confidence. Pay attention if ‘notMatch’  contains reactions, as their  k cat  values had not been successfully applied to the ecModel.  A reason for this can be that the gene association between the model and the input file  match <50%. To resolve such cases, inspect and manually curate the input file.  

▲  Critical step   Functions that populate ‘ecModel.ec.kcat’, for instance ‘selectKcatValue’  and ‘apply Custom K cats’, but also other functions that are introduced below, can be run  in any order. Unless otherwise specified, they will overwrite existing values in ‘ecModel. ec.kcat’, and document in ‘ecModel.ec.source’ where the current  k cat  was derived, e.g., from  ‘brenda’ or ‘custom’.  

# Mean  k cat  of isozymes  

29.	 (Optional) Situations may occur where  k cat  values of a particular reaction are not defined for  all isozymatic variants. The reaction variant that is associated to the isozyme without  k cat  will  then have no protein cost. Consequentially, this reaction variant will be preferred over the  other reaction variants that have a protein cost. To avoid this,  k cat  values across isozymes are  reasonable estimates for isozymes with no other  k cat  knowledge available. Such missing  k cat   values in ‘ecModel.ec.kcats’ can be substituted by the mean  k cat  of their isozymes. To do so, run:  

ecModel = get Kc at Across Iso zy mes(ecModel);  

Note that this situation is not relevant for light ecModels, where only the most efficient  isozyme is considered.  

# Assign a standard  k cat  value  

30.	 (Optional) Reactions that do not have genes associated in the starting GEM (empty entries in  ‘model.grRules’) cannot have enzyme constraints added by GECKO, due to the lack of enzyme  data (i.e., MW and  k cat  value). For those reactions (with the exception of exchange, spontaneous,  transport and pseudo-reactions), a standard  k cat  value and a standard pseudo-enzyme (prot_ standard) can be included to constrain their catalytic capacity. Various standard  k cat  values  are calculated based on (i) the mean of  k cat  values of the reactions involved in the subsystem  in which a particular reaction belongs to or (ii) the mean of all  k cat  values in ‘ecModel.ec.kcat’ if  no subsystem is defined for that reaction. To follow the GECKO formulation that uses MW/ k cat ,  a standard MW is also calculated as median of all the proteins in the organism, and a protein  usage reaction ‘usage prot standard’ is added to the ecModel. To do so, run:  

[ecModel, rxnsMissingGPR, standardMW, standardKcat]   = getStandardKcat(ecModel);  

The standard MW and subsystem agnostic standard  k cat  are reported in ‘standardMW’ and  ‘standardKcat’, respectively.  

# Apply the ECs to the model  

31.	 After populating ‘ecModel.ec.kcats’ with various sources of  k cat  values, apply the ECs with: ecModel = apply Kc at Constraints(ecModel);  

During this step the ‘ecModel.S’ matrix is modified to directly include the protein  cost (Fig.  1 ), based on the  k cat  from ‘ecModel.ec.kcats’, the MWs from ‘ecModel.ec.mw’  and, if relevant, multiplied by the enzyme complex stoichiometry as obtained from  ‘apply Complex Data’. The output ‘ecModel’ has the enzymes directly involved as  pseudo-substrates, to reflect enzyme usage. This function can be run at any point to (re-) apply the  k cat , MW and complex data as ECs.  

# Constrain the protein pool exchange reaction  

32.	 The enzyme pseudo-metabolites that are included as cosubstrates in the above function  are, by default, replenished from the total protein pool. The protein pool exchange reaction  should therefore be constrained by a realistic value. This constraint is calculated from the  σ ,  P tot  and  f  parameters in the model adapter, which stand for the average enzyme saturation,  total protein content and fraction of proteins that are included in the model, respectively.  To set the protein pool constraint, run:  

ecModel = setProtPoolSize(ecModel);  

If quantitative proteomics data are available, for instance via PaxDB, store the data in ‘data/ paxDB.tsv’ and calculate the  f  factor with  

f = calculate F factor(ecModel);  

Then use this anew  f  factor to set the protein pool constraint with:  

ecModel = setProtPoolSize(ecModel, [], f);  

# Stage 3: model tuning ●  Timing  15 min Simulate growth rate  

33.	 After applying ECs, it is necessary to examine whether the ecModel can simulate a realistic  growth rate. In particular, microorganisms should be able to reach their maximum growth  rate if nutrient supply is not limiting. This can be simulated in the ecModel by removing  constraints on nutrient exchange and maximizing biomass production, which can be done  by functions from RAVEN toolbox (for a longer list of relevant RAVEN functions, see Box  2 ).  To allow for unconstrained uptake of carbon source, run:  

ecModel = setParam(ecModel, 'lb', params.c_source, -1000); Or by directly referring to the glucose exchange reaction in the  S. cerevisiae  ecModel: ecModel = setParam(ecModel, 'lb', 'r_1714', -1000);  

34.	 In this example, the glucose exchange reaction is defined as ‘glucose[e] <=>’. A positive,  forwards flux through the reaction thereby represents a dissipation from the system.  Consequentially, a negative flux through this reaction signifies glucose exchange into the  system, which will allow for uptake into the cytoplasm. Modify the constraint on glucose  uptake by setting the lower bound of the exchange reaction, which is a negative number to  allow for negative fluxes. Query the direction of a reaction with:  

construct Equations(ecModel, 'r_1714');

 35.	 Set growth as the objective function, predict the flux distribution and print the growth rate:  

ecModel = setParam(ecModel, 'obj', params.bioRxn, 1);  sol = solveLP(ecModel);  bioRxnIdx = getIndexes(ecModel, params.bioRxn, 'rxns');  fprintf('Growth rate: %f /hour\n', sol.x(bioRxnIdx))

Three reasons can be systematically checked: •  The stoichiometry of the metabolic network is not fully correct •  The protein pool exchange is too tightly constrained •  Incorrect (too low)  k cat  values are assigned to reactions  

# Network stoichiometry limits growth rate  

38.	 Investigate the stoichiometry of the metabolic network itself by predicting the growth rate  in the conventional GEM (i.e., without the enzyme constraints) with the same exchange flux  constraints. To do so, run:  

model = load Conventional GEM();  model = setParam(model, 'lb', params.c_source, -1000);  model = setParam(model, 'obj', params.bioRxn, 1);  sol = solveLP(model);  bioRxnIdx = getIndexes(model, params.bioRxn, 'rxns');  fprintf('Growth rate: %f /hour\n', sol.x(bioRxnIdx))  

If the conventional GEM is also not able to reach the maximum growth rate, then the starting  GEM contains errors in its stoichiometry or constraints that should be resolved before using  it to reconstruct an ecModel.  

# Too tight protein pool constraint  

39.	 Confirm that the protein pool constraint set in Step 32 is realistic. Inspect the  σ ,  P tot  and  f   parameters in the model adapter and modify them to more realistic values if discrepancies  are observed (Step 4).  

40.	 Regardless of whether the protein pool parameters are realistic (Step 39), the simplest solution  to enable the ecModel to reach the intended growth is to relax the constraint on the protein  pool usage reaction, thereby relieving all enzyme contstraints at once. To release the protein  pool constraint, set the lower bound to −1,000 (for reaction direction, see Step 34):  

ecModel = setParam(ecModel, 'lb', 'prot pool exchange', -1000);  

41.	 As the ecModel has now neither a protein pool constraint nor nutrient constraint (Step 33),  it would be reasonable to predict the lowest protein pool usage that still supports the  experimental maximum growth rate. To do this, run:  

ecModel = setParam(ecModel, 'lb', 'r_4041', 0.41); ecModel = setParam(ecModel, 'obj', 'prot pool exchange', 1);  sol = solveLP(ecModel);  protPoolIdx = strcmp(ecModel.rxns, 'prot pool exchange');  fprintf('Protein pool usage is: %.0f mg/gDCW\n', abs(sol.x(protPoolIdx)))  

▲  Critical step   Due to the direction of the exchange reaction (Step 34), minimization of  protein pool usage is implied by using ‘1’ (and not ‘−1’) as objective coefficient.  

42.	 Set the predicted protein pool usage as constraint on the protein pool exchange reaction,  before reverting the previously asserted objective function and constraint of the growth rate:  

ecModel = setParam(ecModel, 'lb', protPoolIdx, sol.x(protPoolIdx));  ecModel = setParam(ecModel, 'lb', 'r_4041', 0); ecModel = setParam(ecModel, 'obj', 'r_4041', 1);  

While this approach always results in an ecModel that reaches the intended growth rate  (assuming that the conventional GEM is also able to simulate this), it often leads to the  protein pool usage reaction set to unrealistic protein content as mentioned before. It is  therefore recommended to instead increase  k cat  values of some reactions.  

# Sensitivity tuning of  k cat  values  

43.	 In contrast to Step 42, it is advised to rather iteratively increase  k cat  values of enzymes that  limit growth, until the desired growth rate is reached. At this stage, the enzyme constraints  are dominated by the overarching protein pool constraint. Therefore, in each deterministic  iteration, the most-limiting enzyme is identified as the one demanding the largest fraction  of the protein pool. Consequentially, in each iteration, one  k cat  is increased, and while the  selected  k cat  is not per se the most deviating from its in situ value, it is the  k cat  whose value  contributes most to over constraining the model. First revert the protein pool constraint as  set in Step 42 to a more realistic value (Step 32), tune the  k cat  values and report the changes:  

ecModel = setProtPoolSize(ecModel);  [ecModel, tunedKcats] = sensitivity Tuning(ecModel);  struct2table(tunedKcats)  

44.	 Look at the report. The ‘tunedKcats’ output structure documents which  k cat  have been  modified, together with their previous value and the reaction that is catalyzed by each  enzyme. It is worthwhile to inspect this information, to find out whether any of the  now-modified  k cat  values were initially wrongly gathered from any of the  k cat  sources.

# Stage 4: integration of proteomics data into the ecModel ●  Timing  15 min  

# Constrain with proteomics data  

53.	 Instead of constraining the total protein content, the concentration of individual enzymes  can be constrained by integration of proteomics data. As this process is not suitable for light  ecModels, during Stage 4 it is assumed that the ecModel is a full version. It is important to  realize that constraining individual enzymes will in most cases generate an overconstrained  ecModel, which is not able to reach the intended growth rate, as there are now uncertainties  in both the individual enzyme levels and the  k cat  values. It is therefore imperative that  the proteomics data is of high quality, and protein concentrations of lower certainty are  discarded.  

54.	 Prepare the proteomics data. It is important to:  

•  Provide the protein concentrations in milligram per gram dry cell weight, which can be  obtained through various methods 59 •  Store the data in ‘data/proteomics.tsv’, which can contain data from multiple  experimental conditions •  Filter out lower certainty data, which include discarding of very low protein  concentrations, proteins with high relative standard deviation and proteins absent  from most of the replicates  

55.	 Check that the proteomics data is in a standardized file format by referring to ‘data/ proteomics.tsv’. Similar to integrating  k cat  in the ecModel, integration of proteomics data  involves four steps:  

•  Load the proteomics data into a ‘protData’ structure •  Populate the ‘ecModel.ec.concs’ with the relevant enzyme concentrations •  Apply the enzyme concentrations as constraints in enzyme usage reactions (for reaction  direction, see Step 34) •  Modify the usage reactions of enzymes with defined concentrations to no longer draw  from the generic protein pool pseudo-metabolite •  While it is possible to manually enter and/or modify the ‘ecModel.ec.concs’ field, we  recommend providing the proteomics data in a standardized file format to be read by  provided functions.  

56.	 Load the proteomics data set. If the proteomics dataset consists of one experiment with  three replicates, load it with:  

protData = loadProtData(3);  

If there are two experiments with three replicates each, the command is:  

protData = loadProtData([3,3]);  

The above function also includes filtering of the data, which is therefore applied on the  whole dataset. When protData contains multiple experiments, specify which experiment  should be used (experiment 1 here):  

57.	 In the same way that the ecModel was populated with  k cat  values, the enzyme concentrations  can be filled in using ‘ecModel.ec.concs’. Introduce these concentrations to ‘ecModel.lb’ with:  

ecModel = constrain Enz Con cs(ecModel);  

▲  Critical step   It is essential that the enzyme concentrations is provided in the correct  unit: milligram per gram dry cell weight (DCW). Relative proteomics is not able to provide  data in such units. Loading proteomics data with protein levels in incorrect units will result  in nonsensical ecModels.  

58.	 Enzymes that are constrained by their concentrations are no longer drawn from the  pseudo-metabolite protein pool, which was earlier defined to represent all enzymes  combined (Step 32). The constraint on the protein pool exchange reaction should therefore  be modified to only represent enzymes that are not constrained by their concentrations.  It is also very common that at least some of the listed enzymes will not have available  concentrations, due to technical challenges in the experimental methods. To do this, enter  the command ‘updateProtPool’. As the proteomics data are derived from an experiment  where the cells might have changed their physiology, a sample-specific experimentally  measured total protein content (e.g., 0.5 g/gDCW) can be explicitly considered, instead of  referring to the ‘params.Ptot’ value in the model adapter, with:  

ecModel = updateProtPool(ecModel, 0.5);  

# Constrain with experimentally measured exchange fluxes  

59.	 As the integrated enzyme concentrations are derived from a particular experiment, it is  relevant to also constrain any exchange reaction for which experimental data are available,  e.g., the glucose uptake, ethanol production or CO 2  exchange rate, determined for the same  experiment. The flux data file, located at ‘data/fluxData.tsv’ by default, can contain data  from multiple experiments, and can contain as many reaction fluxes as desired. Note that  providing a zero flux will result in blocking an exchange reaction. Load the data:  

fluxData = loadFluxData();  

60.	 Decide whether it is most appropriate to use ‘loose’ constraints or ‘strict’ constraints for the  experimentally measured metabolite exchange rates. 1.	 With ‘loose’ constraints, the measured rate is set as upper bound, while the lower bound  is remained at zero (for exchange reactions with a negative rate, this translates to setting  the lower bound to the measured value and the upper bound to zero). 2.	 With ‘strict’ constraints, the lower and upper bounds are set at, e.g., 95% and 105% of the  measured value. The stricter constraints will force the ecModel to adhere to the measured fluxes more  closely, but risk that imprecision in, e.g., the measured fluxes or enzyme concentrations  result in a nonfunctional model that cannot solve FBA. In that case, the use of ‘loose’  is advised.

 61.	 Decide how the growth rate should be applied as either ‘max’ or ‘min’ constraint. •  With ‘max’ the measured growth rate is set as upper bound to indicate the maximum  growth rate that the ecModel should be allowed to reach. This is suitable if growth is  used as objective function (Step 35) •  With ‘min’ the measured growth rate is set as lower bound to indicate the  minimum growth rate that the ecModel should be able to reach. This is suitable  if the minimization of the protein pool exchange reaction is used as objective  function (Step 41)

 62.	 After deciding in Steps 60 and 61 how the experimentally measured fluxes should be  constrained in the ecModel (e.g., set ‘loose’ constraints for the metabolic exchange fluxes  and ‘max’ for the growth rate to yield an ecModel that is least likely to be overconstrained),  data from the first experiment are set and FBA is run to predict the growth rate: ecModel = constrain Flux Data(ecModel, fluxData, 1, 'max', 'loose');  sol = solveLP(ecModel);  fprintf('Growth rate that is reached: %f /hour\n', abs(sol.f))  

63.	 Analyze the results. With ‘max’ and ‘loose’ as parameters, the FBA should be able to  reach a solution (unless too many zero-exchange fluxes are defined in Step 59). If the  resulting ecModel is not able to reach the intended growth rate, it is necessary to make the  overconstrained enzyme levels more flexible.  

# Flexibilize measured protein concentrations  

64.	 Perform an iterative process consisting of calculating the control coefficients of all enzymes  and increasing the concentration of the enzyme with the greatest control coefficient. This  process is repeated until the intended growth rate (here 0.1 h  −1 ) is reached. It is adjustable  by which fold change the enzyme level should be increased in each iteration (here, tenfold).  Once the growth rate is reached, reduce the adjusted enzyme levels to the minimum  concentration that still allows for the same flux distribution.  

[ecModel, flexEnz] = flex i bil ize Enz Con cs(ecModel, 0.1, 10);  

The flexibilized enzyme levels are reflected in changed constraints of their respective  usage_prot reactions (the direction of usage_prot reactions is the same as the protein pool  exchange reaction; Step 40).  

▲  Critical step   The concentrations in ‘ecModel.ec.concs’ remain unchanged and  continue to reflect the measured values that were obtained via ‘fillEnzConcs’. Only the lower  bounds of selected usage_prot reactions are changed to the flexibilized value.

 65.	 Inspect which enzymes were modified in ‘flexEnz’. While flexibilizing enzyme concentrations  is a reasonable approach as it cannot be excluded that the protein measurement was  imprecise, it is possible that instead of the enzyme concentration, it should be the  k cat  that is  modified instead. If the same enzyme concentration is repeatedly modified, the user will also  be prompted to check its  k cat  and manually curate it by defining custom  k cat  values (Step 28  and Step 45, while focusing on the reported problematic enzyme).

# Simulate exchange fluxes at various growth rates  

▲  Critical   The ecModel without proteomics integration can be used to investigate the effect  of the total protein pool constraint on metabolism, for instance, at different growth rates. One  can postulate that at low growth rates, the cellular metabolic activity is low, and the enzymes are  probably present in copious amounts. Contrastingly, at high growth rates metabolic activity is  high, such that the high metabolic fluxes maybe become constrained by the amount of available  enzyme. The effect of this is shown by simulating exchange fluxes at various growth rates. An  example procedure is shown.

# Choose the objective function  

▲  Critical   A common consideration in simulations is the choice of objective function. In FBA  of conventional GEMs, specifically when simulating microbes, the maximization of growth is  often used as the objective function. This still remains relevant for ecModels but is joined by  another critical objective function. Once it is observed what growth rate can be reached, the  ecModel should be minimized for the total protein pool reaction, based on the assumption that  the cell is more likely to present flux distributions that make efficient use of protein resources.

 69.	 Set maximization of biomass production as the objective function before running FBA:  

ecModel = setParam(ecModel, 'obj', params.bioRxn, 1);  sol = solveLP(ecModel);  fprintf('Growth rate that is reached: %.4f /hour\n', abs(sol.f))  

70.	 Set the growth rate that is reached as lower bound for the biomass production reaction,  then change the objective function to minimizing the protein pool exchange reaction  (Step 41) and again run FBA. To avoid potential numeric issues due to, e.g., rounding, use  99% of the growth rate as lower bound:  

ecModel = setParam(ecModel, 'lb', params.bioRxn, 0.99\*abs(sol.f));  ecModel = setParam(ecModel, 'obj', 'prot pool exchange', 1);  sol = solveLP(ecModel);  fprintf('Minimum protein pool usage: %.2f mg/gDCW\n', abs(sol.f))  

Such a simulation yields the flux distribution that has a growth rate that is ≥99% of  the maximum growth rate in that condition with the most efficient enzyme resource  allocation.

Enzyme usages can be particularly insightful when the total protein constraint  is limiting, as it details the resource balance allocation across all enzymes. # Compare flux distributions of different models  

▲  Critical   Flux distributions from ecModels cannot be directly compared with those  derived from their starting GEM, as the structure of the model is changed substantially by 

 ‘makeEcModel’. It is therefore essential to map the fluxes between the two model formulations.

 72.	 Map back an ecModel derived flux distribution to its starting conventional GEM with  ‘mapRxnsToConv’, where flux values from isozymatic reactions and inverted reversible  reactions are combined. sol = solveLP(ecModel);   [mappedFlux, enzUsageFlux, usageEnz] = mapRxnsToConv(ecModel, model, sol.x);  

The resulting ‘mappedFlux’ vector has the same number and order of fluxes as what would  be obtained from performing FBA on the conventional GEM, and can therefore be treated as  any other flux vector derived from a conventional GEM.  

▲  Critical step   When mapping reactions from an ecModel back to a conventional  GEM, it is essential that the ecModel is indeed derived from the conventional GEM, as the  reactions are mapped by their reaction identifiers without further evaluation if the chemical  reactions are identical. Any larger stoichiometric changes that are made to ecModel beyond  applying enzyme and proteomics constraints will not be correctly reflected. Examples of  this are changing the directionality of a reaction, or adding completely new reactions to  the ecModel.

As stated above, common analysis methods such as FVA work  as well with ecModels as with any other constraint-based metabolic models. In RAVEN toolbox  this can be done with ‘get Allowed Bounds’; however, for comparison with the FVA results from  a non-ecModel, it is convenient to directly map the results to the original model, via the ‘ecFVA’  function.

# Context-specific models  

▲  Critical   While ecModels can be reconstructed for higher organisms such as humans,  the starting GEM should be not the generic model but rather context-specific models based  on omics data by approaches such as tINIT. To obtain context-specific ecModels there are two  paths: (i) contextualize the generic model and then convert it to ecModel or (ii) reconstruct  a generic ecModel and then contextualize it. The second path has the benefit that any  contextualized ecModel derived from the same generic ecModel will have the same  k cat  data,  and the second approach is much faster, which becomes essential when, e.g., reconstructing  hundreds of single-cell ecModels. In this step, the demonstration is therefore with the  human-GEM in ‘tutorials/light_ecModel’. As an example, ‘HT29’ is a context-specific model of  the human colorectal adenocarcinoma cell line HT-29 and ‘ecModel’ is the generic ecModel,  which are both derived from Human-GEM.

It is important to note that  the exact output shown here might be somewhat (numerically) different, dependent on, e.g.,  updates available at the different databases and various solvers.  

Step 1: The adapter folder is initiated at the location that was specified by the user, containing  empty subfolders named ‘code’, ‘data’, ‘models’ and ‘output’, together with a template model  adapter file that starts with the specified model name, suffixed with ‘Adapter.m’. Step 2: Typically, the starting GEM file in the ‘models’ subfolder of the adapter folder. Steps 3–7: The model adapter file from Step 1 is populated with organism- and model-specific  parameters. If required, when no appropriate KEGG and/or UniProt parameters could be  identified, custom ‘data/uni prot Conversion.tsv’, ‘data/kegg.tsv’ and/or ‘data/uniprot.tsv’  datafiles are constructed in the adapter folder.  

Step 8: The MATLAB instance is primed for working with a particular ecModel, by setting the  relevant ‘modelAdapter’ as default.  

Step 9: The conventional GEM is loaded into the MATLAB workspace within a structure named  ‘model’. Simulations with this model can be performed as with any GEM. Steps 10–11: Enzyme data are downloaded from UniProt and KEGG databases, and stored in 

 ‘data/uniprot.tsv’ and ‘data/kegg.tsv’. In addition, an empty ecModel is generated and named as 

 ‘ecModel’ in the MATLAB workspace.  

A structure called ‘noUniprot’ may contain a list of genes for which no match could be found  in the UniProt database file.  

For this worked example, in the ‘light_ecModel’ tutorial, noUniprot contains  six genes that could not be mapped. As this is a relatively low number, it is probably  futile to construct different UniProt database files by changing ‘obj.params. uniprot.ID’ in the model adapter. Instead, manual curation of the gene identifiers,  ‘uni prot Conversion.tsv’ or manually adding the relevant entries to the UniProt database  file are recommended.  

Regardless of noUniprot reporting some genes, an empty ecModel is generated with  enzymes for all matching genes. Simulations with this empty ecModel will behave similarly  as the conventional GEM it was derived from, as no enzyme and protein pool constraints are  applied yet.  

Steps 12–13: Enzyme complex data is retrieved and stored in ‘data/ComplexPortal.json’.  The enzyme complex data is subsequently integrated into the ecModel, modifying the  ‘ecModel.ec.rxnEnzMat’ field with the appropriate stoichiometric coefficients, while a  progress bar shows the remaining duration of this process. In the ‘full_ecModel’ tutorial,  the MATLAB command window displays:  

A total of 206 complex have full match, and 17 proposed.  

As an example of the content of ‘proposedComplex’, reaction r_0505_EXP_2 could  potentially be matched with complex CPX-1293, named ‘mitochondrial 2-oxoglutarate  dehydrogenase complex’. In the ecModel, this reaction is annotated with proteins  P20967   (KGD1, or subunit E1),  P19262  (KGD2, or subunit E2) and  P09624  (LPD1, or subunit E3),  

while CPX-1293 in Complex Portal also includes the protein  P19955  (YMR31), which is known as  ‘37S ribosomal protein’. Indeed, in the literature, evidence can be found that  P19955 , also termed  KGD4 or subunit E4, is an essential subunits that is necessary to form a stable complex 61 . On the  basis of this, the starting GEM yeast-GEM should be curated with the updated gene association  for reaction r_0505, which would result in a 100% match when running ‘makeEcModel’ and  ‘apply Complex Data’.  

Step 14: The ecModel is stored in the defined file format (e.g., SBML or YAML) in the ‘models’  subfolder. If stored in SBML, this model file can be loaded by any other relevant software that  accepts SBML files.  

Steps 15–19: The ‘ecModel.ec.eccodes’ field is curated to contain EC numbers.  k cat  data are  collected from the BRENDA database and saved as a ‘kcatList’, e.g., ‘kcatList_fuzzy’, which  contains the following fields:  

•  source: ‘brenda’ when the ‘kcatList’ is generated by ‘fuzzy Kc at Matching’, e.g. ‘dlkcat’ if  generated by ‘read D LK cat Output’ (Step 25) •  rxns: reaction identifiers matching ‘ecModel.ec.rxns’ •  substrates: names of all substrates for each reaction that were used to match to BRENDA  database •  kcats: proposed  k cat  values as derived from fuzzy matching with BRENDA •  eccodes: EC numbers that were used to match to BRENDA database •  wildcardLvl: level of EC wildcard that was necessary to find a match: – 0: w.x.y.z – 1: w.x.y.- – 2: w.x.-.- – 3: w.-.-.- •  origin: level of specificity that was necessary to find a match: – 1: correct organism, correct substrate,  k cat  value – 2: closest related organism, correct substrate,  k cat  value – 3: correct organism, any substrate,  k cat  value – 4: closest related organism, any substrate,  k cat  value – 5: correct organism, specific activity – 6: closest related organism, specific activity A progress bar shows the remaining duration of this process.  

Steps 20–25: matches that ‘findMetSmiles’ finds between metabolite names and SMILES are  stored in ‘data/smilesDB.tsv’, to speed up repeated runs of the function on the same ecModel.  The ‘ecModel.metSmiles’ is generated and filled with SMILES. The MATLAB Command Window  displays:  

Check for local SMILES database… done.  SMILES could be found for 64% of the unique metabolite names.  

An input file for DLKcat is stored in ‘data/DLKcat.tsv’, containing all required data for  k cat   prediction. Once ‘runDLKcat’ is run, predicted  k cat  values are appended to the same ‘DLKcat.tsv’  file. As DLKcat runs via a Docker, no progress is reported in the MATLAB Command Window.  The resulting ‘kcatList’ is similar to the one in Step 18, but lacking the here irrelevant ‘eccodes’,  ‘wildcardLvl’ and ‘origin’ fields, while instead including a ‘genes’ field with entries for each  k cat   prediction.  

Step 26: the generated ‘kcatList’ is similar as those generated in Step 18 and Step 25,  with the exception of a ‘kcatSource’ field that indicates the source for each  k cat  value   individually.  

Step 27: the ‘ecModel.ec.kcat’ field is populated with selected  k cat  values, with ‘ecModel. ec.source’ indicating where each  k cat  is derived from.  

Step 28: the ‘ecModel.ec.kcat’ field is further populated with custom  k cat  values, overwriting any  existing values.  

Step 29: the ‘ecModel.ec.kcat’ field is updated to ensure that all isozymic reactions have  k cat   values assigned, while ‘ecModel.ec.source’ for those reactions indicate ‘isozymes’. Note that  

‘ecModel.S’ remains unchanged. Step 30: the ‘ecModel.ec’ structure is amended to include a ‘standard’ pseudo-enzyme of MW  ‘standardMW’ (44,898 Da), while reactions indicated in ‘rxnsMissingGPR’ and ‘rxnsNoKcat’ have  the ‘standardKcat’ value (12.3 s −1 ) as their ‘ecModel.ec.kcat’ entry. Step 31: the  k cat  values that are added and/or changed in ‘ecModel.ec.kcat’ during Steps 27–30 are  applied to ‘ecModel.S’. The resulting stoichiometric coefficients of the enzyme pseudo-metabolites   are defined as  MW , where the 3,600 factor converts  k  values from  cat × ( complex stochiometry ) s −1  to h −1 , to reconcile with the unit of metabolic rates in GEMs and ecModels.  

It is important to bear in mind that in ‘ecModel.ec’, each enzymatic reaction is assigned  one  k cat  value (Table  3 ), which for reactions catalyzed by enzyme complexes can be the highest  predicted  k cat  across its subunits (Step 27). If assuming the metabolic reaction ‘ X  →  Y ’ is catalyzed  by a complex of protein subunits  A ,  B  and  C  at a ratio 1:1:2, all subunits have the same MW of  27,000 Da and the highest predicted  k cat  value is 30 s −1 , then the three subunits are included as  pseudo-substrates in the reaction as ‘ X  + 0.25  A  + 0.25  B  + 0.5  C  →  Y ’.  

Step 32: the protein pool exchange reaction is constrained by the calculated maximum  enzyme usage. Due to the direction in which the ‘prot pool exchange’ is defined (positive flux  would be dissipation from the system, consistent for all exchange reactions), the constraint is  implemented as negative lower bound. If the total cellular protein content  P tot  is 0.5 g/gDCW,  the metabolic enzyme fraction over all proteins  f  is 0.5 and the average saturation  σ  factor is 0.5,  then the ‘ecModel.lb’ entry for ‘prot pool exchange’ is −125 mg/gDCW.  

Steps 33–36: the growth rate immediately after applying the  k cat  and protein pool constraint  is reported in the MATLAB command window, and dependent on the exact  k cat  values this  appears as:  

Growth rate: 0.107203 /hour  

As uptake of glucose and other nutrients is not limiting, this value represents the maximum  growth rate that the ecModel can reach. Being substantially lower than 0.41 h −1  indicates the  need for  k cat  tuning.  

Steps 37–38: the maximum growth rate of the starting GEM, without any constraints on carbon  uptake, is reported:  

Growth rate: 19.718033 /hour  

While this growth rate is not biologically realistic (due to the unconstrained carbon uptake),  it does signify that the stoichiometry of the conventional GEM is able to reach the maximum  growth rate of 0.41 h −1 , indicating that the problem in Step 35 is due to the ECs.

Steps 53–57: a ‘protData’ structure contains the proteomics data as provided in ‘data/ proteomics.tsv’, or could be manually reconstructed from other sources. Lipid metabolism in yeast-GEM are defined following the SLIMEr formulation 62 , where reactions  for each potential fatty acyl-chain configuration are explicitly included, as seen in the output  above. The resulting alternative reactions are all catalyzed by the same enzyme and therefore  require identical enzyme usages. This introduces a flexibility in the model where there are  multiple paths through lipid metabolism that all reach the same overall acyl-chain distribution.  This flexibility that is inherited from the conventional GEM is not affected by the ECs, once  more demonstrating that ecModels can be used as realistic drop-in alternatives for analyses of  conventional GEMs.
