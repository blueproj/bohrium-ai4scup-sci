In situ transmission electron microscopy was ﬁrst applied to study the precipitation behavior of topolog- ically close-packed (TCP) phase at elevated temperature in Ru-free and Ru-containing Ni-base single crys- tal superalloys. Ru addition can decrease both the nucleation and growth rate, hence to suppress TCP precipitation effectively. Higher exposure temperature reduces the nucleation rate but increases the growth rate, ﬁnally accelerates the precipitation. The TCP phases nucleate homogeneously in  c  matrix without preferential sites, but tend to grow toward primary  c / c 0   interface for element supply. An orien- tation relationship is kept during nucleation and growth of  l  phase in  c  matrix, which is [110] l  // [001] c and (001) l  // ( 110) c .

# 1. Introduction  

During the last decade, the further development of high- temperature structural materials for turbine blade has been focused on Ni-base single crystal superalloys, which contain high level of refractory elements such as Re and W, to enhance their high-temperature properties  [1–5] . However, with the increasing Re addition to 5–6 mass% in third-generation superalloys, topolog- ically close-packed (TCP) precipitation at elevated temperature (>950   C) is promoted severely  [1–3,6–8] . These refractory-rich phases, which have complex crystallographic structures  [9,10] , deplete the solid solution strengthening elements in matrix and serve as damage accumulation sites during creep process [7,8,11] . Thus, mechanical properties of the superalloys are degraded rapidly. Recently, Ru addition has been revealed being capable of stabilizing superalloys with respect to TCP precipitation [5,12–14] . However, the detailed mechanisms related to the effects of Ru addition still remain unclear.  

So far many investigations have been carried out to study the TCP precipitation in Ni-base single crystal superalloys, but none of them used in situ transmission electron microscopy (TEM) to provide direct evidence and details of the precipitation behavior. In this work, dynamic TCP nucleation and growth process was investigated by in situ TEM on Ru-free and Ru-containing samples at elevated temperature. No preferential interface nucleation was observed, and the suppression effect of Ru on TCP precipitation was conﬁrmed and discussed.  

# 2. Experimental procedure  

In reported literatures, many investigations were carried out to study the precipitation behavior of TCP  [7,15,16] , but few of them focused on the original nucleation and growth process. Generally, it was supposed that TCP nucleated preferentially close to or at c / c 0   interface  [16] . The nominal chemical compositions of the experimental alloys were listed in Table 1 . Alloy A is free of Ru. Alloy B contains 3 wt.% Ru and the other elements have the same content as alloy A. Single crystal bars were cast by directional solidiﬁca- tion with respect to [001] direction. Alloy A was solution heat treated at 1325   C for 16 h and 1335   C for 16 h. Alloy B was solution heat treated at 1290   C for 16 h and 1300   C for 16 h. Subsequent annealing process of alloys A and B was the same and was carried out at 1150   C for 4 h and 870   C for 24 h. Ultimately, both alloys were isothermally exposed at 1100   C for 270 h.  

l m thick pieces were cut from single crystal bars per- pendicular to [001] orientation by electrical discharge machining and then they were ground to 60  l m thick by using silicon carbide paper. 3 mm diameter disks were punched out from the pieces and further ground to about 30  l m thick. Finally, they were thinned by argon ion to electron transparent ﬁlm.

In situ TEM observations were carried out on a JEM-2100 electron microscope equipped with a CCD camera and a single tilt heating specimen holder, in which a tiny stove powered by impressed current is ﬁxed under the specimen with a heat- ing temperature up to 1350   C. Both Ru-free and Ru-containing alloy ﬁlms were investigated at elevated temperatures of 950   C and 1050   C for comparison. TCP phase identiﬁcation was also conducted after heating on a double-tilt specimen holder. Bright ﬁeld image and selected area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern were obtained to analyze the microstructure of TCP phase.  

# 3. Results and discussions  

The nucleation and growth processes of TCP at 950   C are pre- sented in  Fig. 1 . For alloy A without Ru, an area of original  c  matrix is selected as shown in  Fig. 1 a, and TCP cores are observed nucle- ating randomly in matrix after 15 minutes’ exposure in  Fig. 1 b. About 30 minutes later some TCP cores grow up into ﬂat rod-like shape while others decompose in matrix ( Fig. 1 c). After 45 min- utes’ growth as shown in  Fig. 1 d, the TCP precipitates become big- ger and its size is nearly 200 nm in length.  Fig. 1 e and f shows the in situ TCP precipitation process of B with 3 wt.% Ru at 950   C. Compared with that of alloy A, TCP phases are smaller and less at the same exposure time. Even after 45 minutes’ growth as shown in  Fig. 1 h, their average size is only about 100 nm long. In situ observation was also carried out at 1050   C as shown in  Fig. 2 . Sim- ilar to that happened at 950   C, the TCP phases in alloy A also expe- rienced a random nucleation ( Fig. 2 b), growth or decomposition ( Fig. 2 c), and further growth ( Fig. 2 d) periods. The TCP phases in alloy B is still less and smaller than those in alloy A. However, after the same exposure time the size of the TCP precipitate at 1050   C is bigger than that at 950   C. For example, the average length of pre- cipitate in  Fig. 2 d is about 300 nm for alloy A, while that in alloy B is about 150 nm long after 45 minutes’ isothermal exposure in Fig. 2 h.  

Since phases or materials containing heavy atoms have strong contrast in the TEM bright ﬁeld images assuming similar matrix thickness and orientation, TCP precipitates appear darker in the images of  Figs. 1 and 2 . Therefore, its area fraction at different exposure time can be measured using image-processing software, which is summarized in  Table 2 . The area fraction of TCP precipi- tates increases from 10.3% (15 min) to 21.2% (45 min) for alloy A exposed at 950   C, while that in alloy B only increases from 0.8% (15 min) to1.2% (45 min). At 1050   C, the precipitated area of TCP phase increased for both superalloys.  Figs. 1 and 2  and  Table 2  give direct evidences on the effect of Ru addition. Ru element plays a signiﬁcant role on suppressing TCP precipitation at elevated tem- perature, which decreases both the nucleation rate and growth rate of precipitates. For example, with Ru addition the number of pre- cipitates and their area fraction are much lower in alloy B than alloy A, indicating a suppressed nucleation rate. Moreover, the average size of TCP phases in alloy B is less than half of those in alloy A, showing a slower growth rate. According to  Table 2 , the suppression effect of Ru addition on the TCP precipitation is about one ﬁfteenth at 950   C and one sixth at 1050   C.  

The effect of exposure temperature can also be deduced com- paring  Figs. 1 and 2 , especially for alloy A. The TCP precipitates formed at 950   C are small in size but with a large quantity, while those formed at 1050   C are less but in a bigger size at the same exposure time. Although the number of precipitates is less at 1050   C, their area fraction is still bigger than that of 950   C as shown in  Table 2 , indicating an accelerated growth at higher tem- perature. These differences can be understood considering the atomic diffusion, whose rate is proportional to temperature. The diffusion ability of atoms is relatively slow at temperature of 950   C. Hence, long range diffusion is severely retarded, making it difﬁcult for refractory elements to diffuse to serve for TCP growth far away. Once nucleated, less TCP cores are decomposed and they can only grow into a small size due to the slow atomic diffusion and limited refractory elements in surrounding. At a higher tem- perature of 1050   C, more small precipitates are decomposed to support the growth of stable ones by diffusion. The decomposed precipitates should be metastable ones whose energy is high after nucleation concerning its size and morphology.

However, after homogeneous nucleation, preferential growth toward the primary  c / c 0   interface was observed especially at the late stage, which could be seen from  Fig. 2 d that all remained big TCP phases locate near the primary  c / c 0   interface. The detailed process was shown in  Fig. 3 a–d, in which the interface was marked with dot line. Five TCP precipitates nucleated homogeneously in  c matrix as indicated by arrowheads in  Fig. 3 a. In the following 30 minutes, precipitate 5 grew up toward  c / c 0   interface and annexed precipitate 1, while other three precipitates decomposed and contributed to the growth of the left precipitate. The left dominant TCP even grew into  c 0   phase after 30 minutes as shown in  Fig. 3 d. It is believed that when refractory elements in  c  matrix are becoming exhausted, traces of these elements remaining in  c 0 phase turn to be the dominant supply source. Thus the left TCP phase prefers growing towards primary  c / c 0   interface, noticing that the interface (or grain boundary) is also the fast diffusion path for elements. At the last stage when diffusion elements are completely exhausted and the growth of TCP phase turns to end, the morphology of the precipitates will change as shown in Fig. 3 e–h, which is the magniﬁed root part of the TCP precipitate framed by white dash line in  Fig. 3 d. When viewed along [001] c  zone axis of  c  matrix as shown in  Fig. 4 a, these  l precipitates have a typical ﬂat rod-like morphology and array along two orthogonal directions in  c  matrix. b and c presents the SAED patterns of  c  matrix and  l  phase at the same sample position, respectively. Twinning structure is observed on (001) l plane of  l  phase as shown in  Fig. 4 c, which results in the striation contrast in the BF image of  l  phase. # 4. Conclusions  

In situ investigations of TCP precipitation during isothermal exposure at 950 and 1050   C in Ni-base single crystal superalloys have been conducted by TEM. Dynamic nucleation and growth pro- cess were ﬁrst reported in both Ru-free and Ru-containing alloys. It is revealed that Ru addition can suppress TCP precipitation by decreasing both nucleation rate and growth rate, and reduce its area fraction down to 1/6–1/15 of the Ru-free alloy. Comparing the precipitation at 950 and 1050   C, low exposure temperature brings large quantity of TCP phases in small size by retarding long range diffusion and decomposition, while high temperature accel- erates the growth of TCP phase and the decomposition of unstable ones. No preferential nucleation close to or at the primary  c / c 0 interface was observed, but latterly the TCP phase grow toward this interface to obtain more refractory elements. The TCP precip- itate was identiﬁed as mainly ﬂat rod-like  l  phase, which is embedded in  c  matrix along [  c  and [  1  c  direction with crystallographic orientation relationship of [110] l  // [001] c  and (001) l  // (  110) c .
