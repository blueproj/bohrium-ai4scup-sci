ABSTRACT:  The design of bifunctional compounds is a promising approach toward the development of strong analgesics with reduced side effects. We here report the optimization of the previously published lead peptide  KGFF09 , which contains opioid receptor agonist and neuropeptide FF receptor antagonist pharmacophores and is shown to induce potent antinociception and reduced side effects. We evaluated the novel hybrid peptides for their  in vitro  activity at MOP, NPFFR1, and NPFFR2 and selected four of them ( DP08/14/32/50 ) for assessment of their acute antinociceptive activity in mice. We further selected  DP32 and  DP50  and observed that their antinociceptive activity is mostly peripherally mediated; they produced no respiratory depression, no hyperalgesia, significantly less tolerance, and strongly attenuated  

withdrawal syndrome, as compared to morphine and the recently FDA-approved TRV130. Overall, these data suggest that MOP agonist/NPFF receptor antagonist hybrids might represent an interesting strategy to develop novel analgesics with reduced side effects.  

# INTRODUCTION  

One of the major challenges in contemporary medicinal chemistry remains the development of safer analgesics for the treatment of moderate to severe pain. To date, the “gold standard” opioids are still the most prescribed analgesics to treat these pain states, but their long-term use leads to adverse side effects including tolerance, respiratory depression, constipation, and nausea as well as hyperalgesia, analgesic tolerance, and dependence. ,   These adverse effects can have a critical impact on the patient’s quality of life and led to the so- called “opioid crisis” in USA, with many deaths as a direct consequence.  

Since the beginning of the twentieth century, different strategies have been used to design novel opioid ligands targeting opioid receptors that display fewer side effects but with very limited success in terms of novel Food and Drug Administration (FDA)-approved drugs. Two recent strategies correspond to the development of G protein-biased  μ -opioid receptor (MOP) agonists and the evaluation of multitarget ligands, also called hybrids, designed multiple ligands (DMLs), or bifunctional ligands. − 6   The physiological antinociceptive responses and side effects of MOP are linked to two different signaling pathways: the G protein pathway and the  β -arrestin pathway.   The G protein signaling pathway is, after intra- cellular binding of the heterotrimeric G protein and dissociation in its corresponding G α  monomer and G β /G γ dimer, responsible for the modulation of second messengers and ion channels. − 9   The latter are eventually responsible for antinociception and analgesia. − 12   After G protein activation, MOP can be phosphorylated by GRKs at the C-terminus, leading to  β -arrestin recruitment, which induces internalization and desensitization of the receptor, as well as G protein- independent signaling events. ,   This process has been proposed to be critically involved in opioid adverse effects including antinociceptive tolerance, respiratory depression, nausea, constipation, and physical dependence. − 12   Currently, only one G protein-biased MOP agonist was approved by the FDA, namely, Oliceridine. Its use is however limited in terms of applicability (e.g., intravenous administration in hospitals  

and other clinical settings). In addition, Oliceridine still induces opioid side effects, like vomiting, hypoxia, dizziness, headache, pruritus, nausea, and constipation.   Although biased ligands could show many benefits, it remains a topic of debate whether or not the reduced side effect profile arises from the partial agonism of the ligand or from the actual bias itself. Recently, several reports have investigated this question, − 19   with a few of them describing the use of partial agonism as a conducive way to develop safer opioid analgesics. ,   In light of the above, the use of biased and/ or partial agonists could be favorable to achieve beneficial side effect profiles. The second strategy to obtain safer analgesics consists in the development of hybrid or bifunctional compounds, acting at MOP and also at other opioid or nonopioid receptors known to be involved in the regulation of opioid-induced side effects. , , , − 24   Among the nonopioid receptors, neuropeptide FF1 and FF2 receptors (NPFFR1 and NPFFR2) contribute to opioid-induced adverse effects, including antinociceptive tolerance. Indeed, pharmacological blockade of NPFFR1/2 has been shown to suppress long- lasting opioid-induced hyperalgesia (OIH) and associated antinociceptive tolerance. − 27  

For these reasons, this study aims to synthesize and biologically evaluate peptidomimetic and multitarget opioid- neuropeptide FF ligands merging efficacious MOP biased and/ or partial agonist pharmacophores with NPFFR1/2 antagonist pharmacophores. Despite these promising properties, MOP  β -arrestin recruit- ment by  KGFF09  was not fully suppressed and this compound also induced an impairment of motor coordination after its first administration. Hence, an extensive second-generation series based on the previously published lead peptide  KGFF09  was designed and synthesized in view of circumventing the abovementioned limitations. Pharmacokinetic properties (e.g.,  C max ,  T max ,  T 1/2 , and the brain/plasma (B/ P)) were determined for these four lead peptides, followed by in vivo  characterization. Here, we report the development route toward two compounds,  DP32  and  DP50 , which exhibit less hyperalgesia and lower global withdrawal syndrome compared to morphine and  KGFF09 . Most importantly,  DP32  and DP50  induce no respiratory depression even at the highest investigated doses, contrasting the results of both morphine and TRV130 (i.e., Oliceridine).  

# RESULTS AND DISCUSSION  

Design and Synthesis.  Starting from the sequence of KGFF09 , we carried out a series of substitutions ( Table 1 , substitutions are indicated in bold) to optimize its pharmaco- logical profile. In position 1, the side chain of Dmt remained mostly unaltered as the dimethyl phenol moiety proved to be an already optimized motif leading to extremely high affinities and activities in a wide array of opioid peptide sequences. − 30 Therefore, only a few changes were performed at that position. The same applies to the Bpa residue in position 5. Previous studies, scrutinizing this position, resulted in the Bpa- containing  KGFF09  lead peptide. Consequently, only two changes were made at that position throughout the new series. All other positions were more extensively investigated. In position 2, different “charged” amino acids, i.e.,  D -Bpa,  D -Orn, D -Lys, Nlys,  D -hArg, and  D -Lys(Nic), were incorporated, as well as  N -alkylated amino acids (i.e.,  D -Pro, NMe- D -Ala). In view of enhanced proteolytic stability, the second residue mostly possessed a  D -chirality. Additionally, the constrained dipeptide Aba- β Ala in positions 3 and 4 was substituted by several other constrained dipeptides (including residues influencing the distance between the two pharmacophores). Finally, in the sixth position, Phe was exchanged with Val, Ile, Leu, Trp, Tyr, D -Phe, D -Val, and Phe- β Ala, as other hydrophobic residues.  

All hybrids were synthesized by Fmoc/ t Bu Solid-Phase Peptide Synthesis (SPPS) methodology using HBTU/DIPEA, DIC/Oxyma Pure, or DIC/HOBt as coupling mixtures and utilizing Rink Amide AM resin as solid support. Of note, during the synthesis of analogues containing Bpa, we observed many deletions and double couplings, due to the basic piperidine moiety in the Bpa side chain. This undesired Fmoc deprotection was prevented by washing the resin after Fmoc deprotection with 3 equiv of HOBt or Oxyma to protonate the Bpa side chain. All analogues were cleaved from the resin with a TFA/TIS/H 2 O 95:2.5:2.5 cleavage mixture and purified using preparative HPLC, yielding the desired hybrid compounds in excellent purity (>95%) and yields ranging from 4 to 48%. Pharmacokinetic (PK) Parameters.  Next, we determined several pharmacokinetic parameters of  KGFF09  and the four lead peptides after subcutaneous administration of a single dose of 10 mg/kg of each compound ( Table 8 ).  KGFF09 showed the highest  C max  value and was comparable to  DP32 , which could be explained by the structural similarity of the two compounds, only differing by the  N -terminal guanidyl group. DP50  displayed a slightly lower  C max , even though this compound is characterized by significantly higher lipophilicity. In contrast, both  DP08  and  DP14  displayed a much lower C max . The same trend was observed for the concentration in plasma 2 h after the administration of each compound. Even though  DP14  and  DP50  are structurally very similar (Gly vs β Ala in position 4), their  C max  and plasma concentration proved significantly different, with  DP50  clearly outperforming DP14 . The highest concentrations were found for  KGFF09 , DP32 , and  DP50 , making them the most plasma-stable compounds among this selection.  DP08  was the most quickly degraded with a half-life of 0.28  ±  0.06 h, while  KGFF09 , DP14 ,  DP32 , and  DP50  displayed comparable half-lives ranging from 3.08 to 3.88 h. Noteworthily, we observed that DP08  was rapidly metabolized in H-Dmt- D -Pro-OH and H- Aba- β Ala-Bpa-Phe-NH 2 , as determined by LC-MS (see Experimental Section  and  Figure S1 ). Importantly, none of the peptides were detected in the brain 2 h after their administration (lower limits of detection ranged from 11 to 55 ng/g depending on the compound). The latter PK parameter indicated that the studied compounds could serve as lead structures toward peripherally active analgesics.  

Acute Antinociceptive Activity.  We further evaluated acute antinociception in mice of the four novel lead peptides DP08 ,  DP14 ,  DP32 , and  DP50  as well as morphine and KGFF09  as reference ligands. The thermal nociceptive threshold of each animal was assessed using the tail immersion test at 47.5  ° C after treatment with three doses of each compound (2.5, 5, and 10 mg/kg) over a period of 6 h (see Figure S2 ). Of note,  DP50  retained an antinociceptive response up to 24 h. Area under the curve (AUC) values were calculated from different dose − response curves and the results are summarized in  Figure 2 A. All four tested compounds displayed significant antinociceptive activity at the investigated doses, as compared to baseline values.  DP14 and  DP50  displayed a similar effect as the reference compounds, but  DP50  reached the maximum antinociceptive effect at a lower dose (5 mg/kg).  DP08  and  DP32  displayed a lower maximal antinociceptive effect than  DP14  and  DP50 , but, advantageously,  DP32  reached its maximum effect at 2.5 mg/kg. Based on their  in vitro  profile, the above PK parameters, and acute antinociceptive effect, we selected DP50  and  DP32  for further evaluation. As our PK study suggested that the acute antinociceptive activity of these lead compounds is mainly peripherally mediated, we first evaluated whether the nonbrain penetrant opioid receptor antagonist naloxone methiodide can antagonize their antinociceptive action as compared to morphine, which induces both peripheral and central acute nociception. As expected, the antinociceptive action of morphine (10 mg/kg, s.c.) was only partially reversed, while the antinociceptive effects of  DP32 and  DP50  at a dose to which they induce their maximal effect (4.2  μ mol/kg, sc) were completely blocked by naloxone methiodide (5 mg/kg, s.c.,  Figure 2 B; see  Figure S3  for full kinetic), thus confirming that their antinociceptive activity is mostly peripherally mediated.

Motor Coordination.  As we observed in our previous study that  KGFF09  induced a significant impairment of motor coordination in mice upon its first administration,   we further evaluated the consequences of a single similar dose of  DP32 and  DP50  in the same experimental paradigm (see Experimental Section ). As shown in  Figure 3 ,  DP32  (12 mg/kg, s.c.) and  DP50  (10.5 mg/kg, s.c.) displayed no significant effect on motor coordination of the animals in the rotarod assay as compared with saline-treated mice, while, as expected, amitriptyline (20 mg/kg, s.c.) induced a strong impairment of motor coordination in this assay.  

Respiratory Depression.  A major opioid adverse side effect that leads to many deaths among chronic opioid users is respiratory depression.   To address this issue, we evaluated in mice the consequences of acute administration of  DP32  and DP50  on respiratory frequency by using whole-body plethysmography and compared them to morphine and TRV130. The latter was included because it is the only FDA-approved G protein-biased ligand at MOP ( vide supra ). As shown in  Figure 4 A,B, morphine and TRV130 both induced a dose-dependent decrease of the pulmonary rate of the animals, indicating that these compounds induced significant respiratory depression ( Figure 4 E). Conversely, DP32  and  DP50  ( Figure 4 C − E) induced no significant decrease in the pulmonary rate as compared with control animals even at the highest dose tested, corresponding to three times the dose that was used in chronic administration experiments. The importance of  β -arrestin-2 in the develop- ment of side effects associated with opioids has been questioned by recent reports. Both  DP32  and  DP50  display only little  β -arrestin-2 recruitment but are also partial agonists at the G protein pathway. This could explain the lack of respiratory depression, in addition to less withdrawal syndrome scores, since recent reports described the beneficial effects of partial agonism versus biased agonism for opioid analge- sics. ,   Moreover, the fact that both  DP32  and  DP50  display low brain penetrance could also explain their reduced side effects. However, it is noteworthy that  DP50  displays a lower withdrawal syndrome as compared to  DP32 , suggesting that peripheral activity is not the only criterion that explains this better activity profile.  

# CONCLUSIONS  

In conclusion, this study reports the design and synthesis of novel hybrid peptides, as well as their  in vitro / in vivo pharmacology and PK parameters. Using the previously described hybrid  KGFF09  as a starting point, we have identified G protein-biased hybrids at MOP. Based on good activity at MOP in the cAMP assay (EC 50  < 10 nM,  E max  > 50%) with little to no  β -arrestin-2 recruitment ( E max  < 25%), in addition to good affinity and antagonist activity on NPFFR1 and NPFFR2, four peptides,  DP08/14/32/50 , were selected for further  in vivo  evaluation. Interestingly, these hybrids all contain the unchanged Bpa-Phe-NH 2  C -terminus moiety. PK parameters displayed the highest  C max  value for the lead peptide  KGFF09 , but comparable to  DP32 , in contrast to DP08  and  DP14 , showing significantly lower values. None of the peptides were detected in the brain, but  KGFF09 ,  DP32 , and  DP50  were demonstrated to be the most plasma-stable. Additionally, the four peptides showed prolonged antinoci- ceptive effects after subcutaneous administration in mice upon acute evaluation. At this point, the  in vivo  evaluation was performed on the lead peptides  DP32  and  DP50 . Upon chronic daily administration, we investigated the development of antinociceptive tolerance and hyperalgesia. The antinoci- ceptive effect of the two compounds was significantly reduced on day 8 as compared to day 1, but importantly the development of tolerance was significantly lower than morphine and TRV130. Moreover, in contrast to morphine and TRV130, upon daily administrations of  KGFF09 ,  DP32 , and  DP50 , no hyperalgesia was observed. Additionally, we determined the development of naltrexone-precipitated with- drawal syndrome. Morphine and TRV130 showed high scores in both vegetative and somatic signs.  DP32  displayed a high score in jumping comparable to morphine and  KGFF09 , while a significantly lower score was observed for wet dog shake, and no significant difference to the vehicle group was obtained for paw tremors and diarrhea. Upon chronic administration of DP50 , no jumping behavior or paw tremors were observed, but the diarrhea score was similar to that of morphine. When combining the different parameters,  DP32  and  DP50  display a global withdrawal score that is significantly lower than morphine and  KGFF09 , with  DP50  displaying no significant difference with vehicle-treated animals. Furthermore,  KGFF09 induced transitory lethargic state with an impaired motor coordination, while both  DP32  and  DP50  showed no significant effect on motor coordination. Finally, we explored a major opioid-induced side effect: respiratory depression. Even at their highest tested dose, both  DP32  and  DP50  did not induce respiratory depression, conversely to morphine and TRV130. In summary, we here report novel hybrid peptides from which  DP32  and  DP50  display potent antinociception and reduced adverse side effects as compared to pure opioid agonists morphine and TRV130 as well as to the previously described hybrid  KGFF09 .   These findings suggest that opioid-neuropeptide FF hybrids might represent an interesting strategy to develop potent analgesics with limited side effects upon acute and chronic administrations.

Peptide Synthesis.  All hybrids were synthesized manually by the standard Fmoc/ t Bu SPPS methodology using a plastic syringe. Removal of the Fmoc-protecting groups was achieved by 4- methyl piper i dine in DMF (20:80 v/v) for 5 and then 15 min. All amino acids (3 equiv) coupled before Bpa was done using HBTU/ DIPEA (3:5 equiv) for 45 min at room temperature. All standard amino acids (3 equiv) coupled after Bpa coupling were treated with DIC (3 equiv) and HOBt or Oxyma Pure (3 equiv) for 45 min at room temperature, while all unnatural amino acids (Bpa, constrained dipeptides,  N -methylated amino acids, THIQ, Dmt, Tic(7-OH),  D - Orn,  D -Arg,  D -Lys(Nic)) were coupled using 1.5 or 2 equiv for 1 h to overnight. After Fmoc deprotection of Bpa-containing peptides, a wash with HOBt or Oxyma Pure (3 equiv) was performed ( vide supra ). All steps were confirmed by Kaiser and Chloranil tests and/or small-scale cleavages were analyzed using LC-MS. After every deprotection and coupling step, the resin was washed with DMF and DCM (both 3 times). Final cleavage (together with side chain deprotection) of the peptides was performed using a TFA/TIS/H 2 O (95:2.5:2.5 v/v/v) solution for 4 h at room temperature. After TFA removal, the peptides were precipitated in cold diethyl ether, decantated, and lyophilized. The final peptides were purified via preparative RP-HPLC using a ReproSil-Pur 120 ODS-3 150 mm  ×  16 mm column (10 − 100% gradient in 15 − 30 min with MeCN and H 2 O, both containing 0.1% TFA, as the eluent). After lyophilization, the pure peptides were obtained as a white powder with a purity of >95% for  in vitro  and >97% for  in vivo  studies.  

In Vitro  Pharmacology.  cAMP Accumulation.  Cyclic adenosine monophosphate responses were measured by monitoring the activity of the GloSensor luciferase. HEK293 cells stably expressing the GloSensor-20F (gift from Julien Hanson, GIGA Institute, University of Lie ge, Belgium) with additional stable expression of human MOP, DOP, KOP, NOP, NPFFR1, or NPFFR2 were loaded with 1 mM  D - luciferin (Synchem UG and Co., KG, Felsberg, Germany) for 2 h at 25  ° C in physiological Hepes buffer (10 mM HEPES, 0.4 mM NaH 2 PO 4 , 137.5 mM NaCl, 1.25 mM MgCl 2 , 1.25 mM CaCl 2 , 6 mM KCl, 5.6 mM glucose, and 1 mg/mL bovine serum albumin, pH 7.4) in white 96-well plates (100 000 cells per well). Kinetic recordings of luminescence were acquired using a FlexStationIII (Molecular Devices, San Jose, California) reader at 25  ° C until signals reached a plateau. For antagonist activity measurements, antagonists were added to the cells 15 min before the agonist. Forskolin concentration used to induce cAMP production was 0.125  μ M for MOP- and DOP-, 1.5  μ M for KOP- and NOP-, 0.4  μ M for NPFFR1-, and 0.3  μ M for NPFFR2-expressing cells. Assays were performed in the presence of 0.5 mM IBMX (except DOP for which we used 0.1 mM) to prevent the degradation of cAMP.  

Calcium Assay.  The activities of  DP08 ,  DP14 ,  DP32 , and  DP50 on GPR10, GPR54, and GPR103 were evaluated in an assay of intracellular calcium release in CHO cells stably expressing hGPR10, hGPR54, or hGPR103 receptors as previously described.  

β -Arrestin-2 Recruitment Assay.  The  β -arrestin-2 recruitment assay was performed as reported.   Briefly, HEK293 cells with stable expression of eYFP-tagged  β -arrestin-2 and transient expression of Rluc8-tagged MOP were incubated in white 96-well plates at 37  ° C in physiological Hepes buffer supplemented with 5  μ M coelenterazine h (Fisher Scientific SAS, Illkirch, France). Signals were acquired with a VictorLight luminometer (PerkinElmer France SAS, Villebon sur Yvette, France) 5 min after stimulation with various concentrations of the compound. A BRET ratio corresponding to the signal in the acceptor channel divided by the signal in the donor channel was calculated. A net BRET ratio was then determined (BRET ratio of drug-activated cells minus BRET ratio of buffer-treated cells) and normalized to the effect of DAMGO.  

Radioligand Binding Competition Assay.  Radioligand binding experiments were performed as described.   Membranes from CHO cells stably expressing hNPFFR1 or hNPFFR2 were incubated for 1 h at 25  ° C with 50 mM Hepes (pH 7.4), 1 mM MgCl 2 , 1 mM CaCl 2 ,

 0.1% bovine serum albumin, 0.01 nM of [ 125 I]-1DMeNPFF

 (Hartmann Analytic GmbH, Braunschweig, Germany), and increasing concentrations of competitor. Radioactivity was quantified using a TopCount counter (PerkinElmer) after rapid filtration on GF/B UniFilters plates (PerkinElmer) presoaked with 0.5% polyethyleni- mine.  

Determination of  DP08  Metabolites.  Human plasma was obtained from the Belgian Red Cross (Vlaams-Brabant, Leuven) and stored at  − 20  ° C. A frozen human plasma sample was thawed and thermostated to 37  ° C in an incubator for at least 30 min. The lyophilized peptide was dissolved in Milli-Q water and subsequently spiked in human plasma (10:90 v/v peptide solution/plasma). 100  μ L of spiked plasma was taken at different time points (30, 60, 120, and 270 min) and 300  μ L of cold (4  ° C) precipitation solvent (methanol containing 0.1% TFA v/v) was added, allowing a protein crash. The resulting suspensions were vortexed for 15 s and placed at 4  ° C for 30 min. After centrifugation for 15 min, 100  μ L of the supernatant was diluted with 100  μ L of water. Metabolites were identified through LC- MS analysis.  

In Vivo  Pharmacology.  Animals.  All  in vivo  experiments were performed on C57BL/6N male mice (25 − 30 g weight; Janvier Laboratories, Le Genest − Saint-Isle, France). Animals were housed in groups of three − five per cage and kept under a 12/12 h light/dark cycle at 21  ±  1  ° C with ad libitum access to food and water. Experiments were performed during the light-on phase of the cycle (10:00 am to 4:00 pm). Mice were habituated to the testing room and equipment and handled for 2 weeks before starting behavioral experiments. All experiments were carried out in strict accordance with the European guidelines for the care of laboratory animals (European Communities Council Directive 2010/63/EU), approved by the local ethical committee and authorized by the French Ministry for Research (authorization number  # 18342). All efforts were made to minimize animal discomfort and to reduce the number of animals used.  

Drug Administration.  All drugs were dissolved in physiological saline (0.9%), except  DP50  for which Kolliphor EL 10% was added, and administered subcutaneously (10 mL/kg mice body weight).  

Pharmacokinetic Study.  The pharmacokinetic parameters in mice ( C max ,  T max ,  T 1/2 , plasma, and brain concentrations 2 h after compound administration) were determined on three animals for each compound by WuXi AppTec (Shanghai, China) by LC-MS/MS (see  Supporting Experimental  procedure and PK graphs for details). The lower limits of quantification of this assay for  KGFF09 ,  DP08 , DP14 ,  DP32 , and  DP50  were 5, 2, 1, 5, and 1 ng/mL of plasma

 (respectively) and 55, 22, 11, 33, and 11 ng/g of brain homogenate

 (respectively).  C max ,  T max , and  T 1/2  were determined by the concentration over time curve where time points taken were at 5, 15, 30 min, 1, 2, 4, 8, and 24 h (8 in total).  

Assessment of Thermal Nociception.  The nociceptive threshold was assessed using the tail immersion test as previously described. Briefly, mice were restrained in a grid pocket and two-thirds of their tail was immersed in a thermostatic water bath heated at 47.5  ±  0.5 ° C. The latency (in sec) for tail withdrawal from hot water was taken as a measure of the nociceptive response. In the absence of any nociceptive reaction, a cutoff value of 25 s was set to prevent tissue damage.  

Acute Antinociceptive Effect of the Tested Compounds.  The antinociceptive effect of the tested compounds was determined in the tail immersion test at three different doses, 2.5, 5, and 10 mg/kg (s.c.) or at a single dose of 4.2  μ mol/kg (s.c.) in the absence or presence of 5 mg/kg, s.c. naloxone methiodide (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, USA). Tail withdrawal latency of each animal was measured before treatment (time point 0) and after drug administration at the following time points: 15, 30, 60, 120, 180, 240, 300, and 360 min.  

Development of Hyperalgesia and Antinociceptive Tolerance. Experiments were designed according to a protocol enabling the visualization of an early antinociceptive effect of each compound at d1, a delayed hyperalgesic effect (d2 − d7), and a tolerance state to antinociceptive action on the last day (d8). On day 1, acute antinociception was assessed by measuring the nociceptive response latencies of the animals after administration of different compounds (s.c.), followed by once-daily administration until day 8, where acute antinociception was measured again. For the assessment of hyper- algesia, basal nociceptive latencies of the animals were measured every day, 1 h before the daily administration of each compound. AUC was calculated according to the trapezoid method. At each time point, the baseline value was subtracted from nociceptive latency. The peak of antinociception was defined as the mean of the nociceptive latencies at 30, 60, 120, and 180 min after treatment. The percentage of tolerance was calculated as (1  − peak antinociception at day 8/peak antinociception at day 1)  ×  100.  

Naltrexone-Precipitated Withdrawal Syndrome.  The opioid physical dependence was tested as reported 2   with minor experimental modifications in C57BL/6N mice by daily subcutaneous admin- istration of test compounds for 8 days before the testing day. On day 9, 2 h after the last drug injection, the withdrawal syndrome was precipitated by administration of naltrexone (5 mg/kg, s.c.) and evaluated over 30 min. Jumping, paw tremors, and wet dog shakes were recorded as a number of events occurring during the total test time. Diarrhea was checked for 30 min with 1 point given for any signs of it every 5 min (maximum score: 6). For each mouse, a global opiate withdrawal score was also calculated by summing the values obtained for each sign. For this purpose, one point was assigned to every 3 jumps and 3 paw tremors, respectively, whereas all other signs were given the absolute values recorded during the test.  

Motor Coordination.  Possible motor dysfunction or sedative effects of test compounds were assessed in C57BL/6 mice using the rotarod test as described previously.  

Respiratory Depression.  Respiratory depression was measured in C57BL/6N mice recording ventilatory parameters using whole-body barometric plethysmography (Emka Technologies, Paris, France). The mice were allowed to acclimatize in a plethysmograph room until a stable baseline was observed. Upon gentle removal of the mice from the chamber, they were s.c. injected with the compound at  T 0  and put back into the chamber until the end of the measurements. The pulmonary rate was recorded for 134 min and used as a measure of respiratory depression. AUC was calculated according to the trapezoid method.  

Data Analysis.  Data were analyzed and graphs were created using GraphPad Prism software (San Diego, California) and represented as means  ±  SEM. The potency EC 50  (nM), efficacy  E max  (%), and affinity  K i  (nM) were determined from their dose − response curves by nonlinear regression analysis. In both the cAMP and  β -arrestin-2 assays, the efficacy was determined relative to DAMGO as a reference ligand. Antinociception was quantified as the AUC calculated by the trapezoidal method.  In vivo  data were analyzed using 1-way, 2-way, or multifactor analysis of variance. The treatments were taken as between-group factors and when needed, the time of measurement was taken as a within-subject factor. Post hoc analyses were performed with Fisher, Tukey, Duncan, or Dunnett’s tests. The level of significance was set at  P  < 0.05. All statistical analyses were performed using StatView, Statistica 12 (Statsoft), or GraphPad Prism software.
