# Synergistic Effect of Fluorination on Molecular Energy Level  Modulation in Highly Efﬁ  cient Photovoltaic Polymers  

Maojie   Zhang  ,   Xia   Guo  ,   Shaoqing   Zhang  , and   Jianhui   Hou  \* Modulation of molecular energy levels is one of the most impor- tant topics in the molecular design of organic semiconducting  materials, especially for active layer materials in polymer solar  cells (PSCs). As is well known, the photovoltaic properties of  PSCs are closely dependent on the molecular energy levels (i.e.,  the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and the lowest  unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO)), of their active layer  materials.  1–9      For example, open-circuit voltages (  V   oc ) of bulk  heterojunction  10      (BHJ) PSCs are roughly directly proportional  to the gaps between the HOMO levels of the electron donors  and the LUMO levels of the electron acceptors in their active  layers,  11      and therefore,   V oc    of BHJ PSCs can be effectively  improved by elevating the LUMO levels of the acceptors or/and  decreasing the HOMO levels of the donors.  12–48      In the past  few years, many successful examples that improve   V oc    of PSCs  by decreasing HOMO levels of conjugated polymers have been  reported.  20–48  

In general, from the point of view of molecular structure  design, two methods have been mainly used to modulate  molecular energy levels of conjugated polymers. First, conju- gated polymers with different backbone structures show varied  molecular energy levels. For example, the homopolymer of a  3-alkylthiophene, like poly(3-hexylthiophene) (P3HT), shows  a HOMO level of ca. −4.9 eV,  49      while the homopolymer of a  phenylene vinylene, like MEH-PPV, shows a HOMO level at  −5.4 eV.  50      Second, side groups on conjugated polymers also  play an important role in molecular energy level control.  51     For example, the homopolymer of a 3-alkoxyl-thiophene, like  P3DOT, exhibits a HOMO level of −4.47 eV,  51a      which is much  higher than that of P3HT. These two methods have also been  successfully applied in molecular design of the D–A poly- mers,  3–5      alternating polymers of electron-rich building blocks  (D-units) and electron-deﬁ  cient building blocks (A-units). For  instance, by selecting different electron-deﬁ  cient units to copo- lymerize with an electron-rich unit, rational control of the  molecular energy levels can be realized, and as a result, when  these polymers are used as donor materials in PSCs, the   V oc    of  the devices can be varied in a broad range.  52–56      Besides manip- ulating the backbone structures, ﬁ  ne tuning of the molecular  energy levels can be realized by changing the substituted side  groups of the D–A polymers. Therefore, ﬂ  uorine atoms (F  atoms) were introduced onto the A-units of D–A polymers to  tune their molecular energy levels. By this method, both the  HOMO and the LUMO levels of the D–A polymers can be  reduced simultaneously with no signiﬁ  cant impact on their  other properties, like mobility, solubility, etc., and as a result,  a higher   V oc    can be obtained.  37–48      According to these studies,  it has been proven that ﬂ  uorination is an effective method of  tuning the molecular energy levels of conjugated polymers and  thus improving their photovoltaic properties.  

Interestingly, it seems that the introduction of F atoms  onto different positions may result in a distinct inﬂ  uence on  the photovoltaic performance. For example, for D–A polymers  of benzo[1,2-b:4,5-b ′ ]dithiophene (BDT) and thieno[3,4-b]thio- phene (TT), when F atoms were attached to their TT units, the  photovoltaic performance of the polymers could be improved  signiﬁ  cantly,       however, when F atoms were attached to their  BDT units, the target polymers showed poor photovoltaic per- formance.  57      According to the reported results, it can be con- cluded that, although great success has been made in ﬂ  uorine- substituted D–A polymers, how to extend the application of the  ﬂ  uorination in D–A polymers is still of great interest and also  of signiﬁ  cant importance to molecular design of highly efﬁ  cient  photovoltaic polymers.  

In this work, a polymer backbone with a D–A structure  based on 4,8-bis(thiophene-2-yl)-benzo[1,2-b:4,5-b ′ ]dithiophene  (BDT-T) and TT with thiophenes as a   π  bridge, which is ben- eﬁ  cial for improving the FF of the corresponding device,  58     named as PBT, was selected as the model structure, and ﬂ  uo- rine atoms were added onto the D-units or/and A-units of the  backbone. Accordingly, a series of D–A polymers,   PBT-0F ,   PBT- 1F ,   PBT-2F  and   PBT-3F  (as shown in   Figure    1 ) were designed  and synthesized. Compared with the non-ﬂ  uorinated polymer,  PBT-0F , all of these ﬂ  uorinated polymers show lower LUMO  and HOMO levels, and hence the PSC devices based on these  polymers show higher   V oc    values. Monoﬂ  uorination on the TT  units of the PBT polymers causes   V oc    to improve by 0.04 V,  while diﬂ  uorination on the BDT-T units causes   V   oc  to increase  by 0.18 V. Interestingly, the   V oc    of the PSC devices based on  the triﬂ  uorinated polymer,   PBT-3F , is 0.22 V higher than that of  the devices based on   PBT-0F , indicating that the ﬂ  uorination on  TT and BDT-T units has perfect synergistic effect on reducing  the molecular energy level of the polymer. These results reveal  that the introduction of F atoms onto both D-units and A-units  of D–A polymers has a synergistic effect on molecular energy  level control of D–A polymer.

Quantum chemistry calculation by the density functional  theory (DFT) method was performed to evaluate the inﬂ  uence  of ﬂ  uorine atoms on the electronic properties of the polymers.  The frontier molecular orbitals and the corresponding HOMO  and LUMO levels are shown in Figure S4 in the Supporting  Information and   Table   1  . As observed, the HOMO surfaces  of the polymers are delocalized along the whole   π -conjugated  backbones while their LUMO surfaces are mainly localized on  the TT-based acceptor segments. Moreover, it is shown that the  trend of variation for the molecular energy levels is consistent  with the results obtained from the CV measurements.
