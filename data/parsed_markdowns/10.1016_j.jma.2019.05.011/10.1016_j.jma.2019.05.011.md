# Abstract  

Research   work   presented   in   this   study   has   the   primary   target   of   exploring   joint   attributes   of   AZ31   magnesium   alloys   using   friction   stir welding   process   with   a   modiﬁed   tool   referred   as   bobbin   tool.   Effects   of   inert   and   open   atmosphere   on   mechanical   properties   are   evaluated over   a   wide   range   of   welding   speed   and   tool   rotation   speed.   Comparison   of   the   research   ﬁndings   from   the   inert   atmosphere   bobbin   tool were   made   with   the   traditional   process   of   friction   stir   welding.   The   results   depicted   improved   joint   properties   for   inert   atmosphere   welding. Low   and   intermediate   range   of   tool   rotational   speed   is   found   to   be   favorable   for   bobbin   tool   friction   stir   welding   without   and   with   an   inert medium,   respectively.   Controlled   atmosphere   due   to   inert   medium   leads   to   less   oxidation   of   the   AZ31   Mg   alloy   leading   to   superior   joint properties.   Microstructural   investigations   are   also   made   with   the   aim   of   evaluating   the   impact   of   bobbin   tool   and   inert   medium   on   joint properties.   In   each   aspect   for   joining   of   AZ31   Mg   alloy,   bobbin   tool   with   inert   medium   is   found   to   be   an   effective   solution   for   joining   with improved   mechanical   properties   compared   to   without   inert   bobbin   tool   as   well   as   conventional   tool   friction   stir   welding.   © 2019   Published   by   Elsevier   B.V.   on   behalf   of   Chongqing   University.

# 1.   Introduction  

Solid   state   joining   processes   offer   a   better   solution   for welding   aluminium   (Al)   and   magnesium   (Mg)   alloys   instead of   fusion   welding   process   due   to   severe   oxidation   during   fu- sion.   In   non-fusion   based   welding   like   friction   stir   welding (FSW)   process,   material   coalescence   occurs   at   temperatures usually   0.5–0.7   times   the   melting   temperature   of   base   material (BM).   FSW   process   avoids   formation   of   porosity,   distortion, hot cracking and other fusion related defects. The heat af-fected   zone   (HAZ)   in   FSW   process   is   less   compared   to   that of   fusion   welding   process   and   it   helps   in   existence   of   the   orig- inal   base   material   properties.   In   FSW   the   joint   is   produced by   plastic   deformation   caused   by   frictional   heating   between the   contact   surfaces   of   a   rotating   tool   and   the   workpiece.   The tool   traverse   line   in   the   weld   area   helps   in   material   soften- ing   to   get   deposited   at   the   rear   side   of   the   pin   and   forming the   weld   joint   due   to   the   pin   stirring   action.   A   detailed   dis- cussion   on   FSW   process   is   available   in   relevant   [1]   technical articles.  

In   conventional   FSW   (C-FSW)   process   the   weld   root   is susceptible   to   root   defect   for   insufﬁcient   length   of   the   tool pin   compared   to   the   plate   thickness.   In   general,   in   C-FSW process   the   tool   pin   length   is   kept   less   than   the   plate   thick- ness   to   avoid   sticking   of   the   material   and   rubbing   of   the   tool pin   on   the   backing   plate.   The   rubbing   of   the   tool   pin   with the   backing   plate   may   create   a   grove   on   the   backing   plate and   the   back   surface   of   the   weld   emerges   uneven   and   non- uniform.   It   is   also   observed   that   in   C-FSW   process,   the   back- ing   plate   is   essential.   However,   the   backing   plate   dissipates heat   rapidly   leading   to   weld   cracking.   Moreover,   use   of   back- ing   plate   limits   the   ﬂexibility   of   the   C-FSW   process   for   com- plex   and   hollow   shapes   joining.   To   eliminate   the   above   cited problems, modiﬁed tool is developed. Usually, the C-FSWtool   has   one   shoulder   as   per   the   regular   design   features   of the   tool   [2 , 3] .   The   modiﬁed   tool   contains   two   shoulders,   both sides   of   the   pin   end,   referred   as   bobbin   tool.   The   presence of   two   shoulders   ensures   uniform   frictional   heat   generation across   the   weld   thickness.   In   bobbin   tool   FSW   (BT-FSW) process   the   tool   pin   length   is   exactly   the   same   or   little   less than   the   plate   thickness   without   any   use   of   backing   plate   and the   tool   completely   penetrates   into   the   workpiece   thickness. The   presence   of   the   lower   (bottom)   shoulder   eliminates   the need   for   any   additional   backing   plate   support.   Thus,   this   pro- cess   is   also   known   as   self-support   BT-FSW   process.   BT-FSW process   eliminates   root   defect   and   accommodate   small   vari- ations   in   the   thickness   of   the   plates,   which   is   not   possible in   case   of   C-FSW   process.   No   additional   plunge   force   is   re- quired   since   the   loads   are   carried   within   the   pin   and   thus known   as   self-reacting   BT-FSW   process.  

The   features   of   the   FSW   tool   is   essential   to   regulate   ﬂow of   material   around   the   tool   pin   that   in   turn   control   the   weld quality.   In   C-FSW   process,   researchers   tested   various   tool   pro- ﬁles   to   control   the   material   ﬂow   and   improve   the   weld   quality. However,   exploration   on   BT-FSW,   speciﬁcally   on   Mg   alloy, is   limited.   The   inﬂuence   of   tool   rotation   and   welding   speed of   BT-FSW   for   joining   AZ31   Mg   alloy   was   studied   by   Li et   al.   [4] .   They   found   that   tensile   properties   improved   with a   rise   in   welding   speed.   Zhou   et   al.   [5]   explored   the   conse- quence   of   rotational   speed   on   weld   qualities   in   BT-FSW   of AZ61   Mg   alloy   and   detected   that   impact   energy   reduces   at higher   rotational   speed.   Sued   et   al.   [6]   considered   the   signif- icance   of   various   bobbin   tool   features   for   AA6082   Al   alloy and   detected   best   joint   with   four   ﬂats   threaded   pin.   Zhang et   al.   [7]   and   Wang   et   al.   [8]   examined   both   metallurgical and   mechanical   attributes   of   2A14-T6   and   AA2198   Al   alloy, respectively,   in   BT-FSW   and   found   joint   efﬁciency   of   75% and   80%,   respectively,   compared   to   the   BM.   Esmaily   et   al. [9]   compared   between   C-FSW   and   BT-FSW   to   thick   AA6005 Al   alloy   and   witnessed   better   properties   in   BT-FSW   speci- mens.   Goebel   et   al.   [10]   explored   on   semi   stationary   shoul- der   BT-FSW   of   AA2198   Al   alloy   and   perceived   performance of   82%   of   BM.   Xu   et   al.   [ 11 , 12 ]   compared   BT-FSW   with C-FSW   process   for   AA7085   Al   alloy   and   noticed   joint   efﬁ- ciency   of   82%   [11]   and   microstructural   homogenization   [12] . Thomas   et   al.   [13]   also   compared   C-FSW   with   BT-FSW   on steel   and   observed   the   effect   of   refractory   material   tool   on   the weld   performance.   Dong   et   al.   [14]   considered   the   microstruc- ture   and   mechanical   attributes   of   FSW   with   bobbin   tool   for AA2024   Al   and   observed   reduced   grain   size   with   increasing welding   speed.   Beside   mechanical   and   microstructural   anal- ysis,   modeling   and   optimization   of   BT-FSW   process   is   also carried   out.   Hilgert   et   al.   [15]   explored   on   shear   layer   mod- eling   for   BT-FSW   of   AA2014   Al   alloy   and   found   that   the predicted   torque   and   shear   layer   shapes   are   well   correlated with   the   experimental   results.   Chen   et   al.   [16]   modeled   the temperature   proﬁle   of   BT-FSW   process   and   found   uniform joint   qualities   throughout   the   weld.   Zhao   et   al.   [17]   devel- oped   empirical   relationship   between   the   welding   factor   and tensile   properties   of   BT-FSW   of   AA2019   Al   alloy.  

The   current   work   derives   inspiration   from   the   published   re- search   work   on   BT-FSW   of   Mg   alloy.   Although   the   research on   BT-FSW   has   increased   in   the   recent   years,   but   it   is   re- stricted   to   aluminium   alloys   in   most   of   the   cases.   There   are limited   research   articles   available   on   BT-FSW   of   Mg   alloy as   referred   in   the   aforementioned   paragraph.   Thus,   scope   for research   in   BT-FSW   process   to   Mg   alloy   is   lucrative.   The   pro- cess   of   BT-FSW   explored   in   this   investigation   can   be   consid- ered   as   pioneering   attempt   in   inert   gas   medium   i.e.,   inert   gas BT-FSW   (I-BT-FSW)   process.   I-BT-FSW   may   enhance   the mechanical   and   metallurgical   properties   of   the   weld   in   com- parison   of   BT-FSW.   In   this   work,   AZ31   Mg   alloy   has   been welded   using   C-FSW,   BT-FSW   and   I-BT-FSW   processes.   The effect   of   bobbin   tool   and   inert   medium   on   the   mechanical   and metallurgy   properties   of   the   joint   are   evaluated   and   compared.  

# 2.   Experimentation  

The   workpiece   metal   welded   in   the   current   investigation is   commercially   available   rolled   AZ31   Mg   alloy   plates   of   di- mension   100   mm   × 100   mm   × 4.5   mm.   Chemical   composition of   the   alloy,   provided   by   the   supplier,   is   Al   3.56%,   Zn   0.85%, Mn   0.25%,   Si,   Ni   and   Cu   each   0.015%,   Fe   0.005%   and   the balance   is   Mg. # 3.2.   Fractography  

Investigations   on   tensile   tested   specimens   were   performed though   fractography   analysis   to   characterize   the   mode   of failure,   as   revealed   in   Fig.   7 .   In   most   of   the   C-FSW   joints, the   fracture   surface   passes   through   the   NZ   due   to   pore defects   because   of   improper   ﬂow   of   plasticized   materials   and experience   lower   tensile   strength.   The   fractograph   of   the   best sample   of   C-FSW   is   presented   in   Fig.   7 (a)–(c).   It   is   observed that   upper   NZ   ( Fig.   7 (a))   exhibits   oxidized   broken   grain   and it   acts   as   fracture   initiated   point.   The   middle   and   bottom part   of   the   weld   contain   some   unbroken   oxidized   grains   as shown   in   Fig.   7 (b)–(c).   The   C-FSW   fractured   surface   rep- resents   a   cleavage   type   fracture   and   suggests   lowest   plastic deformation   with   less   tensile   strength.   In   case   of   BT-FSW ( Fig.   7 (d)–(f))   the   fracture   surface   passed   through   the   inter- face   of   NZ/TMAZ.   The   dimple   features   evident   from   the fractured   surfaces   in   the   upper   and   middle   portions   of   the weld   suggests   ductile   fracture.   The   facture   being   ductile   in nature   may   undergo   uniform   deformation   in   the   plastic   region along   the   thickness   direction.   Some   places   on   the   fractured surface   reveals   dimples   with   small   size   and   less   raised   dim- ples   resulting   in   low   joint   strength   and   percentage   elongation. Upper,   middle   and   bottom   fracture   parts   in   BT-FSW   welds are   different.   The   upper   part   of   the   fractograph   exist   at   TMAZ and   characterized   by   dimple   and   tearing   edge   starting   point with   some   pore   defects   as   shown   in   Fig.   7 (d).   The   middle part   ( Fig.   7 (e))   exists   at   the   boundary   of   the   NZ/TMAZ   and extended   towards   the   HAZ   at   the   bottom   part   ( Fig.   7 (f))   of the   fracture.   The   middle   part   observed   with   a   parallel   crack initiation   line   and   second   phase   particles. # 3.4.   Microstructural   analysis  

Macro   and   microstructure   analysis   is   very   important   in welding   process   because   it   is   directly   related   to   the   weld properties.   Microstructural   specimens   are   extracted   in   trans- verse   to   the   welding   direction.   Bead   geometry   of   one   of   the BT-FSW   specimen   is   presented   in   Fig.   9 (a)   where   differ- ent   zones   are   marked.   The   NZ   seems   like   dumbbell   shape, marked   by   dashed   line,   and   agree   well   with   other   researchers [ 4 , 8 ].   TMAZ   is   situated   besides   the   NZ   where   there   is   min- imal   effect   of   the   tool   pin   stirring.   The   effect   of   shoulder is   prominent   in   the   upper   and   bottom   zones   of   the   TMAZ in   case   of   BT-FSW   due   to   the   presence   of   dual   shoulder. However,   no   effect   of   shoulder   at   the   middle   zone   of   the TMAZ.   The   TMAZ   is   mainly   affected   by   heat   and   achieved modiﬁed   strain   free   grain   and   minor   grain   reﬁnement   due   to recrystallization.   The   HAZ   is   situated   close   to   the   BM   region and   near   to   TMAZ,   where   only   minor   temperature   affects   the grain   structure.   In   Fig.   9 (b)–(e),   microstructures   at   various weld   zones   are   presented.  

The   microstructure   of   the   BM   is   quite   different   from the   other   zones   as   shown   in   Fig.   9 (e).   It   shows   a   non- homogeneous   equiaxed   grain   structure   [4] .   The   microstructure of   BT-FSW   at   815   rev/min   of   rotation   speed   and   22   mm/min of   welding   speed   is   presented   as   Fig.   9 (b)–(d).   The   HAZ   of this   experiment   exhibits   similar   microstructure   r   to   that   of   the BM.   There   is   no   variation   in   the   grain   orientation   however, the   grains   are   slightly   elongated   (shown   in   Fig.   9 (d))   due   to minimal   thermal   effect   in   the   HAZ.   Due   to   the   stirring   effect of   the   pin   the   grains   at   the   central   weld   nugget   zone   (CWNZ) ( Fig.   9 (b))   are   very   ﬁne   (15   µ m,   78%   ﬁner   than   BM)   com- pared   to   the   BM   grains   (68   µ m).   The   TMAZ   grains   as   shown in   Fig.   9 (c)   are   found   to   be   ﬁner,   because   of   higher   heat   input and   recrystallization   compared   to   the   HAZ.   Since   the   grains are   found   to   be   aligned   in   different   directions,   it   can   be   as- sumed   that   recrystallization   has   occurred   in   this   region.   The NZ   consists   of   smaller,   equiaxed   grains,   compared   to   TMAZ. This   is   due   to   the   stirring   action   of   the   tool   and   dynamic recrystallization   occurs   at   high   strain   rate.

It   was discussed   that   the   weld   at   tool   rotation   speed   of   1100   rev/min and   36   mm/min   welding   speed   gives   the   maximum   tensile strength   therefore,   this   condition   is   considered   for   grain   size comparison. The   grain   size   at   the   TMAZ   in   both   the   cases   is bigger   than   the   NZ   because   of   minor   effect   of   the   stirring   ac- tion   at   the   TMAZ   and   absence   of   dynamic-recrystallization. # 4.   Conclusions  

The   above   analysis   implies   an   interesting   study   on   inert gas   effect   to   BT-FSW   process   and   following   conclusions   can be   inferred:  

•   I-BT-FSW   process   gives   best   UTS   of   204.08   MPa   (85%   of BM)   and   YS   (139.99   MPa,   98%   of   BM)   at   1100   rev/min rotation   speed   and   36   mm/min   welding   speed,   compared   to BT-FSW   and   C-FSW.   The   fractography   of   this   specimen shows   close   packed   small   dimples   indicating   no   voids   and tear   ridges   signifying   a   ductile   transgranular   fracture.  

 •   The   EDX   analysis   of   I-BT-FSW   specimens   indicates   oxide free   weld   due   to   inert   gas   environment   and   close   compact- ness   of   plasticized   weld   zone   between   the   dual   shoulders space.   Whereas,   4.2   wt%   and   14.6   wt%   oxide   are   detected in   the   cases   of   BT-FSW   and   C-FSW,   respectively.  

 •   Finest   NZ   grain   was   observed   in   case   of   I-BT-FSW (12   µ m,   82%   ﬁner   than   BM)   due   to   dual   shoulder   effect. The   grain   size   was   the   main   cause   for   the   variation   of micro-hardness   and   mechanical   properties.  

 •   Grain   size   became   coarser   at   higher   tool   rotational   speed due   to   high   frictional   heat   generation   that   lead   to   grain growth.   Because   of   grain   growth   hardness   decreases   in   BT- FSW   and   C-FSW   processes.   However   in   case   of   I-BT-FSW process,   due   to   cooling   effect   of   high   speed   argon   gas   some amount   of   heat   was   compensated   as   a   result   intermediate process   parameter   was   found   favorable   condition.  

•   The   ﬁnding   of   the   research   helps   to   understand   the   me- chanical   properties   of   the   weld   at   different   process   param- eters   and   effect   of   the   environmental   conditions.
