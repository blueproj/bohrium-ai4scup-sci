# Bidirectional Transport of Rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342, Fluorescence Probes of the Binding Sites on P-glycoprotein, across MDCK–MDR1 Cell Monolayers  

FUXING TANG, HUI OUYANG, JERRY Z. YANG, RONALD T. BORCHARDT  

Department of Pharmaceutical Chemistry, The University of Kansas, Lawrence, Kansas 66047  

Received 31 January 2003; revised 24 April 2003; accepted 25 April 2003  

ABSTRACT: The bidirectional permeation characteristics of rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342, ﬂuorescence probes of the binding sites on P-glycoprotein (P-gp), across mono- layers of MDCK cells transfected with the human  MDR1  gene (MDCK–MDR1) were investigated. The ratios of the apparent permeability coefﬁcients ( P app ) of rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342 ﬂux measured in the basolateral (BL) to apical (AP) direction versus the ﬂux in the AP-to-BL direction ( P app BL-to-AP / P app AP-to-BL ) were 115 and 177, respec- tively. The P-gp inhibitor GF-120918 could signiﬁcantly reduce the polarized efﬂux of both rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342. Rhodamine 123 appeared to ‘‘stimulate’’ the polarized efﬂux of Hoechst 33342 across MDCK–MDR1 cell monolayers. In contrast, Hoechst 33342 partially inhibited the polarized efﬂux of rhodamine 123 across these cell monolayers whereas daunorubicin partially inhibited the polarized efﬂux of both rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342. The uptake characteristics of rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342 in MDCK–MDR1 cells were measured in the absence and presence of GF- 120918 and known P-gp substrates (Hoechst 33342, rhodamine 123, and daunorubicin). The uptake of rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342 in MDCK–MDR1 cells was enhanced more than twofold by inclusion of GF-120918 (2  m M) in the incubation medium. Daunorubicin (160  m M) increased the relative ﬂuorescence unit (RFU) values of cytoplasm-associated rhodamine 123 by up to 30%. However, daunorubicin (40  m M) and rhodamine 123 (5  m M) decreased the RFU values of cell membrane-associated Hoechst 33342 by 70% and 40%, respectively. To further explore what appears to be a ‘‘stimulatory’’ effect of daunorubicin and rhodamine 123 on the uptake of Hoechst 33342 and a stimulatory effect of daunorubicin on Hoechst 33342 transport across cell mono- layer, uptake of Hoechst 33342 into liposomes in the presence and absence of GF-120918, daunorubicin, and rhodamine 123 was determined. GF-120918 exhibited no effect on the RFU values of liposome-associated Hoechst 33342. In contrast, rhodamine 123 and daunorubicin decreased the ﬂuorescence of liposome-associated Hoechst 33342 suggest- ing these molecules were either quenching the ﬂuorescence of this chemical probe or displacing it from the lipid bilayer. In conclusion, these bidirectional transport data indicate that rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342 are excellent substrates of P-gp in MDCK–MDR1 cells. The ability of Hoechst 33342 to partially inhibit the polarized efﬂux of rhodamine 123 is consistent with these substrates binding to the same site on P-gp. In contrast, the ability of rhodamine 123 to apparently ‘‘stimulate’’ the efﬂux of Hoechst 33342 in both the transport and uptake experiments suggests the substrates might bind to different sites on P-gp. However, experimental results using liposomes suggested that this ‘‘stimulation’’ phenomenon by rhodamine 123 on Hoechst 33342 uptake and efﬂux might simply be an artifact. Thus, the use of Hoechst 33342 to probe the binding sites on a membrane-bound protein such as P-gp might be problematic.    2004 Wiley-Liss, Inc. and the American Pharmacists Association J Pharm Sci 93:1185–1194, 2004 Keywords: P-glycoprotein; P-gp binding site; rhodamine 123; Hoechst 33342  

# INTRODUCTION  

P-glycoprotein (P-gp) is a 170-kD membrane protein belonging to the adenosine 5 0 -tripho- sphate-binding cassette superfamily of transport proteins.   P-gp was ﬁrst found as a surface phos- phoglycoprotein overexpressed in cultured cells selected for multidrug resistance (MDR).   In the 1990s, this efﬂux protein was found to be widely expressed in the normal human and animal tis- sues, such as the epithelial cells of the intestine, liver, and kidney, and the endothelial cells of the placenta and brain.   P-gp affects the absorp- tion, distribution, and excretion of many structu- rally diverse drugs.   Because of the signiﬁcant impact of P-gp on  in vivo  drug disposition and p harm a co kinetics, understanding its mechanism of transport and its substrate speciﬁcity may be helpful for drug design.  

Based on the ability of several P-gp substrate drugs to inhibit the transport of a photoafﬁnity- 4   labeled vinblastine, Gottesman and Pastan sug- gested that all of the substrates share a single binding site for transport. Other scientists re- ported distinct multiple drug-binding sites on   8   P-gp. For example, Shapiro and Ling reported that P-gp contained two distinct binding sites that were differentiated by rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342, and that these two sites interacted in a positively cooperative manner. Wang et al.   also reported two binding sites using Hoechst 33342 and tetra phenyl ph os ph on i um as chemical probes. Isenberg et al.   suggested at least three binding epitopes on P-gp. Investigators have not yet reach- ed a consensus on the number of binding sites on P-gp. Furthermore, the methods used to deﬁne the drug-binding sites on P-gp were different among these groups. For example, Shapiro and 8   Ling used the continuous ﬂuorescence measure- ment of Hoechst 33342 uptake by inside-out, P- gp rich plasma membrane vesicles, whereas Wang et al.   measured adenosine triphosphatase (ATPase) activities using membrane microsomes. It should be noted that none of these investigators have used bidirectional transport assays across 1 polarized cell monolayers grown in Transwell systems to characterize the binding sites on P-gp.  

The primary  in vitro  methods used to study the P-gp substrate activity of compounds include:  

(i) bidirectional transport assays across polariz- 1 ed cell monolayers grown on Transwells ; (ii) calcein-AM ﬂuorescence uptake assays in 15 cell monolayers ; and (iii) ATPase activity as- says using microsomes.   Each method has its strengths and weaknesses as a drug-discovery tool.   The ATPase and calcein-AM assays offer high throughput and are easily automated, but these assays cannot distinguish P-gp substrates from inhibitors.   Bidirectional transport as- says are labor-intensive because of cell culture and analytical requirements. However, these assays provide both the P-gp substrate characteristics and biomembrane permeability characteristics of drugs/drug candidates; therefore, they are widely used as a screening tool for human absorption, distribution, metabolism, and elimination in the pharmaceutical industry.   Based on results of a direct comparison of these three methods using 66 structurally diverse compounds under rigorously standardized conditions, Polli et al.   suggested that the bidirectional transport assays should be used as the primary P-gp substrate screening method.  

Therefore, the objective of this study was to determine whether the results obtained from the bidirectional transport of rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342 across MDCK–MDR1 cells corre- lated with the results obtained from uptake ex- periments conducted in the same cell culture model and whether this transport across cell monolayers could provide any new insight into the controversy concerning the number of binding sites on P-gp.

# Transport Experiments  

Bidirectional transport experiments were per- 20   formed as previously described with some modi- ﬁcations. All experiments were done at 37 8 C with constant mixing in a shaking water bath (60 rpm). Brieﬂy, both the apical (AP) and the basolateral (BL) chambers of each insert were washed twice with 37 8 C HBSS for 15 min. Test compounds were added to the donor side (1.5 mL for the AP chamber and 2.5 mL for the BL chamber) and fresh HBSS was placed in the receiver compart- ment. To test the inhibitory or stimulatory effects of test compounds on the activity of P-gp, cell monolayers were incubated with HBSS contain- ing GF-120918 (2  m M), rhodamine 123 (5  m M), Hoechst 33342 (5  m M), or daunorubicin (80  m M) for an additional 15 min. The test compounds were added to the donor side along with inhibitors/ stimulators; fresh HBSS containing the inhibi- tors/stimulators [i.e., GF-120918 (2  m M), rhoda- mine 123 (5  m M), Hoechst 33342 (5  m M), or daunorubicin (80  m M)] was placed in the receiver side. Aliquots (100  m L) were withdrawn from the receiver side at various time intervals to 80 min and analyzed using liquid chromatography with tandem mass spectrometric detection (LC/MS/ MS). Fresh HBSS or HBSS containing inhibitors/ stimulators (100  m L) was replaced in the receiver side after sampling.

# LC/MS/MS Analysis  

LC/MS/MS was performed using a Micromass Quattro Micro triple quadruple mass spectro- meter (Micromass, Beverly, MA). The LC was conducted using a Waters 2690 HPLC System (Waters, Milford, MA). A C18 reversed-phase column (50    1.0 mm i.d., 300 ˚ ) (Vydac, Hesperia, CA) was used as the analytical column, and the column temperature was kept at 25 8 C to ensure reproducible separation. Two mobile phases were used to generate a linear gradient with a 0.2 mL/min ﬂow rate. Mobile phase A was water with 0.1% (v/v) formic acid, and mobile phase B was acetonitrile with 0.1 % (v/v) formic acid. The total run time was 8 min with eluent from the ﬁrst 2 min being directed to waste to avoid contaminating the mass spectrometer with salts.  

The HPLC system was interfaced to the mass spectrometer via an electrospray interface. The capillary voltage and cone voltage were 3.2 kV and 65 V, respectively. The source temperature was set at 120 8 C and the nebulizer gas ﬂow was controlled around 600 L/h for effective evaporation of the solvent. The MS1 and MS2 resolutions were both set at 12.0 and the multiplier voltage was set at 650 V. For ion recording, multiple reaction moni- toring of two channels was used with 0.03-s dwell time and interchannel delay set at 0.03 s. Data acquisition and analysis were performed using MassLynx version 3.5 software (Micromass). The low limits of quantiﬁcation of rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342 are 1 and 50 nM, respectively.

# DISCUSSION  

One of the mysteries of P-gp is how this mem- brane protein can recognize such structural diver- sity in its substrates.   A possible explanation is that P-gp has multiple binding sites. Using a P-gp-rich plasma membrane preparation, 8   Shapiro and Ling reported separate binding sites for rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342 (R- and H-binding site). Rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342 are known P-gp substrates. Rhodamine 123 is ﬂuorescent in aqueous media and lipid bilayers, whereas Hoechst 33342 is ﬂuorescent only when it is associated with lipid membrane.   The ﬂuorescence characteristics of rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342 make them convenient probes for screening P-gp ex- pression in cells/tissues   or for investigating P-gp substrate and substrate–substrate inter- actions.   The evidence supporting the exis- tence of R- and H-binding sites included: (i) rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342 mutually stimulated the efﬂux of each other from mem- brane vesicles (i.e., decreasing the membrane vesicle-associated ﬂuorescence); and (ii) anthra- cyclines, such as daunorubicin, stimulated only the efﬂux of Hoechst 33342 but inhibited the efﬂux of rhodamine 123 (i.e., decreasing the membrane-associated ﬂuorescence of Hoechst 33342 but increasing that of rhodamine 123).  

In this study, the bidirectional transport of rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342 was measured in MDCK–MDR1 cells. MDCK–MDR1 cells, a human  MDR1  gene transfected cell line,   over- express P-gp. MDCK–MDR1 cells are a good model for qualitatively screening compounds for their potential substrate activity for P-gp. The data shown in Table 1 indicate that rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342 are good substrates for P-gp. Furthermore, GF-120918, a potent and speciﬁc inhibitor for P-gp at low concentration (less than  1  m M),   could signiﬁcantly inhibit the polarized efﬂux of these solutes. It should be noted that the inclusion of GF-120918 (2  m M) did not reduce the P app BL-to-AP / P app AP-to-BL  ratios of rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342 to unity (Table 1). These data suggest the possible expression of other efﬂux transporters in MDCK–MDR1 cells. In contrast to the ‘‘mutual stimulation effects’’ of rhodamine 123, Hoechst 33342, and daunorubicin on the efﬂux of Hoechst 33342 and rhodamine 123 observed by other investigators in uptake experi- ments,   Hoechst 33342 was shown in the present study to inhibit the polarized efﬂux of rhodamine 123 across MDCK–MDR1 cell monolayers (Table 1). Furthermore, daunorubicin was shown to partially inhibit the efﬂux of Hoechst 33342 (Table 1). These interesting results led us to in- vestigate the uptake of rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342 in MDCK–MDR1 cells.  

As shown in Figures 1 and 2, the uptake of rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342 in MDCK– MDR1 cells was increased more than twofold in the presence of GF-120918 (2  m M) suggesting that these ﬂuorescent probes are good P-gp substrates. These results are consistent with published data about the P-gp substrate activity of rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342.   Daunorubicin is also a 17   substrate for P-gp and has been shown to inhibit the polarized efﬂux of the P-gp substrate digoxin in MDCK–MDR1 and Caco-2 cells.   As shown in Figure 1, daunorubicin increased the uptake of rhodamine 123 in MDCK–MDR1 cells, which is consistent with the known substrate activity of this solute. In contrast to the inhibition effect of daunorubicin on the polarized efﬂux of Hoechst 33342 across MDCK–MDR1 cell monolayers, daunorubicin appeared to stimulate the efﬂux of Hoechst 33342 in MDCK–MDR1 cells in uptake experiments [i.e., the RFU values of cell mem- brane-associated Hoechst 33342 were decreased by 70% (Fig. 2). Furthermore, in the presence of 160  m M daunorubicin, the RFU values of cell membrane-associated Hoechst 33342 were de- creased to the background value (data not shown)]. It is also interesting to note that Hoechst 33342 did not affect the uptake of rhodamine 123 (Fig. 1), whereas rhodamine 123 appeared to stimulate the efﬂux of Hoechst 33342 in a manner similar to that observed with daunorubicin (Fig. 2). Overall, the results of these uptake experiments with rhoda- mine 123 and Hoechst 33342 using MDCK–MDR1 cells are consistent with the data published ear- 8   lier using membrane vesicles.  

However, when only the cell membrane-asso- ciated ﬂuorescence of Hoechst 33342 was mea- sured, it was not possible to determine whether the decrease in ﬂuorescence resulted from the stimu- lation of P-gp or simply from ﬂuorescence quench- ing or displacing of Hoechst 33342 from the membrane by rhodamine 123 and daunorubicin. To differentiate between these possibilities, we decided to use a test system (liposomes) that had lipid but no protein (i.e., P-gp). Liposomes contain- ing no proteins are a simple model that can be used to check whether the decrease in the RFU values of cell membrane-associated Hoechst 33342 pro- duced by rhodamine 123 and daunorubicin is due to stimulation of P-gp or is an artifact of quenching of ﬂuorescence. The data obtained here using liposomes clearly indicate that the decrease in RFU values of lipid membrane-associated Hoechst 33342 caused by rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342 is not related to the stimulation of P-gp but rather appears to be simply a quenching of solute ﬂuorescence. Alternatively, rhodamine 123 and daunorubicin could decrease the RFU values of lipid membrane-associated Hoechst 33342 by displacing Hoechst 33342 from lipid bilayers. The quenching versus displacing mechanism cannot be differentiated in the current experiments. Because the ﬂuorescence of cytoplasm-associated rhodamine 123 was monitored in this study, the potential interference to the ﬂuorescence of rho- damine 123 by Hoechst 33342, daunorubicin, and GF-120918 was checked. These compounds did not change the ﬂuorescence of rhodamine 123 (data not shown). Because GF-120918 inhibited the efﬂux of rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342 in MDCK–MDR1 cells, it is worth analyzing whether GF-120918 has different or similar afﬁnities to the proposed R- and H-binding sites. The IC 50  values determined for GF-120918 effects on the efﬂux of rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342 in MDCK– MDR1 cells were almost identical. These results suggest that either GF-120918 has the same afﬁnity for the proposed R- and H-binding sites or that it is binding to a single site that binds both rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342. It is possible that the ‘‘stimulation effects’’ of rhodamine 123 and daunorubicin on the efﬂux of Hoechst 33342 in uptake experiments resulted from ﬂuorescence quenching or displacing of Hoechst 33342 from lipid membranes rather than from competition for the same binding site.  

# CONCLUSIONS  

The observation that rhodamine 123 and daunor- ubicin decreased the ﬂuorescence of liposome- associated Hoechst 33342 suggests that Hoechst 33342 is not a good ﬂuorescent probe for studying the binding sites on P-gp. The propensity of this ﬂuorescence probe (Hoechst 33342) to be quenched (or displaced) from a lipid bilayer may be misinterpreted as stimulation of P-gp activity. Although the present bidirectional transport studies clearly show that rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342 are substrates for P-gp, they do not help to clarify the controversy about the number of binding sites on this efﬂux transporter. Because GF-120918 was able to inhibit the polarized efﬂux of rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342 and increase the cellular uptake of these ﬂuorescence probes with approximate equal efﬁciency, this P-gp in- hibitor may bind to both R- and H- (rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342) sites on P-gp or simply bind to one site which is responsible for the efﬂux of both rhodamine 123 and Hoechst 33342. Alter- natively, GF-120918 may bind to a site on P-gp which inﬂuences both the binding site(s) for these ﬂuorescent probes.
