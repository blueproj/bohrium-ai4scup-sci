# A Novel Naphtho[1,2- c :5,6- c ′ ]Bis([1,2,5]Thiadiazole)- Based Narrow-Bandgap  π -Conjugated Polymer with Power  Conversion Efficiency Over 10%  

Yaocheng Jin, Zhiming Chen, Sheng Dong, Nannan Zheng, Lei Ying,\* Xiao-Fang Jiang,  Feng Liu, Fei Huang,\* and Yong Cao  

Bulk-heterojunction (BHJ) polymer solar cells (PSCs) com- prising  π -conjugated polymers and fullerene derivatives as the  photoactive layer have attracted tremendous attention due to  their light weight, low cost, and remarkably low energy pay- back time and carbon emission, as well as the potential for  the fabrication of large-area flexible devices.  In the past two  decades, extensive effort has been devoted to the development  of high-performance PSCs through rational molecular design  of novel materials  and delicate optimization of device archi- tectures  and film morphologies.  These efforts indeed have  led to a considerable increase in photovoltaic performances,  for which remarkable power conversion efficiencies (PCEs)  exceeding 10% have been achieved in both single-junction  and multi-junction PSCs.  While a prominent breakthrough  in photovoltaic performance has been achieved, most of these  PSCs rely on a photoactive layer with a thickness of  ≈ 100 nm, which is essentially unfavorable for the manufacture of large- area devices based on high-throughput roll-to-roll processing  technology.  To date, there are only a few  π -conjugated poly- mers that can attain high PCEs based on BHJ film with thick- ness of more than 300 nm.  In this regard, it remains a high  priority that efficient polymers for thick BHJ films, which can  meet the requirement of high-throughput processing tech- nology, are developed, toward future mass-production and  commercialization.  

In principal, to achieve a high PCE for single-junction PSCs  based on a thick photoactive layer, the  π -conjugated poly- mers used should have good light-harvesting properties, high  charge-carrier mobility, and appropriate frontier molecular  orbital levels, as well as good solubility and processibility. The recently emerged donor–acceptor (D–A)-type  π -conjugated  polymers offer a great opportunity to construct state-of-the-art  π -conjugated polymers, since the energy-level hybridization of  the donor and acceptor moieties will generate extraordinarily  narrow bandgaps that can allow for effective light absorp- tion, and the versatile choices of donor and acceptor building  blocks will provide tunable optoelectronic properties associ- ated with outstanding processibility.  Typically, conjugated  polymers with a bandgap below 1.6 eV can be described as  narrow-bandgap polymers.  As a critical issue to construct  highly efficient  π -conjugated polymers, the building blocks  with enlarged planarity are highly appreciated, since they can  affect the intermolecular stacking motif and can significantly  enhance the charge-carrier mobility. To address this issue, we  have previously demonstrated a series of novel conjugated  polymers based on the naphtho[1,2- c :5,6- c ′ ]bis[1,2,5]thiadiazole  (NT) moiety.  It is noted that the photovoltaic performances  of NT-based conjugated polymers outperform those obtained  from polymers consisting of the extensively used benzo[2,1,3]­ thiadiazole (BT) counterpart.  It is of particular interest that  the copolymer PBDTNT, which comprises benz odi thi oph ene  (BDT) and NT units, exhibits a PCE of 7.20% with the BHJ  film thickness of about 1000 nm, implying the great promise  of using NT-based copolymer in thick-film PSCs.  Highly effi- cient NT-based conjugated polymers have also been success- fully developed by copolymerizing with the electron-donating  naphtha[1,2- b :5,6- b ′ ]dithiophene   or  quaterthiophene  derivatives,  for which a high efficiency of about 10% has  been achieved based on the inverted device architecture .  

In this work, we demonstrate two D–A type narrow-bandgap  conjugated polymers based on NT as the electron-accepting  unit, and a high-mobility segment of 2,5-bis(3-alkylthiophen- 2-yl)thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene (BTTT) (the monomer unit of the  high-mobility polymer PBTTT ) as the electron-donating unit.  Here, the regio specifically connected 3-alkylthiophene unit in  the 2,5-position of the centrosymmetric thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene  moiety can reduce the potential steric interaction of the alkyl  side groups and promote self-organization,  while the con- nection with the centrosymmetric NT unit will not disturb the  regioregularity or the adoption of the low-energy backbone con- formation. Thus, it is rational to surmise that the copolymer  based on the combination of such two components will pre- sent a high intramolecular mobility. Additionally, in compar- ison to the axis-symmetric thiophene and BT units, both cen- trosymmetric TT  and NT units with enlarged planarity and  extended  π -conjugation will give rise to significantly enhanced  light-absorption ability and charge-carrier mobility, and thereby  improved PCE.  

The two resulting copolymers have identical backbones of  BTTT-NT but different side chains on two thiophene units,  where the copolymers bearing the side chains of 2-octyldode- cane and 2-de cy lte trade cane are denoted as NT812 and NT1014,  respectively. It is noted that copolymer NT812 exhibits much  higher efficiency than that of NT1014, primarily due to the for- mation of “face-on” stacking of the NT812 polymer chain rela- tive to the substrate. Based on the optimized processing con- dition of the NT812:PC 71 BM blend film, single-junction PSCs  based on both conventional and inverted device architectures  present remarkable PCEs over 10%. Further investigation  indicates that the PCE remains at 8.35% with the thickness  of photoactive layer up to 1  μ m. It is of particular interest that  a high PCE over 10% can also be achieved based on single- junction PSCs with either conventional or inverted architec- tures with a thick photoactive layer processed by a nonhalogen  solvent. These observations demonstrate the great promise of  NT812 for the fabrication of high-performance PSCs toward  mass production based on the high-throughput roll-to-roll  processing technique.  

The synthesis of such NT-based conjugated polymers is car- ried out based on a palladium-catalyzed Stille polymerization in  chlorobenzene (CB). The polymers are afforded as dark purple  solids, which are then purified by Soxhlet extraction, using  methanol, dichloromethane, and chloroform as the eluent,  and the solids are collected by using CB as the eluent. Detailed  synthetic procedures are described in the Supporting Informa- tion. The target copolymers NT812 and NT1014 contain the  bulky alkyl chains of 2-octyldodecyl and 2-decyltetradecyl as  the side groups of the flanked thiophene unit, respectively. The  resulting copolymers cannot be dissolved in organic solvents  such as chloroform, CB, and  o -dichlorobenzene (DCB) at room  temperature, but can be easily dissolved in xylenes, CB, and  DCB at temperatures higher than 70  ° C.  

The molecular weights of NT812 and NT1014 are estimated  by high-temperature gel-permeation chromatography, using  1,2,4-trich l oro benzene as the eluent at 150  ° C. The number- average molecular weights ( M n ) are estimated to be 56.6 and  59.1 kDa with polydispersity indexes of 2.1 and 2.8 for NT812  and NT1014, respectively (see Figure S1 and S2 in the Sup- porting Information). Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC)  measurements do not show discernable glass-transition char- acteristics up to 300  ° C. Both copolymers exhibit high thermal  stability, with a 5%-weight-loss temperature ( T d ) over 360  ° C, as  evaluated by thermo grav i metric analysis (see Figure S3 in the  Supporting Information).  

Theoretical calculations were performed by using the den- sity functional theory based on Gaussian 09 with the B3LYP/6– 31G (d, p) basis set.  The alkyl side chains in the thiophene  units were ignored herein to simplify the calculation. We note  that ignoring the alkyl side chains may lead to unanticipated  inaccuracy,  yet when considering that the building block  of the (2,5-bis(3-alkylthiophene-2-yl)thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene pos- sess an extended, planar  π  electron system,  it is rational to  surmise that the ignorance of the alkyl side chains will not sig- nificantly disturb the planarity of the model compounds. Figure 1c shows the UV–vis absorption spectra in both DCB  solution (with a concentration of about 1  ×  10 − 5  g mL − 1 ) and as  thin films. Both copolymers exhibit broad absorption profiles,  where the absorbance at about 450 nm is attributed to the  π – π \*  transition while the broad low-energy absorption peak at about  700 nm can be correlated with intramolecular charge-transfer  effects. The temperature-dependent UV–vis spectra illustrate  that, with the increase of temperature, the absorbance at the  low-energy band displays a gradual hypsochromic shift, which  can be attributed to the breakup of the aggregation of polymer  chains (see Figure S5 in the Supporting Information). It is also  worth pointing out that NT812 exhibits higher absorbance coef- ficient than NT1014 counterpart both in DCB solution and as  thin films, indicating more solar photons can be absorbed, and  thus a higher short-circuit current density can be afforded by  the former. The optical bandgaps ( E g opt ) of the resulting two  copolymers, as estimated from the onset of thin films, are  about 1.4 eV, which is close to that required by state-of-the-art  narrow-bandgap electron-donating materials.  

The frontier orbital levels of copolymers are evaluated by  cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurements, which are carried  out by using tetra- n -butylammonium hex a flu oro phosphate  (Bu NPF ) (0.1  m  in acetonitrile) as the electrolyte, a saturated  4 6 calomel electrode as the reference, and graphite as the working  electrode. As shown in Figure 1d, the onsets of both the oxi- dation and reduction potentials are nearly identical for both  the resulting copolymers, leading to a very analogous HOMO  level of –5.29 eV and a LUMO level of about –3.40 eV. These  observations indicate that the alternation of alkyl side chains of  2-octyldodecyl and 2-decyltetradecyl does not obviously disturb  the molecular orbitals.  

Our initial investigations of the photovoltaic performances of  the copolymers are carried out based on PSCs with a conven- tional device architecture of ITO/PEDOT:PSS/polymer:PC 71 BM/ PFN-Br/Ag. Here a thin layer of water-/alcohol-soluble poly[(9,9- bis(3-( N , N -dimethyl)- N -ethylammonium)-propyl)-2,7-florene)- alt -2,7-(9,9-dioctylflorene)]dibromide (PFN-Br) is deposited  on top of the photoactive layer from methanol solution, since  it can facilitate electron collection due to the formation of an  interfacial dipole.  The photoactive layer of polymer:PC 71 BM  has an optimal weight ratio of 1:1.5 with a film thickness of  about 300 nm. The BHJ film is spin-cast at 70  ° C from the co- solvent of DCB:CB with a volume ratio of 1:3. The current den- sity vs voltage ( J–V ) characteristics are illustrated in  Figure   2 a,  as measured under illumination of AM 1.5G at 100 mW cm − 2   conditions.  

The device based on NT1014:PC 71 BM as the photoac- tive layer exhibits a moderate PCE of 3.03% with an open- circuit voltage ( V OC ) of 0.77 V, a short-circuit current ( J SC ) of  7.25 mA cm − 2 , and a fill factor (FF) of 52.94%. In contrast, a  remarkable PCE of 9.21% is obtained for the device based on  NT812:PC 71 BM as the photoactive layer, which shows a  V OC  of  0.72 V, a  J SC  of 18.62 mA cm − 2 , and an FF of 66.70%. We also examine the ver- tical charge-carrier mobility of NT812 and NT1014 based on  the space-charge-limited-current model. The measurements  are carried out by fabricating hole-only devices with a structure  of ITO/PEDOT:PSS/polymer:PC 71 BM/MoO 3 /Al, with relevant  J 1/2 – V  characteristics shown in Figure S6 (see the Supporting  Information). The hole mobility of NT812, which is about three orders of  magnitude higher, can be attributed to the highly crystalline  nanostructure with favorable backbone orientation for vertical  charge transport. Note that the high hole mobility of NT812  can effectively reduce charge-carrier recombination, and in turn  lead to an improved efficiency .  

Insight into the film morphology is provided by the  grazing incidence X-ray diffraction (GIXD) measurements  of NT812:PC 71 BM and NT1014:PC 71 BM blend films. GIXD  images of the two blend films are displayed in Figure 2c,d. Both  GIXD images show obvious diffraction with a  q  value of about  1.3 Å − 1 , which can be attributed to the characteristic signal of  the PC 71 BM. From Figure 2c, one notes an apparent signal at  q z   =  1.79 Å − 1  ( π – π  stacking peak, corresponding to a distance  of 0.35 nm), showing high intensity in the  q z  direction. Thus  NT812 in the blend takes a “face-on” orientation, which is ben- eficial for charge transport. In contrast, the NT1014 polymer in  the BHJ blend shows a quite broad  π – π  stacking angular distri- bution (Figure 2d), and the intensity is much lower compared  to NT812 in the blend, suggesting ill-packing of the NT1014  polymer chain and less-preferential crystal orientation, which  retards charge transport across the BHJ thin film.  

The charge transfer in the BHJ film is investigated by exam- ining the photo luminescent (PL) spectra of the pure polymer  film and the polymer:PC 71 BM blend film (see Figure S7 in the  Supporting Information). It is noted that the quench of the PL  intensity of the NT812:PC 71 BM blend film is more pronounced  than that of the NT1014:PC 71 BM blend film, indicating more- efficient charge transfer in the NT812:PC 71 BM blend film.  These observations agree with the significantly improved PCE  of the NT812-based devices.

To understand the charge-recombination mechanism of both  types of devices, we investigate the photocurrent ( J SC ) and  V OC as a function of light intensity, with the relevant characteris- tics plotted in Figure 3c and 3d, respectively. Here, the light  intensity varied from 1 to 100 mW cm − 2 . From Figure 3c, one  notes that the  J SC  increases linearly with light intensity. The  slopes ( S ) of 0.99 and 0.91 are extracted by fitting the data of  the conventional and inverted devices, respectively, suggesting  weak bimolecular recombination in both types of device. Figure 3d depicts the slope of  V OC  versus the natural loga- rithm of the light intensity, which can help us to determine the  degree of trap-assisted recombination in the device. Here the  slope of  V OC  versus the natural logarithm of the light intensity  is described by  k B T / q , where  k B  is the Boltzmann constant,  T is the temperature, and  q  is the elementary charge. Typically,  a slope of 1  ×  k B T / q  implies that bimolecular recombination is  the dominating mechanism. In contrast, a slope of 2  ×  k B T / q implies that the trap-assisted or Shockley–Read–Hall recom- bination is the dominating mechanism.  Both the conven- tional and the inverted devices exhibit nearly identical slopes  of about 1.13 k B T / q . This observation indicates that the charge  dynamics in both types of devices are quite comparable, where  bimolecular recombination is the dominating mechanism and  trap-assisted recombination is relatively weak.

Considering that the practical application of PSCs by roll-to- roll processing techniques requires a thick film of the photo­ active layer, we thereby fabricate conventional devices based on  NT812 with various BHJ layer thickness. The  J – V  characteris- tics of these devices are shown in  Figure   5 a, and the depend- ence of PCE on the film thickness is illustrated in Figure 5b.  As the film thickness increases from 190 to 1000 nm, the  V OC  remains nearly unchanged at about 0.72 V, while the  J SC  slightly varies in the range of 18.00  ±  1.50 mA cm − 2 , and  the FF slightly increases from 72.89% (190 nm) to 74.33%  (250 nm) and then gradually decreases with the increase of  film thickness. The remarkable PCE over 10% can be achieved  with film thickness in the range of 250–340 nm, and the PCE  remains at 8.35% with a BHJ film thickness of 1000 nm. From the safety perspective toward processing BHJ films,  a processing solvent with low flammability and which is less  hazardous to both the environment and human health is highly  appreciated.  In this regard, we fabricate an NT812:PC 71 BM  (1:1.5, wt:wt) blend film by using the nonhalogenated solvent of  o -xylene, consisting of a small amount of diphenyl ether (DPE)  as the solvent additive, where PSCs with both conventional and  inverted architectures are constructed. The  J – V  characteristics  and EQE spectra are depicted in Figure 5c and d, respectively.  Remarkably, at the optimized BHJ film thickness of  ≈ 300 nm,  the conventional device shows a PCE of 10.22% ( V OC   =  0.72 V,  J SC   =  19.91 mA cm − 2 , FF  =  70.64%), and the inverted device  shows a comparable PCE of 10.20% ( V OC   =  0.72 V,  J SC   = 20.61 mA cm − 2 , FF  =  66.99%) (Table 1). Note that an average  PCE of about 10.10% can be achieved based on 15 devices,  implying the excellent repeatability of these results. To our  knowledge, this is the first narrow-bandgap conjugated polymer  that can simultaneously achieve a high PCE over 10% based on  both conventional and inverted structures with a thick photo- active layer processed using a nonhalogenated environmen- tally friendly solvent. The accuracy of  J SC  is also confirmed by  EQE spectra, which demonstrates an EQE higher than 65%  in a wide range from 390 to 760 nm (Figure 5d). We note that  the NT812:PC 71 BM film exhibits a uniform film morphology  with fibrous nanostructures, as revealed by both atomic force  microscopy (AFM) and transmission electron microscopy  (TEM) images (see Figure S9 in the Supporting Information).  Additional EDS investigation of the cross-section devices with  both types of architectures confirms the uniform distribution  of sulfur content in the NT812:PC 71 BM film (see Figure S10  in the Supporting Information). These observations are very  analogous to those found from the BHJ film processed from  the DCB:CB co-solvent system, indicating the great promise of  processing the NT812:PC 71 BM film based on the environmen- tally friendly solvent.  

In summary, we have developed two novel NT-based narrow- bandgap  π -conjugated polymers. The copolymer NT812 with  2-octyldodecyl as the side chain of the thiophene unit shows  a much higher photovoltaic performance than NT1014, which  has 2-do dec yl tetra dec yl as the side chain, primarily due to the  formation of the favorable “face-on” orientation. Single-junction  PSCs based on both conventional and inverted device architec- tures exhibit remarkable PCEs over 10% using NT812:PC 71 BM  as the photoactive layer with a thickness over 300 nm. Detailed  investigation reveals the uniform dispersion of NT812 and  PC 71 BM across the entire film, and both conventional and  inverted devices present nearly identical electron extraction  times. It is of particular interest that, by using environmentally  friendly  o -xylene as the processing solvent, PCEs over 10% can  be achieved based on a thick photoactive layer, based on both  conventional and inverted devices. To our knowledge, this is  among the highest PCEs of single-junction PSCs with a thick  photoactive layer that can be processed from a nonhalogenated  environmentally friendly solvent. These results demonstrate  the great promise of the constructed NT-based narrow-bandgap  conjugated polymers for high-performance PSCs toward high- throughput roll-to-roll processing technology.  

# Experimental Section  

Solar-Cell Fabrication and Characterization : Patterned indium tin oxide  (ITO)-glass substrates were precleaned sequentially by sonicating in a  detergent bath, then with deionized water, acetone, and isopropyl alcohol  at room temperature and in a boiled isopropyl alcohol bath, each for  10 min. The substrates were subjected to ultraviolet/ozone treatment at  room temperature for 20 min. For conventional cells, the precleaned ITO  substrates were coated with PEDOT:PSS by spin-coating (3000 r.p.m. for  30 s, thickness of  ≈ 40 nm) and were then baked at 150  ° C for 15 min  in air. The active layer was deposited in a glove box by spin-coating hot  (70  ° C) CB:DCB  =  3:1 solution containing the polymer and PC 71 BM at a  weight ratio of 1:1.5 at 1100 rpm for 60 s. The active-layer thickness was  controlled by changing the concentration of the solution; for example,  a 10 mg mL − 1  solution (based on polymer concentration) typically gave  an active layer of 300–350 nm thickness. Thermal annealing of the blend  films was carried out by placing them on a hot plate at 100  ° C for 15 min  in a nitrogen atmosphere. A 5 nm PFN-Br layer was then spin-coated  from methanol solution in the presence of a trace amount of acetic  acid onto the active layer. The thin films were transferred into a vacuum  evaporator connected to the glove box, and Ag (100 nm) was deposited  sequentially through a shadow mask under  ≈ 10 –7  Pa, with an active  area of the cells of 0.04 cm 2 . For the inverted cells, the precleaned ITO  substrates (masked at the electrical contacts) were coated with a ZnO  sol which was prepared using a sol–gel procedure, and the films were  spin-coated with a thickness of 40 nm and heated at 200  ° C for 1 h in the  air. Then, a 5 nm PFN-OX layer was spin-cast from its methanol:acetic  acid (99:1 v/v) solution onto the precleaned ITO substrates, and the  crosslinking process was carried out by heating the half-dried films  under 150  ° C and illumination from a fluorescence lamp (no UV lamp  was required) for 20 min. The active layer was deposited as described  above. MoOx (10 nm) and Al (100 nm) were deposited sequentially by  thermal evaporation under  ≈ 10 –7  Pa.  

The current–voltage ( J – V ) curves were measured on a computer- controlled Keithley 2400 sourcemeter under 1 sun, AM 1.5 G spectra  from a class solar simulator (Taiwan, Enlitech); the light intensity was  100 mW cm − 2  as calibrated by a China General Certification Center  certified reference monocrystal silicon cell (Enlitech). Before the  J – V test, a physical mask of an aperture with a precise area of 4 mm 2  was  used to define the device area. The EQE data were recorded with a QE-R  test system from Enli technology company (Taiwan). More than 15 cells  were analyzed to provide average efficiencies for the optimized cells.  No hysteresis was observed in the  J – V  curves (Figure S11, Supporting  Information).
