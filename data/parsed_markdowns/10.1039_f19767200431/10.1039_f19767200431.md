# Summary  

The depression of the freezing point of lithium(previously gettered with yttrium at 4oo°C) caused by deuterium has been determined by ther- mal analysis. The freezing point drops from 180.490 to 180.415 °C at the eutectic composition, 0.013 mol% D. These values are marginally smaller thanforhydrogen(depression0.082°Cat0.016mol%H).The solubilityof deuterium in the liquid metal has been determined up to 2.63 mol% D by electrical resistance methods and can be represented by the equation  

log10 xD = 2.321 - 2873/T 549 ≤ T≤ 724 K  

where xp is the mole fraction of D. The solubility provides values of the partial molar enthalpy, H(soln), and entropy, S(soln) (with respect to the pre- cipitating phase) of solution of deuterium of 54.8 kJ mol-1 and 44.2 J K-1 mol 1, respectively. The value of H(soln) is used to derive a solvation enthalpy of --417 kJ mol-1 for the deuteride ion thereby indicating weaker solvation thanfor thehydrideion.  

# Introduction  

The chemical and physical properties of liquid lithium are of current interestduetothepossibleuseofthemetalasatritiumbreederand/or coolant in the controlled fusion reactor [1, 2]. Although the lithium-tritium system remains largely unexplored, the reactions of the more easily handled isotopes, deuterium and hydrogen, should indicate the probable behaviour of tritium with the metal. Deuterium, like hydrogen and nitrogen, reacts with lithium to form a salt which dissolves and depresses the freezing point of the metal: We have previously found that the depression for lithium hy- dride and for lithium nitride is small(0.082 and 0.250°C,respectively) at the eutectic point, but it can be measured by simple thermal analysis on purified metal [3]. The method is now applied to deuterium solutions. The thermal changes associated with precipitation of very small quantities of salt, as opposed to metal, are too small to detect, however, so that the hyper eutectic liquidus for LiLiD is more suitably determined by changes in elec- trical resistivity, a technique which relies on the greatly different resistivities ofmetalandsalt.  

# Experimental  

The steel apparatus has been described previously [3, 4]. For thermal analysis, deuterium (99.5%) was added in successive, small volumes (ca. 10° mm? at STP) to liquid lithium (30 g, 99.98%) which had been further purified by gettering with yttrium sponge (5 g, 99.9%) at 400 °C. A small electromagnetic pump was used to achieve homogeneity of the solution which was cooled at 10 °C h-1. This slow rate gave reproducible freezing arrests which were detected by a chromel-alumel thermocouple and vernier potentiometer. For solubility measurements [4], the liquid metal was cir- culated through a capillary loop (1.5 mm i.d., 350 mm long) bridged by two circular discs between which the resistance was measured. Resistivity was calculatedfrom theresistance and the dimensions of thecapillary.Successive volumes of deuterium were addedand the equilibrium resistivity determined between each addition. Resistivity increased linearly with concentration up to saturation, which was indicated by an abrupt levelling of the resistivity. The temperature was raised to dissolve precipitated salt and either more deuterium was added to re-saturate at the higher temperature (o-c method) or the solutionwas cooled and the precipitation temperature pinpointed by the decrease in resistivity which accompanied the loss of solute from the solution (o-T method).
