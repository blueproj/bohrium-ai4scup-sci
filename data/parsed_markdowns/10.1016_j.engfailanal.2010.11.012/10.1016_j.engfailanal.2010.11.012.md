# a b s t r a c t  

Two rotor blades in an aero-engine fractured during testing. The fracture mode and cause were analyzed on the basis of fracture surface observation, chemical analysis, and metal- lurgical structure examination. The results show that the two blades failed in the same mode – fatigue fracture caused by recrystallization. In order to ﬁnd out the cause of recrys- tallization, simulation tests were carried out by grit blasting and solution heat treatment. It is found that recrystallization formed during solution treatment because of the plastic deformation by grit blasting, and with the increase of the pressure and time for grit blast- ing, the depth of the recrystallized layer went up.

# 1. Introduction  

Owing to the elimination of the grain boundaries perpendicular to the main stress axis, directionally solidiﬁed (DS) and single crystal (SC) superalloys have excellent mechanical properties, such as high heat resistance, fatigue strength, creep strength and vibration damping ability, and have been introduced into most of the advanced aero-engines.  

An aero-engine failed with the rotational speed declining suddenly from 52,000 r/min to 51,700 r/min and vibration enlarg- ing abnormally during testing. After checking, it was found that two rotor blades fractured. There were totally 39 same rotary blades as these two. None of the blades was found to have tip rubbing trace. Some of the blades were found to have collision trace. These blades are made of a SC superalloy, and the bodies of the blades are covered with coating. In the present work, the fracture cause and mode of the failed blades were analyzed, and some prevention measures for such failure were proposed.  

# 2. Experimental procedure  

Fracture surface and metallographic structure observation was carried out by scanning electron microscopy and optical microscopy. Metallographic specimens were prepared by metallographic polishing. The polished specimens were etched in the reagent consisting of 20 g CuSO 4 , 100 ml HCl and 100 ml H 2 O.  

# 3. Experimental results  

3.1. Macroscopic features  

The failed blades are shown in  Fig. 1 , which are referred to as No. 14 blade and No. 21 blade according to their locations in the aero-engine. For No. 14 blade, the fracture took place at the 2/3 height of the blade body, and the whole transverse section fractured. For No. 21 blade, the fracture took place near the exhaust edge, and a small part of the transverse section fractured. Both of the blades had damage trace at the rabbets, as indicated by arrows in  Fig. 1 . From the fracture surfaces, polycrystalline feature can be seen ( Fig. 2 ).  

# 3.2. Microscopic features  

A penetrating investigation of the fracture surfaces was performed by SEM. Because the fracture surfaces of the two blades have similar microscopic features, only the fracture features of No. 14 blade are described. At the region near the ex- haust edge (Area A in  Fig. 2 ), intergranular cracking feature can be seen and there are also some fatigue striations, as shown in  Fig. 3 . So it can be assumed that cracking initiated from this region. Other regions of the fracture surface all show inter- granular cracking feature, as shown in  Figs. 2 and 4 .  

# 3.3. Microstructure examination  

The sample for microstructure examination was cut down from No. 14 blade at the position about 5 mm away from the fracture surface. It can be seen that the cross section of No. 14 blade was wholly covered with recrystallized grains, as shown in  Fig. 5 . The size of the recrystallized grains is nearly equivalent to that of the grain boundary facets at the fracture surface.  

# 3.4. Chemical analysis  

The chemical composition of No. 14 blade was determined by spectroscopy chemical analysis, and the results are shown in  Table 1 . The chemical composition of No. 14 blade is within the speciﬁed range.  

# 3.5. Simulation tests  

In order to ﬁnd out the cause for the recrystallization, simulation tests were carried out according to the actual manufac- turing procedure. Some as-cast SC blades were sand-blasted under different pressure and for different time. Then they were solution-treated at 1305   C for 3 h, followed by air cooling. After solution treatment, these blades were cut through the cross section, as indicated in  Fig. 6 . Microstructure observation shows that recrystallization has not occurred to the blades sand- blasted under the pressure of 0.05 MPa; however, the blades sand-blasted under the pressure of not less than 0.25 MPa all recrystallized.  Table 2  summarizes the details on the recrystallization of the blades in the simulation tests. With the increase of the pressure and time for sand blasting, the recrystallized grains grew more deeply.  

# 4. Discussion  

# 4.1. Blade failure mode  

The fracture surface of No. 14 blade is nearly wholly covered with grain boundary facets, and the cross section near the fracture surface is wholly covered with recrystallized grains. Thus, it can be assumed that intergranular cracking feature

In DS and SC superalloys, in order to enhance the incipient melting temperature, the addition of the elements that can strengthen the grain boundaries is generally omitted, such as C, B, Hf and Zr. If recrystallization takes place, the recrystallized grain boundaries will become the weakest regions and the mechanical properties will be dramatically reduced  [1–5] . Because of low strength of the grain boundaries, fatigue cracking ﬁrst occurred along the grain boundaries perpendicular to the main stress axis near the exhaust edge, where greater stress existed. Because the whole cross section had recrystallized, the fatigue crack propagated fast along the grain boundaries. Thus, the blade fractured fast at the early stage of testing, and fatigue striations only occurred within a small area near the exhaust edge.  

# 4.2. Recrystallization  

For the recrystallization of metals, there must be two necessary conditions: plastic deformation and high-temperature heat treatment. According to the manufacturing and serving conditions of the SC blades, there are three possible ways resulting in recrystallization as follows: (1) Plastic deformation has occurred to the blades before solution heat treatment. If the plastic deformation reaches some extent and may provide enough driving force for recrystallization, recrystallization will occur during solution heat treatment. That is, recrystallization has occurred before the blades are put into service. (2) After solution treatment, plastic deformation occurs to the blades, and recrystallization may take place during service. (3) Dynamical recrystallization may take place during service.

According to Refs.  [6–8] , for DS and SC superalloys, solution of  c 0   phase is a critical factor for the recrystallization. Below the  c 0   solvus temperature, recrystallization takes place through the growth of cellular microstructure and the depth of the recrystallized layer is very small, while above the  c 0   solvus temperature, recrystallization occurs through the growth of well-developed recrystallized grains, and the depth of the recrystallized layer is much bigger. For the SC superalloy studied here, the  c 0   solvus temperature is about 1300   C, and the service temperature of the blades is about 1000   C. At the service temperature, nearly all of the  c 0   particles exist, and they retard recrystallization. Even if recrystallization takes place at the service temperature, not well-developed grains but cellular microstructure will occur and the recrystallized layer is very thin. So the recrystallization of the blades could not take place at the service temperature owing to cold deformation after solution treatment. According to Ref.  [9] , dynamic recrystallization may occur to the blades of DS and SC superalloys during service. But the dynamically recrystallized grains are only located in the surface  c 0   free layer, with the depth of less than 20  l m. So it can also be assumed that the recrystallized grains could not be caused by dynamic recrystallization during service.

Based on the discussion above and the results of simulation tests, it can be concluded that the recrystallization occurred during solution treatment. Sand blasting before solution treatment resulted in plastic deformation. Then the plastically deforming areas recrystallized during solution treatment. The air pressure and the time for sand blasting are two critical fac- tors controlling the degree of plastic deformation. If the pressure is low and the time is short, recrystallization may be avoided. However, with the increase of the pressure or the time for sand blasting, the recrystallized layer will become deeper.  

# 4.3. Prevention of recrystallization  

In order to prevent recrystallization, it is necessary to pay more attention to the manufacturing processes. The main measures are proposed as follows:  

1. Anti-deformation design of the casting die. 2. Adopting precision casting to reduce machining as much as possible. 3. Strictly controlling deformation during surface treatment. 4. Adopting reasonable procedures to make the cold deformation occur after solution treatment. 5. Strictly controlling recrystallization depth according to relevant standards.  

# 5. Conclusion  

1. The main cause for the failure of the SC blades is fatigue failure caused by recrystallization.

 2. Recrystallization of the blades occurred during solution treatment because of the plastic deformation caused by sand blasting.

 3. In order to prevent recrystallization, more attention should be paid to manufacturing processes, such as strictly control- ling the pressure for sand blasting.
