# Creep-Fatigue Cracking Near the Welded Interface in Friction Welding Dissimilar Superalloys INCONEL 718 and MAR-M247  

TRAN HUNG TRA and MASAKAZU OKAZAKI  

A forged INCONEL 718 and a cast MAR-M247 alloy were joined by a friction welding process. The creep-fatigue strength of this joint was investigated. The life of the joint was signiﬁcantly shorter than that of the base alloys. The joint failed near the interface of the INCONEL 718 side, although the life of INCONEL 718 was longer than that of MAR-M247. To understand this behavior, the stress ﬁeld in the welding was numerically analyzed using a visco-elastic model. The results suggested that triaxiality in the stress state could be promoted near the welded interface and lead to an acceleration of creep-fatigue crack nucleation.

# I. INTRODUCTION  

N ICKEL - BASE  superalloys have emerged as the candidate materials for high-temperature applications, where resistance to fracture under static, fatigue, and creep 1 , ]   conditions is decisive. In gas turbines, for instance, the blades operate at high temperature and, thus, require outstanding creep resistance. On the other hand, the disks operate at lower temperatures but higher loading condi- 1 , ] tions and, thus, require excellent fatigue resistance. Therefore, there are several advantages from the develop- ment of a multifunctional single component. Such a component can work well under distinct temperature regimes and loading conditions,  e.g. , turbine  blisks . Recently, to meet these requirements, there have been a number of new trends in superalloys and processes. NASA designed the dual-microstructure heat-treatment technol- ogy in which the microstructural and property variation 3 , ]   can be achieved within any given component. Another favorable way diﬀerent superalloys are joined into one 5 – 8 ]   structure. As a suitable method of joining dissimilar alloys, friction welding is generally accepted as an alterna- 5 – 13 ] tive process in this hybrid approach.  

Friction welding has been used recently to join a cast MAR-M247 with a forged INCONEL 718.\* They are  

1 , , ] candidate superalloys for turbine blisks. The mechanical properties of the welding were experimen- tally investigated under various loading conditions by 6 ]   Okazaki  et al. The welding was strong under mono- tonic and fatigue loadings. When the dissimilar joint is addressed to use at elevated temperatures, the so-called creep fatigue and thermo mechanical fatigue need to be investigated. Here, several mechanical and metallurgical factors are simultaneously considered. From a metal- lurgical aspect, the dissolution or coarsening of the main strengthening precipitates and the recrystallized 11 , , – 18 ] microstructure might be the important issues.  

From a mechanistic viewpoint, the stress concentration resulting from the incompatibility of mechanical prop- erties could be a critical concern.  

The objective of this work is to explore the role of the stress multiaxiality induced by the inhomogeneous properties along the welding. For this purpose, the creep-fatigue behavior of the welding was assessed by the experiments. Then the stress ﬁeld in the welded zone was numerically analyzed using a visco-elastic model, employing two kinds of damage parameters proposed [ 19 ]   20 ] by Huddleston and Spindler.  

# II. EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURES  

A dissimilar friction joint (denoted DFJ) between cast polycrystalline MAR-M247\*\* (denoted MM247) and

forged INCONEL 718 (denoted IN718) was fabricated by friction welding. This fabrication was performed on a 6 ] continuous drive friction welding machine. The chemical compositions of two base alloys are shown in Table  I . The average grain sizes of IN718 and MM247 were approximately 10  l m and 1.0 mm, respectively. Before welding, both base alloys received heat treat- ments. IN718 was treated at 1368 K (1095   C) for an hour in Ar atmosphere, followed by aging at 993 K (720

  C) for 8 hours in air. MM247 was treated at 1505 K

 (1232   C) for 2 hours in Ar atmosphere, followed by aging at 1143 K (870   C) for 20 hours in air. After welding, a postweld heat treatment was given to the DFJ at 993 K (720   C) for 2 hours in air.  

Several welding regimes were conducted to determine an optimal DFJ. Here, the following fabrication vari- ables were considered for each regime: rotation speed of base metals, preheating time, normal compression stress to induce frictional heating, duration of compression stress, upset pressure, upset clearance, upset hold time, and postweld heat treatment. A ﬁnal DFJ was deter- mined, which had no signiﬁcant defects and tensile strength comparable to that of the base alloy.  

The microstructural characterization of the weld was observed by an electron backscatter diﬀraction (EBSD)    system (JEOL ﬁnite-element–scanning electron micro-  

 JEOL is a trademark of Japan Electron Optics Ltd., Tokyo.  

scope JSM-7000 F) with analytical software (OIM analysis 4.6) and an optical microscope. The EBSD patterns were detected using a charge-coupled device camera and image process background subtraction. Micro-Vickers hardness were measured across the weld interface by a diamond indenter using a 200 gf and 5-second indentation loading cycle. The thermal expan- sion coeﬃcients were measured on the cylinder speci- mens (Figure  1 ), using a high-frequency induction heating system with a 12.0-mm gage length extensome- ter. The Young’s moduli were determined based on the stress-strain loops at the midlife cycle.  

The creep-fatigue specimens were extracted from the DFJ, as illustrated in Figure  1 , in which the weld interface was located at the specimen center. Both the DFJ and its base alloys were tested in isothermal condition at 923 K (650   C). All tests were performed by a servo-electro hydraulic system under a fully reversed uniaxial strain-controlled mode in air (strain ratio  R  = e –1.0). Each strain cycle had a hold time of 600 seconds in tension, as shown in Figure  1 (b). An extensometer was set up to control the overall strain at the center part of the specimen gage section. The gage length of this extensometer is 12.0 mm. The specimens were heated by a high-frequency induction heating system. The unifor- mity of the test temperature was conﬁrmed by a preliminary test. The temperature diﬀerence between the shoulder and the middle point of the gage section on the specimen was within 5 K (5   C) when the specimen was heated to 923 K (650   C). The test temperature was controlled through  R -type thermocouples welded near the specimen shoulders. The creep-fatigue life of a specimen was deﬁned by the number of strain cycles at which the tensile stress was dropped and reduced by 25 pct from the stable value. Before testing, the specimen surface was mechanically polished by SiC papers. The microstructure and fractured surfaces were observed by a scanning electron microscope.  

During the test, the crack initiation and propagation morphology were monitored by taking replicas period- ically on the surface of the specimen. The replicas were taken after each group of cycles. The number of cycles in each group was estimated qualitatively based on the applied load magnitude—the higher the loading, the shorter the cyclic group for taking replicas. Further- more, the cyclical group was changed and reduced gradually from the start to the end of the test.  

# III. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION  

The microstructure around the weld interface is revealed in Figure  2 . There are no visible defects in the welded zone, on a microscopic scale. The microstructure of the welded zone shows a complicated morphology, as indicated by the EBSD analysis shown in Figure  2 . The grain size varied signiﬁcantly from the weld interface in the IN718 side (Figures  2  and  3 ). It was ﬁne near the interface, then it gradually coarsened with increasing distance, and ﬁnally it was back to the base alloy IN718 grain size. Figure  3  presents the average grain size with the maximum/minimum scatter in the welded zone of the IN718 side. An approximate 100- l m-wide zone characterized by large grains can be distinguished. This zone is around 100 to 200  l m from the weld interface. The thermo mechanically aﬀected zone (denoted TMAZ) in the IN718 side is deﬁned by the recrystallized grain microstructure. The width of this zone is approximately 200  l m from the weld interface (Figure  2 ). MM247 possesses a dendritic structure that consists of large polycrystalline grains in millimeter size. The hardness in the DFJ is presented in Figure  4 . Here, it was measured for the DFJ before and after the creep-fatigue test. Under both conditions, a softened zone was formed in the IN718 side with a width of a few hundred microm- eters. The hardness in the softened zone seemed to be improved after testing, in comparison with the hardness before testing. Obviously, a remarkable softening and microstructural recrystallization took place in the IN718 side, in comparison to MM247. The other aspect might associate with the multiaxial stress state induced by inhomogeneous properties in the DFJ. To explore the role of the stress state on the failure of the DFJ, the behavior of the DFJ was numerically simulated by combining a visco-elastic model with a ﬁnite-element method (FEM) tool. This simulation took account of the diﬀerences in mechanical properties among two base alloys and the welded zone. The entire model for the DFJ is illustrated in Figure  12 . Based on the microstructural characteristics (Figures  2  and  3 ) and the hardness distribution (Figure  4 ), the model was constructed by three layers corresponding to three respective materials. Here, there layers include an IN718 base alloy layer with a 5.8-mm length, a TMAZ layer with a 0.2-mm length (in IN718 side), and a MM247 base alloy layer with a 6.0-mm length. The dashed lines labeled Paths 1 through 3 (in Figure  12 ) are the tentative coordinate axes in the model: Path 1 at the axial centerline, Path 2 at a distance of 1.5 mm from the centerline, and Path 3 on the surface. In the FEM calculation, all layers are assumed to be isotropic and homogeneous and perform in a visco-elastic manner. The elastic behavior is assumed to obey Hooke’s law. The parameters were signiﬁcantly promoted in the IN718 side, where the creep cracks were predominantly nucleated (Figure  6 ). These calculations strongly propose that the creep-fatigue failure of the DFJ might be closely related to the stress multiaxiality. In summary, the DFJ possessed a low creep-fatigue life with a representative fracture mode in the IN718 side. Besides the reduction of creep-fatigue resistance caused by the dissolution or coarsening of precipi- 18 , , ]   tates, the FEM calculation revealed that the concentration of stress and strain induced by inhomo- geneous properties along the welding promoted signif- icant damage in the joint.  

# IV. CONCLUSIONS  

Two diﬀerent types of superalloys, forged INCONEL 718 and cast MAR-M247, were joined by a friction welding process. The creep-fatigue behavior of the welding was investigated by both experiment and numerical simulation. The life of the welding was lower than that of the base alloys. While the life of IN718 was higher than that of MM247 under identical test condi- tions, the fracture of the DFJ occurred in the IN718 side. The intergranular mode dominated on the fracture surface. The role of the multiaxial stress state induced by the inhomogeneous properties in DFJ specimens was found to be important here. Both the creep strain and the creep damage parameters were signiﬁcantly pro- moted near the weld interface, resulting in a predomi- nant creep crack nucleation.
