# Mutual solubility of water and hydrophobic ionic liquids in the presence of hydrochloric acid †  

Val´ erie Mazan,   Maria Yu. Boltoeva, \* ab   Evgeny E. Tereshatov c   and Charles M. Folden c III  

Previous studies have shown that the presence of nitric acid, the principal solute in various hydro metallurgical processes, and perchloric acid in the aqueous phase is an important factor for increased aqueous solubility of hydrophobic ionic liquids. In this study, the e ﬀ ect of hydrochloric acid in the aqueous phase on the mutual solubility of water and hydrophobic 1,3-di alkyl i mid a zo liu m- and  N , N - di alkyl pyr ro lid in i um bis(tri ﬂ u oro methyl s ulf on yl)imide ionic liquids, [C n mim][Tf 2 N] ( n  ¼  2, 4, 6, and 8) and [C 3 C 1 pyrr][Tf 2 N], is examined at room temperature and atmospheric pressure. Hydrochloric acid caused a considerable increase in the aqueous solubility of all the studied ionic liquids. The amount of water transferred into the organic phase increases with increasing hydrochloric acid concentration for short alkyl chain ILs, and the opposite trend was observed for long alkyl chain ILs. The e ﬀ ect of the N – H acid bis(tri ﬂ u oro methyl s ulf on yl)imide, H[Tf 2 N], and the salts lithium bis(tri ﬂ u oro methyl s ulf on yl)imide, Li[Tf 2 N], and 1-butyl-3-methyl i mid a zo liu m chloride, [C 4 mim]Cl, dissolved in hydrochloric acid solutions on the mutual solubility of water and the [C 4 mim][Tf 2 N] ionic liquid were also investigated. The salting-out e ﬀ ect is observed and it was shown to be dependent on the nature of the salt, its concentration and the hydrochloric acid concentration in the aqueous phase. A mathematical model has been developed to describe the dependence of the ionic liquid cation and anion concentration on the common ion salt concentration in the aqueous hydrochloric acid phase. This model describes the basic character of ionic liquid dissolution in the aqueous phase and allows for estimation of solubility product values.  

# 1. Introduction  

Ionic liquids (ILs) are a wide class of salts with melting points lower than 100    C, and some of them are liquid at room temperature.   ILs consist entirely of ions, and are o  en asymmetric, with bulky organic cations and organic or inor- ganic anions. The cationic moieties used for the preparation of the most-investigated ILs are alkyl-substituted ammonium and phosphonium, imidazolium, pyridinium, and pyrrolidinium. Common anionic components of ILs are halides, hexa-  uorophosphate, tetra  uoroborate, tri  u oro methane s ulf on ate, and bis(tri  u oro methane s ulf on yl)imide. The physicochemical properties of ILs such as density, viscosity, miscibility with other solvents,  etc.  can be adjusted by the appropriate choice of the cationic and anionic parts of the IL.   Moreover, the enor- mous number of potentially accessible ILs could allow for selecting speci  c ILs for each application, and that is why ILs are o  en called  “ designer solvents ” .  

Ionic liquids can be applied in various chemical   elds such as organic synthesis,   catalysis,   electrochemistry,   and separation chemistry, particularly solvent extraction. – 16 Hydrophobic ionic liquids are widely considered as a promising alternative to traditional organic solvents because of their unique physicochemical properties like high chemical and thermal stability,   non-  ammability,   very low vapor pres- sure.   These properties may o ﬀ er safer and more e ﬃ cient ways to extract metallic ions from acidic aqueous solutions. However, the use of ionic liquids could result in complications for a global extraction process, especially because of: (i) the modi  cation of some thermophysical and transport properties of ionic liquid phase, for example, density and viscosity, a  er contact with aqueous solutions; (ii) the mutual solubility of water and IL and as consequence the pollution of the aqueous phase with IL ions; (iii) the complex mechanism of extraction of metallic ions. These issues must be considered from various points of view prior to the application of ILs for metal separation: (i) from the technological point of view, because of possible phase reversal and undesirable presence of IL component ions in product streams; (ii) from the environ- mental point of view because of the possible release of ILs in aquatic systems; and (iii) from the economical point of view because of the loss of costly ILs. Moreover, the mutual solubility of water and ILs is of key importance because the metal extraction can be driven by transfer of IL constituent ions into the aqueous phase.  

1,3-Di alkyl i mid a zo liu m bis(tri  u oro methyl s ulf on yl)imide ionic liquids are the most studied ILs as solvents for the extraction of metals. These ILs are hydrophobic but somewhat hygroscopic and slightly soluble in water. In our previous works,   the mutual solubility of 1-butyl- and 1-decyl-3- methyl i mid a zo liu m bis(tri  u oro methyl s ulf on yl)imide ILs and water in the presence of nitric, perchloric, and hydrochloric acids was investigated. It was shown that these mineral acids widely used in solvent extraction are an important factor for increased solubility of the studied ionic liquids. Nevertheless, the experimental data on the behavior of biphasic systems composed of IL and hydrochloric acid solutions are limited and fragmentary.   There are no data on the IL anion solubility as a function of hydrochloric acid concentration, the impact of cation alkyl side chain length and the presence of various organic and inorganic solutes in both phases on the solubility of ILs. However, a number of recent studies have reported the extraction of metals such as gold, platinum, iridium and thal- lium from hydrochloric acid media into pure ILs.   To understand the mechanism of extraction in this kind of IL- based systems, the study of mutual solubility of water and IL in the presence of hydrochloric acid is mandatory.  

This paper reports the study of biphasic systems composed of aqueous hydrochloric acid solutions (concentrations from 0 to 10.5 M) and hydrophobic bis(tri  uoromethanesulfonyl) imide-based ILs composed of four 1-alkyl-3- methyl i mid a zo liu m cations with 2, 4, 6, and 8 carbons in the alkyl chain and also the N -propyl- N -methyl pyr ro lid in i um cation. The determination of the equilibrium concentration of the entities, namely H 2 O, in the ionic liquid phase, and cations and anions of ILs and hydrochloric acid in the aqueous phase, as a function of the solutes dissolved in the aqueous phase was carried out. The results obtained in this work give some insights into the behavior of IL-based extraction systems in contact with aqueous hydrochloric acid solutions.

# 2.2. Sample preparation  

ILs were accurately dried under vacuum prior to use following a previously published procedure.   The content of residual water was less than 0.03 wt% a  er drying [C n mim][Tf 2 N] ( n  ¼  2, 4, 6, or 8) and [C 3 C 1 pyrr][Tf 2 N]. The deutero-hydrochloric acid solutions were prepared by diluting concentrated DCl with deuterium oxide to the desired concentration. Li[Tf 2 N] and [C 4 mim]Cl salts were dried under reduced pressure at room temperature for 4 h. The solutions of Li[Tf 2 N] and [C 4 mim]Cl were obtained by exact weighing of a calculated amount of solid followed by dissolving in a volumetric   ask with 1 M or 5 M DCl in D 2 O. An analytical balance (Sartorius, BP 2215 model) with an accuracy of   0.1 mg was used for weigh- ing the components. The concentration of stock salt solutions was close to 1 M; these solutions were then diluted with 1 M or 5 M DCl in D 2 O. The partition experiments were carried out by a standard liquid – liquid extraction technique. Generally, equal volumes of IL and aqueous phase (0.8 mL) were placed into Eppendorf centrifugation tubes (2 mL). The corresponding IL masses were calculated using the known densities (Table 1) and were taken precisely by weighing. The aqueous acidic solution was added into the tube by an Eppendorf Research precision pipette. The biphasic samples were vigorously mixed for 1 h at 1500 rpm on a mechanical shaker (IKA vibrax VXR basic) at room temperature (23    2    C). A  er a given shaking time, the samples were centrifuged for 2 min at 3000 rpm (Micro Star 12, VWR) in order to promote phase separation. Then, aliquots of the equilibrium organic and aqueous phases were taken for further analysis. The experiments were generally repeated twice.

Fig. 6 shows the dependency of the equilibrium water content as a function of the number of carbon atoms on the longer alkyl side chain of the cation at di ﬀ erent aqueous DCl concentrations for the set of [C n mim][Tf 2 N] ( n  ¼  2, 4, 6 or 8) ionic liquids. However, the water uptake in [C 6 mim][Tf 2 N] and [C 8 mim][Tf 2 N] shows a weaker dependence on the alkyl side chain length than in [C 2 mim][Tf 2 N] and [C 4 mim][Tf 2 N]. The in  ection point is located at  n  z  4 – 5. It is interesting to note that other properties of [C n mim][Tf 2 N] ionic liquids ( n  ¼  2 – 10), such as interfacial tension, viscosity, and density, as well as water uptake and various thermophysical parameters, are reported to be depen- dent on alkyl chain length with an in  ection point of  n  z  6.   In the literature, this point is called the critical alkyl chain length (CAL) and is found to be related to the structural segregation in the liquid.   It is recognized that ionic liquids display meso- structural ordering with segregated regions of polar and nonpolar domains formed by the alkyl chains of the cations.   The IL ions interact with water due to ion-dipole forces. The higher a ﬃ nity of short alkyl chains of imidazolium-based ILs ( n  ¼  2 and 4) towards water is probably caused by higher polarity,   that leads to stronger water and ionic liquid interactions. Recently, quantum chemical density functional theory calculations have shown that water is solvated preferentially on the IL polar region, namely on the aromatic head and adjacent methylene groups.   For [C n mim][Tf 2 N] ionic liquids with  n  ¼  2 and 4,  i.e. , lower than CAL, the elongation of the alkyl side chain strongly a ﬀ ects the charge distribution of the cations, and explains the stronger dependency of the water uptake. The molecular dynamics study of the [C n mim][Tf 2 N] homolog series ( n  ¼  2 – 10)  

showed that the nonpolar aggregates in [C 2 mim][Tf 2 N] and [C 4 mim][Tf 2 N] form clusters and start to percolate the IL as a continuous nonpolar subphase for  n  \$  6.   Small-wide angle X-ray scattering measurements of the [C n mim][Tf 2 N] ionic liquids ( n  ¼  2 – 12) provide further support for this theory. Thus, the smoothed change of the water solubility in ILs with  n > CAL could be explained by a less pronounced change in the charge distribution associated with the more separated strands of the polar network.

For all [Tf 2 N  ]-based ILs, the amount of the IL cations in the aqueous phase increases with increasing [DCl] aq,init (Fig. 8a) as was previously observed for H[Tf 2 N] in the aqueous phase (see Fig. 3). The variation of [Tf 2 N  ] anion concentrations is much less pronounced and seems to be non-monotonic.  

A study of the [C 4 mim][Tf 2 N] solubility in aqueous solutions of nitric acid showed that both the IL cation and anion content in the aqueous phase increase as a function of DNO 3  concen- tration.   [C 4 mim + ] and [Tf 2 N  ] concentrations changed from approximately 15 mM in pure water to approximately 90 mM in [DNO 3 ] aq,init  ¼  6 M. The concentration of the [C 4 mim + ] cation was previously found to be 293 mM when [C 4 mim][Tf 2 N] was in contact with 5.9 M HClO 4 , but no [Tf 2 N  ] solubility was re- ported.   In this study, in [HCl] aq,init  ¼  6 M, the amounts of IL cationic and anionic components for [C 4 mim][Tf 2 N] are approximately 91 and 16 mM, respectively. The lower concen- trations of [Tf 2 N  ] anions than corresponding IL cations can be explained by the extraction of H[Tf 2 N] from the aqueous phase into the IL phase (Fig. 4). The higher aqueous solubility of the [C 4 mim][T 2 N] ionic liquid in nitric and perchloric acid solu- tions is probably caused by higher e ﬃ ciency of anion exchange between [Tf 2 N  ] and the acid counterions. It is evident that the observed salting-in e ﬀ ect ( i.e. , the increase of IL aqueous solu- bility) of these mineral acids followed the rank order HClO 4  > HNO 3  > HCl, corresponding to the Hofmeister series. The same trend was reported for the aqueous solubility of betainium and choline bis(tri  uorosulfonyl)imide ILs, [Hbet][Tf 2 N] and [Chol][Tf 2 N], contacted with 1 M HClO 4 , HNO 3 , and HCl solu- tions. The possible equilibria in the biphasic system composed of IL and hydrochloric acid solutions is discussed below.  

# 3.3. Mutual solubility of water and IL in the presence of Li[Tf 2 N], [C 4 mim]Cl and HCl  

The presence of various acids in the aqueous phase is not the only factor that can a ﬀ ect the mutual solubility of water and ILs. Some metal and organic salts, such as NaNO 3 , NaCl, Li[Tf 2 N], [C n mim]Cl,  etc. , are also known to be able to considerably modify the properties of biphasic systems with ILs.   The e ﬀ ect of the salts [C 4 mim]Cl and Li[Tf 2 N], containing common cationic or anionic parts with the [C 4 mim][Tf 2 N] ionic liquid, was investigated in this work because the introduction of these compounds in the aqueous acidic phase is widely used for understanding the extraction mechanism of metallic ions in IL- based biphasic systems.  

Fig. 9 shows the experimental results on the solubility of water in the [C 4 mim][Tf 2 N] ionic liquid that was contacted with an aqueous hydrochloric acid solution ([HCl] aq,init  ¼  1.1 or 5.7 M) with various amounts of dissolved [C 4 mim]Cl or Li[Tf 2 N] salts.  

These two salts have opposing in  uences on the water content in the [C 4 mim][Tf 2 N] ionic liquid. The introduction of [C 4 mim]Cl in HCl solutions leads to a slight decrease in water content in the IL, while the presence of Li[Tf 2 N] noticeably increases water solubility. The e ﬀ ect of Li[Tf 2 N] and [C 4 mim]Cl salts dissolved in the aqueous acidic phase on the water uptake in ILs is more pronounced at higher aqueous HCl concentra- tions. These results will be discussed below.

# 4. Conclusion  

In this work, novel experimental data on the in  uence of hydrochloric acid, on the mutual solubility of water and hydrophobic ionic liquids, [C n mim][Tf 2 N] ( n  ¼  2, 4, 6 or 8) and [C 3 C 1 pyrr][Tf 2 N], at room temperature and atmospheric pres- sure, have been obtained. Hydrochloric acid noticeably enhances water uptake and aqueous IL solubility, and the amount of IL cations dissolved in the acidic aqueous phase is higher than that of anions. For a given hydrochloric acid concentration, the mutual solubility of water and Tf 2 N- based ILs decrease with increasing hydrophobicity of the ILs: [C 2 mim][Tf 2 N] > [C 3 C 1 pyrr][Tf 2 N] > [C 4 mim][Tf 2 N] > [C 6 mim][Tf 2 N] > [C 8 mim][Tf 2 N]. The introduction in the aqueous acidic phase of compounds bearing the same anion with IL, H[Tf 2 N] acid and Li[Tf 2 N] salt, results in the increase of water solubility in imidazolium-based ILs, while the [C 4 mim]Cl salt slightly reduces the water uptake. The salting-out e ﬀ ect of these compounds is dependent on their nature and concen- tration, and the hydrochloric acid concentration in the aqueous phase. A mathematical model for the equilibrium concentra- tions of IL anions and cations in the presence of a common ion in the acidic aqueous phase has been developed. The results of this study are of key importance for designing the extraction systems composed of hydrophobic di alkyl i mid a zo liu m ILs and hydrochloric acid solutions, and for understanding metal extraction mechanisms in these kinds of systems.
