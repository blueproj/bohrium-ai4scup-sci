# INTRODUCTION  

THE importance of the role of water in magmatic processes is widely recog- nized, and extensive experimental studies have been made on certain aspects of this role.The emphasis in a majority of these studieshasbeen on the role of water in granitic magmas, perhaps not so much because it is regarded as more important in these magmas than in mafic magmas,but largely because of technical difficulties attending laboratory investigations of iron-rich mafic melts at high temperatures and water pressures.  

The work of Kennedy (1948) and of Osborn (1959, 1962) has clearly empha- sized the role of water as a reservoir of oxygen in the crystallization and differentiation of mafic magmas. They, as well as Bowen (1947), Tilley (1960), and Yoder & Tilley (1962) have discussed the effects of the level of oxygen fugacity on liquid lines of descent. In addition, Yoder & Tilley (1956) have stressed the effect of water in lowering the melting temperatures of basaltic rocks.In both these roles,the actual amount of water in solution in mafic magmas can be a highly important factor.  

Few attempts have been made previously to determine experimentally the solubility of water in mafic melts (Khitarov et al., 1959); nor have there been any known previous attempts to investigate the combined effects of water pressure (PH.o) and oxygen fugacity (fo) on the crystallization of these melts. The objectives of the work presented here were to determine the solubility of water in mafic melts and to investigate the effects of oxygen fugacity on the crystallization of mafic melts at high water pressures. We report results obtained on water solubility at 1,100° C in the pressure range from 1,000 to 6,000 bars, and on the crystallization sequence at 1,ooobars water pressure.  

# EXPERIMENTALAPPARATUSANDSTARTINGMATERIALS  

Apparatus  

All hydrothermal experiments were carried out in internally heated pressure vessels, as described by Burnham (1962), which are similar to those used by Yoder (1950). Pressure was obtained by pumping argon into the vessel and then heating; the final pressure was adjusted to the desired value when the experiment was at the desired temperature. Pressures were measured by bulk modulus cells which were standardized against a Heise gauge (certified accuracy :

 ±0:1%, 10,000-100,000 p.s.i.) and are estimated to give a reading within

 ±1% of the true value. The pressure variation during a given run was main- tained within ±3% of the stated value.  

Temperatures were measured by platinum:platinum-10% rhodium,or chromel:alumel thermocouples,whichwere standardized periodically against the melting point of gold. Usually, three thermocouples were used to monitor the temperature distribution over a length of 2 in. In the best runs there was no significant gradient along the 1-in. length of the sample; in the worst acceptable runs the gradient was of the order of 1o° C per inch (i.e. the sample was within ±5° of the desired temperature). One of the three thermocouples was used to control, as well as to indicate, the temperature. It was connected to a Minnea- polis-Honeywell circular indicating controller which, in turn, actuated a motor- driven Powerstat. This arrangement maintained the experiment within ±4° of the set point. The combined gradient and control range yields a maximum temperature uncertainty of ±9°, although in many experiments the uncertainty was abouthalf themaximum.In Table 4 the temperature of each experiment is shown together with an estimate of the temperature uncertainty.  

Quenching was achieved by switching off thefurnacepower.Itrequired approximately 10 sec for a detectable temperature drop to occur; thereafter the temperature dropped 50°C during the first 35 sec.

# THESOLUBILITYOFWATERINMAFICMELTS  

Methodsofsolubilitydetermination  

The solubility of water in the basalt melt was determined by the following four methods: (1) the presence or absence of excess water-vapor pits in charges of known water content (Burnham & Jahns, 1962), (2) a modified Penfield method of water analysis (Ingamells, unpublished; Hartwig-Bendig, 1941),

 (3) weight loss at 110°C (Kennedy et al., 1962),and (4) weight loss at 1,000° C

 (Goranson,1931).  

Method (l) has yielded reliable data on the solubility of water in iron-poor melts, but the results on Columbia River basalt melt were erratic and somewhat lower on the average than the results obtained by the other methods. These low and poorly reproducible results doubtless are largely the result of partial oxida- tion of the iron in the basalt melt by the water, thereby reducing the water content by an amount that is not constant from run to run. Moreover, the hydrogen produced by this reaction forms vapor pits that are indistinguishable fromwater-vaporpits.  

The second method was applied to the products of five runs which had been held at 1,100° C and at 1,000, 2,000, 3,000, 4,000 and 5,340 bars. The charges were ground in air to—80 mesh and analyzed for water by a modified Pen field method (Hartwig-Bendig, 1941),in which PbO and PbCrO, are used as a flux and to prevent reduction of thewater.The resultswerein fair agreement with those obtained by methods(3)and(4)uptoabout 2,000bars (4·5weightper- cent water), but at higher pressures they were much lower. The discrepancy probably resulted from loss of water during grinding, perhaps owing to increased surface area and localized heating  

The method based on the loss in weight of the run products at 11o° C (3) yielded the most reproducible, and probably the most accurate results. The details of this method,which was adopted in this study,areasfollows:  

Platinum tubing of 5·0 mm internal diameter and 0·2 mm wall thickness was cutintolengths of about 3 cm,cleaned,and annealed.Oneendwas welded shut and, to determine the loss of platinum during the welding process, a number of tubes were weighed before and after welding; the average loss in weight was 0·0001 g. About 0.8 g of rock powder plus water was inserted into each tube. The approximate solubility was known from preliminary work; hence, the proportion of powder to water weighed into each tube could be adjustedtojustoversaturatethemeltundertheanticipatedconditionsof the run. In this way the amount of excess water vapor plus dissolved rock was kept toa minimum.Finally,thesecond end of the platinum tube was welded, and anylossof weight in excess of 0·ooo1g,duringwelding,wasassumed to represent a loss of water.  

After therun,theplatinum capsules usually had several rounddimplesin thetop surface--a sign of excess vapor.The dimples were pierced andwater usually exuded under some pressure.Thepierced capsuleswere then heated in an oven at 110°C for 10-15 min, cooled in a desiccator for 20 min, and weighed; this procedure was repeated as many as eight times. After an initial large loss, which was assumed to be excess water not in solution, the capsule weighteither became constant or decreased at a small constant rate.Charges having a dissolved water content less than about 5 percent reached a constant weight after two or three heating periods; charges that contained more than this amount of dissolved water continued to lose weight on heating. To determine the weight of water in solution, a graph was drawn with weight of the capsule plus contents as ordinate, and heating time as abscissa.The straight line portion of this graph, representing a small constant loss of dissolved water, was extrapolated back to intersect the ordinate for the time of beginning of heating. The weight of capsule plus contents at this inter- sectionwas used tocalculate thepercentage ofwaterin thecharge.Thisvalue wascorrected for oxidationduring therun,as describedbelow.  

The amount of water used in oxidation was calculated to be 0·36 percent of the weight of the anhydrous rock powder. This figure was obtained by a comparison of theferrous oxide, ferric oxide and water content of the rock powder (Table 1, column 1) and the same three oxides from a typical run (run no. 200, Table 2, line 1). In order to determine the actual changes in ferrous and ferric oxide contents, the percentages by analysis were recomputed for the anhydrous sample on the assumption that the water content of the powder (-80 mesh) obtained by grinding the products of run no. 200 was the same as that determined, for a similar sample, by the Penfield method.  

In applying this correction for the amount of water used in oxidation,it was assumed that the extent of oxidation was the same in all runs.This assumption is not strictly valid, as water pressure doubtless affects the degree of oxidation, but the effect is considered negligible for the present purposes. Of far greater significance, however, is the length of time each run was held at temperature and pressure, as the rate of oxidation was dependent upon the rate at which hydrogen diffused out through the walls of the platinum tubes.As an example, the FeO content of run no. 188 (Table 2) is 6.46 percent, whereas that of run no.200 is only 2.73 percent and the only difference between the two runs was their duration, i.e. 2 h and 20 h respec- tively.For this reason,as well as to ensure homogenization of the silicate- water solution, all solubility runs were held at temperature and pressure for20h.

The principle on which these buffers is based, briefy, is that an element of variable valence (iron in the present case) in two or more crystalline phases will react with water at a given temperature and total pressure to produce a fixed fo, provided all phases of the buffer system are present. The fo, in equili- brium with a buffer varies with temperature, as shown in Fig. 5, and to a much smaller extent with total pressure. Ernst (1960) and Eugster & Wones (1962) clude d that it is small.

# Experimental procedures and results  

Rock powder and crushed glass, plus enough water just to oversaturate the charges at the temperature and pressure of the run, were weighed into separate platinum tubes, each 2·5 mm internal diameter and 10 mm long. After sealing. 6233.1  

these capsules were placed inside a larger (5 mm interna1 diameter, 30 mm long) platinum or gold tube, together with the appropriate buffer plus enough water to completely oxidize the buffer, and the outer tube sealed. Outer tubes of platinum were used in all experiments above about 1,025° C, whereas gold tubes were used below this temperature in an attempt, largely unsuccessful, to reduce the loss of hydrogen from the system.  

The loss of hydrogen outward through the walls of the outer tubes and the consequent oxidation of thebuffers imposed severe limitations on the duration of runs involving  the fayalite+magnetite+quartz and magnetite+wistite buffers. The magnetite hematite buffer was not completely oxidized during runs as long as 24 h at 4,000 bars total pressure; in fact, occasionally the original hematite was reduced to magnetite, probably as a result of introduction of hydrogen from the inner capsule. The fayalite+magnetite-+quartz buffer, on the other hand, could be run for about 4 h at 1,ooo bars and for only a few minutes at 4,ooo bars before the buffer became oxidized and ceased to maintain a fixed fo. in the sample. The loss of hydrogen from the magnetite+wuistite buffer was so rapid, even at 1,ooo bars, that its use, alone, was precluded.  

This problem eventually was overcome by mixing hydrogen with the argon pressure medium in proportions such that the back pressure of hydrogen in the vessel at run temperature was approximately equal to the fa, inside the capsule. The amount of hydrogen to be added to the pressure medium was estimated from calculations similar to those of Eugster & Wones (1962). The highest hydrogen pressure used, approximately 500 bars, caused no detectable damage to the steel pressure vessel or to the Ptso Rhzo heating element.  

After each run the buffer materials were examined to ascertain that all buffer phases were present. All the buffers were examined microscopically and with a magnet; in addition, an X-ray powder diffraction pattern usually was made of the magnetite-+ wistite buffer. If the buffer was found to have been completely oxidized or reduced, the fo, had not been constant during the run and the results were disregarded. Generally, complete oxidation was indicated by a loss in weight of the charge equal to the total hydrogen content of the water in the buffer.  

The inner sample tube was weighed after removal from the surrounding buffer material and commonly was found to have gained weight as a result of minor solid solution, in the platinum, of iron from the buffer. The tubes were then opened and a crushed-grain mount examined under the petrographic micro- scope. X-ray powder diffraction patterns also were made of several charges that contained quench silicates as an aid in distinguishing amphibole from pyroxene and in the identification of magnetite  

The precise determination of the temperature of appearance of a primary phase was handicapped by the presence of quench phases in the products of some runs. Charges which consisted of melt, magnetite, and minor amounts of crystalline silicates, under the conditions of the run quenched to the primary phases plus a mass of quench magnetite, pyroxene, or amphibole, and variable amountsofglass.  

In runs using the magnetite+hematite buffer,sufficient amounts of quench silicate were present to cause difficulty in distinguishing small amounts of primary pyroxene from quench material. Estimates of the maximum tempera- ture of existence of pyroxene, as given below, are considered to be a minimum. In runs that did not contain magnetite as a primary phase, the amount of quench silicate was much lower than in those containing magnetite, and several runs made with the magnetite+quartz+fayalite and magnetite+ wistite buffers were quenched to glass or glass plus primary silicate crystals. This suggests that the presence or absence of quench material is dependent more on the availability of nuclei than on the rate of cooling.

# DISCUSSION  

It is clear from the present work that fo, as well as water content, has an important effect on the crystallization sequence in a basaltic magma. The range of fo,'s covered in this work varies from about 10-3 bars for the magnetite+ hematite buffer at 1,230° C to about 10-i3 bars for the magnetite-+ wistite buffer at 1,000° C. Fudali et al. (1963) have determined the fo2's in equilibrium with the ferrous-ferric ratio of a number of basaltic rocks from widely different mag- matic environments. In this latter study, the equilibrium fo, at 1,200° C for the series of rocks was found to be within the rather limited range 10-64 to 10-85 bars. The narrowness of this range probably is due in large part to the moderat- ing effects of the earth's upper crust; the range of fo, will, or course, be larger for these rocks if a range of temperature is considered.  

The equilibrium fo. of a magma is dependent mainly on the ferrous-ferric ratio of the enclosing rocks with which the magma is in equilibrium, and the temperature of the magma. Both the magma produced by partial melting of mantle rocks and the parent rock will be in equilibrium with the same fo,; the parentrockservestobufferthemagma.  

The depth of origin of basaltic magma has been variously estimated to be from 30 miles to 120 miles (cf. Eaton & Murata, 1960). The mineralogy of rocks at such depths is unknown, but it is possible that a significant range in ferrous- ferric ratios exists.This range may include part orallof the stability field of magnetite(magnetite-+hematitebuffer to magnetite+wistitebuffer) and,if the composition of stony meteorites is taken as evidence for the composition of the mantle,someofthesourcerocksalsomaycontainelementaliron.  

The temperature of a magma generated at depthwill be greatly influenced by the prevailingfo, of the source rocks, as well as by pressure and degree of water saturation. A completely dry gabbroic magma at a depth of 30 miles (approxi- mately14,000bars)wouldbe essentiallyall liquid at a temperature between 1,350° and 1,450° C, assuming that the effect of confining pressure on the liquidus temperature is comparable to the observed effect on the diopside liquidus (Boyd, 1961). If, on the other hand, the same magma were saturated with water under the fo, conditions of the fayalite + silica+iron buffer, liquidus temperatures in the range 900°-1,000° C might be expected. It is possible, then, that the effects of variable ferrous-ferric ratio in the source rocks andvariable water content of the magma might be combined in a way such that a magma can have an initial fo. anywhere in the range 10-o5 bars (magnetite+hematite buffer at 1,400° C) to 10-18 bars (fayalitequartz-+iron buffer at 900° C). On rising toward the surface, a highly reduced magma will become progressively more oxidized, generally through: (a) reaction with more oxidized wall rocks. (b) selective diffusion of hydrogen into the wall rocks, and (c) absorption of water which had been in equilibrium with more oxidized rocks or, perhaps, even the atmosphere. Thus, if differentiation of a mafic magma by crystal fractiona- tion begins at depth, the liquid line of descent will be greatly influenced both by the initial equilibrium fo, of the magma and the changes in fo: that occur as themagma ascends to higher levels in theearth'scrust.
