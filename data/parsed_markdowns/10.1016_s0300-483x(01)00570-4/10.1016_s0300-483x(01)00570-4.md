# Inﬂuence of redox-active compounds and PXR-activators on human MRP1 and MRP2 gene expression

# Abstract  

In the present study, we investigated the inducibility of the drug conjugate transporter genes MRP1 and MRP2 by redox-active compounds such as  tertiary  butylated hydroquinone ( t BHQ) and quercetin and by chemicals known to activate the pregnane X receptor (PXR) such as rifampicin and clotrimazol and by the metalloid compound arsenite. The human MRP2 gene was found to be inducible in HepG2 cells by rifampicin, clotrimazol, arsenite and  t BHQ. As MRP1 expression is extremely low in HepG2 cells, its inducibility was studied in MCF-7 cells. However, only  t BHQ and quercetin acted as inducers, but not the other compounds investigated. Reporter gene assays demonstrated that proximal promoter regions of the genes contribute to the induction by  t BHQ, quercetin (MRP1) and clotrimazol (MRP2). However, the deletion of binding sites supposed to mediate the induction process (a PXR-binding element-like sequence for the clotrimazol effect and an ARE (antioxidative response element) for the  t BHQ / quercetin effect) did not result in a signiﬁcant decrease in the induction factor indicating that other parts of the promoter are probably involved in the induction process. In summary, expression of both genes can be up-regulated by redox-active compounds, while the other compounds tested induced only MRP2 but not MRP1 expression. © 2002 Elsevier Science Ireland Ltd. # 1. Introduction  

The subfamily C of the ATP-binding cassette (ABC) transporter superfamily comprises seven multidrug resistance proteins (MRP1–MRP7), among which MRP1 and MRP2 are best charac- terized (Hipfner et al., 1999; Ko ¨nig et al., 1999). Human MRP1 (ABCC1) is able to mediate multi- drug resistance of tumor cells, and to protect cells from toxic insults by eliminating conjugates of toxic compounds like  S -glutathionyl aﬂatoxin B1 (Loe et al., 1997). MRP2 (ABCC2) is the most important biliary efﬂux pump known so far. Both, xenobiotic drugs like methotrexate and en- dogenous compounds such as leukotriene C 4  or bilirubin glucuronide are eliminated via MRP2. In addition, MRP2 is able to confer multidrug resis- tance (Cui et al., 1999) in tumor cells, probably by eliminating drugs like cisplatin or vincristine in association with glutathione. Furthermore, it was suggested to be involved in the biliary elimination of carcinogenic heterocyclic amines like PhIP (Di- etrich et al., 2000).  

Changes in the expression of drug transporters might inﬂuence hepatic detoxiﬁcation, drug distri- bution, and multidrug resistance of tumor cells. Therefore, it was the aim of the present study to investigate the inﬂuence of selected drugs and toxins on both, human MRP1 and MRP2 gene expression.  

In this study, we focussed ﬁrst on compounds (clotrimazol, rifampicin) described as activators of the pregnane X receptor (PXR) inducing human cytochrome P450 (CYP) 3A4 via this pathway. Human PXR, also named steroid and xenobiotic receptor (SXR) is an orphan receptor cloned 3 years ago (Blumberg et al., 1998; Lehmann et al., 1998) for which various xenobiotic and steroid ligands are described (Moore et al., 2000). PXR mediates the induction of several human CYP 3A genes. Furthermore, gene expression of transmembrane transporters like Oatp2 or MDR1 is regulated by PXR / SXR (Staudinger et al., 2001; Synold et al., 2001). Interestingly, PXR / SXR is able to mediate resistance to lithocholic acid in- duced liver damage (Staudinger et al., 2001; Xie et al., 2001). Bile acid conjugates such as 3  -sul- fa to litho c holy l taurine are described as MRP-sub- strates (Ko ¨nig et al., 1999). Therefore, it is of interest if PXR is also involved in MRP regulation.  

As many redox-active compounds induce phase II enzymes of drug metabolism like UDP-glu- curonosyl-transferases (Mu ¨nzel et al., 1999; Bock et al., 2000) thus generating MRP substrates, the study includes compounds such as  tert -butylated hydroquinone ( t BHQ) and the ﬂavonoid quercetin. Finally, arsenite was included in the study, as MRP1 confers resistance to arsenite and because it was previously described as MRP1 inducer in a resistant leukemia cell line (Cole et al., 1994; Ishikawa et al., 1996). In the second part of the study, we aimed at elucidating the relevance of the proximal promoter regions of both genes for the induction process.  

# 2. Materials and methods  

# 2.1.  Cell culture  

HepG2 cells were purchased from ATCC (Manassas, VA, USA), MCF-7 cells were kindly provided by Dr T.W. Gant (MRC Toxicology Unit, Leicester, UK). Cell culture was performed on 94 mm petri dishes (treated with collagen) in DMEM containing 1% penicillin / streptomycin and 20% FCS. Substances tested as inducers were purchased from Sigma (Deisenhofen, Germany). All test compounds were dissolved in DMSO, controls were treated with DMSO only.  

# 2.2.  RNA isolation ,  Northern blot analysis and RT - PCR  

Total RNA from HepG2 and MCF-7 cells was isolated by a phenol–chloroform extraction method (Chomczynski and Sacchi, 1987). MRP2 mRNA expression was analyzed by RT-PCR which was performed as previously described (Kauffmann et al., 1998), except that no radioac- tivity was used. PCR-products were visualized by ethidium bromide staining in agarose gels and analyzed with the Eagle Eye system (Stratagene Europe, Amsterdam, The Netherlands). The fol- lowing primers were used: MRP1 forward GAA GAC CAA GAC GTA TCA GGT, reverse CAA TGG TCA CGT AGA CGG CAA (fragment size 260 bp); MRP2 forward ACT TGT GAC ATC GGT AGC ATG, reverse GTG GGC GAA CTC GTT TTG (fragment size 495 bp); GAPDH for- ward TGG GGA AGG TGA AGG TCG GA, reverse GAA GGG GTC ATT GAT GGC AA (fragment size 110 bp).  

For PXR detection, cDNA preparation was carried out as described by Lacave et al. (1998), with a cDNA synthesis time of 50 min. The reaction was stopped by heating at 70 °C for 15 min. PCR using [  32 P] dCTP was carried out as described by Dotzlaw et al. (1999), using the same primers for PXR. The samples were ethanol pre- cipitated and re-dissolved in a sequencing buffer containing formamide and run on a 6.5% acrylamide / 7 M urea gel. The gel was dried and radioactive bands were visualized by autoradiog- raphy.  

# 2.3.  Western blot analysis  

The preparation of plasma membrane fractions and Western blotting were performed as previ- ously described (Kauffmann et al., 1997). For the detection of human MRP1 and MRP2, the mono- clonal mouse-antibodies MRPm6 and M 2 III-6 (Alexis Biochemicals, Gru ¨nberg, Germany) were used. The immunoreaction was visualized using a horseradish peroxidase-conjugated goat anti- mouse antibody (Sigma Immunochemicals, St. Louis, USA) followed by enhanced chemolu- minescence detection with the ECL plus technique (Amersham Pharmacia Biotech, Braunschweig, Germany).  

# 2.4.  Construction of reporter gene   ectors and reporter gene assays  

5186 bp of the human MRP2 5  -ﬂanking region were ampliﬁed from genomic HepG2 DNA using the Advantage Genomic PCR kit (Clontech, Heidelberg, Germany). CCT CCC AAA GTG CCC AGG ATC CCA GCC A was used as forward primer, CAG CAT GAT TCC TGG ACT GCG TCT GGA AC as reverse primer. The fragment was subcloned into a TA vector (pCRII, Invitrogen, Groningen, Netherlands), sequenced and ﬁnally inserted in a pGL3 enhancer vector (Promega, Heidelberg, Germany) using  Kpn I and Xho I. The same strategy was used to construct 887 bp MRP1-promoter-luciferase construct. PCR was performed with the Advantage-GC Ge- nomic PCR kit (Clontech, Heidelberg, Germany) using the primers AGG CGC TCT GCA CAC AGG TGA GGT C and CAG AAG CCC CGG AGC GCC ATG C. Deletions were inserted in the MRP1 promoter construct by  P  u II / Cpo I di- gest, and in the MRP2 promoter construct by Kpn I / Aﬂ II (DEL1) and Aﬂ II / Sma I (DEL2) digest.  

Reporter gene activity was measured in tran- siently transfected HepG2 and MCF-7 cells 48 h after drug administration as described elsewhere (Kauffmann and Schrenk, 1998). As control vec- tor pRL-SV40 was co-transfected simultaneously.  

# 2.5.  Cotransfection experiments  

HepG2 cells were seeded on 24-well plates at a density of 5 × 10 5   cells per well 1 day prior to transfection in DMEM medium containing 10% charcoal-stripped fetal calf serum (FCS). Cells were transfected using Fugene 6 (Roche) accord- ing to manufacturer’s instructions. Each well was transfected using 1   l of Fugene 6 transfection reagent and 100 ng CYP3A4 INS luc promoter construct (Goodwin et al., 1999) or the 5186 bp MRP2 promoter construct, 10 ng pSG5-hPXR (Lehmann et al., 1998), 10 ng CMV   -gal and 380 ng pSG5. Approximately 17 h after transfection the medium was changed to F12 medium contain- ing 10 mg / ml penicillin-streptomycin and 10% delipidated calf serum (Sigma) and rifampicin or DMSO.  

# 3. Results  

In the ﬁrst part of this study, the inducibility of MRP1 and MRP2 by selected types of com- pounds as mentioned above was tested. MRP1 mRNA and protein expressions were not changed markedly after treatment with rifampicin or ar- senite. In contrast, the redox-active compound t BHQ increased MRP1 mRNA expression up to 1.8-fold and also a slight increase in the MRP1 protein level was noted. Therefore, additional pro- / antioxidants were tested. In these experi- ments quercetin treatment resulted in the strongest increase in expression of both, MRP1 mRNA and protein levels in MCF-7 cells (Fig. 1). Additionally, butylated hydroxytoluol and buty- lated hydroxyanisol were able to induce MRP1 gene expression (data not shown).  

Rifampicin,  t BHQ and arsenite were also tested as inducers of MRP2 expression. All these com- pounds were able to increase MRP2 mRNA and protein levels in HepG2 cells. Additionally, we tested clotrimazol since it was described to medi- ate induction of gene expression by a similar mechanism, i.e. activation of the pregnane X re- ceptor (Lehmann et al., 1998), as rifampicin. In fact, clotrimazole also increased MRP2 expression on the mRNA and protein level at concentrations of 5 and 20   M (Fig. 2).  

In the second part of the study, we focussed on induction mechanisms. For these purposes, re- porter gene assays using constructs comprising 0.9 kb of the human MRP1 promoter or 5 kb of the human MRP2 promoter were performed. The MRP1 promoter construct was activated by quercetin, conﬁrming the inducing effect on the endogenous gene detected by Western blotting and semi quantitative RT-PCR. In agreement with these experiments quercetin treatment resulted in a higher average reporter gene activation (nearly 2-fold) than  t BHQ treatment (1.5-fold) (Fig. 3). The effect of  t BHQ on the intact construct did not reach statistical signiﬁcance. It is described that redox-active compounds like  t BHQ can me- diate induction of gene expression by activation of antioxidative response elements in the promoter of responsive genes (Xie et al., 1995). Analysis of the 5  -ﬂanking regions revealed that the proximal human MRP1 promoter comprises in fact one ARE (TGACTCAGC) which is identical to an ARE in the human NQO1 (NAD(P)H, quinone oxidoreductase 1; Xie et al., 1995) gene. The ARE is located 665 bp upstream of the translation start site of MRP1. In order to test the relevance of this putative transcription factor binding site, this se- quence and the adjacent promoter region (bp − 887 to  − 612) was deleted and the inducibility of the construct was tested again. However, the deleted constructs still were inducible by  t BHQ and quercetin, indicating that the proximal pro-

Concerning MRP2, reporter gene assays re- vealed that only clotrimazol but not  t BHQ or rifampicin was able to activate the 5 kb promoter sequence signiﬁcantly ( P  0.05; Fig. 4). So far only the PXR-pathway is known to mediate clotrimazol induction, clotrimazol being one of the most potent PXR activators (Lehmann et al., 1998). RT-PCR demonstrated, that the HepG2 cells used in this study actually express human PXR (Fig. 5). Subsequently, we screened the iso- lated MRP2 promoter fragment (comprised in GenBank accession no. AL133353) for putative PXR-binding elements. While no exactly match- ing PXR-binding site was found, we identiﬁed a PXR-binding element-like sequence, an ER 10 (TGAACT-N 10 -AGGTCA) located 4941 bp up- stream of the transcription start site. The se- quence partially matches the PXR-binding ER 6 element (TGAACT-N 6 -AGGTCA) identiﬁed in the human CYP3A4 gene (Lehmann et al., 1998). A related ER6 element which was activated by the PXR was identiﬁed in the human CYP3A7 pro- moter (Pascussi et al., 1999). No other sequences matching known PXR-binding sites to a similar degree were detected in the isolated promoter fragment.  

Subsequently, we deleted this PXR-binding ele- ment-like sequence and the adjacent promoter region (bp  − 5186 to  − 4821, DEL 1) and also introduced a large deletion between bp  − 4821 and  − 2657 (DEL2) in the reporter construct. Interestingly, both deletion mutants showed a 1.5- fold higher basal expression compared with the full-length construct, indicating the presence of silencers in the deleted regions. However, the ac- tivity of both deletion constructs could still be signiﬁcantly induced ( P  0.05) by clotrimazol (Fig. 4). In order to test whether another part of the isolated promoter fragment—beside the dele- tions—is possibly responsible for PXR-binding we performed co-transfection assays with the full- length reporter construct and a PXR expression vector (Table 1). However, these assays demon- strated clearly that PXR is not able to activate the isolated 5 kb proximal promoter fragment. The positive control experiment (co-transfection with a CYP3A4 promoter construct, Goodwin et al., 1999) showed the expected increase in reporter gene activity.  

# 4. Discussion  

The results of this study demonstrate that both, MRP1 and MRP2 are inducible by  t BHQ. More- over, we observed that beside  t BHQ, other redox- active compounds such as quercetin are able to induce MRP1 expression. Reporter gene assays suggest that the inducing effect of quercetin and t BHQ on MRP1 expression is at least partially mediated by sequences in the 5  -ﬂanking region of the gene. A recent study demonstrates that MAPK (mitogen-activated protein kinase) path- ways may link signals to the transcription factor Nrf2 (NF-E2-related factor 2), which subse- quently activates ARE-dependent genes (Yu et al., 2000). However, in the case of MRP1 the induc- ing effect seems to be mediated neither by an ARE located in the proximal promoter nor by adjacent sequences. Our results suggest that the proximal promoter (612 bp upstream of the trans- lation start site) contributes to the induction by quercetin. However, this region comprises no presently known sequences mediating induction of gene expression by oxidative / antioxidative signals. Other sequences located further upstream might

The p53 status, which is described to be involved in MRP regulation, is obviously not relevant for the induction by prooxidants as re- ported recently (Sullivan et al., 2000; Lin-Yee et al., 2001).  

In the isolated fragment of the human MRP2 promoter, one ARE-like sequence, TG C - CTCAGC ( − 2638 bp upstream of the translation start site), was identiﬁed. However, reporter gene assays revealed that the isolated 5 kb promoter fragment including this ARE-like sequence is not inducible by  t BHQ. Possibly, sequences located further upstream-such as another ARE-like se- quence TGAC C CAGC ( − 9757 upstream of the translation start site)—may mediate induction of the endogenous gene by  t BHQ.  

In contrast to MRP1, MRP2 expression is also inducible by rifampicin and arsenite. The observa- tion that rifampicin induces human MRP2 but not MRP1 expression corresponds to a recent in vivo study with duodenal biopsies (Fromm et al., 2000).  

Recent studies also revealed pathways mediat- ing induction of gene expression by arsenite. Sim- ilar to heat stress, arsenite induces heat shock proteins, and stress-response kinases like JNK (JunN-kinases) or p38 are activated. By JNK and p38 pathways, arsenite is able to activate immedi- ate early genes such as c-fos and egr-1 which can induce target genes (Bernstam and Nriagu, 2000). Arsenite is also able to activate AREs (Yu et al., 2000), but the ARE in the MRP1 promoter region is obviously not targeted. Rifampicin and clotrimazol are able to induce target genes via the pregnane-X-receptor (Kliewer et al., 1998; Goodwin et al., 1999). Surprisingly, our 5 kb MRP2 promoter construct was only activated signiﬁcantly by clotrimazol but not by rifampicin. We speculated that induction of MRP2 expression might be mediated by a PXR- binding element-like sequence located 4.9 kb up- stream of the transcription start site. However, reporter gene assays with deleted constructs demonstrated that this sequence is not relevant for clotrimazol-mediated induction. Moreover, the whole 5 kb region is not activated by PXR in co-transfection experiments. These results suggest that the inducing effect of clotrimazol is at least partially mediated by a PXR-independent path- way. However, it is reasonable to speculate that sequences further upstream are targeted by the PXR pathway and contribute to both, clotrimazol and rifampicin-mediated induction of MRP2. Concerning induction of the related MDR1 gene, the responsible PXR-binding site is also located far (8 kb) upstream (Geick et al., 2001).  

In summary, the present study demonstrates that the induction patterns of human MRP1 and MRP2 are quite different except of the inﬂuence of redox-active compounds. An ARE located in the proximal promoter of the MRP1 gene seems to be irrelevant for the induction of the endoge- nous gene by quercetin. The proximal 5 kb of the human MRP2 promoter contributes to the induc- tion by clotrimazol—obviously not via a PXR dependent mechanism—while it does not mediate induction by rifampicin and  t BHQ.
