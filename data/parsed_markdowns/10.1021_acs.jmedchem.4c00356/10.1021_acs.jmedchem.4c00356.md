# INTRODUCTION  

Diabetes mellitus (DM) is a systemic disease that imposes a significant socioeconomic burden. ,   It has become a grave global health issue, affecting both developed and developing countries, with an estimated 537 million adult patients in 2021.   Approximately 90 − 95% of these cases are classified as type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM).   In the clinical treatment of T2DM, there are two main classes of first-line hypoglycemic drugs based on their mode of action: insulin secretagogues (such as sulfonylureas, dipeptidyl peptidase-IV inhibitors, and glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) agonists) and insulin sensitizers (including biguanides and thiazolidine dione s). T2DM patients often exhibit insulin resistance and require multiple antidiabetic drugs.   However, the development of insulin sensitizers has been slow since the introduction of thiazolidine dione s in the 1990s.  

Protein tyrosine phosphatase 1B (PTP1B) is a phosphatase enzyme that de phosphor y late s phosphotyrosine residues and serves as a crucial negative regulator of various signaling pathways, including insulin and leptin signaling. ,   In the insulin signaling pathway, PTP1B de phosphor y late s the insulin receptor (IR) and insulin receptor substrates (IRS) pro- teins. ,   Knockout (KO) mice lacking PTP1B have shown enhanced insulin sensitivity and improved glycemic con- trol. ,   Further studies have revealed that PTP1B deletion promotes insulin sensitivity in the liver by restoring IRS-1- mediated PI3K/Akt/FoxO1 phosphorylation and inhibiting gluconeogenic enzymes. ,   These findings make PTP1B an intriguing therapeutic target for the discovery of PTP1B modulators as insulin sensitizers in diabetes. To date, both industry and academic institutes have focused on developing small-molecule PTP1B inhibitors. − 17   A few of these inhibitors, including ertiprotafib, trodusquemine, and JTT- 551 ( Figure 1 ), have entered clinical trials. − 20   Unfortunately, all of them were discontinued due to poor selectivity, insufficient efficiency, and side effects.  

PTP1B is one of the nonreceptor protein tyrosine phosphatases, and its crystal structure was first reported in 1994.   Structurally, PTP1B consists of three domains ( Figure 2 ): an N-terminal catalytic domain (1 − 300), a regulatory domain (301 − 400) crucial for protein − protein interactions, and a C-terminal domain (401 − 435) that targets PTP1B to the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) membrane. ,   Trodusque- mine (MSI-1436), a small-molecule PTP1B inhibitor, primarily binds to the PTP1B C-terminal domain and exhibits excellent specificity and potency for PTP1B (IC 50  = 1  μ M). Proteolysis-targeting chimeras (PROTACs) represent a therapeutic technology that leverages the ubiquitin-proteasome system to degrade target proteins, offering potential solutions to overcome limitations associated with small-molecule inhibitors. − 33   PROTACs are bifunctional small molecules that possess the unique ability to simultaneously bind to both an E3 ubiquitin ligase and the target protein ( Figure 3 ). Subsequently, PROTACs undergo recycling to target addi- tional copies of the protein. Unlike conventional small- molecule inhibitors that rely on occupancy, PROTACs operate through an “event-driven” mechanism, directly reducing pathogenic proteins without inhibiting the functional activity

This specific degradation mechanism confers several advantages over traditional enzyme inhibitors, including lower drug doses, reduced administration frequency, and decreased development of drug resistance.   The first example of PROTAC comprising fragment-based PTP1B inhibitor and von Hippel-Lindau (VHL) E3 ligase ligand was recently reported for cancer immunotherapy.  

In our pursuit of developing hypoglycemic agents, our focus lies on PTP1B inhibitors as insulin sensitizers. However, discovering direct PTP1B inhibitors poses significant chal- lenges, primarily in terms of selectivity and bioavailability, and no small-molecule inhibitors have been successfully approved for market use thus far. To overcome this obstacle, we have adopted an interesting and promising strategy that involves utilizing the PROTACs approach for the discovery of PTP1B agents. This approach offers several advantages, including the ability to administer PROTACs at low doses and frequencies, leveraging their catalytic-type mechanism of action. Capitaliz- ing on these advantages, we have designed a series of PROTACs targeting PTP1B, aiming to develop long-term antitype 2 diabetes agents. In this study, we present the design, synthesis, and evaluation of these PROTACs both in vitro and in vivo.  

# RESULTS AND DISCUSSION  

In the design of PTP1B degraders, the identification of an appropriate coupling site for a PTP1B inhibitor and an E3 ligase ligand is crucial. For this purpose, we utilized three potent PTP1B inhibitors, namely, oleanolic acid, ursolic acid, and ZINC02765569, as PTP1B ligands within our PROTAC degraders.   To assess the binding interactions, we initially conducted molecular docking studies of oleanolic acid with the crystal structure of PTP1B (PDB: 1NL9). As depicted in Figure 4 , the rigid structure of the pentacyclic triterpenoids occupied the hydrophobic pocket of PTP1B. Key binding interactions were observed between 3-hydroxyl and 28- carbonyl groups of oleanolic acid with PTP1B. Interestingly, the hydroxyl group in the carboxyl tail region was located in close proximity to the solvent, making it a suitable site for attaching to an E3 ligase ligand without significantly affecting

Evaluation of PTP1B Degradation in HepG2 Cells.  We investigated a set of small PROTACs ( Table 1 ) designed to induce PTP1B degradation at concentrations of 5, 50, 500 nM, and 5  μ M in HepG2 cell line using Western blotting. To establish the modeling, HepG2 cells were treated with insulin for 48 h to upregulate the expression of PTP1B. Subsequently, the cells were treated with small PROTACs for an additional 48 h, and then, the activity of PTP1B degradation was determined. As listed in  Table 2 , all 25 compounds exhibited PTP1B degradation activity. Compounds  17 ,  18 ,  19 ,  20 ,  62 , and  66  effectively reduced the protein level of PTP1B at medium concentrations (50 and 500 nM), while compounds 68  and  69  showed effective reduction at low and medium concentrations. Additionally, compound  75  demonstrated excellent PTP1B degradation at all tested concentrations.

Next, we analyzed the structure − activity relationship of the compound series, focusing on the three PTP1B inhibitors moiety, and the linker structure and length associated with degradation efficiency. Among these PROTACs, compounds containing oleanolic acid or ursolic acid as inhibitors exhibited potent degradation activity, consistent with the inhibition activities of oleanolic acid, ursolic acid, and ZINC02765569. Moreover, the length and type of the linker were found to be crucial for the degradation potency of the compounds. We introduced straight-chain alkanes with or without oxygen atoms (polyethylene glycol fragment, PEG). Generally, PROTACs with oxygen atoms on the linker displayed better degradative potency (compounds  18 − 20 ,  62 ,  66 ,  68 ,  69 , and 75 ). Furthermore, a clear trend was observed, with compounds possessing longer linkers exhibiting increased degradation activity (compounds  68 ,  69 , and  75 ). However, more than half of the compounds showed the weakest reduction in the PTP1B protein level at the high concentration (5  μ M), likely due to the “hook” effect.   Consequently, we proceeded to test several highly potent degraders at a wide range of concentrations (1 − 1250 nM) to accurately explore their efficacy. Additionally, aiming to discover long-term hypoglycemic agents, we evaluated their degradation after 48 and 72 h of administration, respectively.  

We assessed five compounds at concentrations ranging from 1 to 1250 nM at two time points (48 and 72 h). Further Western blot analysis demonstrated that all five compounds effectively induced dose-dependent degradation of PTP1B at the tested concentrations of 1, 5, 25, 125, 625, and 1250 nM ( Figure 5  and  Table 3 ). Mechanism of Hypoglycemic Activity of Compound 75.  The IRS-1/PI3K/Akt signaling pathway plays an important role in insulin signal transduction, activation, and glucose metabolism.   In severe cases, it can cause abnormal glucose metabolism in the brain, induce cognitive impairment, and insulin resistance, among other diseases. After being activated in the form of phosphorylation in the body, IRS-1 interacts with downstream receptor proteins and can activate its downstream PI3K. PI3K further activates Akt, which can further activate downstream proteins, promote intracellular energy metabolism, increase glucose consumption in the body, promote fatty acid oxidation metabolism, and inhibit liver lipid synthesis. The activation of IRS-1 protein can also promote insulin activation, regulate the body’s gluconeogenesis process, and inhibit the conversion of nonsugar substances into glucose in the body.   As shown in  Figure 7 D, compound  75 significantly upregulated the expression of IRS-1, PI3K, and Akt phosphorylation in HepG2 cells after administration, suggesting that the hypoglycemic mechanism of  75  may be related to the regulation of the IRS-1/PI3K/Akt signaling pathway.  

Oral Glucose Tolerance Test of Potent Compounds in Mice.  Based on the promising PTP1B degradation potency of several compounds, we further launched the oral glucose tolerance test (oGTT) in KM mice. At the beginning, we evaluated compound  20  for the oGTT with 3 doses (10, 20, and 40 mg/kg) to optimize the suitable dosage because of its relatively simple production and promising degradation effect. The results are outlined in  Figure 8 A; compound  20  reduced the area under the curve of blood glucose from 0 to 120 min (AUC 0 − 120 min ) by 8.6, 14.8, and 21.8%, which revealed that compound  20  could show the hypoglycemic activity in a dose- dependent manner.  

Subsequently, compounds  18 ,  21 ,  68 , and  75 , which demonstrated strong degradation activity, were further investigated for oGTT at a dose of 40 mg/kg. The data showed that compounds  21  and  68 , with medium-length oxygen linkers (14 and 16 atoms), exhibited potent reduction in blood glucose levels (15.3 and 18.3%, respectively). Importantly, compound  75 , which contained a longer PEG fragment, displayed the most significant potency, with a 29% reduction in blood glucose AUC 0 − 2h  at a dose of 40 mg/kg ( Figure 8 B).

# CONCLUSIONS  

This study focused on the discovery, synthesis, and evaluation of a series of PROTACs targeting PTP1B, a type of phosphatase, using PROTAC strategy to treat PTP1B- mediated insulin sensitivity. All PROTAC molecules exhibited PTP1B-degrading activities within the concentration range of 5 nM to 5  μ M. Among the 25 PROTACs tested, compounds  18 , 20 ,  21 ,  62 ,  68 , and  75  showed promising PTP1B degradation effects. These five compounds could engage and inhibit PTP1B with a similar potency with original inhibitor oleanolic acid. Western blot data demonstrated that PROTAC molecules enhanced the degradation efficiency (DC 50 ) by nearly 50-fold compared to the inhibition activity (IC 50 ) of the PTP1B inhibitor. Compound  62 ,  68 , and  75 , which contained a flexible linker with PEG group, exhibited strong potency (DC 50 = 210 − 290 nM) in inducing PTP1B degradation after 48 h of incubation in HepG2 cells. Compounds  18 ,  68 , and  75 , with a longer oxygen linker, showed greater reduction in PTP1B levels after 72 h compared to 48 h, indicating the potential to develop long-term hypoglycemic drugs. Notably,  75  was verified a potent, selective, and authentic PROTAC targeting PTP1B. The oGTT conducted in KM mice using compounds 18 ,  20 ,  21 ,  68 , and  75  revealed their blood glucose reduction effects, with compound  20  showing dose-dependent activity and compound  75  exhibiting a 29% reduction in blood glucose AUC 0 − 2h  at a dose of 40 mg/kg. Importantly,  75  could upregulate the IRS-1/PI3K/Akt signaling pathway to enhance the hypoglycemic effect. As a result, our study highlights the potential discovery of long-term hypoglycemic agent based on the catalytic feature of the PROTAC-induced degradation. Further studies are ongoing including oral bioavailability and modification of PROTACs targeting PTP1B by employing more potent inhibitors and various linkers, and the results will be reported in due course.

Western Blot Analysis.  HepG2 cells were counted and plated in 96-well plates at a density of 8  ×  10 4   cells per well and treated with various concentrations of tested compounds. After 48 or 72 h of drug exposure, the cells were washed with PBS and the total protein was extracted using RIPA buffer (Solarbio Life Sciences, Beijing, China) containing protease and phosphatase inhibitors, followed by a 30 min incubation and 10 min centrifugation at 4  ° C. The protein concentrations were determined by BCA assay, and equal amounts of protein (30  μ g) were subjected to 7.5 or 12.5% SDS-PAGE and transferred to PVDF membranes. After nonspecific blocking with nonfat milk for 2 h at room temperature, the membranes were incubated with primary antibody overnight and with secondary antibody for 1 h at room temperature. The blots were then scanned using an infrared laser imaging system (Biored, Model: Universal hood).  

PTP1B Inhibitory Assay.  The enzyme inhibitory assay was performed by using  p -nitrophenyl phosphate ( p -NPP) as a substrate, which could be hydrolyzed by PTP1B to occur the change of color. The absorbance at 405 nm was detected to represent the enzyme activity. First, the buffer solution was prepared containing Tris − HCl

 (11 mM, pH 7.5), sodium chloride (55 mM), DTT (2.2 mM), MnCl 2

 (1.1 mM), EDTA (1.1 mM), and  p -NPP (33 mM). The assays were performed in 96-well plates by adding the given buffer (180  μ L) and various amount of inhibitors solution (100 mM in DMSO) at 37  ° C for 5 min and then enzyme (12.5  μ g/mL, 20  μ L). The absorbance at 405 nm was monitored by TECAN Infinite M200 Pro NanoQuant microplate reader during 30 min of incubation at 37  ° C. The absorbance without adding inhibitor was defined as  A 0 , while the absorbances at initiation reaction and 30 min were defined as  A 1  and A 2 . The experiments were carried out in triplicate, and then, the percentage of inhibition was calculated as [ A 0  − ( A 2  − A 1 )]/ A 0  × 100%.
