ABSTRACT:  The design of a functional electrolyte system that is compatible with the LiNi 0.8 Co 0.15 Al 0.05 O 2  (LNCA) cathode is of great importance for advanced lithium-ion batteries (LIBs). In this work, chelated lithium salts of lithium di ﬂ uoro(bisoxalato) phosphate (LiDFBOP) and lithium tetra ﬂ uoro(oxalate) phosphate (LTFOP) are synthesized by a facile and general method. Then, the complexes of LiDFBOP, LTFOP, and lithium di ﬂ uorophosphate (LiDFP), all of which have a central phosphorus atom, were selected as the salt-type additives for the LiPF 6 -based electrolyte to improve the electrochemical performances of LNCA/Li half- batteries, respectively. The results of electrochemical tests, quantum chemistry calculations, potential-resolved  in situ  electrochemical impedance (PRIs-EIS) measurements, and surface analyses show that the interface property and the battery performance are closely associated with molecular structures of phosphorus-centered complex additives. It indicates that LiDFP with the P  O bond can signi ﬁ cantly reduce the interfacial impedance of LNCA/Li half-batteries due to the increase of Li 3 PO 4  and the decrease of Li 2 CO 3  in the cathode electrolyte interface (CEI). While in LiDFBOP, according to the calculated vertical ionization potential (VIP), the two oxalate-chelated ligands bring about a bidirectional cross-linking reaction, which makes it preferential to be oxidized. This process is self-healing and can form a dense and stretched CEI, which is favorable to the cycle performance at the late stage. In contrast, the polymerization reaction will occur in one direction for LTFOP due to its lone oxalate ligand. Additionally, an unfavorable side reaction between LTFOP and EC has been proposed by the aid of Gibbs free energy calculation. This is a good explanation for the formation of the uneven and unstable CEI, as well as the continuous decomposition of the electrolyte in PRIs-EIS measurement. This work has an extensive applicability and practical signi ﬁ cance not only for molecular designing of novel lithium salts, but also for the construction of a functional electrolyte system that is compatible with di ﬀ erent electrode materials.  

KEYWORDS:  salt-type additives, cathode electrolyte interface, functional group, LiNi 0.8 Co 0.15 Al 0.05 O 2 , phosphorus-centered complex  

# 1. INTRODUCTION  

In order to make the batteries of electric vehicles or o ﬀ -grid energy storage systems have high energy density and long cycle life, advanced and improved cathode materials have been − 3   studied in depth. Among the many novel cathode materials, layered nickel-rich oxides (LNCA) are considered as one of the most promising candidates for new-generation lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) owing to their high capacities of 220 mAh g − 1 .   Hence, it has been commercialized by Panasonic and SAFT − 7   successfully. Although a large reversible capacity is successfully improved by increasing the Ni content, the cycling performance and thermal stabilities simultaneously decrease,  

, ,   which restrict its use in practical applications. Currently, there are four schools-of-thought to explain the decayed cyclic performance namely: (1) the content of the inert product, Li 2 CO 3 , generated by synthesis and electrochemical reaction at the interface is high, which leads to the loss of lithium and

(2) Ni 2+   occupies the Li position when lithium is removed from the positive electrode, and therefore, a part of Li +   is unable to re-intercalate into the original position in the cathode, resulting in structural deterioration and phase transition.   (3) The low amount of Li +   in LNCA makes the anion oxygen in the oxidizing lattice unstable and highly active, leading to the irreparable loss of oxygen and forms an oxygen- poor layer on the surface of oxide particles. (4) Microcracks occur in the LNCA lattice during a deep de-lithium state.  

Recently, a lot of e ﬀ ort has gone into ameliorating these problems in the  ﬁ eld of material synthesis and modi ﬁ cation, such as element doping and surface coating. Additionally, application of  ﬁ lm-forming electrolyte additives can also improve the cycle performance of LNCA. The additive should be oxidized prior to the lithium hexa ﬂ uorophosphate (LiPF 6 ) baseline electrolyte, generating a dense passivation  ﬁ lm on the LNCA/electrolyte interface to improve the interface stability by preventing the occurrence of side e ﬀ ects and the formation of inert products, and phosphorus-centered complex additives are one of the 7 , − 15   ﬂ additives applied As a result, lithium tetra uoro oxalate phosphate (LTFOP), with one oxalate-chelated ligand instead of two  ﬂ uorine atoms was utilized to weaken the action of ﬂ uorine atom.   The decrease of  ﬂ uorine atoms leads to a decreased oxidation potential, making it prior to the oxidation of LiPF 6 , according to the calculated vertical ionization potential (VIP). Lithium di ﬂ uoro(bisoxalato) phosphate (LiDFBOP) was obtained by replacing four  ﬂ uorine atoms with two oxalate- chelated ligands to further reduce the oxidation potential. Preliminary experiments found that LTFOP made little improvement in the cyclic performance, and LiDFBOP showed more favorable cycle performance at the late period. We postulate that the oxalate-chelated ligand has particular e ﬀ ects on the cathode electrolyte interface (CEI), and hence, another additive without the oxalate-chelated ligand, lithium di ﬂ uor-  

,   ophosphate (LiDFP), was adopted to further express the e ﬀ ect of the oxalate-chelated ligand.  

Herein, the cycle performance of LNCA/Li half-batteries with LTFOP, LiDFBOP, and LiDFP was investigated. To further investigate the e ﬀ ects of these additives with the central phosphorus atom on the interface of LNCA materials, interface components and decomposition reactions were analyzed. It is found that the capacity retention ratio of the LNCA/Li half- battery was improved from 69.4 to 88.6% after the 100th cycle by LiDFP. The addition of LiDFBOP reduced the initial capacity of the cells for its decomposition. But the capacity retention was improved to 93.6% after the 100th cycle. However, the addition of LTFOP was not bene ﬁ cial to LNCA, but was detrimental. From the results of electrochemical tests, quantum chemistry calculations, potential-resolved  in situ electrochemical impedance (PRIs-EIS) measurements, and surface analyses, it is concluded that the interface property and the battery performance are closely associated with the molecular structures of the phosphorus-centered complex additives.  

# 2. EXPERIMENTAL SECTION  

2.1. Preparation of LiDFBOP and LTFOP.  All reagents were purchased from standard suppliers (Chaoyang Guangda Chemical Co. Ltd.) and were used without further puri ﬁ cation. All reactions were performed in round-bottom  ﬂ asks (capacity of either1000 or 250 mL). 19 F NMR and  13 C NMR spectra were recorded in DMSO- d 6  on Bruker AVANCE Neo-300 at 500 MHz. All chemical shifts are given on the  δ scale in ppm.  

2.2. Electrolytes and Electrodes.  The baseline electrolyte (STD), a mixed solvent of ethylene carbonate (EC) and diethyl carbonate (DEC) at a volume ratio of 1:1 with 1 M LiPF 6  dissolved in, was purchased from Guangdong Zhongke Tianyuan New Energy Technology Co. Ltd. The additive-containing electrolytes were prepared by introducing 1 wt % of LiDFBOP (synthesized by our laboratory), LTFOP (synthesized by our laboratory), and LiDFP (purchased from Huizhou City Avenue New Materials Technology Co. Ltd.) to the baseline electrolyte, respectively. The cathode electrode was prepared by coating a slurry on the aluminum foil, containing 80 wt % LNCA (purchased from Ningbo Ronbay New Energy Technology Co. Ltd.), 10 wt % carbon black and 10 wt % polyvinylidene  ﬂ uoride (PVDF). A polyethylene membrane was employed as a separator.  

2.3. Theoretical Calculations.  The VIP and vertical electron a ﬃ nity (VEA) of LiPF 6  and additive molecules, corresponding to the oxidative and reductive properties of the molecules were calculated at the B3LYP/6-31 G(p,d) in advance to design the electrolyte composition. Gibbs free energy calculations were performed by the density functional theory method at the B3LYP/6-311 + G (2dp, 2df). All calculations were performed using a Gaussian 16 program package.  

2.4. Electrochemical Measurements.  The half-cells (2025 coin- type) were assembled in an argon- ﬁ lled glovebox (with moisture and oxygen contents controlled below 1 ppm) using the above prepared cathode and electrolytes. A coin-type half-cell based on a LNCA cathode was cycled in the potential window between 2.7 and 4.3 V using a computer-controlled battery measurement system (Land cell tester CT2001A) at room temperature. Precycling and subsequent cycling were performed at C/20 and C/1.  

All the following tests were performed on a CHI660E electro- chemical workstation with a three-electrode system, which used the LNCA electrode as the working electrode, and lithium sheets as both the reference and counter electrodes, respectively. Linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) for the electrolytes was carried out at a scan rate of 2 mV s − 1   in the voltage range of 2.7 − 7.0 V. EIS tests of cells with two electrolytes were conducted at the fully delithiated state of 4.3 V at di ﬀ erent cycling numbers over the frequency range of 100 kHz - 10 MHz.  

2.5. Characterization.  Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images were measured on an S-4800 (Hitachi, Japan) microscope, operating at 15 kV. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images were captured on a Tecnai F30 (PhilipFEI, Netherlands) apparatus, operating at 300 kV. The samples were placed in ethyl alcohol and ultrasonically treated for 10 min to obtain a homogeneous dispersion. Two drops of the solution were then placed on a standard copper mesh. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) measurements were equipped with a Quantum 2000 ESCA spectrometer (Physical Electronics, USA) with monochromatic Al K α  radiation ( h ν  = 1486.6 eV), operating at 23.2 W and in a vacuum of <10 − 8   Torr. The dismantled electrodes were then transferred into the XPS vacuum. Attenuated total re ﬂ ectance- Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (IR-ATR, Nexus 670 FT-IR, America Nicolet) was performed in the frequency range of 900 − 4000 cm − 1 . Raman spectra of the mixed solvent and the solvent containing LiDFP were carried out on a LabRAM HR Evolution (French Horiba Jobin Yvon S.A.S.).

3.3. The Interface Characterization.  The lithium salt additives with di ﬀ erent anions showed completely di ﬀ erent electrochemical properties, which has been con ﬁ rmed above. Through previous studies, we suspect that di ﬀ erent lithium anions will lead to di ﬀ erent decomposition reactions.   To demonstrate the point, we investigated the decomposition paths of three additives in the oxidation reaction on the surface of LNCA.  

Figure 6  shows three oxidation paths for LiDFBOP in LNCA/ Li half-cells. No matter which oxidation path is involved, the  ﬁ rst step is the electron loss for LiDFBOP during potential rise, and then, oxalate phosphate free radicals containing only a ring of monocyclic are derived from a chelated compound phosphate ring opening, accompanied by the release of 2 equiv of CO 2 . As shown in path I in  Figure 6 , the monocyclic phosphate-free radical can react with another LiDFBOP molecule itself to form semicarbonate species on the surface of LNCA. In this process, the LiDFBOP molecule may undergo a longer chain reaction releasing more CO 2 , but two oxalate-chelated ligands are still retained. Moreover, as shown in paths II and III, the monocyclic phosphate radical can also react with the EC molecule to form less and more reactive radical intermediates, respectively. Through further polymerization reaction, polymerization products with long chains are generated, which would attach to the surface of LNCA and form a CEI with a large volume due to continuous accumulation. This makes the cycle performance of electrolyte containing LiDFOP unstable in the early process, but stable in the later stages because of the gradual formation of a more compact protective CEI. In order to verify the agreement between the theoretical calculation and the actual situation, the interface components were analyzed, and the LNCA electrode plates were tested by XPS and IR-ATR spectroscopy.  

As displayed in  Figure 8 , the peaks of C − F ( ∼ 290.7 eV), C  O ( ∼ 289.8 eV), C − O, C − H ( ∼ 285.5 eV), and C − C ( ∼ 284.8 eV) can be observed on the surface of LNCA. The C − C was attributed to the conductive carbon, and the C − H and C − F correspond to the presence of the PVDF binder in the positive electrode, respectively. In the O 1s spectrum, there are three ﬁ tted peaks, associated with M − O ( ∼ 532.1 eV), C  O ( ∼ 533.0 eV), and C − O ( ∼ 534.4 eV) on the surface of LNCA. From XPS of the C 1 s spectrum and O 1 s spectrum of the positive electrode, it is found that less PVDF and a large number of Li 2 CO 3  were detected on LNCA cycled in STD, indicating that the CEI  ﬁ lm is formed and covered the surface of LNCA. However, the intensity of PVDF was weaker, and less Li 2 CO 3 was detected on the surface of LNCA cycled in LiDFP- containing electrolyte as compared with that of STD and fresh LNCA, which is in good agreement with the  eq 5 : LiDFP can react with Li 2 CO 3  on the surface of LNCA.  

For the F 1s spectrum, there are peaks of C − F ( ∼ 688.3 eV), corresponding to PVDF, LiPO x F y  ( ∼ 687.6 eV), and LiF ( ∼ 685.2 eV), respectively, on the surface of LNCA. According to P 2p, the peaks located at 136.9 eV and 134.7 eV were attributed to Li x PF y  and LiPO x F y , respectively.   By comparison, Li 3 PO 4  ( ∼ 133.6 eV) exists on the electrode cycled in electrolyte- containing LiDFP but does not exist on others, which means the reactions mentioned in  eqs 1 − 5  have occurred. It is known from previous reports that Li 3 PO 4  can inhibit the decomposition of LiPF 6  due to its common-ion e ﬀ ect, and thus, the electrolyte added with LiDFP shows good cycling performance and rate performance.   However, there are less Li x PF y  and LiPO x F y  on LNCA cycled in electrolyte containing LiDFBOP, which is consistent with the decomposition path, as shown in  Figure 6 . The decomposed product of LiDFBOP contains a long carbon chain, which is continuously decomposed to form a uniform protective  ﬁ lm with increasing cycle time.   Thus, the direct contact between material particles and electrolyte is prevented, and the decomposition of LiPF 6  is reduced. To sum up, there are two reasons for the excellent performance for the electrolyte with LiDFP: (1) LiDFP decomposes to produce Li 3 PO 4 , which can inhibit the decomposition of LiPF 6  and slow down the failure of electrolyte, thus improving the cycle and rate performance; (2) PO 2 F, a decomposition product of LiDFP, will react with inert Li 2 CO 3  on the surface of LNCA to generate more favorable products, thus reducing the production of inert products on the material surface and the impedance, which are consistent with the phenomena described in  Figures S4  and  5 . For LiDFBOP, the cycling and rate performance of the half- battery with LiDFBOP are poor at the initial stage of the cycle because the decomposition reaction of LiDFBOP has to go through a long process. In the later stage of the cycle, LiDFBOP is decomposed to form a long-chain polymerization product,

# 4. CONCLUSIONS  

We compared the di ﬀ erent performances of LiDFP, LiDFBOP, and LTFOP with di ﬀ erent functional groups and observed that the number of F atoms and oxalate would a ﬀ ect the performance of LNCA/Li half-cells dramatically. Here are the main conclusions:  

1. LiDFP can greatly improve the cycle performance of LNCA/Li half-cells for the following reasons: during decom- position of LiDFP, the formation of the inert component Li 2 CO 3  on the LNCA surface is suppressed, e ﬀ ectively reducing the impedance; the formation of CEI containing Li 3 PO 4  inhibits LiPF 6  decomposition. It shows that LiDFP is more favorable in LNCA/Li half-batteries.  

2. The addition of LiDFBOP shows high and stable capacity in the later cycle stage. Additionally, LiDFBOP can obviously improve the rate capacity in 5 C, because the decomposition products are polymer macromolecules, with a long carbon chain. The  ﬁ lm formation is unstable in the early stage and tends to be stable after 100 cycles. It can be seen that LiDFBOP has good ﬁ lm-forming performance and can be applied in low temper- atures, high voltage, and other  ﬁ elds in future research.  

3. Although LiDFBOP and LTFOP have similar structures, it is the di ﬀ erence in the number of oxalate and  ﬂ uorine atoms that leads to the di ﬀ erent reactions and performances, which can be concluded from the calculated Gibbs free energy.  

Generally, we demonstrated that the number of  ﬂ uorine atoms and oxalate-chelated ligands in additives will a ﬀ ect the properties of the additives themselves. Moreover, the di ﬀ erent functional groups will lead to di ﬀ erent decomposition reaction of the additives and even a ﬀ ect the overall performance of the added batteries. Comparing LiDFBOP and LiDFP, P  O can promote the formation of Li 3 PO 4  and reduce the content of Li 2 CO 3  in CEI. Oxalate-chelated ligands make LiDFBOP to form polymerization products with a long chain. In contrast, LTFOP with four F atoms but one oxalate-chelated ligand shows poor polymerization reaction. This work applies the complexes of LiDFBOP, LTFOP, and LiDFP to improve the electro- chemical performances of LNCA/Li half- batteries, all of which have the central phosphorus atom. This method provides a new approach to research the e ﬀ ects of functional groups in additives on LIBs and can be extended for research about other functional groups.
