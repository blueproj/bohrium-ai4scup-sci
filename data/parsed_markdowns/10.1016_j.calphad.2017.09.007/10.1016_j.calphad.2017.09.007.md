# A B S T R A C T  

# A R T I C L E I N F O  

The Al-Co-W system and its binary sub-systems Al-Co, Al-W and Co-W were critically reviewed. The thermo- dynamic description of the Al-Co-W system including all three binaries was developed considering thermo- dynamic and constitutional data of the systems. Results from density functional theory calculations were em- ployed to improve reliability of the descriptions. The Gibbs energy for the thermal vacancy (G Va ) in the BCC_A2 phase is discussed. The revised descriptions obtained for the Al-W and Co-W systems describe the thermo- dynamic and phase equilibrium data well and are free of undesired artefacts for temperatures below 6000 K. The ordered  γ ’  phase of the Al-Co-W system is described as a metastable phase in the entire temperature range. The calculated Gibbs energy of the  γ ’  is only slightly above that of the equilibrium state, which indicates that there is good possibility of stabilizing the  γ ’  phase with the addition of  γ ’ -stabilizing elements, such as Ti, Ta, Hf, Nb and Ni.

# 1. Introduction  

Superalloys are used in aircraft engines, turbo superchargers and chemical plants that require high performance at elevated temperatures [1 – 3] . The most widely used superalloys are Ni-based alloys which are strengthened by  γ ’  phase (L1 2 , AlNi 3 ) while traditional Co-based alloys, lacking this phase, have limited applications due to their lower high- temperature strength. The e ﬃ ciency of gas turbine cycles increases with increasing turbine inlet temperature, thus continuous e ﬀ orts are made to increase the  γ ’  solvus temperature of Ni-based alloys, such as the addition of  γ ’  stabilizing elements. Although these Ni-based super- alloys have a very high ratio of absolute working temperature to melting temperature, the higher melting point of Co compared to Ni makes Co-based alloys attractive candidates for high temperature ap- plications. In addition, Co-based alloys are receiving more attention because of the potential for superior hot corrosion resistance, better weldability and thermal fatigue resistance compared to Ni-based su- peralloys  [4] . The key for the application of Co-based alloys as super- alloys is improving their high-temperature strength. Already in 1971, Lee  [5]  found that a Co-0.04 Al-0.25 W alloy (mass fraction) with or- dered  γ ’  phase (L1 2 ) precipitates in a  γ  phase matrix (FCC_A1) exhibited precipitation-hardened characteristics. # 2. Literature review  

# 2.1. Al-Co system  

Several thermodynamic descriptions are available for the Al-Co system  [16,20,39] . Of these descriptions, the description by Stein et al. [20]  reproduces the available experimental data best. For the re-opti- mization of the description, Stein et al. used all available experimental data from the literature and their own data. Although their calculations agree well with the experimental data sets, an inverse miscibility gap, as shown in  Fig. 1 , occurs above 4800 K. Due to the relatively high melting point of the refractory element W (3695 K), the presence of an inverse miscibility gap at a temperature of 4800 K could interfere with obtaining a reliable description for the Al-Co-W system  [40] . Although Al evaporates at 2791 K it is imperative for systems containing re- fractory elements to have an artifact-free description of the liquid phase even for temperatures that are signi ﬁ cantly higher than their melting points. Thus, it was considered necessary to eliminate the occurrence of this inverse miscibility gap in the Al-Co system and to re-assess the thermodynamic description of this system.  

McAlister  [41]  performed a comprehensive review of the experi- mental data for the Al-Co system that were available in 1985. Dupin and Ansara  [16]  also reviewed the data that were available in 1997.

Kimura et al.  [44]  prepared alloys with 0.4 Co to 0.98 Co using arc melting. The alloys were annealed at 1273 K for seven days or at 1423 K for two days. The phase equilibria of the AlCo (BCC_B2) and ( α Co) (FCC_A1; also named  γ  in superalloys) phases were measured for an- nealed alloys using a scanning electron microscope (SEM) equipped with an energy dispersive X-ray spectrometer (EDS). Kimura et al.  [44] also measured the solidus and liquidus for alloys with 0.63 Co to 0.98 Co using di ﬀ erential thermal analysis (DTA) with a heating rate of 10 K/min.  

Dmitrieva et al.  [45]  used DTA with a heating rate of 60 K/min to study alloys with more than 0.65 Co. Some of the experimental data for the Al-Co system from this work show large deviations (> 40 K) from the accepted Al-Co phase diagram. Considering that the heating rate of 60 K/min for DTA is relatively high for the temperature determination of phase transformations in metals, the experimental data from Dmi- trieva et al. were not used in the present work. This also indicates that the experimental data from Dmitrieva et al. for the ternary Al-Co-W system may have low accuracy.  

Stein et al.  [20]  studied the homogeneity range and the melting behavior of the AlCo (BCC_B2) phase. The alloys were prepared by arc melting and annealing at 1173 K for three weeks or 1373 K for one week. The compositions of the phases in the annealed alloys were de- termined using electron-probe microanalysis (EPMA). The solidus, li- quidus and temperature for the invariant reaction L + AlCo  ↔ Al 5 Co 2 were measured using DTA with a heating rate of 5 K/min.  

The Al-rich part of the Al-Co system has been subject to several investigations. Originally, Gödecke  [46]  reported only two inter- metallic compounds, Al 13 Co 4  and Al 3 Co, near the composition of 0.25 Co. Gödecke and Ellner  [43]  investigated the phase equilibria of the Al- Co system in the composition range with 0.04 Co to 0.28 Co and found a number of intermetallic compounds. The liquidus and invariant reac- tions were determined using DTA with heating/cooling rates between 2 K/min to 10 K/min. Strong supercooling was observed during the DTA cooling experiments. Combining the experimental results from DTA, magnetic susceptibility versus temperature analysis (MTA), X-ray di ﬀ raction (XRD), optical microscopy, Gödecke and Ellner  [43]  found that instead of only one stoichiometric compound Al 13 Co 4  (0.235 Co), three variants of this phase, o-Al 13 Co 4  (orthorhombic,  Pmn 2 1 ), m- Al 13 Co 4  (monoclinic, C2/m), y-Al 13 Co 4  (monoclinic, C2/m), exist in the composition range from 0.24 Co to 0.25 Co. The composition and temperature range of the three Al 13 Co 4  phases obtained by Gödecke and Ellner are summarized in  Table 2 .  

Grushko et al.  [47]  investigated the Al-Co system in the composition range in 0.192 Co to 0.286 Co. The annealed alloys were analyzed using XRD, SEM-EDS and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and DTA (heating rate 20 K/min). They also found the three variants of the Al 13 Co 4  phases but with di ﬀ erent compositions compared to the results of Gödecke and Ellner  [43] , as shown in  Table 2 . The data from Grushko et al. were not used in the present assessment, considering the following three facts: (1) Grushko et al. prepared only two alloys (0.241 Co, 0.245 Co) in the Al 13 Co 4  phase range while many more alloys were prepared by Gödecke and Ellner; (2) for the determination of the phase compositions, Grushko et al. used EDS, which is semi-quantitative for this narrow composition range; (3) Grushko et al. used a fairly high DTA heating rate of 20 K/min compared to Gödecke and Ellner. How- ever, one important experimental result was that XRD and microscopy of samples annealed at 1073 K for 1580 h showed that the m-Al 13 Co 4 phase may not be stable at this temperature. Thus, m-Al 13 Co 4  phase could also be a high temperature form or a metastable phase.  

Priputen et al.  [48]  investigated the Al-Co system in the composi- tion range with 0.23 Co to 0.29 Co using di ﬀ erential scanning calori- metry (DSC), XRD, SEM-EDS and electron backscattered di ﬀ raction (EBSD). The invariant reaction temperatures determined by this work are consistent with those from Gödecke and Ellner  [43] . The crystal structures obtained from the XRD results for the three Al 13 Co 4  phases are: o-Al 13 Co 4  (orthorhombic, P nma , called o ′ -Al 13 Co 4  in the original paper  [48] ), m-Al 13 Co 4  (monoclinic C2/m), y-Al 13 Co 4  (monoclinic, C2/ m). The ranges of the three Al 13 Co 4  phases were determined by EDS, EBSD and DTA are listed in  Table 2 . Although the crystal structure of the o-Al 13 Co 4  (orthorhombic, P nma ) determined by Priputen et al. is slightly di ﬀ erent from the one determined by Gödecke and Ellner (or- thorhombic, P mn 2 1 ), the homogeneity ranges for the o-Al 13 Co 4 , m- Al 13 Co 4 , y-Al 13 Co 4  are consistent with each other.  

Although it appears that there are signi ﬁ cant di ﬀ erences between the results from Gödecke and Ellner, Grushko et al. and Priputen et al. it must be noted that the composition range in which theses phases occur is very narrow (at most 0.237 – 0.257 Co) and that their crystal struc- tures, decagonal quasicrystal approximants, are closely related. First principles calculations from Mihalkovi č  and Widom  [49]  show that the 0 K energies of formation of these and similar hypothetical structures are very close but, except for one structure which is slightly below the convex hull. For the present work, it was decided to introduce the de- scription of three phases, o-Al 13 Co 4  (0.24 Co), y-Al 13 Co 4  (0.245 Co), m- Al 13 Co 4  (0.245 Co) in addition to Al 3 Co (0.255 Co), to represent the phases (Al 13 Co 4  phase bundle) and invariant reactions observed in this regime of the phase diagram.

# 2.3. Co-W system  

The most recent thermodynamic assessments of the Co-W system were carried out by Markström et al.  [67]  and Sato et al.  [19] . The phase diagram calculated from the description of Markström et al. shows relatively large deviations from the experimental data, especially for the µ phase. Sato et al. also optimized the description of the Co-W system considering all phase equilibrium data. However, the calculated enthalpies of formation di ﬀ er signi ﬁ cantly from those from DFT cal- culations suggesting that the Gibbs energy values of the entire Co-W system from this description may be unrealistic. Therefore, the ther- modynamic description of this system also needed to be re-assessed.

Neumeier  [71]  used dilatometry with a heating rate of 0.5 K/min to determine the peritectoid reaction ( α Co) + µ  ↔ Co 3 W. The reaction temperature was determined to be 1666 K and was veri ﬁ ed by micro- scopy and XRD at temperatures above and below 1666 K. Hashimoto et al.  [42]  observed a transformation around 1333 K using a di ﬀ erential dilatometer in the same composition regime. However, microscopy showed that the samples were in ( ε Co) + µ two phase region and ac- cording to the currently accepted phase diagram these two phases cannot be in equilibrium. Thus, this temperature was not considered in the present assessment.  

On the W-rich side of the system only data regarding the solid so- lubility of Co in (W) are available. Neumeier et al.  [71]  determined the solubility of Co in (W) at the eutectic temperature using XRD data. However, the precision of the XRD method for the very dilute solutions is questionable. In fact, Neumeier et al. labeled the values for the so- lubility of Co in solid (W) as approximate. Sato et al.  [19]  measured the solubility of Co in (W) in µ+(W) two phase alloys by EDS. However, microscopy showed that  ﬁ ne µ phase particles precipitated along the (W) grains indicating that the (W) phase compositions may not be ac- curate. This also suggests that the W-rich alloy  [19]  may not be in equilibrium raising doubts whether the compositions reported for the µ phase in (W)+µ two phase alloys correspond to equilibrium values. # 4.1. Thermal vacancy in the BCC_A2 phase  

Deviations from ideal stoichiometry in many ordered BCC_B2 phases, such as the AlCo phase, are result of substitution of Al by Co atoms on the Co-rich side of the ideal stoichiometry while on the Al-rich side, Al atoms are replaced by vacancies, Va  [104] . This behavior was re ﬂ ected by the (Al,Co) 1 (Co,Va) 1  model description in early assess- ments of the Al-Co system. To take into account the crystallographic equivalence of the sites in this structure a (Al,Co,Va) 1 (Al,Co,Va) 1  model description must be used. If this model is used with the order/disorder formalism vacancies are also introduced in the disordered phase, re- presenting thermal vacancies in this phase. As a result, an end-member phase that contains only vacancies must be described. In many assess- ments, the Gibbs energy of this end-member,  G Va , was set to 0. How- ever, recent studies by Dinsdale et al.  [105]  and Franke  [106]  de- monstrated that setting  G Va  to 0 could dramatically stabilize the BCC_A2 phase, which may result in incorrect phase equilibria. For ex- ample, setting  G Va  to 0, raises the melting point of Cr by 8 – 2188 K.  

The current consensus within the CALPHAD community is that a positive value should be assigned to  G Va . Dinsdale et al.  [105]  discussed the e ﬀ ects of various values for the = ⋅  G Va  on the total Gibbs energy and J ⋅ suggested to use G Va 30 T . The same value is also suggested by mol K Lukas et al.  [107] − . The Al-Co system was re ﬁ ned based on the thermodynamic assess- ment of Stein et al.  [20] . The inverse miscibility gap at about 4800 K was successfully eliminated in the present work, while the phase equilibria data and thermodynamic data were well described. The current work shows improvements for the description of the Al-rich liquidus compared to previous work  [20] . One remaining problem for the Al-Co system is that, due to the observed supercooling, the ex- perimental liquidus of the Al-Co system is not well determined.  

For the description of the ordered AlCo phase with BCC_B2 structure using the order/disorder formalism, it is necessary to introduce thermal vacancies into the description of the disordered BCC_A2 phase. The thermodynamic description of these vacancies is an important but often ignored issue. Instead of using a value of 0 that has been widely used in previous assessments, a positive temperature dependent parameter was used to describe the Gibbs energy of the end-member phase consisting only of vacancies. In addition, the interaction parameters between elements Al, Co and W and vacancy were assigned large positive, temperature independent values.
