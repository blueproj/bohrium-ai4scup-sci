# Correlation between ultrasonic velocity and indentation-based mechanical properties with microstructure in Nimonic 263  

G.V.S. Murthy, Sabita Ghosh   ∗ , Mousumi Das, G. Das, R.N. Ghosh  

Materials Science and Technology Division, National Metallurgical Laboratory, Jamshedpur 831007, India Received 14 August 2007; received in revised form 7 November 2007; accepted 7 November 2007  

# Abstract  

Microstructure of a crystalline material is a key factor in its technological applications as it determines a wide variety of properties including mechanical strength, toughness, corrosion resistance and hardness. Precipitation takes place during processing and on prolonged exposure to service at elevated temperatures. Consequently, knowledge about the precipitation phases and their inﬂuence on the various properties is of fundamental interest not only for its intrinsic study, but also for its technological signiﬁcance. The purpose of the present study is to explore the possibility of using ultrasonic velocity measurements and ball indentation technique (BIT) for investigating and correlating them for a complete characterization of the precipitation behavior in a nickel-based super alloy Nimonic 263. The present investigation reveals that both these techniques can be used for monitoring the strengthening process as a result of the precipitation on ageing at different times and temperatures.

# 1. Introduction  

Nimonic 263 is a wrought Ni-based super alloy, which has found applications in gas turbines because of its attractive creep strength and good oxidation resistance. It was developed and introduced by Rolls Royce in 1960. It was primarily intended as a material having the weld ductility and fabrication character- istics of Nimonic 75 combined with the creep-rupture strength of Nimonic 80A  [1,2] . The alloy can be age-hardened by con- trolled precipitation of intragranular submicroscopic   ′ , which contributes to creep resistance by acting as a barrier to disloca- tion movement. The presence of carbon leads to the formation of a series of carbide phases: (a) intragranularly occurring pri- mary carbides, nitrides or carbo-nitrides of the general form M(C, N) where M is usually titanium and (b) chromium-rich grain boundary carbides like M 7 C 3  and M 23 C 6  [3,4] . Nimonic 263 is designed primarily for use in the stationary components like combustion chamber, casing, liner, exhaust ducting, bear- ing housing and many others. These components are fabricated from plate/sheet of this alloy. The formability and weldability of the alloy hence become an important factor for the fabrication of these components. Controlled precipitation of the   ′   phase greatly enhances the strength of the Ni-based super alloys.  

In the optimum heat-treated condition, the microstructure of the Nimonic 263 alloy shows a ﬁne discontinuous precipita- tion of carbides at the grain boundaries and only a few particles of   ′   in the matrix. The shape and size of   ′   precipitates can- not be resolved under optical microscope. However, electron microscopic studies show that the mean particle diameter is about 18–20 nm  [2] . The   ′   phase that forms is metastable in nature because of the high titanium to aluminium ratio, the stable precipitate being    (Ni 3 Ti). On prolonged exposure at temper- atures in excess of 1073 K, the   ′   phase gradually coarsens and acicular    begins to form at the grain boundaries  [5] . The primary objective of solution treatment is to dissolve the pre- cipitated phases, mainly   ′   and carbide precipitates, prior to their controlled precipitation during ageing. Ageing treatments are primarily concerned with the precipitation of the hardening phase, normally   ′   in a form to obtain the required mechani- cal properties. The response to the ageing treatment depends on time and temperature used for the heat treatment.  

The ultrasonic technique has been extensively used for char- acterization of the microstructures and assessment of the defects and evaluation of material properties. The use of ultrasonic measurement during fabrication and heat treatment ensures the absence of unacceptable discontinuities and the presence of desired microstructures with acceptable properties. Ultrasonic in-service inspection is carried out to detect any unaccept- able degradation in microstructure and formation or extension of defects during operation of a component  [6] . Ultrasonic velocity (UV) measurements have been correlated with the microstructural features evolved during solutionising  [7]  and heat treatments in ferritic steels, super alloys  [8,9] , aluminium alloys  [10]  and many other materials. In the Nimonic alloy PE 16, a correlation has been established between the ultrasonic shear and longitudinal velocities with changes in the density and elastic modulus resulting from the occurrence of secondary phases such as   ′   [9] . The velocity has been shown to increase linearly with the increase in the volume fraction of the   ′   precipi- tates in this alloy  [11] . The UV depends on the Young’s modulus and density of the material  [12] , whereas the mechanical proper- ties, such as yield strength (YS), ultimate tensile strength (UTS) depends on the degree of coherency, ﬁneness and distribution of the precipitates  [13] .  

Among the various small specimen techniques to determine mechanical properties of materials  [14] , ball indentation tech- nique (BIT) plays an important role. The BIT has some unique features and it has certain advantages over the conventional mechanical test  [15] . This technique requires small amount of test materials. A test coupon of the order of 5 mm in length, 5 mm in width and uniform thickness in the range of 2–5 mm is sufﬁcient for BIT. To characterize a newly developed alloy, this technique is very useful. It avoids fabrication of compli- cated test specimen as required in conventional test. BI test is localized and covers a small area so that spatial variations in properties can be determined, such as that of weld and heat affected zone on the same specimen. It is relatively rapid com- pared to conventional test. It can be extended and employed to actual components and on-site, making possible in situ evalu- ation of degradation in mechanical properties due to long time service exposure. Reproducibility of the experimental results and their accuracy are comparable. Tests can be conducted at various strain rates and varying test temperatures, both low and high  [16–18] .  

BIT has been developed initially to meet the need of evaluat- ing mechanical properties of nuclear irradiated materials. Later, this method was found to be equally useful in assessing the health of a used component or where a limited amount of material is available. Many groups  [14–18]  of researchers are engaged in characterizing different materials under various conditions (heat treatment, cold working, hydrogen exposing, etc.) by BIT. Hag- gag  [16]  investigated the effect of neutron irradiation on the steel reactor vessel for the modular high-temperature gas-cooled reactor. Haggag and Murty  [19]  conducted several automated ball indentation tests on nuclear pressure vessel steels in the unirradiated, neutron irradiated and post-irradiated thermally annealed condition using strain-controlled multiple indentations at a single penetration location. Using the BIT, Mathew et al. [20]  investigated alloy 625 (nickel base super alloy) tube, which was removed after failure from an ammonia cracker plant after 60,000 h of service at a temperature of 993 K. The tube was re- solution annealed at 1433 K for 1 h, and ageing was carried at six different temperatures between 873 and 1173 K for 500 h each.

They found that ﬂow stress increased in the temperature range between 873 and 973 K, and that was attributed to precipita- tion of the   ′′   phase. In the temperature range 973–1173 K, ﬂow stress decreased with ageing temperature due to transformation of the   ′′   phase into stable    phase.  

For any particular alloy to validate the use of any technique which characterizes its behavior in service, a judicious selec- tion of the alloy heat treatments is required to be planned. This enables to unfold for example, the inﬂuence of the precipitate on the strength and such other measurable parameters like yield strength and ultrasonic velocity for a comprehensive understand- ing and utilization of the established correlation for practical applications. Such a correlation would aid in non-destructive and/or minimally invasive technique for monitoring the compo- nents as they are being formed as well after being put in service. This is the objective of the present study.

This is attributed to the fact that the UV depends on the elastic moduli of the material, which are very sensitive to any compositional changes, whereas hardness is inﬂuenced by the precipitation of coherent phases that hinders the movement of dislocations  [23] .

The process of precipitation of intermetallic phases upon ageing the solution annealed alloy can be divided into two stages: (I) incubation period, when the excess solute atoms tend to migrate to certain crystallographic planes, forming clus- ters or embryos of the precipitate and (II) precipitation, when these clusters form an intermediate crystal structure or transi- tional lattice, maintaining registry (coherency) with the lattice structure of the matrix. This phase will have lattice parameters different from those of the solvent, and as a result of the atom matching (coherency) there will be considerable distortion of the matrix. This distortion extends over a distance larger than the size of a discrete (precipitate) particle. It is this distortion that interferes with the movement of dislocations and accounts for the increase in hardness and strength during ageing. The ﬁrst stage of the precipitation (i.e. incubation) does not inﬂuence the hardness; however, it drastically inﬂuences the elastic moduli of the material and in-turn the UV. Hence, the response is faster for UV and it also saturates before the hardness, during ageing of the solution-annealed alloy. Further, as the coarsening of   ′ phase and its conversion to acicular    phase do not change the matrix composition, they do not inﬂuence the ultrasonic veloci- ties. # 4. Conclusions  

In the present study, the precipitation process in a Ni-based super alloy Nimonic 263 has been studied using UV and BIT. It has been observed that Ni 263 undergoes signiﬁcant microstruc- tural changes on ageing at 1073 K. For ageing time up to 50 h the formation of   ′   phase is observed leading to the strengthening of the alloy. This is shown by the increase in the UV and the cor- responding mechanical properties like UTS and YS which were obtained from the BIT. From the present study, it is also seen that that UV measurements are more sensitive to the initial stages of the precipitation, whereas the inﬂuence of precipitation on hard- ness can be felt only after the precipitates attain a minimum size to inﬂuence the movement of dislocations. These observations are corroborated with the electron microscopy studies. Further, ultrasonic shear wave velocity is found to be more sensitive than longitudinal wave velocity in assessment of precipitation of intermetallic phases. It is also evident from this study and also from the literature that both UV measurements and the BIT can be used in a complimentary way for the microstructural assess- ment in Nimonic 263 on ageing. Such measurements would aid in non-destructive and/or minimally invasive technique for mon- itoring the components as they are being formed as well after being put in service.
