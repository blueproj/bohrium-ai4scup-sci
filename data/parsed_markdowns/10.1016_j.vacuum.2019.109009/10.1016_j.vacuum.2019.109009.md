# Crystallographic texture and mechanical properties by electron beam freeform  fabrication of copper/steel gradient composite materials  

Guoqing Chen\*, Xi Shu, Junpeng Liu, Binggang Zhang and Jicai Feng  State Key Laboratory of Advanced Welding and Joining, Harbin Institute of Technology, Harbin 150001, China  

Abstract :  Copper/stainless steel gradient material was preparated by electron beam freeform  fabrication. With the increase of deposition layers, the interfaces between them become smooth.  Crystallographic textures at the first, third and fifth copper layers were determined by means of  electron backscatter diffraction analysis. The results yielded clear information that grain growth was  significantly affected by heat dissipation. The grain gradually altered from competitive growth to  epitaxial growth with the increase of the layers. A strong crystallographic texture in <0 0 1> direction  is produced on the top of the deposition, but weak at the bottom of the deposition. Cracks and  shrinkages were found in the deposition, which are attributed to the liquid copper. The average shear  strength of the depositions exceeds 240 MPa. The fracture is primarily characterized by a ductile mode.  

Keywords:   Electron beam freeform fabrication; Gradient material; Crystallographic texture; Cracks;  Copper; Steel  

# 1. Introduction  

The joining of copper and steel has been widely applied in the field of aerospace, such as the  nozzle of high thrust rocket engine [1,2]. However, copper would be burned seriously during welding  of copper/steel, which affected the performance of the joint. It is important to control the melting ratio  to achieve defect-free welding of copper and steel [3]. No microcracks appear at the weld by focusing  the laser beam 0.2 mm into the steel [4]. Grains at iron side grow into the weld, while the solidification  occurred from the melt pool on the copper side [5]. The amount of the fusion copper should be reduced  during the laser welding of stainless steel and copper. Because excess copper melting lead to  microcrack inside the fusion zone [6]. Interestingly, no cracks or porosity exist in the copper and steel  joint welded by electron beam. But the distribution of copper is extremely uneven [7]. Electron beam  oscillation is beneficial to the copper and steel joint [8]. The matter of serious burning of copper caused  by difference of melting point between copper and steel can be solved by additive manufacturing (AM).  Various heat input can be adopted to reduce the evaporation when depositing different materials.  

Functionally graded materials (FGMs) is a class of materials whose chemical composition or  structure varies with the position, and the materials can be fabricated by the AM. FGMs are  distinguished as the smooth [9,10] and abrupt [11,12] gradients. The chemical composition gradually  changes from layer to layer in smooth graded FGMs, while it changes from one layer to another via a  single interface in a direct way. The preparation of FGMs is affected by quantities of factors, such as  differences in physical and chemical properties, crystal structure, and lattice parameters, which can  lead to significant diversities in the mechanical properties.  

The mechanical properties have a great susceptibility to the crystallographic textures. Thus, it is  significant to study the crystallographic textures of the fabricated parts. The crystallographic texture  can be controlled by altering the parameters. Weak cube crystallographic texture was obtained along  the building and scanning directions from strong fibrous <1 0 0> crystallographic texture by rotating  the scanning directions [13,14]. A crystallographic <0 0 1> fibre texture can be altered into a cube- texture via increasing the heat input [15]. The grains with a sharp crystallographic texture in alloy  IN738 are coarse and elongated, Thus, these grains presented a pronounced mechanical anisotropy  [16,17]. The scan strategy and the build direction have a significant influence on the crystallographic  texture of the sample. However, the crystallographic orientation is indirectly affected by the local  temperature gradient [18]. It can be deduced that the scan strategy plays an important role in the  formation of cracks due to the existence of the high level of misorientation between the grains [19,20].  The crystallographic textures of nickel-based superalloy CM247LC are extremely susceptible to the  temperature of the heat treatment. The crystallographic textures are still strong and similar to that of the  as-SLM (Selective Laser Melting) when the temperature of the heat treatment is below 1230 °C.  However, when the samples are treated at 1240 °C and above. The precipitates were observed to be  finer and more uniform after the heat treatment [21-23]. The prior  β  grains possess a preferred <1 0 0>  crystallographic texture along the main grain growth direction for TC4, which inclines 0\~20° to the  build direction [24,25]. The conclusion changes greatly when fabricating Ti-Nb alloy. The  β  phases,  which consist of non-columnar but elongated grains, show an epitaxial growth with a crystallographic  texture of {0 0 1}<1 0 0> [26]. The SLM-processed TiAl alloy are dominated by high-angle (>15°)  grain boundaries. When the energy density of the heat flux increases from 250 J/mm 3  to 350 J/mm 3 , the  crystallographic textures varied from a strong (0 0 0 1) orientation to a combination of (0 0 0 1), (1 0 -1  1) and (1 1 -2 1) orientations [27,28].  

In this study, the copper and 304 stainless steel (304SS) gradient deposition was fabricated by  electron beam to avoid the evaporation of copper during welding of copper/steel. The crystallographic  textures were analyzed to reveal the diversity of different layers. Defects and mechanical properties  were also reported.  

# 2. Experimental  

# 2.1 Materials and design of deposition  

Two 304 stainless steel (304SS) layers were deposited firstly on 304SS base metal with the  dimensions of 200mm x 200mm x 5mm. Subsequently, the following five copper layers were  fabricated on the former two 304SS depositions. The diameters of the copper and 304SS wire used in  the experiment were both 1.2mm. The 304SS plate was machined, polished and cleaned with acetone  before fabrication. The 304SS base metal was fixed using the clamps to ensure the stability during the  electron beam freeform fabrication (EBF 3 ) process. The experiment was carried out in a vacuum  environment with pressure 5 x 10 -2  Pa, due to the vacuum environment can greatly prevent the  pollution of molten pool [29,30]. The clamping diagram is shown in Fig. 1. The parameters used in the  experiment were presented in Table 1.

# 2.2 Characterization of the depositions  

Microscopic observation specimens were cut perpendicular to the deposition using a wire-cut  electric discharge machining (EDM). Subsequently, each specimen was polished using a series of SiC  emery papers with 200 to 5000 grit sizes in succession with water, and then polished by a 0.05  µ m  diamond paste. To reveal the microstructure, the aquaregia (75% HCl + 25% HNO 3 ) and the solution  of 5 ml FeCl 3  + 50 ml HCl + 100 ml water were used as the etchants for 304SS and copper side,  respectively. The etched samples were studied under the optical microscope (OM) (Keyence: VHX- 1000), and the polished samples and the fracture were investigated under the scanning electron  microscope (SEM) (Zeiss: Merlin Compact). Analysis of the crystallographic textures was carried out  by the SEM-based electron back scatter diffraction (EBSD) (FEI: Quanta 200FEG). For shear tests, the  electronic universal material testing machine (Shimadzu: AG- 20 kN) were used.  

# 3. Results and Discussion  

3.1 Microstructure and element distribution  

Fig. 2(a) clearly demonstrates that the distribution of iron element in the copper layer is uneven.  The cooling paths are limited in the existing deposition with the increase of copper layers, leading to  the heat accumulation. Additionally, the thermal conductivity of copper is large, which result in the  weakening of the focused heating effect of the electron beam. As a result, the temperature distribution  in the liquid copper layer is uniform. Considering the uniform temperature distribution, the solid-liquid  interfaces of the copper layers are perpendicular to the direction of heat dissipation, which can explain  why the interfaces between copper layers are becoming smooth when the number of copper layer  increases.  

The distribution of Fe among the different layers was quite different. The bulk austenite phases  are produced in the former copper layers. With the increase of the layers, the Fe content decreases  gradually and the distribution of Fe is more uniform.   However, the boundaries between the copper  layers are still clear, as shown in Fig. 2(b). Few globular austenite phases are detected in the top copper  layer(Fig. 2(c)).  

![](images/c1415a19250bdfb5199d18da627bc57603a4b408e048b77fa06cc1cde9591b28.jpg)  
Fig. 2.  Cross-section of the copper/SS gradient deposition. (a) total deposition. (b) the interface of the third and fourth copper  layers. (c) globular austenite phase in top layer. (d) bottom of first copper layer. (e) grain size at different positions in the  deposition.  

Cracks are observed at the interface between the first copper layer and steel, which are formed in  the copper deposition and propagate into the austenitic stainless steel, as shown in Fig. 2(d). Further  analyses concerning the formation mechanism of the cracks will be explained below. Fig. 2(e)  expounds the grain size at different regions in the copper layers by EBSD. Position P1\~P3 respectively  represent the bottoms of the first copper layer, the third copper layer and the fifth copper layer. From  the distribution of grain size in different regions, it is tempting to presume that the grain size at P1 and  P2 is small, while the grain size at P3 is large. This can be attribute to the difference of the heat  dissipation condition. Most of the metals at bottom of each layer are formed by melting the top metal of  the former layer. When the number of layers was little, the liquid metal solidified immediately with a  fast cooling rate, which produces fine grains. As the deposition process goes on, the metals of the  former layers are superheated due to the continuous heat accumulation. Thus, the grains exhibit a  characteristic of epitaxial growth mode during the crystallization process, and no obvious fine grains  are found.  

The TEM results of the region A in Fig.2 are shown in Fig.3. Globular phases are distributed in  the matrix. The chemical compositions of different points in Fig. 3 are presented in Table 2. It can be  concluded from the EDS results that point A is mainly composed of Fe elements, while point B is  almost Cu elements. It can be inferred that   the matrix is mainly consisted of  α  phases and the  ε  phases  are distributed in the  α  phases evenly.

# 3.2 Crystallographic texture and grain size  

The states of the heat dissipation are extremely diverse among the different layers. Even in the  same layer, the growth of the crystals at the top and the bottom are quite different, so it is necessary to  analyze the grain orientation of crystals at different locations of the deposition. Fig. 4 shows the EBSD  results of position P3.   The microstructures of P3 are mainly composed of coarse copper grains with  nearly no Fe. Some differences exist in the orientation of Cu grains. However, the difference between  the colors of two adjacent grains is little, as shown in Fig. 4(a). Statistics of grain boundary angle at  this location is presented in Fig. 4(b).  These observations clearly indicate that the small angle  boundaries are dominate in region P3. It can be explained as follows: There is no convection in the  vacuum environment. When the number of the copper layers is large, the heat dissipation path of the  top liquid metal was limited to the existing deposition. The ratio of height and width of the existing  deposition is quite large, which induces an almost vertically downward direction for the heat  dissipation, as shown in Fig. 4(d). The direction of the heat dissipation on both sides of the layer is  almost similar to that in the middle, resulting in that the direction of the maximum temperature gradient  at every position is basically the same. As a result, the direction of the crystal growth is parallel to the  low-angle grain boundaries.   Fig. 4(c) is the pole figure (PF) of the copper crystal at P3. A strong  crystallographic texture in <0 0 1> at the top of the deposition can be concluded from the PF. In  addition, it can be inferred that the grains at the top may grow vertically in the direction of <0 0 1>,  which means that the direction of <0 0 1> is parallel to the direction of electron beam movement.  

![](images/e505a132ae04ebafa2fa81a22cb3fcdb518454c98f7fbe68205c3175bce6d811.jpg)  
Fig. 4.  EBSD results of P3. (a) grain orientations of P3. (b) misorientation angle. (c) PFs of copper. (d) schematic diagram of  heat dissipation path.  

The EBSD results of P2 is shown in Fig. 5. A dramatic difference in grain orientation between the  adjacent two grains can be detected, and the grain size at P2 is much smaller than that at P3. The  statistics of Fig. 5(b) point out that more high-angle grain boundaries are existed in P2 than that in P3.  This is indicative of the fact that the heat dissipates mainly through the substrate rather than the former  deposition when the number of deposit layer is small. Directions of the heat dissipation are not parallel  resulting from the large substrate. Differences existed in the direction of heat dissipation at different  positions (Fig. 4(d)), causing that the maximum temperature   gradients at different positions are not  consistent during the solidification. For this reason, the grains grow in competition, which produces  more high-angle grain boundaries. Due to the competitive growth of the grains, the crystallographic  textures at P2 are not as strong as that at P3, as shown in Fig. 5(c). However, considering that both the  austenite and the copper were  fcc  structure and they are easy to form solid solutions with each other,  the PFs of the copper and the austenite are approximately the same, as shown in Fig. 5(d).  

![](images/08d1cf1c6991b7fdf94d66152898a9bb1616dcf02accd641c4fec67c85ac32cb.jpg)  
Fig. 5.  EBSD results of P2. (a) grain orientations of P2. (b) misorientation angle. (c) PFs of copper. (d) PFs of austenite.  

Fig. 6 shows the EBSD data of position P1. Because the conditions of P1 and P2 are basically  similar except the Fe content and the heat dissipation speed, the grain orientations and the distribution  of grain boundary angles are almost the same as P2, which exhibits the competitive growth and high- angle grain boundaries. The PFs of copper and austenite at P1 are approximately the same, as shown in  Fig. 6(c) and (d).  

![](images/76b874324fc1350557776fab44857f066d965868be8b45f4d59d8135166dd90e.jpg)  
Fig. 6.  EBSD results of P1. (a) grain orientations of P1. (b) misorientation angle. (c) PFs of copper. (d) PFs of austenite.  

The crystallographic texture analysis shows that the heat dissipation has a great influence on the  grain growth. Even at the bottom of each layer, the grain growth gradually changes from the  competitive growth to the epitaxial growth with the increase of the layers. The number of the high- angle grain boundaries decreases and the number of the low-angle grain boundaries increases. Obvious  crystallographic texture would be occurred in the deposition with the increased layers, that is, the grain  growth direction is gradually parallel to the build direction, showing anisotropy.  

# 3.3 Defects and mechanical properties  

To reveal the formation mechanism of cracks, deposition with obvious cracks are analyzed. Fig. 7  shows the defects of the copper/304SS deposition. Great quantities of cracks are observed in  α  phases  and 304 SS layers, and some cracks extend to   a great deep inside the 304SS layers when the liquid  copper is adequate. As a result, the closed type of cracks is formed, as shown in Fig. 7(b). When the  liquid copper is not sufficient, the cracks can not be backfilled by copper, and the open type of cracks  generate, as shown in Fig. 7(c) [8,31]. The mechanism of crack formation is mainly due to the  following reasons. On the one hand, the surface energy of Fe (s) -Cu (l)  is less than that of Fe (s) -Fe (s) . On  the other hand, the capillary action of the liquid copper along austenite grain boundaries is strong. Thus,  the cracks are produced under the tensile stress formed by thermal cycle and differences in  thermophysical properties of copper and steel.  

Shrinkages also exists in the depositions. During solidification, the iron solidifies firstly. The  liquid copper is locked in the solidified iron, and then the liquid copper solidifies. With the temperature  decreasing, the degree of shrinkage of the copper is greater than that of iron, and there is no more  copper to supply. Thus, shrinkages generated at the boundaries between the copper and iron, as shown  in Fig. 7(d).  

![](images/d5f8d848ab634623c913667e00155eed3f6e3012df4a6fa8102e9cba282769c7.jpg)  
Fig. 7.  Defects in the deposition. (a) microcracks. (b) closed type crack. (c) open type crack. (d) shrinkages.  

The shear strength of the depositions is shown in Fig. 8. The horizontal axis of Fig. 8(a) presents  the shear strength of different specimens and standard shear strength for different processing conditions  in the manual. The ‘1 # -5 # ’is the shear strength of the first and second copper layers at different  locations of the deposition. The values of ‘Cu hardening’ and ‘Cu annealing’ are referred to the  handbook. The fracture locations are all located at the first and second copper layers. The average shear  strength of the copper layers is 248 MPa, which is greater than that of hardened pure copper displayed  in red horizontal line (210 MPa). The fracture is primarily characterized by a ductile mode according to  Fig. 8(c) and (d). Fig. 8(b) shows the micro-hardness profile of the gradient deposition. With the  increase of the copper layers, the hardness of the deposition decreases continuously and then becomes  invariant. It can be deduced that under the effect of solid solution and dispersion strengthening, the  mechanical properties of the deposition are improved.

# 4. Conclusions  

The microstructures, crystallographic texture and mechanical properties of copper/304SS gradient  material fabricated by EBF 3  has been investigated via OM, EBSD and SEM. The findings obtained are  summarized below:  

1)   Due to the heat dissipation, the interface between copper layers is becoming smooth when the  number of copper layer increases. With the increase of the layers, the Fe content decreases  gradually and the distribution is more uniform.  2)   The heat dissipation has a great influence on the grain growth. The grain growth gradually  changes from the competitive growth to the epitaxial growth with the increase of the layers.  The number of the high-angle grain boundaries decreases and the number of the low-angle  grain boundaries increases.  3)   Obvious crystallographic textures occurred in the deposition with the increasing layers, and  the grain growth directions are gradually parallel to the build direction.  4)   Cracks are produced in the deposition due to the difference in surface energy between Fe (s) - Cu (l)  and Fe (s) -Fe (s) . The type of the cracks depends on the liquid copper. It can be changed  from the closed type to the open type due to the insufficient liquid copper to backfill the  cracks.  5)   The average shear strength of the copper layers is 248 MPa. The fracture is characterized by a  ductile mode.
