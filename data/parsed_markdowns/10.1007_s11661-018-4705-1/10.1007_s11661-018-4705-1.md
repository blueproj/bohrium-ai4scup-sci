# Thermophysical and Mechanical Properties of Advanced Single Crystalline Co-base Superalloys  

N. VOLZ, C.H. ZENK, R. CHERUKURI, T. KALFHAUS, M. WEISER, S.K. MAKINENI, C. BETZING, M. LENZ, B. GAULT, S.G. FRIES, J. SCHREUER, R. VAßEN, S. VIRTANEN, D. RAABE, E. SPIECKER, S. NEUMEIER, and M. GO ¨ KEN  

A set of advanced single crystalline  c ¢  strengthened Co-base superalloys with at least nine alloying elements (Co, Ni, Al, W, Ti, Ta, Cr, Si, Hf, Re) has been developed and investigated. The objective was to generate multinary Co-base superalloys with signiﬁcantly improved properties compared to the original Co-Al-W-based alloys. All alloys show the typical  c / c ¢ two-phase microstructure. A  c ¢  solvus temperature up to 1174   C and  c ¢  volume fractions between 40 and 60 pct at 1050   C could be achieved, which is signiﬁcantly higher compared to most other Co-Al-W-based superalloys. However, higher contents of Ti, Ta, and the addition of Re decrease the long-term stability. Atom probe tomography revealed that Re does not partition to the  c  phase as strongly as in Ni-base superalloys. Compression creep properties were investigated at 1050   C and 125 MPa in  h 001 i  direction. The creep resistance is close to that of ﬁrst generation Ni-base superalloys. The creep mechanisms of the Re-containing alloy was further investigated and it was found that the deformation is located preferentially in the  c channels although some precipitates are sheared during early stages of creep. The addition of Re did not improve the mechanical properties and is therefore not considered as a crucial element in the design of future Co-base superalloys for high temperature applications. Thermodynamic calculations describe well how the alloying elements inﬂuence the transformation temperatures although there is still an oﬀset in the actual values. Furthermore, a full set of elastic constants of one of the multinary alloys is presented, showing increased elastic stiﬀness leading to a higher Young’s modulus for the investigated alloy, compared to conventional Ni-base superalloys. The oxidation resistance is signiﬁcantly improved compared to the ternary Co-Al-W compound. A complete thermal barrier coating system was applied successfully.

# I. INTRODUCTION  

I NVESTIGATIONS  on  c ¢  strengthened Co-Al-W- based superalloys with up to ﬁve alloying elements revealed that they have higher solidus and liquidus 1 , ]   temperatures, their as-cast microstructure shows less   2 , ] ¢ segregation, their lattice misﬁt between  c  and  c  is   4 ]   positive, their ﬂow stress above 900 C is similar or [ 5 ]   even higher compared to Ni-base superalloys and their creep properties are similar to 1st generation Ni-base   1 ] ¢ superalloys. In contrast, such  c / c  Co-base superalloys often have a low  c ¢  phase stability, low  c ¢  solvus temperature, poor oxidation resistance, and high 1 , ] density.  

The use in harsh environments at high temperatures, however, requires alloys with improved, well-balanced properties. Previous studies have shown the beneﬁcial eﬀects of individual alloying elements. Ni is essential to stabilize the L1 2 -Co 3 (Al,W) phase and to widen the   ¢ 6 ] narrow  c / c  two-phase region. The elements Ti, Ta,   ¢ 7 , ] and Hf partition to the  c  phase, enhance its stability, 9 , ]   increase its solvus temperature, decrease the density  

9 , ] ¢ [ 12 ] (Ti), and improve the  c  hardness as well as the 13 ]   yield strength (Ta). Cr and Si signiﬁcantly improve 14 ] the corrosion and oxidation resistance.  

However, so far only a few Co-base superalloys have been developed that consist of more than ﬁve alloying elements, which are essential to guarantee well-balanced properties and to be able to compete with advanced Ni-base superalloys in terms of both strength and 10 , ]   corrosion resistance. Recently, Lass  et al. devel- oped new Co-base superalloys with the highest  c ¢  solvus temperature (1218   C) reported in the literature. How- ever, they contain no Cr which is indispensable for corrosion resistance. Only the alloy series of Pollock’s [ 16 ]   group and the alloy ERBOCo-0 previously published [ 3 ]   by our group are considered to achieve suﬃcient high temperature strength as well as corrosion resistance for single crystalline applications. ERBOCo-0 is the com- positionally most complex Co-base superalloy that has been reported so far (Co-Ni-Al-W-Ta-Ti-Hf-Cr-Si). However, it forms additional undesired phases during 3 ]   solidiﬁcation. To further improve the properties, three additional multinary Co-base superalloys based on ERBOCo-0 were developed. This paper will discuss how diﬀerent contents of Ni and  c ¢  forming elements inﬂuence their phase stability, microstructure and mechanical properties, and how Re, a crucial element used in 2nd–4th generation of single crystalline Ni-base superalloys, inﬂuences the creep strength. Additionally, elastic stiﬀness, thermal expansion coeﬃcients and phase transformation temperatures will be presented. The oxidation behavior was investigated to prove the enhanced oxidation resistance and samples were coated to study the applicability of bond coats on these advanced  c / c ¢  Co-base superalloys. Thermodynamic 17 ]   calculations using the TCNI8 database, that includes a model of the L1 2 -Co 3 (Al,W) phase, are compared to the experimental results. The aim of this study is to characterize these advanced single crystalline Co-base superalloys and to derive implications for further alloy development and high temperature applications.  

# II. EXPERIMENTAL METHODS  

The nominal chemical compositions of all investi- gated multi-component alloys of the ERBOCo series are given in Table  I . Single crystalline rods were produced by using the Bridgman process.  

The alloys were solution heat-treated at 1300   C/4 hours (ERBOCo-0) or at 1280   C/8 hours (ERBOCo-1, -3, -4), subsequently age hardened at 1050   C/4 hours and 5 hours, respectively, and at 900   C/16 hours. The thermal stability of all alloys was investigated by an additional long-term heat treat- ment at 1000   C for 1000 hours. To evaluate the elemental partitioning between  c  and  c ¢  at the creep test temperature of 1050   C, specimens for atom probe tomography (APT) analysis of ERBOCo-1 and -3 have again been aged at 1050   C for 30 minutes followed by water quenching.  

The  c ¢  solvus temperature was determined by diﬀer- ential scanning calorimetry (DSC) using samples with a diameter of 5 mm and a thickness of 1 mm in a  Netzsch STA 409 . Measurements were performed up to 1500   C with heating and cooling rates of 5   C/minute between 900   C and 1500   C. After a metallographic standard sample preparation with a ﬁnal chemical mechanical polishing, the microstructure was characterized using a scanning electron microscope (SEM)  Zeiss Crossbeam 1540 EsB . The chemical compositions were checked in the SEM by using energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS). The  c ¢  volume fraction was evaluated by image analysis using  ImageJ , measuring the  c ¢  area fraction and calculate the volume fraction assuming the precip- itates to be globular or cubic, respectively.  

Scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) 3   was carried out with a  FEI Titan Themis at 300 kV acceleration voltage. Slices of about 100  l m were cut and thinned by electro twin-jet polishing using a 60 pct perchloric acid in methanol and 2-butoxyethanol elec- trolyte ( A3 electrolyte, Struers ) at – 30   C.  

The accurate composition of  c  and  c ¢  phases was measured using a reﬂectron-equipped local electrode atom probe ( LEAP 5000 XR, Cameca Instruments Inc. ) in laser pulsing mode with a base temperature of 60 K and a detection rate at 0.005 ions per pulse. Data reconstruction and analysis were performed using the software package  IVAS   3.6.14. Atom probe tomogra- phy (APT) samples were prepared using a dual-beam SEM/focused-ion-beam (FIB) system ( FEI Helios Nano- lab 600 ) following the protocol described in Reference  18 .  

Single crystalline bars in the aged state were oriented using the Laue or electron backscatter diﬀraction (EBSD) technique for subsequent cutting. Such speci- mens were required to determine elastic constants and creep properties. # III. MICROSTRUCTURE AND PARTITIONING BEHAVIOR  

The microstructures of the alloys ERBOCo-0, -1, -3, and -4 in the as-cast state and after solution and aging heat treatment are given in Figure  1 . In the as-cast state all alloys form a typical dendritic microstructure. Further intermetallic precipitates such as B2-, Laves-, and D0- 24 -phases are present in the untreated state of ERBOCo-0 with the lower content of Ni which is in 3 ]   agreement with thermodynamic calculations. In com- parison, the alloys ERBOCo-1, -3, and -4, which have a higher Ni-content, show no or only a small amount of undesired intermetallic phases in the as-cast state. Additionally, according to thermodynamic calculations only  c  and  c ¢  phase—and some  l  phase in ERBO- Co-4—are in equilibrium at temperatures above 1000   C.  

After the full heat treatment, all alloys exhibit the typical  c / c ¢  microstructure and no additional intermetal- lic phases are present. The SEM micrographs acquired with the back-scattered electron detector (BSE) reveal a decrease in contrast between  c  and  c ¢  with increasing Ni-content and a change in shape of the  c ¢  precipitates. This indicates a redistribution of the alloying elements between  c  and  c ¢  phase. ERBOCo-0, -1, and -4 show cubic precipitates, however, ERBOCo-4 shows precip- itates with smoothed edges compared to the one of ERBOCo-0 and -1. In contrast, ERBOCo-3 has almost spherical  c ¢  particles indicating a lattice misﬁt close to zero. It is known from literature that Re increases the  c / c ¢  lattice misﬁt in nickel base superalloys to more 21 ]   negative values. Starting from positive misﬁts, which 22 – 25 ]   is typical for cobalt base superalloys, the misﬁt decreases by the addition of Re and gets closer to zero. The  c ¢  volume fraction reaches 79 pct for ERBOCo-0 and decreases with the addition of Ni in ERBOCo-1 and -3 to 56 and 58 pct, respectively. The increase in  c ¢ [ 7 , , ]   formers Ti and Ta again increases the volume fraction to 72 pct in ERBOCo-4 in the standard heat-treated condition. The thermodynamic calculations reveal a  c ¢  volume fraction of 49 pct for ERBOCo-0 which is 30 pct less compared to the microstructure of this alloy. Nevertheless, for ERBOCo-1 and -3 volume fractions of about 36 pct were calculated which repre- sents well the trend that the  c ¢  volume fraction decreased in these two alloys. Figure  2  shows the microstructure of ERBOCo-0, -1, -3, and -4 after the long-term heat treatment at 1000   C for 1000 hours. The  c / c ¢  microstructure of ERBOCo-0 (see Figures  2 (a) and (e)) is still present but a small amount of short topologically closed packed (TCP) phases is formed. The  c ¢  precipitates in this alloy grow to sizes over 5  l m and the loss of the cubic shape indicates that they are not coherently embedded into the matrix anymore. The higher content of Ni in ERBOCo-1 compared to ERBOCo-0 leads to a more stable microstructure (see Figures  2 (b) and (f)). No additional phases form after the 1000 hours heat treat- ment and the coarsening is much slower while the cubic shape of the  c ¢  phase is retained. The addition of Re destabilizes the  c / c ¢  microstructure in ERBOCo-3 (see Figures  2 (c) and (g)) and leads to the formation of large needle-shaped TCP phases, which is already known 27 – 29 ] from Ni-based superalloys with a high Re content. The residual  c ¢  precipitates are still globular and coarsen with a similar growth rate as the precipitates in ERBOCo-1. The alloy ERBOCo-4 develops a higher amount of needle-shaped TCP phases compared to ERBOCo-3 (see Figures  2 (d) and (h)). A possible explanation for this phenomenon is that the additional content of Ti and Ta, which are strongly enriched in the c ¢  phase, pushes the TCP-forming elements like W, 30 ]   similarly to Ni-base superalloys, into the matrix leading to a supersaturation in the  c  phase and an increased tendency to form W-rich TCP phases. The majority of the residual  c ¢  precipitates still show a cubic shape. However, there are regions with varying precip- itate shapes similar to those observed in ERBOCo-0 indicating semi-coherent c / c ¢  interfaces. The locally diﬀerent microstructures are probably to some extent a result of persisting cast microstructure.

# IV.THERMOPHYSICAL PROPERTIES  

The transformation temperatures determined by DSC and thermodynamic calculations are shown in Figure  4 (a). The  c ¢  solvus temperature of 1111   C for ERBOCo-0 increases with the addition of Ni up to 1174   C for ERBOCo-1. ERBOCo-3 and -4 exhibit  c ¢ solvus temperatures of 1159   C and 1167   C, respec- tively. The solidus temperature is in the range of 1318   C to 1340   C and is therefore roughly constant for the alloys investigated. The liquidus temperature reaches 1410   C in ERBOCo-3 and is around 1400   C for the other alloys, too. Qualitatively, the calculations describe well how the alloying elements inﬂuence the  c ¢ solvus temperature and c ¢ volume fraction (see Figure  4 (a), hatched bars). However, the calculated phase transformation temperatures and phase fractions strongly deviate from the experimentally determined values. With increasing Ni-content both the experimen- tal and the calculated data indicate that the  c ¢  solvus  

temperature increases while the solidus temperature and the  c ¢  volume fraction decreases, but the predicted solvus temperature is around 100   C lower and the  c ¢ volume fraction up to 30 pct smaller than the measured ones.  

The  c ¢  volume fraction has been investigated at temperatures up to 1100   C and is shown in Figure  4 (b). The initial c ¢  volume fraction of ERBOCo-0 is the highest of the alloy series and it decreases stronger with increasing temperature. Fur- thermore,  c ¢  volume fractions between 40 and 60 pct are reached at 1050   C. Thus, the  c ¢  solvus temperatures of these Co-base superalloys are lower than that of advanced single crystal Ni-base superalloys, also shown 38 ]   in Figure  4 (b) in comparison with CMSX-4. Never- theless, the  c ¢  volume fraction is similar to that of cast     [ 39 , ] Ni-base superalloys up to 1050 C which are typical application temperatures for such alloys.  

The thermal expansion of the alloy ERBOCo-1 was investigated up to 1300   C and the corresponding linear thermal expansion coeﬃcient  a  is given in Figure  5 (a) in 41 ]   comparison with ERBO/1C, a multinary Ni-base superalloy with similar composition to CMSX-4. Below 500   C the thermal expansion is characteristic for thermally induced lattice vibrations. Starting from about 600   C signiﬁcant and fully reversible anomalies develop at higher temperatures, leading to a sharp discontinuity close to  c ¢  solvus temperature,  i.e ., the anomaly in thermal expansion is closely related to dissolution and, respectively, precipitation of the  c ¢ phase. However, disso- lution of the  c ¢  phase at temperatures as low as 600   C appears unlikely because the diﬀusivities of alloying elements at this temperature are too slow for signiﬁcant changes of phase fractions on laboratory timescale. # VI.MECHANICAL PROPERTIES  

Due to the much higher  c ¢  solvus temperature of the ERBOCo alloys compared to most other Co-base superalloys published in literature, creep tests on single crystalline samples at high temperature of 1050   C could be performed at 125 MPa. A similar study was con- ducted on single crystalline Co-base superalloys with a signiﬁcantly lower Ni-content and no Si, Hf, and Cr by 34 ]   Kolb  et al . Interestingly, it was found that Re leads only to a marginal improvement of the creep strength in this material, although Re is known to signiﬁcantly improve the creep properties in Ni-base superal- 58 – 60 ]   loys. This is a consequence of a positive  c / c ¢  lattice misﬁt, causing tensile stresses in the matrix channels and formation of rafts perpendicular to the compressive stress axis, when the internal and external applied stresses superpose. In ERBOCo-3 no rafts are formed due to the low lattice misﬁt caused by the addition of Re. Due to the lower misﬁt, there is no driving force for directional coarsening of the  c ¢  precip- itates. A reason for the diﬀerence in creep strength might be the shape of the  c ¢  precipitates which are still more cubic in ERBOCo-1 and globular in ERBOCo-3, leading to a smaller  c  channel width in ERBOCo-1 and therefore the precipitates in ERBOCo-3 can be easily overcome by climbing of dislocations. The initial, coherency induced stresses and the rafts perpendicular to the stress axis in ERBOCo-1 might therefore be additional obstacles for dislocation movement which leads to an improved creep strength up to 1.5 pct strain of this alloy compared to ERBOCo-3.  

Still, the lower creep strength of the Re-containing alloy ERBOCo-3 is surprising, since Re is the slowest 61 , ] diﬀusing element in Co, which is similar to Ni. However, the diﬀusion barrier of Re is lower in Co [ 61 , ]   compared to Ni and the lower interdiﬀusion coeﬃcient of Re in Co compared to Ni is only caused by the higher vacancy formation energy in Co than in Ni. This indicates that Re has a lower impact in Co than in Ni. It might be also possible that Re diﬀuses faster along dislocation cores of the interfacial dislocation networks if pipe diﬀusion takes place, as this mechanism does not require the formation of vacancies. This would also explain why the Re-eﬀect is not present in Co-base superalloys. Similar ﬁndings were also considered in the other Re-containing Co-base superalloys with lower 34 ] Ni-content.  

Another reason for the absence of the Re–eﬀect might be the less pronounced partitioning of Re towards the c  phase in Co-based superalloys. It is known from literature that the creep deformation in Ni-base superal- 63 – 66 ] loys is localized in the  c  matrix at high temperatures. Re acts as a good solid solution hardening element in [ 59 , ]   Ni-base superalloys and therefore strongly improves the creep properties. As Re does not partition to the c  phase in Co-base alloys as strongly, the eﬀect of solid solution hardening is much lower. Additionally, it was 34 ]   shown by Kolb  et al. that there is a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the eﬀective diﬀusion coeﬃcient between Ni- and Co-based superalloys, due to the less pronounced partitioning of Re towards the  c  matrix. Therefore, Re leads to a much smaller change of the eﬀective diﬀusion coeﬃcient in Co-base superalloys. Furthermore, a possi- bly lower planar fault energy of the  c ¢  phase in Co-base superalloys might lead to an early cutting of the  c ¢  pre- cipitates and the deformation is not localized in the c  matrix as known from Ni-based alloys which would decrease the inﬂuence of the Re-eﬀect in the matrix phase. Figure  9  shows the microstructure of ERBOCo-1 and -3 after compression creep tests until 0.5 pct plastic strain acquired with a high angle annular darkﬁeld detector in scanning TEM mode (HAADF-STEM). ERBOCo-1 is shown in Figure  9 (a), revealing disloca- tion networks and single dislocations located in the c  matrix channels, indicating that the overall plastic strain is exclusively caused by dislocation activities in the  c  channels. No extended defects like stacking faults or anti-phase boundaries could be found inside the precipitates. In Reference  34  no change in deformation mechanisms during compression creep was found by the addition of Re in a Co-base superalloy with lower Ni-content and without Cr. However, the micrograph in Figure  9 (b) showing ERBOCo-3 in fact indicates that some dislocations are already able to cut into the  c ¢ precipitates at 0.5 pct plastic strain, forming stacking faults (marked by black arrow in Figure  9 (b)), but the main deformation again takes place in the  c  matrix shown by the dislocations bending around the precip- itates. Therefore Re seems to reduce the stacking fault energy of the  c ¢  phase in the investigated alloy.  

This veriﬁes the assumption mentioned before that the solid solution hardening contribution of Re is not that pronounced in Co-base superalloys because it does not partition as strongly to the  c  matrix. If both eﬀects, the lower solid solution hardening contribution of Re and the early cutting of the precipitates are taken together, they may explain the absence of the Re-eﬀect in single crystalline Co-base superalloys.  

# VII. CONCLUSIONS  

Advanced single crystalline  c / c ¢  Co-base superalloys with at least nine alloying elements (Co-Ni-Al-W-Ta- Ti-Hf-Cr-Si-Re) were studied with the objective of achieving improved properties compared to previous Co-Al-W-based superalloys. The ﬁrst alloy, ERBOCo-0, forms deleterious phases due to the low nickel content. Therefore, ERBOCo-1 was developed with a 12 at. pct higher Ni-content compared to ERBOCo-0 to widen the c / c ¢  two–phase region. This alloy shows promising properties in long-term phase stability,  c ¢  solvus tem- perature and compatibility with coatings. Additionally, the creep properties are very promising. With the aim of further increasing the creep strength, Re was added to ERBOCo-3. Furthermore, ERBOCo-4 contains a higher content of Ti and Ta compared to ERBOCo-1 to increase the  c ¢  volume fraction. In conclusion, the following goals have been achieved for these alloys:  

1. The c ¢ solvus temperature of ERBOCo-1 approaches 1174   C and c ¢ volume fractions between 40 and 60 pct at 1050   C are achieved in all investigated alloys.

 2. Thermodynamic calculations using the database TCNI8 anticipate well the phases formed and the trend how alloying elements influence phase trans- formation temperatures and phase fractions, how- ever, the  c ¢  solvus temperature and  c ¢  volume fractions are strongly underestimated.

 3. The elemental partitioning investigated by APT shows similar trends for most elements known from other Co- and Ni-based superalloys. However, Re partitions less strong to the  c  matrix compared to Ni-base superalloys.

 4. Long-term heat treatments show that ERBOCo-1 does not form any TCP phases while ERBOCo-0 or ERBOCo-3 and -4 form short and needle-shaped TCP phases, respectively.

 5. A full set of elastic stiffness coefficients is given for ERBOCo-1 revealing a higher Young’s modulus for Co-based superalloys compared to Ni-based super- alloys up to about 500   C.

 6. The thermal expansion of ERBOCo-1 reveals an interesting anomaly starting at 600   C which might be related to a change in phase fraction or the arrangement of the atoms in the ordered  c ¢  phase.

 7. The oxidation resistance is strongly improved com- pared to Cr- and Si-free Co-Al-W-based alloys.

 8. A Co-based MCrAlY bond coat and a TBC top coat have been successfully applied and only a small fraction of short TCP phases form in the interdif- fusion zone. No delamination was found even after 500 hours of cyclic heating. The coating can be used at high temperatures for protecting advanced Co-base superalloys.

 9. Re partitions less strongly to the  c  phase and does not improve the creep properties at 1050   C. Therefore, Re is not considered as a crucial alloying element for Co-base superalloys.

 10. ERBOCo-1 forms rafts perpendicular to the stress axis during compression creep deformation while the microstructure of ERBOCo-3 stays nearly unchanged due to the lower  c / c ¢  misfit and therefore lower coherency stresses.

 11. STEM investigations reveal that early creep defor- mation in ERBOCo-1 and ERBOCo-3 is mainly located in the matrix, however, Re seems to decrease the stacking fault energy leading to early cutting of the precipitates in ERBOCo-3.
