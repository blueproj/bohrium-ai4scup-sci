# a b s t    r    a c    t  

Transient liquid    phase bonding (TLP)    is well-known to be essential technology to repair damaged components for    gas    turbines. In    this    article, directionally solidiﬁed  ′ -precipitation strengthened GTD- 111 superalloy was TLP    bonded    using    Ni-Cr-Si-B ﬁller    metal and    the    effect    of    bonding    parameters on microstructure of    the    joints was    investigated. Isothermal solidiﬁcation was    not completed for the    joint bonded at 1150   ◦ C for 60 min, and    this    joint    consisted of three distinct zones:    the    athermal solidication zone, the    isothermal solidiﬁcation zone    and    the diffusion affected zone. A    phase-free TLP joint,    solidiﬁed isothermally, was attained at 1150   ◦ C    for 90 min    and    the    crystallographic orientation of    the ISZ    coincided exactly with    that    of    the    base    metal. Various    phases of    Ti were    observed in    the joints    formed at 1170   ◦ C and 1190   ◦ C,    indicating that    temperatures higher    than    1170   ◦ C induce    a reduction of    the    isothermal solidica- tion rate. It was    found that increase in Ti concentration and    decrease    in B diffusivity in    the    joints    bonded at temperatures above 1170   ◦ C was the main reason for this    reduction.

# 1. Introduction  

For higher efﬁciency of gas turbine power plants, components such as vanes and buckets are required to operate at more extreme conditions. This often leads to damage such as cracking and wear of the components by creep, fatigue and oxidation [1,2] . Because of high price of the components, repairing the damaged components is generally considered to be more attractive economically than complete replacement, and repair technologies for these compo- nents are recently being developed [3,4] .  

Transient liquid phase (TLP) bonding is an essential technol- ogy to repair cracking generated along the airfoils of buckets and vanes. TLP bonding, which is also called diffusion brazing, is a simi- lar bonding technology to brazing in many ways. Both TLP bonding and brazing need ﬁller metals which contain melting point depres- sants (MPD) such as B and Si [5] . Bonding temperatures are set to be higher than the melting points of the ﬁller metals but lower than those of the base metals [1] . Neverthless, TLP bonding has a clear difference from brazing in that isothermal solidiﬁcation can prevent formation of harmful phases such as eutectic constituents and intermetallic compounds in the joint. Furthermore, TLP bonded joints solidiﬁed isothermally become chemically indentical to their base metals through homogenization heat treatment after comple- tion of isothermal solidiﬁcation [6–8] . The joints have outstanding mechnical properties and resistance against corrosion, wear and oxidation. Nakao and Oh have reported that the tensile strength of TLP joints in their studies was almost the same as that of base metals [9,10] .  

Meanwhile, optimization of bonding parameters such as bond- ing temperature and holding time, especially for isothermal solidiﬁcation, is of great importance to obtain sound and reliable joints because the bonding pararmeters signiﬁcantly inﬂuence the microstructure of joints [11,12] . Isothermal solidiﬁcation is con- trolled by diffusion of MPD from joints ﬁlled with liquid into base metals and it is generally understood that increasing bonding temperature results in shorter holding time required for comple- tion of isothermal solidiﬁcation, since diffusion is a function of temperature [7] . Additionally, a linear relation between time for isothermal solidiﬁcation and bonding temperature was proposed by Sakamoto et al. [13] . In contrast to these results, some previous studies emphasized the importance of optimizing bonding param- eters with the result that reduction in isothermal solidiﬁcation rate could be induced by an increase in bonding temperature above a critical point [1,3,7,11,14] .

# 2. Materials and experimental methods  

Rectangular coupons (10 × 10 × 5 mm) were taken from the service-exposed gas turbine blades of GTD-111 using an electro- discharge machine. A commercial Ni–Cr-Si–B ﬁller metal (MBF-50) was used for bonding. The ﬁller metal is 40  m thick amorphous foil and its solidus and liquidus temperatures are 1065   ◦ C and 1140   ◦ C, respectively. Chemical compositions of the base metal and the ﬁller metal are listed in Table 1 . The total content of  ′   precipitating elements (Ti + Al + Ta) in GTD-111 is about 10 wt%, while MBF-50 contains no  ′   precipitating elements. B and Si were added to serve as the melting point depressants in the ﬁller metal.  

The surfaces of the coupons were ground using # 1500 silicon carbide paper in order to remove the oxide layer, and this was followed by ultrasonic cleaning. The ﬁller was also ultrasonically cleaned and inserted between two base metal coupons.  

Two base metal coupons and the ﬁller metal were aligned for bonding in consideration of the solidiﬁcation direction of the base metal as depicted in Fig. 1 . Bonding temperatures were determined based on the liquidus temperature of the ﬁller metal.  

TLP bonding operation was carried out under vacuum of 10 − 4   Torr. The samples were heated up to 1150, 1170 and 1190   ◦ C at 20   ◦ C/min and were held at the bonding temperatures for 0–120 min, followed by furnace cooling to room temperature at vacuum atmosphere after the bonding ﬁnished.  

For microstructural characterization, the TLP bonded speci- mens were sectioned perpendicular to the joint and polished up to 1  m surface ﬁnish. Mables reagent (12 g CuCl 2  + 10 ml HCl + 10 ml ethanol) was used to etch the polished specimens. The microstruc- ture of the joints was examined by optical microscope (OM) and ﬁeld emission scanning electron microscope (FE-SEM). Chemical analyses of various phases formed in the joints were conducted using electron probe X-ray  

microanalyzer (EPMA). Crystallographic orientations of the joint and the base metal were determined by electron back-scattered diffraction (EBSD).  

# 3. Results and discussions  

The OM microstructure around the joint formed at 1150   ◦ C for 60 min is shown in Fig. 2 . As indicated in Fig. 2 , the joint consists of three distinct zones, namely the athermal solidiﬁcation zone (ASZ) along the centerline of the joint, the isothermal solidiﬁca- tion zone (ISZ) adjacent to the ASZ and the diffusion affected zone (DAZ) between the joint and the base metal. At a bonding temper- ature above the melting point of the ﬁller metal, the ﬁller metal melts and dissolution of the base metal in a narrow region contact- ing the liquid interlayer occurs. Then, the liquid interlayer attains equilibrium with the base metal and MPD elements such as B in the interlayer diffuse into the base metal. Continuous diffusion of B decreases the content of B in the liquid and isothermal solidiﬁ- cation starts from the base metal towards the centerline when the concentration of B in the liquid reaches a critical point.  

As isothermal solidiﬁcation progresses, the volume of the liquid in the interlayer decreases. However, if sufﬁcient holding time for completion of isothermal solidiﬁcation is not given, residual liq- uid in the interlayer solidiﬁes by cooling, in other words athermal solidiﬁcation occurs and an ASZ eventually develops in the middle of the joint. During athermal solidiﬁcation, solutes such as B are rejected to the liquid and the liquid enriched with the solutes pro- duces brittle phases such as borides and eutectic phases which are known to deteriorate the properties of the joint [6,12,17] .  

Fig. 3 is the EMPA elemental map on the ASZ of the joint formed at 1150   ◦ C for 30 min, and Cr borides (A) and Ni borides(B) with Ti are ﬁrst observed. Ohsasa et el. reported that these phases resulted from a ternary eutectic reaction (L →  + Ni 3 B + CrB) of residual liq- uid in their study on TLP bonding phenomena of Ni-B-Cr ﬁller metal [18] . In addition,  -  ′   eutectic is observed in the region enriched with Ti as well as Al (enlarged region “a” in SE image) around these borides. This eutectic phase shows a distinctive crown region grow- ing in the direction of solidiﬁcation [7] . The base metal solutes of Ti and Al producing  -  ′   eutectic phase in the joint was considered to be brought into the molten interlayer by dissolution of the base metal at the initial stage of TLP bonding.  

Meanwhile, the formation of the undesired phases stated above was not observed in the joint formed at 1150   ◦ C for 90 min where isothermal solidiﬁcation completed as can be seen in the SEM image of the EPMA result (see Fig. 4 ). The reason for this result is that segregation of solutes into the liquid was prevented during isothermal solidiﬁcation due to the equilibrium nature of isother- mal solidiﬁcation [12] . And the elemental map in Fig. 4 reveals that concentrations of alloying elements are uniform across the joint. According to this analysis result, it could be concluded that a holding time of 90 min was sufﬁcient for completion of isothermal solidiﬁcation in this joint between GTD-111 superalloy and MBF-50 ﬁller metal at 1150   ◦ C.  

Another characteristic of isothermal solidiﬁcation is that it grows epitaxially from base metals and the crystallographic ori- entation of TLP joints solidiﬁed isothermally coincides with that of their base metals [19–21] . Especially for TLP bonding of superalloys solidiﬁed directionally, there should be no difference in the orien- tation between joints and their base metals for the joints to achieve

This means that increasing bonding temperature induces more dissolution and more diffusion of Ti and Al from the base metal into the molten interlayer in comparison to 1150   ◦ C, and that the liquid at 1170   ◦ C is enriched by Ti and Al more than that at 1150   ◦ C. Pouranvari and Kim stressed [7,14] that the reason for the decrease in isothermal solid- iﬁcation rate in TLP joints with a high concentration of base metal solutes was the low diffusion rate of solutes such as Ti in Ni-based superalloys. Furthermore, the solidiﬁcation partition coefﬁcient of Ti is well-known to be less than 1, hence Ti from the base metal will play a role as MPD elements and Ti enrichment in the liquid can cause a depression of the solidiﬁcation temperature of the liquid [3] . Therefore, for the start of isothermal solidiﬁcation in the liquid interlayer enriched with Ti from the base metal, the concentration of Ti should decrease to the critical point by diffusion from the liq- uid interlayer into the base metal inversely. However, isothermal solidiﬁcation by solid-state diffusion of Ti could not be completed for the given holding time because the diffusion coefﬁcient of Ti, a substitutional element, was signiﬁcantly lower than that of B, an interstitial element. Consequentially, the residual liquid ended up solidifying by cooling and forming various phases containing Ti as discussed, with results shown in Figs. 9 and 10 .  

Meanwhile, Tokoro et al. asserted that holding time for isother- mal solidiﬁcation completion depended on bonding temperature as well as B solubility in base metals, and the reduction in isothermal solidiﬁcation rate above a critical temperature was related to the decrease of B solubility in Ni-based alloys [1,28] . B solubility in pure Ni declines steadily above the eutectic temperature in Ni-B binary system. With this relationship between B solubility in pure Ni and temperature, it can be understood that increasing bonding tem- perature not only increases B diffusivity, but decreases the driving force of B to diffuse from joints into base metals of Ni based super- alloys due to reduced B solubility. Ultimately, it could be concluded that elevating bonding temperature generally reduces the time for isothermal solidiﬁcation completion since it increases B diffusivity, but if the bonding temperature exceeds a critical point, the effect of reduced B solubility overrides that of improved B diffusivity and a reduction of isothermal solidiﬁcation rate occurs.  

Another interesting feature observed in the joint formed at 1170   ◦ C for 90 min was that the DAZ of the joint, the region between the solid line and the dotted line in SEM image in Fig. 9 , contained smaller amounts of Cr carbo-borides precipitation than those of the joints formed at 1150   ◦ C (compare Figs. 8 and 11 ). This was also thought to be caused by the reduced B solubility at 1170   ◦ C. Pouranvari et al. attributed the suppression of Cr boride formation in the DAZ to the reduced B solubility in the ISZ [7] . Precipitation of ﬁne  ′   particles was identiﬁed in the ISZ of the joint formed at 1170   ◦ C for 90 min in Fig. 10 and it proved that the concentration of the base metal elements except Ti in this ISZ was higher than that in the ISZ formed at 1150   ◦ C. Among the various base metal alloy- ing elements, W and Mo are known to shift the boride solvus line upward and resultantly to decrease B solubility in Ni-based alloys [7] . The W and Mo    concentration in the ISZ of the joints bonded at 1170   ◦ C for 90 min was measured at 1.97 wt% and 0.76 wt%, respec- tively, and this result reveals that the ISZ at 1170   ◦ C is more enriched with these elements than that at 1150   ◦ C. Accordingly, it could be expected that diffusion of these elements from the base metal into the ISZ reduces B solubility of the ISZ and B atoms are unlikely to move to the base metal across the ISZ with lower B solubility at 1170   ◦ C.

In this article, directionally solidiﬁed  ′ -precipitation strength- ened GTD 111 was TLP bonded using MBF-50 ﬁller metal. And bonding phenomena according to the bonding parameters were investigated and discussed carefully in order to optimize the bonding parameters for achievement of isothermal solidiﬁcation. Bonding temperature had a critical effect on isothermal solidiﬁca- tion and precipitation in the DAZ of TLP joints. Increasing bonding temperature above a critical temperature (1170   ◦ C) reduced the rate of isothermal solidiﬁcation and retained various phases in the joints.  

# 4. Conclusions  

According to the results of this study, the following conclusions can be drawn:  

(1) Isothermal solidiﬁcation was governed by B diffusion in bond- ing carried out at 1150   ◦ C. The ASZ, which consists of borides and  -  ′   eutectic, decreased with increased holding time and disappeared in TLP joint formed at 1150   ◦ C for 90 min, i.e. isothermal solidiﬁcation was completed. The ISZ grew epi- taxially from the base metal with the same crystallographic orientation that the base metal showed. Fine Cr carbo-borides were detected in the diffusion affected zone along the joints.

 (2) The rate of isothermal solidiﬁcation reduced at bonding tem- peratures above 1170   ◦ C. It was suggested that isothermal solidiﬁcation at these bonding temperatures was signiﬁcantly affected by an increase in concentration of Ti with low diffu- sivity and a decrease in B diffusivity in the liquid interlayer. Reduction of Cr carbo-boride precipitation was observed in the DAZ and this was also thought to be caused by the decrease in B diffusivity. Additionally, the high Ti concentration induced ﬁne  ′   particle precipitation in the ISZ of the joints made at 1170   ◦ C and 1190   ◦ C.  

(3) The proper bonding temperature should be suggested after careful consideration of the melting points of the ﬁller metal and the base metal. Based on the experimental results in this study, it could be concluded that the proper bonding temper- ature and holding time combination was 1150   ◦ C × 90 min for isothermal solidiﬁcation in TLP bonding between GTD 111 and MBF-50.
