# 1. Introduction  

Thermal barrier coatings (TBCs) are widely applied onto super- alloy components in gas turbine engines to provide hot corrosion resistance and thermal insulation. Commercial advanced TBCs usually process a two-layered structure [1] , including a ceramic top coat for thermal insulation [2] , and an underlying metallic bond coat for oxidation and corrosion resistance [3,4] . Typically, the bondcoat is a MCrAlY (M = Ni, Co or Ni + Co) [5–7] over- lay or a diffusion aluminide coating [8] with a thickness of 50–150  m.  

IC21 superalloy (Beihang University) is a newly developed Ni 3 Al based single crystal superalloy for turbine blades and other high temperature structural components. This superalloy exhibits excel- lent high temperature mechanical properties. However, the high Mo content (8 ∼ 10 at.%) within the superalloy results in a poor oxi- dation resistance, due to the formation of volatile Mo    oxides [16] . To address this problem, IC211 superalloy with reduced Mo content (5 ∼ 8 at.%) was further developed. Moreover, elements including Cr, Y and Ta were doped in the IC211 aiming to improve its oxidation performance. Our previous work demonstrated that Si modiﬁed aluminide (Al–Si) coating could effectively improve the oxidation performance of the high Mo    containing superalloy by suppressing the detrimental effect of Mo [17] . Compared with the IC21, it can be expected that the coated IC211 will exhibit different oxidation and inter-diffusion behaviours.  

Considerable past research has focused on Pt-modiﬁed nickel aluminide  -(Ni, Pt)Al coating, due to its excellent capability of forming a protective alumina scale [9–11] . However, the indus- trial application of (Ni, Pt)Al is always limited by its complicated fabrication process and high cost. Low cost aluminide coatings pre- pared by pack cementation can be the alternative choice in most  

In this work, an Al–Si coating was prepared by pack cementa- tion onto a novel Ni 3 Al based single crystal superalloy IC211 with high Mo content. Cyclic oxidation was conducted at 1150   ◦ C. The microstructure evolution, oxidation and inter-diffusion behaviours of the coating systems were investigated. Possible mechanisms related to the oxidation and elements inter-diffusion behaviours were also discussed.

For the cross-sectional observation, the specimens were embedded in epoxy, ground and ﬁnally polished. # 3. Results and discussion  

# 3.1. Microstructure of as-prepared Al–Si coating  

The XRD pattern of the as-prepared Al–Si coating is shown in Fig. 1 (a). Peaks of Ni 2 Al 3  and  -NiAl were detected. Meanwhile, weak signals of  -Al 2 O 3  and  -Al 2 O 3  were also identiﬁed, possi- bly caused by slight oxidation and embedding of inert ﬁller oxide particles during the pack cementation.  

Fig. 2 (a) shows the cross-sectional SEM micrographs of the as- prepared Al–Si coating, which reveals a thickness of ∼ 150  m. Higher magniﬁcation image ( Fig. 2 (c)) shows that the coating con- sists of two layers: an outer layer of ∼ 5  m thick and an inner layer of ∼ 145  m thick. The elements depth proﬁles analyzed by EDS are depicted in Fig. 2 (b). It can be seen that compared with the inner layer, the outer layer is relatively richer in Al with a maximum concentration of ∼ 56 at.%. According to the XRD and the compo- sition analysis results, it can be inferred that the upper layer is mainly composed of  -NiAl and the inner layer consists of  -NiAl and Ni 2 Al 3 . High Al activity was maintained throughout the alu- minizing process, continuous inward diffusion of Al resulted in the formation of the inner layer of the coating. Meanwhile, the outer layer might form through the slight outward diffusion of Ni. In addi- tion, the as-prepared coating contains a certain amount of Si with a homogeneous concentration of ∼ 1 at.%. It is worth noting that a dispersion of ﬁne granular particles (bright, Fig. 2 (c)) was observed within the inner layer of the coating. Previous studies demonstrated that these precipitates (rich in Si, Mo and Cr) were formed during the pack cementation due to the low solubility of alloying elements in Ni 2 Al 3  and  -NiAl [19,20] .  

As shown in Fig. 2 (b) and (d), there is an abrupt composition change as well as a microstructure transition zone across the sub- strate/coating interface. Elements inter-diffusion, including inward diffusion of Al and Si and outward diffusion of Ni and Mo, occurred during the pack cementation at 1000   ◦ C.

# 3.2. High temperature cyclic oxidation behaviour  

Fig. 3 shows the cyclic oxidation kinetic curves of the two types of specimens at 1150   ◦ C for 100 h. The uncoated superalloy IC211 yielded a mass gain of ∼ 6.99 mg/cm 2 . Compared with our previous oxidation results of IC21 ( ∼ 60 mg/cm 2   at 1150   ◦ C for 100 h [17] ), which contains 8 ∼ 10 at.% Mo, the oxidation rate of bare IC211 is greatly decreased. The reduced oxidation rate is ascribed to the newly designed composition of IC211 (relatively lower content of Mo    and addition of Cr, Y and Ta, see Table 1 ). It is believed that the continuous outward diffusion of Mo    to the alloy surface at high temperature can destroy the integrity of the protective oxide layer due to the formation of gaseous oxides (MoO 3  and MoO 2 ) [21] . Severe spallations were detected just after 20 h oxidation for IC211 ( Fig. 3 (b)). The application of an Al–Si coating can slow down the oxidation rate, to a mass gain of ∼ 3.65 mg/cm 2 . Spallations can hardly be seen even after 100 h oxidation, see the mass change curve in Fig. 3 (b). However, the oxidation kinetic curve for the Al–Si coating exhibits an abrupt increase of mass gain just after 20 h oxidation and this will be discussed in the following sections.

Signiﬁcant growth and coarsing of the precipitates can be seen after 20 h oxidation ( Fig. 5 shows the comparison). Fig. 4 (c) and (d) plots the elements depth proﬁles of the oxidized coating. In order to investigate the formation mechanism of the layered structure of the oxidized coating system, EDS element mapping was performed and displayed in Fig. 6 . It can be seen that there is an enrichment of Si and Mo    in the IDZ, which corresponds to the dispersion of precipitates mentioned above. Because of the high concentration in the superalloy IC211, Mo diffused outwardly very rapidly at high temperature. Meanwhile, inward diffusion of Si took place due to its high afﬁnity with Mo    atoms (formation energy = 130 kJ/mol at 1173–1623 K) [23] . XRD test was performed to conﬁrm the phases of the Mo–Si compounds. Because of the limited penetration depth of X-ray, a surface layer of ∼ 150  m was removed from the oxidized samples by careful grinding. It can be seen from Fig. 1 (b) that only MoSi 2  signals were detected. There- fore, the interaction between Si and Mo effectively prevented Mo atoms from outward diffusion and trapped them in the IDZ. The precipitated ﬁne particles located at the grain/phase boundaries could play the role of diffusion barrier and suppress the subsequent outward diffusion of other alloying elements from the substrate [23,24] .

# 3.4. Microstructure evolution of Al–Si coating: after 100 h oxidation  

The layered structure can still be seen even after 100 h oxidation, as shown in Fig. 7 (a). The long term cyclic oxidation resulted in a much thicker TGO, with numerous voids at the TGO/coating inter- face. It is generally believed that the formation of these voids was a result of Sulphur segregation [26,27] . Moreover, voids caused by the volatilization of Mo    oxides were also found within the TGO layer ( Fig. 7 (b)). Once the integrity of TGO was damaged by the voids, degradation would be accelerated by the development of deleteri- ous mixed oxides [28] , such as spinels and other alloying oxides (as indicated by arrows in Fig. 7 (b)). This explains why there was an abrupt increase of mass gain for Al–Si coating just after 20 h oxida- tion. With continued thickening of the TGO, cracks and spallations appeared due to local high thermal cycling stress.  

By comparing the elements depth proﬁles shown in Figs. Fig. 4 4(d) and Fig. 7 7(f), it can be seen that the Mo content in layer 1 decreased slightly during the process of oxidation. Corresponding to this was the reduced density of precipitates in this layer. As for layer 2, it was still free of precipitates. However, with the dropping of average Al content in the coating ( ∼ 30 at.% after 100 h oxidation, as shown in Fig. 7 (e)), discrete  ′ -Ni 3 Al precipitates were observed in both layers 1 and 2.  

As shown in Fig. 7 (f), there were still high concentrations of alloying elements (such as Mo    and Re) in the IDZ, indicating its excellent diffusion barrier effect. Compared with the 20 h oxida- tion results, the morphology of the IDZ didn’t change signiﬁcantly, except for the thickening of layers 3 and 4 in some degree (see Fig. 7 (c)). Another phenomenon should be mentioned is the devel- opment of topological close-packed phase (TCP) beneath the IDZ ( Fig. 7 (d)), enlarged microstructure of which is shown in Fig. 4 (a). # 4. Conclusions  

A Si modiﬁed aluminide (Al–Si) coating was prepared on a Ni 3 Al based single crystal superalloy with high Mo    content by high- activity pack cementation. Cyclic oxidation test at 1150   ◦ C was carried out and the microstructure evolution of the coating was investigated. The conclusions can be drawn as follows:  

(1) Compared with the bare alloy, the oxidation of the Al–Si coating yielded a much lower mass gain of ∼ 3.65 mg/cm 2 , indicating its better oxidation resistance. Signiﬁcant degradation of the coating was observed after 100 h oxidation.

 (2) Elements inter-diffusion occurred during high temperature oxi- dation. The continuous formation of TGO promoted a severe outward diffusion of Mo, which was effectively blocked by the interaction between Si and Mo.

 (3) Numerous precipitates were formed in the inter-diffusion zone (IDZ). The IDZ acted as a diffusion barrier and suppressed fur- ther outward diffusion of other alloying elements from the substrate, eventually resulting in a layered structure of the Al–Si coating.
