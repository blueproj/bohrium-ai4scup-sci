# Dual Photoredox and Nickel Catalysis in Regioselective Diacylation: Exploring the Versatility of Nickel Oxidation States in Allene Activation

ABSTRACT:  We present a nickel-catalyzed regioselective radical diac- ylation of allenes with ketoacids to produce 1,4-dione products by dual photoredox and nickel catalysis. This integrated approach merges redox- active oxidative addition and reductive elimination steps with migratory insertion. The acyl radical generated in the photoredox cycle sequentially adds to Ni(I) and Ni(II) intermediates following a Ni(I) − Ni(II) − Ni(II) − Ni(III) − Ni(I) catalytic cycle. This methodology, supported by DFT calculations, demonstrates the potential of nickel catalysis in the creation of complex molecular architectures.  

I n recent years, nickel catalysis has emerged as a powerful and versatile tool for facilitating a wide range of valuable organic transformations.   This is largely due to the versatility of nickel catalysts, which can readily adopt different oxidation states, including Ni(0)/(I)/(II)/(III)/(IV), enabling their use in a wide range of chemical reactions. Furthermore, the combination of nickel catalysis with photoredox catalysis has provided even more significant advantages in addressing various challenges in organic synthesis with stability across a spectrum of open and closed shell nickel species.   This advanced platform has led to the development of various bond- forming reactions, with one noteworthy example being the C − C cross-coupling reactions that introduce acyl groups into alkyl partners, yielding valuable ketone compounds. − 6   Ketones find widespread use as essential building blocks in pharmaceuticals, natural products, advanced materials, and fine chemicals. Researchers in the field, such as the MacMillan,   Li,   Xie, Doyle,   Molander,   and Fensterbank 13   groups have explored ketone synthesis through dual photoredox and nickel catalysis. This approach involves the oxidative addition of nickel to aryl or acyl halides, the interception of acyl or alkyl radicals generated via photoredox catalysis, and subsequent reductive elimination steps to yield ketones ( Scheme 1 a).

Our methodology involves the use of  α - oxocarboxylic acids as acyl precursors, which undergo photo decomposition to generate acyl radicals.   These radicals subsequently combine with a nickel(I) complex, forming an acyl nickel(II) intermediate. Subsequently, 1,1-disubstituted allenes undergo regioselective migratory insertion at the 2,3- position into the Ni-acyl intermediates yielding an acyl alkenyl- Ni(II) complex.   A subsequent addition of an acyl radical to the alkenyl-Ni complex, followed by reductive elimination, yields a 1,4-dione product. Notably, the key to the success of this transformation lies in the efficient migratory insertion of un functional i zed allene substrates into Ni-acyl complexes. The validity and efficiency of this reaction pathway have been further corroborated through density functional theory (DFT) calculations.

Under the optimized reaction conditions, we explored the versatility of diacylation for the synthesis of 1,4-diones using various 1,1-disubstituted allenes and  α -oxocarboxylic acids ( Scheme 2 ). Symmetrical diaryl allenes with both electron- withdrawing (F, Cl, Br) and electron-donating (Me, t Bu, and OMe) substituents underwent diacylations well, yielding the corresponding 1,4-diones ( 3aa − 3gb ). Unsymmetrical diaryl allenes also proved to be suitable substrates for this transformation despite moderate selectivity outcomes ( 3ha − 3ka , 1:1 − 5:1). Next, the substrate scope of  α -oxocarboxylic acids  2  was examined using  1a  as the allene partner. The aryl- containing  α -oxocarboxylic acids bearing both electron- donating and electron-deficient groups worked well and yielded the corresponding diacylation products ( 3ab − 3am ). The para- ( 2b ,  2c ,  2f ,  2g ,  2i ,  2j ,  2k ) and meta-substituted  α - oxocarboxylic acids ( 2d ,  2e , and  2h ) exhibited good to excellent reactivity. Notably, the mild reaction conditions tolerated various functional groups, including heterocycles and halogens such as Cl and Br substituents, which are typically sensitive to nickel-catalyzed and photoredox reactions.

Unfortunately, aryl-aliphatic and aliphatic-aliphatic 1,1-disub- stituted allenes as well as aliphatic  α -oxocarboxylic acids were not suitable substrates for the transformation (see Figure S1 in the  Supporting Information ).  

The diacylation process was completely inhibited upon the addition of stoichiometric 2,2,6,6-tetramethyl-1-piperidinoxy (TEMPO) or butylated hydroxy toluene (BHT) under the standard conditions. The relevant radical trapping adducts were detected by mass spectrometry (see Scheme S1 in the Supporting Information ). These results indicated that acyl radicals were involved in the diacylation. The measurement of the quantum yield ( Φ  = 0.041) indicated a closed catalytic cycle for this transformation.  

We propose a plausible mechanism using  1d  and  2a  as substrates, and it is supported by DFT calculations ( Scheme 3 ). Initially,  2a  undergoes base-mediated deprotonation to form the  α -oxocarboxylate  A . Upon exposure to blue LED irradiation, the ground-state Ir(III) catalyst is photoexcited and oxidizes  α -oxocarboxylate  A  ( E 1/2  = +1.03 V vs SCE) through a single electron transfer, resulting in the formation of an acyl radical species  B  and the reduced Ir(II) photocatalyst ( E [ \* Ir(III)/Ir(II)] = +1.21 V vs SCE).   Then, the Ir(II) photocatalyst is oxidized to the original Ir(III) complex by either molecular oxygen (O 2 ) or Ni(II) species, thus completing the photocatalytic cycle. On the other hand, the Ni catalytic cycle begins with the formation of Ni(I) intermediate  C  by reduction of Ni(II).  C  reacts with acyl radical  B  to generate benzoyl-Ni(II) intermediate   3 D  ( − 21.72 kcal/mol). Due to the strong affinity for  π -electrons, 3 D coordinates with allene  1d  to give allene-Ni(II) complex   3 E ( − 27.40 kcal/mol). 3 E  is transferred to more stable singlet state   1 E  through the singlet − triplet minimum energy crossing point (MECP,  − 23.10 kcal/mol,  Δ G  = 4.3 kcal/mol) as the transition state energy for migratory insertion of   3 E  ( TS MI , − 9.94 kcal/mol,  Δ G ‡ =17.46 kcal/mol) is high. Alternatively, intermediate  C  can undergo allene coordination to give intermediate   1 D ′  ( − 19.75 kcal/mol) before radical addition to give the single state   1 E  directly. Then   1 E  undergoes highly exothermic migratory insertion ( TS ′ MI ,  − 21.14 kcal/mol, Δ G ‡ =15.94 kcal/mol) to give alkenyl-Ni(II) complex  F ( − 60.40 kcal/mol), which has a barrier of 15.93 kcal/mol. The second acyl radical addition takes place at the vacant coordination site of Ni intermediate 1 F , resulting in the formation of Ni(III) complex  G  ( − 66.89 kcal/mol). Finally,  G undergoes reductive elimination ( TS RE ,  − 59.96 kcal/mol, Δ G ‡ =6.93 kcal/mol), yielding 1,4-dione  3da  ( − 76.13 kcal/ mol) and regenerating Ni(I) intermediate  C .
