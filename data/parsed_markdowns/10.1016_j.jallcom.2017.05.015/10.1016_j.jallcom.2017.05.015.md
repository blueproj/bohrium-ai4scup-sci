# a r t i c l e i n f o  

Destruction of microstructural stability caused by topologically close-packed (TCP) phase precipitation restricted the industrial application of advanced single crystal superalloys. However, systematic in- vestigations on microstructural nature and evolution of Co-rich TCP phases in Ru-containing single crystal superalloys with high level of Co addition have been rarely reported. In this study, experimental results indicated that the main TCP phase was  m  phase after thermal exposure at 950    C for 1000 h, while R phase was the main phase after thermal exposure at 1100    C for 1000 h in the alloys containing high level of Co and different levels of Cr and Mo. The experimental and thermodynamic calculation results showed that R phase was more stable at 1100    C than that of  m  phase in the experimental alloys. Meanwhile,  m  phase started to transform to R phase at 1100    C for 50 h, and the stress accelerated the transformation of  m  phase and R phase during creep test at 1100    C/140 MPa in an alloy containing high levels of Co, Cr and Mo additions. Both R phase and  m  phase were enriched in Re, W, Mo, Cr and Co, while R phase contained more Cr and  m  phase was more enriched in Mo. This study contributes to better understand the microstructural evolution of TCP phases at different temperatures and to optimize the alloy design and thermodynamic database of Ru-containing single crystal superalloys with high level of Co addition.

# 1. Introduction  

In the recent decade, Ru was found to suppress the formation of TCP phases effectively in the new generations of Ni-base single crystal superalloys  [3 e 5] . Moreover, the increasing level of Co content is one of the trends in alloy design to achieve the outstanding microstructural stability and creep properties in advanced single crystal superalloys  [2] . Although Co was regarded as one of the principal elements in TCP phases  [6,7] , there has been controversial about the effect of Co on microstructural stability [8 e 10] . Meanwhile, the application to add more Co as the substi- tute for a part of expensive Ru to achieve acceptable microstruc- tural stability and cost has been studied in our group  [11] . However, the TCP phases were still observed after long term exposure at 1100    C in the alloys with high level of Co and Ru additions  [11] . The microstructural stability was also not improved by high level of Ru addition af ﬁ rmatively, and the alloy compositions should be balanced (e.g. Re and Cr contents)  [12,13] . Therefore, in order to reveal the effect of Co and Ru additions on microstructural stability  

The continuous requirements for the elevated temperature ca- pabilities of aircraft engines and power generation systems pro- mote further investigation on the advanced Ni-base single crystal superalloys with multiple alloying additions  [1] . The additions of refractory alloying elements, such as Re, W and Mo, enhanced high temperature properties, especially the creep strength, however, the excessive additions of these elements led to the formation of topologically-close-packed (TCP) phases, which destroyed the microstructural stability and served as the initial crack accumula- tion sites during creep  [2] . Therefore, it is a challenge to balance the excellent mechanical performance, microstructural stability as well as the economic cost in further alloy development, in which high  

and to optimize alloy design in Ru-containing single crystal su- peralloys with high level of Co addition, the systematic under- standing on microstructural features and evolution of TCP phases are required.  

Considering the chemical features and prototype of TCP phases, m  phase and R phase were easily precipitated in Co-rich single crystal superalloys  [14] . In addition, many studies of TCP phases focused on  s ,  m  and P phases, but limited research were conducted on microstructural nature and evolution of intermetallic R phase in Ni-base single crystal superalloys. The crystal information of R phase was analyzed in detail in Co-Cr-Mo system  [15] , and had the rhombohedral crystal structure with 53 atoms in an unit cell with a prototype of Cr 18 Mo 31 Co 51  [16] . Previous studies showed that R phase was found in the interdendritic areas of Ru-free single crystal superalloys with 9.5 wt% content of Co after thermal exposure at 900    C for 500 h  [6] . However, R phase stability in Ru-containing single crystal superalloys was rarely reported. Furthermore,  m phase (Co 7 W 6  or Co 7 Mo 6 ) was also a Co-rich TCP phase and could be easily precipitated below 1050    C in Ru-free superalloys  [6,17,18] , and the phase transformations between  m  phase and  s  phase  [14]  as well as  m  phase and P phase  [19]  were reported. However, the detailed microstructural characterization and phase trans- formation between  m  phase and R phase in Ru-containing single crystal superalloys with higher Co additions have not been referred. Thus, it is necessary to investigate the microstructure nature and evolution of  m  phase and R phase in Ru-containing single crystal superalloys.  

In order to better understanding of microstructural nature and the evolution between  m  phase and R phase in Ru-containing single crystal superalloys with high Co content, thermal exposure exper- iments were conducted at 950    C and 1100    C for 10 h e 1000 h, creep experiments were tested under 1100    C/140 MPa in air. The effect of microstructure, morphology, chemical composition, orientation relationship and stress on Co-rich  m  phase and R phase were investigated in this study. These information are expected to get better understanding of TCP phase stability; meanwhile, to improve the thermodynamic modeling database in Ru-containing single crystal superalloys.

# 4. Discussion  

In order to investigate the microstructural nature and stability of m  phase and R phase, thermal exposure and creep tests were taken in three experimental alloys. In this study, the morphologies, chemical compositions, phase stability and thermodynamic calcu- lations of Co-rich  m  phase and R phase in Ru-containing single crystal superalloys with high level of Co addition were character- ized in detail and will be discussed systematically.  

# 4.1. Stability of  m  phase and R phase  

High level of refractory alloying additions in advanced single crystal superalloys led to the formation of TCP phases; meanwhile, analyses on the thermodynamics and kinetics were important to understand the precipitation of TCP phases. It was reported that  m phase generally formed after thermal exposure below 1050    C in Ru-free single crystal superalloys  [6,17,18] . However, the stability of m  phase was elevated and observed after thermal exposure at 1100    C in Ru-containing single crystal superalloys  [19,21] . Mean- while, R phase was rarely reported in Ni-base single crystal su- peralloys. It was known to precipitate at 800    C and 900    C in Ru- free single crystal superalloys with containing 9 wt % Co  [6] . Furthermore, Sato et al. postulated that 0.04% area fraction of R phase precipitated from the matrix after thermal exposure at 1100    C for 300 h in a Ru-free single crystal superalloy containing 6 wt% Co and 3.0 wt% Cr, while it was not observed in a Ru- containing single crystal superalloy exposed at 900    C for 1000 h [5] . It could be deduced that Ru addition and thermal exposure condition had effects on the formation of R phase, respectively.  

In this study,  m  phase precipitated with a high area fraction after thermal exposure at 950    C in alloy SC2221 ( Fig. 1 ). Meanwhile, large amount of  m  phase was observed in alloys SC2212 and SC2122 after thermal exposure at 950    C for 1000 h, respectively ( Fig. 3  and Table 5 ). These results indicated that  m  phase was the dominant phase and more stable at 950    C in these alloys which containing high level of Co addition. Furthermore, the thermodynamic

Meanwhile, R phase was also observed as the dominant phase after thermal exposure at 1100    C for 1000 h in alloys SC2212 and SC2122, respectively ( Fig. 3  and  Table 5 ). The thermodynamic cal- culations predicted that the soluvs temperatures of R phase were markedly higher than those of  m  phase ( Fig. 9 ), and it indicated that R phase was more stable than  m  phase at 1100    C in three alloys. Although the values of the soluvs temperatures exhibited differ- ence between the calculation and the thermal exposure results in three alloys, the trends were consistent well. Therefore, it is worth to note that R phase could act as the equilibrium phase at 1100    C in experimental alloys with high level of Co addition.  

Indeed, the precipitation and transformation of TCP phases could attribute to the thermodynamics and kinetics effects which corresponding to temperature and time in Ni-base single crystal superalloys. In alloy SC2221, the formation of R phase was extremely sluggish and occurred obviously until 500 h exposure at 950  C ( Fig. 1  and  Table 5 ). Once the temperature was increased, R phase precipitated quickly at 1100    C for 50 h ( Fig. 8 and  Table 5 ). The results indicated that the temperature served as the primary effect on the formation of R phase. Furthermore, the high applied stress accelerated the formation and the thermo- dynamic equilibrium of TCP phases at 1100    C in this study. The formation of TCP phases was promoted during creep process at 1100    C and 140 MPa, and the actual stress near the fracture surface was much larger than 140 MPa when the necking happened until the fracture. As a result, the area fraction of TCP phase was 7.7  ±  0.7% ( Fig. 10 (a)) in the region of high stress after creep rupture (rupture life, 39.6 h) and it was much higher than that after thermal exposure at 1100    C for 1000 h ( Fig. 4  and Table 5 ). Besides the formation of TCP phases, it could be deduced that R phase was able to transform from  m  phase rapidly under the in ﬂ uence of high level of stress, based on the morphology analyses ( Fig. 10 ).  

# 4.2. Chemical features of  m  phase and R phase  

Generally, the chemical features of TCP phases were in ﬂ uenced by alloy composition. Many studies indicated that TCP phases were enriched in Re, W, Cr and Mo  [3,11,14] , especially Re, which is considered as the most important TCP former. Usually,  m  phase was enriched in Mo and Co, while R phase was enriched in Cr and Co; the Re content in  m  phase was higher than that of R phase in Ru-free single crystal superalloys  [14] . Meanwhile, other study showed that Re was considered as the main forming element of  m  phase in the Ru-containing single crystal superalloy, where the Re content in  m phase was above 30 at. %  [22] . Furthermore, the content of Re in  m phase in some Ru-containing single crystal superalloys was elevated compared to the Ru-free single crystal superalloys. The investigation by Tan and coworkers indicated that the Re content in m  phase was 18.6 at. % in a Ru-free alloy and 22.8 at. % in an alloy containing 3 wt % Ru  [21] . # 4.3. Elemental effect on TCP phase formation  

The signi ﬁ cant additions of alloying elements, such as Re, W, Mo and Cr, increased the tendency of TCP phase precipitation during thermal exposure in Ni-base single crystal superalloys. Many studies focused on the effect of elemental additions on the pre- cipitation behaviors for different kinds of TCP phases  [6,23] . The excessive additions of Cr and Mo had strong in ﬂ uence on the for- mation of  s  phase and  m  phase (or P phase), respectively  [14,24,25] . Furthermore, both Co and Ru were considered as the potential microstructural stabilizer in previous studies  [6,26] , while the microstructural stability at high temperature in the experimental alloys was not improved because of the high level of Co and Ru additions in this research. In addition, previous study suggested that a decrease in  g 0   volume fraction or the degree of partitioning of the TCP formers in  g  phase could reduce the level of  g  supersatu- ration  [3] . The alloy SC2221 showed the lower  g 0   volume fraction after thermal exposure at 1100    C for 2 h and the higher TCP phase area fraction after 1100    C for 1000 h compared to alloy SC2122 ( Figs. 4 and 5 ). Therefore, the decreased  g 0   volume fraction could not reduce the level of  g  supersaturation in this study. Since the thermodynamic driving force for TCP phase formation resulted from the supersaturation of refractory elements in the  g  matrix, the elemental partitioning between  g  and  g 0   phases, especially element Re had strong in ﬂ uence on the formation of TCP phases. There was a large propensity to form TCP phases at 1100    C in alloy SC2221 due to high level of TCP formers Cr and Mo additions (8.8 at. %) and resulted in a high degree of the Re partitioning ratio (16.0) and the total content of TCP formers (Co  þ  Cr  þ  Re  þ  Mo  þ  W  ¼  47.1 at. %). In addition, the partitioning ratio of Re were 8.0 and 8.8, while the total content of Co  þ  Cr  þ  Re  þ  Mo  þ  W in the  g  matrix were 40.3 at. % and 41.5 at. % after thermal exposure at 1100    C for 2 h in alloys SC2212 and SC2122, respectively. The values were lower than that of alloy SC2221, since the total content of Cr and Mo in alloys SC2212 (7.9 at. %) and SC2122 (5.7 at. %) was less than that in alloy SC2221 (8.8 at. %). Therefore, the high level of Re partitioning ratio  

and the total content of TCP formers in the  g  matrix of alloy SC2221 accounted for the larger TCP area fraction after thermal exposure at 1100    C, compared to those of alloys SC2212 and SC2122 ( Fig. 4  and Table 2 ).  

Based on the microstructural characteristics and phase identi- ﬁ cation in this study, it seems that the amount of R phase in alloy SC2212 ( Fig. 3 (a)) was obviously higher than that in alloy SC2122 ( Fig. 3 (c)) after thermal exposure at 950    C for 1000 h. In addition, the thermodynamic calculations predicted that the mole fraction of R phase in alloy SC2212 with high Cr content was higher than that of alloy SC2122 ( Fig. 9 (b) and (c)). However, the  m  phase mole fraction in high Mo alloy SC2122 exhibited higher value than that of alloy SC2212. Therefore, it is postulated that R phase preferred to precipitate in alloy with high level of Cr addition while  m  phase preferred to form in alloy with high level of Mo addition, respec- tively. Furthermore, it is worth noting that the thermodynamic calculations are of great potential in precipitation simulation of multicomponent alloys, although some results are demanded to improve the accuracy and reliability.  

# 5. Conclusion  

Detailed information are presented on the morphologies, chemical compositions, phase transformation and thermodynamic calculations of  m  phase and R phase after thermal exposure at 950    C and 1100    C in three Ru-containing single crystal superalloys containing high Co (14.5 wt. %) and different levels of Cr (3.5 e 6.2 wt. %), Mo (1.0 e 2.4 wt. %) and Ru (2.6 e 3.9 wt. %) additions. The conclusions can be summarized as follows:  

(1) In the alloy with high level of Co, Cr and Mo additions,  m phase precipitated in the matrix of the dendrite core after thermal exposure at 950    C for 50 h, while the precipitation of R phase was observed with extending the exposure time to 200 h. After thermal exposure at 1100    C for 50 h, R phase was formed quickly and became the dominate phase for 1000 h. (2) Based on the microstructural evolution,  m  phase was the dominant phase after thermal exposure at 950    C for 1000 h, while R phase was served as the thermodynamic equilibrium phase after thermal exposure at 1100    C for 1000 h in three experimental alloys. (3) R phase and  m  phase were enriched in Co, Re, W, Cr, and Mo, especially Co. R phase contained more Cr, while  m  phase contained more Mo in the alloy with high level of Co, Cr and Mo additions. (4) The stress accelerated the thermodynamic equilibrium of R phase during creep process at 1100    C and 140 MPa. The precipitation of R phase was promoted under the in ﬂ uence of high stress and temperature. (5) The R phase and  m  phase preferred to precipitate in high Co alloys with high level of Cr and Mo additions, respectively.
