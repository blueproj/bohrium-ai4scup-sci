# Inﬂuence of composition on monomodal versus multimodal  c 0 precipitation in Ni–Al–Cr alloys  

T. Rojhirunsakool •   S. Meher •   J. Y. Hwang •       S. Nag •   J. Tiley •   R. Banerjee  

Received: 11 April 2012 / Accepted: 13 August 2012 / Published online: 29 August 2012   Springer Science+Business Media, LLC 2012  

Abstract This study investigates the inﬂuence of alloy   composition on  c 0 precipitation in Ni–8Al–8Cr and Ni–10Al– 10Cr at.% during continuous cooling from a supersolvus temperature. When subjected to the same cooling rate, Ni–8Al–8Cr develops a monomodal population, whereas Ni–10Al–10Cr develops a multimodal (primarily bimodal)     population of  c 0 precipitates. The bimodal  c 0 precipitate size distribution in Ni–10Al–10Cr alloy can be attributed to two successive nucleation bursts during continuous cooling while   the monomodal  c 0 size distribution in Ni–8Al–8Cr results from a single nucleation burst followed by a longer time— wider temperature window for nucleation resulting in a larger number density of precipitates. Three-dimensional atom probe investigations reveal that while local equilibrium is   achieved across the  c / c 0 interface, far-ﬁeld  c  compositions still retain an excess of Al and a depletion of Cr with respect to equilibrium, thus giving direct experimental evidence of the driving force for subsequent nucleation bursts during con- tinuous cooling in case of Ni–10Al–10Cr. Contrastingly, in case of the Ni–8Al–8Cr alloy, while the  c  phase retains an excess of Al and a depletion of Cr with respect to equilibrium after continuous cooling to room temperature, the additional driving force is consumed during subsequent isothermal  

annealing by growth of the large number of  c 0 precipitates without any new nucleation.  

# Introduction  

Ni–Al–Cr forms the basis of many technologically important and commercially used Ni-base superalloys. The microstruc- ture primarily consists of ﬁnely dispersed precipitates of the   ordered  c 0 phase, with the L1 2  structure, within a disordered   face-centered cubic  c  matrix. The  c – c 0 microstructure in these alloys can be controlled via a combination of composition and cooling rate from the high temperature single  c  phase ﬁeld during processing. The resulting morphology and size distri-   bution of  c 0 precipitates determine the mechanical properties of these alloys [ 1 ,  2 ]. Microstructural evolution in Ni–Al–Cr system is strongly dependent on the solute concentration.   Increase in Al content increases the  c 0 solvus temperature,   whereas increase in Cr content typically decreases the  c 0 solvus   temperature [ 3 ,  4 ]. The inﬂuence of cooling rate on  c 0 precip- itation has been extensively investigated. Faster cooling rates from supersolvus temperatures, typically lead to the formation   of a monomodal size distribution of reﬁned  c 0 precipitates [ 5 – 7 ]. In contrast, relatively slower cooling rates lead to the   formation of  c 0 precipitates with a bimodal size distribution or in some cases even a multimodal size distribution [ 5 ,  8 – 12 ].   The development of multiple size ranges of  c 0 precipitates during continuous cooling has often been attributed to multiple distinct nucleation bursts at different undercoolings below the   c 0 solvus [ 8 – 10 ]. These multiple nucleation bursts in turn result from the complex interplay of continuously increasing ther- modynamic driving force for nucleation (chemical free energy difference) due to increasing undercooling, reduction in this driving force due to previous nucleation events, and,the rapidly declining diffusivity of alloying elements with decreasing temperature. At lower undercoolings (or higher temperatures,   just below the  c 0 solvus temperature) lower driving force for nucleation coupled with higher diffusivities leads to the ﬁrst   burst of c 0 nucleation forming the ﬁrst generation of precipitates with low nucleation density, which will be referred to as pri-   mary  c 0 precipitates. It should be noted that industry often refers   to this ﬁrst generation of  c 0 precipitates formed during contin-   uous cooling, as secondary  c 0 , since they refer to the  c 0 retained from the casting process, that does not dissolve during super- solvus solutionizing, as primary  c 0 . At higher undercoolings reduced diffusivity of atoms leads to supersaturation of c 0 -forming elements away from the diffusion ﬁelds of the   growing primary  c 0 precipitates, and coupled with a greater thermodynamic driving force results in further bursts of nucleation consequently forming what will be referred to as   secondary (or tertiary in some cases)  c 0 precipitates with a high nucleation density [ 5 ,  9 ,  10 ]. Radis et al. [ 9 ] in a recent paper have employed classical nucleation theory involving long- range diffusion of atoms to understand nucleation kinetics of both monomodal, as well as multimodal size distributions of   c 0 precipitates in the commercial Ni-base superalloy UDIMET 720. Furthermore, Wen et al. [ 10 ] have developed a phase ﬁeld method to understand the formation of bimodal size distribu-   tion of  c 0 precipitates during continuous cooling at various cooling rates.  

Atom probe tomography (APT) has been extensively used for three-dimensional (3D) characterization of the morphol- ogy, size distribution, and, composition of ﬁne nanometer   scale  c 0 precipitates in Ni-base alloys and these studies have been extensively reviewed in recent articles in the published literature [ 1 ,  2 ,  6 ,  7 ,  13 – 16 ]. The primary emphasis of these studies has been the determination of the size, morphology,   and composition of the  c 0 precipitates within the  c  matrix during the early stages of precipitation in these alloys during annealing after rapid quenching the alloy from the high tem- perature single  c  phase ﬁeld. Therefore, typically these studies have focused on a monomodal size distribution of reﬁned   c 0 precipitates [ 5 – 7 ]. In addition, such APT studies have also focused on the partitioning of the alloying additions between these two phases and the segregation of certain alloying   additions to the  c / c 0 interface as well as to grain boundaries.  

In this study, precipitation of  c 0 during continuous cooling of two Ni–Al–Cr alloys, Ni–8Al–8Cr and Ni–10Al–10Cr (compositions in at.%), at the same nomi- nal cooling rate of 14   C/min (substantially slower than water quenching) has been thoroughly investigated high- lighting the following salient features:  

i. Effect of solute content on temperature–time window   for precipitation of  c 0 during continuous cooling in samples subjected to prior supersolvus annealing.   ii. Interplay of  c 0 solvus temperature and retained solute supersaturation during continuous cooling affecting  

the c – c 0 microstructural development speciﬁcally focusing on the multiple nucleation bursts of   c 0 precipitates.  

# Experimental  

Arc-melted ingots of nominal composition Ni–8Al–8Cr and Ni–10Al–10Cr at.% were used for these experiments. As-cast materials were electro-discharge machined into thin sections and were subsequently  c  solution heat treated at 1150   C for 30 min and then continuous cooled in furnace at an average rate of 14   C/min in an argon atmosphere. Samples for transmission electron microscopy (TEM) were prepared via mechanical grinding, followed by dimple grinding and,ﬁnally ion-beam milling until electron transparency. Ion-beam millingwasdonebylow energyArona Fischionemodel 1010 system, Export, PA. TEM samples were conducted on a FEI Tecnai F20 ﬁeld emission gun transmission electron micro- scope, Hillsboro, Oregon operated at 200 kV. Energy ﬁltered transmission electron microscopy (EFTEM) images were obtained using Cr–M edge (at 42 eV). Samples for APT were prepared using the dual-beam focused ion-beam technique using a FEI Nova Nanolab 200 system, Hillsboro, Oregon. APT experiments were conducted using a CAMECA local electrode atom probe 3000X HR instrument, Madison, WI. All APT experiments were carried out in the laser evaporation mode at a temperature of 50 K with pulse rate of 160 kHz and target evaporation of 0.5 %. Data analyses were carried out TM   using CAMECA IVAS 3.6 software.

In addition, the lower temperatures and diffusivities also kinetically hinder any second nucleation burst. This also   explains the monomodal  c 0 size distribution in case of the Ni–8Al–8Cr alloy.  

# Summary and conclusions  

This study investigates the inﬂuence of composition on   precipitation of  c 0 during continuous cooling of two Ni–Al– Cr alloys, Ni–8Al–8Cr and Ni–10Al–10Cr (compositions in at.%),  at the same nominal cooling rate . The results of this investigation can be summarized as follows:  

• While Ni–8Al–8Cr exhibits a monomodal size distri-   bution of  c 0 precipitates, a multimodal size distribution is observed in Ni–10Al–10Cr sample when both alloys are subjected to the same nominal cooling rate.

 • In case of Ni–10Al–10Cr, the primary  c 0   occurs as a result of the ﬁrst nucleation burst at higher temperatures near the   c 0 solvus temperature,i.e., at low undercooling. While these   primary  c 0 precipitates grow rather rapidly due to the fast diffusion rates, thereby reduce the average supersaturation of the  c  matrix, only local equilibrium is established   between the  c  and  c 0 compositions across the interface. The far-ﬁeld  c  composition still retains a supersaturation of Al and an under-saturation of Cr, leading to sufﬁcient driving force for a second nucleation burst at lower temperatures.  

• In case of Ni–8Al–8Cr, the substantially lower  c 0   solvus   temperature results in the primary  c 0 nucleation burst taking place at a much lower temperature as compared to the Ni–10Al–10Cr alloy. Nucleation takes place over a wider temperature window, leading to a larger   number density of highly reﬁned  c 0 precipitates. The shorter inter-precipitates distances and slower kinetics prevent any second nucleation burst in this alloy.

 • The higher equilibrium volume fraction of  c 0 , coupled   with the higher  c 0 solvus temperature for the Ni–10Al– 10Cr alloy, as compared with the Ni–8Al–8Cr alloy,   has a strong inﬂuence on the number of  c 0 nucleation bursts observed during continuous cooling, as well as the temperatures at which these occur.
