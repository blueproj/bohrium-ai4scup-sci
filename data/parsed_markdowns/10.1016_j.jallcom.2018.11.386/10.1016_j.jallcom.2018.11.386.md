The microstructure evolution and strengthening mechanism of a  g 0 -strengthening superalloy prepared by induction-assisted laser solid forming (LSF) were clari ﬁ ed by an investigation of the LSFed IN-738LC alloy with different solution treatments (at 1070    C, 1120    C and 1160    C). The results show that both the as-deposited and heat-treated deposits were dominated by the columnar grains with a width of 100 e 270  m m. The equiaxed grains mainly originated from the columnar-to-equiaxed transition (CET) during LSF while a small portion of the equiaxed grains originated from recrystallization during the heat treatment, due to the relatively low temperature gradient in the induction-assisted LSF. Fine grain strengthening was still working in the columnar-grains dominated LSFed IN-738LC deposits. However,  g 0 strengthening was found to play a major role in the strengthening mechanism by comparing the microstructure and properties of different heat-treated deposits. The  g 0   dissolution and coarsening ki- netics were calculated and the interaction mechanism between the dislocation and  g 0   phase in LSFed IN- 738LC was clari ﬁ ed. The deformation of the coarse (d  >  200 nm) near cubic  g 0   was found to be bene ﬁ cial to the increase of ultimate tensile strength (UTS) and elongation (EL), while a brittle continuous phase formed at the grain boundary (GB) which was rich in Ni, Cr, Al and Ti was found to be detrimental to the deposit's plasticity. The  g 0   phase deformation and desquamating, carbide breaking and GB phase breaking are the predominant fracture failure mechanisms. On the basis of relatively high strength, the IN-738LC deposits with a lower yield strength (YS)/UTS ratio than the cast and SLMed ones were ob- tained when solution-treated at 1070    C.

# 1. Introduction  

As one of the typical laser-based additive manufacturing tech- nologies, laser solid forming (LSF) is characterized by near-net  

As a typical  g 0 -strengthening nickel-base superalloy, IN-738LC is strengthened by precipitating ordered Ni 3 (Al,Ti)- g 0   phase in the  g matrix. Owing to its exceptional combination of excellent high temperature creep properties and remarkable resistance to hot corrosion, it is widely used in the hot section components of both land-based and aero gas turbine engines [ 9 , 10 ]. However, the weldability of IN-738LC alloy is poor due to its high Al and Ti content (Al  þ  Ti  >  6 wt%) [ 11 e 13 ]. Thus, under the large thermal stress generated by repeated rapid heating and cooling, the LSF of IN-738LC is prone to cracking [ 14 , 15 ], which limits its performance at high temperatures [ 16 e 18 ].  

In recent years, researchers used induction preheating of the original LSF system (known as induction-assisted LSF), and it has been applied to the formation and reparation of multiple alloys including nickel-based alloys [ 19 e 21 ], titanium alloys [ 22 , 23 ], steels [ 24 , 25 ] and Ni-WC composite [ 26 , 27 ]. These studies proved that induction preheating can signi ﬁ cantly reduce stress, prevent deformation and cracking and improve the deposition layer quality.  

However, the microstructure of the deposit is often affected by preheating [ 28 ]. Our previous work systematically studied the microstructure of LSFed IN-738LC with and without induction preheating [ 19 ]. It found that high-temperature induction pre- heating can cause the following changes: increasing the equiaxed grains volume fraction, aggravating micro segregation, changing carbide morphology and, importantly, increasing the  g 0   particle size.  

Many studies have been carried out on the strengthening mechanism of  g 0 -strengthening nickel-base superalloy, most of which were in the  ﬁ eld of traditional casting and focused on the strengthening of the  g 0   phase (closely related to their size and morphology) [ 29 , 30 ]. Certainly, some researchers also considered the effect of grain size on the mechanical properties of the poly- crystalline alloys [ 31 , 32 ]. Furthermore, heat treatment has been proved to have a profound effect on the microstructure (modifying g 0   characteristics), thereby in ﬂ uencing the mechanical properties of Ni-based superalloy [ 33 e 36 ].  

Nonetheless, as stated above, the microstructure of induction- assisted LSFed alloy is usually different from that of the typical cast ones [ 6 ], as well as LSFed ones. Therefore, to develop a suitable heat treatment schedule for the induction-assisted LSFed alloys, the dissolution and coarsening behaviors of  g 0   phases and the inter- action mechanism between dislocations and  g 0   particles should be further understood.  

In this study, the grain size and texture in the deposits of as- LSFed and heat-treated states were analyzed to investigate the ef- fect of columnar grain width on the mechanical properties. The  g 0 dissolution and coarsening kinetics were calculated and the inter- action between the precipitation phase and dislocations are also discussed under different heat treatment conditions in order to obtain a deeper understanding of the relationship between the microstructure and mechanical properties of induction-assisted LSFed IN-738LC alloy.  

# 2. Experimental procedures  

The IN-738LC gas-atomized powder, with size ranges from 53 to 150  m m in diameter, was used as a deposition material, the nominal composition (wt.%) of which is listed in  Table 1 . The 304L stainless steel (with dimensions of 80    12    70 mm) was used as a sub- strate. The substrate surface was sanded by sandpaper and cleaned by acetone before LSF was applied.  

The experiment was carried out on a LSF system which consisted of a 2 kW YAG laser, a four-axis numerical control working table, a powder feeding system with a coaxial nozzle and a glove box  ﬁ lled with Argon (Ar) gas. The preheating device added to the original LSF system was operated using a middle-frequency inductive power while an infrared thermometer was used to monitor the temperature of the substrate in real time. The pre- heating of the system was controlled through a negative feedback control. The LSF processing parameters are listed in  Table 2 . The laser scan path and the LSFed IN-738LC deposit (with dimensions of 70  10  10 mm) are shown in  Fig. 1 a and b, respectively.  

After LSF, the deposits were heat treated at different solution temperatures (1070    C, 1120    C and 1160    C) for 2 hours and then air cooled (AC) and age treated at 845    C for 24 hours with AC. Of these, solution-treated at 1120    C for 2 h with AC and aged at 845    C for 24 h with AC is the standard heat treatment regime (SHT, 1120    C/2 h/AC and 845    C/24 h/AC) for cast IN-738LC alloy [ 37 ]. The as-deposited IN-738LC alloy was used for comparison.  

Specimens were cut at the same position for each deposit (see the vertical section marked in  Fig. 1 b) by a wire electro discharge machining device in order to allow microstructure observation; the remaining parts were machined to a standard tensile specimen with a 22 mm gauge length along the y direction ( Fig. 1 b) and 4 mm gauge diameter. In order to reduce the effect of the position on microstructure observation, the middle of the section in each specimen was studied. The specimens were chemically etched (5 g FeCl 3 , 20 ml HCl  þ  100 ml C 2 H 5 OH) for optical metallography and further etched electrolytic ally in a solution of 12 ml H 3 PO 4  þ  48 ml H 2 SO 4  þ  40 ml HNO 3  to examine the morphology of the precipitates, including  g - g 0   eutectic,  g 0   phases and carbides. The microstructure and texture were analyzed by an OLYMPUS-GX71 optical microscope (OM) and a TESCAN VEGA IILMH scanning electron microscope (SEM) equipped with an en- ergy dispersive X-ray (EDS) and an Oxford Nordlys Max2 electron backscatter diffraction (EBSD). Phase analysis was carried out by a TESCAN MIRA3 XMU  ﬁ eld emission scanning electron microscope

 (FE-SEM) and a TECNai F30 G2 transmission electron microscope

 (TEM). The micro segregation was characterized by a SHIMADZU- 1720 electron probe microanalysis technique (EPMA). Tensile tests were performed at room temperature using an INSTRON11- 3382 tensile testing machine, which was controlled by displace- ment and the displacement rate was 2 mm/min. The fracture sur- face was characterized by SEM.

# 4. Discussion  

# 4.1. Fine grain strengthening  

# 4.1.1. Grain morphology evolution  

It is generally known that the  < 100 >  directions are the preferred growth directions in Ni-based superalloys. The existence of a ﬁ brous texture in the deposits occurs because only one of the < 100 >  directions of columnar dendrites was oriented antiparallel to the heat  ﬂ ow direction and the two other  < 100 >  directions were free [ 39 ]. During LSF, the heat  ﬂ ow direction of the area near the center is nearly antiparallel to the deposition direction, whilst that of both side edges on the vertical section of the deposit is inclined to the left and right, at an angle to the vertical direction. This is due to the cooling of the already deposited layers [ 20 ]. As a result, the growth direction of columnar grains at both side edges on the vertical section of the deposit will deviate from the vertical at a certain angle, as shown in  Fig. 2 b 1  and c 1 . For the equiaxed grains in the deposits, it is reasonable to think that most of them are formed during the solidi ﬁ cation, since their volume fraction changes slightly before and after the heat treatment. This relates to the columnar-to-equiaxed transition (CET) [ 40 e 42 ], which is consid- erably dependent on the temperature gradient  G  and growth rate  V . At such a high preheating temperature (1050    C), the temperature gradient could be reduced by an order of magnitude [ 19 ] and will be low enough for the occurrence of CET [ 43 ]. A certain proportion of equiaxed grains appeared in the as-deposited IN-738LC specimen ( Fig. 2 a 1 ).  

Meanwhile, after the heat treatment, some of the equiaxed grains formed as the result of the recrystallization of the deposits. Generally, the LSF process undergoes a high cyclic heating and cooling regime and the accommodation of thermal expansion and then contraction, caused by the severe thermal excursions and steep thermal gradients local to the deposited material, will lead to large thermal stress in the LSFed specimens. After deposition, the thermal stress that is not released from within the deposits will ﬁ nally result in the residual stress, which is the driving force of recrystallization [ 44 e 46 ]. However, preheating has proven to be effective in reducing the thermal stress and residual stress in the deposits [ 24 ], which can reduce the driving force for recrystalliza- tion. Accordingly, the equiaxed grains volume fraction did not in- crease much after the solution treatment ( Fig. 2 ).

# 4.3. Fracture failure mechanism  

As mentioned above, the coarse  g 0   particles will be stretched into a rod shape and eventually detached from the  g  matrix to form the micro-voids during the tensile deformation ( Fig. 11 ), then the micro-voids will combine and grow to form dimples ( Fig. 6 a 1 -d 1 ), which is the characteristic of ductile fracture. However, more  ﬁ ne- dimple zones, which are close to the brittle fracture morphology, appear with the increase of solution temperature ( Fig. 6 a e d).  Fig.13 shows the microstructure on the longitudinal section of fracture surfaces of the deposits. It can be seen that some carbides were broken by the tensile stress and the cracks were almost perpen- dicular to the tensile direction ( Fig. 13 a, d and e). Combining Fig. 13 b e d with  Fig. 6 b e d, the secondary cracks were mainly the result of the breaking of the GB phases and they propagate along the GBs. Moreover, the  ﬁ ne-dimple zone should correspond to the intergranular fracture faces, on which the broken GB phases can still be found ( Fig. 13 c).  

The growth of carbide is closely related to the element segre- gation and growth space during the solidi ﬁ cation. The segregation is usually lighter and the growth space is always smaller during LSF, which leads to the formation of  ﬁ ne, blocky (near cubic) carbides with a size approximately 1 e 2  m m in LSFed IN-738LC [ 19 ] ( Fig. 13 ). The blocky carbides are usually de-bonded from the matrix during the tensile fracture of nickel-base superalloy [ 79 , 80 ]. The carbides breaking has also been found during the tensile deformation of nickel-based superalloys, Alloy 617 and Haynes 230 and 282 [ 81 , 82 ]. Interestingly, both phenomena were observed during the tensile deformation of LSFed IN-738LC alloy ( Fig. 11 b e c). More importantly, the cracks in the broken carbides always form along the diagonal of the near octahedron block ( Fig. 13 a, c and f). Dis- locations always move along the  < 110 >  directions on the {111} planes in the fcc-carbides [ 83 ]. The outer surface of the octahedron is usually formed along {111} planes during the growth of the fcc structure [ 84 ]. Accordingly, the breaking of carbides in the LSFed IN-738LC is not caused by the dislocation cutting since the cracks always form along the diagonal of the near-octahedron block ( Fig. 13 a, c and f). Because the stress is very likely to concentrate on the corners of the near-octahedron block, as indicated by the dis- locations pile up in  Fig. 13 f, the carbides will be broken along the diagonal of the near-octahedron block once the concentrated stress exceeds their strength limit ( Fig. 14 a). Both the breaking and shedding of the carbides will form the micro-voids, the coalescent of which will  ﬁ nally form the dimples with the carbides in the center ( Fig. 6 a 1 -d 1 ). Since the carbides are frequently found in the large-dimple zone, the trans-granular fracturing should mainly occur along the interdendritic region where the carbides are distributed ( Fig. 14 b).  

Since the presence of re ﬁ ned carbides should be helpful for the improvement of strength and plasticity, the decrease of EL with the increase of solution temperature should be attributed to the continuous distribution of GB phases ( Fig. 4 ). The GB  g - g 0   eutectic in the typical cast,  g 0 -strengthening superalloy is usually evenly distributed, which is good for the ductility [ 85 ]. However, the  g - g 0 eutectic forming elements will be signi ﬁ cantly enriched at the narrow GB only at the end of solidi ﬁ cation due to the high cooling rate in LSF, which makes the GB  g - g 0   eutectic in the LSFed IN-738LC have a near-continuous distribution [ 19 , 20 ] ( Fig. 4 a). The dissolu- tion of the  g - g 0   eutectic should increase with the solution tem- perature, but the eutectic composition may not be diffused evenly during the solution treatment. # 5. Conclusions  

The relationship between microstructure and mechanical properties was investigated through induction-assisted laser solid forming (LSF) of IN-738LC alloy. The main conclusions are:  

1) The columnar-to-equiaxed transition (CET) could occur during the induction-assisted LSF due to the relatively low temperature gradient and the recrystallization could also occur during the heat treatment. However, the deposits were still mainly composed of the columnar grains, with a width of 100 e 270  m m. The columnar width and the yield strength (YS)/ultimate tensile strength (UTS) of the deposits followed the Hall-Petch rela- tionship well when standard heat treatment (SHT) was applied but not when the heat treatment regime deviated from this. 2) The dissolution rate of the  ﬁ ne  g 0   particles (radius  z 46 nm) could be approximately 1.6 times that of the coarse ones (radius z 83 nm) when solution treated at 1160    C. The  g 0   coarsening still occurred during the solution treatment below the  g 0   solvus temperature and the coarsening rate was approximately 3 3 0.068 nm /s and 0.17 nm /s for the solution treatments at 1070    C and 1120    C, respectively. 3) A modi ﬁ ed relational map between the  g 0   diameter and strength of LSFed IN-738LC was established. According to this, the theoretically maximized strength of the deposits is approxi- mately 200 nm and the predominant strengthening mechanism was the dislocation cutting and not bypassing. 4) The coarse (d  >  200 nm), near cubic  g 0   particles were deformed during tensile deformation, which contributes to the increase of ultimate tensile strength (UTS) and elongation (EL). 5) The carbides fracture was a brittle fracture process, which is not caused by dislocation cutting. More brittle continuous phases that are rich in Ni, Cr, Al and Ti would precipitate during the aging treatment with the increase of solution temperature and this was detrimental to the deposit's plasticity. 6) The predominant fracture failure mechanism was the  g 0   phase deformation and desquamating, carbides breaking and desquamating and grain boundary GB phase breaking. The relative best match between tensile strength and plasticity can be obtained when solution-treated at 1070    C.
