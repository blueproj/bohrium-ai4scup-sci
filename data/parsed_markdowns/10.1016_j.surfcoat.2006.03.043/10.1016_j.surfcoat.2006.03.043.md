# Hot corrosion behavior of MCrAlY coatings on IN738LC  

⁎ R. Mobarra , A.H. Jafari, M. Karaminezhaad  

Metallurgy and Materials Engineering Department, School of Engineering Technology, Shahid Bahonar University, Kerman, Iran  

Received 6 December 2005; accepted in revised form 21 March 2006 Available online 22 May 2006  

# Abstract  

The hot corrosion behavior of CoNiCrAlY coatings deposited on IN738LC super alloy using low pressure plasma spray (LPPS) was 2   investigated using samples immersed in a solution of Na 2 SO 4 – 10 wt.% NaCl and dried as to be covered with a 2.5 mg/cm costing. Specimens were heat-treated in furnace at 850 °C and after 24 h in the furnace were accurately weighed. Microstructural characterizations were carried out by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) equipped with energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) indicated interactions within microstructure of the coating with clearly diminished thickness of MCrAlY coating. Phase transformation and  β -NiAl phase depletion in the MCrAlY coatings were shown to be directly related to the thermal cycles experienced by the samples and revealed outward diffusion of Al in the coating and the inward migration of Ni toward the coating causing  β → γ ′  phase transformation as the main cause of instability of the  β -NiAl.

# 1. Introduction  

Gas turbines as power generation facilities transform kinetic energy of combustion products of a fuel to rotation of the turbine blades and mechanical energy thus supplied turns into electrical power energy. As such, any temperature and pressure increase in the inlet gases can directly translate to improved efficiency of power generation. Therefore, intensive efforts have been made to increase the operating temperature up to 1500 °C  [1] . MCrAlY (M=Ni, Co, Fe or their combination) overlay coatings are commonly utilized to protect the blade surfaces against oxidation and hot corrosion in gas turbines. Such coatings are usually deposited on turbine blades of NI or Co based super alloys by vacuum plasma spraying (VPS), chemical vapor deposition (CVD) or low pressure plasma spray (LPPS) processes. Unfortunately, these coatings degrade during service due to oxidation and hot corrosion as well as some mechanical reasons. The most serious degradation mechanisms apart from the aforementioned are cracking and spallation of the coating itself by a mismatch in thermal coefficient of expansion between the coating and substrate. Degradation could be caused by frequent oxide scale spalling or by formation of less protective scales  [2] . Cracking, spalling and mechanical aspects of substrate – coating interaction has been studied by several researchers.  β -NiAl phase transformation and Al depletion have been studied by Cheruvu et al.  [3]  while Taylor and Evans  [4] have studied the formation of diffusion zone and phase transformation in LPPS MCrAlY coatings. Despite copious research on cyclic oxidation, relatively little research is carried out on hot corrosion of these coatings. In this study, using energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS), equipped scanning electron microscope (SEM) and X-ray diffraction (XRD) the chemical composition as well as microstructural characteriza- tion and the subsequent evolutions in the course of simulated conditions was investigated.

# 3.2. Microstructural evolution of MCrAlY coatings during hot corrosion  

Fig. 5  depicts the microstructure of coating following a 24- h cycle. In addition to the  β -NiAl and  γ  phases, it displays clearly identifiable interdiffusion zone at the interface of coating and substrate, and a  β -NiAl depleted region in the boundary of coating and oxide layer formed under the coating. According to  Fig. 6 , specimens exposed to hot corrosion conditions exhibit a divergent change in the thickness of CoNiCrAlY coating and the oxide layer with the former decreasing and the latter increasing. It is well known that MCrAlY coatings protect the substrate super alloy against oxidation and hot corrosion by forming a protective oxide layer  [5] . Therefore, any reposition rendering a heavier, more substantial oxide layer can only slow down the degradation rate.  

In fact in these coatings  β -NiAl acts as an aluminum reservoir for the formation of the protective alumina oxide layer. As the protective oxide spalls during hot corrosion, aluminum in the coating diffuses outwards to replenish the surface oxide layer. Phase transformation prediction in multi-component systems is notoriously complicated and most often accessible only through approximation by reducing the system into the closest ternary system available  [2] .  

Examination of the NiCrAl and NiCoAl phase diagrams reveals that both chromium and cobalt stabilize the  γ + β  two- phase field. By considerations of atomic radius of Cr and Co

 (140 pm (pico meter) and 135 pm) and their crystal structure

 (bcc and hexagonal), chromium can be considered as substitutional interchange for cobalt. Following the same argument, it is possible to extend this treatment to the chromium substitutional elements of tungsten and molybdenum (135 and 145 pm) in the NiCrAl model representation adventing them for cobalt atoms and thus quantitatively speaking as additions to the cobalt concentration. The aluminum type elements of titanium, niobium and tantalum, that their atomic radius and crystal structure are 140 pm and hexagonal, 145 pm and bcc, and 145 pm and bcc respectively, could be considered to follow the same behavior in the NiCrAl model. Thus, the NiCoAl approximation should be as follows: Ni – (Co, Cr, W, Mo) – (Al, Ti Nb, Ta)  [2] . For IN738LC and CoNiCrAlY, the prototypical examples are 61Ni – 31Co – 8Al and 34Ni – 57Co – 9Al respectively  [2] .

In an attempt to ascertain the hot corrosion behavior of the thus formed, direct weight change of coating as a function of hot corrosion cycles at 850 °C is presented in  Fig. 13 . Up to 120 cumulative hours of experimentation, all specimens initially gained weight owing to the formation of protective oxide scale resultant to the thermal cycling they experienced reaching a 2   maximum value of 0.015 mg/cm as the ensuing spallation of the coating starts affecting the results. Owing to the escalating nature of such weight losses with increased cycles, a steep negative slope develops with a scatter in results, which is the direct consequence of the nature of this phenomenon.  

# 4. Conclusions  

The following conclusion can be drawn from the present investigation:  

(1) Microstructure of the as-deposited CoNiCrAlY coatings consisting  β -NiAl (b.c.c) and  γ  (f.c.c) is the primarily solid solution of cobalt, chromium and nickel. (2) During the hot corrosion phenomenon, the interdiffusion of substrate and the coating constituents occur and a  β - NiAl depleted zone forms retarding the growth and the thickness of the coating. (3) The  γ ,  β -NiAl and  γ ′ -AlNi 3  are the three main phases consistently formed throughout the interdiffusion zones in CoNiCrAlY – IN738LC clearly enhanced consequent to accelerated diffusion of aluminum into substrate during hot corrosion. (4) After experiencing 168 h of continuous hot corrosion conditions, phases such as  β -NiAl, Al 2 O 3 ,  γ ′ -AlNi 3 , and γ , Y 2 O 3 , AlYO 3  and Y 2 Al 5 O 12  start to propagate. (5) As compared with the specimens before the hot corrosion experiments, the XRD peaks representing the major phases of  β -NiAl and Al 2 O 3  decreased and increased respectively indicating the final rush of the aluminum atoms resulting from decomposition of  β -NiAl to  γ ′ - AlNi 3  and more aluminum to build up the alumina “ protective wall ” . (6) The ever increasing initial weight gain of the hot corrosion specimens due to the formation of a protective oxide scale continues to rise up to approximately 120 h of exposure when the samples exhibit a maximum value and following that they show considerable loss of weight with increasing cycles, due to oxide spallation.
