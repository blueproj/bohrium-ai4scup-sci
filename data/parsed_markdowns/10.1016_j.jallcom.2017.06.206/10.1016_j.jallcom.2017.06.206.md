# Abstract  

The microstructure of a transient liquid phase (TLP) bonded nickel base superalloy using B- containing filler metal after completion of isothermal solidification can usually be described by a  eutectic-free joint centerline with extensive in-situ boride precipitation in the diffusion affected zone  which in turn can affect the joint properties. Therefore, designing a proper post-bond heat treatment is  needed to produce a robust joint. This paper addresses the microstructure evolution mechanism during  post-bond heat treatment (PBHT) of TLP bonded wrought IN718 nickel base superalloy. PBHT at  1150  ο C, which is lower than the solvus temperature of the borides, for 12 hrs resulted in boride-free  joint with uniform chemical composition and enhanced aging response. The microstructure evolution  of the bond during PBHT was featured by fragmentation, break up and dissolution of Cr-Mo-Nb rich  borides. The dissolution of the borides at a temperature lower than their initial solvus temperature can  be attributed to the modification of boride composition during diffusion-induced homogenization  process associated with PBHT. Increasing PBHT time resulted in enhanced contribution of solid  solution strengthening in the joint centerline and stronger response to aging treatment due to alloying  elements homogenization.  

# Keywords  

Transient liquid phase bonding; Superalloy; Boride; Post-bond heat treatment; Homogenization  

# 1. Introduction  

High temperature brazing using boron-containing nickel based filler is an attractive standard process  for repairing/joining of superalloy [1]. Boron plays as the melting point depressant and provides  sufficient fluidity of the molten filler metal. However, considering the very low solubility and strong  positive segregation of B in the nickel based alloys, the liquid phase is transformed to boride- containing eutectic-type micro-constituents during cooling of the brazement [2-7]. This brittle and  low-temperature non-equilibrium solidification product can affect serviceability of the brazed  component [8-9].  

Transient liquid phase (TLP) bonding, also referred to as diffusion brazing MANUSCRIPT  can overcome this  reliability issue by allowing the liquid phase to solidify in different mechanism (i.e. diffusion-induced  isothermal solidification vs. cooling-induced athermal solidification) [10-11]. The diffusion of B into  the base metal (BM) during bonding process modifies the liquid composition and increases its  liquidus temperature leading to isothermal solidification of the liquid phase at the bonding  temperature. Therefore, the formation of segregation-induced boride phase in the joint centerline is  prohibited [12-13]. The solidification mechanism of the residual liquid in the joint gap depends on the  bonding time. When the bonding time is not sufficient for the completion of isothermal solidification,  the residual liquid in the joint centerline is transformed into non-equilibrium products during the  subsequent cooling (i.e. athermal solidification). The microstructural development in the athermal  solidification zone is mainly controlled by segregation of the melting point depressant (MPD)  elements, such as B and Si. The microstructure of athermal solidification zone usually consists of hard  and brittle intermetallic phases formed [3, 9, 10]. Despite the fact that isothermal solidification leads to fabrication a eutectic-free joint centerline, there  is still extensive borides precipitation at the substrate zone which is so called diffusion affected zone  (DAZ) [14-20]. The formation of DAZ is due to diffusion of boron into the BM during isothermal  solidification. The presence of strong boride formers such as Mo, W, Cr, and Nb in the BM helps the  formation of boride precipitates in DAZ. Pouranvari et al. [3, 5, 9] reported precipitation of Cr-Mo-Nb  rich borides in TLP bonding of IN718 using B-containing filler metals. Idowu et al. [12], reported  formation Cr-Mo-W rich M (C,B) blocky and plate-like borides in the DAZ of the IN738/Ni-Cr- 3 B 2  and M 5 B 3  boride in the TLP bonding of a  single crystal nickel based superalloy using a Ni-Cr-B interlayer.  

In metallurgical terms an ideal TLP joint is a bond (1) free from eutectic-type structure in the joint  centerline, (2) free from secondary precipitates in the DAZ and (3) with chemical composition and  microstructure as similar as possible to that of the base metal.  Therefore, after completion of  isothermal solidification, there is a need for a proper post bond heat treatment (PBHT) due to  following reasons:  

(A) To remove the boride precipitates in the DAZ: The presence of boride precipitates in the  DAZ can affect the bond properties from several aspects:  

(1)  Influence on the corrosion behavior:  Precipitation of Cr and Mo rich borides in the  DAZ requires short-range diffusion of Cr and Mo form the adjacent matrix creating an  

Cr and Mo depleted zone surrounding the precipitates. This reduces the local corrosion  resistance of the matrix [3, 5,11].  

(2)   Influence on the mechanical properties : Despite the fact that the boride- precipitates can have marginal effect on the shear properties of the bond due to their  spatially non-continuous distribution in along the joint/base metal interface [21],  however, it has been shown that they can affect high temperature tensile properties [22].  

(3)  Influence on the aging response:  nickel base superalloy can be influenced by compositional changes induced borides  precipitation in the DAZ. For example, it has been shown that the precipitation of Nb- rich precipitates in the DAZ of IN718 TLP joint leads to a significant depletion of Nb  in the surrounding of the borides making available less amount of Nb for  γ ´´  precipitation during aging treatment [23].  

(B) To produce a chemical homogeneity across the bond: The chemical composition of the as- isothermally solidified bond centerline differs from the BM. This can affect the strengthening of  bond zone, its corrosion behavior and also its aging response [24].  

Designing a proper PBHT and homogenization of the bond is a vital issue for producing a strong  robust joint. However, there is little emphasis on this issue in the open literature [21, 23, 25-28]. Some  researchers [25, 26] studied the elemental homogenization of the TLP bonded superalloys. For  example, kim et al. [25] studied the bonding of CMSX-2 single crytal superalloy using a Ni-Cr-B  interlayer and reported that the microstructure, hardness distribution and alloying elements in the  bonded zone was identical to those in the base metal after the homogenization treatment at 1250  o C/5h. Nakao et al. [26] found that homogenization process which is controlled by the low diffusivity  element can be modeled by using Fick's second law. Some other researchers [21, 23, 27, 28] reported  the PBHT conditions leading to removing boride precipitates in DAZ. For example, Pouranvari et al.  [21, 23] proposed that boride precipitate in TLP bonded GTD-111 and IN718 superalloy can be  o C/12 hr, respectively. Bakhtiari et al. [27]  reported that the best condition for PBHT of FSX-414 Co-based superalloy is 1225  o C/3 h, however,  some liquated zone was observed in the bond region due to high temperature of the PBHT.  

In the above mentioned work, there was no emphasis on the dissolution behavior of boride precipitates  in the DAZ and microstructural evolution mechanism during PBHT. This is the aim of this paper. In  this paper an approach to produced boride-free TLP bonded IN718 is introduced. The boride- containing centerline eutectic was removed by achieving complete isothermal solidification. Then, a  PBHT is proposed to fabricate the boride-free TLP bond in IN718 nickel based superalloy. Boride  evolution and homogenization process during PBHT of TLP bonded IN718 are analyzed.  

# 2. Experimental procedures  

The 10mm×5mm×5mm coupons of annealed wrought IN718 nickel based superalloy was TLP  bonded using a 50  µ m thick BNi-2 filler alloy. The chemical composition of the base metal was Ni– 18.26Fe–18.23Cr–4.85Nb–3.10Mo–0.93Ti–0.61Al–0.19Si in wt.%. The chemical composition of the  interlayer was Ni-7Cr-4.5Si-3.2B-3Fe in wt.%. The thickness of interlayer was 50 µ m. Bonding  

operation was carried out in a furnace under a vacuum of approximately 10 −5  Torr. The thermal  history of the samples is detailed as follows:  

(1) Bonding cycle: The bonding was carried out using standard solution treatment cycle for  wrought IN718 at 1050  ο C for 1h.  

(2) PBHT cycle: The as-bonded joints were post bond heat treated at 1150  C for 3, 6, 9 and 12  h.  

(3) Aging treatment: After TLP bonding and PBHT, the samples were double aged at 760  ºC/10h and 650 ºC/8h, according to standard double aging heat treatment of the IN718 alloy.  

Bonded specimens were sectioned perpendicular to the bond and then microstructural observation was  MANUSCRIPT made on cross-section of specimens using an optical microscope and a field emission scanning  electron microscope (FESEM). For microstructural examinations, specimens were etched using 10ml  HNO 3 -10ml C 2 H 4 O 2 -15ml HCl. Semi-quantitative chemical analyses of phases formed in the  diffusion affected zone were conducted by a JEOL 5900 FESEM equipped with an ultra thin window  Oxford Energy Dispersive X-ray Spectrometer (EDS) working at 20 kV accelerating voltage. Element  distribution across the joint region was analyzed using a JEOL JXA-8900R electron probe X-ray  microanalyzer (EMPA, Japan Electron Optics Ltd., Tokyo, Japan) equipped with line scan wavelength  dispersive spectrometry working at 20 kV accelerating voltage. The chemical composition of  isothermal solidification zone was measured using a calibrated EPMA analysis. Vickers micro- indentation test using a load of 100 g and dwell time of 20 s was used to assess the degree of the  homogenization after PBHT and aging response of the bond after aging treatment. The indentations  were performed along the centerline of the isothermal solidification zone which were spaced apart a  minimum 300 µm to prevent interactions between neighboring indentations. # 3.2. Approaches to produce boride-free DAZ  

To obtain a boride-free DAZ two approaches can be followed: The first one is bonding at high  temperature. It has been shown that increasing the bonding temperature can suppress the precipitation  of boride phase in the DAZ [30, 15].  However, this approach may induce incipient melting in the  base metal or prolong the required time to achieve complete isothermal solidification across the bond  [30]. The second approach is to apply a proper post bond heat treatment after completion of  isothermal solidification.  Two distinct types of PBHT can be utilized to remove boride precipitates:  

(1)  PBHT above the initial solvus temperature of the borides: temperature above the boride solvus temperature followed by rapid cooling will result in a boride-free  bond. According to Ni-B phase diagram, the solvus temperature for Ni However, as it was seen, the borides in IN718/BNi-2/IN718 are Cr-Mo-Nb rich precipitates. The  presence of strong boride former elements like Mo, Nb and Cr significantly increases the boride  solvus temperature.  Using Thermo-Calc [32] and the TCNI8 [33] thermodynamic database, the quasi- binary phase diagram for IN718/B system is calculated (Fig.3). According to Fig.3, the maximum  boron solubility in IN718 B is 0.00011 wt% at a temperature of 1225 °C. Therefore, PBHT at such  high temperature may leads to re-melting of the bond centerline and/or liquation of the BM.  

(2)  PBHT at temperature lower than the initial solvus temperature of the borides:  It is shown that  prolonged annealing of the joint at a temperature lower than the initial boride solvus temperature can  lead to reducing/eliminating the boride precipitates [23, 28].  Since, the dissolution of a precipitate is  diffusion-controlled process, the selection of temperature and time of the PBHT is critical. The PBHT  temperature (T ) should be less than re-melting temperature of the solidified zone but high enough  to dissolve the precipitates in reasonable time without inducing liquation in the base metal. In this  study, this approach is utilized to remove borides.  In this work, the bonding temperature was 1050 ºC,  therefore, considering the increase in re-melting temperature of the bond-line after isothermal  solidification, the PBHT temperature was selected as 1150  ο C. It should be noted that PBHT at lower  temperature was not successful in removing boride precipitates in a reasonable time. In the following  section, the mechanism of boride evolution during PBHT is discussed.

The as-bonded isothermally solidified joints post-bonded heat treated at 1150  ο C for 3-12h. Fig.4a  shows an overview of the bond after PBHT for 3h. The process of producing a boride-free joint via PBHT can be explained by a  dynamic process involving two simultaneous stages:  

# (1) Stage I (Precipitate Break-up)  

The grain boundary and needle-like borides experience fragmentation during PBHT. Fig.6 (a-b)  shows the process of break-up and separation of the boride. The driving force for particle break- up is the reduction of the surface energy of the boride/matrix interface. Due to Gibbs–Thomson  effect [34], the differences in curvature (see Fig.6) and hence chemical potential across a grain  boundary boride or a needle-like boride sets up a diffusion flux leading to initiation of particle  break-up.

This phenomena can be  explained by the fact that diffusion induced homogenization modifies the chemical composition  of the boride precipitates. #  

In-situ boride precipitation during transient liquid phase bonding can affect the mechanical behavior,  corrosion resistance and aging response of nickel base superalloy. In this work, a boride-free TLP  joint in wrought IN718 nickel base superalloy was fabricated by combination of isothermal  solidification which precludes formation of eutectic borides in the joint centerline, and applying a  proper solid state post bond heat treatment which eliminates boride precipitates in diffusion affected  zone.  The following conclusions can be drawn from this study:  

(1) Bonding of wrought IN718 with Ni-Cr-B-Si-Fe filler metal at 1050  ο C /1hr followed by PBHT at  1150 ºC for 12hrs resulted in a chemically homogenous eutectic-free and boride-free joint with aging  response similar to the base metal.  

(2) The borides evolution during PBHT was featured by a two concurrent dynamic processes: break  up and dissolution of Cr-Mo-Nb rich borides. The break-up of the needle-like and grain boundary  borides is driven by diffusion flux induced by Gibbs-Thomson effect. The dissolution of the borides at  a temperature lower than their initial solvus temperature can be attributed to the modification of  boride composition (i.e. lowering the Cr, Mo and Nb content of the borides) during diffusion-induced  homogenization process associated with PBHT.  The dissolution process was dependent on boride  morphology and their initial Cr+Mo+Nb content.  

(3) The change in the micro-chemistry of the ISZ affects the hardness characteristics and aging  response of the ISZ. There is a direct relationship between Cr, Mo, Nb+Al+Ti content of the joint  centerline and square root of the PBHT time. It was found that kinetics of the homogenization was  faster than that of predicated by Crank solution of the Fick's second law. It was attributed to the  increased diffusion flux due to dissolution of Cr-Mo-Nb rich borides during PBHT.
