# Microstructural Instability and Precipitation Behaviors of Intermetallic Phases in a Nb-Containing CoNi-Based Superalloy  

ZHONGDING FAN, XINGUANG WANG, YANHONG YANG, HAO CHEN,ZHIGANG YANG, and CHI ZHANG  

Exploring the alloying eﬀect of various elements plays a crucial role in optimizing the alloy design for superalloys. In this study, the inﬂuence of Nb additions on a  c ¢ -strengthening CoNi-based superalloy has been investigated. Undesirable intermetallic phases including the topologically close-packed D8 5  phase and the ordered D0 19  phase were observed to preferentially form in the dendritic and interdendritic regions of the investigated alloy, respectively. Precipitation behaviors of the D0 19  phase and the D8 5  phase including growth mechanisms and crystallographic orientation relationships have been characterized and analyzed in detail using advanced electron microscopy techniques. First-principle calculations using density function theory have been carried out to evaluate the eﬀect of Nb additions on the thermodynamic stability of the L1 2 - c ¢  phase. It turns out that the supercell with L1 2  structure would be energetically destabilized when the Nb-substituted atom sits on the Co or Al sites, which could correspond to the microstructural instability of the Nb-containing CoNi-based superalloy.

# I. INTRODUCTION  

T HE  development of aviation industry has undoubt- edly brought considerable convenience for humans, but the increasing air traﬃc causes the emission of green- house gas to be uncontrollable. Accordingly, global warming has gradually been exacerbated. One of the main solutions is to maximize the operating temperature of turbine engines so as to increase the thermal eﬃciency, thus decreasing the toxic emission. To this end, the pivotal requirements are enhancements and innovations of turbine materials, which have troubled material scientists for decades. Recently, the re-discov- ery of superlattice ordering in Co-Al-W alloys by Sato 1 ]   2 ]   et al ., which was ﬁrstly reported in 1971, has attracted considerable research interest. Similar to commercial Ni-based superalloys, the Co-Al-W alloy consists of coherent L1 2 - c ¢  precipitates,  c ¢ -Co 3 (Al,W), and a A1- c  matrix, such two-phase microstructure leads to an anomalous yield stress behavior. The combination of yield stress anomaly and the higher solidus temper- ature, approximately 1450   C, renders the ternary Co-Al-W superalloy possessing great potential to become an improved turbine material compared to 1 , ] Ni-based superalloys.  

However, major drawbacks of the Co-Al-W superal- loy including high density and relatively low c ¢ -Co 3 (Al,W) solvus temperature considerably limit its applications. The low  c ¢  solvus temperature leads to an insuﬃcient stability of the microstructure at elevated temperatures and further undermines the high-temper- ature mechanical properties. For the purpose of devel- oping a Co-Al-W alloy with enhanced and well-balanced properties, the investigation on alloying eﬀects of various elements is necessary. It has been reported that Ni and Ti are prone to partition to the  c ¢ -Co 3 (Al,W) [ 4 , ]   6 – 8 phase and thus increase its solvus temperature, while improving the high-temperature mechanical prop- erties of Co-Al-W superalloys including 0.2 pct ﬂow [ 9 – 11 ]   12 – 14 ]   stress and creep resistance. Moreover, excep- tional enhancements on high-temperature mechanical properties could be achieved by moderate additions of refractory metals that are known to be strong solid solution strengtheners in superalloys. Previous studies have shown that refractory elements such as Ta and Nb are eﬀective for increasing the  c ¢  solvus temperature of Co-Al-W superalloys, substituting 1 at. pct of Ta for Co  

¢   3 , , , ] would raise the  c  solvus temperature by 45 C. Besides, addition of Ta and Nb was also proved to stabilize the  c ¢  phase of the newly developed Co-Al-Mo 17 – 19 ]   alloys. It should, however, be noted that excessive additions of refractory elements would largely increase the susceptibility to forming topologically close-packed (TCP) phases, the so-called Frank–Kasper phases, and some other undesirable intermetallic compounds such as 20 , ]   D0 19 -Co 3 W phase. Owing to the brittle nature of intermetallic compounds, these phases are generally regarded as deleterious precipitates in terms of mechan- ical properties. During deformation process, the dislo- cation pile-up is likely to occur at the interfaces of precipitates and which results in strain localizations and initiations of cracks. On the other hand, formations of intermetallic phases would cause the depletion of refractory elements in the matrix and thus weakening 22 ] the eﬀect of solid solution strengthening.  

To date, numerous in-depth researches with respect to TCP phases in Ni-based superalloys have been pub- 22 , ]   lished, whereas quite limited studies from our understandings are focused on the behaviors of detri- mental intermetallic phases in Co-Al-W superal- 24 – 26 ] loys. Deepen the knowledge towards these undesirable phases could provide useful guidance for the further alloy designs. In this paper, we introduce Nb addition to a CoNi-based superalloy so as to investigate the precipitation behaviors of the undesirable inter- metallic phases. There are two major objectives of the present study: (i) elucidate the microstructural instabil- ity caused by the addition of Nb and (ii) explore the precipitation behaviors of the intermetallic phases including D8 5 -Co 7 W 6  and D0 19 -Co 3 W phases.  

# II. EXPERIMENTAL PROCEDURES  

In this study, single-crystalline CoNi-based alloys were considered. The chemical compositions (at. pct) of the investigated alloys, determined by the X-ray ﬂuo- rescence (XRF) technique, and details of heat treat- ments are given in Table  I . The single-crystalline rods were grown along the [001] crystallographic orientation using a Bridgeman furnace with a withdrawal rate of 6 mm/min. The electron backscattered diﬀraction (EBSD) technique was utilized to conﬁrm the complete- ness of the single-crystalline specimens and the deviation of crystal orientation to be less than 10 deg.  

The X-ray diﬀraction (XRD) and the EBSD tech- niques were utilized to determine the crystal structure of precipitates in the investigated alloys. The Bruker D8 ADVANCE X-ray diﬀractometer was used to conduct XRD with CuK radiation. Measurements were carried out for 2 h  from 20 to 100 deg with a step of 4 deg/min. The specimens for EBSD observations were mechani- cally ground and electropolished in an electrolyte of 20 pct perchloric acid and 80 pct ethanol with the voltage of 15 V for 40 seconds. The equilibrium com- position of intermetallic compounds and the matrix phase were determined by the electron probe micro- analysis (EPMA) using a JEOL JXA-8230 detector. The distribution coeﬃcients of the CoNi-4 alloy were determined using the EPMA measurements in dendrite cores and interdendritic regions.  

Microstructures were characterized by scanning elec- tron microscopy (SEM) using a Zeiss GeminiSEM500 and the specimens were mechanically ground and polished following a standard metallography procedure. In order to precisely determine the morphologies of intermetallic phases in the investigated alloys, the electrolytic extraction technique was used to dissolve the  c / c ¢  matrix. The extraction was carried out by immersing the as-aged specimen in an electrolyte of 10 vol pct hydrochloric acid in methanol with 1 wt pct 2   tartaric acid. A current density of 200 mA/cm was chosen, while the specimen was maintained as the anode and a stainless steel rod was used as the cathode for around 30 minutes. The values of the volume fraction of intermetallic compounds in the SEM microstructures were estimated using the function ‘‘Analyze Particles’’ in the software ImageJ. The transmission electron micro- scopy (TEM) analysis was conducted using a JEOL JEM2100 TEM. The ﬁnal thinning for TEM specimens was prepared by the ion milling technique.

# B.  Powder XRD Measurements  

Figure  3  shows the results of the powder XRD measurements plotting the diﬀraction intensity against the 2 h  for the investigated alloys at diﬀerent heat treatment stages. It is worth mentioning that the powder XRD method may not be an ideal approach to determine the phase composition of the microstructure for single-crystalline samples. Nevertheless, the XRD results here can more or less provide qualitative iden- tiﬁcations of phases possessing lattice planes fulﬁlling the Bragg condition and parallel to the sample surface simultaneously. As shown in Figures  3 (a) and (c) that the XRD spectra of the CoNi-2 and CoNi-4 alloys both exhibited the two peaks representing the {100} planes of the L1 2 - c ¢  phase and the  c  matrix with extremely high intensity. In addition to these two peaks, no other peaks possessing obvious diﬀraction intensity were found in the XRD spectrum of the CoNi-2 alloy, as shown in Figure  3 (c). For the as-cast CoNi-4 alloy, a moderate diﬀraction intensity of the C15 phase was present, while D8 5  and D0 19  phases produced barely detectable diﬀrac- tion intensity. The spectrums of as-solutionized and as-aged CoNi-4 alloys exhibited no intensity of the C15 phase, and the diﬀraction intensity of D8 5  and D0 19 phases continuously increased as the heat treatment proceeds from solution to aging. The occurrence of the intermetallic phases from the observation of SEM micrographs was basically consistent with the powder XRD results.  

Additionally, numerous planes of the D8 5  phase exhibited detectable intensity in XRD spectrums but merely limited planes of the D0 19  phase were diﬀracted during the experiments. This is an indication that most D0 19  phases were grown with strong orientation rela- tionships (OR) with the matrix phase; conversely, D8 5 phases tended to grow freely without OR with the matrix phase. The OR between intermetallic phases and the matrix will be further discussed in later sections.

# 2.  Topologically close-packed D8 5  phase  

The determination of the morphology and crystal orientation of the D8 5  phase formed in the dendritic cores of the CoNi-4 alloy has been accomplished by SEM observations on electrolytic ally extracted speci- mens and EBSD measurements, as shown in Figure  6 . Apparently, most of the D8 5  phases grew into plate-like morphology after heat treatments, while some of them grew into rod-like morphology instead. It is interesting to note that the rod-like D8 5  phases were prone to form at the edges of dendritic cores, while the plate-like D8 5 phases tended to form at the center of dendrite cores. (Figure  6 (a)) The formation of rod-like precipitates was likely to stem from the limited growth space near the edges of dendritic cores. The OR between the D8 5  phase and the matrix has been investigated by EBSD in Figure  6 (d), and the EBSD results show no OR at D8 5 /matrix interfaces when the specimen was sectioned along the (001) plane of the A1 matrix phase. Never- theless, the stereographic projection in Figure  6 (g) illustrates that most of the EBSD data points corre- spond to only three variants. This is to say that the D8 5 phase is likely to form following an orientation relationship with the matrix, which will be determined using TEM in the next paragraph. Moreover, a small amount of the D0 19  phase has been detected to form at the D8 5 /matrix interfaces, which seems to be related to the peritectoid transformation. It will also be discussed in detail in the following section.  

TEM investigations have been carried out to further conﬁrm the OR between the D8 5  phase and the matrix. Figures  7 (a) and (b) demonstrates that the D8 5 -1 phase exhibited an OR with the matrix, and the habit plane of the HCP- D8 5  phase was parallel to the close-packed plane of the FCC matrix. This result is diﬀerent from the 29 ]   previous study by Carvalho  et al . claiming that no OR can be found between the D8 5 -Co 7 W 6  phase and the FCC-Co matrix. Furthermore, the atomic arrangement of the D8 5 /matrix interface has been observed using the high-resolution TEM (HRTEM). The Fourier trans- form pattern of the HRTEM image in Figure  7 (a) reveals that the matrix phase, exhibiting an OR with the D8 5  phase, can be determined as the L1 2 - c ¢  phase since the presence of (110) and (0 to 21) planes veriﬁes its superlattice nature. The OR can therefore be distin- [ 11 – 20 ] ¢ guishedas-D85//[-112]-cand(0001)-D85//(-11-1)- c ¢ . When zooming into the D8 5 / c ¢  interface, a series of edge dislocations were present to accommodate the disregistry caused by the dissimilar crystal structures between D8 5 / c ¢  phases, and thus, this interface could be reasonably termed semi-coherent or partially coherent, as shown in Figure  7 (c). In addition, numerous TEM observations were conducted on diﬀerent D8 5  phases, and the results show that only those plate-like D8 5 phases with a thickness less than 150nm possessed a semi-coherent interface with the  c ¢  phase. It agrees with the phase transformation theory that as the precipitates grow larger, the coherency strain energy caused by the lattice misﬁt between precipitates and the matrix becomes more important than the interfacial energy. Eventually, the interphase interface would lose its coherency to prevent the increased total energy. It is reasonable that the broad faces of plate-like D8 5 precipitates exhibit a good lattice matching with the  c ¢ phases to reduce the interfacial energy of the interfaces. From the standpoint of precipitate growth rate, the mobility of the semi-coherent D8 5 / c ¢ interface was likely to be relatively low due to its coherency, and thus, the interface migration can only take place by a ledge mechanism. The nucleation of new ledges could hardly be observed by TEM, which accounts for the slow thickening rate of plate-like D8 5  precipitates.

# D.  The Peritectoid Transformation  

Figure  9  shows SEM backscatter electron images of the CoNi-4 alloy illustrating the microstructural change after the aging heat treatment. Observed from Figure  9 (a) that the D8 5  phases in the as-solutionized specimen showed an uniformly bright contrast under the BSE mode. After the aging process, an additional phase with a darker contrast appeared in the microstructure, and it seemed to form in consumption of original D8 5 phases. The identity of this unknown phase was deter- mined as the D0 19  phase by EBSD, as shown in Figure  6 (e). Additionally, Figure  9 (c) reveals that the growth behaviors of the this phase is consistent with the D0 19  phase located in the interdendritic regions,  i.e., stop lengthening due to the intersection of other D0 19 phases and thickening by combining neighboring paral- lel D0 19  phases. According to the Co-W equilibrium 37 ]   phase diagram given by Naidu  et al ., this phe- nomenon can be distinguished as the peritectoid trans- formation, D8 5 -Co 7 W 6  +  c -Co  !  D0 19 -Co 3 W, and the product phase can therefore be denoted as P-D0 19 .

Avoiding the formation of undesirable intermetallic phases is therefore an important concern for superalloy design. # IV. CONCLUSIONS  

The precipitation behaviors of the undesirable inter- metallic compounds formed in the CoNi-4 alloy have been explored in detail. First-principle calculations are employed to analyze the microstructural instability due to Nb additions. The following conclusions are drawn from the current study:  

1. The displacive part of the L1 2  ﬁ D0 19  transforma- tion is likely to be accomplished by the propagation of Shockley partial dislocations, which may also be able to accelerate the diffusion of D0 19 -rich ele- ments through pipe diffusion.

 2. In the early stages of the formation of D8 5  phases, the strain energy originated by the D8 5 / c ¢  lattice misfit can be accommodated by the misfit disloca- tions wrapped around the D8 5  phase. As the D8 5 phase grows larger, the coherency of the interphase interface becomes lost since the strain energy begins to dominate over the interfacial energy.

 3. The high W content of the D0 19  phase, formed by the peritectoid transformation, may be attributed to the diffusion flow originated from the W-rich D8 5 phase and the diffusion of Al away from the transformation interface.

 4. It is energetically unfavorable when the Nb atom replaced the Co or Al atom in the L1 2  supercell, which can induce the decomposition of the L1 2 - c ¢ phase and further undermine the microstructural stability of the Nb-containing CoNi-based superalloy.
