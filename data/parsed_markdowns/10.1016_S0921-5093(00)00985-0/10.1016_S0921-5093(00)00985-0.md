# Abstract  

A substantial undercooling up to 210 K was produced in the DD3 single crystal superalloy melt by employing the method of molten salt denucleating combined with thermal cycle, and the microstructure evolution with undercooling was systematically investigated. Within the achieved range of undercooling, 0–210 K, the solidiﬁcation structure could be classiﬁed into four categories. At the lower range of undercooling, 0–25 K, the dendrite growth is dominantly controlled by solute diffusion, so the formed dendritic morphologies are similar to those of the conventional as-cast structure. With the increase of undercooling, the alloy undergoes two grain reﬁnements. The ﬁrst grain reﬁnement occurs in a certain range of undercooling, 30–70 K, because of the dendrite break-up or ripening owing to remelting. At a higher range of undercooling, 78–150 K, however, the severe solute trapping that results from high dendrite growth velocity weakens the effect of solutal diffusion on the dendrite growth. In this case, highly developed ﬁne dendrite is formed as a result of the restrained ripening process. The decrease of the grain size, i.e. the formation of ﬁne equiaxed structure above the critical undercooling ( D T \* = 180 K), can be attributed to the stress that originates from the extremely rapid solidiﬁcation process, which results in the dendrite distortion, disintegration and recrystallization ﬁnally.

# 1. Introduction  

Solidiﬁcation of undercooled metallic melts is of great interest in investigating the microstructure evolu- tion and phase selection in some alloy systems. Sub- stantial undercoolings of melts have been widely applied since some important thermodynamic parame- ters such as undercoolings for nucleation and velocities of the solid–liquid interface may be available in situ in experiments. High undercooling plays an important role in determining the microstructure development and phase selection because nucleation and subsequent crys- tal growth govern the microstructure and phase forma- tion in as-solidiﬁed alloys. The microstructures at various undercoolings have been well studied in differ- ent systems including eutectic [1], peritectic [2], metallic compound [3], and solid solution [4] alloys. General crystal reﬁnement has been conﬁrmed when the alloys are undercooled beyond the critical undercoolings. However, in comparison with other alloy systems, there has been very little corresponding research about mi- crostructure evolution with undercooling in superalloy up to now. In the work of Lux et al. [5], dynamic undercooling of more than 150 K below the liquidus temperature was obtained in two commercial superal- loys, MAR-M-200 and MAR-M-509, provided that a temperature gradient of about 10°C cm − 1   was main- tained within the melt during the solidiﬁcation and cooling process. Subsequently, in order to explore an alternative method for directional and / or single crystal (SC) casting, the directional solidiﬁcation of an under- cooled superalloy melt, as proposed by Lux et al [5], was taken up [6]. The improvement of this method resulted in a new technique for producing SC superal- loy turbine blades [7]. At the Foundry Institute of the Technical University of Aachen, 1992, autonomous di- rectional solidiﬁcation (ADS) processing was developed [8]. The process was based on the mechanism to under- cool the superalloy in the ceramic shell mold to a certain degree, thus leading to a rapid solidiﬁcation after nucleation at the bottom of the mold, as ﬁrst presented in 1981. ADS processing made great progress in the development of superalloy SC casting and com- puter simulation; however, it is the combination of

# 2. Experimental procedure  

Commercial DD3 single crystal superalloy was cho- sen (see Table 1) in the undercooling experiment, which was established by the application of molten salt cover- ing, denucleating and high-frequency induction heating under the protection of an argon atmosphere. Prior to melting, the surfaces of the metal charges were cleaned mechanically by grinding off the surface oxide layer and chemically by etching in HCl solution diluted by alcohol. When beginning the experiment, the alloy charges were placed in a cleaned and dried noncatalytic coating mold [9], and covered with a 5 mm layer of salt granules. The mold was then sealed, evacuated and subsequently back-ﬁlled with 99.999% argon gas. Each sample was melted, superheated and solidiﬁed several times, i.e. it was processed in a mode of superheating– cooling cycles, in order to obtain large undercoolings. It was clear that no chemical reaction occurred among the mold material, the salt granules and the melt, so the composition of the as-solidiﬁed specimens was equal to that of the original composition. The thermal behavior of samples was monitored by an infrared pyrometer with an absolute accuracy, relative accuracy, and re- sponse time of less than 10 K, 3 K, and 5 ms, respec- tively [4]. The cooling curve was calibrated with a standard PtRh30–PtRh6 thermal couple, which was encapsulated in a tube made of the same material as the noncatalytic coating mold and then immersed into the melt in the identical condition. The melting temperature and the undercooling of the alloy in the cooling curves could be read after the comparison with the absolute temperature recorded by the standard thermal couple. Each of the samples had a weight of 10–15 g and a diameter of 8–12 mm, and was sectioned through the triggering spot, then polished, and etched with water solution of FeCl 3  and HCl. Structure observation was carried out with an optical microscope.  

# 3. Experimental results  

High undercooling up to 210 K was achieved in a DD3 single crystal superalloy melt. With increasing undercooling, the alloy undergoes two grain reﬁne- ments: one takes place at low undercooling, and the other at high undercooling. The grain size and typical microstructure at various undercoolings are shown in Fig. 1 and Figs. 2–5, respectively. The characteristic undercoolings  D T 1 ,  D T 2 ,  D T 3 ,  D T 4 ,  D T 5  and  D T \* are 25, 30, 70, 78, 150 and 180 K, respectively. Within the achieved range of undercooling, 0–210K, solidiﬁcation structure with undercooling could be classiﬁed into four categories. Introducing a small undercooling into the alloy promotes the formation of highly branched den- drite (see Fig. 2) and enlarges the grain size (see Figs. 1 and 2b). When  D T \ D T 1 , a substantial ripening of the dendrites comes into being. # 4. Discussion  

# 4.1.  The solidiﬁcation characterization of the commercial DD 3  single crystal superalloy  

The constituents of DD3 single crystal superalloy, shown in Table 1, have been classiﬁed as solid solution and / or precipitate formers, i.e. two phases; matrix and strengthening phases predominantly exist in the as-cast structure. The solidiﬁcation process of undercooled DD3 single crystal superalloy is as follows. Dendrites are ﬁrst formed at the nucleation point and propagate rapidly through the volume of the melt, in which only single-phase  Y -solid solution Ni (Al, Ti, W, Mo, Co, Cr) is obtained [10]. Then the rapid release of heat of fusion during dendrite growth results in rapid recales- cence, and thus the dendritic network remelting occurs. Finally, the remaining interdendritic melt starts to so- lidify onto the dendritic network at low melt undercool- ing in post-recalescence. Furthermore, during the relatively long duration of this stage, coarsening by Ostwald ripening may occur, and  Y / Y %  eutectic (1 vol- ume percent in the cross-section of the sample) is formed in the boundaries of crystal grains at the end of this stage. After that, phase transformation of diffu- sional precipitation takes place and  Y %  phase precipi- tates progressively in the cooling process [13].  

It was shown in Ref. [14] that the Ni–Cr–Al–Mo system is of particular interest in representing the equi- libria among  g  (nickel-rich solid solution),  g % (based on Ni 3 Al) and  s  phases, and that the Ni–Cr–Al–Mo systems consist of single-phase  g -Ni (Al, Cr, Mo) solid solution above 800°C if having the same Al, Cr, Mo content as that in DD3 single crystal superalloy, respec- tively. Machlin and Shao [15] have proposed the exten- sion of the Ni–Cr–Al–Mo quaternary to higher order alloys by adopting the scheme of equivalents (Ni, Co, Fe):Cr:(Al, Ti):(Mo, W, Nb, V, Ta, Hf). Therefore, DD3 single crystal superalloy melt can solidify as sin- gle-phase  Y -solid solution in the rapid dendritic solidiﬁ- cation process, which infers that similarity exists in the solidiﬁcation characterization between the Ni–Cr–Al– Mo system and DD3 single crystal superalloy. Without considering the effects of element Ti, W, Co on the solidiﬁcation behavior, the physical parameters of the Ni–Cr–Al–Mo (9.5 wt.%Cr, 5.9 wt.%Al, 3.8 wt.%Mo) Y -solid solution, listed in Table 2, are rendered as the approximant of the DD3 single crystal superalloy for BCT model calculation.

# 4.3.  Mechanism of grain reﬁnement  

A simple model of grain reﬁnement in solidiﬁcation of undercooled melts based on a fragmentation mecha- nism has been proposed and described by Karma [22]. It clearly demonstrated that the critical transitional undercooling could be determined by comparing two times: the time for break up of dendrite to occur,  D t bu , and the plateau duration  D t pl , which is the time it takes for the interdendritic melt to completely solidify after recalescence. It then followed that coarse-grained den- drite and grain-reﬁned equiaxed structure should form if  D t bu B D t pl  and  D t bu \ D t pl , respectively. This model can be applied to both low and high undercooling reﬁnements. In present paper, however, different physi- cal origins play a dominant role in grain reﬁnement with the undercooling increasing. In the undercooling range of  D T 2 – D T 3 , dendrite growth is dominantly con- trolled by the solutal diffusion, so  C \*  and  D T c  increase obviously with undercooling increasing, which is in agreement with the results of the BCT model calcula- tion shown in Figs. 6 and 7. Therefore, combined with the results ob- tained from Figs. 1 and 3, it can be concluded that the dendrites solidiﬁed in the undercooling range of  D T 2 – D T 3  have the maximum tendency to be remelted and ripened in recalescence and subsequent post-recales- cence, respectively. As a result, the ﬁrst reﬁnement is attributed to the dendrite break-up or ripening owing to remelting.  

When the undercooling is beyond  D T 5 , on the con- trary, the recalescence superheating of the dendrite decreases to the lowest temperature range (Table 3); consequently, the remelting is not severe enough to make the dendrite disintegrate into ﬁne granule. The grain reﬁnement at high undercooling must be induced by other factors rather than remelting. In connection with the microstructure shown in Fig. 5c,d, we suggest that the grain reﬁnement at high undercooling should result from the recrystallization process as argued by Powell and Hogan [23,24], which appears to occur during, or immediately after, solidiﬁcation, while the solid metal is very close to the melting point. The occurrence of recrystallization requires a driving force, which is provided, under normal conditions, by prior plastic deformation. Nevertheless, in the present case, it is the high stresses produced during the extremely rapid solidiﬁcation process, but not any external agency, that result in the dendrite distortion and fragment or plastic deformation, and, ﬁnally, recrystallization. As already described, if the dendrite growth is too great to permit complete diffusion of solute out of the melt in contact with the advancing tip, effective solute trapping may occur in the solid [19]. As a result of high-velocity growth after large undercooling (beyond  D T \*), a high energy content of the ﬁrst-formed structure also can be expected to be built in, in the form of a high population of crystalline defects. The higher the undercooling, the greater the number of defects. Also, it should be noted that the center of mass of an undercooled melt should undergo a substantial acceleration during freezing due to the rapid change from liquid to solid density at the very high freezing rates involved [23]. Therefore, the ﬁrst-formed dendrite would be subject to fracture, dis- integration owing to high stress arising from this kind of rapid heterogeneous shrinkage process, which results in the recrystallization structure shown in Fig. 5d.  

In addition, it should be noted that, in some samples, especially the samples that obtain higher undercooling before nucleation, the microstructure varies over vari- ous parts of the sample. Upon solidiﬁcation of the samples, the solid moves into a liquid that has a thermal gradient where the surface of the sample is cooler than the center, the magnitude of this gradient is dependent on the stirring force and cooling rate. The samples are cooled by forced convection and radiation at the surface. The heat within the sample must be transferred to the surface by conduction through the material or by redistribution resulting from electromag- netic stirring forces. As the undercooling increases, the effect of the stirring forces is diminished as a result of the increased viscosity of the liquid. The cumulative effect is the development of a temperature gradient within the sample, the magnitude of which cannot be quantiﬁed at this time. On the other hand, the gradients that are present at the onset of solidiﬁcation are not changed appreciably during the recalescence event. This is conﬁrmed by estimates of the ratio of the dimension- less kinetic coefﬁcient to the Biot number [25]. This ratio, which is a measure of the predominance of the recalescence rate over the rate of heat loss to the environment, is calculated to range from 10 3   to 10 5 . These large values indicate that solidiﬁcation is essen- tially adiabatic during rapid recalescence. Therefore, as shown in Fig. 4c and Fig. 5b,d, morphology near the surface of sample is slightly different from that near the center, owing to the cumulative effect of temperature gradient. Fortunately, the results of this paper are not yet inﬂuenced.  

# 5. Conclusion  

The complex method of molten salt denucleating combined with thermal cycle is applied to deal with the DD3 single crystal superalloy. The highest undercool- ing up to 210 K is obtained.  

The solidiﬁcation morphologies of the undercooled single crystal superalloy are found to be dependent on the degree of undercooling. The two main points are stated as follows. First, in the range of undercooling, 78–150 K, thermal diffusion plays an important role in the crystal growth, and the undercooled single phase can directionally solidify as the highly developed ﬁne dendrite owing to the restrained ripening process. Then, within the achieved undercooling, 0–210 K, the alloy undergoes two grain reﬁnements. One takes place in the lower undercooling range, 30–70 K, as a result of the dendrite remelting; whereas the other occurs above the critical undercooling temperature, 180 K, and can be attributed to the stress originating from the extremely rapid solidiﬁcation process, which resulted in the den- drite distortion, disintegration and recrystallization ﬁnally.  

The undercooled DD3 single crystal superalloy can directionally solidify when thermal diffusion plays the dominant role in the crystal growth. The appropriate undercooling range for directional solidiﬁcation is 78– 150 K, namely that it is solely in this undercooling range that SC and / or directional solidiﬁcation of super- alloy can be realized.
