# High-temperature oxidation of duplex stainless steels in air and mixed gas of air and CH 4  

b a Lian-Fu Li   , Zhou-Hua Jiang   , Yves Riquier  

a  Metallurgy Unit, Facult e  Polytechnique de Mons, 56 rue de l’Epargne, B-7000 Mons, Belgium b  Department of Metallurgy, Faculty of Materials Science and Metallurgy, Northeastern University, Shenyang 110006, China c  Department MTM, Katholieke Universiteit Leuven, Kasteelpark Arenberg 44, B-3001 Leuven, Belgium

# Abstract  

TGA, isothermal and thermal-cycle tests were carried out to investigate the oxidation behaviour of duplex stainless steels at 1220   C in various atmospheres. The weight gain in air is initially in parabolic relation to time. This is followed by a linear relationship for super alloy and a dramatic growth one for standard alloy that is subject to breakaway oxidation by forming nodules. The thickness of  c -layer increases with increasing annealing time and oxygen content in the atmosphere, and it is slightly higher in standard alloy than in super alloys. A single layer of (Fe,Cr) 3 O 4  spinel is noticed in the external oxide scale after annealing for 2 h, and two layers are found after 4 h with an inner layer of the spinel and an outer layer of iron oxides. Nodules grow on the  d = c  packets where the oxidation of austenite is accelerated and that of ferrite is suppressed. Both chains and Si-enriched scales grow inside the  c -layer along grain and phase boundaries.

# 1. Introduction  

Duplex stainless steels (DSSs) are balanced with two phases, ferrite and austenite. Although the ﬁrst reference to such alloys appeared in 1927 [1], their development was not greatly improved until the introduction of vacuum oxygen decarburisation (VOD) and argon oxygen decarburisation (AOD) practices.  

The DSSs are increasingly used because of their several superior properties [2–5]. High-mechanical strength, resulting from both the balance of the duplex phases and the interstitial solid solution with high-nitrogen content, makes it possible to employ the alloys in some particular ﬁelds with least weight. High resistance to both general and localised corrosion due to high chromium, molybdenum and nitrogen contents, especially high resistance to pitting and crevice corrosion in chloride containing media, extends their applications to pollution control, chemical vessels, and oﬀshore oil and gas industries, etc.  

After casting, the DSSs are subjected to a reheating at temperatures ranging from 1200 to 1250   C for a few hours in order to facilitate the subsequent hot-forming. The DSSs balanced with 50% ferrite can readily be hot-formed at temperatures higher than 950   C; but a temperature up to above 1000   C is particularly needed to avoid any detrimental structural transformation in ferritic, especially  r -phase pre- cipitation [6–8]. After hot- and cold-forming, the DSSs are annealed at temperatures higher than 1100   C to relieve the work hardening induced by the deformation and to modify the grain structures. Since the DSSs are treated at elevated temperatures in an oxidising atmosphere for an extended duration, surface oxidation is inevitable. The resulting oxide scales and Cr-depleted layer have a great eﬀect on forming, pickling, and even the surface ﬁnish of a ﬁnal product. Therefore, the investigation of such an oxidation is of signiﬁcance. High-temperature oxidation of austenitic [9–12] and ferritic [12–14] stainless steels has been extensively investigated. However, studies on the oxidation behaviour of DSSs at elevated temperatures, especially at temperature higher than 1200   C, and the eﬀect of the oxidation behaviour on surface ﬁnish are limited [8,15]. For this reason, the thermal-cycle, isothermal and thermo grav i metric analysis (TGA) tests have been carried out to obtain an insight on the oxidation kinetics of DSSs and the behaviour of oxide scales and Cr-depleted layer.

TGA tests were carried out in air. The samples were heated from room temper- ature at a rate of 15   C min  1   up to 1220   C, while preheated argon was introduced in the furnace to protect the samples from oxidation. The samples were kept at 1220  C in argon for 10 min to stabilise the temperature at surface. Then the oxidation tests were started with replacing argon by preheated air. After 3 h, the samples were cooled down to room temperature. Both isothermal and thermal-cycle tests were performed with various oxidising atmospheres. These included low- and high-oxygen mixed gas in the gas furnace, and air in electric furnace. In isothermal tests, the furnaces were ﬁrst heated up to 1220   C by using either mixed gas or electricity, and then the samples were introduced in the furnaces. The annealing times were respectively 2 and 4 h for the samples to be characterised, and 0.5, 1, 2, and 3 h for those to be compared with TGA results. After annealing, the samples were taken out of the furnaces and cooled down. The atmosphere for a thermal-cycle test remains the same during heating-up and testing. The samples were heated from room tem- perature up to 1220   C at a rate of 13.33   C min  1   and remained at that temperature for 1, 3 and 6 h, respectively. All samples were cooled down under argon protection after tests.  

The samples obtained from the tests were mounted in resin, polished until No. 4000 emery paper followed by a ﬁnish of 1.0  l m diamond paste, and etched by the Beraha’s tint to reveal ferrite and austenite. Light optical microscope (LOM) and scanning electron microscope (SEM) were used to characterise the microstructure. Energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX) and X-ray diﬀraction (XRD) were respectively used for chemical microanalysis and structure determination.  

# 3. Results and discussion  

# 3.1. Kinetics of oxidation

The weight of the samples started to increase when the protective gas was replaced by air. At the beginning, the weight gain of every sample is in parabolic relation to time. One hour later, the weight of super alloys continues to increase in a linear regime until the end of the tests, while

the weight of standard alloy increases dramatically at rates much higher than that of the super alloys. During the initial parabolic stage, the weight gain of the three steels is rather close because oxide scales grow uniformly on each steel. Solid state diﬀusion of elements through the oxide scale is the rate-determining step for oxidation at that stage [16], and a parabolic oxidation rate is thus obtained [17].  

Oxidation of duplex alloys at high temperatures is preferential to chromium due to higher aﬃnity of chromium for oxygen than iron and nickel. As a result, a metallic layer that is depleted in the ferrite former element, chromium, and correspondingly enriched in the austenitic former element, nickel, is formed beneath the oxide scale. Therefore, this metallic layer becomes solely austenitic in contrast to the duplex phases in the base metal. This metallic layer is so-called Cr-depleted layer, or  c -layer because it is solely austenitic. The growth of the  c -layer is usually related to the oxidation kinetics of a particular alloy.  

The thickness of  c -layer was measured as follows. The cross-section of an oxidised sample was polished and etched to distinguish phases, and certain number of images was taken with LOM. The thickness of the  c -layer on each image was measured, and the average value and standard deviation of the  c -layer was obtained. The thickness of the  c -layer obtained from isothermal tests increases with increasing oxygen con- tent (Fig. 2). It was claimed that the diﬀusion coeﬃcient of a metal outwards is much higher than that of oxygen inwards in oxide scale [18], thus oxidation of metals usually takes place at the outer surface of the oxide scale. Accordingly, higher oxygen content in oxidising gas results in higher oxidation rate of chromium, which further enhances the diﬀusion of chromium outwards and promotes the growth of the  c -layer (Fig. 2). The averaged  c -layer thickness obtained from the thermal-cycle tests increases with time (Fig. 3). Indeed, higher oxygen content in the mixed gas induces a thicker  c -layer.  

The weight gain obtained from isothermal tests is compared with the thickness of c -layer obtained from TGA tests, and good consistency is noticed during the initial one hour when the Wagnerian parabolic law prevails (Fig. 4). After one hour, the c -layer of all alloys grows in linear relation to time; the weight gain of super alloys

# 3.2. Character is ation of oxide scales  

The oxide scales formed on duplex stainless steels are roughly classiﬁed into external and internal oxide scales. External oxide scale that is mostly dense in structure and regular in thickness continuously covers on metal. Internal oxide scale forms inside the  c -layer at the sites with high energy, i.e., grain boundaries, phase boundaries and defects, and is usually isolated from the external oxide scales. In general, the composition of the internal oxide scale diﬀers from the external one because of the diﬀerence in local oxidising atmosphere.  

![](images/ee6643bc65a7d5a96d4d54f96e670b57648b022afdf14ec01daba4ea0a1f2810.jpg)  
Fig. 4. Comparison of weight gain obtained from TGA tests and the growth of  c -layer obtained from isothermal tests at 1220   C in air.  

The external oxide scale induced by isothermal oxidation at 1220   C for 2 h in air was characterised with EDX and XRD, and a single layer of (Fe,Cr) 3 O 4  spinel was conﬁrmed. However, the external oxide scale formed at the same temperature for 4 h consists of an outer layer of iron oxides and an inner layer of (Fe,Cr) 3 O 4  spinel. SEM–EDX mapping in Fig. 5 shows that silicon and chromium are enriched in the inner layer while iron in the outer layer of external oxide scales, and nickel is en- riched in the underlying metal. XRD analysis shows that the external oxide scale of all alloys is composed of the same phases, Fe 2 O 3 , Fe 3 O 4  and (Fe,Cr) 3 O 4  spinel. In combination with the EDX mapping, iron oxides should be in the outer layer, and (Fe,Cr) 3 O 4  spinel in the inner layer. This consists with the oxide scales formed on the austenitic and ferritic stainless steels [12]. Nickel determined with EDX mapping should be present as NiO (Fig. 5) [15]. Moreover, it was noticed that the composition of the external oxide scale is independent of atmosphere.  

Nodules are formed because of the breakaway oxidation [12,16] of Cr-depleted layer when the external oxide scale is broken, and the thickness of oxide scale thus becomes irregular (Fig. 6). The nodules are especially noticed on the standard alloy, especially after an extended oxidation for more than one hour. This is related directly to the diﬀerence in composition in diﬀerent alloys as well as the duplex phases. The standard alloy contains more iron and less chromium in the base material, in se- quence, in the  c -layer and in the external oxide scales than the super alloys do. The oxide scale enriched in iron and depleted in chromium is brittle and tends to be broken when thermal stress is present. Once the oxide scale is broken, the underlying c -layer tends to oxidise [19]. However, the oxide scale on super duplex alloys is less brittle, even it is broken the underneath  c -layer may be self-passivated by forming a thin ﬁlm because of its high enrichment in chromium. Therefore, more nodules are noticed on standard alloy. This may also account for the exceptionally high-oxida- tion rate of this alloy after 1 h (Figs. 1 and 4). Higher oxidation rate results in a c -layer more depleted in chromium in standard alloy than in super alloy, and thus, the chromium content gradient between the  c -layer and the underlying base metal is

It is also noteworthy that nodules are promoted by annealing in a mixed gas and suppressed by annealing in air alone. The cross-section of the nodules can be clearly divided into an inner and an outer layer by a crack that might be formed either during oxidation or during cooling (Fig. 6). The crack indicates a poor adhesion between the two layers, and therefore it was noticed that the outer layer easily ﬂakes oﬀfrom the inner layer, but the inner layer is ﬁrmly adherent to the metal surface. The interesting ﬁnding is that the nodules grow mostly on the  c -crystals, and the thinner sites between the nodules grow on the ferritic phase (Fig. 6), meaning that while the austenite is corroded, the neighbouring ferrite is protected. This preferential oxidation is due to the diﬀerence in chromium contents in ferritic and austenitic phases [20,21]. The chromium content in the ferrite is higher than that in the austenite, a thin and dense chromium oxide-enriched ﬁlm preferentially forms in ferrite and this ﬁlm prevents the underlying metal from fur- ther oxidation. On the other hand, the chromium content in austenite is compara- tively lower, the produced oxide scale is thicker and less protective for the underlying metal. As a result, local oxidation takes place and leads to the growth of nodules.  

The composition of the internal oxide scale diﬀers from the external one because of locally lower oxygen partial pressure available. The internal oxide scale particles are usually located randomly. However, in some particular cases, a group of particles grow along grain or phase boundaries and deeply penetrate into the  c -layer. The penetrating internal oxide scale is referred as a chain or a Si-enriched scale depending on its composition.  

On a 2-dimension cross-sectioning image, chains locate only along the former  d = c or  c = c  boundaries, with oxide particles lining up in a queue like ‘‘chains’’ (Fig. 8). The boundaries are more active in comparison with the rest regions. Oxygen diﬀuses along the boundaries and is reduced while the active metals are oxidised. The metallic elements such as chromium, manganese and silicon can be oxidised at the sites where the oxygen partial pressure becomes thermodynamic ally high enough. Therefore, oxide scale particles preferentially form at the boundaries. The growth of chains was noticed to be independent of oxidising atmosphere. The composition of chains is similar to the internal oxide scale that is randomly located. The chains indicated with circles in Fig. 8 are almost parallel to each other. They occupy the sites of former  c = c -crystal boundaries. The parallel spatial arrangement of the chains is due to the local parallel orientation of  c -crystals in the  d = c  packets. Furthermore, diﬀerent oxide scales are noticed and indicated with A–C, respectively (Fig. 8). EDX analysis shows that A is a typical chain and C is an inclusion enriched in aluminium. B is a Si-enriched scale. Being composed of silicon, manganese and chromium

This is so-called hot-shortness [22]. Diﬀerent from the chains, Si-enriched scales are not only located along the former  d = c  and  c = c  boundaries, but also at the sites where a ferrite was initially located before transforming into  c -layer. The formation of the Si-enriched scale is correlated directly to the oxidising atmo- sphere. This scale is usually rare in a duplex stainless steel annealed in air but noticeable in that annealed in mixed gas (see the dark and elongated internal oxide scales in Fig. 9). This is because the oxidation rate of the alloys in air is higher than in a mixed gas, and the Si-enriched scales are adjacent to the external oxide scales. The Si-enriched scale formed on the steels annealed in air may be shortly annexed by the extending external oxide scales. For the same reason, it was noticed that the other types of internal oxide scale are also less in the steels annealed in air.

Accordingly it can be concluded that the external oxide scale grows depending on the microstructure of the  d = c  packets, and the internal oxide scale depending on the external oxide scale. This is schematically illustrated in Fig. 10. Since the external oxide scale grows faster on austenite than on ferrite, nodules form on the former  d = c packets and the external oxide scale with a regular thickness covers the sites where d = c  packets are unclear. The thickness and composition of the external oxide scales vary with time. The same as the nodules, the external oxide scales after an extended oxidation are distinctly composed of two layers, an inner layer of (Fe,Cr) 3 O 4  spinel and an outer layer of iron oxides. The internal oxide scales are mostly in the  c -layer. Chains and Si-enriched scales are usually located along the former  d = c  and  c = c boundaries, and Si-enriched scale also in the areas where a previous ferrite is located.  

# 4. Conclusions  

Oxidation of duplex stainless steels was performed mainly at 1220   C in air and mixed gas, and the following conclusions can be drawn.  

1. The weight gain initiates by a parabolic increase in relation to annealing time, fol- lowed by a linear one for a super alloy and a dramatic growth one for a standard alloy. The exceptional behaviour of the standard alloy is due to the ‘‘breakaway oxidation’’, which results in nodules. Nodules usually grow on the  d = c  packets where the oxidation of austenite is promoted while that of ferrite is suppressed.

 2. The thickness of the  c -layer increases with increasing annealing time and the oxy- gen content in the atmosphere. The layer is thicker on the standard alloy than on the super alloys.

 3. The external oxide scale obtained after an annealing for 2 h consists of a single layer of (Fe,Cr) 3 O 4  spinel, whereas after a 4h annealing it consists of two layers with an inner layer of the spinel and an outer layer of iron oxides.

 4. Both chains and Si-enriched scales grow inside the  c -layer, along grain or phase boundaries or defects. The Si-enriched scale is especially noticeable on a sample annealed in a mixed gas.
