Abstract  

The effect of temperature on the tensile deformation mechanism in a new  directionally solidified Ni-base superalloy, M4706, is investigated by transmission  electron microscopy after yielding in the temperature range from room temperature to  1000 °C. It is found that below 750 °C, anti-phase boundary shearing and stacking  ACCEPTED fault shearing dominate the initial plastic deformation; above 900 °C, plastic  deformation is accomplished predominantly by individual matrix dislocations  bypassing   precipitates, although a few isolated superlattice stacking faults are  visible in the   precipitates; whereas between these two temperatures, a transitional  behavior is observed. Finally, based on the experimental observations, the variation of  the formation of stacking faults with temperature and the relationship between the  yield strength and the operative deformation mechanisms are discussed.  Keywords : Ni-base superalloy; Dislocations; Stacking faults; Temperature effect;  Deformation mechanisms

# 1.   Introduction  

Ni-base superalloys are widely applied to gas turbine components because of  their excellent mechanical properties and corrosion resistance at elevated  temperatures [1, 2]. As the operating temperature of gas turbines increases, alloys  with a higher temperature capability are desired. Recently, a new directionally  MANUSCRIPT solidified (DS) Ni-base superalloy, M4706, has been developed and evaluated as a  candidate material for industry gas turbine blades. Tensile creep tests have  demonstrated that M4706 exhibits much better creep rupture properties compared to  the first generation DS superalloy GTD-111 [3], as illustrated in Fig. 1. The creep  rupture strength of this new alloy at high temperatures is well known. However,  before M4706 can be effectively used in gas turbines engines, it is of great urgency to  understand the deformation behavior and corresponding deformation mechanisms  

In the literature, numerous studies have been performed to investigate the  ACCEPTED temperature effect on the tensile deformation mechanism in these superalloys [4-18].  For instance, previous work [4] on the Ni-base single crystal superalloy PWA 1480  revealed that at low temperatures (below 760 °C), anti-phase boundary (APB)  shearing controls the plastic deformation; at the high temperatures (above 815 °C),  deformation is achieved mainly by dislocations bypassing  γ′  precipitates (L1 2   structure, Ni 3 (Al, Ti, Ta)), and at intermediate temperatures, APB shearing and  stacking fault shearing as well as precipitate bypassing prevail simultaneously during  tensile deformation. Subsequently, such behavior was also observed by Sajjadi et al.  [8] in GTD-111 and by Chu et al. [9] in the DS Ni-base superalloy DZ951.  

Furthermore, Tian et al. [17] investigated the influence of temperature on the  operative tensile deformation mechanism in a Co-rich Ni-base polycrystalline  superalloy and obtained the results that as the temperature increases from room  temperature (RT) to 725 °C, the dominant deformation mechanism changes from APB  MANUSCRIPT shearing to stacking fault shearing, and then to microtwinning. Nevertheless, most of  these studies were generally performed on the severely deformed or fracture  specimens to identify the predominant deformation mechanism. However, it is  worthwhile to note that the deformation substructures usually evolve as a function of  plastic strain for Ni-base superalloys [4, 6, 19]. Thus, in order to correlate the yield  strength with the activation of plastic deformation mechanisms, it is imperative to  understand the interaction of matrix dislocations with  of plastic deformation. For these reasons, the aim of this paper is to investigate the  tensile deformation mechanisms that operate in M4706 during initial yielding at  

In the following, the experimental procedures are described in Section 2. In  Section 3, transmission electron microscopy (TEM) observations provide evidence for  population of different crystal defects in the specimens deformed to about 0.2%  plastic strain at various temperatures. These defects are analyzed in detail, and the  effect of temperature on their formation is discussed. Meanwhile, discussions on the  relationship between the yield strength of M4706 and the operative deformation  mechanisms are also included. Finally, the conclusions are drawn in Section 4.

# 3.   Results and discussion  

# 3.1.   Variation of the yield strength with temperature  

The temperature-dependent yield strength  σ 0.2  of M4706 is plotted in Fig. 3. For  comparison, the variations of the yield strength of the alloys GTD-111 [8] and DZ951  MANUSCRIPT [9] with temperature are also included. Similar to GTD-111 and DZ951, the  experimental alloy also exhibits a three-stage temperature dependence of flow stress.  In the low temperature range, the yield strength decreases slightly from 1046 to 915  MPa with increasing temperature from RT to 650 °C. In the intermediate temperature  range, a peak value (about 1043 MPa) is observed between 700 and 800 °C. In the  high temperature range, the yield strength decreases rapidly from 879 to 322 MPa as  the temperature increases from 800 to 1000 °C. Nonetheless, it is evident that the  experimental alloy M4706 is stronger than GTD-111 and DZ951 at equivalent  ACCEPTED temperatures, especially in low and intermediate temperature regimes. The origin of the excellent high-temperature yield strength of  M4706 will be discussed in great detail in the following sections.

# 3.2.2.   At intermediate temperatures  

Fig. 5 shows the typical microstructures of the specimens deformed to around  0.2% plastic strain at 700 (Fig. 5(a) and (b)) and 750 °C (Fig. 5(c)-(d)). It is clear from  the figure that the deformation patterns observed at these two temperatures are almost  identical to those observed at low temperatures, both of which display three striking  ACCEPTED features. Firstly, a large number of isolated SISFs/SESFs, Shockley loops as well as  ESFs are visible, which is the same scenario as that shown in Fig. 4, and suggests that  stacking fault shearing also operates actively at these two temperatures. Secondly,  numerous  /2<101> dislocations are observed. These dislocations usually appear in  pairs within the matrix channels, but are difficult to be distinguished insides the  γ′   precipitates, such as the ones indicated by the white arrowheads in the Fig. 5(a) and  (c). These experimental observations demonstrate that the APB energy of the  γ′  phase  is very high, and shearing of  γ′  precipitates by APB-coupled dislocation pairs has also  occurred during tensile deformation at intermediate temperatures, as pointed out by  Milligan et al. [4] and Sun et al. [16]. Finally, a close inspection of the dislocation  configuration reveals that many of the dislocation pairs, which are deposited at the  γ / γ′  interfaces, are strongly curved and surround the  γ′  precipitates, as indicated by the  white triangles in the Fig. 5(a) and (c). # 3.2.3.   At high temperatures  

The typical microstructures of the specimens deformed to about 0.2% plastic  strain at 800 and 900 °C are shown in Fig. 6(a)-(b) and Fig. 6(c)-(d), respectively.  Comparing the deformation microstructures with those observed at low and  ACCEPTED intermediate temperatures, one can find that the content of isolated SISFs/SESFs  within   precipitates is much higher. Additionally, two directions of superlattice  stacking faults (SSFs), which lie under 90° relative to each other, are frequently  g {200} two-beam conditions when  orienting the specimen near the [001] zone axis. This fault configuration is in  principal identical with those observed in GTD-111 after tensile deformation at 800 °C  [8] and in SRR99 at 850 °C [7], revealing that the formation of SSFs becomes easier  with increasing in temperature. However, as the temperature increases further, only a  few SSFs are visible in the slightly deformed specimens (see Fig. 7(a)-(b) (950 °C)  and Fig. 7(c)-(d) (980 °C)), indicating that while stacking fault shearing has also  occurred, it does not play a pivotal role in the initial plastic deformation.  Notwithstanding, it is interesting to find that isolated SSFs formed at the four  temperatures are usually connected by individual matrix dislocation and are  surrounded by Shockley loops. Therefore, it is believed that the formation mechanism  of these faults created at high temperatures is similar to that of the faults produced at  low and intermediate temperatures.  

With regards to the dislocation configurations, it is interesting to note that paired  MANUSCRIPT dislocations are rarely visible within the matrix channels of the slightly deformed  specimens. Instead, individual bowed dislocations and zigzag dislocations are more  frequently observed with increasing temperature, as shown in Figs. 6 and 7. The  content of dislocations inside the  γ′ occasionally visible in some regions of Figs. 6 and 7. All these observations suggest  ACCEPTED that APB shearing does not control the initial plastic deformation at high temperatures.  Instead, Orowan bowing and cross-slip of dislocations start to prevail. Moreover, upon  close inspection, it is interesting to find that many wavy dislocations, which are   interfaces, are oriented almost parallel to the cube edges of the  γ′   precipitates and tend to weave with nearby dislocations in the specimens deformed to  about 0.2% plastic strain at 950 and 980 °C, as shown in Fig. 7. These dislocation  structures were also observed in DZ951 after tensile deformation at 1000 °C [9] and in  the superalloy TMS-75(+Ru) after creep deformation at 1100 °C [28, 29]. They  pointed out that these configurations are the result of combined activation of glide and  climb of dislocations. Thus, it is tempting to envisage that such processes have also  taken place under the present deformation conditions. In order to confirm this, the  dislocation structures of these specimens were further investigated. As shown in Fig. 8,  apart from a few straight dislocations, numerous strongly curved dislocations and  wavy dislocations are visible when orienting the specimen on the {200} Kikuchi band,  MANUSCRIPT which is close to the [011] zone axis. According to Edington [22], if all the  dislocations are on the same {111} slip planes, the dislocations should be observed to  be straight. However, the fact is not the case. Therefore, these observations  demonstrate that both dislocation climb and dislocation cross-slip have taken place  massively. Furthermore, with respect to the dislocation structures, another noteworthy  feature is that the distribution of matrix dislocations is more homogeneous as  compared to those at low and intermediate temperatures. This is consistent with the  results reported by Takeuchi and Argon [30] that when dislocations can readily  disperse by cross-slip and climb as well as Orowan bowing after they have emitted  from the dislocation sources, a homogeneous dislocation distribution will appear.  

# 3.3.   Variation of the formation of stacking faults with temperature  

As stated above, extensive isolated SISFs/SESFs and ESFs are produced in  M4706 after about 0.2% plastic strain at low and intermediate temperatures, and the  SSF density increases gradually as the deformation temperature is raised from RT to  900 °C. However, when the temperature is beyond 900 °C, the SSF density in the  slightly deformed specimen starts to decrease with temperature. This behavior is  somewhat surprising with respect to the results reported in the literature [5, 7-9, 12,  13], where in general showed that the faults formed at low temperatures are faulted  loops and their density decreases as the deformation temperature increases from RT to  about 600 °C (depending on the alloy), while the faults created at intermediate  temperatures are isolated SISFs/SESFs or ESFs and their density increases with  increasing temperature from 650 to around 800 °C. Basically, all these  theories assumed that the anomalous increase in the flow stress with temperature is  due to the thermally activated cube cross-slip that inhibits the gliding of mobile  dislocations.  As the deformation temperature increases further dislocation cross-slip and dislocation climb become more and more prevalent (see  Figs. 6-8). It has been generally understood that the stresses required for  the initiation of these processes are much lower than those needed for the shearing  processes to occur, especially in the case of dislocation climb [7, 9, 40, 41]. Therefore,  the yield strength of M4706 drops rapidly with increasing temperature.  

Now it is clear that extensive stacking fault shearing can decrease the yield  strength of M4706 at low and intermediate temperatures. Then, a question arisen is  why the experimental alloy is still stronger than GTD-111 and DZ951. Actually, this  can be easily understood in light of the chemical compositions of these alloys.  Compared with GTD-111 and DZ951, the experimental alloy M4706 contains a  higher concentration of metallic elements Ta and W (see Table 1). According to  Crudden et al. [42] and Titus et al. [37], if these metallic elements are added into  superalloys, the APB energy of the  γ′  phase would increase significantly. It is well  known that as a first approximation, the critical shear stress associated with APB  shearing is proportional to the APB energy, i.e.   τ = γ 2 b  [7, 27]. So, a higher  APB APB APB energy requires a higher applied stress for paired dislocations to enter the  MANUSCRIPT precipitates. Moreover, it is clear from Fig. 9 that the contribution to the yield strength  originates mainly from APB shearing. Thus, the highest yield strength can be found in  the experimental alloy at the same temperature in low and intermediate temperature  regimes.  As the deformation temperature rises further respects, possesses similar plastic deformation features compared to GTD-111 [8] and  DZ951 [9]. Hence, the experimental alloy should not be stronger any more at high  temperatures. However, the fact is not the case. This can be because important  differences in the deformation modes exist in these alloys. As manifested in Figs. 6  ACCEPTED and 7, apart from these similar deformation mechanisms, stacking fault shearing also  operates in M4706, although the SSF density is very low in the specimens after slight  deformation at temperatures above 900 °C. In contrast to the deformation conditions  at low and intermediate temperatures, stacking fault formation within  γ′  precipitates  could lead to a very high work hardening rate due to the more homogeneous  dislocation distribution at high temperatures and thus, to some extent, would sustain  the strength above the peak temperature [41]. Therefore, M4706 still exhibits superior  yield strength as compared to GTD-111 and DZ951 in the high temperature range.  

# 4.   Conclusions  

1.   Dislocation pairs, ESFs and isolated SISFs/SESFs enclosed by Shockley loops are  

frequently observed in the specimens after slight deformation at low and  intermediate temperatures, thereby demonstrating that their formations are not  thermally assisted.  

2.   As the deformation temperature increases from RT to 650 °C, more and more  MANUSCRIPT precipitates are sheared by a/3<112> partial dislocations accompanied by the  formation of SSFs during initial plastic deformation, which might result in the  slight decrease in the yield strength of M4706 with temperature at low  temperatures since the stress required for the operation of stacking fault shearing  is much lower than that that needed for APB shearing to occur.

# Highlights  

   A new directionally solidified Ni-base superalloy M4706 is developed.  

   M4706 possesses excellent creep rupture properties and tensile properties.  

   Isolated SISFs/SESFs are observed in a wide temperature range.  

   The deformation mechanisms of M4706 are insensitive to temperature below  800 °C.
