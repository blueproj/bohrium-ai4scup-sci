# A B S T R A C T  

The construction of Solid Electrolyte Interface (SEI)  ﬁ lm in Li-ion batteries with functional electrolyte additives is able to passivate the active material surface and inhibit the decomposition of the electrolyte continuously. In addition, safety issue is also an important factor restricting the large scale application of present lithium-ion batteries. Therefore, the additives for  ﬁ lm-forming and safety enhancement are a class of cost-effective compo- nents that promote the application and development of batteries. Fluorine is a kind of bipolar  element, which  “ ” has strong electronegativity and weak polarity. Fluorine-containing electrolyte additives have excellent kinetic reactivity, which can preferentially generate stable SEI  ﬁ lms and uniform Cathode-Electrolyte Interface (CEI) ﬁ lms to effectively improve the electrochemical performance of the batteries. Meanwhile,  ﬂ uorine-containing electrolyte additives can also be used as  ﬂ ame-retardants to improve safety performance. In this review, we summarize the research status of  ﬂ uorine-containing additives in recent years and elaborate its reaction mech- anisms of improving battery performance. Finally, a personal perspective on the future of the development of ﬂ uorine-containing additives is presented.  

Keywords: Lithium-ion batteries Electrolyte additive Fluorine-containing Electrode-electrolyte interface  

During the  ﬁ rst charge and discharge of lithium-ion battery, the organic electrolyte decomposes partially, forming a Solid Electrolyte Interphase (SEI) layer on the anode surface or a Cathode Electrolyte Interphase (CEI) layer on the cathode surface. These interfacial layers in principle are electronically insulated and only allow lithium ion to pass through freely, which should not affect the charge and discharge per- formance of the battery. What is more important, the interfacial layers could prevent the direct contact between the electrode and the electro- lyte, effectively passivating the electrode, and inhibit the strong redox reaction of the electrolyte at the electrode interface, which depresses the irreversible damage of the electrode [ 13 ]. The relationship between interfacial  ﬁ lm formation, capacity decay, lithium ion diffusion and interface reaction kinetics in lithium-ion batteries was studied through modeling. The results showed that capacity decay is largely affected by interface reaction kinetics as compared to the lithium ion diffusion  

# 1. Introduction  

Nowadays, Lithium-Ion Batteries (LIBs) have been widely used in portable electronic devices, electric vehicles and large-scale energy storage [ 1 5 ]. However, there are still several challenges existing with – lithium-ion batteries while pursuing higher energy density and power capability. Electrolyte is an important component dictating the cycle stability, high-voltage stability, and safety of LIBs [ 6 9 ]. Although the – content is generally less than 5%, functional electrolyte additives play a critically important role in improving the electrochemical/chemical stability of the electrode-electrolyte interface, which is considered one of the most cost-effective methods to improve the performance of lithium-ion batteries. With the advantages such as small dosage and obvious effect, it is easy to achieve a balance between performance and cost [ 10 12 ].

Safety is another concern in the large scale application of lithium-ion batteries. At present, most of the electrolytes used in lithium-ion batteries are organic car- bonate electrolytes. The  ﬂ ash point of carbonate solvent, especially those chain carbonate solvent molecules, is pretty low and can be burnt easily. Especially under abuse conditions, such as high temperature operation, overcharge, overdischarge and so on, the increase of side reactions of electrolyte may lead to battery thermal runaway and even explosion [ 15 ]. Therefore, improving the safety performance of lithium-ion battery is of great signi ﬁ cance to protect the personal and property safety of users and further expand the application of lithium-ion battery [ 9 , 16 18 ].  

– Fluorine [ 19 ] has extremely strong electronegativity and weak po- larity. Fluorinated additives typically show relatively lower Lowest Un- occupied Molecular Orbital (LUMO) energy and lower Highest Occupied Molecular Orbital (HOMO) energy [ 20 ]. Electrochemical experiments such as linear sweep voltammetry and cyclic voltammetry [ 21 ] can also verify whether additives can be oxidized or reduced before electrolyte solvents. These characteristics are bene ﬁ cial for enhancing the interfacial ﬁ lm forming capability on the anode side and the stability against oxidation of solvent molecules on the cathode side at high voltage [ 22 ], improving the compatibility between electrolyte and active materials [ 23 ], and prolonging the cycling performance of the LIBs. Some ﬂ uorine-containing additives can passivate the cathode interface and inhibit the oxidative decomposition of the electrolyte. In addition,  ﬂ uo- rinated compounds have higher  ﬂ ash points, so that thermal stability of the electrolyte and the safety performance of lithium-ion batteries could be signi ﬁ cantly improved in the presence of  ﬂ uorinated additives [ 24 ]. Some disadvantages of the  ﬂ uorine-containing additives may react with water and produce toxic gases [ 25 ]. In this review,  ﬁ rstly, the application of  ﬂ uorine-containing additives for manipulating the functionality of SEI/CEI  ﬁ lms is discussed. Then, the use of  ﬂ uorinated additives for enhancing the safety performance and the related functioning mecha- nism in lithium-ion batteries is introduced. Finally, some personal per- spectives on future development of  ﬂ uorinated additives for higher energy density and high safety LIBs are proposed.  

# 2. SEI  ﬁ lm-forming additives  

The anode is one of the important components that affect the battery performance. Currently, the most intensively studied anodes are graphite, silicon-based materials and lithium metal. Solvent co- intercalation, large volume variation and growth of lithium dendrites are the main reasons that affect the stability of the anode. The SEI  ﬁ lm is an anode interface protecting  ﬁ lm, which generally could separate the electrode from the electrolyte and do no harm to the charge and discharge performance of the battery. The dense interface  ﬁ lm could inhibit the decomposition of small solvent molecules at the interface which diffuse from electrolyte bulk to the electrode surface. The strong and elastic interface  ﬁ lm could adapt to the volume change of the anode, avoiding the unwanted destruction and regeneration of the interface ﬁ lm. The uniform interface  ﬁ lm could achieve the uniform lithium deposition, prevent the growth of lithium dendrites and improve safety and stability of lithium metal battery. The interface  ﬁ lm is composed of decomposition products of electrolyte components, and the properties of the interface  ﬁ lm could be manipulated by  ﬁ lm-forming additives. Therefore, the use of an effective  ﬁ lm-forming additive is regarded one of the important approaches to improve the electrochemical performance of batteries.  

# 2.1. FEC  ﬁ lm-forming additive  

Fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC) is currently considered as a  ﬁ lm- forming additive with excellent performance, and is also a well- established  ﬂ uorine-containing additive [ 26 32 ]. Quantum chemical – calculation results show that [ 33 ] the LUMO energy of FEC is much lower than that of ethylene carbonate (EC), indicating that FEC can be reduced at a higher potential and inhibit the reductive decomposition of the main solvents of the electrolyte (EC, DEC, EMC) at \~0.8 V [ 34 ]. In addition, FEC can also form a more stable SEI  ﬁ lm with low impedance at the anode interface, which can effectively improve the cycle stability and rate performance of the battery, especially for the silicon-carbon anode. The low temperature performance of lithium-ion batteries can be improved by the FEC-oriented interface layer, which has less resistant and is favorable for the reversible intercalation/de-intercalation of lithium ions [ 35 , 36 ]. Therefore, FEC is considered an ideal  ﬁ lm-forming additive according to the previous reports by battery researchers.  

Studies have shown that the kinetics of lithium ion transfer at the graphite interface is affected by the composition of the SEI layer and the electrolyte [ 37 , 38 ]. Xu et al. [ 39 ] showed that the speci ﬁ c discharge capacity and cycle performance of graphitized mesocarbon microbeads (MCMB)//Li batteries could be signi ﬁ cantly improved by adding FEC into the electrolyte. The SEI  ﬁ lm on the anode surface was mainly composed of the decomposition product of FEC at about 1 V. The  ﬁ lm was thin and stable, which bene ﬁ ted the deintercalation of lithium ions. The SEI  ﬁ lm structure generated by FEC contains polymer constituents enriched more LiF, which reduced the interface kinetical barrier for fast lithium ion insertion and extraction, therefore improving the low tem- perature performance of lithium ion batteries [ 29 , 40 , 41 ]. Liao et al. [ 42 ] found that SEI  ﬁ lm formed at the interface of MCMB electrode in FEC-containing electrolyte had higher ionic conductivity, which could be the reason for the improved rate performance and low temperature cycle performance. Via the three-electrode experiment of the pouch-type bat- tery, Bian et al. [ 43 ] further proved that the use of FEC reduced the charge and discharge polarization of graphite anode at low temperature. Especially, the charge transfer resistance (R ct ) of the graphite interface was effectively reduced. LiF is one of the important components of the anode interface  ﬁ lm, because the high-modulus LiF shell could suppress the Li x Si pulverization [ 44 ], and its high chemical stability could effec- tively suppress side reactions at the interface [ 45 ]. In addition, LiF-rich SEI exhibits a high interfacial energy with Li metal, which effectively promotes the planar growth of Li metal and inhibits the formation of Li dendrites [ 46 ]. The current quantitative analysis methods used for LiF are mainly XPS and cryo-electron microscopy [ 21 ]. FEC is conducive to the formation of LiF-rich SEI layer. Therefore, the addition of FEC can also generate a smooth and stable SEI layer on the surface of the silicon thin  ﬁ lm [ 31 , 47 ] and the lithium metal [ 32 ]. Electrolyte containing 2 vol % FEC had better rate performance for graphite//Li[Ni 0.5 Mn 0.3 Co 0.2 ]O 2 batteries, and could also suppress the uneven lithium deposition [ 48 ]. Mogi et al. [ 49 ] studied the effects of FEC on the deposition and stripping of lithium. The results showed that the addition of 5 wt% FEC could effectively improve the cycle ef ﬁ ciency and generate an interface  ﬁ lm with low impedance. The  in situ  Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM) research showed that a uniform and tightly packed granular deposit layer with a diameter of about 100 to 150 nm appeared on the surface of the electrode in FEC-containing electrolyte. The mechanism by which FEC improved the lithium deposition was that lower interface impedance was bene ﬁ cial for the uniform distribution of current and reduced the growth of lithium dendrites. Through Fourier Transform Infrared (FTIR) spectra, Markevich et al. [ 50 ] found that the relative content of the polycarbonate species and the reduction products of the cyclic carbonate in the surface ﬁ lms formed in FEC-containing electrolyte was higher than in EC-containing electrolyte. Thus, in FEC-containing electrolyte, the SEI on Li electrodes was enriched with FEC and salt-reduction products, ensuring long-term stable cycling of Li metal electrodes. Zhang et al. [ 51 ] found that FEC additives could achieve an LiF-rich SEI and effectively protect Li metal. The  ﬁ rst-principles calculations indicated that FEC could easily be reduced on the surface of Li metal anode to form LiF-rich SEI, which was ef ﬁ cient to suppress Li dendrite growth. 5wt% FEC electrolyte is capable of enabling the uniform and dense deposition of Li, while improving the coulombic ef ﬁ ciency of Li//Cu cells (98%) and reducing polarization.  

The silicon-carbon anode is being developed for next generation lithium ion batteries in pursuit of higher energy density. However, silicon-based anodes have signi ﬁ cant volume changes during lithiation and delithiation, leading to the instability and structural degradation of SEI  ﬁ lms on silicon-carbon anode surface, which is considered the main reason for the rapid capacity decline of Si anode-based battery. Marke- vich et al. [ 52 ] studied the in ﬂ uence of FEC on the cycling performance of Si//Li batteries. The result presented that Si//Li batteries could reach about 2000 stable cycles in FEC-containing electrolyte. In both EC-based and FEC-based electrolyte ( Fig. S1a ), the Si electrodes would turn into separate islands after cycling, about 10 30  μ m in diameter. In the case of – the FEC-containing electrolyte, these islands were coated with homoge- neous and relatively thin surface  ﬁ lm. F 1s and C 1s XPS spectra of Si electrodes after 500 cycles were shown in  Fig. S1b . The ratio of LiF was higher for the FEC-containing electrolyte than the EC-based one. The C 1s spectra demonstrated a pronounced difference between the electrodes ’ surface chemistry in the two kinds of electrolyte in terms of the oxygen-containing species in the surface  ﬁ lms. The rising ratio of LiF, the predominance of the oxygen-free organic species (probably polymeric), the higher content of Li x PO y F z  species, were the causes of improved performance of Si//Li batteries with FEC. Nie et al. [ 53 ] used nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy to study the composition of the SEI of Si electrode after cycling in FEC-containing electrolyte. The pri- mary components of the SEI formed with EC-containing electrolyte were lithium ethylene dicarbonate, LiF, and Li x SiO y . The predominant prod- ucts of SEI formed with FEC-containing electrolyte are LiF, an insoluble polymeric species, and Li x SiO y . As FEC was an additional source of LiF, the concentration of LiF was much higher for Si electrodes cycled with FEC than with EC. The SEI formed with the FEC-containing electrolyte provides superior passivation of the Si electrodes, leading to better ca- pacity and structure retention. Schroder et al. [ 54 ] used X-ray Photo- electron Spectroscopy (XPS) and Time-of-Flight Secondary Ion Mass Spectrometry (ToF-SIMS) to explore the SEI structure and composition, which evidenced that FEC reduction led to  ﬂ uoride ion and LiF forma- tion. They also found that these species decrease the solubility of Li 2 O in the SEI and thus increase the reactivity of the silicon surface. They concluded that the effectiveness of FEC at improving the coulombic ef- ﬁ ciency and capacity retention was due to  ﬂ uoride ion formation from reduction of the electrolyte, which led to the formation of a kinetically stable SEI on the silicon-oxide anode surface.  

FEC has a good  ﬁ lm-forming effect as an electrolyte additive. Therefore, in some special materials, FEC is considered indispensable and often used in combination with other additives for achieving the desired results. Therefore, it is necessary to develop suitable additives to work with FEC. Han et al. [ 55 ] reported lithium malonato(di ﬂ uoro)borate (LiFMDFB) showed good electrochemical performance in the silicon graphite composite (SGC)//lithium-rich (Li 1.17 Ni 0.17 Mn 0.5 Co 0.17 O 2 ) battery when it was used in combination with FEC. Whereas the energy density of SGC//Li-rich full cells with baseline electrolyte faded severely over 200 cycles, the use of LiFMDFB led to an obvious improvement in the retention of energy density ( Fig. 1 a). The severely cracked surfaces of the SGC particles revealed that the FEC-derived SEI did not tolerate the volumetric strain induced by the expansion and contraction of Si during cycling. In contrast, the combination of LiFMDFB and FEC produced a thin and uniform SEI on the SGC particles and the FEC  þ  LiFMDFB-dic- tated SEI was mechanically robust upon repeated cycling ( Fig. 1 b). The SEM and mapping ( Fig. 1 c – j) also showed that FEC  þ  LiFMDFB could effectively improve the structural stability of SGC anode. In addition, LiFMDFB was also a dual-functional additive. It is possible that additives participate in the formation of SEI and CEI in a LIB cell. When the redox potential of the additive is between the solvent and the lithium salt, the additive can undergo preferential oxidation and reduction reactions. The LiFMDFB-oriented CEI functioned to trap O 2  underneath it, thereby reducing the formation of reactive superoxide anion radicals, which promoted solvent decomposition. Moreover, the controlled cath- ode electrolyte interface promoted by LiFMDFB could prevented severe – intergranular cracking and mitigated the phase transformation from a layered to spinel phase of the Li-rich cathode. Jo et al. [ 56 ] used FEC and bis(2,2,2-tri ﬂ uoroethyl) carbonate (DFDEC) as additives for silicon-carbon anodes. The study found that the SEI  ﬁ lm formed by this binary additive contains not only Li 2 CO 3 , anhydride and carboxylate formed by FEC, but also alkyl carbonate, LiF and OPF 3-y (OR) y /Li x PF y O z produced by DFDEC. This SEI layer with a mixture of organic and inor- ganic compounds could effectively passivate the anode interface [ 57 ], stabilized the electrode structure, and signi ﬁ cantly improved the cycling performance of the silicon-carbon anode. Ren et al. [ 58 ] studied the synergistic effect of LiAsF 6  and FEC. The results showed that the SEI layer contained Li x As alloy and LiF was generated by the reduction of LiAsF 6 . The addition of FEC not only realized the uniform distribution of Li x As crystal nucleus, but also improved the  ﬂ exibility of SEI layer. Therefore, the electrolyte containing LiAsF 6  and FEC effectively improved the cycle life of Li metal battery. In addition, there may be a certain degree of competitiveness or con ﬂ ict effects between the combination of various additives, and unsuitable combinations will have a negative effect on the battery performance. Jeff Dahn [ 59 ] found that different combinations of additives and different electrolyte solvents will cause great diversity in the electrochemical performance of the same battery system.  

As an important  ﬁ lm-forming additive, the study on the reaction mechanism of FEC could help to design more superior additives. Li et al. [ 60 ] studied the composition, structure and distribution of SEI on silicon carbon composite electrode with or without FEC additive by NMR spectroscopy, XPS and X-ray Photoemission Electron Microscopy (X-PEEM). The results showed that a large amount of LiF produced by FEC decomposition could cover the surface of silicon particles and alle- viate the volume change during the cycle. In addition, the decomposition of FEC formed a dense SEI layer with more uniform component distri- bution at different depths. This effectively inhibited the migration of LiPF 6  and small molecules generated by its hydrolysis to the inner layer

The composition and structure of the SEI layer evolve dynamically during charging and discharging. There are multiple reaction pathways for electrochemical organic reactions inside the battery. Many active intermediates and transition states are dif ﬁ cult to track through in-situ detection. Even some stable reaction products are dif ﬁ cult to analyze and quantify. The complexity and variability of the internal reaction of the battery has greatly hindered the study of the mechanism of additives. Theoretical calculation and modeling insight [ 63 ] are quantum-level research methods and are bene ﬁ cial for understanding the mechanism of additives. Studies found that EC and FEC would adsorb to amorphous silicon through carbonyl oxygen atoms. Moreover, FEC could induce EC molecules to form an orderly orientation on the surface of Si [ 64 ], which contributed to the diffusion of Li þ . In addition, FEC coordinated with Li þ would reduce decomposition at a higher potential [ 65 ], and preferen- tially generate a passive SEI  ﬁ lm at the anode interface. The proportion of LiF in the FEC-containing electrolyte reduction product was higher than that of the EC-containing electrolyte. Julibeth et al. [ 66 ] used density functional theory and ab initio molecular dynamics simulations to study the reduction mechanism of FEC at the silicon surface. The results showed that FEC could react in three different ways. In some cases, FEC and VC would form the same reduction products. However, FEC molecules could also react through two other ways to generate some active small molecules which further polymerize to form SEI layer of different components. In addition, in FEC decomposition pathways, the presence of  ﬂ uorine atoms made FEC easy to be decomposed reductively, thereby forming LiF on the anode surface. Yukihiro et al. [ 67 ] found through theoretical calculations that the most reasonable product of FEC by reductive decomposition was LiF, and systematically studied its effect towards SEI. The results showed that LiF played a role of gluing in the organic components in the SEI layer. In addition, the interface structure between this LiF aggregate and the lithiated silicon anode was also strongly bonded. The effect of this strong adhesion may be well corre- lated with the enhanced performance of the silicon anode. Moreover, FEC would be reductively decomposed more easily in silicon anodes at a higher lithiated state or a full battery with a higher state of charge [ 68 ]. The reasons for the differences between the experimental test and the theoretical calculation may be as follows: (1) In the actual charging and discharging process, the FEC reaction is affected by many factors (the environment of electrolyte solvent, partial voltage changes, the property of the electrode-electrolyte interface, etc) and the product is complicated. And the experimental test can only detect the stable parts of the products, so the NMR analysis also has a certain deviation; (2) Theoretical calcu- lation infers the possible reaction products of FEC from the perspective of energy. It cannot truly reappear the complex and changeable environ- ment of electrolyte solvent and electrode interface. Although there are some differences between experimental tests and theoretical calcula- tions. However, in order to explore the mechanism of FEC well, it is necessary to combine experiments and theoretical calculations. Both of them are important veri ﬁ cation methods.

# 2.2. Non-FEC  ﬁ lm-forming additives  

In addition to the  ﬂ uorinated additives represented by FEC, some other  ﬂ uorinated molecules also show good anode  ﬁ lm-forming effects. Li et al. [ 73 ] showed that SEI  ﬁ lm formed by 4- ﬂ u oro phenyl acetate (4-FPA) is more protective than phenyl acetate (PA), which could effectively prevent the co-intercalation of PC [ 74 ] and suppress the peeling of graphite layered structure, so as to improve the cycle stability of lithium-ion battery. The  ﬂ uorine in 4-FPA made it easier to be reduced than PA, and the introduction of  ﬂ uorine and aromatic ring reduction product into SEI  ﬁ lm in 4-FPA was helpful to improve the protective effect of SEI  ﬁ lm on graphite. Yamagiwa et al. [ 75 ] found that the elec- trolyte containing 1,2-bis (di ﬂ uoromethylsilyl) ethane (FSE) could form a thin and stable SEI layer containing Si and F compounds on the graphite interface, which could effectively inhibit the decomposition of electro- lyte and self-discharge even at high temperatures. Chen et al. [ 76 ] syn- thesized allyl tris(2,2,2-tri ﬂ uoroethyl) carbonate (ATFEC) as a ﬁ lm-forming additive. It was found that ATFEC had good electro- chemical compatibility with natural graphite anode and LiCoO 2  cathode material. The electrolyte containing 1 vol% ATFEC showed high thermal stability, high ionic conductivity and good  ﬁ lm-forming properties. Also, the co-intercalation of PC into graphite anode could be suppressed. Tong et al. [ 77 ] synthesized trimethylsilyl ( ﬂ uorosulfonyl) (n-non- a ﬂ u oro butane s ulf on yl) imide (TMS-FNFSI) as a SEI  ﬁ lm-forming additive for lithium metal anodes ( Fig. 4 ) and showed excellent electrochemical performance. The studies have shown that it could be preferentially reduced prior to the reductive decomposition of the main components of the electrolyte. XPS results showed that the organic SEI  ﬁ lm rich in LiF and Silicon-compound was produced, which effectively inhibited the growth of lithium dendrites. Ma et al. [ 78 ] found that tris(2,2, 2-tri ﬂ uoroethyl) borate (TTFEB) could improve the interface properties of lithium metal anodes, promote the uniform deposition of lithium, and suppress the formation of lithium dendrites. The reason was that the ﬂ uorine-containing groups in TTFEB could promote the formation of LiF-rich SEI  ﬁ lms, which stabilized the lithium metal/electrolyte inter- face. Lee et al. [ 79 ] synthesized a series of  ﬂ uorinated aryl and  ﬂ uori- nated alkyl borate compounds, functioning as anion acceptors, which were bene ﬁ cial for improving the ionic conductivity of the electrolyte. In addition, the stronger the electron-withdrawing ability of the substituent groups in the borate compound, the higher the conductivity enhancement.  

In addition to  ﬂ uorinated organic additives,  ﬂ uorinated lithium salts also show good  ﬁ lm-forming ability, and have been demonstrated to improve the battery cycling performance. Liao et al. [ 80 ] found that lithium di ﬂ uorobis(oxalate) lithium phosphate (LiDFBOP) could signi ﬁ - cantly improve the low-temperature performance of lithium-ion batte- ries. Under low temperature conditions (0    C), the baseline electrolyte was severely decomposed during the cycling, resulting in a large amount of deposits covering the surface of the electrode (especially the graphite anode). This would cause quick increase of interface impedance and slow down the kinetics of lithium ion insertion/extraction process. However, LiDFBOP could take precedence over the reduction reaction of the elec- trolyte solvent to form an interface protective  ﬁ lm on the graphite anode. In addition, the interface  ﬁ lm generated by LiDFBOP could not only suppress the decomposition of the electrolyte and show a higher ionic conductivity, but also could improve the cycle stability of graph- ite//LiNi 0.5 Co 0.2 Mn 0.3 O 2  full battery at low temperature. Other research results [ 81 ] showed that 5 wt% lithium di ﬂ uoro(sulfate) borate (LiBF 2 SO 4 ) could signi ﬁ cantly reduce the interface impedance of the battery, show higher ionic conductivity on the MCMB surface and improve the rate performance of the battery. Sharova et al. [ 82 ] added lithium  ﬂ uorosulfonyl(tri ﬂ u oro methane s ulf on yl)imide (LiFTFSI) into the electrolyte and found that the  ﬁ rst coulombic ef ﬁ ciency and long-term cycling stability of the graphite//LiFePO 4  battery was signi ﬁ cantly improved. Their studies showed that the electrolyte containing LiFTFSI could obtain a thinner SEI  ﬁ lm and promote the diffusion of Li þ   in the SEI layer.

The irreversible phase trans- – formation of the cathode structure, the catalytic reaction at the active site of the cathode, and the dissolution of transition metal ions are the key factors affecting the performance of the battery. In particular, the con- ventional electrolyte composed of carbonate solvents and LiPF 6  un- dergoes severe oxidative decomposition when the voltage exceeds 4.5 V [ 91 ]. This leads to quick depletion of electrolyte and fast capacity degradation of lithium-ion batteries. Developing functional electrolyte additives for interface protection is one of the most cost-effective methods to solve these problems. Among them,  ﬂ uorine-containing ad- ditives are inclined to form a stable CEI  ﬁ lm, which is able to hinder the direct contact between cathode and electrolyte, and reduce the oxidation decomposition of electrolyte. Furthermore, the stabilized CEI layer could also inhibit the dissolution of transition metal ions, improving the high-voltage cycling performance of lithium-ion batteries.  

Fluorinated esters have been reported as CEI-forming additives, showing a protective effect on cathode material interface. Lee et al. [ 92 ] demonstrated using methyl (2,2,2-tri ﬂ uoroethyl) carbonate (FEMC) to stabilize cathode surface, and improved the high voltage cycling per- formance of Li//LiNi 0.5 Co 0.2 Mn 0.3 O 2 (NCM523) battery at 3 4.6 V – ( Fig. S2a ). Studies showed that the addition of 5 wt% FEMC could generate metal  ﬂ uoride and C F-containing compounds in the CEI layer, – effectively passivating the cathode surface ( Figs. S2b and c ). Zheng et al. [ 93 ] introduced two kinds of  ﬂ uoroester additives, methyl 3,3,3-tri ﬂ uor- opropionate (MTFP) and ethyl 3,3,3-tri ﬂ uoropropionate (ETFP), for graphite//NCM111 batteries. The results presented that with 0.5 wt% ETFP, the capacity retention of the battery after 100 charge-discharge cycles was increased to 76.1% as compared to 45.6% for the baseline. The improved battery performance was ascribed to the thinner and more stable CEI layer derived from MTFP and ETFP, which reduced the interface impedance and suppressed the oxidative decomposition of the electrolyte at high voltages. Zhu et al. [ 94 ] synthesized per ﬂ uorooctyl substituted ethylene carbonate (PFO-EC) which was then used as an electrolyte additive for graphite//Li 1.2 Ni 0.15 Mn 0.55 Co 0.1 O 2  batteries. This per ﬂ uoroalkane-substituted compound had amphiphilic groups, leading to the formation of a double-layer SEI  ﬁ lm on the electrode surface. This SEI  ﬁ lm prevented direct contact between the electrolyte and the cathode/anode active materials, inhibited the parasitic reaction of electrolyte, and improved the cycling stability of the battery.  

Fluorinated additives containing phosphorus and nitriles also show positive effects on improving the high-voltage stability of lithium-ion batteries. Representative ly, ethoxy-(penta ﬂ uoro)-cyclotriphosphazene (PFN) has been reported as a good additive for high-voltage LNMO bat- teries [ 95 ]. Studies found that PFN could preferentially undergo oxida- tive decomposition, producing linear polymers, polycyclic polymers, LiNO 3 , RONO 2 Li (R stands for organic residues) etc., and simultaneously generating Li 3 PO 4  and ROPO 3 Li (ROPO 3 : phosphate monoester). These substances gathered at the surface of the cathode, facilitating the for- mation of a dense, uniform and thin CEI  ﬁ lm, which suppressed the decomposition of the electrolyte and protected the LNMO material from structural damage. Besides, Xu et al. [ 96 ] found that tris (penta- ﬂ uorophenyl) phosphine (TPFPP) additive could be preferably oxidized on LNMO cathode during charge process, forming a protective CEI layer on the cathode which prevents the electrolyte from oxidation decom- position. Cresce et al. [ 97 ] used 1 wt% tris(hexa ﬂ uoro- iso -propyl)phos- phate (HFiP) in graphite//LNMO batteries (charged to 5.0 V vs. Li/Li þ ) and found that HFiP could form a protective interphase layer not only on the cathode but also on anode surface, which hindered the direct contact between the electrolyte and the active materials. As a result, the capacity retention after 200 cycles in the HFiP-containing electrolyte (88%) was signi ﬁ cantly higher than the baseline electrolyte (58%). Huang et al. [ 98 ] developed 4-(tri ﬂ uoromethyl)-benzonitrile (4-TB) as an additive for high-voltage LNMO battery system, which improved the capacity reten- tion to 91% after 300 cycles (vs. 75% for baseline). Moreover, 4-TB as an electrolyte additive can also improve the rate capability and cycling performance of 5 V high-voltage LNMO [ 99 ]. The performance improvement could be explained by the preferential oxidation of 4-TB (prior to solvents), forming a CEI  ﬁ lm on the cathode surface to sup- press the oxidative decomposition of the electrolyte and the dissolution of Mn and Ni. Zheng et al. [ 100 ] used tris(penta ﬂ uorophenyl)borane (TPFPB) as an anion receptor could effectively improve the stability of the electrode/electrolyte interface. As shown in  Fig. 5 a and b, due to the strong coordination ability and high oxygen solubility of TFPPB, the reactivity of highly active oxygen species released from the structural lattice was effectively depressed, and the deposition of by-products on the surface of the cathode was reduced. The use of TPFPB slowed down the accumulation of side reaction products and stabilized the interface between the cathode and the electrolyte during the cycling, thereby signi ﬁ cantly improving the cycling performance ( Fig. 5 c). In addition, for Li//LiFePO 4  battery, TPFPB could not only be able to increase the dissociation of LiPF 6  in electrolyte but also help LiF formed at the surface of electrode materials dissolved back into electrolyte. This meant that the blocked Li þ   insertion and extraction channels could be reopened and the electrochemical activity could be restored [ 101 ].  

In addition to  ﬂ uorinated organic additives, several  ﬂ uorinated lithium salts could also function well as cathode CEI-forming additives. Zhao et al. [ 102 ] demonstrated the effectiveness of using lithium di ﬂ uorophosphate (LiPO 2 F 2 ) as an electrolyte additive for the NCM523 cathode system. The results showed that 1 wt% LiPO 2 F 2  could signi ﬁ - cantly improve the initial coulombic ef ﬁ ciency, cycling stability both at RT and 55    C, and rate performance of Li//NCM523 batteries. Studies showed that the use of LiPO 2 F 2  not only reduced the surface  ﬁ lm impedance of the electrode, but also greatly suppressed the increase of charge transfer impedance during battery operation. As shown in  Fig. S3 , a part of LiPO 2 F 2  would be converted to more stable LiF and Li 3 PO 4 species during cycling, which contributed to forming a stable and dense interface  ﬁ lm. This is because LiF [ 33 ] is a robust electrical insulator (  10  31   S cm  1 ) which can prevent electrons from crossing the CEI layer, it can effectively passivate the electrode interface and inhibit the decomposition of electrolyte. Song et al. [ 103 ] found that traditional additives, such as FEC, accelerated the decomposition of LiPF 6  in the electrolyte. The addition of LiPO 2 F 2  not only stabilized LiPF 6 , but also inhibited the side reactions and self-discharge of the battery under high temperature storage. Ma et al. [ 104 ] studied the combination of LiPO 2 F 2 þ  FEC, LiPO 2 F 2  þ  VC and LiPO 2 F 2  þ  DiFEC for NCM111 lithium-ion batteries. The combination of additives could effectively improve the coulombic ef ﬁ ciency of the battery, reduce side reactions and suppress the impedance-rise, prolonging the cycle life as a result. With these combined additives, the  ﬂ uorine content increased while the oxygen content decreased in the CEI layer. The modi ﬁ ed CEI layer components are considered to be responsible for the improved electrochemical per- formance of Li-ion batteries when LiPO 2 F 2  was combined with other additives.  

Hu et al. [ 105 ] investigated the effect of lithium di ﬂ uoro (oxalate) borate (LiDFOB) for high-voltage LiCoPO 4  batteries, showing higher reversible charge/discharge capacity and better cycling stability. LiDFOB was found to be oxidized and decomposed at 4.35 V to form a stable interphase  ﬁ lm and passivate the surface of the cathode. Another study found that LiDFOB could not only improve the cycling stability, but also reduce the self-discharge of the Ni-rich LiNi 0.83 Mn 0.05 Co 0.12 O 2  battery during storage at 4.4 V at high temperature and room temperature [ 106 ]. The mechanism was that the interface  ﬁ lm enriched with inorganic boron,  ﬂ uorine and carbonate as derived from LiDFOB was bene ﬁ cial for interface protection and Li þ   migration. Zhu et al. [ 107 ] found that the CEI  ﬁ lm formed in the presence of LiDFOB at the lithium-rich (Li 1.2 Ni 0.15 Mn 0.55 Co 0.1 O 2 ) cathode surface could suppress the oxidative decomposition of the electrolyte and reduce the dissolution of Ni, Co, and Mn ions. Li et al. [ 108 ] proposed a new series of 2- ﬂ uoro-2-alkylmalonate borate additives. The results showed that this kind of additives could form a dense SEI  ﬁ lm at the interface of both graphite and LNMO elec- trode, and improve the initial coulombic ef ﬁ ciency and cycling stability of LNMO-based lithium-ion battery. The electrochemical  ﬂ oating-charge test showed that when the voltage was higher than 5.0 V, the oxidation current for the electrolyte containing borate additives was much lower, indicating a good passivation effect. Han et al. [ 55 ] used lithium  ﬂ uo- romalonate borate (LiFMDFB) to improve the structural stability of lithium-rich (Li 1.17 Ni 0.17 Mn 0.5 Co 0.17 O 2 ) cathode. This borate additive could produce a stable CEI  ﬁ lm on lithium-rich cathode, suppressing the irreversible layered to spinel phase transformation and delaying the voltage decay of lithium-rich cathode.  

# 4. Safety-enhancing additives  

In order to improve the safety performance of batteries, the most effective and economical method is to manipulate the electrode/elec- trolyte interfacial stability by using functional electrolyte additives [ 109 111 ]. The safety additives we often mention include two main – categories:  ﬂ ame retardant additives and anti-overcharge additives. The main mechanism of the  ﬂ ame retardant additive is the free radical trapping mechanism [ 10 ], that is, when the  ﬂ ame retardant is heated, it

The free radicals can capture the hydrogen free radicals or oxyhydrogen free radicals in the gas phase, block the chain reaction, and make the organic electrolyte dif ﬁ cult to be burnt. The boiling point of  ﬂ ame-retardant additives and the con- tent of  ﬂ ame retardant elements are important indicators of the  ﬂ ame retardant properties. So far, most  ﬂ ame retardant additives are com- pounds rich in one or more phosphorus, nitrogen and  ﬂ uorine elements. Previously, organic phosphorus-based  ﬂ ame retardant additives have been widely recognized and applied because of their good  ﬂ ame retarding effect, but their higher viscosity lowers down the conductivity of the electrolyte [ 112 , 113 ] and deteriorates battery performance. Many traditional  ﬂ ame retardants, such as phosphate esters [ 109 ], when used in large amounts, will result in some worse effects on the electrochemical performance of the battery. However, in recent years, the negative effects of modi ﬁ ed  ﬂ ame retardant additives ( ﬂ uoro-modi ﬁ ed) on performance have been greatly reduced. Due to its weak polarity, the introduction of ﬂ uorine in phosphorus additives could reduce the viscosity of the elec- trolyte (in comparison with their un ﬂ uorinated counterparts), improve the conductivity of the electrolyte [ 114 ], help to generate a stable SEI ﬁ lm [ 115 ], and improve the electrochemical performance of the battery. In addition, the  ﬂ uorinated additive will generate  ﬂ uorine free radicals when it is heated a certain temperature [ 116 ]. Then they will combine with hydrogen radicals generated by the decomposition of gaseous car- bonate solvents to inhibit the combustion chain reaction of the electro- lyte and improve the safety performance of the battery [ 117 ]. The anti-overcharge additive is stable within the normal operating voltage range of the battery. However, under overcharge conditions, it could be decomposed irreversibly and consumes charge [ 118 ], which plays an overload protection role and should not affect the normal working state of the battery.

# 4.2. High-temperature additives  

In addition to phosphazene additives, other  ﬂ uorine-containing compounds also have good high temperature stability, which can improve the safety performance of lithium ion batteries. SEI is consisted of the decomposition product of electrolyte on the electrode surface. It has ion conductivity and is an important component to maintain the normal operation of the battery. Wang et al. [ 131 ] found that the main component of the SEI  ﬁ lm formed on the surface of lithium metal in the electrolyte containing (tetra ﬂ uoro-1-(2,2,2-tri ﬂ uoroethoxy)ethane was LiF with good thermal stability, which was bene ﬁ cial for the stable operation of the electrode at high temperatures. On the contrary, the SEI produced by lithium metal in the traditional carbonate electrolyte was thicker, and the outer layer contained a lot of thermally unstable or- ganics, which was unfavorable for the high temperature operation of the batteries. Jung et al. [ 132 ] found that 3- ﬂ uoro-1,3-propane sultone (FPS) was able to improve the interface stability of both anode and cathode. The SEI derived from FPS exhibited higher onset temperature (129    C) than the base (120    C). Thus, the thermal stability of the SEI derived from FPS was supposed to be higher than that from baseline. In addition, it could also improve the high-temperature cycling stability of the Ni-rich cathode and 5 V-class lithium ion batteries. Chen et al. [ 76 ] synthe- sized allyl tris(2,2,2-tri ﬂ uoroethyl) carbonate (ATFEC) as a bi-functional additive of  ﬂ ame retardant and interphase layer formation in electrolytes for lithium ion batteries. As shown in  Fig. S4 , the results of DSC and SET showed that ATFEC could improve the thermal stability of the baseline electrolyte and showed certain  ﬂ ame-retardant properties. Adding 1 vol % ATFEC in 1 M LiPF 6 /propylene carbonate (PC) could effectively enhance the thermal stability of the electrolyte and suppress the co-intercalation of PC into the graphitic anode. Lu et al. [ 133 ] found that bis(2,2,2-tri ﬂ uoroethyl) carbonate (TFEC) could reduce the self-extinguishing time of the electrolyte. Nail penetration experiments showed that the addition of TFEC could signi ﬁ cantly reduce the tem- perature of the battery under short-circuit condition. The thermal shock test at high temperature showed that the addition of TFEC could signif- icantly improve the battery's endurance time. Similarly, propyl-2,2, 2-tri ﬂ uoroethyl carbonate (PTFEC) also showed good high temperature performance as an additive [ 134 ]. Besides, Zhang et al. [ 135 ] found that tris (2,2,2-tri ﬂ uoroethyl) phosphite (TTFP) could combine PF 5  decom- posed from LiPF 6 , effectively inhibiting the reaction activity and side reactions of PF 5 , and improving the stability of LiPF 6 . Furthermore, the safety performance of the battery in high temperature environment was improved.  

# 5. Summary and outlook  

Input of bipolar characteristics of  ﬂ uorine element in the additive molecule has been proved to be a good strategy to improve  ﬁ lm-forming additives. Fluorination can increase the ring-opening and reduction reactivity of some cyclic and short-chain additives, and improve the effectiveness of  ﬁ lm formation. Fluorine-addition can further improve the compactness and  ﬂ exibility of the SEI  ﬁ lm, which is compatible with the volume change of the anode. Fluorocarbon at es,  ﬂ uoronitriles and ﬂ uoroborates are excellent cathode  ﬁ lm-forming additives and can sta- bilize the catalytic sites of the cathode surface. As an auxiliary additive, per ﬂ uorinated chains and anionic cyclic compounds play a role of modifying the interface membrane. In addition, the  ﬁ lm-forming addi- tives that improve the electrochemical performance of lithium ion bat- teries should be helpful to  ﬁ lm-forming process. The fast and uniform ﬁ lm formation process should be bene ﬁ cial for the formation of a dense and stable interfacial  ﬁ lm. The high lithium mobility further stabilizes the structure of the interfacial  ﬁ lm. This means molecular components with fast kinetics and high reactivity are expected to become excellent ﬁ lm-forming additives. Therefore,  ﬂ uorinated additives contain highly reactive groups and transform into uniform and thickness-controllable ﬁ lm.  

Safety is the top concern of lithium-ion batteries using non-aqueous liquid organic electrolytes, which still limits their application for large- scale energy storage. To date, most effective  ﬂ ame retardants are  ﬂ uo- rinated phosphazene additives. In addition, some  ﬂ uoroesters also have good  ﬂ ame retardancy and high temperature stability, which can improve the safety performance of lithium ion batteries. However, the in ﬂ uence of the position and the number of  ﬂ uorine substitution on the performance of additives still needs further investigation. Therefore, the choice of  ﬂ ame retardant elements and structure is particularly important for safe additives. Effective  ﬂ ame retardant elements can inhibit the progress of combustion, and ef ﬁ cient mechanism requires reasonable structural characteristics. Some future additives containing  ﬂ ame- retarding elements and active functional groups are expected to achieve better safety performance.  

Clarifying the working mechanism of  ﬂ uorinated additives will readily contribute to the development of functional electrolytes with improved effectiveness. But we must also realize that the working mechanism of the  ﬂ uorinated additives is still very vague due to the complexity and stability of SEI layer and battery reaction. The problem mainly remains in the relationship between composition, structure and evolution of the SEI layer and its electrochemical performance. At pre- sent, the research on the mechanism of  ﬁ lm-forming additives mainly focuses on FEC and the more effective research methods are mainly ex- situ liquid NMR and solid state NMR. However, in the battery system, the SEI  ﬁ lm has been in the process of dynamic evolution. Therefore, there is an urgent need to develop some operando and reliable charac- terization techniques to understand SEI layer, so that we can design and develop better functional electrolytes. In addition, theoretical calcula- tions can also assist in proposing the correct reaction pathways, on the basis of the experimental evidence.
