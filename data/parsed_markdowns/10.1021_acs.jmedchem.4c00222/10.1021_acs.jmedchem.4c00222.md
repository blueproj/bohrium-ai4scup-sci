ABSTRACT:  Anemoside B4 (AB4), a triterpenoidal saponin from  Pulsatilla chinensis , shows significant anti-inflammatory activity, and may be used for treating inflammatory bowel disease (IBD). Nevertheless, its application is limited due to its high molecular weight and pronounced water solubility. To discover new effective agents for treating IBD, we synthesized 28 AB4 derivatives and evaluated their cytotoxic and anti-inflammatory activities  in vitro . Among them, A3 − 6 exhibited significantly superior anti-inflammatory activity compared to AB4. It showed a significant improvement in the symptoms of DSS-induced colitis in mice, with a notably lower oral effective dose compared to AB4. Furthermore, we discovered that A3 − 6 bound with pyruvate carboxylase (PC), then inhibited PC activity, reprogramming macrophage function, and alleviated colitis. These findings indicate that A3 − 6 is a promising therapeutic candidate for colitis, and PC may be a potential new target for treating colitis.

# 1. INTRODUCTION  

Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) is a chronic idiopathic inflammatory disease, including ulcerative colitis (UC) and Crohn’s disease (CD). The classic hallmarks of IBD include abdominal pain, watery diarrhea, bloody stools, bowel obstruction, and weight loss. These symptoms are often sporadic and can last intermittently for many years.   The pathogenesis of IBD has not been completely elucidated. It is currently accepted that IBD is associated with internal (genetic) and external s usc ep tibi li ties, alterations of intestinal microbiota, and dysregulated immune responses.   Amino- salicylates, corticosteroids, antibiotics, and immuno suppress ive drugs are the primary agents used in the clinical treatment of IBD.   These drugs for IBD can relieve symptoms, minimize progression and complications of disease, and avoid further hospitalization and surgery.   Unfortunately, the side effects of anti-inflammatory drugs and immuno modulators cannot be avoided, significantly impacting the patient’s quality of life. Hence, there is an urgent need for new drugs and novel targets in the clinical management of IBD.  

Increasing studies have recently focused on NOD-, LRR- and pyrin domain-containing protein 3 (NLRP3) inflamma- some, a critical component of innate immune system. Activated NLRP3 inflammation leads to the activation of caspase-1 and the subsequent secretion of IL-1 β  and IL-18. ,  

The dysfunction of NLRP3 inflammasome has been reported to be involved in the pathogenesis of several human diseases, including IBD.   Additionally, there are many natural products that treated IBD by regulating the NLRP3 inflammasome  

A complex inflammatory response is involved in the pathogenesis of IBD. Nuclear factor kappa B (NF- κ B) is a crucial signaling molecule that acts as a transcription factor, binding to the promoter regions of inflammatory factors to regulate their expressions. In the active NF- κ B signal pathway, the first protein to be activated is I κ B kinase (IKK), which controls the serine phosphorylation of the I κ B subunit in the NF- κ B I κ B complex. Pulsatilla chinensis  (Bunge) Regel, a herbal medicine, has been widely used for the treatment of IBD-like syndromes in traditional Chinese medicine for thousands of years. , Anemoside B4 (AB4) is an active compound from  P. chinensis , with a content of up to 10.7%.   Our previous study found that AB4 had significant anti-inflammatory and immuno modulator y activities, which were comparable to those of dexamethasone. Intraperitoneal injection of AB4 alleviated cisplatin-induced oxidative damage, improved LPS-induced inflammation, and lowered DNFB-induced delayed hypersensitivity.   Addition- ally, AB4 showed significant improvement in the TNBS- induced colitis by inhibiting the NF- κ B signaling pathway through deactivation of S100A9. AB4 specifically bound to the His879 site of PC and altered the protein spatial conformation, thereby inhibiting the enzymatic activity of PC, causing metabolic reprogramming, and eliminating colonic epithelial inflamma- tion.

However, the treatment of IBD with multiple injections of AB4 presents safety concerns and poses challenges for patient compliance. AB4, a triterpenoidal saponin with five sugar units in its structure, has a molecular weight of 1220 and high water solubility, which leads to its extremely low oral bioavailability and short elimination half-life. ,   Therefore, oral admin- istration of AB4 for IBD treatment requires higher doses, resulting in high production costs.   To address these limitations, the modification of AB4 derivatives by removing part sugar units was proposed.  

In this study, we aimed to enhance the anti-inflammatory activity and oral suitability of AB4 by preparing derivatives through structural modifications. By removing certain sugar units, the resulting AB4 derivative exhibits reduced molecular weight and improved water solubility. This modification facilitates easier penetration of the cell membrane and mitochondrial inner membrane, enhancing its binding to PC (target proteins) located in the mitochondrial matrix. Additionally, the improved physicochemical properties of the AB4 derivatives enable better absorption and a longer elimination half-life, potentially leading to a decrease in the required oral dose for treating IBD.   In order to improve AB4 in terms of anti-inflammatory activity and its suitability as an oral dosage form, a series of AB4 derivatives were prepared through structural modifications in the present study. The toxicity of these derivatives in THP-1 cells was measured using CCK-8 kits. Then, the inhibitory activity of AB4 derivatives on PC, which showed no significant cytotoxicity, was evaluated using the LPS-induced THP-1 cell model. Among these derivatives, A3 − 6 was found to possess the highest anti- inflammatory potency. Furthermore, the anti-inflammatory mechanism of A3 − 6 on PC and downregulated signaling pathway were investigated  in vitro  and  in vivo .

2.5. Anti-Inflammatory Effects of AB4 Derivatives  In Vitro .  The transcription factor nuclear factor- κ B (NF- κ B) plays a crucial role in regulating adaptive responses to pathogens and stress. It can be activated by a variety of pathogenic stimuli, including bacterial products (i.e., LPS) and viral proteins, cytokines, and growth factors.   Activation of NF- κ B is mediated by the phosphorylation and degradation of I κ B α , an inhibitor of NF- κ B activation.   Therefore, regulating the interaction between I κ B α  and p65 is a critical step in controlling NF- κ B signaling activity.   To explore the anti- inflammatory activity of AB4 derivatives, we investigated the effect of these seven compounds (A3 − 6, A3 − 7, A3 − 10, A3 − 16, A3 − 24, A3 − 26, and A3 − 28) on the activation of the NF- κ B signaling pathway since it has been documented to be a key signaling pathway of inflammatory diseases. THP-1 cells were incubated with indicated concentrations of the derivatives or positive control for 1 h and then treated with LPS (1  μ g/mL) for 6 h, then p-I κ B α  was measured by Western blotting analysis. The results of the Western blotting analysis showed that A3 − 6 and A3 − 24 were more effective in inhibiting the protein level of p-I κ B α  and exhibited better anti-inflammatory activity than AB4 ( Figure 3 ).  

Exposure of cells to a variety of extracellular stimuli leads to the rapid phosphorylation, ubiquitination, and ultimately proteolytic degradation of I κ B, which frees NF- κ B to translocate to the nucleus where it regulates gene tran- scription.   To further confirm anti-inflammatory activity of A3 − 6, we investigated the activation of the NF- κ B signaling pathway by Western blotting. The results showed that A3 − 6 could inhibit the increased p-p65 and p-I κ B α  induced by LPS on a concentration-dependent manner. Notably, at a concentration of 1  μ M, A3 − 6 showed superior anti- inflammatory activity compared to A3 − 3 or AB4 ( Figure 4 A,B). Based on the ability of A3 − 6 to significantly inhibit the NF- κ B signaling pathway, we speculated that it might be able to inhibit NLRP3 inflammasome. In this work, an NLRP3 inflammasome activation model was established in THP-1 cells using LPS and ATP as described previously.   The stimulation of LPS and ATP resulted in the production of NLRP3, Pro-IL- l β , P20, and IL-1 β  in THP-1 cells. It was interesting to find that pretreatment with compound A3 − 6 rather than AB4 significantly reduced NLRP3, Pro-IL-l β , P20, and IL-1 β protein levels when compared with the LPS-induced group ( Figure 4 C − E). This suggested that compound A3 − 6 was more effective than AB4 in inhibiting the cleavage and secretion of IL-1 β , and to a certain extent, reduce the inflammatory response by inhibiting the activation of NLRP3 inflammasome.

2.10. The Anti-Inflammation Effect of A3 − 6 Was Dependent on PC  In Vitro .  The above results demonstrated that A3 − 6 could specifically bind to PC, but it was still unclear how A3 − 6 modulated PC. To investigate whether the anti- inflammatory activity of A3 − 6 is related to PC  in vitro , levels of PC were first measured in LPS-induced THP-1 macrophages using Western blotting analysis. The result demonstrated that the levels of PC remained unchanged in THP-1 cells at different concentrations of A3 − 6 (1 or 10  μ M) with the presence of LPS ( Figure 8 A,B), which indicated that A3 − 6 did not affect PC at the protein level. PC is a rate-limiting enzyme that regulates the conversion of pyruvate to oxaloacetate. To investigate whether A3 − 6 influences the enzymatic activity of PC, we measured the PC activity using commercial kits according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The results demonstrated that the activity of PC increased 6 h after THP-1 cells were stimulated by LPS. Meanwhile, A3 − 6 (10 μ M) significantly decreased PC activity induced by LPS ( Figure 8 C).

4.2. Biology.  4.2.1. Cell Culture.  THP-1 cells were cultured in RPMI-1640 containing 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) at 37  ° C in a humidified atmosphere containing 5% CO 2 . THP-1-derived macro- phages were induced by phorbol-12-myristate-13-acetate (PMA, 100 ng/mL) for 12 h.  

4.2.2. Cell Viability.  Cell viability was evaluated by the CCK-8 assay according to our previously described method.   In brief, THP- 1-derived macrophages were seeded into 96-well plates (1  ×  10 5 / well) overnight and treated for 24 h with various concentrations from 12.5 to 50  μ M of the AA3 derivatives. Then, 10  μ L of CCK-8 was added to the wells and incubated at 37  ° C for another 2 h. The resulting solution was assayed at 450 nm using a microplate reader.  

4.2.3. Western Blot Analysis.  THP-1 macrophages were seeded into a 6-well plate with 1  ×  10 6   cells per well and maintained at 37  ° C in 5% CO 2  for about 12 h. The cells were exposed to AA3 derivatives (1 or 10  μ M) for 1 h, then incubated with LPS (1  μ g/mL) for 2 h. The cells were lysed with ice-cold RIPA buffer containing proteinase and phosphatase inhibitors (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO). The lysate was centrifuged at 12,000 rpm for 10 min at 4  ° C and the supernatant was harvested. Then, the protein concentration was determined by bicinchoninic acid (BCA) protein assay kit (Beyotime, Nanjing, China). Proteins were separated via sodium dodecyl sulfate polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) and transferred to poly(vinylidene fluoride) (PVDF) membranes. The PVDF mem- branes were blocked with 5% nonfat dry milk for 1 h at room temperature, and incubated with appropriate primary antibodies [including: anti-I κ B α , anti-p-I κ B α , anti-p-p65, anti-p65, and anti-actin overnight at 4  ° C. Then, the membranes were washed in TBST and incubated with HRP-linked secondary antibody (Beyotime). The protein bands were exposed to ECL reagent and detected with the ChemiDoc XRS Imager (Bio-Rad), following quantification by ImageJ software.  

4.2.4. ELISA.  THP-1 macrophages were seeded into a 6-well plate with 5  ×  10 5   cells per well and maintained at 37  ° C in 5% CO 2  for about 12 h. The cells were pretreated with compounds (0.1, 1, or 10 μ M) for 1 h, then incubated with LPS (1  μ g/mL) for 24 h. The cell culture was collected, and the cytokine production was determined using human TNF- α  and IL-6 ELISA kits. Colonic tissues were homogenized mechanically in lysis buffer. The homogenate was centrifuged at 12,000 rpm for 10 min. Supernatants from homogenized tissues were collected, and the cytokine production was determined using mouse IL-6 ELISA kits according to the manufacturer’s instructions.  

4.2.5. Molecular Docking Analysis.  Molecular docking analysis was performed and slightly modified according to a method previously described.   In brief, the three-dimensional (3D) structure of A3 − 6 was constructed using ChemBio 3D Ultra software (Chemical Structure Drawing Standard; Cambridge Corporation, 2010) and energetically minimized by using MMFF94 with 5000 iterations and a minimum RMS gradient 0.05. The PC protein (PDB ID: 5LS6) was prepared by the protein preparation wizard of Schro dinger Maestro 2019. The generated binding site was the active pocket of A3 − 6, including several key amino acid residues. Types of interactions of the docked protein with ligand were analyzed at the end of docking. The image files were generated using PyMol 1.1.  

4.2.6. Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR) Analysis.  The biomolecular interaction between A3 − 6 and PC was analyzed by SPR using the Biacore 8K system (GE Healthcare, Uppsala, Sweden) at 25  ° C. Briefly, CM5 sensor chip was activated by using sulpho- NHS/EDC chemistry and subsequently immobilized with the recombinant human PC protein at a concentration of 10  μ g/mL in sodium acetate (pH 4.0) and then blocked with ethanolamine for 7 min. Then, A3 − 6 (0.975 to 15.6  μ M) was added into the running buffer to measure the interaction between A3 − 6 and PC. Kinetic parameters were computed with Biacore evaluation software.  

4.2.7. Cellular Thermal Shift assay (CETSA).  HepG2 cells were incubated with DMSO or A3 − 6 (20  μ M) for 1 h, the cell was collected and divided into 100  μ L aliquot in tubes. Then, the cells were then subjected to gradient heating in a PCR instrument for 4 min. This was followed by repeated freeze − thaw cycles to obtain lysate for further Western blotting analysis.  

4.2.8. Drug Affinity-Responsive Target Stability (DARTS) Assay. HepG2 cells were lysed and diluted with 1  ×  Tris buffer. The lysate was divided into five aliquots (50  μ L per aliquot). One aliquot was used as a control (PBS) group, while the other aliquots were incubated with gradient concentrations of AB4 (0, 1, 10, 100  μ M) at room temperature for 1 h. 2  μ L of diluted Pronase solutions (2  μ g/ mL) was added, respectively, to the aliquots except the control group at room temperature for a further 15 min. Then, a loading buffer was added to cease the reactions. Finally, DARTS samples were tested by Western blotting with the specific antibody of PC.  

4.2.9. PC Enzymatic Activity and PA Metabolite Assay.  PC enzymatic activity was measured by a coupled spec tro photometric assay, coupling OAA production from pyruvate by PC to NADH oxidation by malate dehydrogenase using PC activity kit (CAK1262, Cohesion, U.K.) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Pyruvate assay kits were from Solarbio (BC2200, Beijing. China). All of these metabolites were measured according to the manufacturer’s instructions.  

4.2.10. Animals.  The ICR mice weighing 20 to 25 g were obtained from the Experimental Animal Center of Soochow University. All mice were housed in cages at a temperature of 22  ±  2  ° C in a 12 h light/dark cycle, and fed with standard food and water  ad libitum . All animal experiments were conducted in accordance with the procedure approved by the Ethical Review Committee for Laboratory Animal Welfare of Soochow University (Ethical No. 202112A0319).  

4.2.11. Microsomal Stability and Plasma Stability.  Microsomal stability was determined by using 10  μ M A3 − 6 to incubate with mouse microsomes (0.5 mg/mL) for 5 min at 37  ° C in phosphate buffer (100 mM, pH = 7.4), and the reaction was then initiated by 1 mM NADPH. Subsequently, the cold acetonitrile was used to precipitate the protein. Finally, the samples were centrifuged and further analyzed by liquid chromatography and tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS).   Plasma stability was determined as following steps: 55   (1) A 10 mM DMSO stock of A3 − 6 was first prepared; (2) the 10 mM stock was then diluted to 1  μ M by mouse plasma; (3) the plasma sample was divided into 50  μ L each and transferred into new tubes; (4) the plasma samples were incubated in a water bath at 37  ° C; (5) the reaction was stopped using 250  μ L acetonitrile at 10, 30, 60, 120, 240, and 480 min, respectively; and (6) the concentrations of A3 − 6 were measured by LC-MS/MS.  

4.2.12. Comparative Study on the P harm a co kinetics of A3 − 6 and AB4.  This study was conducted in ICR mice and Sprague − Dawley rats to investigate the pharmacokinetic profiles of A3 − 6. The mice and rats supplied by Sibeifu Biotechnology Co., Ltd. (Beijing, China) were raised at the Animal Experimental Center of Henan University of Traditional Chinese Medicine (Ethical No. SYXK 2020- 0004). A3 − 6 and AB4 were dissolved or suspended in 10% SDS solution, and then were administered to mice (20 − 22 g) at a dose of 15 mg/kg by gavage. Approximately 600 blood samples were collected at 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1, 1.5, 2, 4, 6, 8, and 12 h after administration. Finally, the contents of A3 − 6 and AB4 in the blood or colon were analyzed by LC-MS/MS (TSQ Quantis & Altis).   A3 − 6 encapsulated with hydroxypropyl- β -cyclodextrin was administered to rats, and the contents of A3 − 6 in plasma samples were tested according to those in mice experiments.  

4.2.13. Animal Model of Colitis.  Male ICR mice were randomly divided in the eight groups ( n  = 10 in each group): normal group, DSS group, A3 − 6 (15 mg/kg), A3 − 6 (30 mg/kg) group, A3 − 6 (60 mg/kg), A3 − 6 (60 mg/kg) + DADA (50 mg/kg), AB4 (100 mg/kg), and AA3 (60 mg/kg). Compound A3 − 6 or AA3 were encapsulated with hydroxypropyl- β -cyclodextrin to provide desired concentrations. Mice were fed with 5% DSS in the drinking water and treated by intragastric administration of the respective compounds for 8 days. The normal group only received pure water. During the experiment, mice were weighed every day and DAI was recorded for 7 days after the induction of colitis.  

4.2.14. Histological Analysis.  The colonic tissues were fixed in 4% para formaldehyde solution, embedded in paraffin, and then cut into 5- μ m-thick sections. The sections were stained with hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining. Light microscopy and a panoramic viewer camera system (CX31, Olympus Optical Co., Ltd., Tokyo, Japan) were used to examine, scan, and analyze the histopathology of colon.  

4.2.15. Statistical Analysis.  Statistical analysis was performed using GraphPad Prism 8. Results were expressed as mean  ±  standard deviation (SD). Statistical analysis of the results was conducted by using Student’s  t  test or one-way ANOVA. Two-way ANOVA followed by Dunnett’s multiple comparisons test was used to analyze the body weight change and DAI scores in the animal experiments.  P - values less than 0.05 ( P  < 0.05) were considered statistically significant. All experiments were repeated at least three times.
