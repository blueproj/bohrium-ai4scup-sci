# 1. Introduction  

The pea aphid, Acyrthosiphon pisum (Hemiptera: Aphididae) (Harris), is a soft-bodied insect that feeds by sucking the phloem of plants on several legume species (Fabaceae). It can cause crop damage directly by feeding and indirectly by transmission of pathogenic plant viruses and favoring the development of fungi on the excreted honeydew ( Dixon, 1998; The International Aphid Genomics Consortium, 2010 ).  

The red ﬂour beetle, Tribolium castaneum (Coleoptera: Tene- brionidae) (Herbst), is one of the most important cosmopolitan pest insects, causing damage in stored products directly by feeding ( Rees, 2004 ) and indirectly by the secretion of quinones that are responsible of human allergies ( Hodges et al., 1996 ).  

The use of chemicals is still the most important method to con- trol insect pests ( Grifﬁths et al., 1989; Zettler and Arthur, 2000 ), but the intensive use of conventional insecticides has produced nega- tive effects on non-target organisms, the environment and human health, and arise the development of resistant populations ( Zettler, 1991; Unal and Jepson, 1991; Ware et al., 2003; Andrews et al., 2004; Cao et al., 2008 ). These facts added to restrictions in use of insecticides ( http://ec.europa.eu/food/plant/index en.htm ), sup- port the importance of ﬁnding new substances able to be used in pest control in an environment-friendly way.  

Plant derivatives have traditionally been used as insecticides and new insecticide research is focusing on plant origin substances in order to avoid some negative aspects associated with conven- tional insecticides ( Isman, 2006; Koul et al., 2008 ). Additionally, these compounds interfere in several cell functions ( Enan, 2005; Bakkali et al., 2008; Abdelgaleil et al., 2009; López and Pascual- Villalobos, 2010 ), which is described as a way to minimize the development of resistant populations ( Yang et al., 2009; Machial et al., 2010 ). Some of them were successfully assessed as aphid and beetle toxicants and/or repellents ( Petterson et al., 1994; Rozman et al., 2007; Zapata and Smagghe, 2010 ), but differences in com- position depending on the used plant parts, harvest time and population origin are a problem to be solved before practical appli- cation ( Oliveira et al., 2005; Mu˜ noz-Bartomeu et al., 2007 ).  

As a part of an ongoing project aimed to re-use glycerol coming as a by-product from the biodiesel industry ( www.biodiesel.org ), several allyl esters were synthesized and their insecticidal prop- erties on three Lepidopteran species were successfully assessed ( Escribà et al., 2009, 2011; Giner et al., 2012a ) due to the fact that the ester chemical group is present in several compounds with insec- ticidal properties ( Petterson et al., 1994; Ojimelukwe and Adler, 1999; Daferera et al., 2000; Skaltsa et al., 2003; Puterka et al., 2003; Rajendran and Muralidhoran, 2005; Bakkali et al., 2008 ). In addition, Giner et al. (2012b) investigated with the use of differ- ent insect cell lines the mechanism of allyl ester action and could report that the insect cell membrane is a major target for these chemistries. Interestingly, these ﬁndings may open new opportu- nities to employ these compounds in the control of important pest insects also other than Lepidoptera.  

This paper describes for the ﬁrst time the insecticidal and repellent properties of allyl octanoate, allyl heptanoate, allyl hex- anoate, allyl cinnamate and allyl 2-furoate against the pea aphid A. pisum and the red ﬂour beetle T. castaneum as these represent two important insect pest orders of Hemiptera and Coleoptera, each with a speciﬁc feeding behavior using piercing–sucking and biting–chewing mouthparts, respectively. In the insect bioassays, the compounds were mixed in the diet of the insects and their insec- ticidal and repellent action measured. In addition, we dosed the insects by topical application with the compounds. In continuation, in the binary choice experiments, we determined the potency of allyl cinnamate for repellency and compared with the commercial repellent DEET ( Hou et al., 2004 ). Finally, a Y-tube olfactometer test was performed to investigate the repellent action of allyl cinnamate also via olfaction.  

# 2. Materials and methods  

# 2.1. Chemicals  

Allyl octanoate, allyl heptanoate, allyl hexanoate, allyl cinna- mate and allyl 2-furoate ( ≥ 97% purity), acetone of analytical grade and DEET ( N,N -diethyl- m -toluamide, 98%) were purchased from Sigma–Aldrich (Bornem, Belgium).  

# 2.2. Insects  

All insects were reared under standard conditions at the Depart- ment of Crop Protection of Ghent University (Belgium).  

A continuous colony of the A. pisum was maintained on young plants of Vicia faba L. (Fabales: Fabaceae) at 23–25   ◦ C and 65 ± 5% relative humidity (RH) under a 16:8 h light:dark photoperiod ( De Geyter et al., 2012 ). Less than 24-h old nymphs were used in inges- tion bioassays, while adults were used in contact bioassays.  

All stages of T. castaneum were reared on a 10:1 (w:w) mixture of wheat ﬂour and brewer’s yeast at standard conditions of 25–27   ◦ C and 65 ± 5% (RH) under a 16:8 h photoperiod and unsexed adults were used to carry out all the bioassays ( Vandenborre et al., 2011 ).  

# 2.3. Insect bioassays  

# 2.3.1. Toxicity by feeding  

Toxicity of allyl esters to A. pisum by feeding was assessed using the aphid feeding apparatus as described by Sadeghi et al. (2009a) . Ten newborn (<24 h old) ﬁrst-instar nymphs were transferred to each aphid feeding apparatus, and mortality and number of molts were daily recorded during three days. In all bioassays, insects were considered dead when no leg or antennal movements were observed upon gentle prodding with a brush. We performed 3 replicates each consisting of 10 insects, for each compound con- centration, and at least ﬁve different concentrations per each allyl ester were done. In the control series the diet was supplemented with an equal amount of carrier solvent (acetone), and the mortality was <20%.  

Probit analysis of mortality vs. concentration using POLO-Plus program v1.0 (LeOra software, Petaluma, CA) was carried out and lethal concentrations (LC 50 , LC 90 ) and their corresponding 95% con- ﬁdence intervals (95% CI) were calculated. LC’s were considered to be signiﬁcantly different when the 95% CI’s did not overlap.  

The mean numbers of molts of A. pisum at sublethal concen- tration (0.01 mg    mL − 1   for allyl cinnamate and 0.1 mg mL − 1   for the other allyl esters) were compared with the controls by one-way ANOVA followed by Dunnett’s test, P < 0.05) using the JMP 8.0.1 software (SAS Institute, Cary, NC).  

# 2.3.2. Toxicity by contact  

In the toxicity assays by contact, 0.1  L of allyl ester solution was directly applied on the thorax of an adult of A. pisum using a 10  L-syringe (Hamilton, Reno, NV) connected to an injection pump (Harvard apparatus model 11, Holliston, MA). The same methodology was used for T. castaneum adults, but then 0.5  L of allyl ester solution was applied. In the control series, acetone was applied and mortality was <20%.  

After treatment, the aphids were maintained in the aphid feeding apparatus containing non-treated diet and mortality was recorded at 2 and 4 h after the application. For T. castaneum ,    the treated adults were placed in a glass Petri dish ( Ø = 11 cm) and pro- vided with wheat ﬂour as food, and mortality was recorded at 2 h, 24 h and daily during a week.  

In all experiments with aphids and beetles, we performed 3 replicates each consisting of 10 insects, per allyl ester and concen- tration, and at least 5 different concentrations were assessed per active allyl ester.  

Probit analysis of mortality vs. concentration was done using POLO-Plus program v1.0 as abovementioned. LT 50  (median lethal time) for the lowest dose producing 100% mortality at the seventh day was calculated with the same software.  

# 2.3.3. Behavioral effect after ingestion or contact with allyl esters  

Two binary choice bioassays were carried out in order to inves- tigate whether allyl esters had an effect on A. pisum after feeding or after contact.  

The methodology to assess behavioral effects by ingestion consisted of two joined aphid feeding apparatuses, as described by De Geyter et al. (2012) . Five aphids were put in each part of the aphid feeding apparatus, one containing untreated diet (aphid diet + acetone) and the other containing treated diet (aphid diet + allyl ester solution). Percentages of repellence (PR) were scored at 1, 2, 4 and 24 h from the beginning of the assay using the following formula: PR = [(C − T)/(C + T)] × 100; where C = numbers of insects in the untreated arena and T = numbers of insects in the treated arena ( Zapata and Smagghe, 2010 ). Each experiment consisted of 6replicatesperallylesterandperconcentration(0.01,0.1and0.5 mg mL − 1 ), and the experiment was repeated 3 times.  

The methodology to assess behavioral effects in adult aphids by contact to allyl esters consisted of a glass Petri dish ( Ø = 5.0 cm) lined with a ﬁlter paper ( Ø = 4.5 cm) that was cut in two halves. One half ﬁlter paper was treated with 100  L of allyl ester solution and the other half with the same volume of acetone. Petri dishes were kept into an air ﬂume during 5 min for solvent evaporation, and then 10 aphid adults were released on the center of the Petri dish. The PR was calculated at 1 and 2 h from the beginning of the assay as abovementioned. One experiment consisted of 6 replicates per allyl ester and dose (10, 100 and 1000  g cm − 2 ), and the experiment was replicated 3 times.  

Repellency on beetle adults of T. castaneum was evaluated by a binary choice bioassay that was similar to the one used for A. pisum except we used here a 9 cm-diameter glass Petri dish as described before by Zapata and Smagghe (2010) . In brief, one half of a ﬁlter paper ( Ø = 8.0 cm) was treated with 0.5 mL    allyl ester solution dis- solved in acetone and the other half with the same volume of the solvent acetone alone. Ten unsexed adults were transferred into the center of each Petri dish after evaporation of solvent, and the PR was scored at 2, 4, 8 and 24 h from the beginning of the assay. One experiment consisted of six replicates per allyl ester and dose (1, 10, 100 and 1000  g cm − 2 ), and the experiment was repeated three times.  

In both contact bioassays, a positive control using the known insect repellent DEET ( Watson and Barson, 1996; Ditzen et al., 2008 ) at 10  g cm − 2   was performed.  

Mean values of PR ( n = 3) per treatment and concentration per each species were compared by one-way ANOVA, followed by Tukey HSD test ( P = 0.05), and the same was done per each treat- ment by time. Mean PR values of T. castaneum at 10  g cm − 2   were compared to DEET by one-way ANOVA followed by Dunnett’s test ( P = 0.05). JMP 8.0.1 software was used to carry out the statistical analyses.  

# 2.3.4. Olfactory response  

In order to evaluate the olfactory response of adults of T. cas- taneum when exposed to volatile allyl cinnamate, a glass Y-tube olfactometer was used essentially as described by De Boer and Dicke (2004) . In brief, the olfactometer consisted of a central tube ( L = 15 cm; Ø = 4 cm) connected to two arms ( L = 35 cm, Ø = 4 cm). After carbon ﬁltering and moisturing, the air was passed through two glass odor ﬂasks. The ﬁrst ﬂask contained the treatment with a ﬁlter paper (2 cm × 2 cm) containing 0.4 mL of pure allyl cinna- mate. In the second control ﬂask, distilled water was pipetted on the ﬁlter paper. PFA tubing was used to exclude sorption of allyl cinnamate. The bioassay was performed under standard laboratory conditions. To prevent possible positioning effects, the ﬂask con- taining allyl cinnamate was connected to the other arm after each repetition. Then adults of T. castaneum were placed individually at the entrance of the central tube and allowed to choose between the two arms.  

This experiment was repeated 4 times where each repeat consisted of 10 adults. The data were analyzed with a Student’s t -test ( P = 0.05).

In addition to the dose-dependent response, we    noted that aphid mortality by the active allyl esters was produced rapidly. Most of the lethal effects were obtained during the ﬁrst day. There were no differences ( P > 0.05) in the numbers of molts in treated aphids as compared to the controls ( Fig. 1 ), indicating that there was no evident retardation of aphid development after feeding of allyl esters.

# 3.3. Behavioral effect by ingestion or contact  

No sublethal effects were observed in aphids when fed on diet containing the allyl esters at 0.1 mg    mL − 1   (PR < 20%). Similarly, there were no repellent effects in aphids when exposed to sur- faces treated with allyl octanoate, allyl heptanoate, allyl hexanoate and allyl 2-furoate. Only with allyl cinnamate at 10  g cm − 2   the repellent effect reached a signiﬁcant level of 34 ± 12%; the effect did not increase with the increase in time of exposure ( P < 0.05). With the commercial repellent DEET at the same concentration of 10  g cm − 2 , a signiﬁcant PR of 50 10% ( P < 0.05) was measured.  

With beetle adults of T. castaneum , we scored signiﬁcant repel-      lency activities by the ﬁve allyl esters at 10  g cm − 2 already after 4 h from the beginning of the assay ( Fig. 2 ). The strongest repel- lent effect was scored with allyl cinnamate, demonstrating a PR of 80 ± 2%. By contrast, allyl hexanoate showed the lowest effect ( Fig. 2 ). In addition, it was of interest that the effect was rapid. As shown in Fig. 3 , maximum repellency was obtained already after 2 h with a concentration of 10  g cm − 2 , and this was after 8 h with the lowest concentration tested (0.1  g cm − 2 ) ( P = 0.003, d.f. = 3; F = 16.9). For the commercial repellent DEET, the data conﬁrmed its potency with a PR of 60 ± 10% at 4 h after the application of 10  g cm − 2   ( Fig. 2 ). Hence, the repellency by DEET at 10  g cm − 2 increased to 84 ± 2% at 24 h, being this PR not signiﬁcantly different from 4th h of exposition ( P < 0.05). PR produced by DEET was signif- icantly equal to the repellency effect produced by allyl cinnamate at 0.1, 1 and 10  g cm − 2   ( Fig. 3 ).  

# 3.4. Olfactory response  

With use of the Y-tube olfactometer, it was clear that allyl cinna- mate provoked a signiﬁcant ( P < 0.05) repellent action via olfaction toward T. castaneum as 72.5 ± 6.3% of the beetles were pushed in the control arm.  

# 4. Discussion  

To our knowledge, this is the ﬁrst report about insecticidal action of allyl esters against aphids and beetles, as exempliﬁed here with A. pisum and T. castaneum ,    respectively. This action includes insect mortality and sublethal effects on behavior, depending on insect, allyl ester and mode of application.  

Mortality was produced after topical application of allyl cinna- mate on A. pisum and after topical application of all allyl esters assessed on T. castaneum ,    however, this was at different doses. Here we    believe that differences in the integument thickness and structure of both insects could explain differences in action. The topical insecticidal action took place rapidly after the application in all cases, similarly as observed when assessing allyl esters on Lepidopteran species ( Escribà et al., 2009; Giner et al., 2012a ). It is suggested that this rapid effect is related with a direct action on the membrane of the insect cells ( Cohen and Quistad, 1998 ) which

In the experiment with T. castaneum beetles, we also observed a speciﬁc phenomenon that the ﬂour colored pinkish after contact with treated beetles. We expect that this results from a defensive reaction by the treated beetles after allyl ester application. Indeed, Alexander and Barton (1943) and Hodges et al. (1996) described the reaction of quinones from defensive substances segregated by T. castaneum with starch. This fact was also observed by Garcia et al. (2005) after insecticide application, indicating a perturbation on T. castaneum due to allyl ester application.  

High toxicity of allyl esters by ingestion was recorded on A. pisum . The LC 50  values after aphid feeding were in the same range as obtained by other authors when assessing protease inhibitors, saponins, kinins, lectins or pyrokinin analogs using the same methodology ( Sadeghi et al., 2009b; Carrillo et al., 2011; Smagghe et al., 2010a; De Geyter et al., 2012; Nachman et al., 2012 ). This fact gives relevance to the allyl ester action by ingestion, what is in agreement to the higher cytotoxicity as observed on insect midgut cells ( Giner et al., 2012b ).  

As no differences in molting were observed among treated and control aphids from the feeding bioassay, deterrency or an effect in aphid development was not suggested to be produced by allyl esters, as observed on Lepidopterans after allyl ester ingestion (unpublished data). A direct effect in the gut of the treated aphids would be suggested as target of allyl ester action, similarly as pro- duced by saponins ( De Geyter et al., 2012 ). This lack of deterrency was ascertained on behavior bioassays. A modiﬁcation on aphid behavior was not observed on A. pisum versus diet containing allyl esters or to surfaces topically treated with allyl esters. The signif- icant high values of PR scored by the commercial repellent DEET and the fact that PR did no increase along time, as observed by other authors when assessing aphid repellents ( Bruce et al., 2005; Lowery and Isman, 1993; Smagghe et al., 2010b ), validate the methodology used and reafﬁrmed the lack of deterrent effect produced by allyl esters on A. pisum. Lack of aphid repellent properties refuse the use of allyl esters in order to prevent virus transmission. Even though low amount of allyl cinnamate was needed to cause insect death by ingestion, and insect gut tissue is an interestingly target for pest control ( Hakim et al., 2010 ), do not avoid proving behavior so risk of viurs transmission.  

In contrast to aphids, a strong repellent effect was observed in beetles of T. castaneum ,    as similar to the one produced by some straight short-chain fatty acids in beetles ( Cohen et al., 1974 ). Allyl cinnamate was the most active allyl ester assessed, being active in the same dose-ranges as some essential oils ( Garcia et al., 2005; Zapata and Smagghe, 2010 ) and DEET. Based on the olfactometer bioassay, T. castaneum is able to detect allyl cinnamate responding negatively. These results joined to the fact that the action increased with longer exposures, suggests a potential role of allyl cinnamate to control stored-product pest insects as T. castaneum .  

# 5. Conclusion  

Toxic effects of allyl esters, both by contact application and repellency, were recorded in T. castaneum ,    what could be useful in pest control, both to kill insects present on stored-products and to maintain stored-products free of these. The potential use of allyl esters against aphids may be limited. Even high toxicity was produced by ingestion, feeding behavior was not avoided, so not protecting against virus transmission.
