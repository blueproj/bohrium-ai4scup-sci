ABSTRACT:  The lack of selective and safe in vivo IRE1 α  tool molecules has limited the evaluation of IRE1 α  as a viable target to treat multiple myeloma. Focus on improving the physicochemical properties of a literature compound by decreasing lipophilicity, molecular weight, and basicity allowed the discovery of a novel series with a favorable in vitro safety profile and good oral exposure. These efforts culminated in the identification of a potent and selective in vivo tool compound,  G-5758 , that was well tolerated following multiday oral administration of doses up to 500 mg/kg.  G-5758  demonstrated comparable pharmacodynamic effects to induced IRE1 knockdown as measured by XBP1s levels in a multiple myeloma model (KMS-11).  

# INTRODUCTION  

Eukaryotic cells rely on an intracellular organelle called the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) to mediate the three-dimensional folding and assembly of proteins and protein complexes destined for cellular membrane insertion or extracellular secretion.   Excess in demand for protein folding or imbalances in ER parameters, such as redox potential or calcium concentration, cause ER stress. The unfolded protein response (UPR) is an intracellular sensing − signaling network that adapts the ER in response to ER stress in order to enable stress mitigation. If adaptation fails, the UPR triggers self-elimination of the cell through apoptotic cell death.   The mammalian UPR consists of the ER-resident sensors IRE1, PERK, and ATF6, which detect misfolded proteins in the ER lumen and send signals to the nucleus to turn on genes that mediate ER adaptation.   Multiple myeloma (MM) is a hematologic malignancy that arises from immunoglobulin-secreting plasma cells in the bone.   Despite several recent therapeutic advances, MM remains a significant unmet medical need, with frequently acquired resistance to available therapies.   The UPR sensor IRE1 α  plays an important pathological role in MM, based on its known involvement in plasma cell differentiation and in supporting immunoglobulin protein secretion.   IRE1 α  is an ER-resident protein, consisting of a luminal domain, a transmembrane domain, and a cytoplasmic kinase-RNase enzymatic module. IRE1 α  phosphorylation controls its oligomerization and RNase activation: a key function of the RNase is to generate the transcription factor XBP1s via nonconventional splicing of the precursor mRNA, while an

We have previously reported genetic and pharmacological evidence validating IRE1 α  as a potential therapeutic target for MM and demonstrating that allosteric inhibition of its RNase activity through the kinase ATP-binding pocket blocks in vitro and in vivo growth of MM tumor xenografts.   Importantly, our findings indicated that the kinase domain of IRE1 α  is an effective lever for small-molecule inhibition of this unique dual- function enzyme, both in vitro and in vivo. We also showed that kinase-based IRE1 α  inhibition selectively reduced the viability of malignant plasma cells derived from newly diagnosed or post-treatment relapsed MM patients, while sparing normal bone marrow cells. We recently reported a series of selective kinase-based IRE1 α  small-molecule inhib- itors that provided important tools for early preclinical investigations.   In the present work, we describe the design, synthesis, and characterization of kinase-based IRE1 α  small- molecule inhibitors with improved physicochemical and pharmacological properties and safety profiles. This work provides an important advance toward clinical translation of IRE1 α -targeted therapies.  

# RESULTS AND DISCUSSION  

At the outset of the program, a number of IRE1 α  kinase inhibitors have been described in the literature ( Figure 1 ): two very potent and kinase selective inhibitors  1  and  2  disclosed by Amgen,   compound  3 , known as KIRA6,   which demonstrated significant XBP1s cellular potency but exhibited poor kinase selectivity and a Type III inhibitor  4  (GSK2850163A) that showed only minimal cellular XBP1s activity.   More recently, we and others have reported the discovery of IRE1 α  in vitro tool compounds with inhibitory activity and selectivity. ,   In addition, Quentis reported multiple potent inhibitors in subsequent patents.  

Among the inhibitors described at the time, naphthalenes  1 and  2  represented the best in vitro tools to interrogate the pathway because they showed the most profound IRE1 biochemical and cellular activities attributable to IRE1 target engagement. However, these compounds could not be progressed to in vivo studies due to dose-limiting acute toxicity in mice. For example, compound  2  was not tolerated at 45 mg/kg dosed intra peritoneal ly (IP). We hypothesized that this was likely caused by off-target effects, as compound  2  is highly promiscuous and binds to several ion channels, the serotonin receptor, and other G protein-coupled receptors

 (GPCRs). This is consistent with its high lipophilicity (cLog  P

 = 6.5), coupled with high basicity (cp K a  = 9.2). Highly lipophilic compounds have an increased propensity for nonspecific interactions, driving unwanted pharmacology (off-targets) and toxicity,   and the presence of strongly basic amines is known to be a compounding factor. Consequently, we focused on decreasing the lipophilicity and basicity of the naphthalene series to decrease off-target activities while maintaining the favorable kinase selectivity and strong IRE1 α  potency of our starting point. In addition, compound  2  exhibited limited oral exposure ( F  = 2%) due to poor passive permeability (MDCK A to B = 1.0  ×  10 − 6   cm/s) and low solubility. Oral dosing was strongly preferred to facilitate dosing and minimize the frequently observed variability with intraperitoneal dosing. Thus, our goal was the identification of an orally available compound as a pharmaco- logical tool to validate the approach of using IRE1 ATP pocket binders to confidently assess the efficacy and safety associated with IRE1 inhibition.  

Our work to identify kinase inhibitors that allosterically inhibit the RNase domain of IRE1 relied on three major assays. First, the HTRF IRE1 α  binding assay measured ATP- competitive IRE1 α  kinase binding potency. Then, the IRE1 α -RNase assay was used to measure enzymatic endonuclease inhibition via the cleavage of the XBP1 mRNA stem-loop. Third, the XBP1s luciferase reporter cell assay provided a cellular luciferase read-out of functional IRE1 α - RNase inhibitory activity. As many of our compounds exceeded the sensitivity limits of the HTRF IRE1 α  binding assay (bottom of the assay  ∼ 0.12 nM) and the IRE1 α  RNase biochemical assay (bottom of the assay  ∼ 4 nM), we drove our SAR campaign with the XBP1s assay.

The profound activity differences between the ( S )- and ( R )- enantiomers of compound  2  ( 8 ) suggested very specific interactions in the corresponding binding domain. Pyrazole  9 , which aimed to interact with E651, was not tolerated. With the goal of decreasing off-target potential, we focused on decreasing the basicity of the piperidine by adding electron- withdrawing groups. However, bisfluorination at the 5-position of the piperidine ( 10)  and tri flu oro ethyl ation of the piperidine ( 11 ) significantly decreased potency. We attributed this potency loss to a weakened salt bridge interaction between the piperidine and E651 due to decreased basicity. Since piperidine changes were not tolerated, we directed our attention instead to improving the physicochemical properties of the sulfonamide tail.

Keeping in mind a piperidine moiety is required for binding potency and a sulfonamide group for RNase activity, we looked for core replacements that would decrease the lipophilicity of our compounds and thus mitigate off-target activities. First, we confirmed that the benz yl s ulf on amide tail ( 21 ) was also a very potent tail in the 1,4-naphthalene series. Based on the modeling of this compound, we recognized that the phenyl ring of 1,4-naphthalene acts as a spacer and does not significantly contribute to binding to the IRE1 α  protein. Encouraged by the fact that  22  shows some cellular potency, we leveraged our learnings from the naphthalene series and increased the acidity of the NH-sulfonamide to drive potency. Indeed, mono flu ori nation at the 2 position in compounds  23 and  24  indeed led to a 10-fold potency gain. Additional fluorination of the phenyl ring with 2,3-bifluorophenyl ( 25 ) and trifluoroaryl ( 26 ) afforded further potency increases. These compounds with decreased lipophilicity compared to our starting points demonstrated not only better potency but also improved passive permeability. Recognizing that increas- ing the zwitterionic character of our compounds would impact passive permeability, we evaluated a number of aryl substitutions ( 27  and  28 ) to fine-tune the acidity of the sulfonamide; however, no improvement was realized.  

With the goal of increasing passive permeability and decreasing promiscuity, we revisited the tactic of reducing the basicity of the piperidine ( Table 4 ). Based on the crystal structure of  2 , it appeared that the 5-position would be best suited for this purpose, as there is little space available at the 4- position due to the close proximity of Leu644. Indeed, introduction of fluorine ( S ) at the 5-position of piperidine ( 29, G-5758 ) was tolerated and significantly increased passive permeability (MDCK A to B  P app  = 8.7  ×  10 − 6   cm/s), presumably due to the lower basicity of the amine (cp K a  = 78.1). The 5-( S )-isomer ( 29 ) was found to be at least 10-fold more potent than the 5-( R )-isomer ( 30 ) in the HTRF-binding assay. While the crystal structure suggested sufficient space at both positions to accommodate both diastereomers, the experimentally observed potency difference is likely due to differential conformational preferences and a lower energy state associated with the preferred binding conformation of the (3 S ,5 R )-5-fluoropiperidin-3-amine moiety. To further increase potency and fine-tune the basicity of the piperidine, we prepared the 5,5-fluoro, methyl-substituted piperidine  31 , and 5-(fluoromethyl) substituted piperidines  32  and  33 . Addition of methyl in  31  improved the cellular XBP1s potency by 2-fold while maintaining low promiscuity in a secondary pharmacol- ogy panel. To further decrease lipophilicity, we replaced the benz yl s ulf on amide tail with the potent aliphatic tails in the naphthalene series (tri flu oro prop yl s ulf on amide, propylsulfona- mide); however, this led to a significant loss of cellular potency ( Table S3 ).

Having in hand potent and selective compounds with moderate passive permeability, we characterized the metabolic stability of  29 ,  31 , and  32  in liver microsome (LM) and hepatocyte stability assays and progressed them into a mouse pharmacokinetic (PK) study ( Table 5 ). Following intravenous administration to mice,  29  had a moderate blood clearance of 33.9 mL/min/kg, corresponding to  ∼ 38% of the hepatic blood flow. Vss was 1.0 L/kg, and  t 1/2  was 0.55 h. Following a dose of 10 mg/kg administered orally, the compound showed high oral bioavailability.  31  had a similar PK profile, especially with respect to unbound clearance (CLu = 1172 mL/min/kg for  29 and CLu = 1429 mL/min/kg for  31 ). Concurrently,  32  was progressed with the hope of increasing  t 1/2  by increasing the volume of distribution. The volume increased by 3-fold; however,  32  had a high blood clearance of 61.4 mL/min/kg, corresponding to  ∼ 68% of the hepatic blood flow, translating to a similar  t 1/2  as  29 . In addition,  29  had low clearance and favorable exposure in rats (Cl = 3.9 mL/min/kg, Vss = 0.43 L/ kg, F = 60%,  t 1/2  = 1.52 h).  

With high potency and selectivity against kinases and non- kinase off-targets combined with high oral bioavailability,  29 represented an excellent tool for in vivo studies with a superior overall profile compared to  31 ,  32 , and other published IRE1 inhibitors.  

To assess in vivo safety,  29  was orally administered to male Sprague − Dawley rats twice daily (BID) for 7 days and was found to be well tolerated up to the top dose of 500 mg/kg/ dose. At this dose, a  C max  of 104,000 ng/mL and an AUC of 909,000 h \* ng/mL were achieved. Minor safety findings included nonadverse findings in the liver and thymus. Findings in the liver manifested as elevated hepatic enzyme levels at the top dose along with a dose-dependent increase in liver weight and hepatocellular hypertrophy at dose levels  ≥ 150 mg/kg/ dose ( C max  = 28,900 and AUC = 170,000 h \* ng/mL). Furthermore, decreased thymus weight and thymic lymphoid depletion were noted at 500 mg/kg BID.  

In line with the rationale of IRE1 inhibition as a therapeutic strategy for MM, we investigated the pharmacodynamic profile of  29  in a representative MM model. We had previously shown that the KMS-11 MM model is sensitive to genetic IRE1 knockdown both in a cellular context and in vivo and therefore selected this model for in vivo pharmacodynamic (PD) evaluation.  

As the workhorse assay for assessing the cellular efficacy of our compounds, specifically, the ability to inhibit XBP1 splicing, we established an XBP1s luciferase reporter assay. This assay utilizes an engineered HEK293 cell line that measures compound-induced inhibition of IRE1 RNase activity resulting from splicing an XBP1 reporter construct and provides the throughput required to support a drug discovery program. For more physiological and accurate cellular PD assessment in a matching cell line, we developed a KMS-11 cell-based bDNA assay that detects endogenous spliced XBP1 mRNA through solid-phase capture and bDNA amplification followed by a luminescent enzyme reaction. Using this assay, we measured the cellular efficacy of  29  at 24 h and determined an IC50 of 64 nM, a value close to the IC 50 value of 38 nM determined by the XBP1s reporter assay. # CONCLUSIONS  

In summary, we describe here the discovery of an orally bioavailable and selective IRE1 α  inhibitor series based on a phenyloxy scaffold. A structure-based drug design approach focused on decreasing the molecular weight of literature compound  2  led to the identification of compound  25 , which exhibited balanced physicochemical properties and potency as well as improved passive permeability. Fine-tuning of the acidity of the sulfonamide group and the basicity of the piperidine moiety culminated in the discovery of compound  29 ( G-5758 ), a potent IRE1 α  inhibitor with good oral exposure in rodents and low promiscuity.  G-5758  demonstrated a robust PD response in the KMS-11 tumor xenograft model, mirroring the effect of genetic IRE1 knockdown up to 12 h after dosing. In combination with a strong safety profile in vivo, these data reveal  G-5758  as a well-suited in vivo tool molecule for further evaluation of IRE1 α  inhibition toward potential multiple myeloma therapies.
