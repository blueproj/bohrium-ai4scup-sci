# In ﬂ uence of the backbone conformation of conjugated polymers on morphology and photovoltaic properties  

Guangzheng Zuo,   Zhaojun Li,   Maojie Zhang,   Xia Guo,   Yue Wu, Shaoqing Zhang,   Bo Peng,   Wei Wei \* a   and Jianhui Hou \* b  

Two conjugated polymers, PBDT – TBT and PBDT – TTBT, were designed and synthesized. PBDT – TBT has a zigzagged chain conformation (Z-type), while PBDT – TTBT has a linear chain conformation (L-type). The results show that the polymer chain conformation signi ﬁ cantly a ﬀ ects the optoelectronic properties of the polymers. With the conformation change from Z-type to L-type, the J-aggregation of the polymer was enhanced greatly,  i.e.  PBDT – TTBT shows a much more prominent absorption shoulder peak at the long wavelength direction compared to PBDT – TBT. In the X-ray di ﬀ raction analysis, it was found that the L-type polymer shows much stronger crystallinity than the Z-type polymer. Bene ﬁ ting from the enhanced inter-chain  p – p  stacking e ﬀ ect, the blend of PBDT – TTBT/PC 71 BM showed higher hole mobility compared to the blend of PBDT – TBT/PC 71 BM. The power conversion e ﬃ ciencies (PCEs) of the polymer solar cells based on PBDT – TTBT/PC 71 BM and PBDT – TTBT/PC 71 BM are 6.3% and 4.9%, respectively. These results clearly reveal that the backbone conformation is an important issue for improving the intermolecular interaction of conjugated polymers, and hence provides a guideline for the molecular design of conjugated polymers.

For example, our recent work showed that when the donor units (benz odi thi oph ene, BDT) in a D – A polymer named as PBDTTT – S – T were replaced by dith ie no benz odi thi oph ene (DTBDT),   the backbone conformation of the polymer can be changed from a zigzagged conformation to a linear confor- mation. Consequently, stronger intermolecular stacking as well as better hole mobility can be realized, which is favorable for achieving higher photovoltaic performance.   Recently, more and more reports have shown that the bridge units between the donor and the acceptor moieties in D – A polymers play important roles in molecular design, and when di ﬀ erent bridge units were employed in D – A polymers, the morphol- ogies of the BHJ blends, as well as their photovoltaic properties, varied distinctly. – 35   However, the in  uence of the bridge units on backbone conformation, as well as the morphologies of the BHJ blends, has been seldom investi- gated. Therefore, from the point of view of the molecular design of D – A polymers, we synthesized and characterized a pair of D – A polymers with very similar donor and acceptor moieties but di ﬀ erent  p -bridge units, named as PBDT – TBT and PBDT – TTBT and shown in Scheme 1, to provide insight into the correlation among the backbone conformations of the polymers, the morphologies and the photovoltaic properties of the BHJ blends.  

# Introduction  

In recent years, polymer solar cells (PSCs) have attracted considerable attention due to their unique features such as low cost, low weight and capability to be fabricated into   exible large- area devices. – 5   The bulk heterojunction (BHJ) structure,   in which a donor – accepter bicontinuous inter penetrating network can be formed, has played a leading role in improving the photovoltaic performance of PSCs. – 10   Since the photovoltaic performance of PSCs is closely correlated with the morphology of the BHJ blend, how to control the morphology is an important topic towards achieving high photovoltaic performance. – 25 Therefore, considerable attention has been drawn to modulate the morphologies of the BHJ blends, and many useful processing methods, such as slow growth,   thermal annealing 27   and the use of additives 28   and mixing solvents,   have been successfully employed to promote the formation of nano-scale phase sepa- ration with appropriate aggregation size as well as the ordered inter-chain packing of polymers. Thereby the power conversion e ﬃ ciencies (PCEs) of PSCs can be improved signi  cantly.

# Results and discussion  

# Synthesis and thermal properties  

Three monomers, 4,8-bis(5-(2-ethylhexyl)thiophen-2-yl)benzo- [1,2- b :4,5- b 0 ]dithiophene (BDT – T),   5,6-bis((2-ethylhexyl)oxy)- 4,7-di(thiophen-2-yl)benzo[ c ][1,2,5]thiadiazole (TBT), and 5,6-bis((2-butyloctyl)oxy)-4,7-bis(thieno[3,2- b ]thiophen-2-yl)benzo- [ c ][1,2,5]thiadiazole (TTBT), were synthesized by reported methods. PBDT – TBT and PBDT – TTBT were prepared by the Stille coupling polycondensation reaction using Pd(PPh 3 ) 4  as the catalyst and toluene as the solvent. These two polymers show good solubility in commonly used organic solvents such as chloroform, toluene, chlorobenzene and  o -dichorobenzene. The molecular weight and polydispersity (PDI) of the polymers were estimated by gel permeation chromatography (GPC) using monodispersed polystyrene as the standard and chloroform as the eluent. The thermal stability of these two polymers was investigated by thermo-gravimetric analysis (TGA). As shown in Fig. 1, the TGA plots reveal that the onset decomposition temperatures with 5% weight loss ( T d ) of PBDT – TBT and PBDT – TTBT are 326    C and 320    C, respectively.

X-ray di ﬀ raction (XRD) measurements were used to reveal the crystallinity of the two polymers in thin   lms. The XRD patterns of the   lms of PBDT – TBT and PBDT – TTBT casted from o -DCB are shown in Fig. 3. It is very clear that no peak can be observed in the di ﬀ raction pattern of the PBDT – TBT   lm, implying that the PBDT – TBT   lm is amorphous and no ordered packing can be formed in the   lm. The results indicate that the L-type back- bone conformation is more favorable to the formation of ordered laminar packing and stronger  p – p  stacking compared with the Z-type backbone conformation. Consequently, the enhanced  p – p  stacking is bene  cial to inter-chain charge transport, so the PBDT – TTBT   lm should possess a higher hole mobility compared to the PBDT – TBT   lm.  

The UV-Vis absorption spectra of PBDT – TBT and PBDT – TTBT in solution and in thin   lms are shown in Fig. 4a and b respectively. In solution, PBDT – TBT shows an absorption peak at 556 nm; in a solid   lm, the absorption peak is red-shi  ed to 578 nm, and meanwhile, a shoulder peak at 612 nm appears, which should be due to the enhanced intermolecular  p – p \* transition. Interestingly, PBDT – TTBT shows a very strong J-aggregate in solution (on account of its strong low energy absorption peak), indicating that the L-type backbone confor- mation leads to strong intermolecular interactions, which is similar to what we observed previously.   Furthermore, from solution to solid   lm, the absorption peak of PBDT – TBT is red- shi  ed by 20 nm, and this phenomenon is quite similar to what has been observed in many other conjugated polymers, implying that the backbone units of PBDT – TBT are twisted in solution but become more planar in the solid state with the assistance of the intermolecular  p – p  stacking e ﬀ ect. However, from solution to solid   lm, the absorption spectrum of PBDT – TTBT is almost unchanged. The comparison between the absorption spectra of PBDT – TBT and PBDT – TTBT clearly con  rms that the L-type backbone conformation is bene  cial to keep the functional groups of the polymer in a well-de  ned plane without twisting, and therefore enhances intermolecular p – p  interactions. These results are coincident with the obser- vations in the XRD measurements.

# Experimental section  

# Materials  

The monomer, BDT – T, was purchased from Solarmer Materials Inc. The monomers, TBT 37   and TTBT,   were prepared by the methods reported in the literature. Pd(PPh 3 ) 4  was purchased from Frontier Chemical Co. The other materials were common commercial level and used as received.  

# Instruments and measurements  

UV-Visible absorption spectroscopy was measured by a Hitachi U-3100 UV-Vis spectrophotometer. Electrochemical cyclic vol- tammetry (CV) was performed on a CHI650D electrochemical workstation with a three-electrode system in a solution of 0.1 mol L  1   tetra but yl ammonium hexa  uorophosphate (Bu 4 NPF 6 ) in acetonitrile at a scan rate of 50 mV s  1 . The polymer   lm was coated on a Pt plate electrode by dipping the electrode into the corresponding solutions and then drying. A Pt wire was used as the counter electrode, and Ag/Ag +   was used as the reference electrode. The molecular weight of the polymers was measured by the GPC method, with polystyrene as the standard and chloroform as the eluent. Thermo grav i metric analysis (TGA) was performed on a TA Instruments, Inc., TGA-2050 instrument under a nitrogen atmosphere. Thin-  lm X-ray di ﬀ raction (XRD) was recorded on a Bruker D8 Discover thin-  lm di ﬀ ractometer with Cu K a  radiation ( l  ¼  1.54056   A) operated at 40 keV and 40 mA. Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM) was performed to charac- terize the surface morphology of the   lms on a Nanoscope IIIA (Vecco) instrument using the tapping mode. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) was performed using a JEOL 2200FS instrument at a 160 kV accelerating voltage.  

# Device fabrication and characterization  

PSCs with the structure of ITO/PEDOT:PSS/polymers:PC 71 BM/ Ca (20 nm)/Al (80 nm) were fabricated as follows: the ITO glass was pre-cleaned and modi  ed with about 40 nm thick PEDOT:PSS (Bayer Baytron 4083) as the anode bu ﬀ er layer, which was spin-cast from a PEDOT:PSS aqueous solution onto the ITO substrate. The active layer was then deposited on top of the PEDOT:PSS layer by casting from a 10 mg mL  1   o -DCB solution. Finally, Ca (20 nm)/Al (80 nm) layers were successively deposited under vacuum onto the photosensitive layer at a pressure of  ca.  4    10  4   Pa. The thickness of the photosensitive layer was  ca.  100 nm, measured on an Ambios Tech. XP-2 pro-  lometer. Except for the deposition of the PEDOT:PSS layers, all of the fabrication processes were carried out under a nitrogen atmosphere. The current density – voltage ( J – V ) characteristics were recorded on an Agilent B2912A Precision Source/Measure unit. The power conversion e ﬃ ciency (PCE) of the polymer solar cells was measured under a 1 sun, AM 1.5G (air mass 1.5 global) spectrum from a solar simulator (100 Mw cm  2 ) using a XES-70S1 (SAN-EI Electric Co., Ltd.) solar simulator (AAA grade, 70 mm    70 mm photo-beam size). The external quantum e ﬃ ciency (EQE) was measured by a Solar Cell Spectral Response Measurement System QE-R3011 instrument (Enli Technology Co., Ltd.).

# Conclusions  

Two conjugated polymers with di ﬀ erent backbone conforma- tions, PBDT – TBT and PBDT – TTBT, were synthesized and char- acterized. The backbone formation of conjugated polymer plays an important role in realizing ordered and stronger intermo- lecular packing. When the backbone conformation of the polymer was changed from Z-type to L-type, the polymer showed very strong J-aggregation even in the solution state, and also demonstrated more ordered laminar packing as well as stronger intermolecular  p – p  stacking in the solid   lm, and conse- quently, the hole mobility and photovoltaic performance of the polymer can be improved. Therefore, the results in this work clearly reveal that the backbone conformation is an important issue for improving the intermolecular interactions of conjugated polymers, and hence provides a guideline for the molecular design of conjugated polymers.
