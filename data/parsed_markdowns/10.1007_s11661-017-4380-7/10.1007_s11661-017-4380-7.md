# Comparison of Thermodynamic Predictions and Experimental Observations on B Additions in Powder-Processed Ni-Based Superalloys Containing Elevated Concentrations of Nb  

STOICHKO ANTONOV , JIAJIE HUO, QIANG FENG, DIETER ISHEIM, DAVID N. SEIDMAN, EUGENE SUN, and SAMMY TIN  

Boron additions to Ni-based superalloys are considered to be beneﬁcial to the creep properties of the alloy, as boron has often been reported to increase grain boundary cohesion, increase ductility, and promote the formation of stable boride phases. Despite the importance, it is not well understood whether these improvements are associated with the presence of elemental boron or stable borides along the grain boundaries. In this investigation, two experimental powder-processed Ni-based superalloys containing elevated levels of Nb were found to exhibit increased solubility for B in the  c  matrix when compared to similar commercial Ni-based superalloys. This resulted in an overall lower B concentration at grain boundaries that suppressed boride formation. As the predictive capability of CALPHAD database models for Ni-based superalloys have improved over the years, some discrepancies may still persist around compositionally heterogeneous features such as grain boundaries. Improved quantiﬁcation of the characteristic partitioning of B as a function of the bulk alloy composition is required for understanding and predicting the stability of borides.

# I. INTRODUCTION  

E XCELLENT  mechanical properties, such as high temperature creep and fatigue resistance, along with high corrosion and oxidation resistance, while operating at high stress levels and elevated temperatures are the properties that make nickel-based superalloys the mate- rial used for the ‘‘hot section’’ components in modern 1 – 9 ]   gas turbine engines. These superior high temperature properties can largely be attributed to the unique microstructure of Ni-based superalloys, which consists of ordered L1 2  intermetallic precipitates  c ¢  (Ni 3 Al) distributed coherently within a disordered FCC A1 matrix  c  (Ni) that provides substantial order and coherency type strengthening by inhibiting dislocation motion during plastic deformation. The composition of modern Ni-based superalloys is complex and tends to incorporate a variety of elemental alloying additions. Diﬀerent concentrations of  c ¢  formers Al, Ti, Nb, and Ta in modern polycrystalline Ni-based superalloys are utilized to produce an optimized size and volume fraction of the  c ¢  strengthening precipitates, as these elements will preferentially partition to the  c ¢  phase and serve to lower the anti-phase boundary energy as well as strengthen the precipitate’s resistance to dislocation 10 ]   shear. However, elevated concentrations of these elements lead to some residual solubility in the  c  phase that will serve as potent solid solution strengthen- 11 – 14 ]   ers. In conjunction with conventional refractory alloying elements, such as W, Mo, Cr, and Co that strengthen the  c  matrix phase through solid solution strengthening, the overall chemistry of the alloy must be balanced as excessive alloying may promote the forma- tion of various ordered Ni 3 X as well as topologically closed packed (TCP) phases, which can be detrimental 15 – 17 ] to the properties due to their brittle nature.  

Minor additions of grain boundary strengthening elements, such as C, B, Zr, and Hf, are typically introduced to the chemistries of polycrystalline Ni-based superalloys, with the aim of enhancing the overall creep properties as well as preventing grain boundary embrit- 18 – 20 ]   tlement from undesired trace elements. Bieber and Decker have studied trace elements in superalloys and classify B as an element that is beneﬁcial to properties in 21 ] small amounts, but harmful in large quantities. Speciﬁcally, additions of trace amounts of B to Ni-based superalloys have been reported to be beneﬁcial to the creep properties by decreasing grain boundary diﬀusiv- ity, increasing the grain boundary interfacial strength, lowering grain boundary surface energy, preventing conglomeration of M 23 C 6  carbides, and promoting the formation of discrete boride particles, when elevated concentrations of Mo, W, and Cr, along with adequate 18 , – 24 ]   B levels are present at the grain boundaries. A study on Alloy 617, showed that a minor boron addition to the alloy’s chemistry, resulted in as much as 25 pct higher creep rupture strength over the boron-free 25 ]   variant, and furthermore, recent work by Kontis et al . showed that an optimized nominal content of boron is required to positively inﬂuence ductility and 24 ]   creep performance. As a result, the chemistries of most commercial polycrystalline Ni-based superalloys contain minor additions of B, such that a monolayer of elemental B forms along the grain boundaries. A study by Zhou  et al . suggested that doping a nickel-rich grain boundary with boron tends to induce the formation of disordered  c  phase on both sides of the grain boundary, through which dislocations can be transmitted and the strains associated with dislocation pile up can be 20 ] dissipated, decreasing the grain boundary hardening. On the other hand, such softening of the grain boundary prevents premature initiation of micro-cracks. It is generally understood that the elemental boron along the grain boundaries of Ni-based alloys will eventually contribute to the formation of some sort of M 5 B 3  or M 3 B 2  boride with the available Cr, Mo, and/or W solute 23 , , ]   atoms. However, a recent study by Antonov et al . has shown that some experimental high Nb-con- tent powder-processed polycrystalline Ni-based super- alloys possess an elevated solubility of B in the  c 28 ]   phase. This resulted in a reduced degree of elemental B segregation to grain boundaries which was inadequate for boride formation, and permitted the available boride-forming solute elements Cr, Mo, and W to form 28 ]   r  phase instead of the borides. This synergistic interaction between B and Nb appears to be consistent with other Nb-bearing Ni-based superalloys as Table  I lists the occurrence of boride formation as a function of Nb, Cr, and B contents for a range of commercial and experimental alloys. It is apparent that boride formation tends to be suppressed in the higher Nb alloys.  

Despite the many existing commercial Ni-based superalloys, which utilize B addition in hopes of improving the creep properties, the role of B in the microstructure of such alloys is not fully understood and limited literature exists on the preferred state of B in these alloys. In order to better understand how to optimize the B concentration in superalloys, it is important to understand the characteristic partitioning behavior of B, as well as its preferred state  i.e ., solid solution or discrete precipitation. Thus, the aim of this study is to elucidate how B atoms behave in high Nb-content powder-processed superalloys and compare experimental observations with thermodynamic predictions.

# IV. DISCUSSION  

Boron additions to Ni-based superalloys are generally understood to increase creep performance due to boron’s favorable eﬀect on grain boundary properties, 18 , , ] ductility, and the ability to form stable borides. Recently, it has been found that an optimized boride volume fraction can promote superior ductility and 44 ]   creep performance. Even though minor B additions are added to most modern superalloys in order to exploit the aforementioned advantages, few studies exist on quantifying and understanding the partitioning behavior and solubility limits of B, partly due to the complexity of studying such light elements with great 23 , , – 48 ]   accuracy, when present in trace amounts. As such, thermodynamic database models rely on limited data points for the prediction of B behavior. Further- more, complex higher-order interactions between B and other constituent elements present in modern superal- loys as well as materials processing techniques can result in characteristically diﬀerent behaviors.  

The thermodynamic stability of two experimental high Nb-content Ni-based superalloys with similar compositions, given in Table  II , was assessed using the TCNi8 database of Thermo-Calc and the corresponding phase fraction plots are given in Figures  1 (a) and (b). The main diﬀerences appear to be in the secondary phase formation as the higher Nb-content superalloy is predicted to form Ni 3 Ta-DO a  d  phase, while the high Al-content superalloy is predicted to form  r  phase, as well as have a higher volume fraction of  c ¢  phase. Upon the suppression of the more thermodynamic ally stable M 2 B boride, the commonly observed M 3 B 2  boride can be predicted. Although detailed comparison of the predictions shows that minor diﬀerences in the stability and volume fraction of borides in the two alloys exist, where RRHT4 has more stable borides, these diﬀerences can likely be attributed to the slightly higher nominal content of B in the alloy, Table  II . Despite being predicted to form, detailed microstructural investiga- tions on RRHT4 have shown that no boride formation occurred. These contradictory results can likely be attributed to the unusually low concentration of ele- mental boron that segregates to the grain boundaries, \~  1 at. pct corresponding to a  \~  24  l m grain size. Compared to other boride-forming, powder-processed Ni-based superalloys, such as RR1000 with  \~  5 at. pct B measured along the grain boundaries for a  \~  5  l m grain 28 , ]   size, it would appear that the grain boundary B concentration is below a critical level to induce the formation of borides. Moreover, a separate APT study on a Nb-lean alloy ME3, with a comparable grain size of

 27.1 to 30.6  l m and a nominal B concentration of

 0.11 at. pct, showed that even at a grain boundary B concentration of 0.98  ±  0.14 at. pct, boride formation occured. These results appear to suggest that Nb plays a critical role in inﬂuencing the partitioning behavior of B

Due to the notable diﬀerences in the characteristic partitioning behavior of B within this class of similar alloys, it is essential to investigate and understand where the B atoms reside. The production method of the alloy, i.e ., whether it was cast, wrought, or powder-processed plays an important part in the concentration of B at grain boundaries. For example, as-cast alloys typically exhibit larger grain sizes along with higher degree of 6 , , ]   chemical heterogeneity. With B additions exhibit- ing limited solubility in the primary  c  phase during solidiﬁcation, it segregates preferentially to the liquid and the concentration will gradually accumulate in the residual liquid, prior to ﬁnal solidiﬁcation within the 19 ] interdendritic regions or along grain boundaries. Consistent with the predictions shown in Figures  1 (a) and (b), once borides form in cast superalloys, they persist as thermodynamic ally stable phases and post-so- lidiﬁcation heat treatments intended to homogenize or solutionize the  c ¢  phases will not aﬀect their phase fraction or chemistry. Powder-processed alloys, on the other hand, utilize rapid solidiﬁcation and solid-state mechanisms during processing to minimize dendritic segregation and achieve a higher degree of both microstructural and compositional homogeneity when compared to cast and wrought alloys. Since the extremely high cooling rates experienced during spray atomization processing of Ni-based superalloy powders have been shown to suppress dendritic segregation and equilibrium carbide and boride transformations, the powders contain a high degree of solute supersaturation. During subsequent consolidation of the powder to form a billet, solid-state diﬀusion mechanisms serve to redis- tribute the solute and minimize the chemical potentials in an eﬀort to move towards a more thermodynamic ally stable state. Despite the much reduced length scales associated with solid-state diﬀusion, chemical segrega- tion does occur as excess interstitial solutes that have limited solubility in the parent  c  and  c ¢  phases accumu- late along grain boundaries and eventually lead to the formation of carbides and borides. An APT study on a commercial powder-processed Ni-based superalloy, Astroloy, reported the existence of up to 20 at. pct B 51 ]   along the grain boundary, showing that even in powder-processed alloys elemental B segregates heavily to the grain boundaries. Typically, both the  c  and  c ¢ phases in Ni-based superalloys exhibit limited solubility for B, and concentrations of 0.08 and 0.04 at. pct reported for the respective phases in a commercial 36 ]   Ni-based superalloy RR1000, are consistent with thermodynamic predictions showing extremely limited solubility for boron in any phase other than the borides, Table  III . Gabb  et al . looked at the formation of minor phases in powder-processed disk alloy, LSHR, and noted that numerous borides existed as ﬁne particles in the extruded material; however, the number of grain boundary borides was greatly reduced during forging and mostly re-dissolved during the solution heat treat- ment, as the boride liquation temperature was found to 52 ] be slightly higher than the solutioning temperature. Upon long exposure times at 1033 K and 1126 K (760   C and 853   C) the borides were observed to reprecipitate. In a recent study, Du  et al . showed that borides in cast IN782 were absent in the as-cast, solutioned, and high temperature aged conditions; however, M 5 B 3  borides precipitated at grain boundaries     48 ] after a 24-hour exposure at 1118 K (845 C). Upon further exposure at 1023 K and 1118 K (750   C and 845   C), these precipitates signiﬁcantly coarsened. How- ever, aging at 1173 K and 1223 K (900   C and 950   C) caused the M 5 B 3  borides to transform to M 23 C 6 carbides and with a complete transformation at 100 and 50 hours, respectively.  

Atom probe tomography of RRHT4 shows a clear partitioning of B to the  c  phase, with moderate segrega- tion to the  c / c ¢  interface, Figures  2  and  3 . The average concentration of B in the  c  matrix is  \~  0.18 at. pct which is more than twice that reported for RR1000, Table  IV . Since the higher solubility of B in the  c  matrix also aﬀects the resulting grain boundary composition and may result in local phase instabilities, the grain boundary chemistries were studied. Coupling of the grain boundary  c  phase composition obtained from APT measurements with thermodynamic database models still results in the prediction of boride formation, Figure  4 , although no borides were observed along the grain boundaries, the local solute enrichment was found to preferentially stabilize and induce the formation of  r  phase which was consistent with the observation in Reference  28  where  r phase was detected to form early on during the aging of the alloy. As the  r  phase largely comprised Cr, W, and Mo solute atoms or the same elements contributing to the formation of borides, it appears that the formation of  r phase reduces the availability of those elements to form [ 28 ]   borides and suppresses their formation. Thermo-Calc predictions made with the intragranular  c  phase compo- sition instead of the grain boundary  c  phase composition, Figure  4 (b), also predict the occurrence of borides and  r phase. However, in this instance, the borides are predicted to exhibit comparatively higher temperature stability and form prior to the  r  phase. This was not experimentally observed as only  r  and no boride formation was detected even after a 1000-hour exposure at 1073 K (800   C), 28 ] Figure  5 .  

A higher Nb-content superalloy, RRHT2, which favors  g  phase formation over  r , for reasons discussed in References  14  and  15  was also shown  via  APT to possess an elevated concentration of B in the  c  matrix, \~  0.17 at. pct, Table  IV . The  c  phase composition, given in Table  IV , coupled with thermodynamic predictions, gives a similar phase fraction  vs  temperature relation- ship when compared to that of RRHT4, Figure  6 ; however, rather than  r ,  d  phase is the predicted preferred secondary phase. Upon exposure at 1073 K (800   C) for 1000 hours,  g -Ni 6 AlNb phase forms a mixed structure along with  d -Ni 3 Nb, Figures  6 (b) and 7 (b). This lamellar structure is similar to the mixed phases observed in a commercial Ni-based superalloy [ 53 ]   718Plus, which forms  g  with small  d  strips   along the grain boundaries. Since the  d  and  g  phase structures exhibit limited solubility for Cr, Mo, and W, they do not compete with the borides for these solutes when they form. Unlike RRHT4 where r phase formation appeared to have suppressed the formation of borides, M 3 B 2  was found to form in RRHT2 upon thermal exposure. The characteristic diﬀraction peaks were detected in the XRD patterns from the electrolytic extractions, Figures  7 (a) and (c). Comparing the GSAS-II reﬁned parameters for the XRD pattern in Figure  7 (a), the relative fraction of M 3 B 2  phase was found to be only 2.2 pct of the MC phase, which is much lower than the thermodynamic ally predicted relative fraction shown in Figure  1  of 63.7 pct. Looking at the TEM-EDX determined composition of the M 3 B 2  bor- ide, it is apparent that it has high solubility for Nb, which likely substitutes for Mo. This does not appear to agree with the thermodynamic predictions of low to no Nb solubility in borides, Figure  1 . Studies on other Nb-containing alloys, also suggest a higher than pre- 20 , ] dicted solubility for Nb in the borides.  

In order to gain insight on how thermodynamic predictions are inﬂuenced by B and Nb additions, predicted phase diagram plots of the RRHT4 composi- tion can be used to look at how the formation temper- atures are aﬀected by additions of each element, Figure  8 . Outside of the M 3 B 2  borides that may be stabilized by higher concentration of B, the addition of B does not seem to have a major eﬀect on the stability of the predicted phases. Interestingly, boride formation is predicted to occur when a concentration as low as  \~ 0.003 at. pct of boron is added to the alloy chemistry, which is signiﬁcantly less than the B content in RRHT2 and RRHT4, Table  II . Looking at Figure  8 (b), the changes in the concentration of Nb appear to have a much larger eﬀect on all of the phases. Looking speciﬁcally at the M 3 B 2  phase, increasing Nb decreases its stability up to \~  7 at. pct Nb, at which point the stability starts to slowly increase as the BCC and C14 laves phases are also stabilized. From the nominal compositions of RRHT2 and RRHT4, with  \~  5.6 and 4.7 at. pct, the predictions suggest that boride formation is more favorable in RRHT4, which is clearly not the case.  

Zhao  et al . discuss the challenges with CALPHAD predictions of superalloys and conclude that limited data on TCP phases leads to inaccurate phase stability predictions when alloys are prone to the formation of 54 ]   such phases. Challenges with obtaining quantitative data on elemental B and ﬁne boride particles using conventional characterization techniques are quite high due to elemental B being a low atomic number element, which tends to segregate to grain boundaries. As such, few data exist on boride formation and boride segregation in superalloys, leading to discrepancies with thermody- namic predictions. With the attributes of grain bound- aries and B additions playing an important role in determining the physical properties of the alloys there is a need for both an improved understanding of the governing mechanisms and high ﬁdelity data to populate gaps in existing thermodynamic databases to aid eﬀorts in the computational design of Ni-based superalloys.  

# V. CONCLUSIONS  

Based on the results and observations presented in this study, the following conclusions can be reported:  

1. High Nb-content Ni-based superalloys exhibit a comparatively higher solubility of B in the  c  phase, which leads to an overall lower concentration of B along grain boundaries.

 2. Formation of  r  phase affects boride formation, as it depletes the boride-forming elements and dilutes the elemental B while creating new interfaces.

 3. Thermodynamic database models have limited pre- diction capabilities when it comes to boride forma- tion and composition in powder-processed Ni-based superalloys, partly due to the lack of quantitative data on B behavior.
