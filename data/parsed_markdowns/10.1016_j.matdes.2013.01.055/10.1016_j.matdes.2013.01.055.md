# 1. Introduction  

Ni-base superalloys with high volume fraction of the hardenin  g c 0 -phase are widely employed as blade material in aero-engi  nes  and land-base gas turbines, due to their outstanding high-tempe  r-  ature creep and fatigue strength as well as excellent oxidation and  corrosion resistance  [1–3] . Nevertheless, service-indu  ced micro-  structural degenerations in nickel-bas  ed superalloy components  subjected to high temperatures and stresses in aggressive environ-  ments are inevitable, including  c 0   coarsenin  g, carbide decomposi-  tion, formatio  n of topologically close packed (TCP) phase and  creep cavitation  [2,4,5] . The mechanical properties are thus signif-  icantly degraded due to the microstructural instability, ultimately  resulting in the loss of life.  

Fortunately  , it has been demonstrated that the degraded micro-  structure of many superalloys can be recovered to its desired mor-  phology through an appropriate rejuvenati  on heat treatment (RHT) which consists of solutioning and aging heat treatments  [6,7] . Furthermore, hot isostatic pressing (HIP) has been successfu  lly em-  ployed to heal creep induced cavities in a wide variety of superal-  loys  [8,9] . So far, HIP rejuvenation technology (involving HIP and  RHT) has been widely utilized to extend the service life of hot-sec-  tion components. The develope  d rejuvenation schedules, however  vary depending on the alloy type and the accumulate  d damage.  Hence, a series of experiments have to be conducte  d in order to  determine an optimal schedule. Extensive research efforts have  been made in verifying the effectivenes  s of HIP and rejuvenation  

# 2. Experimen  tal details  

A ﬁrst stage blade which was service-expose  d for 1200 h was  removed from an aero-engine turbine. It was manufactur  ed from  K465 alloy by means of investment casting, exhibiting an equiaxed  crystal structure. The alloy composition (wt.%) was: 0.15C, 8.72Cr,  9.87Co, 10.31W, 1.57Mo, 1.03Nb, 5.29Al, 2.65Ti and balance Ni.  Service-damag  ed samples having a dimensio  n of 8    8    2 mm  3 were sectioned from the mid-airfoil sections of the blade where  the temperat  ure was highest due to the gas ﬂow. They were sub-  jected to various HIP schedules in a laboratory HIP unit (ABB, Quin-  tus). Since the  c 0 -solvus temperat  ure of K465 alloy is 1503 K, two  soaking temperature  s (i.e., 1453 K and 1503 K) were selected in or-  der to observe the healing features. Meanwhile, two hydrostatic  pressure  s (50 MPa and 150 MPa) were used to investigate the  interactio  n of HIP parameters on cavity healing. The soaking time  during HIP was ﬁxed to 1 h. In addition, the HIPed samples were  furnace cooled from the soaking temperature to 773 K for about  20 min, and then air cooled to room temperature and 1 atm atmo-  sphere. The detailed HIP conditions are listed in  Table 1 .  

For microstructural characteri  zation, the samples were sec-  tioned, mechanically ground, and polished. After cleaning, they  were etched in a solution of 10 g CuSO  4 , 50 ml HCl and 100 ml  H 2 O which dissolves  c 0   precipita  tes. A Hitachi S4800 ﬁeld-emission  scanning electron microscop  e (FESEM) was used to characterize  the microstructur  al features.

It can be observed that the continuo  us  and thick  c 0   ﬁlms were distributed along the grain boundari  es,  resulting from the local enrichment of  c 0 -forming elements (e.g., Al and Ti) which stems from the decomposition of MC carbides. Moreove  r, a large  sized creep cavity nucleated at the grain boundary was observed,  and it would continue to propagat  e further along the weak  c 0   ﬁlms.  

Fig. 2  shows the microstructur  e around the creep cavity in the  HIPed sample subjected to 1453 K/50 MPa/1 h. Due to the relative  low soaking temperat  ure and applied pressure, the cavities still ex-  isted in a cylindrical shape without any obvious morphology  changes, except for the coarsenin  g of primary  c 0   precipitates which  commonly occurs at elevated temperat  ure. When increasing HIP  pressure up to 150 MPa (i.e., HIP condition was 1453 K/150 MPa/  1 h), several signiﬁcant morphological changes can be observed,  as shown in  Fig. 3 . The coalesced  c 0   precipitates in the vicinity of  the cylindrical creep cavity were concentrically arranged, forming  a so-called concentrically-or  iented  c 0   rafting structure. In contrast,  those precipitates away from the cavity appear to have experienc-  ing usual evolution under common heat treatments  . This kind of  raft structure was also found around the cavity exhibiting a spher-  ical form, as reported in our previous work  [15] . It has been gener-  ally accepted that the rafting kinetics is expected to be a diffusion-  controlled process  [16–18] . The investigated sample is subjected to  the applied hydrostatic pressure  , thus a complex stress state that  inﬂuencing cavity evolution exists in the vicinity of the cavity.  Based on the mechanical analysis, this complex stress state can  be equivalent to an effective radial tensile stress with a negative  stress gradient present in anywhere of stress concentration. From  a thermodynam  ic viewpoint  [19,20] , the chemical potential gradi-  ent induced by the effective stress gradient was one of the driving  forces for vacancy diffusion from the cavity surface to grain bound-  aries, which are supposed to be ideal vacancy sinks. Due to the lim-  ited size of the rafting structure shown in  Fig. 4 , it is thus  reasonable to consider that its occurrence is dominated by atomic  diffusion driven by the stress ﬁeld around the cavity. Comparing  the cavity evolutions shown in  Figs. 2 and 3 , it can be concluded  that solute diffusion was much more obvious under higher HIP  pressures when HIP temperature was kept at 1453 K.

In the hot isostatic pressed sample subjected to 1503 K/50 MPa/  1 h, large-siz  ed creep cavities were hardly observed, but some  incomplete healing structures can be occasionally found, as shown  in  Fig. 4 . The coarsened  c 0   were completely dissolved into the ma-  trix at 1503 K, and the  c 0   phase was re-precipitated in the cuboidal  form during HIP cooling. Compare  d with the experimental results  under 1453 K/50 MPa/1 h and 1453 K/150 MPa/1 h, it can be found  that the applied hydrostatic pressure required to sinter creep cav-  ities in an acceptable time can be reduced by improvin  g the soak-  ing temperature  , while the lowest HIP temperat  ure required for  healing can be also reduced via improving HIP pressure  . Therefore,  both the soaking temperature and the hydrostatic pressure act as  two driving factors for cavity healing. Although the maximum tem-  perature and pressure are respectively limited by some metallurgi-  cal factors and HIP unit, the optimal HIP condition can still be  determined by adjusting the soaking temperat  ure and hydrostati  c pressure.  

# 4. Conclusion  

In summary, the concentrically-or  iented  c 0   rafting structure  was observed in the vicinity of the creep cavity having a cylindrical  form, resulting from the atomic diffusion related to the nearby  stress ﬁeld. Meanwhile, the morphological instability of the cylin-  drical cavity occurred during cavity healing, tending to be split into  a row of spherical voids. HIP temperature and pressure, acting as  two driving factors for solute diffusion, interact with each other  and inﬂuence the healing behavior of creep cavities.
