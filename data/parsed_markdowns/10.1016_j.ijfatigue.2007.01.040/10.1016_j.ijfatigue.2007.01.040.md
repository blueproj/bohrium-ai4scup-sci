# Crack growth in a coated gas turbine superalloy under thermo-mechanical fatigue  

Arnd Jung   \* , Alexander Schnell  

Alstom (Switzerland) Ltd., Materials and Chemistry, Brown Boveri Straße 7, CH-5401 Baden, Switzerland  

Accepted 15 January 2007 Available online 20 March 2007  

# Abstract  

The out-of-phase thermo-mechanical fatigue (TMF) behaviour of the single crystal superalloy CMSX-4 was studied in the uncoated and in the low-pressure plasma sprayed (LPPS) coated condition. In addition, growth measurements of in-depth coating cracks were performed. Cyclic hardening of the substrate and superimposed multiple crack initiation/propagation within the coating leads to cyclic softening that consumes a signiﬁcant portion of TMF lifetime. A three-step crack propagation mode of the coated material is described. Special attention is paid to the role of the inter-diﬀusion zone, since coating cracks are decelerated in the inter-diﬀusion zone before pene- trating into the substrate.

# 1. Introduction  

The major failure cause in current single crystal blades and vanes of industrial gas turbines is thermally induced strains caused by temperature gradients across the wall thickness. The actual deformation behaviour of coated superalloys is closely simulated by thermo-mechanical fati- gue (TMF) tests which reproduce the temperature and strain cycles experienced by critical volume elements of a component. In contrast to isothermal fatigue tests, the coating is repeatedly cycled through the ductile–brittle transition temperature (DBTT) during TMF loading, resulting in speciﬁc damage mechanisms. Furthermore, dif- ferences in temperature dependent Young’s modulus and thermal expansion of the coating and the substrate, respec- tively, give rise to additional stresses which cannot be reproduced in isothermal fatigue tests.  

For reﬁning lifetime prediction methods of overlay coated gas turbine components, the failure mechanisms under thermo-mechanical fatigue (i.e. the crack initiation and the crack propagation), have to be understood. This facilitates the selection of the suitable chemistry of a coat- ing and the coating process (e.g. low-pressure plasma spraying or high velocity oxygen fuel) since each overlay coating is designed to meet the demands of a given applica- tion. Subsequent ranking of coatings for optimum lifetime should be based on both oxidation/corrosion resistance, and on resistance against thermo-mechanically induced cracking.  

# 2. Experimental details  

# 2.1. Material  

The single crystal superalloy CMSX-4 was obtained in the form of cast slabs. Smooth cylindrical specimens with a diameter of 7 mm and a gauge length of 18 mm were taken from these castings in the [001] crystallographic ori- entation with less than 5   deviation. The chemical compo- sition of the substrate is given in  Table 1 . The specimens were coated over their gauge length by low-pressure plasma spraying (LPPS) with a NiCoCrAlY-alloy containing 30%

Co, designated as 30Co. The coating material was ﬁne- grained with a grain size of 1–10  l m and was applied with a nominal thickness of 300  l m and 600  l m. Both types of specimen, coated and non-coated substrate material, were heat treated (standard heat treatment, 1140   C/ 6 h + 870   C/20 h). As a result of the heat treatment the coating microstructure consists of a  c / b  matrix including c 0   precipitates. No ﬁnal surface preparation was carried out. Thus, the samples were tested in the as-coated (sprayed + heat treated) condition.  

The TMF tests were performed on a closed loop servo- hydraulic testing system that is equipped with a 200 kHz induction coil heating element. The cooling was achieved by the heat ﬂux into water-cooled hydraulic specimen grips. A high temperature axial extensometer was attached to the substrate surface, even in the case of coated samples. A triangular command signal was used for both, mechani- cal strain and temperature resulting in a constant mechan- ical strain rate of  _ mech  ¼  6    10  5   s  1 . Test temperature was controlled with PtRh–Pt thermocouples wound around the circumference of the specimen. Mechanical strain ranges  D e mech  of 0.6% up to 0.9% were applied with a maximum value of  e mech = 0 ( R e mech  =  1 ). Hence, the mechanical strain was in compression during the entire TMF cycle. A dwell time of 5 min was introduced at the minimum mechanical strain. The TMF tests were always started at zero mechanical strain at the minimum tempera- ture, and continued with an out-of-phase (OP) relation- ship, i.e. maximum mechanical strain coinciding with the minimum temperature. Two maximum cycle temperatures ( T max  1000   C and 800   C), and two minimum tempera- tures ( T min  100   C and 400   C) were studied. Five reference cycles were carried out at zero stress in order to measure the thermal strain  e th  as a function of temperature. The mechanical strain was determined using the well-known equation  e mech  =  e t    e th , where  e t  is the total strain. The substrate cross section was taken into account when calcu- lating stresses of the coated specimens. Most of the tests were stopped when the maximum stress dropped below 50% of the stabilised or maximum value. Microstructure and failure mechanisms were investigated mainly using light and scanning electron microscopy.  

# 2.2. Non-destructive crack depth measurement method  

In a number of studies, the fatigue crack initiation and propagation were studied by optical imaging  [1] , acetate ﬁlm crack replica  [2]  or optical analysis in connection with acoustic emission  [3] . These methods allow an analysis of the length of the surface cracks but do not give a detailed information about the more relevant depth of cracks.  

Hence, the present work aims to provide a method to mon- itor the crack initiation phase and subsequent crack prop- agation into the depth of a coating. An eddy current (EC) technique was applied to measure the crack depth. A triple probe (KAS-3H1680) was specially designed, on which the single probes are displaced by 90   in relation to each other. The triple probe was ﬁxed into a device maintaining a con- stant distance and a normal orientation to the coating surface.  

After deﬁned TMF cycle intervals the triple probe was moved longitudinally along the sample axis measuring the movement by a micrometer and c irc um fe rent i ally within the gauge length in order to scan the whole coated surface of the sample. The measurement was done at room temperature without removing the extensometer after ter- minating the TMF test at zero stress. The depth of the crack detected by EC has been estimated comparing the maximum amplitude or the phase of the EC signal with those of notches machined into a calibration sample of the same substrate material and coating. Up to three single cracks were monitored with respect to their length after several predeﬁned intervals of TMF cycles.  

# 2.3. The software package DICTRA  

The formation of the inter-diﬀusion zone for the CMSX-4/30Co coating system during the standard heat treatment has been modelled using the software package DICTRA, version 23  [4] . DICTRA (Diﬀusion Controlled Transformations) is a one-dimensional ﬁnite-diﬀerence dif- fusion code, which numerically solves the diﬀusion equa- tion for multi-component systems  [5] . The DICTRA software is interfaced with the software package Thermo-Calc (version Q), which provides all necessary thermodynamic data. In the present study Saunders  [6] thermodynamic NI-DATA database for Nickel-based superalloys has been used. The validated diﬀusion data for the present system, which are linked to the DICTRA software, are stored in a corresponding kinetic database [7] . Consequently, the DICTRA software package gives the possibility to compute the concentration proﬁles and the resulting phase fractions across the substrate/coating interface for any user-deﬁned temperature/time condition.

# 4. Discussion  

During thermo-mechanical fatigue, diﬀerent failure mechanisms operate below and above the DBTT of the coating. Kowalewski et al.  [8]  have found that for a  c / c 0 LPPS coating, dislocation glide controls the deformation behaviour below the DBTT, whereas above the DBTT deformation takes place without any obvious change in dis- location density and grain aspect ratio, which indicates superplasticity. Nevertheless, the DBTT has to be deter- mined as a function of temperature rate and is only a semi-quantitative criterion since TMF behaviour is not predictable only by the ductility as a function of the tem- perature. Czech et al.  [9]  have shown for NiCoCrAlY coat- ings that the addition of Rhenium did not change DBTT but did signiﬁcantly inﬂuence thermo-mechanical fatigue resistance.  

Since the strain of the TMF tests was always controlled at the substrate surface, diﬀerences in the stress–strain behaviour between coated and uncoated specimens can directly be attributed to the coating. During cooling in a TMF cycle, the coating gains signiﬁcant strength and takes over thermal mismatch stresses due to the diﬀerent Young’s moduli and thermal expansion coeﬃcient of coating and substrate material, which lead to high tensile stresses in the coating. Hence, the tensile stresses in the coating are higher than the measured integral stress responses of the coated substrate. Applying a simple iso- strain composite model (in which coating and substrate are combined in parallel with respect to the total strain, see  [10] ) the stress response of the coating can be predicted. Residual stress measurements by Sequeira et al.  [11]  have conﬁrmed high tensile stresses at low temperatures at the coating surface that are still present after TMF loading.  

After reaching a peak stress due to cyclic hardening, the drop in tensile stress is typically a slow process, consuming a signiﬁcant portion of the cyclic life. As the crack depth is small, the inﬂuence on the sample stiﬀness is negligible and cyclic hardening dominates. With each additional crack, the stiﬀness is reduced by a small amount, leading to the observed slow but continuous reduction in maximum stress. As a result, a large density of cracks evolves before few dominant cracks manage to propagate into the sub- strate material. One of those substrate material cracks will outrun the others and will ﬁnally lead to a sharp drop in stress as observed for the uncoated specimen. Therefore, lifetime determination by just a ﬁxed drop in stress crite- rion, which is usually used for uncoated base materials, is not necessarily applicable to coated specimens. A detailed understanding of the coating crack propagation behaviour is required.  

Fatigue crack growth dominates at low temperatures when the coating is brittle and loaded by tensile stresses. The three stages of crack propagation can be correlated with the microstructure of the CMSX-4 + 30Co (see Fig. 8 ). In the ﬁrst stage, a certain number of TMF cycles is needed to establish a coating surface roughening (rum- pling) where crack initiation takes place in the respective valleys (see  Fig. 8 ). Rumpling of the free coating surface is a prerequisite for subsequent fatigue crack growth in the valleys. It has been observed under either thermo- mechanical fatigue  [12]  or only thermal cycling  [13]  that, beside thermal mismatch stresses, mechanical straining is not necessarily a pre-condition for rumpling.  

In the second stage, during subsequent fast crack prop- agation, the cracks continue to grow perpendicular to the main stress axis and no crack deﬂection at the  b  phase in the  c / b  coating was observed. The deceleration in propaga- tion of the longest coating cracks during stage III is related to the inter-diﬀusion zone, especially to the formation of a c  layer. The formation of the distinctive  c  layer at the inter- face is due to the local uphill diﬀusion of Cr, which signif- icantly increases the solubility of Aluminum in the Nickel matrix. This in turn decreases the stability of the  c 0   phase in that particular region.  

It appears that the observed interface layer is capable of decelerating a continuously growing crack. It is assumed, that the ductile  c  layer absorbs a part of the energy by a sig- niﬁcant degree of local plastiﬁcation around the crack tip. A portion of the mismatch stress will be compensated by the ductile  c  layer. The resulting stress state in the  c  layer may favor the cracks to branch, see  Fig. 9 . The increased  c 0 fraction in the CMSX-4 directly adjacent to the inter-diﬀu- sion zone may contribute additionally to the crack branching since the crack is deﬂected in this particular region. The gen- eration of these additional crack surfaces by crack branching requires additional energy which is considered to contribute to the deceleration of the crack growth. As soon as the crack penetrates into the substrate (nominal  c 0   fraction), only the substrate stress is applied to the crack, i.e. the crack propaga- tion is controlled by the substrate.  

Further DICTRA simulations revealed, that during the actual TMF testing of coated CMSX-4 only a negligible inter-diﬀusion between the substrate and the 30Co coating occurs. The dwell (testing) times and the applied maximum temperatures are too short and low for signiﬁcant diﬀu- sion-controlled microstructural changes at the interface.  

# 5. Conclusions  

The growth of established coating cracks under thermo- mechanical fatigue can be monitored by an eddy current technique. A three-step crack propagation mode slow– fast–slow can be derived and correlated with microstruc- tural observations.  

The formation and consequently the appearance of the inter-diﬀusion zone is mainly controlled by the 1140   C/ 6 h standard heat treatment. This understanding allows the pro-active modelling and computation of the inter-dif- fusion zone. The inter-diﬀusion zone appearance with spe- ciﬁc regard to the TMF behaviour can be included in the coating selection considerations. A continuous layer of brittle phases such as topological closed packaged (TCP) or  b  phases have to be avoided.
