The mechanism by which mammalian liver cell responses are coordinated  during tissue homeostasis and perturbation is poorly understood,  representing a major obstacle in our understanding of many diseases.  This knowledge gap is caused by the difficulty involved with studying  multiple cell types in different states and locations, particularly when these  are transient. We have combined Stereo-seq (spatiotemporal enhanced  resolution omics-sequencing) with single-cell transcriptomic profiling  of 473,290 cells to generate a high-definition spatiotemporal atlas of  mouse liver homeostasis and regeneration at the whole-lobe scale. Our  integrative study dissects in detail the molecular gradients controlling liver  cell function, systematically defining how gene networks are dynamically  modulated through intercellular communication to promote regeneration.  Among other important regulators, we identified the transcriptional  cofactor TBL1XR1 as a rheostat linking inflammation to Wnt/β-catenin  signaling for facilitating hepatocyte proliferation. Our data and analytical  pipelines lay the foundation for future high-definition tissue-scale atlases of  organ physiology and malfunction.

This creates a physiological phe- nomenon termed zonation consisting of the tightly organized and  intertwined division of labor for all cell types along the porto-central  axis. Another major characteristic is that hepatocytes have a remark- able capacity to repopulate the injured liver 3 , . Liver regeneration is  a highly orchestrated process dependent on intercellular communi- cation that restores liver mass to the pre-injury level with surprising  fidelity. Understanding the mechanisms controlling liver zonation and  proliferation holds clues for regenerative medicine and for clarifying  the roadmap to many diseases 4 .  

Single-cell omics and spatially resolved transcriptomic technolo- gies are providing relevant insights into liver zonation and regen- eration 1 , , – 9 . However, the former suffers from the loss of topographic  information and the stress caused by dissociation, and the latter is  limited by the imbalance between gene capture, resolution and the  field-of-view of current methodologies. Here, we have combined spa- tiotemporal enhanced resolution omics-sequencing (Stereo-seq),  a recently developed high-resolution, high-sensitivity and large  field-of-view whole transcriptome capture method 10  with an integrated  single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) dataset to investigate mouse  liver homeostasis and regeneration upon partial (2/3) hepatectomy  (PHx) with high definition and at the whole-lobe scale.  

# Results Spatiotemporal atlas of the mouse liver  

To dissect the spatiotemporal dynamics of liver homeostasis and regen- eration with high resolution, sensitivity and at the whole-lobe scale,  we applied Stereo-seq 10  to a mouse model of PHx 11 . We collected 30  whole liver lobe sections from day 0 (D0), the priming stage (8 h), the  proliferation stage (days 1–3 (D1, D2, D3)) and the termination stage  (day 7, (D7)) 4 . The cumulative area was 11.22 cm 2 , which was divided  into 878,334 non-overlapping bins (Supplementary Table 1a). Each  bin contained 50 × 50 DNA nanoballs, with a center-to-center distance  of 715 nm between each nanoball; thus, each bin had a resolution of  36 × 36 μm. Additionally, we performed matched scRNA-seq, which  yielded 435,413 cells (Supplementary Table 1b). We also retrieved  previously reported NPC scRNA-seq datasets 12 ,  to generate a wider  homeostatic liver cell matrix of 51,275 cells. This captured all main liver  resident cell types, which could then be mapped on the Stereo-seq  slides using RCTD 14  (Fig.  1a , Extended Data Fig. 1a and Supplementary  Table 2a). Immuno fluorescence for the periportal marker E-cadherin  correlated well with periportal hepatocytes defined by Stereo-seq  (Fig.  1a  and Extended Data Fig. 1b).

# Zonated genes in homeostatic hepatocytes  

We searched all genes displaying non-random spatial distribution  among zonation layers in the homeostatic Stereo-seq slides using a  Kruskal–Wallis test. This identified 8,127 total genes ( Q  < 0.05), from  which we selected hepatocyte-specific genes in the homeostatic liver  cell matrix (log 2 (fold change) > 0.25). This yielded 2,456 genes, of which  around half (52.1%) overlapped with the scRNA-seq-based zonation  reconstruction from a previous study 1  (Fig.  1d,e , Extended Data Fig. 3a  and Supplementary Table 3a).  

Among the newly identified zonated genes, we observed peri- central enrichment of the hepatitis B virus binding receptor  Slc10a1   (NTCP), correlating with pericentral vulnerability to hepatitis B virus in  humans 17 , and periportal enrichment of the unfolded protein response  modulator  Xbp1  (Fig.  1f ). Moreover, we detected midzonal enrichment  of the T3 thyroid hormone receptor  Thrb . T3 has more potent mitogenic  activity in this area 18 . Single-molecule fluorescence in situ hybridization  (smFISH) for  Thrb  and immuno fluorescence for NTCP confirmed these  results (Fig.  1g  and Extended Data Fig. 3b). Likewise, the EGFR ligand  Fgl1  (hepassocin) 19  was also enriched in the midzonal layers. In this  regard, homeostatic hepatocyte proliferation is mainly midzonal 3 , .  Functional analysis for all hepatocyte zonated genes identified known  and unknown zonated pathways (Fig.  1h,i  and Extended Data Fig. 3c–g;  see Supplementary notes). For example, we observed autophagy in the  pericentral area, which was confirmed by immuno fluorescence for LC3  and LAMP2 (Fig.  1j  and Extended Data Fig. 3h).  

We then studied the gene regulatory networks (GRNs) control- ling liver cell identity by applying SCENIC 21  to the Stereo-seq slides.  This identified 307 regulons, of which 172 were in hepatocytes (90  zonated; Kruskal–Wallis test,  Q  < 0.05) (Fig.  1k,l , Extended Data Fig. 3i  and Supplementary Table 4). In addition to pericentral and periportal  enriched regulons associated with metabolic activities (see Supple- mentary notes), we identified midzonal-enriched regulons such as  TBL1XR1 and CEBPA. # Zonated genes in homeostatic NPCs and non-zonated  structures  

A Stereo-seq bin 50 can include several NPCs because they are smaller  than hepatocytes (Extended Data Fig. 4a). We calculated the zonal  distribution of NPCs on the Stereo-seq slides (Extended Data Fig. 4b,c).  In addition to the expected periportal distribution of cholangiocytes  and Kupffer cells 26 , immune cells had a variable localization and hepatic  stellate cells (HSCs) were more abundant pericentrally.  

We next focused on studying zonated genes for liver sinusoidal  endothelial cells (LSECs) and HSCs. We extracted LSEC-specific and  HSC-specific genes from the homeostatic liver cell matrix (log 2 (fold  change) > 1) and intersected them with the global list of zonated  genes. This yielded 636 and 1,158 zonated genes for LSECs and  HSCs, respectively (Supplementary Table 3b,c). Among the LSEC  genes (Fig.  2a–c ), 132 (\~20.8%) overlapped with the zonation profile  described by paired-cell sequencing 2 , including the pericentral  Wnt2 the periportal  Dll4  and the midzonal  Lyve1 . Among the unknown  genes, we noticed the angiopoietin receptor  Tie2  ( Tek ) and the tran- scription factor  Gata4 , both of which regulate Wnt cytokine expres- sion 27 , , in the pericentral area. Functional analysis identified zonated  pathways such as neuropilin interactions with VEGF and VEGFR path- way in the pericentral area and regulation of Notch signaling pathway  in the periportal area (Fig.  2a,d,e  and Extended Data Fig. 4d,e). By  contrast, the Notch inhibitor  Egfl7  (ref.  29 ) was moderately enriched in  the pericentral layers, which was confirmed using smFISH (Extended  Data Fig. 4f). Analysis of HSC zonated genes showed 54 overlap- ping with those found in a previous study 13  (Fig.  2f–h ), including  the pericentral  Rspo3  and the periportal  Ngfr , and 1,104 unknown  genes. Functional analysis also identified zonated pathways such  as pericentral BMP signaling and Wnt signaling pathways (Fig.  2f,i,j and Extended Data Fig. 4g,h), whereas genes related to cell matrix  adhesion and chemotaxis were enriched periportally. Although most  BMP signaling components were enriched pericentrally,  Bmp4  was  reduced in the midzonal area, which we validated by smFISH (Fig.  2k ).

# Zonated cell–cell communication networks in homeostasis  

Ligand–receptor interactions within the liver lobule are poorly under- stood. We took ligand–receptor pairs from CellTalkDB 32  and predicted  their interaction score based on the product of the expression of ligands  multiplied by that of the corresponding receptors. This showed 1,140  interactions (interaction score of >10 −5  in any layer), out of which 686  were zonated (Kruskal–Wallis test,  Q  < 0.1; Supplementary Table 6).  We also measured the overall communication probabilities among cell  types in the single-cell dataset using CellChat 33 . Putative interactions  were more abundant in HSCs and fibroblasts (Extended Data Fig. 6a).  

We observed known Wnt-related pericentral and Notch-related  periportal interactions 2 ,  (Fig.  3a,b  and Extended Data Fig. 6b). Among  the unknown zonated interactions, we detected interactions involving  the periportal non-canonical Wnt factors WNT4 and WNT5B. We also  detected  Egfl7  interacting with  Notch1  in pericentral LSECs. Given that  inhibition of Notch promotes Wnt factor secretion 35 , it is conceivable  that  Egfl7  modulates Wnt signaling. The interaction between  Bmp10 and  Acvrl1  (ALK1) in Kupffer cells was enriched in the midzonal area.  BMP10–ALK1 promotes Kupffer cell self-renewal 7 , . The interaction  of  Bmp4  with  Bmpr1a  was enriched in the pericentral and periportal  layers. Interactions between  Fgf1/2/21  and  Fgfr1  were more enriched in  periportal hepatocytes (Fig.  3a,c  and Extended Data Fig. 6b). Periportal  enrichment of  Fgfr1  and  Wnt4  was confirmed by smFISH (Fig.  3d  and  Extended Data Fig. 6c).

# Cell proliferation kinetics during regeneration  

We first analyzed cell proliferation using the scRNA-seq dataset  (Fig.  4a,b , Extended Data Fig. 7a and Supplementary Table 2c). The  pan-cycling marker  Mki67  was expressed in a cluster of hepatocytes at  D1, increasing remarkably at D2 (35.2% of all hepatocytes, 32.5% of all  cells) and then declining. LSEC proliferation appeared at D2 and peaked  at D3 (15.4% of all LSECs, 0.49% of all cells). Low levels of proliferation  were observed in immune cells.  

Before measuring proliferation in the Stereo-seq slides, we exam- ined whether the layer distribution remains stable throughout the  regeneration process. This was critical because otherwise mapping  dynamic molecular events across time and space could be problematic.  Using the same algorithm defined to study homeostasis, we verified  the maintenance of polarized zonation patterns after PHx (Fig.  4c,d and Extended Data Fig. 7b,c). Specific genes ( Cdh1 ) commonly used  to define zonal boundaries 3 ,  changed expression levels and zonal  distribution, but the overall porto-central gradient remained constant  (Fig.  4e  and Extended Data Fig. 7b,d). Principal component analysis of  pseudobulk values corroborated layer maintenance (Fig.  4f ). It also  showed that the largest transcriptomic changes occurred at 8 h, with  a more severe disruption (mostly downregulation) in pericentral and  midzonal genes (Extended Data Fig. 7e), whereas at D7 values returned  to a near-homeostatic steady state. This indicates a transient shift  towards a relative increased periportal gradient, as observed in the  regenerating human liver 38 .

We plotted  Mki67  onto the Stereo-seq maps (Fig.  4g  and Extended  Data Fig. 7f).  Mki67  appearance kinetics correlated with that of hepato- cytes in the scRNA-seq.  Mki67  signal at D2 was stronger in the midzonal  area, consistent with recent reports 3 , , but the pericentral and peripor- tal areas also displayed substantial levels (Fig.  4h ). We then analyzed  the kinetics of cell cycle phases by assessing a panel of related genes  (Fig.  4i,j  and Extended Data Fig. 7g). The S-phase score was higher in  the midzonal layers at D1, followed by an overall induction to a higher  level at D2 but with a peak in the pericentral layers. The G2/M-phase  score peaked in the midzonal layers at D2. These results suggest that  proliferation initiates in the midzonal layers. We detected a comparable  distribution of the S-phase and G2/M-phase scores among different  fields-of-view of individual sections (Extended Data Fig. 7h). Previous work pro- posed that proliferation in the innermost pericentral hepatocyte area  is disproportionately lower than other areas 3 .  

Zonated transcriptomic responses during regeneration We studied the topography of cell-specific transcriptomic responses  upon PHx, first focusing on hepatocytes. Analysis of the liver regenera- tion cell matrix identified 4,310 hypervariable genes, 92.8% of them  were zonated (Kruskal–Wallis test,  Q  < 0.05 at any time point), which  were divided into seven clusters (Fig.  5a  and Supplementary Table 7a)  

Functional enrichment showed two major waves of metabolic  gene modulation (Fig.  5b,c  and Extended Data Fig. 8a,b). The first wave  was a reduction of genes related to body homeostasis that started at  8 h and was partially restored from D3 to D7 (cluster 1). Genes in the  second wave were related to energy production (clusters 2 and 3);  these partially decreased at 8 h and became activated from D1 onwards.  They included pericentral fatty acid β-oxidation, periportal gluco - neogenesis and mitochondrial electron transport chain. Similar  results were observed when mapping specific KEGG metabolic func- tions grouped as scores on the Stereo-seq sections (Extended Data  Fig. 8c,d and Supplementary Table 8). Upregulation of lipid metabolism  genes at D1 is consistent with lipids being the major source of energy

Increased genes were divided  into a sustained activation group from 8 h until D7 (cluster 2) and an  8 h-transient activation group (cluster 3). The former contained genes  related to Wnt signaling ( Wnt2 ) in the pericentral area and EGFR signal- ing ( Hbegf ) in the midzonal and periportal areas. The latter contained  pericentral genes related to shear stress ( Klf2 ) and eNOS activation. # Dynamics of cell–cell interactions during regeneration  

To investigate the spatiotemporal dynamics of intercellular commu- nication, we calculated the ligand–receptor interaction score across  time points. This yielded 1,487 interactions (interaction score > 10 –5 in any layer), 75% of which were zonated (Kruskal–Wallis test,  Q  < 0.1  in any time point) (Fig.  6a  and Supplementary Table 6). We mapped  the expression of ligands and receptors in the liver regeneration cell  matrix (Fig.  6b ), observing that increased interactions at 8 h were  mostly related to immune cells.  

We studied the zonal distribution of a panel of ligand–recep- tor pairs with known and unknown roles in hepatocyte proliferation  (Fig.  6c,d  and Extended Data Fig. 9a,b). The pericentral interaction  of  Wnt2 ,  Wnt9b  and  Rspo3 , contributed by LSECs and HSCs with their  corresponding receptors, increased with a broader range that reached  the periportal area. This was more pronounced at D1 but higher than  homeostatic levels remained throughout the regeneration process,  correlating with the persistent Wnt/β-catenin target gene activation  until D7. The interaction of non-canonical  Wnt4  and  Wnt5a  with their  receptors increased mostly at D1 with periportal and pericentral  dominance, respectively. Other interactions involved components  of mitogenic pathways such as IL6, EGF, FGF, HGF and Hedgehog, and  anti-mitogenic signals like the BMP–BMPR1A axis, activin and IL1. Mito- genic pathways were mainly activated in the midzonal and periportal  areas, albeit at different time points of regeneration. We validated  the induction of  Fgfr1  by smFISH (Extended Data Fig. 9c). HGF sign- aling in hepatocytes involves ECM storage of HGF and activation by  the plasminogen activators tPA ( Plat ) and uPA ( Plau ) 4 , . At 8 h,  Plat increased in the midzonal area, whereas the plasminogen activator  inhibitors  Serpine1  and  Serpine2  (ref.  50 ), and the HGFR antagonist  Tac1 (ref.  51 ), increased in the pericentral and midzonal areas (Extended  Data Fig. 9d). Moreover, we detected enhanced antagonistic  Ihh–Hhip signals pericentrally. Among the interactions inhibiting regeneration,  we noticed a pericentral increase of  Bmp2 – Bmpr1a 52  from 8 h to D3,  whereas  Inhba –/ Inhbc –/ Inhbe – Acvr2a / Acvr2b 53 ,  and  Il1a / b – Il1rap interactions were globally depleted at 8 h and recovered in the peri- central or periportal areas at D7.  

We also investigated the incoming signals of LSECs (Fig.  6c,d and Extended Data Fig. 9a,b). We found enhanced interaction of  Mif and  Cxcl12  (encoding SDF-1), with  Ackr3  (encoding CXCR7) across the  lobule but more obviously in the pericentral area. SDF-1 interacting  with CXCR7 induces WNT2 and HGF 55 , but the zonal distribution and  kinetics upon PHx had not been investigated. Pericentral induction  of  Ackr3  in endothelial cells at 8 h post PHx was validated by smFISH  (Fig.  6e ). In addition, we observed that the  Dll4–Notch  interaction  decreased at 8 h and recovered steadily from D3, whereas  Egfl7 – Notch1 increased from D2, especially in the pericentral and midzonal areas.  Similarly, the interaction of  Lect2  expressed by hepatocytes with the  angiopoietin receptor  Tie1  increased from D1 onward with pericentral  dominance. LECT2 prevents TIE1 phosphorylation and the subsequent  dimerization with TIE2, which is known to induce Wnt factor secretion  in other settings 28 , . Moreover, we detected enhanced angiogenic  interactions ( Angpt2 / 4–Tek, Vegfa/c–Kdr ) at D3 and D7 with periportal  dominance, correlating with the peak of LSEC proliferation (Fig.  4b ).  Angpt2  promotes angiogenesis in the late stages of liver regeneration  upon PHx 57 .

# Spatiotemporal GRNs during regeneration  

We next studied the GRNs controlling regeneration, observing a striking  change in regulons accompanying the transcriptional changes at 8 h  (Fig.  7a,b , Extended Data Fig. 10a and Supplementary Table 4).  

Among the downregulated regulons at 8 h (Fig.  7a ), we detected  midzonal CEBPA, consistent with a previous report 25 , and several  metabolic regulons (HLF). Concomitantly, epithelial–mesenchymal  transition-related regulons such as SOX5, ZEB1 and RREB1 58 ,  were  activated, consistent with a partial de-differentiation at the onset of  liver regeneration 41 . As for the upregulated regulons at 8 h, although  most were transient, some extended until D3 (Fig.  7a,b  and Extended  Data Fig. 10a; see Supplementary notes). As expected, they included  transcription factors involved in the reparative inflammatory response  (EGR1, JUN) and others like TBL1XR1. These regulons were enriched in  all zones, in some cases with modest periportal preference. At D1 and  D2, Wnt-related regulons were upregulated throughout the lobule with  pericentral dominance. At D2, almost all enriched regulons were related  to mitosis. At D7, metabolic regulons recovered to near-homeostatic  levels, supporting a progressive restoration of downregulated liver  functions including metabolism.  

# TBL1XR1 modulates liver regeneration  

We focused on TBL1XR1. Stereo-seq, scRNA-seq and immunofluores- cence confirmed its activation in hepatocytes (Fig.  7c  and Extended  Data Fig. 10b,c). Analysis of an scRNA-seq dataset for mouse hepat- ocytes cultured in vitro 60  showed that  Tbl1xr1  is induced by TNFα  (Extended Data Fig. 10d,e). Supporting this observation, TNFα neu- tralization before performing PHx blocked the induction of TBL1XR1  at 8 h (Fig.  7d,e ). In addition, we noticed that  Tbl1xr1  is a downstream  target of inflammatory 8 h regulons including EGR1, JUN and CEBPD  (Extended Data Fig. 10f), and their motifs were found at the  Tbl1xr1 open chromatin locus in a study using mouse homeostatic liver in an  assay for transposase-accessible chromatin sequencing (ATAC-seq)  dataset 61  (Extended Data Fig. 10g).  

We performed in vivo shRNA knockdown of  Tbl1xr1  using  adeno-associated viruses specifically targeting hepatocytes (Extended  Data Fig. 10h). At 4 weeks post infection, we performed PHx and  extracted the liver at D2. Immuno fluorescence and RT–qPCR showed  efficient  Tbl1xr1  knockdown as well as a reduction of cell cycle-related  genes and induction of the cyclin-dependent kinase inhibitor  Cdkn1a (p21) compared to the control (Fig.  7f,g ). The proportion of Ki67 + hepatocytes, assessed by immuno fluorescence, was lower in the  Tbl1xr1  knockdown (20.9%) than in the control (38.9%) (Fig.  7h,i ).  However, hepatocyte size was enlarged and the total liver mass mod- erately increased upon  Tbl1xr1  knockdown, indicating compensatory  hypertrophy (Fig.  7h  and Extended Data Fig. 10i). The phenotype was  maintained, albeit to a lesser extent, at D7 (Extended Data Fig. 10j–l).  

This compensatory effect is in line with the effects of  Cdk1  knockout 62 or the perturbation of other factors relevant for liver regeneration,  such as β-catenin and TNFα 63 – 69 .  

Bulk RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) demonstrated that  Tbl1xr1 knockdown not only led to reduced cell cycle genes but also impaired  fatty acid β-oxidation (Fig.  7j,k ). This was validated by RT–qPCR  (Extended Data Fig. 10m). Moreover, Oil Red O staining showed  diminished lipid droplets upon  Tbl1xr1  knockdown at D2 post PHx  (Extended Data Fig. 10n). Interestingly, we also observed a reduc- tion of Wnt pathway genes in  Tbl1xr1  knockdown livers (Fig.  7j  and  Extended Data Fig. 10m). Given that Wnt signaling regulates liver  fatty acid β-oxidation, this finding suggests abnormal energy produc- tion upon  Tbl1xr1  knockdown 70 , . TBL1XR1 partners with β-catenin  to activate oncogenic growth-related genes in colorectal cancer by  removing the NCoR co-repressor complex 72  (Extended Data Fig. 10o).  We reanalyzed previous chromatin immuno precipitation sequenc- ing (ChIP-seq) datasets 73 – 75 . NCoR binding sites in the homeostatic  liver were co-bound by the β-catenin effector T cell factor 4 (TCF4)  in  Apc −/−  liver, whereas histone H3 lysine 27 acetylation (H3K27ac)  levels increased at 40 h post PHx at the same loci. These loci included  multiple genes related to cell cycle, Wnt signaling and fatty acid  β-oxidation downregulated by  Tbl1xr1  knockdown (Fig.  7l–n ). These  results suggest a model in which cooperative binding of TBL1XR1  and β-catenin at key loci facilitates the activation of the hepatocyte  proliferation program by removing the NCoR co-repressor complex  and promoting histone acetylation. Understanding the detailed  mechanisms will require further investigation, such as using condi- tional knockout models.  

# Discussion  

We have generated a comprehensive spatiotemporal atlas of the  homeostatic and regenerating mammalian liver by combining spa- tially resolved transcriptomic information generated by Stereo-seq and  high-throughput scRNA-seq data. Our atlas constitutes a large-scale  resource for generating hypotheses for further functional validation.  It can be interactively explored using the Liver Spatio-Temporal Atlas  (LISTA) portal ( https://db.cngb.org/stomics/lista ).  

Not only have we captured thousands of previously unnoticed  zonated genes but also propose putative models explaining how they  are regulated (Fig.  8a ). For example, the periportal enrichment of  Fgfr1 may explain why FGF has a stronger ameliorating effect on fatty liver in  this area 76 , highlighting the importance of considering zonal heteroge- neity when designing rational therapies for liver disease. In addition,  we identified previously unnoticed interactions potentially mediating  immune zonation and described the existence of immune spots. Similar  structures have been reported in inflamed skin and human colorectal  cancer 77 , . In addition, we have dissected the spatiotemporal responses  of hepatocytes and NPCs during liver regeneration (Fig.  8b ). This led  to the identification of TBL1XR1 as an inflammation-induced rheostat  that facilitates hepatocyte proliferation. Manipulation of TBL1XR1

# Methods Ethical statement  

All animal experiments were carried out in accordance with ethical reg- ulations regarding animal research and were approved by the Animal  Care and Use Committee of the Guangzhou Institutes of Biomedicine  and Health under license number IACUC2018038.  

# Animal study  

Specific-pathogen-free grade mice (male C57BL/6J) were bred and  housed in a temperature-controlled and light-controlled (12 h light– dark cycle) animal facility.  

PHx .  For the PHx procedure, 12-week-old mice were subjected to 2/3  liver mass resection 11 . The procedure was performed from 09:00– 12:00 h. In brief, animals were anesthetized using 2% isofluorane (RWD,  R510-22) in an induction chamber and transferred to the operating pad  with anesthesia by isofluorane inhalation. A midline skin and muscle  incision was performed and retractors were used to hold the peritoneal  cavity open. A knot was made using a silk thread in the proximity of  the base of both the median and the left lateral liver lobes. Once the  knots were tight and the blood flow was interrupted, both lobes were  cut using a surgical blade. After this procedure, both the peritoneum  and abdomen were sutured, and the animals were placed on a warm- ing pad until physical activity was resumed. Following recovery, mice  were transferred to their cages and killed at the indicated time points  after resection. Untreated mice (D0) were used as baseline controls.  

In vivo  Tbl1xr1  knockdown .  To perform  Tbl1xr1  knockdown, 8-week-old  mice were treated once with 3 × 10 11  viral particles of AAV8-TBG-  sh Tbl1xr1  (5′-GG AT GT CAC GTC TC TAG AT TG-3′) or AAV8-TBG-shNC  (5′-GAT CT GA TCG AC ACT GTA AT G-3′) diluted in 200 μl PBS through tail  vein injection. After 4 weeks, transduced mice were subjected to PHx.  Viral particles were purchased from PackGene Biotech (Guangzhou,  China).  

In vivo TNFα antibody neutralization .  For TNFα antibody neutraliza- tion, 12-week-old male mice were injected intra peritoneal ly with 200 μl  of PBS containing 250 μg isotype control (Leinco Technologies, I-536)  or 250 μg neutralizing antibody against TNFα (Leinco Technologies,  T-703). After 18 h, mice were subjected to PHx, followed by another  injection of 250 μg isotype control or 250 μg neutralizing antibody.  Mice were killed and the livers were dissected 8 h post PHx.  

# Stereo-seq library construction  

Fresh liver tissue was washed three times in PBS and then snap-frozen  in liquid nitrogen pre-chilled isopentane (Macklin, M875875-4L) in  Tissue-Tek OCT (Sakura, 4583). The tissue was then transferred to a  −80 °C freezer. Homeostatic sections were taken from the caudate  lobes and the regeneration sections from both the right and cau- date lobes. For Stereo-seq, 10 μm cryosections were adhered to the  Stereo-seq chip surface and incubated at 37 °C for 3 min. Tissues were  fixed in methanol (Sigma-Aldrich, 34860) at −20 °C for 40 min fol- lowed by 0.1% pepsin (Sigma-Aldrich, P7000) per me abi liz ation for  6 min at 37 °C in 0.01 M HCl buffer. Then, chips were washed twice with  0.1× SSC buffer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, AM9770) supplemented  with 0.05 U μl −1  RNase inhibitor (New England Biolabs, M0314L). After  per me abi liz ation, RNA released from tissues was captured in situ  by capture oligo-probes, after which reverse transcription was per- formed on the chip at 42 °C overnight using 10 U μl −1  SuperScript II  reverse transcriptase (Invitrogen, 18064), 1 mM dNTPs (Thermo,  18427088), 1 M betaine solution (Sigma-Aldrich, B0300), 7.5 mM MgCl 2 (Thermo, AM9530G), 5 mM DTT (Invitrogen, 707265ML), 2 U ml −1  RNase  inhibitor, 2.5 μM Stereo-seq-TSO and 1× First-Strand buffer (Thermo,  18064). After reverse transcription, tissues were treated with tissue  removal buffer (10 mM Tris-HCl (Invitrogen, AM9856), 50 mM EDTA  (Thermo, 15575020), 200 mM NaCl (Thermo, AM9759) and 1.5% SDS 

 (Thermo, AM9820) at 37 °C for 30 min. Next, cDNA release buffer 

 (10 mM Tris-HCl, 50 mM EDTA, 200 mM NaCl, 1.5% SDS and 40 U ml −1 proteinase K (Thermo, EO0491) was used to allow cDNA release from  the chip by incubating at 55 °C for 3 h. Finally, the cDNA was purified  from the mixture using 0.8× Ampure XP beads (Vazyme, N411-03). The  resulting cDNAs were amplified using KAPA HiFi Hotstart Ready Mix  (Roche, KK2602) and cDNA PCR primers with the following steps: 95 °C  for 5 min; 15 cycles of 98 °C for 20 s, 58 °C for 20 s, 72 °C for 3 min and  additional 72 °C for 5 min. The amplified cDNA was purified by 0.6×  Ampure XP beads and quantified using Qubit 3.0 with a Qubit dsDNA HS  Assay Kit (Thermofisher, Q32854). A total of 20 ng cDNA was incubated  in Tn5 fragment mix at 55 °C for 10 min and then 0.02% SDS buffer was  added to stop the fragmentation reaction. After this step, the purified  fragmented product was amplified using KAPA HiFi Hotstart Ready  Mix, Stereo-seq-library-F and Stereo-seq-library-R with the following  steps: 95 °C for 5 min; 13 cycles of 98 °C for 20 s, 58 °C for 20 s, 72 °C  for 30 s and additional 72 °C for 5 min. PCR products were then used  for sequencing library construction and loaded on a DIPSEQ T1 at the  China National GeneBank (CNGB) for sequencing with the following  read length: 35 bp for read 1 and 100 bp for read 2. At least two technical  replicates were performed on each mouse. All detailed information for  the Stereo-seq libraries is listed in Supplementary Table 1a.  

# ScRNA-seq library construction  

Single-cell isolation .  Single liver cells were isolated using a standard  two-step collagenase perfusion approach 40 . Mice were killed and placed  on a surgical pad, the abdominal cavity was opened and the hepatic  portal vein was cannulated using a needle. The liver was perfused  through the portal vein and blood flowed out via the inferior vena cava  with a prewarmed chelating solution (0.5 mM EGTA in HBSS without  CaCl 2  and MgCl 2 ) for 5 min, followed by prewarmed collagenase solu- tion (1.6 mM CaCl 2  and 0.1 mg ml −1  Collagenase IV (Sigma-Aldrich,  C5138)) until the liver became soft and transparent. All subsequent  procedures were performed on ice or at 4 °C. After perfusion, the  liver capsule was carefully removed and placed in PBS in a petri dish  to release the dissociated single cells. The cell suspension was filtered  through 100 μm, 70 μm and 40 μm cell strainers (BD FALCON, 352360,  352350, 352340). The collected cell suspension was centrifuged at 50 ×g for 3 min. The pellet containing the hepatocyte fraction was washed  twice with DMEM supplemented with 10% FBS (Cellcook, CM1002L) and  1× GlutaMax (Gibco, 35050-061), and then purified through 40% Percoll  (Sigma-Aldrich, P1644) gradient centrifugation at 100× g  for 10 min. The  supernatant containing the NPC fraction was first centrifuged twice  at 50× g  for 2 min to remove the remaining hepatocytes and then at  500× g  for 5 min at 4 °C for NPC collection. The NPC fraction was puri- fied using an annexin V-based dead cell removal kit (Miltenyi Biotec,  130-042-201). After purification, hepatocytes and NPCs were washed  twice using DMEM supplemented with 10% FBS and 1× GlutaMax. Cell  viability was checked by trypan blue staining, and only samples with  viability higher than 90% were used for scRNA-seq library construction.  Freshly isolated hepatocytes and NPCs were washed with 0.04% BSA  (Absin, abs49001013a) in PBS.  

Library construction .  After counting cell number and viability, the  cell suspensions were diluted to 1,000 cells per μl in PBS + 0.04% BSA.  The DNBelab C series HT scRNA library preparation set v.2.0 (MGI,  1000021082) was used according to the manufacturer’s instructions.  In brief, 20,000 hepatocytes or NPCs were mixed with an index carrier  to form a cell reaction solution. The solution, cell beads and oil were  loaded sequentially on the chip to generate single-cell droplets. After  20 min of mRNA hybridization with beads at 25 °C, beads were collected  for reverse transcription using the following program: 10 cycles of 50 °C  for 2 min then 42 °C for 2 min. Single-strand cDNA-containing beads  were purified for second-strand cDNA synthesis. cDNA-containing  beads were amplified using the following steps: 95 °C for 3 min; 16  cycles of 98 °C for 20 s, 60 °C for 30 s and 72 °C for 3 min; 72 °C for  10 min; heat-sealed at 105 °C. The amplified cDNA and barcoded oli- gonucleotide products were sorted and purified by AMPure XP beads.  Quality was assessed with an Agilent Bioanalyzer using an Agilent High  Sensitivity DNA kit (Agilent, 5067-4626). Libraries were sequenced in a  DIPSEQ T1 platform at the CNGB with the following read length: cDNA:  30 bp for read 1 and 100 bp for read 2; barcoded oli go nucleotides: 20 bp  for read 1 and 30 bp for read 2. All details for the scRNA-seq libraries  are in Supplementary Table 1b.  

# Immuno fluorescence, smFISH and histological staining  

For immuno fluorescence, paraffin-embedded sections (5 μm thick - ness) were dewaxed in xylene and subjected to heat-mediated antigen  retrieval (Beyotime, P0083) at 100 °C for 15 min. Then, sections were  blocked with 5% donkey serum (Jackson ImmunoResearch, 158130) and  5% goat serum (Beyotime, C0265) for 1 h. Cryosections (10 μm, for the  Tbl1xr1  knockdown experiment) were fixed in 4% para formaldehyde for  20 min, permeabilized and blocked in 0.3% Triton X-100 diluted with  blocking buffer for 1 h. Primary antibodies were incubated overnight  at 4 °C in blocking buffer. The next day, sections were first washed  three times with PBST and incubated with fluorescent-conjugated  secondary antibodies (Jackson ImmunoResearch, 711-165-152 and  715-545-150; 1:500) diluted in blocking buffer for 1 h at 25 °C. Finally,  0.25 μg ml −1  DAPI (Sigma-Aldrich, P9416) was used for counterstaining.  The following primary antibodies were used: GS (BD Transduction  Laboratories, 610518; 1:500), E-cadherin (BD Transduction Laborato- ries, 610181; 1:100), LC3 (Sigma-Aldrich, L7543; 1:100), LAMP2 (Abcam,  ab13524; 1:100), SLC10A1 (Abcam, ab131084; 1:100), CD3 (Abcam,  ab16669; 1:200), CCR2 (Abcam, ab273050; 1:200) and TBL1XR1 (Abcam,  ab190796; 1:100). They were incubated overnight at 4 °C in blocking  buffer. FITC-conjugated phalloidin (Invitrogen, A12379; 1:50) and  PE-conjugated Ki67 antibody (eBioscience, 12-5698-82; 1:50) were incu- bated for 1 h at 25 °C. For smFISH, paraffin-embedded sections (5 μm)  were stained using RNAscope Fluorescent Multiplex and RNAscope  Multiplex Fluorescent V2 (Advanced Cell Diagnostics, 323100) accord- ing to the manufacturer’s instructions. Probes for the target genes are  in Supplementary Table 9. Fluorescence images were obtained with a  Zeiss LSM 900 confocal microscope. For histological staining, paraf- fin sections were stained with a hematoxylin and eosin kit (Beyotime,  C0105S). For Oil Red O staining, cryosections were stained with the Oil  Red O staining kit (Beyotime, C0158S). For quantification of smFISH or  immuno fluorescence staining signals, the average signal intensity of  the targets was calculated based on at least three biological replicates,  and the signal intensity was measured using ImageJ (v.1.53c). Cell size  was calculated using ImageJ after cell segmentation by Cellpose (v.2.0).  Proliferation was quantified as the ratio of Ki67 +  nuclei to all nuclei.  

# RNA extraction, RT–PCR and bulk RNA-seq library  construction  

Total RNA was extracted from mouse liver using TRIzol (MRC, RN190).  RT–qPCR reaction was prepared using FastStart Essential DNA Green  Master (Roche, 06924204001) and ran on a Roche Light Cycler 96  real-time PCR machine. RT–qPCR primers are in Supplementary Table 9.  Data were processed using the comparative 2 ΔΔCt  method that normal- ized to  Actin . Bulk RNA-seq libraries were prepared using MGIEasy RNA  Library Prep Set (MGI, 1000006384) according to the manufacturer’s  instructions and sequenced on an MGISEQ-2000 sequencer at CNGB  with paired-end 100 bp.  

# Stereo-seq raw data processing  

The read 1 sequence of Stereo-seq data containing the coordinate  identity (CID, 1–25 bp) and UMIs (26–35 bp) were mapped to CID  allowing 1-base mismatch, and the corresponding read 2-containing  cDNA sequences were aligned to mm10 (Ensembl 93) mouse reference  genome using STAR (v.2.7.9a). Mapped reads were filtered with the crite- ria of MAPQ ≥ 10 and annotated. UMIs with the same CID mapped to the  same gene were aggregated to generate the count matrix-containing  columns including geneID,  X  coordinates,  Y  coordinates and UMI  counts. UMIs with 1-base mismatch were collapsed to correct for  sequencing or PCR errors. The whole procedure is integrated in an  in-house pipeline available on GitHub (SAW;  https://github.com/  BGIResearch/SAW ). The count matrix was binned into bin 50 (50 × 50  DNA nanoballs, with a center-to-center distance of 715 nm between  each nanoball; each bin had a resolution of 36 × 36 μm) for down- stream analysis.  

# Zonation layers  

We calculated the central and portal scores for each bin from the indi- vidual slides of the homeostatic and regenerating liver using pericen- tral ( Akr1c6 ,  Aldh1a1 ,  Car3 ,  Ces1c ,  Cyp1a2 ,  Cyp2c37 ,  Cyp2c50 ,  Cyp2e1 ,  Gsta3 ,  Mgst1 ,  Mup11 ,  Mup17 ,  Mup18 ,  Oat ,  Pon1 ,  Rgn ) and periportal  (Alb, Aldob, Arg1, Asl, Cyp2f2, Fbp1, Hal, Hpx, Hsd17b13, Mup20, Pck1, Slc25a47 ,  Trf ,  Ass1 ) hepatocyte landmark genes 1  with the  AddModule- Score  function in Seurat (v.4.2.0). The central score was subtracted from  the portal score to define the zonation score for each bin. All bins were  ordered by the zonation score from low to high and cut into nine equal  parts, which we regarded as layer one to layer nine from the central vein  to the portal vein. The number of layers can be arbitrarily defined by  further splitting the total number of bins.  

# Zonated genes in homeostasis  

After defining the zonation layers, we interrogated all genes for their  zonation patterns by performing a Kruskal–Wallis test for all bins from  the homeostatic sections with the  kruskal.test  function implemented  in R (v.4.0.3).  P  values were further subjected to Benjamini–Hochberg  correction to generate the  Q  values. Genes with  Q  < 0.05 were recog- nized as zonated. This created a list of global zonated genes. We defined  the cell type-specific zonated genes by overlapping the differentially  expressed genes (DEGs) for hepatocytes (log 2 (fold change) > 0.25;  Supplementary Table 3a), LSECs or HSCs (log 2 (fold change) > 1;  Supplementary Table 3b,c) with the global zonated genes.  

# Spatially auto-correlated modules  

The top 5,000 variable genes expression matrix and coordinates of each  Stereo-seq bin were imported to Hotspot (v.0.9.1) 31 . Then, a  k -nearest  neighbor graph of genes was constructed using the  create kn n graph function and this was used to calculate the spatial auto-correlated  genes using the  compute local correlations  function. Genes with a  false discovery rate of <0.05 were used for spatial module identifica- tion using the  create_modules  function with the minimum number of  genes in each module set at five.  

# Data imputation  

To visualize genes in the Stereo-seq maps, we applied MAGIC (v.2.0.3) 82 .  The resulting values were visualized using ggplot2 (v.3.4.0). Statistic  calculations were not performed based on imputed data.  

# Spatial ligand–receptor interaction analysis  

Ligand–receptor pairs were defined using the CellTalkDB database 32 .  The spatial interaction score was calculated based on the product of  the average ligand expression multiplied by the average expression  of the defined receptor within the same layer for all sections. Interac- tion scores smaller than 10 −5  in any layer were filtered out. We then  randomly grouped the Stereo-seq bins within the same layer into 50  groups and generated the interaction score for each interaction pair  in each group. A Kruskal–Wallis test was performed to determine the  zonated ligand–receptor pairs ( Q  < 0.1). For regeneration, zonated  ligand–receptor pairs were defined as  Q  < 0.1 at any time point. To  overcome the sparsity of ligand and receptor genes, the visualization  of the interaction score of each bin was calculated by first performing  MAGIC on the ligands and receptors and then further calculating the  interaction score within each bin using the imputed data.  

# Analysis of spatially resolved GRNs  

Gene co-expression modules were determined by GRNboost2 imple- mented in the arboreto package (v.0.1.3). Then, GRNs were inferred  using pySCENIC 21  in Python (v.3.6.9), resulting in an area under the  recovery curve (AUC) score matrix representing the activity of each  transcription factor. Transcription factors with an average normalized  expression of >0.001 in pericentral, periportal or cycling hepatocytes  in the liver regeneration cell matrix were kept for further analysis. The  AUC score for each bin was mapped to the physical space according to  the spatial coordinates of each bin. The average AUCell score of each  regulon within each layer was visualized by heatmap. Zonated regulons  were determined with a Kruskal–Willis test ( Q  < 0.05).  

# scRNA-seq raw data processing, clustering and cell type  annotation  

Raw data processing .  Raw sequencing reads were filtered, demul- tiplexed and mapped to the mouse reference genome (mm10) using  an in-house workflow ( https://github.com/MGI-tech-bioinformatics/  DN Be lab C Series HT s cRNA-analysis-software ) to generate the count  matrix. The resulting libraries were filtered by number of UMIs > 1,000,  genes > 500 and mitochondria content < 10%. Doublets were identified  and filtered by DoubletFinder (v.2.0.3).  

scRNA-seq clustering and cell type annotation .  For homeosta- sis, D0 scRNA-seq data generated in this study were combined with  previously published NPC scRNA-seq data from homeostatic liver  ( GSE129516  and  GSE137720 ) 12 ,  to generate the homeostatic liver cell  matrix using SCANPY (v.1.9.1) 83 . Data were subjected to library-size  corrected normalization, top variable gene identification, regress- ing out effects of total counts and the percentage of mitochondrial  genes, Harmony-based data integration, clustering and projection  into uniform manifold approximation and projection. Analysis of the  liver immune cell matrix was performed on scRNA-seq data available  at  GSE192742 (ref.  7 ). The liver regeneration cell matrix data gener- ated in this study was analyzed with the same approach. Clusters  were annotated according to canonical markers (Supplementary  Table 2). Data for mouse hepatocytes cultured in vitro were taken  from GSE120067(ref. 60).  

# Cell type deconvolution  

To deconvolute the cell type composition in each Stereo-seq bin, we  applied RCTD (v.1.2.0) 14 . The expression matrix of the homeostasis  liver cell matrix and the cell type information were exported to gener- ate a reference object using the  Reference  function. The expression  matrix and coordinate information from each homeostatic section  were exported to construct the query object using the  SpatialRNA function. The final decomposition results were obtained by the  core functions  create. RCTD  and  run. RCTD . The final distribution of  NPCs was determined by the average cell proportion weights of each  Stereo-seq bin in each zonation layer. For Fig.  1a , the cell proportion  weights of the Stereo-seq bins were binarized by the  k -means method,  and bins belonging to the cluster with greater centroid of each cell  type are shown.  

# Definition of hypervariable genes during regeneration  

For hypervariable genes, we extracted DEGs (log 2 (fold change) > 0.25)  in hepatocytes (pericentral, periportal and cycling hepatocytes), LSECs  (LSECs and cycling LSECs) or HSCs in the liver regeneration cell matrix.  The expression of these genes was pseudobulk aggregated across time  points, and genes with a coefficient of variation across time points of  <0.1 were removed. Average expression for the resulting hypervariable  genes were then  Z -score-transformed, and  c -means fuzzy clustering  by mFuzz (v.2.50.0) was applied. Hypervariable genes in each group  were subjected to a Kruskal–Wallis test for all bins from the regenerat- ing liver sections at the same time point. For each group, genes with a  Kruskal–Wallis test  Q  value of <0.05 at any time point were recognized  as zonated.  

# Single-cell ligand–receptor interaction intensity analysis  

We applied CellChat (v.1.1.0) 33  to infer intercellular interactions based  on ligand and receptor expression from scRNA-seq data, without imply- ing that cells have physical contact within the tissue. The expression  matrix of the Seurat object and the cell type information were imported  into CellChat. We calculated the overall communication probability  among the cell clusters using the  compute Com m uP rob  function.  

# Bulk RNA-seq analysis  

RNA-seq data for  Tbl1xr1  knockdown were mapped to the mm10  (Ensembl 93) reference genome with STAR, and gene expression was  quantified in an RSEM-based pipeline. DEGs were determined by  DESeq2 (v.1.30.1) and defined as having an absolute fold change of  >1.5 and  P  value of <0.05. Functional annotation was performed with  Metascape (v.3.5) 84 .  

# Reanalysis of bulk ATAC-seq and ChIP-seq  

Raw data taken from CNP0000198 (homeostatic liver ATAC-seq) 61 ,  GSM863767  (TCF4 ChIP-seq in  Apc −/−  mouse hepatocytes) 73 ,  GSM5689142  (H3K27ac ChIP-seq of 0 h) 75 ,  GSM2040959  (H3K27ac  ChIP-seq of 40 h post PHx) and  GSM3478053  (homeostatic liver NCoR  ChIP-seq) 74  were first aligned to the mm10 mouse genome assembly  using Bowtie2 (v.2.2.5) with the settings ‘–very-sensitive’. Aligned reads  with a mapping quality lower than 30 were removed. Peaks were iden- tified by MACS2 (v.2.1.0) with the settings ‘ Q  < 0.01’ and annotated by  HOMER (v.4.10). Aligned reads were normalized as counts per million  mapped reads by deepTools (v.3.3.2) with the  bamCoverage  module  and mapped at NCoR binding sites with the  ComputeMatrix  and  plot- Profile  modules. Motif analysis for ATAC-seq peaks was performed by  motifmatchr (v.1.12.0).  

# Statistics and reproducibility  

Statistical analysis was done with GraphPad Prism (v.8.3.1) or R (v.4.1.3).  Bar charts are presented as means ± s.e.m. The method of calculation  of  P  values and the number of replicates for each experiment are pre- sented in the figure legends. For boxplots, the central line is the median,  the boxes indicate the upper and lower quartiles and the whiskers  indicate 1.5× the interquartile range.
