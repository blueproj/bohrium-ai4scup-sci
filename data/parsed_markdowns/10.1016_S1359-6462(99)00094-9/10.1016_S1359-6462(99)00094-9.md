# Introduction  

Nickel-base superalloys are a specialty class of heat treatable engineering materials that derive their mechanical properties from the precipitation of coherent or semi-coherent particles. (1) Thermal processing schedules are used to control which phases are selected during heat treatment processing, and how and where these phases form.  

The precipitation of  g 9  is one of the most important reactions in nickel-base superalloys, and is the focus of all industrial heat treatments. The precipitation of  g 9  is preceded by the development of a supersaturated  g -phase solid solution. Superalloy N18 has been documented to develop 35°C of undercooling prior to precipitation of  g 9 . (2)  

It is believed that  g 9  particles precipitate in a series of nucleation events. (2) This process of precipitation results in a multi-modal distribution of  g 9  particle sizes in which each size fraction is precipitated at different temperatures and from a different chemistry  g  phase. For supersolvus heat treatments, the initial nucleation event is controlled by the nucleation kinetics of the fully supersaturated g  phase. Subsequent nucleation events are controlled by results from the initial precipitation, subsequent cooling path, new  g 9  phase nucleation kinetics, and diffusional growth kinetics of the previously precipitated  g 9 . Studies on superalloy Rene ´ 95 have indicated that the  g 9  formed from the initial nucleation event makes up 85% of the heat treat precipitated  g 9  in this material. (3)  

The growth morphology of  g 9  has been proposed to progress from spheres to cubes, to doublet of plates, to octet of cubes and then to thin plates. (4) These morphology changes are driven by thermodynamic driving forces, and misfit strains between the  g  and  g 9 . Departures from these fundamental stable morphologies lead to the formation of solid state dendrites. Others have studied the change in  g 9  morphology during cooling, processing and heat treating (5,6,7) because of its significant influence on final properties.  

Superalloy U720LI has been previously studied with regard to current production subsolvus solution and aging heat treatment cycles. (8) This work has shown that  g 9  develops a bi-modal distribution during precipitation from solution heat treatments cycles. The relationship between  g 9  size and mechanical properties has been reviewed and a new aging practice for subsolvus solution heat treated U720LI has been identified for optimum mechanical properties. (8)

# Experimental Procedure  

To study the microstructural evolution in superalloy U720LI, laboratory experiments were performed to characterize the microstructure and phases present after various thermal treatments. Initial studies were performed on billet samples from both powder metallurgy (P/M) and cast and wrought (C&W) (i.e. ingot metallurgy) materials. Laboratory thermal treatment studies focused on solution heat treatment cycles.  

The specific chemistry for the U720LI materials investigated in this program is listed in Table 1. The g 9  solvus for the P/M and cast and wrought U720LI billet material was determined to be 1155°C, and 1160°C respectively.  

Billet slices of each material were sectioned by wire electric discharge machining (EDM) to produce 12.7  3  12.7  3  25.4 mm heat treatment blocks. Holes 1.6 mm in diameter were drilled in the center of the 12.7  3  12.7 mm face to a depth of 12.7 mm for insertion of a friction fit sheathed thermocouple.  

Solution heat treatment studies were performed on the heat treatment blocks with thermocouples attached. Controlled cooling at different cooling rates was achieved by imposing different cooling media, such as air, oil and water, and by wrapping the blocks with various layers of insulating media to retard cooling to simulate large turbine disc-type components. An extensive matrix of heat treatment conditions were performed on the program samples, which included supersolvus and subsolvus solution cycles.  

After solution heat treating, the samples were sectioned at the middle of the block, normal to the thermocouple hole for metallographic analysis and further aging studies. Aging studies were performed on select samples to determine the changes in phase types, quantities and morphologies.

# Results  

The thermocouple traces from the laboratory heat treatments showed the onset of the initial precipitation event in nearly 100% of the samples evaluated. The reaction was characterized as a break in the slope of the cooling curves for the rapidly cooled samples and distinct peaks, often showing recoalescence, for slower cooling rate samples.  

Undercooling below the  g 9  solvus was measured for the supersolvus heat treated samples. The undercooling level ranged from 31–55°C over the range in cooling rates examined (0.12–14.3°C/sec). Subsolvus solution heat treated samples also exhibited undercooling below the respective solution temperature by an average of 45°C.

The grain boundary  g 9 , which changes in size and shape as a function of cooling rate, produces morphology changes to the  g 9  grain boundaries. At the fastest cooling rates, very low amplitude, high frequency (grain boundary wave-shaped serrations were observed. As the cooling rate decreases, the grain boundary  g 9  grows and produces larger amplitude, lower frequency  g  grain boundary wave- shaped serrations. At very low cooling rates, the formation of the solid state dendrites and the fan-type g 9  results in a very tortuous, jig-saw puzzle type  g  grain boundary morphology. These results are consistent with previous work on grain boundary precipitation and grain boundary morphology changes. (9)  

Although the  g 9  morphology changes discussed so far have been as a result of cooling from the solution heat treatment cycle alone, other changes were encountered with aged material as well. The most interesting change was the transformation of the irregular-shaped  g 9  precipitates to uniform cubes during elevated temperature aging cycles. This shows that the irregular-shaped  g 9  precipitation morphology is coherent with the  g  matrix.  

# Discussion  

The thermal processing of U720LI is very complex, with many potential processing options. The type, quantity and morphology of the phases developed and the resultant mechanical properties will define the selection of the optimum heat treatment practices. Previous work dealt with U720LI material that had 10–20 volume percent primary  g 9 . (8) The study of  g 9  precipitation in U720LI material with no primary  g 9  has not been previously presented, and is shown to be distinctly different, since a much higher volume fraction of all  g 9  is in the form of secondary  g 9 . It has been shown that alternate heat treatment processes involving supersolvus heat treatments will result in enhanced properties over conventionally processed U720LI. (10)  

Tailoring of the  g 9  morphology and distribution in U720LI can be accomplished by variations in solution heat treatment temperature, solution heat treatment cooling rate, and aging conditions. Successful utilization and optimization of these factors in the production of turbine engine components can be readily achieved by understanding the effects of each processing variable and through tight process controls.  

# Conclusions  

● g 9  phase in alloy U720LI can nucleate and grow into various morphologies depending on thermal processing conditions.

 ● The size of precipitated  g 9  in U720LI is controlled largely by solution heat treatment cooling rate.

 ● g 9  precipitation in alloy U720LI can be defined in terms of continuous cooling transformation kinetics.

 ● Grain boundary  g 9  formation controls the grain boundary morphology in alloy U720LI from fine serrations, to coarse serrations, to large amplitude, jig-saw puzzle type morphologies.
