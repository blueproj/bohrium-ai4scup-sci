# Sol–gel coating to reduce 1.25Cr–0.5Mo steel oxidation at 700   C: Catalyst type effect

Article history: Received 1 July 2010 Accepted 30 November 2010 Available online 4 December 2010  

With the use of sol–gel it is possible to apply zirconia coatings to reduce high temperature oxidation of steel. Some limitations of this technique are high hydrolysis rates and the formation of cracks during the drying stage. In this work, the mole ratio of zirconium butoxide to ethylacetate, and the nature of the cat- alyst were varied. SEM-EDX was used to evaluate the continuity of the zirconia coatings, and thermo- gravimetric analysis for the oxidation rate of coated samples. Thin and continuous coatings were obtained. With a basic catalyst, the coatings were crack-free and presented lower oxidation rate than acid-catalyzed coatings.

Atik and Aegerter  [6]  evaluated the mor- phology of a sol–gel zirconia coating on AISI 316L stainless steel obtaining homogeneous ﬁlms after drying, however, after the ther- mal treatment the formation of cracks was observed. Shane and Mecartney  [7]  studied the use of zirconia coatings as a thermal bar- rier; they noticed that when the coating was dried at higher tem- peratures than 750   C, the formation of cracks is more noticeable than the same coating dried at 750   C.  

Zirconia coatings are known to reduce high temperature oxida- tion of steel. Although this type of coating has been investigated in the past, there are still some difﬁculties obtaining a continuous and free-of-cracks sol–gel monolayer coating when deposited on low carbon alloy. Li et al.  [1]  deposited zirconia sol–gel coatings on mild steel. They obtained a single layer composed of zirconia par- ticles with holes between them, with low protection against oxida- tion at 500   C in air. Perdomo et al.  [2]  deposited a ZrO 2  ﬁlm on mild steel surface for corrosion protection in acidic medium. They obtained a discontinuous coating formed by islands of zirconia and iron oxides, with lower cracking of the coating when a higher tem- perature heat treatment was applied. Di Maggio et al.  [3]  deposited ZrO 2  ﬁlms with different complexing agents, on low carbon steel sheets. They found that during drying and thermal treatment of the sol–gel cracks are formed in the coatings, due to stresses devel- oped throughout those stages. Baron et al.  [4]  deposited a zirconia coating on 1.25Cr–0.5Mo surface using the sol–gel technique. They obtained islands of the coating on the steel surface that can reduce the oxidation rate of this material due to the formation of pairs be- tween the zirconium cations and the iron vacancies during the for- mation of iron oxides. These pairs reduce the mobile vacancy concentration and, thus, the diffusion of iron and the oxidation rate are decreased.  

The ﬁnal properties of a sol–gel zirconia coating can be im- proved using a multilayered system  [9,10] , however, when using several dipping steps, there are limitations with the thickness that can be achieved. Although multiple layers are continuous and have higher thickness, above the oxidation limit of the substrate, the ra- pid growth of oxides (due to high temperatures) can generate stresses that cannot be accommodated by a thicker coating [5,11] . These stresses can cause the delamination or decohesion of the coating, leaving the substrate exposed to the environment at high temperatures, thus increasing the oxidation rate of the sub- strate  [1,4] . Another challenge in the design of sol–gel coatings is to reduce the rapid formation of precipitates (fast hydrolysis). Alkox- ides are commonly used as the precursors in sol–gel methods to obtain zirconia coatings. These reagents are easily hydrolyzed in the presence of water  [7,8,12] . The addition of a complexing agent reduces the hydrolysis rate due to the formation of a new and sta- ble compound  [3,13] . The ﬁnal properties of a sol–gel zirconia coat- ing can also be improved using different types of catalysts. Acidic catalysts promote the protonation of the alkoxide groups, produc- ing linear polymeric chains. Basic catalysts promote the de-proton- ation of the alkoxide group, reducing the hydrolysis while increasing the condensation rate and promoting the formation of highly branched polymers  [14] . In this study, partially stabilized zirconia coating was prepared on 1.25Cr–0.5Mo wt.% steel using the sol–gel method. Sixteen solutions were prepared using different amounts of zirconium butoxide and yttrium acetate (precursors of the sol–gel). Ethyl ace- tate was used as a complexing agent to reduce the hydrolysis rate of the zirconium precursor. Two types of catalyst were used to determine the inﬂuence of them in the ﬁnal properties of the coat- ing: HNO 3  as an acidic catalyst and NaOH as a basic catalyst.  

# 2. Experimental procedure  

# 2.1. Substrate  

Substrates of 1.0    1.5    0.3 cm 3   of 1.25Cr–0.5Mo steel (compo- sition in  Table 1 ) were used in this study. The substrates were ground using 180–600 grit silicon carbide paper, degreased ultra- sonically in acetone and stored in a desiccator until the coating was applied.  

# 2.2. Coating preparation  

Zirconium (IV) butoxide (80 wt.% solution in 1-butanol from Al- drich) and yttrium acetate (purity 99.9% from Aldrich) were used as the precursors for the sol–gel, ethyl acetate as a complexing agent and 1-butanol as the solvent. Two groups of solutions were prepared; for Group A 1 ml of concentrated HNO 3  (pH 3) was used as the catalyst, and for Group B 1 ml NaOH (pH 10.5) was used as the catalyst. The mixing of the solutions for each group was done in two parts according with the concentrations of  Table 2 . For the ﬁrst solution, zirconium butoxide, ethyl acetate, 1-butanol and the cat- alyst were mixed for 5 min to allow establishment of equilibrium between the zirconium precursor and the complexing agent. Then yttrium acetate, 1-butanol and the catalyst was separately mixed and added to the ﬁrst solution. The resulting solution was mixed for 30 min. All the solutions were aged during 15 days at 25   C. The coating was applied by manually dipping for 3 s the ground substrates in the aged solutions. Coated samples were dried in a tubular oven with an argon atmosphere, heating from 25   C to 500   C at 3   C/min and holding the organometallic decomposition temperature (500   C) for 30 min.  

# 2.3. Characterization  

The morphological characterization of the coatings obtained from different solutions was done with a Scanning Electron Micro- scope (SEM, JEOL JSM-6390), and the elemental composition was estimated with the use of Energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX, Oxford INCA x-sight). After the SEM-EDX characterization, two coatings were selected to evaluate their behaviour at 700   C in air oxidation: Group A-composition 6 and Group B-composition 5. These two samples showed the more continuous and cracks-free coatings. The reproducibility of each coating condition was evalu- ated measuring the weight difference between un-coated and coated samples, using three samples for each condition ( Table 3 ).

# 2.4. Oxidation test  

Coated samples were exposed isothermally at 700   C to air. The weight gain versus time curve was measured with a Cahn 100 ther- mobalance for 96 h. The tests were carried out twice; averages of these two curves are presented in the results and discussion sec- tion. The coefﬁcient of variation ( C v ), deﬁned as the ratio of the standard deviation to average curve, was less than 10% for the Group A-composition 6 curve and less than 5% for Group B-compo- sition 5 curve.  

# 3. Results and discussion  

Fig. 1  shows the SEM of the surface of the Group A coatings after the drying stage. In general, all the coatings presented similar mor- phologies; they were formed by islands or fragments on the metal- lic surface, and some of the islands showed cracks. The fragments of the coating are indicated by black arrows and cracks by white arrows on the ﬁgure. The drying of these coatings was carried out in two steps: (1) constant rate period or CRP and (2) falling rate period or FRP. During the CRP, evaporation occurs as in a ‘‘free li- quid surface’’. When the evaporation of the solvent starts to expose the solid phase of the gel, the liquid phase spreads over the solid phase to minimize the surface energy of the system. The solid/va- pour interface has higher energy than the solid/liquid interface. During this stage tension is generated on the liquid and compres- sion stresses on the solid phase. The stresses on the solid phase produce the shrinkage of the solid network of the gel  [16] . In this period, the solid network has a shrinkage rate that is proportional to the evaporation rate of the liquid phase, and this rate is indepen- dent of time but depends on the concentration of the precursors of the sol–gel  [17] . During the drying step of the sol–gel, solvent evaporation increases the concentration of the precursors on the li- quid surface (liquid/vapour interface), creating a concentration gradient into the liquid. To balance this gradient, liquid from inside the pores of the solid network (of the gel) diffuses to the outside

This compression causes the solid network to shrink, remaining under the liquid level  [16–18] . If the stiffness of the gel is high, the stres- ses generated during this drying period can cause the formation of islands or coating fragments  [17,20] .  

Fig. 1  also shows the formation of fragments on different sam- ples. These fragments are more evident in the coatings with higher concentration of zirconium (Group A-composition 4). As men- tioned before, the evaporation rate of the liquid phase depends on the concentration of the precursors; in this way, concentrated solutions have less liquid available to diffuse to the liquid/vapour interface. In this case higher gradients of concentration develop be- tween the inside and the outside of the pores, causing higher stres- ses and elevated shrinkage of the solid network. Therefore, higher concentrated solutions produce a coating formed by fragments iso- lated from each other, as for coating from Group A-composition 4. On the other hand, less concentrated solutions have less shrinkage and produce a lesser fragmented or more continuous coating (Group A-composition 5 and Group A-composition 6 from  Fig. 1 ).  

During the second stage of the drying process or FRP, the evap- oration continues, but the liquid/vapour interface moves to the in- side of the pores of the solid network. At this point, the shrinkage stops and the drying rate is now controlled by the transport of li- quid from the inside to the outside of the coating through capillar- ity mechanisms. This mechanism depends on the pore size of the solid network. During this period, less liquid is in the outside and more liquid in the inside of the pore, causing a gradient of stresses through the thickness of the coating and reducing the drying rate [16,17] . Due to the stresses generated by the differences in liquid content, the fragmented coating (from the constant rate period of the drying process) tends to fold. This is because the outer surface of the coating is under compression, and the coating/substrate interface is under tension, causing the formation of spiral or sec- ondary cracks  [19] . In this way, spiral cracks are formed during the contraction in the plane parallel to the surface and not due to the shrinkage of the coating (as in the CRP)  [19] .  Fig. 2  shows a schematic representation of the drying stages of the sol–gel coatings.  

Coatings from Group A-composition 5 and Group A-composi- tion 6 from  Fig. 1 , showed less formation of spiral cracks. These two samples are the less concentrated solutions, with higher amount of solvent and less density. Because of the low concentra- tion of zirconium, there is lower possibility to observe the zirconia coating to cover the surface. Due to the lower density, these coatings have lower stiffness; then during the lateral contraction (FRP) the gel can better accommodate the stresses and reduce

The difference between these two coatings is the complexing agent to precursor ratio. In this kind of coatings, the ethyl acetate acts as a complexing agent that forms a new and more stable compound that have a lower hydrolysis rate  [20] . Slowing the hydrolysis rate  [21,22] , enhances the formation of Zr–O–Zr chains through the condensation reactions of the zirco- nium butoxide  [23,24] . In this way, for the same volume, the sol–gel of Group A-composition 6 has a higher apparent density than the sol–gel with composition 5. Higher apparent density makes a stiff solid network that reduces the formation of frag- ments during the constant rate period  [18] .  

Fig. 3  shows the SEM of the surface of the coatings from Group B (basic catalyst). In general, all the samples present high continuity and are free of cracks. The basic catalyst acts over central groups or the middle of the polymeric chains, allowing the formation of highly interconnected polymers  [25] . These structures are non- continuous and non-uniform in the whole volume of the sol–gel; they are formed by clusters of highly branched polymeric chains. These clusters cannot be mixed or interpenetrated with other clus- ters due to strong internal forces such as directional covalent bonds and steric screening effects. During the drying process, each cluster shrinks individually and rearranges to reach higher coordi- nation numbers. These structures have higher pore size, and there- fore reduced capillary pressure when compared with coatings from Group A  [14] . Coatings from Group A were formulated with acidic catalyst, which acts over terminal groups producing poorly branched and linear polymeric chains, with lower steric effects. These low steric effects allow the stacking of many polymeric chains, resulting in a structure with small pore size; but, as said be- fore, smaller pore size generates higher stresses (capillary pres- sure) producing a fragmented coating ( Fig. 1 ). In this way, acid catalyst allows the formation of thicker coatings, but fragmented or with defects on the surface.  

In addition to the catalyst inﬂuence on the coating properties, the hydrolysis rate has a strong inﬂuence on the morphology of the zirconia. Coating from Group A-composition 6 has the lowest zirconium amount and the higher complexing agent to zirconium ratio. This amount of ethyl acetate (complexing agent) reduces the hydrolysis rate, and promotes condensation reactions. In this case, there is high probability to obtain a branched structure. This branched structure reduces the ﬁnal thickness of the coating, form- ing a thinner coating that can copy the roughness of the substrate with a lesser fragmented structure  [26] .  

To evaluate the behaviour of the coating from Group A-compo- sition 6 at high temperatures, oxidation tests in air at 700   C were performed.  Fig. 4  shows the weight gain versus time curve, and Fig. 5  the kinetic law of coated and un-coated samples. For the un-coated sample the kinetic law is divided in two parts. The ﬁrst part of the curve ( t  < 30 h) corresponds to a transient stage charac- terized by a rapid formation of FeO and internal oxidation of chro- mium; the last part of the curve ( t  > 30 h) is characterized by the formation of Fe 2 O 3  in the outer layer due to cationic diffusion [4,27] . With the use of the coating from Group A-composition 6 the weight gain was reduced in about 35% when compared with the bare substrate, allowing the use of this kind of material at high- er operational temperatures. The behaviour of the solution with composition 5 of Group B was also investigated. This coating was prepared with different catalyst type, but the same yttrium and zirconium concentration than the selected coating from Group A. With the coating of the Group B, the weight gain due to oxidation was reduced in about 62% when compared with the bare substrate. All the evaluated samples show a parabolic oxidation rate indicat- ing that the transport of ions across the oxide is the rate controlling process  [27–29] . # 4. Conclusions  

Thin and continuous coatings were obtained with the use of a basic catalyst on 1.25Cr–0.5Mo steels due to the formation of highly branched polymeric structures. The control of the concen- tration of the solutions reduced the appearance of cracking and the fragmentation of the sol–gel coatings.  

The higher protection was achieved with the use of the coating from Group B-composition 5. This coating reduces the oxidation rate of the 1.25Cr–0.5Mo steel in about 62% when compared with the bare substrate.
