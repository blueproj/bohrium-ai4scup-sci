The unirradiated tensile properties for several different heats and thermo mechanical treatment condi- tions of precipitation strengthened Hycon 3HP™CuNiBe (Cu–2%Ni–0.35%Be in wt.%) have been measured over the temperature range of 20–500   C for longitudinal and long transverse orientations. The room temperature electrical conductivity has also been measured for several heats, and the precipitate micro- structure was characterized using transmission electron microscopy. The CuNiBe alloys exhibit very good combination of strength and conductivity at room temperature, with yield strengths of 630–725 MPa and electrical conductivities of 65–72% International Annealed Copper Standard (IACS). The strength remained relatively high at all test temperatures, with yield strengths of 420–520 MPa at 500   C. How- ever, low levels of ductility (<5% uniform elongation) were observed at test temperatures above 200– 250   C, due to ﬂow localization near grain boundaries (exacerbated by having only 10–20 grains across the gage thickness of the miniaturized sheet tensile specimens). Scanning electron microscopy observa- tion of the fracture surfaces found a transition from ductile transgranular to ductile intergranular fracture with increasing test temperature. Fission neutron irradiation to a dose of   0.7 displacements per atom (dpa) at temperatures between 100 and 240   C produced a slight increase in strength and a signiﬁcant decrease in ductility. The measured tensile elongation after irradiation increased with increasing irradi- ation temperature, with a uniform elongation of   3.3% observed at 240   C. The electrical conductivity decreased slightly following irradiation, due to the presence of defect clusters and Ni, Zn, Co transmuta- tion products. Considering also previously published fracture toughness data, this indicates that CuNiBe alloys have irradiated tensile and electrical properties comparable or superior to CuCrZr and oxide disper- sion strengthened copper at temperatures <250   C, and may be an attractive candidate for certain low- temperature fusion energy structural applications. Conversely, CuNiBe may not be preferred at interme- diate temperatures of 250–500   C due to the poor ductility and fracture toughness of CuNiBe alloys at temperatures  P 250   C. The potential deformation mechanisms responsible for the transition from trans- granular to intergranular fracture are discussed. The possible implications for other precipitation-hard- ened alloys such as nickel based superalloys are brieﬂy discussed.

# 1. Introduction  

High strength, high conductivity copper alloys are being consid- ered for ﬁrst wall heat sink, divertor and blanket collar structural applications in fusion energy systems such as the ITER device that is currently under construction in France  [1] . High strength, high conductivity copper alloys are also candidates for the normal con- ducting magnets of proposed next-step fusion nuclear science  

Copper–beryllium alloys (  2 wt.% Be) exhibit very high strengths (  1 GPa at room temperature) but typically have electri- cal conductivity values that are only about 20–25% that of pure copper  [3] . Copper alloys containing   2 wt.% Ni and 0.35 wt.% Be (CuNiBe) have been developed that display signiﬁcantly higher conductivity while retaining much of the strength of Cu–2%Be alloys  [5,6] . This solute composition corresponds to an approxi- mately 1:1 Ni:Be ratio on an atomic basis. A wide range of strength and conductivity values can be achieved in CuNiBe depending on the speciﬁc thermo mechanical treatment (TMT) conditions, rang- ing from relative simple solution quench and aging treatments (AT) and solution quench, cold-work and aging (CWA) or hardened and tempered (HT) treatments to multistep aging sequences. As summarized in  Fig. 1 , increased conductivity is generally achieved at the expense of reduced strength. The CuNiBe properties range from room temperature yield strengths near 1000 MPa and ther- mal conductivity values near 50–55% IACS to yield strengths near 600 MPa and conductivity near 70–75% IACS  [5–9] . A signiﬁcant enhancement in strength while retaining good conductivity has been achieved by utilizing a proprietary multistep TMT consisting of solution annealing, pre-aging, cold-work and ﬁnal aging  [5,7] . In particular, a suite of commercial alloys known as Hycon 3HP™ CuNiBe (with carefully controlled Ni/Be ratios and reduced trace element impurities) have superior unirradiated room temperature strength compared to other candidate copper alloys while retain- ing good thermal conductivity  [3,8,9] . The room temperature yield strength and electrical conductivity of large (600–1900 kg) heats of Hycon CuNiBe has been measured to be 630–725 MPa and 68–74% IACS  [8] , and tensile property measurements up to 200   C indicate good retention of strength  [10] . However, there is very limited open literature information on the elevated temperature (>200   C) properties of these alloys  [3,9–11] .  

The anticipated operating environment for copper alloys in fu- sion energy applications is very challenging. The operating temper- atures for the high heat ﬂux divertor, ﬁrst wall and blanket structures will be   100–350   C in near term devices such as ITER, and may be signiﬁcantly higher for follow-on fusion machines with high thermo mechanical stresses and signiﬁcant ﬂuxes of high en- ergy D–T fusion neutrons. The main anticipated radiation effects in copper alloys in the temperature regime of 100–350   C are radi- ation hardening (with accompanying loss of tensile elongation) and void swelling  [3,12,13] . Due to the low anticipated damage levels in the ITER device (<3 dpa), void swelling is not considered to be a design-limiting factor. However, radiation hardening be- comes signiﬁcant in copper alloys at damage levels as low as 0.01 dpa in this temperature range. # 2. Experimental procedure  

Plates of dimensions   15    15    2.5 cm were cut from larger plates of two different Hycon 3HP™CuNiBe heats (33667 and 46546) by Brush-Wellman and shipped to Oak Ridge National Lab- oratory. Both heats were received in HT temper (  40% cold- worked and aged) conditions with a proprietary heat treatment. Limited testing was also performed on two other heats provided as 3.2 mm plate (heat 35562) and 0.89 mm plate (heat 28626) in cold worked and aged (TH04 temper) conditions. The key differences for the Hycon 3HP grade com- pared to standard wrought C17510 CuNiBe are tighter tolerance on the range of Be content (0.25–0.45 wt.% for Hycon), control of residual impurities to <0.01 wt.%, and use of a more complex ther- momechanical treatment process (two aging treatments separated by a cold work treatment). The ultimate strength and uniform plastic elongation was determined by locating the maxi- mum load point and then measuring the plastic offset from the elastic loading line (note: the slope of the elastic loading line is sig- niﬁcantly less than the elastic modulus of CuNiBe due to low load train stiffness and pin hole deformation effects). The tensile tests on the neutron irradiated samples were performed on one speci- men at each of three irradiation temperatures.  

The fracture surfaces of the unirradiated broken tensile speci- mens were examined using a Hitachi 4100 scanning electron microscope (SEM) equipped with a ﬁeld emission gun. The micro- structure of the unirradiated alloys was examined using a Philips CM12 transmission electron microscope (TEM) operating at 120 kV. TEM specimens cut from as-supplied material and de- formed gage regions of selected broken tensile specimens were prepared in a Tenupol jetpolishing apparatus with a solution of 90% orthophosphoric acid in water, cooled to 0   C.  

Four-point probe electrical resistivity measurements were per- formed following the general recommendations summarized in ASTM Standard Method of Test for Resistivity of Electrical Conduc- tor Materials, ASTM B193. Measurements were made on SS-3 sheet tensile specimens at room temperature prior to tensile testing, with a distance of 7.10 mm between the spring-loaded voltage contacts. The temperature was recorded for each measurement and the resistivity data were corrected if necessary to a reference temperature of 20   C using the copper temperature coefﬁcient for resistivity of  d q / dT  = 6.7    10  11   X  m/K. The typical measured resistance drop was  D R    150  l X  (ASTM B193-87 recommends D R  > 10  l X ). The 100 mA current was supplied by a Keithley 237 Source Measure Unit, and the voltage drop was measured with a Keithley 182 Sensitive Digital Voltmeter equipped with a low ther- mal connector cable (resolution limit of 1 nV). Thermal emf offset potentials were subtracted using the ‘‘relative reading’’ offset func- tion on the Keithley 182. Ohmic behavior was veriﬁed on several specimens by varying the applied current between 10 and 100 mA and measuring the corresponding voltage drop. The typical scatter in the data due to thermal drift was ±40 nV. The main source of experimental error was associated with variations in the cross-sectional area of the specimen. The specimen thickness and width were measured to a nominal accuracy of ±1  l m in two different gage locations with a Mitotoyo precision digital micrometer in order to convert the resistance measurements to resistivity values. Nonuniformities in the width and thickness in the specimen gage region caused the typical experimental uncer- tainty of individual resistivity measurements to be ±0.5%. The rela- tion 17.241 n X  m = 100% IACS was used to convert the resistivity measurements to electrical conductivity values. A minimum of two different unirradiated specimens were tested for each experi- mental condition, whereas only one specimen was available for each of the three irradiation temperatures.

# 3. Results  

# 3.1. Unirradiated conductivity and tensile behavior  

The conductivities of all examined CuNiBe heats were in the range of 65–72% that of pure copper. The room temperature (20   C) electrical resistivity of Hycon 3HP™heat 33667 was mea- sured to be 26.27 ± 0.11 n X  m (65.6% IACS). The corresponding resistivity of heat 46546 was 24.05 ± 0.06 n X  m (71.7% IACS) for the HT condition and 26.78 n X  m (64.3% IACS) for the AT condi- tion. No signiﬁcant orientation dependence was observed for the transverse and longitudinal directions in either heat. The measured conductivities were slightly lower than the values reported by the vendor (Brush-Wellman) of 68% IACS for heat 33667 and 74% IACS for heat 46546. The measured resistivities for heat 28626 were 25.45 n X  m (67.7% IACS) and 25.62 n X  m (67.3% IACS) for the HT and AT conditions, respectively. The measured resistivity for heat 35563 (HT temper) was 25.41 n X  m (67.9% IACS).

# 4. Discussion  

Figs. 16 and 17  compare the temperature-dependent unirradi- ated yield strengths and uniform elongations, respectively, of Hy- con 3HP™CuNiBe heats obtained in the present study with measurements by other researchers  [11,17,22,36,37] . The results from all investigations indicate a good combination of strength and tensile ductility (>500 MPa yield strength, 3–16% uniform elongation) for test temperatures up to 200–250   C. High yield strength (>300 MPa) is apparently maintained up to temperatures as high as 700   C. These strengths are much higher than that obtainable with precipitation hardened CuCrZr or as-wrought Glid- Cop Al25 oxide dispersion strengthened copper (340 MPa at 200   C and 250 MPa at 400   C)  [3,4] . However, the uniform and total (cf. Fig. 5) elongations of Hycon 3HP™CuNiBe drop below 2% for testtemperatures above 300–350   C. Qualitatively similar tempera- ture-dependent strength and ductility trends have also been re- ported for another commercial (Treﬁmetaux) grade of CuNiBe tensile tested at 20–350   C  [11,14,16,22,23] .  

One useful parameter for evaluating the suitability of a material for high-heat ﬂux structural applications is the thermal stress ﬁg- ure of merit,  =  r y k th (1    m )/ a E . This parameter can be calculated for the two Hycon 3HP™CuNiBe heats from the present data by assuming that the temperature-dependent pure copper values  for Young’s modulus ( E ), Poisson’s ratio ( m ) and the coefﬁcient of thermal expansion ( a ) are also valid for the alloy, and by utiliz- ing the Wiedemann–Franz relation to convert the electrical con- ductivity measurements to thermal conductivity ( k th ). The calculated values at room temperature are  M  = 60 and 57 kW/m for heats 33667 and 46546 (longitudinal orientations), respec- tively. These values are signiﬁcantly higher than the thermal stress ﬁgure of merit of other high-strength, high-conductivity copper al- loys. For example, the calculated room temperature values for as- wrought GlidCop AL25 dispersion strengthened copper and cast & aged CuCrZr are  M  = 39 and 37, respectively  [3,4] . The calculated thermal stress ﬁgure of merit decreases with increasing tempera- ture for all of the copper alloys, mainly due to the decrease in yield strength (and also minor decreases in thermal conductivity and an increase in Poisson’s ratio which are partially balanced by a de- crease in Young’s modulus). The values at 500   C are  M  = 36 and 34 kW/m for CuNiBe heats 33667 and 46546, respectively. These values are about a factor of two larger than the calculated values at 500   C for GlidCop Al25 and CuCrZr of  M  = 14 and 18 kW/m, respectively.  

# 4.1. Potential elevated temperature embrittlement mechanisms  

Despite the favorable strength of CuNiBe at elevated tempera- tures, the low uniform and total elongations may limit the applica- bility of this material for high temperature structural applications. There are at least ﬁve possible causes for the low tensile ductility at high temperatures:  

# 4.1.1. Test geometry artifact  

All of the tensile tests performed in the present study utilized miniature sheet tensile specimens with a typical gage thickness of   0.76 mm and materials with grain sizes of 25  l m (heat 33667) to 75  l m (heat 46546). In order to obtain bulk properties during tensile testing, the gage thickness should be greater than 10–25 grains  [38] . Therefore, the miniaturized specimen thickness for the tests on CuNiBe heat 46546 was near the lower bound of acceptable values and could be a potential contributor to lower measured elongations. Direct evidence for a gage thickness effect on tensile elongation is obtained by comparing the uniform elon- gation results by Slattery  [37]  obtained on specimens with gage dimensions of   3    6    25 mm with the present results for CuNiBe heat 28626 (grain size   75  l m) at 500   C ( Fig. 17 ). The larger ten- sile specimens exhibited uniform elongations of 3–6% compared to 0.6–1.3% for the miniaturized tensile specimens. Similarly, the ten- sile results by Singh et al.  [11,22]  on longitudinal oriented speci- mens of heat 33667 obtained on miniaturized specimens with a gage thickness of   0.3 mm produced lower strength and uniform elongations at elevated temperatures compared to the present re- sults obtained on 0.76 mm thick specimens ( Figs. 16 and 17 ). An additional precaution for miniaturized tensile specimens is the importance of careful alignment of the test frame ﬁxtures to min- imize introduction of shear stress components during tensile test-

# 4.1.2. Precipitate free zones near grain boundaries  

The presence of relatively soft precipitate free zones next to grain boundaries could be a potential contributor to the fracture mode transition from transgranular to ductile intergranular frac- ture (e.g.,  Fig. 13 ) that coincided with the sharp reduction in tensile elongations ( Figs. 4 and 5 ) at test temperatures above   250   C. Large precipitate free zones up to 250 nm near grain boundaries have been observed in some CuNiBe alloys for certain heat treat- ments  [11,22] . However, the precipitate free zone for the Hycon CuNiBe alloys examined in the present study was very small (<20 nm,  Figs. 9, 12 and 14  and Ref.  [11] ) and the elevated temper- ature ductilities measured by Singh et al.  [11,22]  were higher for CuNiBe alloys with signiﬁcantly larger precipitate free zones than the Hycon CuNiBe alloys.  

# 4.1.3. Environmental embrittlement effects  

Localized attack at grain boundaries by certain elements in the surrounding atmosphere can produce grain boundary embrittle- ment in copper and its alloys at intermediate temperatures (300– 600   C)  [39,40] . Copper alloys are particularly sensitive to liquid metal (Bi, Pb, Sn) or gaseous impurity (O, C) embrittlement. Previ- ous studies on CuCoBe and CuNiBe alloys have identiﬁed an impor- tant role of testing environment on the elevated temperature ductility  [41,42] . Creep rupture and fatigue tests performed in air and various partial pressures of oxygen at 200   C produced low ductility and intergranular fracture, whereas tests performed in vacuum did not exhibit intergranular cracking. The presence of 400 ppm Zr was found to signiﬁcantly improve the resistance to intergranular cracking in air at 200   C due to oxygen scavenging or another mechanism  [41] . However, environmental testing ef- fects should not be chieﬂy responsible for the low ductility and intergranular fracture observed in the present study since the ele- vated temperature tensile testing was performed in vacuum. It should be noted that the Hycon CuNiBe tensile tests in air at 350 and 450   C reported by Edwards  [36]  had lower uniform elonga- tions compared to the present results performed in vacuum at comparable temperatures (cf.  Fig. 17 ).  

# 4.1.4. Impurity segregation to grain boundaries

Potential mechanisms for this ‘‘dynamic embrit- tlement’’ include reduction in grain boundary surface energy and modiﬁcation of the grain boundary diffusivity. However, according to the chemical analyses for the tested alloys ( Table 1 ), the concen- tration levels for these impurities were below detection limits (<30–100 ppm by weight). Similar low toughness behavior has also been reported for oxide dispersion strengthened copper at elevated temperatures, whereas CuCrZr alloys appear to retain good toughness up to at least 350   C  [4,53,55] . There are no known results on the fracture toughness of neutron irradiated CuNiBe.

In order to further explore the underlying cause of the low ten- sile ductilities in neutron irradiated CuNiBe, Fabritsiev et al.  [17] performed tensile tests at a variety of temperatures above and be- low the irradiation temperature after neutron irradiation at  150   C and   300   C to   0.2 dpa  [17] . They observed a pro- nounced effect of test temperature on CuNiBe ductility. Hycon CuNiBe specimens irradiated up to 0.2 dpa at 300   C and tested at room temperature showed good strength and plasticity proper- ties (yield strengths of   600 MPa, uniform elongations of   10%) with ductile transgranular fracture. Specimens of this alloy irradi- ated under the same conditions but tested at 300   C displayed zero plasticity, a low level of the fracture stress, and were fractured nearly exclusively along the grain boundaries. Specimens irradi- ated to   0.2 dpa near 150   C and tensile tested at room tempera- ture exhibited high strength and low to moderate ductility (  700 MPa yield strength,   2% uniform elongation) whereas nom- inally identical irradiated specimens tested at 500   C exhibited low strength and very low ductility (  0.7%) and intergranular fracture. In summary, the main effect of low-dose neutron irradiation at temperatures  6 300   C appears to be a shift in the transition from ductile transgranular to intergranular fracture to lower tempera- tures compared to that observed in unirradiated CuNiBe. The low ductility observed in irradiated CuNiBe specimens irradiated and tested at 300   C does not occur for room temperature tensile test- ing. Conversely, elevated temperature (>300   C) tensile testing of neutron irradiated CuNiBe specimens appears to produce more pronounced intergranular embrittlement compared to unirradi- ated specimens. The high ductility during room temperature test- ing of CuNiBe irradiated at 300   C suggests the grain boundary embrittlement observed at higher test temperatures is not simply due to radiation-induced solute segregation to grain boundaries.  

The transition to low ductility and predominantly intergranular fracture mode in unirradiated and irradiated CuNiBe at elevated test temperatures (  300   C; homologous temperature  T / T M    0.4) is qualitatively similar to the behavior of neutron-irradiated Ni base alloys such as Alloy 718 and PE16 at similar homologous tem- peratures  [62,63] . In the case of Ni base alloys, the intergranular embrittlement has been attributed to high matrix hardening  [63] along with contributions from solute segregation and/or precipita- tion that produce weakened grain boundaries  [62,63] . The CuNiBe tensile data summarized in  Figs. 16 and 17  suggest that matrix strengths of 440 to 500 MPa at 300 to 400   C produce low elevated temperature elongations of <1%. The elevated temperature (350   C) tensile results by Singh et al.  [11]  for unirradiated CuNiBe sub- jected to different heat treatment conditions demonstrate that poor ductility is not an intrinsic feature in CuNiBe since uniform elongations up to 50% at 350   C were obtained for relatively weak solution quenched specimens (yield strength   100–125 MPa). This is consistent with elevated temperature embrittlement being asso- ciated with high matrix strength (i.e., the high stresses required to initiate dislocation glide causes grain boundary sliding deforma- tion mechanisms to become operative). Conversely, the low ductil- ity (<1% uniform and total elongation) at elevated temperatures ( P 300   C) of all grades of neutron irradiated CuNiBe ( Fig. 20  and Refs.  [11,17] ) including relatively weak solution quenched speci- mens suggests that radiation induced solute segregation and pre- cipitation near grain boundaries may also be important in irradiated CuNiBe; further work is needed to deﬁnitively identify the full range of physical and chemical phenomena that control the elevated temperature deformation behavior after irradiation.  

# 5. Conclusions  

CuNiBe has very good unirradiated mechanical properties at temperatures up to   250   C (along with relatively high conductiv- ity,   65–70% that of pure copper), but exhibits low tensile ductility and fracture toughness at high temperatures due to ductile inter- granular deformation. The low elevated temperature ductility and susceptibility for ductile intergranular fracture was observed in multiple heats of Hycon 3HP™CuNiBe in cold worked and aged and solutionized and aged conditions, and has also been observed in other grades of CuNiBe.  

An evaluation of potential mechanisms responsible for the low ductility at elevated temperatures suggests grain boundary sliding becomes the predominant deformation mode in CuNiBe at inter- mediate temperatures (>250–300   C for strain rates near 10  3   s  1 ) due to the high matrix strength (>400–500 MPa) associ- ated with the high density of semi-coherent precipitates (suppres- sion of dislocation glide). Heat treatment conditions such as overaging that cause a moderate reduction in matrix strength (e.g., yield strengths below   300 MPa at 300   C) should be ex- plored to determine if they may enable operation up to higher tem- peratures while maintaining acceptable tensile elongation (uniform elongations >2%).  

Low temperature (100–350   C) neutron irradiation causes fur- ther reductions in the tensile ductility of CuNiBe. This is mani- fested as a reduction in uniform elongation at low irradiation and test temperatures up to   250   C due to radiation hardening (similar to what is observed in all metals at relatively low irradia- tion temperatures), and in a more extensive intergranular fracture regime at elevated temperatures > 250   C (i.e., a shift to lower tem- peratures for the transition from ductile transgranular to inter- granular deformation in irradiated compared to unirradiated CuNiBe).  

CuNiBe may be a potential candidate for fusion energy high heat ﬂux or electrical applications if the operational temperature is maintained below 250   C. In general, CuNiBe exhibits compara- ble or superior mechanical properties to alternative copper alloys such as CuCrZr or oxide dispersion strengthened copper in this temperature regime for both unirradiated and low dose neutron irradiation conditions. However, CuNiBe does not appear to be a suitable option for higher temperature structural applications un- less improved thermo mechanical treatment conditions can be developed to alleviate the low fracture toughness and tensile elon- gations at temperatures >250   C.
