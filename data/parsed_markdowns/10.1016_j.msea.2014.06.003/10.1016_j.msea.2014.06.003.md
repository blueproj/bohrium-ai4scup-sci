The in ﬂ uence of post-solutionizing cooling rate on low cycle fatigue (LCF) and cyclic stress – strain behavior at 650  1 C has been investigated for a double solution-treated and aged nickel-based superalloy (Ni – 8.09Cr – 9.35Co – 9.41W – 0.52Mo – 3.24Ta – 0.82Ti – 5.51Al – 1.53Hf – 0.075C). The LCF behavior of this alloy has been characterized by dual-slope Cof ﬁ n – Manson relationship. The bilinear Cof ﬁ n – Manson plot for the samples subjected to the slowest post-solutionizing cooling rate has shown relatively lower transition plastic strain for the slope change, and this observation is attributed to their higher cyclic work hardening exponent and more uniform plastic deformation. The increase in amount of cyclic hardening with increasing total strain range and decreasing cooling rate is considered to be due to the increase in dislocation density. The presence of higher volume fraction of  ﬁ ne spherical secondary  γ 0   between the cuboidal primary  γ 0   precipitates, absence of  γ 0 -depleted region near the grain boundaries, and uniform distribution of discrete secondary carbides (such as Cr 23 C 6  and HfC) along the grain boundaries contribute to higher fatigue life and stable cyclic stress – strain behavior for the samples subjected to the slowest cooling rate.

Bhowal et al. have shown that size, distribution, and volume fraction of  γ 0 c  (subscript c stands for the  γ 0   precipitates, whose formation takes place during cooling, and volume fraction depends on cooling rate) precipitates strongly depend on both solutionizing temperature and quench severity used for age-hardening heat-treat- ment of the RENÉ 95 alloy  [5] . The rate of cooling from the solutionizing temperature also in ﬂ uences the degeneration of primary carbides (MC carbides in as-cast micro- structures), which in turn leads to the formation of more stable M 23 C 6 or MC type carbides at the grain boundaries  [7] .  

# 1. Introduction  

Cast nickel based superalloys are high performance materials and their importance as hot components of aircraft gas turbine engine has grown several folds during the last four decades. The in ﬂ uence of various constituent alloying elements of well-known nickel based superalloys on their microstructural evolution and mechanical properties has been discussed in several review articles [1 – 3] . The cast and age-hardenable CM247LC alloy (Ni – 8Cr – 9Co – 9W – 5.5Al – 3Ta – 1.5Hf – 0.8Ti – 0.5Mo – 0.07C) is of interest for use in blades and vanes of aircraft gas turbine, because of its excellent castability, high temperature phase stability, oxidation and corrosion resistance, notch toughness, as well as outstanding tensile, creep and fatigue properties  [4] . The afore- mentioned superalloy is known to have a complex multiphase microstructure containing austenitic (fcc)  γ -matrix, L1 2  (ordered fcc) structured coherent  γ 0   precipitates and various types of carbides.  

The hot components of aircraft gas turbines are subjected to cyclic thermal stresses during start-up and shut-down of the engine. The maximum temperature experienced in this thermal cycling process is around 650 – 700  1 C. The life of aircraft gas turbine materials subjected to such thermal cycles can be predicted by carrying out strain- controlled fatigue tests in this temperature range. Therefore, it is important to evaluate the low cycle fatigue (LCF) behavior with emphasis on life prediction and cyclic stress – strain relationship for speci ﬁ c nickel-based superalloys, which are candidate materials for  

The effects of various types of solutionizing and aging heat- treatments on both microstructural evolution and mechanical proper- ties of the CM247LC alloy have been studied in detail with the objective of  ﬁ nding the optimum heat treatment schedule  [4]  

use in turbine blades. The LCF behavior is largely in ﬂ uenced by the type of microstructure produced as a result of the extent of quench severity that the superalloy is subjected to during cooling from the solution-treatment temperature  [6] . Based on the previously docu- mented results of LCF studies on Nimonic PE16  [8] , Nimonic 80A  [9] , DZ951  [10] , IN 718  [11,12]  and Waspaloy  [13] , it has been commonly reported that microstructural variations in ﬂ uence the degree of dislocation – particle-interactions in most of these nickel based super- alloys, which in turn result in signi ﬁ cant variations in cyclic hardening or softening characteristics as well as low cycle fatigue behavior. In earlier studies, the effects of cyclic frequency  [14] , strain range [15,16] , strain rate  [17 – 19] , and test temperatures  [20]  on cyclic stress – strain response and LCF life have been investigated for different nickel based superalloys.  

Based on examination of the literature, it is apparent that optimization of the heat-treatment procedure by choice of suitable post-solutionizing cooling rates is required in order to tailor desirable microstructures in the nickel based superalloys, so that superior mechanical properties are obtained. Therefore, in the present study, an attempt has been made to investigate low cyclic fatigue and cyclic stress strain behavior of the Ni – 8Cr – 9Co – 9W – 5.5Al – 3Ta – 1.5Hf – 0.8Ti – 0.5Mo – 0.07C (equivalent to grade CM247LC) alloy as a function of post-solutionizing cooling rate. The objectives of this work include (i) examination of the relationship of microstructural evolution with post-solutionizing cooling rate; as well as (ii) assessment of the effect of post-solutionized microstructure on tensile properties, cyclic strain hardening/softening behavior, deformation homogeneity, and nature of Cof ﬁ n – Manson (C – M) plots used to predict the LCF life. Further- more, the cyclic work hardening coef ﬁ cients and exponents obtained from the best linear  ﬁ ts of the C – M plots have been correlated with the microstructures of the samples subjected to different post- solutionizing cooling rates.  

# 2. Experimental procedure  

# 2.1. Processing and heat-treatment  

A nickel based superalloy with composition as shown in  Table 1 was prepared at Mishra Dhatu Nigam (MIDHANI) Limited, Hyderabad, India, by vacuum induction melting, and then cast into ingots having average diameter of 80 mm. These ingots were remelted in a twin- chambered vacuum melting furnace, and then investment cast into ceramic molds containing 28 equiaxed sample blanks with 12 mm diameter and 125 mm length at the Defense Metallurgical Research Laboratory, Hyderabad, India. Subsequently, the as-cast sample blanks were heat-treated in vacuum following the schedule shown in  Table 2 . The post-solutionizing cooling rates were varied by introducing argon gas at different pressures in order to induce forced convection. For precise temperature measurements, the thermocouple tip was attached to the samples. Cooling curves depicting the variation of temperature ( T ) with time ( t ) were drawn for each of the aforemen- tioned sample groups. The cooling rate was considered as the slope of the temperature – time plot at 650  1 C. This temperature corresponds to the point of in ﬂ exion in the plot of d T /d t  against temperature. As shown in  Table 2 , the samples have been classi ﬁ ed into three groups, CR-A, CR-B and CR-C (with 28 samples in each group), on the basis of their cooling rates from the solution-treatment temperature. It is obvious from this table, that the CR-A samples were subjected to the highest cooling rate, whereas CR-B and CR-C were cooled at inter- mediate and the lowest rates, respectively.

# 2.3. Low cycle fatigue tests  

Low cycle fatigue tests in axial strain control mode under fully reversed pull – push type loading ( R ¼  1) condition were conducted using a servo-hydraulic universal testing machine (model DARTEC, M1000/RK, UK) at 650  1 C in air till fracture. Strain-controlled LCF tests were carried out following the ASTM E 606-04 standard procedure. The LCF test specimens having gage diameter of 5 mm, parallel length of 16 mm, and shoulder radii of 20 mm were machined from the heat- treated sample blanks. These specimens were prepared with surface ﬁ nish better than 0.2  m m in both gage and curvature regions, such that circumferential machining marks were absent. The LCF tests were carried out by subjecting the specimens to total axial strain ranges varying between 7 0.2% and 7 0.8% ( 7 0.2%, 7 0.25%, 7 0.3%, 7 0.35%,  7 0.4%,  7 0.5%,  7 0.6%,  7 0.8%). The strains were measured and controlled by using an axial type ceramic extensometer (Sander, model EXH12-1.25A, Germany) speci ﬁ ed for gage length of 10 mm. The strain cycling was carried out at a frequency of 0.3 Hz. The samples were heated inside a 3-zone resistance-heating split furnace (model 3652, Lenton, Thermal Design Ltd., UK) attached to the fatigue testing machine. During LCF testing, the temperature  ﬂ uctuation near the specimen gage length was maintained within  7 2  1 C using a K-type (chromel – alumel) thermocouple kept very close to the speci- men. Three specimens were tested for each strain range to get reproducible results. The cyclic Young's modulus ( E n ) was determined by subjecting three specimens to strain cycles within the elastic range at 650  1 C.

# 3. Results  

# 3.1. Microstructural characterization  

Studies of the microstructures of the differently heat-treated samples using optical microscopy and SEM have shown that the amount of change in the average grain size of matrix  γ -phase with post-solutionizing cooling rate is insigni ﬁ cant. Furthermore, observa- tions using XRD, SEM, and TEM have revealed the presence of fcc- structured  γ -matrix phase, cuboidal-shaped primary  γ 0   precipitates, ﬁ ne spherical secondary  γ 0 precipitates, and discrete secondary carbides in the microstructures of the heat-treated specimens ( Fig. 1 ). Analysis of the XRD peaks ( Fig. 1 (a)) and the spots in the SAED pattern ( Fig. 1 (d)) con ﬁ rms the presence of aforementioned phases in the microstructures. The SAED pattern as shown in Fig. 1 (d) shows evidence of cube-on-cube orientation relationship between  γ  and  γ 0   phases, as expected for coherent or semi-coherent precipitate – matrix interfaces. The SEM images of the heat-treated samples, as shown in  Fig. 2 , exhibit minor coarsening of primary  γ 0 precipitate with decrease in the cooling rate, which is apparent on comparing the microstructures of CR-A and CR-C samples. The volume fractions of the constituent phases and average sizes of primary  γ 0 precipitates in the samples subjected to three different cooling rates are shown in  Table 3 . The average sizes of primary  γ 0   as reported in this table were obtained from the micrographs recorded during TEM examination of the undeformed samples, by calculating the mean of the dimensions measured for more than 100 precipitates. The average dimension of four sides of each cuboidal primary  γ 0   precipitate was taken as its size. In this manner, the sizes of 100 such precipitates were measured, and subsequently overall average as well as standard deviation was calculated. The size of the spherical secondary  γ 0 precipitates in the investigated superalloy samples subjected to different cooling rates has been found to range from 44 to 70 nm, with the average value being 57 7 13 nm. This result has been obtained by measuring the sizes of  E 50 secondary  γ 0   precipitates, and does not seem to vary with change in cooling rate.  

The volume fractions of primary and secondary  γ 0   precipitates as well as those of carbides as listed in  Table 3  were measured from various SEM micrographs using the point counting method. The average volume fraction and standard deviation of  γ 0   precipitates and carbides for each type of sample (CR-A, CR-B and CR-C) were calculated by taking average from all the micrographs. The volume fraction of the  γ  phase matrix as reported in  Table 3  was obtained by calculating the difference between unity and the combined volume fraction of  γ 0   precipitates and carbides. It can be seen from the results in  Table 3  that the volume fraction of  γ 0   precipitates increases marginally with decrease in the post-solutionizing cooling rate of the investigated nickel based superalloy.  

The formation of  γ 0 -depleted zones near the grain boundaries has been observed in the samples subjected to the highest cooling rate (CR-A), as shown in  Fig. 3 (a). However, in the samples subjected to the slowest cooling rate (CR-C), no such  γ 0 -depleted regions have been observed ( Fig. 3 (b)). Moreover, irrespective of the cooling rates, the grain boundaries appear to be lined with a row of discrete secondary carbides (Cr 23 C 6 ) having more or less uniform shape and size. The degeneration of primary MC carbides originally present in the as-cast microstructures of the CM247LC alloy into stable and discrete secondary carbides (such as Cr 23 C 6  and HfC) has been reported in an earlier study  [4] . In the CR-A samples, partially degenerated coarse carbides having the composition of (Ti,Hf)C are also observed in between the discrete secondary carbides (Cr 23 C 6 ) along the grain boundaries ( Fig. 3 (a)). On the other hand, the CR-C samples show absence of such coarse and blocky carbides in between the discrete secondary carbides along the grain boundaries. It has been noticed that the microstructures of both CR-A and CR-B samples are more or less similar, but these differ quite signi ﬁ cantly with those of the CR-C samples. Hence, further attention has been focused on comparative investigation of the LCF behaviors of CR-A and CR-C samples, which were subjected to the highest and the lowest post-solutionizing cooling rates, respectively.

# 3.3. Cyclic stress – strain behavior during LCF  

Plots depicting the variation of maximum tensile stress obtained from the hysteresis loops for different strain amplitudes, with number of cycles to failure, are shown in  Fig. 5 . The results in this  ﬁ gure show that the cyclic stress response of the alloy is related to both post-solutionizing cooling rate and total strain amplitude. However, as the total strain amplitude increases, the cyclic hardening capability increases but the fatigue life decreases. This trend is observed in all types of samples irrespective of their cooling rates.  

Irrespective of the post-solutionizing cooling rate used during heat-treatment, the samples subjected to LCF test at the lowest total strain amplitude ( E 0.2%) have shown cyclic instability characterized by sharp  ﬂ uctuations in maximum tensile stress (i.e. repeated events of softening or hardening) after about  E 100 cycles, as shown in  Fig. 5 (a) – (c). Interestingly, the fatigue life exceeds more than 10 5   cycles in spite of such large  ﬂ uctuations.  

Examination of the results in  Fig. 5  also leads to the following inferences: (i) for the tests with total strain amplitudes between 0.2% and 0.4%, the CR-C samples are cyclically more stable than the CR-A samples for the initial 10 3   strain reversals; and (ii) more or less stable to cyclic hardening stress response occurs with increase in total strain amplitude from 0.2% to 0.8% in all types of samples, irrespective of the post-solutionizing cooling rate. However, an exception is found in case of the CR-A samples at total strain amplitude of 0.3%, wherein cyclic softening behavior is displayed throughout the fatigue life.

# 4. Discussion  

# 4.1. Evolution of  γ 0   precipitates, carbides and formation of  γ 0   depleted regions  

The investigated nickel based superalloy has exhibited cuboidal-shaped primary  γ 0   along with  ﬁ ne spherical secondary γ 0   precipitates in the  γ -matrix phase ( Fig. 1 (c)). In tune with the observations of the present study, Boittin et al.  [22]  have shown that the formation of tiny spherical  γ 0   precipitates (  25 nm) in between cuboidal  γ 0   precipitates takes place in the last stage of post-solutionizing cooling of the nickel based superalloy, PM N18. It has been also shown that the size and volume fraction of such spherical  γ 0 precipitates can be controlled by altering post- solutionizing cooling rate and/or aging treatment. Dahal et al. [23]  have also shown that  γ 0   precipitates in the turbine disc alloy, ME3, have bimodal size and shape distribution. In this alloy, the precipitates with larger and smaller sizes appear as cubical and spherical, respectively. Thus, it can be inferred that the post- solutionizing cooling stage of heat-treatment has a very signi ﬁ cant effect on the formation of bimodal  γ 0   microstructure in the present alloy.  

The script-shaped primary carbides [(Ti, Ta, Hf)C] found in the microstructures of the investment cast CM247LC alloy are known to be enriched in Ti, Ta and Hf  [4] . It has been also reported in this study that these carbides are degenerated after solutionizing to form secondary carbides. The appearance of coarse and blocky carbides [such as TiC and (Ti, Hf)C] at some places along the grain boundaries of the CR-A sample ( Fig. 3 (a)) indicates incomplete degeneration of primary carbides found in the as-cast microstruc- ture due to faster cooling rate. On the other hand, stable,  ﬁ ne and discrete secondary carbides (such as Cr 23 C 6  and HfC) observed all along the grain boundaries of the CR-C sample are formed by degeneration of primary carbides ( Fig. 3 (b)). In earlier studies, the partial and complete degeneration of blocky and script type MC-1 (TiC) carbides into MC-2 (TaC), MC-3(HfC) and M 23 C 6  have been explained in detail for different nickel based superalloys, such as Mar-M 200, B-1900, René 80, René 41 and AF-1753  [3] .  

It is possible to relate partial degeneration of primary carbides into coarse and blocky carbides in the CR-A sample with formation of  γ 0 -denuded zone in the vicinity of its grain boundaries. Agglom- eration of coarse carbides having the composition of (Ti, Hf)C at some places along the grain boundaries leads to depletion of Ti in the surrounding  γ -phase matrix. Due to the reduced Ti concentra- tion in the solid solution,  γ 0   denuded zones are formed near the grain boundaries. # 4.2. Low cycle fatigue behavior  

It is observed that in spite of repeated occurrence of cyclic hardening and softening after 10 3   cycles for  Δ ε t ¼ 0.2%, the fatigue life exceeds more than 10 5   cycles and overall stability is main- tained in the plots of maximum tensile stress against number of strain reversals to failure ( Fig. 5 (a – c)). As the rise in stress is quite signi ﬁ cant after each event of softening, it is not appropriate to consider drop in peak tensile stress as failure. Therefore, fracture has been considered as the criterion for failure, as in several other earlier studies  [8 – 11,24] .  

The decrease in fatigue life with increase in total strain amplitude as shown in  Fig. 4 (c) for each of the three post-solutionizing cooling rates is commonly reported in the literature on LCF tests  [8,10,25,26] . For a given total strain amplitude, the occurrence of relatively lower fatigue life of the samples subjected to higher post-solutionizing cooling rates can be explained on the basis of  γ 0 -depleted regions and sizes of carbide particles near the grain boundaries ( Fig. 3 ). The presence of  γ 0 -depleted region near the grain boundaries leads to unrestricted movement and pile-up of dislocations within this zone, which results in signi ﬁ cant strain localization followed by fatigue crack nucleation and early failure. It has been shown that the deformation bands developed in the  γ 0 -depleted regions promote the formation of extrusions or intrusions, causing premature fatigue crack initiation and failure  [27] . On the other hand, due to absence of γ 0 -depleted regions, strain localization is not promoted in the CR-C sample, such that formation of extrusions and intrusions is delayed causing increasing in its fatigue life.  

Presence of carbides with non-uniform size distribution, such as a mixture of  ﬁ ne Cr 23 C 6  and coarse (Ti, Hf)C along the grain boundaries in the CR-A samples is expected to produce high stress concentration under load ( Fig. 3 (a)). This may cause strain localization followed by fatigue crack initiation, thereby reducing the fatigue life. In contrast, presence of  ﬁ ne and discrete secondary carbides such as Cr 23 C 6  and HfC along grain boundaries of the CR-C samples ( Figs. 3 (b) and  4 (b)) is expected to increase their fatigue life. Erickson et al.  [4]  have shown that complete degeneration of primary cast carbides after solution treatment to more stable,  ﬁ ne and discrete secondary carbides is bene ﬁ cial for creep and fatigue properties of the CM247LC alloy. Lamberigts et al.  [28]  have also shown similar bene ﬁ cial effect for the presence of  ﬁ ne and discrete secondary carbides along the grain boundaries of nickel based superalloy on its LCF life. Furthermore, Zhong et al.  [25]  have shown that crack initiation at or underneath the surfaces during cyclic deformation at 650  1 C is associated with plastic strain damage accumulation and initiation at blocky carbide clusters due to their relatively large size in a cast Ni – Co-based superalloy called as TMW-4M3.  

As shown in  Table 4 , the fatigue ductility exponents ( c 1  and  c 2 ) increase with decreasing cooling rate. This observation is consis- tent with the observation of increase in fatigue life with decrease in cooling rate. Higher values of fatigue ductility exponents is probably due to relatively more homogeneous cyclic deformation of the CR-C sample, which delays the onset of crack nucleation and therefore, the fatigue life is increased.  

It can be seen from the results in  Table 4  that both fatigue strength co-ef ﬁ cient ( σ 0 f ) and fatigue strength exponent ( b ) increase with decreasing post-solutionizing cooling rate, in a manner similar to the tensile strength at 650  1 C ( Table 5 ). Similarly, it is observed that fatigue ductility exponents,  c 1  and  c 2 , ( Table 4 ) as well as uniaxial tensile elongations ( Table 5 ) at 650  1 C increase with decreasing post- solutionizing cooling rate. These results suggest that LCF parameters and related tensile properties have similar nature of dependence on the post-solutionizing cooling rate.  

The occurrence of dual slope C – M relationship for all the samples subjected to different post-solutionizing cooling rates can be explained on the basis of the microstructures of cyclically strained samples. The microstructures of CR-A and CR-C samples obtained using TEM studies are shown in  Figs. 6 – 8 . Relatively lower density of dislocations with non-uniform distribution has been found in the samples subjected to LCF tests at relatively lower total strain amplitudes ( Δ ε t /2 ¼ 0.2%), as shown in  Figs. 6 (a) and  7 (a). In contrast, higher density of dislocations with more or less even distribution is observed in the samples tested at higher strain amplitudes [ Δ ε t /2 ¼ 0.6%,  Figs. 6 (b) and  7 (b)]. This observation is common for all the investigated alloy samples, irre- spective of the post-solutionizing cooling rate. Thus, it is appropriate to infer that there are two distinct stages of cyclic plastic deformation as indicated by the C – M plots for the samples subjected to any of the three cooling rates: inhomogeneous at lower strain amplitudes, and homogeneous at higher strain amplitudes. The results of earlier studies  [8,9,26]  have also shown that the transition from heteroge- neous plastic deformation at lower strain amplitudes to homogeneous at higher strain amplitude results in bilinear C – M relationship. The values of strains corresponding to slope transitions in C – M plots are found to vary with the post-solutionizing cooling rates. The highest and lowest values of slope transition strains are observed in case of CR-A and CR-C samples, respectively. Observation of the slope-transition in the bilinear C – M plot at lower strain amplitude and at higher fatigue life (number of strain reversals to failure) for the CR-C sample as compared to CR-A ( Fig. 4 (b)) implies that homogeneous deformation occurs for larger plastic strain range in the sample subjected to the slower post-solutionizing cooling rate. The increase in cyclic deformation homogeneity with decrease in post-solutionizing cooling rate is attributed to the increased volume fraction of secondary γ 0 -precipitates located between cuboidal primary  γ 0 , which possibly results in more uniform distribution of dislocations ( Figs. 7 and 8 ). Similar to this observation of the present study, Bashir et al.  [29]  have also shown that the microstructure containing the coarsest cuboidal  γ 0 along with very  ﬁ ne spherical  γ 0   precipitates in the René 95 alloy results in homogeneous cyclic deformation and longer fatigue life.  

The increase in fatigue life with decreasing post-solutionizing cooling rate ( Fig. 4 ) may be due to the increase in the degree of strain inhomogeniety as compared to samples subjected to higher cooling rate. The TEM images ( Figs. 6 (b) and  7 (b)) imply that the dislocation structures change from isolated pairs in CR-A samples to uniform mesh in CR-C samples at total strain amplitude of 0.6%, which further con ﬁ rms that the strain homogeneity is relatively higher in the samples heat-treated using slower post-solutionizing cooling rate. Heterogeneous deformation leading to strain locali- zation in the CR-A samples is consistent with reduced fatigue life observed for this material. It may be noted that Zhaokuang et al. [10]  have also shown that the formation of a uniformly distributed dislocation mesh results in stable hardening behavior of the nickel based superalloy DZ951 during LCF test at 700  1 C  [10] .

The back stress due to the network of dislocation tangles restricts the movement of trailing dislocations, which in turn are expected to pile up against barriers [10] . The large  ﬂ uctuations in maximum tensile stress after nearly 10 3 cycles, while conducting LCF test at  Δ ε t /2 ¼ 0.2% [ Fig. 5 (a – c)] is considered to be due to the exposure of samples at the test temperature for long durations. This difference in duration of exposure at 650  1 C by 4 orders of magnitude between the LCF tests carried out at the lowest and the highest total strain amplitudes is signi ﬁ cant, and therefore could lead to variations in dislocation substructure as suggested by Lu et al.  [30] The softening could be attributed to annihilation of co-planar disloca- tions with Burgers vectors of opposite sign or re-arrangement of geometrically necessary dislocations into low angle boundaries during exposure at the test temperature. The increase in vacancy concentra- tion during cyclic deformation is expected to ease internal stress at dislocation pile-ups by the process of climb, and aid in the process of recovery by formation of low angle boundaries. Subsequently, strain hardening is expected to occur by multiplication of dislocations and their interaction with precipitates. As the overall stability (neither cyclic hardening nor softening) is maintained in spite of such  ﬂ uctua- tions, the fatigue life exceeds 10 5   cycles. The reason for softening in the CR-A sample at  Δ ε t /2 ¼ 0.3% may be attributed to shearing of the coherent precipitates by dislocations  [12] , but further investigation is required to con ﬁ rm this mechanism.  

# 5. Conclusions  

The effect of post-solutionizing cooling rate on its microstruc- ture and low cycle fatigue behavior has been investigated for a Ni-based superalloy having composition as Ni – 8.09Cr – 9.35Co – 9.41W – 0.52Mo – 3.24Ta – 0.82Ti – 5.51Al – 1.53Hf – 0.075C. The follow- ing conclusions may be drawn from the present study.  

The Cof ﬁ n – Manson plots drawn from the results of LCF tests follow bilinear relationship between plastic strain amplitude and number of strain reversals, irrespective of the post-solutionizing cooling rate used during heat treatment. With decrease in the post-solutionizing cooling rate, the strain amplitude correspond- ing to the slope transition in the bilinear Cof ﬁ n – Manson plot decreases.  

For the same total strain amplitude, the fatigue life increases with decrease in the post-solutionizing cooling rate. The presence of  γ 0 -depleted region and coarse, primary carbides in the vicinity of grain boundaries in the sample subjected to higher cooling rate results in premature microcracking and non-uniform distribution of slip, which in turn decreases the fatigue life. On the other hand, the absence of  γ 0 -depleted regions, uniform distribution of sphe- rical secondary  γ 0   precipitates between the cuboidal primary γ 0   precipitates, and uniform distribution of  ﬁ ne discrete secondary carbides along the grain boundaries in the sample subjected to relatively slower cooling rate leads to more homogeneous slip, which in turn increases the fatigue life.  

The stabilized nature of cyclic deformation behavior during LCF test of the samples subjected to slower cooling rates is consistent with higher values of  n 0 ,  n ″ ,  K 0   and  K ″  (cyclic strain hardening exponent and cyclic strength co-ef ﬁ cient). The increase in cyclic hardening response with slower post-solutionizing cooling rate is attributed to increase in dislocation density as well as greater uniformity of dislocation distribution.
