# A B S T R A C T  

# A R T I C L E  I N F O  

L1 2  structured  γ 0   phase is the main strengthening phase of Ni-base superalloys, which contain multiple alloying  elements. Locations of these elements in the lattice are crucial for understanding the mechanical/physical  properties of  γ 0   phases. Here, we reveal the site occupancy of various elements in  γ 0   phases of two multi-  component Ni-base single crystal superalloys by using advanced scanning transmission electron microscopy  and energy disperse spectroscopy. Cr, Mo, Re, Ta, W and Ti atoms preferentially locate at Al sublattices, and Co  and Ru atoms prefer Ni sublattices. Moreover, the addition of Ru does not affect the site occupancy of other  alloying elements.

# 1. Introduction  

Ni-base single crystal (SX) superalloys have been widely used as  turbine blade materials in aerospace jet engines [ 1 ], due to their  excellent oxidation resistance, superior strength and creep resistance at  elevated temperatures. The excellent mechanical properties are ach­ ieved by the unique microstructure of the alloy, typically, consisting of  cuboid shaped strengthening  γ 0   phase uniformly distributed in a  γ  phase  matrix. The volume fraction of  γ 0   phase in modern SX superalloys can be  as high as 70%. The  γ  phase is a solid-solution phase with disordered  face centered cubic (FCC) structure; and the  γ 0   phase is an intermetallic  compound Ni 3 Al with ordered L1 2  structure. In order to achieve excel­ lent mechanical performances under service conditions, large amounts  of alloying elements are added in superalloys. Some of the alloying el­ ements mainly participate into  γ  phase (e.g., Mo, Re, Cr, and Co), which  provides the solid solution strengthening to the  γ  matrix phase. Because  the  γ  phase is a disordered FCC phase, the alloying elements are sup­ posed to randomly occupy the FCC lattice. Meanwhile, some of the  alloying elements participate into  γ’  phase (e.g., Ta, Ti, and Nb) [ 2 ]. Recently, with the development of spherical aberration (C s ) cor­ rected scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM), ultra-high  resolution down to 63 pm can be achieved. STEM with advanced EDS  has been widely used in microstructural and composition analysis of  crystals at atomic scale resolution [ 29 – 32 ] and might be an ideal tool for  determination of site occupancy in complicated alloying systems. Here,  by using the state-of-the-art C s -corrected STEM microscope equipped  with Super-X, we have successfully determined the site substitution  behaviors of various alloying elements (include Re and Ru) in  γ 0   phases  of two multicomponent Ni-base SX superalloys.  

# 2. Materials and methods  

Table 2  shows the measured compositions (at. %) of the alloys (ob­ tained by EDS quantification). Alloy A with 1.2 at. % Re without Ru, is a  typical second generation Ni-base SX superalloy, while alloy B with 0.9  at. % Re and 1.6 at. % Ru, is a typical fourth generation Ni-base SX  superalloy. Typical two-phase structures in these two alloys have been  obtained after solid solution and aging heat treatments. The composition  (at. %) of  γ 0   and  γ  phases in alloys A and B has been obtained by EDS,  shown in  Table 3 .  γ 0   phase of alloy A contains 9.9 at. % alloying elements  in total, while in alloy B alloying concentration is much higher (up to 

 16.0 at. %). For comparison, a stoichiometric Ni 3 Al with 75 at.% Ni 

 (99.999%) and 25 at.% Al (99.999%) is also arc-melted and drop-casted.  TEM samples are prepared by a twin jet electro-polisher in a solution  containing 5 vol% perchloric acid and 95 vol% ethanol at  30   C.  

Prior to STEM and EDS analysis, the TEM samples are ion cleaned to  avoid the deleterious effect of contamination. High angle annular dark  field (HAADF) STEM images of the superalloys are obtained on a C s -  corrected ThermoFisher Themis Z TE microscope operated at 300 kV  with inner collection angle 85 mrad, and the HAADF-STEM images of  Ni 3 Al compound are obtained on a C s -corrected FEI Titan G 2  TE mi­ croscope operated at 200 kV. Note that high-quality atomic resolution  EDS analysis depends on many factors, such as signal-to-noise ratio,  quantification method, sample thickness, sample tilt angle, etc [ 29 , 33 ,  34 ]. EDS mapping data are recorded using a four windowless  silicon-drift detector (Super-X) system equipped at Themis Z TE micro­ scope with convergence angle 21 mrad and probe current 150 pA. The  EDS maps and HAADF images are achieved at relatively thin area ( < 10  nm) to minimize the influence of elastic and thermal scattering of the  probe. EDS acquisition time are about 8 – 10 min. Tilt angles during  acquisition are controlled as low as possible ( < 3  ) to ensure signal  reception of the four EDS detectors. Drifting correction during acquisi­ tion are employed. Standard Cliff-Lorimer method has been used during  quantification.  

# 3. Results  

We start with the stoichiometric Ni 3 Al, a simple binary compound,  under HAADF-STEM imaging mode, and the result is shown in  Fig. 1 .  Because contrast is almost proportional to Z 2  (Z is average atomic  number) in such imaging mode, alternatively bright and dim contrasts in  Fig. 1 b represent Ni (Z   28) and Al (Z   13) atomic columns, respec­ ¼ ¼ tively. In L1 2  structured Ni 3 Al, clearly, Ni occupy  α -site and Al occupy  β -site. With viewing direction along [001], there are two kinds of atomic  rows, which has been indicated by red and cyan dash rectangle in  Fig. 1 b. The red rectangle represents all Ni atomic columns (all bright,  Fig. 1 b) and the cyan rectangle represents alternating Ni (bright) and Al  (dim) atomic columns.  

However, the site occupancy becomes complicated in multi-  component Ni-base SX superalloys, in which many alloying elements  are added. These elements substitute either  α -site or  β -site, resulting  completely different contrast of corresponding atomic columns from  that in Ni 3 Al compound. For example,  Fig. 2 a shows a HAADF-STEM  image of  γ 0   phase in alloy A.  α -site columns show almost uniform con­ trasts, as indicated in the averaged intensity profile ( Fig. 2 b). In contrast,  the brightness of  β -site columns has a great degree of fluctuation  ( Fig. 2 c). Roughly, the intensity of  β -site columns can be categorized by  three groups, including high, medium and low contrasts, indicated by  red, cyan and white arrows, respectively. The significant variations of  contrasts indicate fluctuating average atomic numbers of the  β -site  columns, which suggests that atomic occupancy on  β -site is very  complicated. In addition, the HAADF-STEM image of  γ 0   phase in alloy B  ( Fig. 2 d) is similar with that in alloy A. In HAADF images, the brighter  columns contain more high-Z elements (e.g. Ta, W, Re and Mo), darker  columns contain more low-Z elements (e.g. Al, Ti and Cr). However, the  detail information of site occupancy of each column cannot be obtained  solely from the HAADF-STEM image.  

To further clarify the details of the atomic occupancies of  γ 0   phases in  alloys A and B, high resolution EDS mapping has been conducted. To  better distinguish the atomic occupancy details, mixed atomic column  should be avoided. During EDS signal acquisition, beam direction is  along [001], thus every atomic column only contains  α -site or  β -site. The  EDS results and simultaneously captured HAADF-STEM images are  shown in  Fig. 3 . The EDS results are only moderately Gaussian blurred  without other post-filtering. In EDS maps with atomic resolution, the  brighter the atomic column, the stronger the EDS signal, and the higher  the concentration of corresponding element at specific column. There­ fore, it is possible to distinguish not only site occupancy but also con­ centration of each element at specific columns. In  Fig. 3 , location of  bright and dark  β -site columns under HAADF images are indicated by  yellow dash circles and red dash rectangles, respectively. Yellow dash  circles correspond to the bright dots in EDS maps of Ta, W, Mo and Re. Ti  and Cr atoms are preferentially locate at  β -site. In Co and Ru maps, a  majority of Co and Ru columns locate at the position between those  β -sites, indicating that Co and Ru prefer  α -site. Therefore, the atomic site  occupancy of various elements in  γ 0   phases of the two Ni-base SX su­ peralloys is experimentally determined. Along the same line, medium sized Ru atoms (134 Å) should occupy  both sites without preference, and small sized Cr atoms should occupy  α -site, which are however contrary to our EDS results, indicating that  other factor may also play important roles. The electronic structures of  these alloying elements should also be considered. Calculation [ 6 ]  shows that elements whose d-band is less than half-filled (i.e., the  d-electron count is less than 5), preferentially occupy  β -site. In all the  elements we considered, elements with less than half-filled d-band (Ti,  Cr, Mo, Ta, W, Re) all substitute for  β -site, elements with more than  half-filled d-band (Co and Ru) prefer  α -site. Therefore, electronic  structures might be crucial in determining site preferences of alloying  elements, and should be considered when predicting lattice parameters,  lattice misfits and/or local chemical orders of  γ 0   phase.  

# 5. Conclusions  

Collectively, by using the state of the art TEM and EDS, we study the  atomic site occupancy of the various alloying elements in  γ 0   phases of  two multi-component Ni-base SX superalloys. EDS mapping directly  show atomic distribution of all elements in  γ 0   phases. It is found that Cr,  Mo, Re, Ta, W and Ti atoms preferentially occupy Al sublattices, while  Co and Ru atoms prefer Ni sublattices. Moreover, the addition of Ru has  limited effect on site occupancy of other alloying elements in  γ 0   phase.  Revealing the site occupancy of alloying elements in  γ 0 -Ni 3 Al phase  would help understanding strengthening mechanisms of alloying ele­ ments and may provide new strategies for superalloy design.
