# ARTICLE HISTORY  

# ABSTRACT  

In this study, tapered slots with an opening width about 200  μ m were artificially fabricated in IN738LC superalloy to imitate service cracks. The ‘cracks’ were repaired by diffusion brazing with a Ni–Cr–Co–Al–Ta–B filler alloy at 1150°C and then heat treated at 1180°C (HT-A) and 1190°C (HT-B), respectively. A joint with uniform microstructure, chemical composition and hardness was obtained using HT-B. The mechanisms of the borides evolution during homogenisation were discussed.The tensile strength of the HT-B joint tested at 20,600and800°C reached up to approx- imately 96, 90 and 87% of the base metal, respectively. The fracture modes of the joints tensile tested at various temperatures were discussed.

Ni-based superalloys are widely applied to hot-section components of gas turbine engines such as blades and vanes, due to their excellent high temperature strength, hot corrosion resistance and oxidation resistance [ 1 ]. However, the components are degrading by thermal fatigue, oxidation and creep during serving at the harsh conditions. As the high cost of manufacturing new tur- bine components, the repair techniques of the superal- loys have already attracted wide attention [ 2 – 4 ]. Nickel superalloys, especially those with a high content of Al and Ti (Al  +  Ti  >  6 wt-%), have poor weldability due to their high susceptibility to solidification crack- ing within fusion zone and heat-affected zone (HAZ) during welding or post-weld heat treatment [ 5 – 8 ]. To overcome it, transient liquid phase (TLP) bonding, also known as diffusion brazing, has been developed as an effective repair technique for superalloy components. During diffusion brazing process, the melting point depressants (MPDs), such as boron and silicon, are added to the braze alloy to lower the bonding tempera- ture and increase the fluidity of the filler metal. A joint, free of defects, with excellent mechanical properties can be obtained after diffusion brazing with optimised process [ 9 – 14 ].  

In the published studies, the gaps to be bonded were uniform, differed from taper-shaped cracks in reality. Moreover, most of the gaps were less than 100  μ m, which were smaller than the real cracks. Studies have been mainly focused on the bond- ing process and parameters, such as bonding tem- perature, bonding time, gap size and composition of filler alloy [ 15 – 24 ]. However, plenty of borides, which are detrimental to the mechanical of the joint, would be formed in the diffusion-affected zone (DAZ) after bonding. Thus post-bonding heat treatment (PBHT)

Since the superalloy components are subjected to high tem- perature in service, the mechanical properties of the repaired joint at high temperature count for much. However, the mechanical properties of the repaired joint at high temperature were rarely reported. In our present work, simulated service cracks with maximum 200  μ m width fabricated by a femtosecond laser were used for the diffusion brazing repair. This research was aimed to optimise the repairing process of the prefabri- cated ‘crack’ and obtained a reliable joint with uniform microstructure and excellent mechanical properties. Tensile performance of the repaired joints was evalu- ated at both room and high temperatures. The mecha- nisms of the microstructure evolution of the joints dur- ing homogenisation were discussed. Fracture modes of the joints at various temperatures were analysed.  

# Experiment procedure  

As-cast IN738 superalloy was used as the base metal in the present study. The filler metal was a powder named DF-4B. The corresponding chemical compositions are listed in Table  1 . The solidus and liquidus of the DF- 4B are 1065 and 1120°C, respectively. And 1150°C was chosen as the bonding temperature.  

IN738 pieces with dimensions 45  ×  12  ×  1.5 mm were ground using SiC paper and then acetone cleaned for 10 min. The experimental procedure is shown in Figure  1 . The tapered kerfs, with approximately 200  μ m opening width and 1.2 ∼ 1.3 mm depth, were fabricated by femtosecond laser. The previous work showed that the cutting had not brought about the damage of the base metal [ 30 ]. The DF-4B powders were made into pastes by mixing a liquid binder, which will evaporate after heating up. Then the pastes were put on the tapered kerfs to repair the ‘cracks’ by diffusion brazing. The diffusion repairing was con- ducted in a furnace under a vacuum of 10 − 4   Torr to 10 − 5   Torr at 1150°C for 6 h (1150°C–6 h) and 18 h (1150°C–18 h). PBHT was carried out after bonding at 1150°C for 18 h with the processes 1180°C/24 h (HT- A), 1180°C/24 h  +  1190°C/12 h (HT-B), respectively.  

The cross sections of the joints were polished and then etched in a solution of 10 g CuCl2  +  40 mL HCL  +  60 mL methanol. Microstructure was observed under optical microscopy (OM) and scanning elec- tron microscopy (SEM). Semi-quantitative analysis of

The driving force of the borides dis- solution and Ostwald ripening is the decrease of inter- facial energy between the boride and the substrate. To estimate and characterise the process of borides dis- solution, two simultaneous stages can be defined [ 33 ].

The first stage is the break-up of precipitates as shown in Figure  6 . The second stage is the dissolution of precipitates. The reference [ 33 ] shows that the solvus temperature of Cr–Mo–W rich boride is higher than 1190°C, while the borides dissolved at HT-A (1180°C) and HT-B (1190°C) in this study. This was because that the dissolution of borides was controlled by diffusion and equilibrium solubility of the borides. The solu- bility of the borides in the  γ  solid solution increased when temperature increased from 1150°C (as-bonded) to 1190°C (HT-B). With a long time at a high tem- perature (HT-B), Cr, Mo, W and B separated from the borides and diffused into the base metal due to the con- centration gradient between the borides and matrix, resulting in the dissolution of borides.  

# Uniformity of the joint  

# Isothermal solidification zone  

The microstructures of ISZs undergone various heat treatments were observed from the top to the bottom, as schematically marked in Figure  7 . The ISZs of the samples 1150°C–18 h, HT-A and HT-B were compara- ble, i.e. composed of  γ  solid solution and tiny cubical  γ   ′ precipitate. The corresponding chemical compositions are listed in Table  3 . It should be noted that the chemical compositions were very close among the region I, II and III in the same sample. In order of 1150°C–18 h, HT-A and HT-B, the content of Ni and Co in ISZ decreased, while the Cr and Ti showed the opposite distribution. The chemical compositions gradually came close to that of base metal (listed in Table  1 ) due to the inter- diffusion of elements between filler metal and the base metal [ 34 ]. Table  3  shows that the chemical composi- tion of the ISZ heat treated with HT-B was nearly the same as that of base metal.  

To investigate the chemical uniformity of the joints with different heat conditions, concentrations of Cr, Ni and Al across the joints were measured by EDS, as shown in Figure  8 . Ni is the matrix element of the base metal, Al and Cr are important constitute of  γ   ′   and γ  , respectively. It can be seen that the Ni and Al were uniform across the joints at different heat treatments. Nevertheless, there were a lot of Cr peaks appeared in the 1150°C–18 h joint, indicating that Cr-rich borides were distributed in the joint. After post heat treated with HT-A and HT-B, the Cr peaks greatly decreased. Especially, when heat treated with HT-B, the borides in the DAZ significantly dissolved and a homogeneous joint was achieved.  

# Hardness  

Figure  9  shows the hardness distribution across the joints of 1150°C–18 h, HT-A and HT-B. The hardness of joint gradually turned to be uniform in order of 1150–18 h, HT-A and HT-B. This was due to the dif- fusion of  γ   ′   promoting elements (Al and Ti) and solid solution strengthening elements (Cr and Co) into the joints [ 25 ]. It should be noted that the DAZ hardness of the 1150°C–18 h sample was much higher than that of the base metal and ISZ. This was contributed to the presence of borides formed in the DAZ. Therefore, the DAZ hardness decreased along with the dissolu- tion of borides with heat treatments of HT-A and HT-B. The result further corroborates that a relatively uniform joint could be obtained with heat treatment of HT-B.  

# Tensile strength and fractography  

# Tensile strength  

Figure  10  shows the ratio of tensile strength of joints with different heat conditions to the base metal at 25, 600 and 800°C. The tensile strength of the base metal was approximately 842, 820 and 800 MPa at 25, 600 and 800°C, respectively. The tensile strength of the samples with the same condition decreased with testing tem- perature increasing. The effect of PBHT on the tensile strengths was significant. The tensile strengths tested at those temperatures increased in order of 1150°C–18 h, HT-A and HT-B:  

(i) The tensile strength of the 1150°C–18 h sample at 25°C was approximately 75% of the base metal. The highest tensile strength at 25°C of the joint reached up to approximately 96% of the base metal when heat treated with HT-B.  

(ii) The tensile strength of the 1150°C–18 h joint at 600°C was approximately 72% of the base metal, which increased to 90% when heat treated with HT-B.

# Conclusions  

Cracks prefabricated by femtosecond laser with a max- imum width of about 200  μ m in IN738LC superalloy were diffusion brazed at 1150°C and then homogenised under different conditions. The main conclusions are drawn as follows:  

(1) When diffusion brazed using DF-4B filler alloy at 1150°C for 18 h, isothermal solidification was completed, without intermetallics in the middle of the joint. However, a large amount of acicular and blocky borides were distributed in the DAZ of the joint. The tensile strength at 25°C was only 75% of the base metal.

 (2) With HT-A, most of the blocky borides dissolved and a small number of acicular precipitates were still distributed in the DAZ. The tensile strength at 25°C increased to 90% of the base metal. The joint with uniform microstructure (few of borides), chemical composition, hardness and the high- est tensile strength (96% of the base metal) was obtained after homogenised with HT-B.

 (3) After heat treated with HT-B, the tensile strength of the joint increased from 72% to 90% of the base metal at 600°C, and increased from 71% to 87% of the base metal at 800°C, respectively.

 (4) The fracture morphology of 1150°C–18 h joint tensile tested at 25°C presented cleavage steps cor- responded to the crossed acicular borides. How- ever, the fracture path gradually turned to the matrix around the borides due to the depletion of Cr, Mo and W in the vicinity of the precipitates, when tensile test was carried out at 600°C. The fracture surface presented a morphology of steps with dimples.

 (5) The fractography of HT-B joints tensile tested at various temperature was uniform with a morphol- ogy featured by dimples and interspersed parti- cles, which corresponded to the residual borides scattered on the matrix.
