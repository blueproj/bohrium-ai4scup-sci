Article history: Received 10 February 2013 Accepted 7 June 2013 Available online 19 June 2013  

An emerging class of cobalt-based  c 0 -strengthened alloys promises higher temperature capabilities com- pared to current Ni-base superalloys commonly used in aerospace and power generation applications. As with Ni-base alloys, high temperature coatings that enhance environmental resistance are desirable. Sin- gle crystal samples of Co–9.2Al–9.0W and Co–7.8Al–7.8W–4.5Cr–2.0Ta (at.%) were coated with vapour phase nickel aluminide and  M CrAlY. Samples were subjected to cyclic oxidation at 1100   C with 300– 450 1-h cycles. Compared to NiAl-based coatings, the  M CrAlY coatings exhibited superior adherence and an interdiffusion zone free of detrimental intermetallic phases. Evolution of microstructure during cycling is discussed with reference to the available thermodynamic data.

# 1. Introduction

For Co-based superalloys this is partic- ularly important, as small variations in Al and W content result in dramatic microstructural changes, which may be detrimental to high temperature properties  [3] . The discovery of the  c 0   (L1 2 ) phase in the Co–Al–W system, and a thermodynamic ally stable  c  +  c 0   phase ﬁeld, has led to a strong interest in the development of a new class of  c 0 -strengthened Co- based alloys  [1,2] . The development of these alloys is motivated by their higher melting temperatures: the simple Co–Al–W ternary alloy has solidus and liquidus temperatures   100   C higher than commercial Ni-based superalloys  [3] . With higher order alloying additions that increase the  c 0   solvus temperature, these  c 0   strength- ened Co-based superalloys may become a suitable alternative to Ni-based superalloys in the near future  [3–6] .  

Initial work by Pollock et al. attempted to improve oxidation resistance by aluminising and chromising the surface of the new cobalt-based alloys before carrying out oxidation tests  [3] . Typi- cally, the high temperature oxidation resistance of alloys depends on the stability of oxides of Cr and Al on the surface, because both metal and oxygen atoms have low diffusion coefﬁcients in these oxides. The specimens were exposed to 168 h at 900   C in air. Alu- minisation led to an unstable spinel-based oxide (CoAl 2 O 4 ), which is unsuitable as oxidation protection. Chromising slowed the oxi- dation of the alloy and prevented formation of the less protective cobalt oxide. The improved oxidation behaviour was due to the establishment of a protective Cr 2 O 3  scale  [3] . However, chromia is not expected to be protective above 950   C. Therefore, appropri- ate coating approaches remain to be identiﬁed.  

Despite their promising high-temperature properties, the oxi- dation resistance of these alloys remains a concern. Initial studies at 900   C showed inferior intrinsic oxidation resistance compared to Ni-based superalloys. Indeed, during oxidation, a brittle non- protective Co-based oxide forms and spalls during isothermal and cyclic high temperature excursions  [3,7] . Additions of boron and chromium have been found beneﬁcial up to 800   C  [6,8] . In iso- thermal tests at 900   C and 1000   C, Klein et al. have also found that additions of boron and silicon promote the formation of pro- tective Al 2 O 3  due to enhanced selective Al-oxidation  [9] . Although Si-containing phases improve the oxidation resistance, they are also expected to decrease the mechanical properties of the alloy [9] .  

M CrAlY coatings ( M  = Ni, Co or NiCo) have been widely used be- cause of their good ductility, high strength and high-temperature oxidation and hot corrosion resistance  [10–14] . In particular, the M CrAlY coatings have been used to protect Ni-based superalloys due to their performance in service  [15,16] . Their oxidation resis- tance is widely attributed to their ability to form a stable, dense and slow-growing Al 2 O 3  layer  [17] . M CrAlY coatings may be able to generate a desirable oxide scale while preserving the microstructure of the substrate near the interface  [18,19] . These coatings can also serve as bond coatings for nickel-based alloys when yttria-stabilized zirconia thermal barrier coatings are applied  [20–23] .  

In this study, a NiCoCrAlY coating with a nominal composition of Ni–28.5Co–18.5Cr–20.1Al–0.2Y (at.%)  [24,25]  was deposited on two separate alloys, Co–9.2Al–9.0W and Co–7.8Al–7.8W–4.5Cr– 2.0Ta (at.%), by plasma spraying. Cyclic oxidation tests were carried out and weight gain as a function of number of cycles at 1100   C was used as a measure of coating performance. The microstructure and composition of the substrate, coating and interface were char- acterised using Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) and Energy Dispersive Spectroscopy (EDS).  

# 2. Material and methods  

# 2.1. Compositions  

The nominal compositions of the two base alloys, measured via electron probe microanalysis  [26] , and the  M CrAlY coating investi- gated are listed in  Table 1 . The ternary composition, Co–9.2Al– 9.0W (at.%), has been compared with an alloyed composition in- tended to increase precipitation strengthening as well as oxidation performance (‘‘+CrTa’’), Co–7.8Al–7.8W–4.5Cr–2.0Ta (at.%). A se- vere oxidation cycle with a maximum temperature of 1100   C was selected, and it is worth noting that this is above the  c 0   solvus temperature of both alloys (1033   C and 960   C for the simple ter- nary and +CrTa alloy respectively)  [26] . As such, ageing treatments were not employed prior to cyclic testing. Furthermore, since as- cast single crystals exhibit unusually low levels of solidiﬁcation-in- duced segregation  [26] , coatings were applied to as-cast samples. It is anticipated that future alloy modiﬁcations will increase solvus temperatures to well above 1100   C. Therefore, to gain insight into the interdiffusion between Co-based substrates and the MCrAlY coating, the testing temperature was chosen to be in the range of the likely future operating temperature for these alloys.  

# 2.2. Processing and coating  

Single crystal cast rods (diameter of 16 mm and 150 mm in length) with compositions listed in  Table 1  were used. The rods had been grown in a Bridgman furnace (ALD Technologies INC., Ha- nau, Germany) with investment moulds. Moulds were preheated to 1550   C prior to melting and a withdrawal rate of 200 mm h  1 was utilised  [3,26] . Processing parameters were similar to those utilised for the growth of commercial nickel-based single crystals. Following a solution heat treatment at 1300–1350   C for 12 h and ageing in the temperature range of 900–1100   C, these alloys pro- duce a high volume fraction of ﬁne-scale  c 0   precipitates in a  c  ma- trix, very similar to traditional Ni-based superalloys  [4,5] . For the oxidation tests carried out in this work, however, the samples were kept in the as-cast condition.  

Optical micrographs of the alloys sectioned normal to the growth direction were used to determine the primary dendrite arm spacings (PDAS). Average values of PDAS have been measured

l m b -NiAl layer was deposited by vapour phase aluminising (VPA) on all sample surfaces  [27,23] . The original  b -NiAl coating layer formed by the aluminising process contains roughly 39 at.% Al [27] . Second, the aluminide coating was ground off from the top surface and the samples were mounted by spot welding their bot- tom surface to a plate. Third, the samples were grit blasted for coating preparation and a 250  l m layer of the  M CrAlY was depos- ited by plasma spraying. The samples were then vacuum heat-trea- ted at 1121   C for 2 h. Both coatings were present on the samples for reasons of efﬁciency.  

# 2.3. Cyclic oxidation  

The cyclic oxidation resistance of both coated and uncoated Co- based alloys was evaluated using 1-h furnace cycles at 1100   C. The hour cycle included 15 min travel time into and out of the hot zone so that the samples experienced 45 min in the furnace hot zone at 1100   C (see schematic in  Fig. 1 ). An alumina boat supported by alumina rods afﬁxed to an automated stage was used to hold all four samples (two coated alloys with the different base composi- tions; two uncoated alloys) during cyclic oxidation tests. The tube furnace (Lindberg/Blue) was equipped with an automated stage, heat shield, and cooling fan to enhance convective cooling during the 15 min travel time. Every 20–50 cycles, the boat was held out- side of the furnace at room temperature to allow for weight gain measurements. All weight gain measurements were made using a FisherScientiﬁc A-200D high precision balance with an accuracy of ±0.0001 g.

# 3.2. Cyclic oxidation  

The speciﬁc weight-change measurements as a function of number of cycles are shown in  Fig. 4 . Weight gain results were interpreted in accordance with the  time-to-zero-mass-gain-method of Smialek et al.  [16,29] . The performance of the coated alloys far exceeds that of the uncoated alloys. The coated alloys fail between 200 and 450 cycles while the uncoated alloys fail within 20 cycles. Of the coated alloys, the pure ternary survives 450 cycles while the composition with tantalum and chromium additions fails between 200 and 250 cycles. Note that exposure of the coated pure ternary alloy was interrupted after 450 cycles because it experienced excessive oxidation at the sample circumference, where the vapour phase nickel aluminide was deposited and subsequently spalled.  

Due to the presence of both types of coatings (VPA and  M CrAlY) on each of the coated samples, examination of weight-gain results is limited to the comparison of the coated versus uncoated  sub- strates , no further comparison of the two types of coatings can be made from the weight-change measurements alone. Indeed, the weight-gain measurements reinforce the need for suitable coatings for these new alloys, while revealing that the additions of Cr and Ta at the present levels are ineffective in fortifying oxidation resis- tance, with or without the additional protective coatings present. # 3.3. Surface characterization  

# 3.3.1. Coated surfaces  

The initial  M CrAlY coated surfaces with their corresponding coated surfaces post-failure, where failure is deﬁned by crossing the zero-mass gain line or the end of testing in the case of the coated ternary, are shown in  Fig. 7 . There is little apparent differ- ence in the initial coating surface ( Fig. 7 a and e). Similarly, there is no signiﬁcant difference in surface morphology of the cycled samples post oxidation (450 cycles for the coated ternary and 300 for the coated +CrTa). Comparisons of SE and BS images of coated surfaces ( Fig. 7 c, d, g, and h) suggest minimal spallation occurred.  

XRD of the coated samples following oxidation reveals the pres- ence of alumina-Al 2 O 3 , NiAl, and Ni 3 Al ( Fig. 8 ). The Ni 3 Al phase has previously been observed in similar coatings after exposure to 1100   C . NiAl is consistent with the dual phase  c  +  b  structure of the coating that is displayed in the subsequent cross-sectional analysis discussed below. EDS analysis conﬁrmed the presence of alumina on the surface of the  M CrAlY coating.  

# 3.3.2. Uncoated surfaces  

The SE and BS imaging of the failed surfaces of the uncoated ter- nary and +CrTa alloys after 20 cycles at 1100   C can be seen in Fig. 9 . In both cases severe interdendritic attack was observed, however, the attack is more pronounced in the case of the +CrTa alloy. This is in direct contrast to the expectation that Cr and Ta additions would improve oxidation performance. XRD and SEM of the uncoated alloy surfaces suggests the formation of the W-rich spinel CoWO 4  ( Figs. 10 and 11 ).

# 4. Discussion  

The results and character is ation presented above suggest that the Cr and Ta additions at the levels investigated are ineffective in fortifying the oxidation resistance of uncoated systems. Very ra- pid oxidation and spalling occurred in both the ternary and +CrTa based materials for cycles at 1100   C. Surface imaging showed that the uncoated alloys both experienced preferential oxidation at interdendritic regions and no protective scale was formed during oxidation. From the results of Klein et al. under isothermal oxida- tion at 900   C, a similar alloy, Co–9.0Al–8.0W–2.0Cr–2.0Ta (at.%), also provided insufﬁcient intrinsic oxidation resistance  [6] . Thus, coatings are required for this system to withstand thermal excursions.  

The oxidation behaviour of the coated alloys suggests that the M CrAlY systems are promising candidates for protective coatings of Co-based superalloys. The degradation mechanisms of MCrAlY coatings are usually related to the repeated formation and spalla- tion of oxide scales on the surface of the coatings or serious inter- diffusion between the coating and substrate as these lead to the rapid loss of the beneﬁcial elements (Cr and Al). These degradation mechanisms are not active in this system. Comparison with the as- received coated alloy sections shows that the coating did not un- dergo signiﬁcant changes in thickness, adhesion, or chemical com- position (see  Figs. 5 and 6 ). Most importantly, the  M CrAlY applied to new  c 0 -strengthened Co-based superalloys was able to form a dense, slow-growing Al 2 O 3  scale.  

A salient feature of the coating-substrate interaction is the ab- sence of outward diffusion of Al, W and Ta from the superalloys substrate towards the Al 2 O 3  scale. This is in contrast to aluminisa- tion-based coatings where signiﬁcant Al diffusion from the sub- strate to the coating results in dissolution of the  c 0   phase and precipitation of topologically closed-packed (TCP) phases. Indeed, for the  M CrAlY coated alloys, no additional precipitation of TCP phases was observed near the interface, unlike what has been ob- served for aluminised samples  [3] . Limited amount of interdiffu- sion between the coating and the substrate has been observed for Co, Ni and Cr. In the case of new  c 0 -strengthened superalloys, this is expected to hinder the formation of detrimental brittle intermetallic phases, such as  l -Co 7 W 6  and D0 19 –Co 3 W, and pro- mote the stability of the  c  and  c 0   phases. Finally, the utility of the preliminary CALPHAD database used to investigate phase stability has been established and suggestions for future use in guiding coating composition modiﬁcations have been made.  

# 5. Conclusions  

The cyclic high-temperature oxidation behaviour of two new baseline  c 0 -strengthened cobalt alloys shows that  M CrAlY systems are promising candidates as protective coatings for these materials. Interactions between the substrate and the  M CrAlY coatings are beneﬁcial in establishing the physical and chemical stability re- quired for Co-based superalloys to operate in extreme environ- ments. Adherence between the substrate and the coating is maintained during thermal excursions.
