# 1. Introduction  

Ni-base superalloys are widely used in manufacturing of gas turbine components that require strength at high temperatures. It is well known that the precipitation of coherent   ‚Ä≤   phase in the matrix    is responsible for the strength of these materials. The morphology of   ‚Ä≤   precipitates has been well documented and a variety of shapes have been observed  [1‚Äì4] . The morphology of  ‚Ä≤   particles depends not only upon the elastic energy associated with the lattice misÔ¨Åt between    and   ‚Ä≤   but also upon the nucle- ation rate  [2] . When the initial density of   ‚Ä≤   is high so that the overlap of diffusion Ô¨Åelds occurs, the supersaturation is reduced and   ‚Ä≤   grows in a stable manner. In this condition the precipitates undergoing Ostwald ripening will assume the equilibrium shapes of spheres, cubes, octocubes, aligned cubes, doublets of cubes and plates depending on   ‚Ä≤   size, lattice misÔ¨Åt and thermal process- ing conditions  [1‚Äì3,5,6] . On the other hand, when the nucleation rate is low, the precipitates grow to large sizes in the supersatu- rated matrix, developing solid-state morphological instability. In this condition, when the misÔ¨Åt is high,   ‚Ä≤   precipitates develop cru- ciform dendrites  [2,3]  and Ô¨Çower-like dendrites when the misÔ¨Åt is nearly zero  [4,7] .  

Several studies have been carried out regarding the effect of cooling rate on   ‚Ä≤   precipitation  [1,10‚Äì15] . In almost all of the cases, the solution process was performed above the   ‚Ä≤   solvus and nucleation and growth of high density   ‚Ä≤   from the matrix   was investigated. The reasons for complete dissolution of   ‚Ä≤ were twofold. Firstly, it was desired to provide the maximum vol- ume of coherent   ‚Ä≤   precipitates within the    matrix. Secondly, in coarse grain microstructures, effects on mechanical properties are more easily observed and quantiÔ¨Åed when dealing with bimodal  ‚Ä≤   distributions. Solution heat treatment at temperatures high enough to dissolve all the primary   ‚Ä≤   precipitates has been severely restricted in the alloy IN738LC due to the occurrence of incipient melting  [16] . Consequently the standard solution heat treatment generally adopted for this alloy has been limited to a subsolvus solu- tion temperature. The resultant microstructures therefore exhibit incomplete dissolution of primary   ‚Ä≤ , reduced    grain size and non- stoichiometric secondary and tertiary   ‚Ä≤   chemistries.  

A number of papers have been published concerning the effect of various heat treatments on microstructure in IN738LC (see for example  [17‚Äì19] ). These studies however, have not investigated the effect of cooling rate on   ‚Ä≤   in details. The observed morphological changes of   ‚Ä≤   with cooling rate in the present study, is believed to be associated with supersat- uration degree in the matrix. Nucleation of cooling precipitates was almost suppressed and supersaturation was increased at high cooling rates, and consequently primary precipitates which were incompletely dissolved during the subsolvus heat treatment, could easily grow in the supersaturated matrix and showed morphologi- cal instability developing Ô¨Çower-like structures. But as the cooling rate decreased, nucleation of cooling   ‚Ä≤   precipitates occurred and the supersaturation in the matrix was depleted. In this condition the morphological instability was not observed and the primary  ‚Ä≤   assumed equilibrium shapes. Previous studies have shown that morphological instability (dendritic growth) can be obtained by slowly cooling from above the   ‚Ä≤   solvus or direct aging at a tem- perature very close to   ‚Ä≤   solvus  [4,5,7] . In these conditions the nucleation rate is expected to be low and well-dispersed precip- itates can grow to large sizes in the supersaturated matrix before the diffusion Ô¨Åelds around them begin to overlap. Doherty  [9]  has stated that solid-state dendrites may only be expected to form if four conditions are met: (i) the precipitate‚Äìmatrix energy must be isotropic, (ii) diffusivity within the growing particle must be low, (iii) misÔ¨Åt between the two phases must be low and (iv) the precip- itate nucleation density must be low, in order that full development of dendritic precipitates can occur before the diffusion Ô¨Åelds from adjacent particles overlap.   ‚Ä≤   precipitates in Ni-base superalloys normally fulÔ¨Åll requirements (i), (ii) and (iii)  [2] . The condition (iv) was satisÔ¨Åed in the works mentioned above by reducing the nucle- ation density of   ‚Ä≤ . In the present study, condition (iv) was met as a consequence of supersaturation build up at high cooling rates, although the point density of   ‚Ä≤   was constant. These observations show that regardless of thermal history morphological instability of   ‚Ä≤   in Ni-base superalloys occurs whenever there is enough super- saturation in the matrix (i.e. the point effect of diffusion is operative and dominant).  

# 4. Conclusion  

The effect of cooling rate after a subsolvus heat treatment on  ‚Ä≤   precipitate growth and morphology was studied. It was found that the subsolvus heat treatment results in partial dissolution of  ‚Ä≤   and that   ‚Ä≤   growth occurs during the cooling period. At high cool- ing rates the   ‚Ä≤   shows dendritic instability and as the cooling rate is decreased precipitate growth occurs in a stable manner. Mor- phological instability of   ‚Ä≤   in Ni-base superalloys occurs whenever there is enough supersaturation in the matrix   .
