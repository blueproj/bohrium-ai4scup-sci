# Inﬂuence of Ta content on hot corrosion behaviour of a directionally solidiﬁed nickel base superalloy

Hot corrosion behaviour of a directionally solidiﬁed nickel base superalloy with different tantalum (Ta) addition in fused sodium sulphate (Na 2 SO 4 ) under an oxidizing atmosphere at 900   C has been studied. It was shown that the hot corrosion resistance was improved by increasing of Ta content. The hot corro- sion kinetics of the alloy with lower Ta content deviated from parabolic law after 60 h corrosion test, whereas the corrosion kinetics of the alloy with high Ta content followed the parabolic law before 60 h and with less mass change afterwards. A detailed microstructure study using scanning electron microscopy (SEM) equipped with an energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS), transmission electron micro- scopy (TEM) and X-ray diffraction (XRD) was performed to investigate the corrosion products and mechanisms. The beneﬁcial effect of Ta was found to be resulted from a Ta-enriched (Cr, Ti)TaO 4  layer inside the corrosion scale, which led to the retarding of the element diffusion so as to decrease the hot corrosion kinetics.

# 1. Introduction

While there have been extensive studies of the effect of Ta addi- tion on mechanical properties  [9,10] , its inﬂuence on hot corrosion behaviour has been the subject of limited study, with even less detailed analysis of the mechanisms. Fryburg and co-workers  [11] investigated the hot corrosion of four nickel-base superalloys, and the beneﬁcial effect of Ta was reported. On the other hand, Cui and co-workers  [12]  reported that in the alloys with high Cr content, Ta deteriorated the hot corrosion resistance. Some researchers reck- oned that the beneﬁcial effect of Ta can be contributed to the change of the composition of corrosion products through forming NaTaO 3 or compound oxides to prevent hot corrosion attack  [11] . However, there is still certain confusion about the role of Ta on hot corrosion and the exact mechanism should be studied in detail.  

Hot corrosion is accelerated oxidation of superalloys. It takes place in combustion atmospheres containing contaminants and is observed to a greater extent in industrial and marine gas turbines as compared to aircraft gas turbines. A particular form of this accel- erated attack is attributable to Na 2 SO 4  condensing as a molten layer on the metal. The major reaction product is a loose, non-protective oxide layer, in which fragments of metals may be incorporated  [1]  

It is generally agreed that increasing the Cr content in superal- loys increases the resistance to attack, while opposite trend is shown with the increase of W, Mo and Nb  [2–5] . In recent years, there has been a steady increase in the operating temperature of gas turbines. To accommodate this increase, it has been necessary to change the composition of superalloys to improve their strength. Ta is an alloying element used to improve the high temperature mechanical properties of nickel base superalloys. One of the problems in studying the Na 2 SO 4 -induced hot corro- sion of alloys is to devise tests which provide meaningful data with regard to the conditions actually encountered in practice. Geobel has summarized the frequently used three type laboratory experi- ments of hot corrosion tests  [13] :  

(a) Materials are coated with a controlled amount of Na 2 SO 4  and then subjected to an oxidizing environment at elevated temperatures.

These three type experiment methods are extensively used by researchers  [13–24] . Salt-coating method is one of the common ways to value the hot corrosion resistance properties of superalloys and this type of test does allow the development of hot corrosion mechanisms for the case where Na 2 SO 4  is on the surface of the alloy  [13,14,18–20,22,25] . In this study, the hot corrosion beha- viour of DS superalloys with different Ta content were conducted at 900   C by using salt-coating method. The surface and microstructure of corroded specimens were observed and the cor- rosion products were analysed with the aim of understanding the effect of Ta on hot corrosion mechanism.  

# 2. Experimental procedure  

The nominal compositions of the experimental alloys used are listed in  Table 1 (All data are given in wt.%.)  

The cylindrical rods of alloys measuring 16 mm in diameter and 220 mm in length were directionally solidiﬁed by high rate solidiﬁcation (HRS) process with a pouring temperature of 1500   C and withdraw rate of 7 mm/min. All rods received a ﬁrst solution heat treatment at 1240   C for 3 h followed by air cooling (AC) and two step aging heat treatment of 1120   C/4 h/AC and 900   C/24 h/AC. The hot cor- rosion specimens with dimensions of 20 mm    10 mm    2 mm were cut by electro- spark wire-electrode cutting from the as heat-treated rods with the 20 mm-sides of the samples kept parallel to (001)-direction, while the rest sides of the samples were in random orientation. The surfaces of these specimens were prepared by grinding on metallographic SiC papers to #800, and ultrasonically cleaned in ace- tone before hot corrosion test.  

Hot corrosion test was conducted at 900   C. The specimens were sprayed with saturated Na 2 SO 4  aqueous solution on all surfaces and dried on a heating plate. They were weighted before and after salt to assure a salt supply of about 0.3– 0.5 mg/cm 2   on the surface. Each specimen was put into an Al 2 O 3  crucible and all crucibles were placed into a mufﬂe furnace to conduct the hot corrosion test. The test was interrupted every 20 h and samples were removed from the furnace to monitor the weight change and examine the microstructure evolution. Before weighting, the samples were washed with boiling distilled water to remove the residual salt. The time interval between they were taken out and washed was 1 h in every cycle. Then the coating procedure was repeated to allow the hot corrosion test to be continued. Each data of the mass change for alloys was the average of three samples.  

Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) equipped with an energy dispersive spec- troscopy (EDS), transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and X-ray diffraction (XRD) were used to characterize the original microstructure of the alloys, the cor- rosion morphology and to identify the corrosion products.

XRD analysis with the combination of SEM and EDS results suggests that the corrosion scale includes two layers for all alloys. The outer layer of alloy 1 and 2 was composed of Cr 2 O 3 , (Cr, Ti)TaO 4 , NiCr 2 O 4 , and Na 2 Cr 2 Ti 6 O 16 . The second layer for the three alloys was a mixing layer composed of Al 2 O 3  and inner chromium sulphides.  

The average thickness of scale formed on alloy 1 was about 65  l m after corrosion test for 200 h. XRD analysis with the combi- nation of SEM and EDS results suggests that the corrosion product includes four layers. The outer layer was mostly (Ni, Co)O. The sec- ond layer was spinel composed of NaTaO 3 , CoCr 2 O 4 , NiTiO 3  and Ni 3 TiO 5 . The third layer was Al 2 O 3 . The innermost layer was chromium sulphides layer ( Fig. 6 a and e). The white phase in the second layer (marked by white arrows in  Fig. 6 a) was NaTaO 3 detected by XRD and EDS ( Figs. 3 b and  6 d). For alloy 2, the scale formed on the specimen was thinner (about 45  l m thick) and less porous than alloy 1, but with similar hot corrosion products ( Fig. 6 b). However, there was no obvious (Ni, Co)O layer on the outer surface of alloy 2 and the amount of NaTaO 3  (marked by the white arrow in  Fig. 6 b) increased compared to alloy 1. The cor- rosion scale of alloy 2 can be divided into three layers roughly: the outer layer composed of (Ni, Co)O, NaTaO 3 , CoCr 2 O 4 , NiTiO 3  and Ni 3 TiO 5 , the Al 2 O 3  layer and the innermost chromium sulphides layer ( Fig. 6 b and e). The corrosion scale of alloy 3 ( Fig. 6 c) consist- ed of several layers: an external layer composed of Cr 2 O 3  and (Ni, Co)Cr 2 O 4 , a relatively intact (Cr, Ti)TaO 4  layer (determined by Fig. 3 b and  Fig. 6 f), a Al 2 O 3  layer, and an inner chromium sulphides layer. The overall thickness of scale was only about 20  l m.

By comparing with the results shown in  Fig. 6 a and b, the formation of a multi-layered and compact scale on the surface of alloy 3 protected the alloy from hot corrosion obviously. An external layer was rich in Cr, Ti, Ta and O, and doped with small amount of Ni, Co and Al (Although the X-ray map of Co was not shown in  Fig. 7 , it had similar distribution as Ni in the scale accord- ing to test results). The redistribution of Ta showed increasing trend from surface to inward in external layer. The second layer was rich in Al and O and the inner layer was enriched in Cr and S.

The corrosion kinetics of the alloys with Na 2 SO 4  coating is simi- lar and follows parabolic law in the ﬁrst 60 h. The beneﬁcial effect of Ta is not obvious in this stage, but the corrosion products are dif- ferent in the alloys with different Ta content. Na 2 Cr 2 Ti 6 O 16  and stronger peaks of NiCr 2 O 4  detected on the surface of alloy 1 and 2 corroded for 20 h indicate that extensive oxides such as Cr 2 O 3 , TiO 2 , and NiO may take part in the complex reactions. The forma- tion of Na 2 Cr 2 Ti 6 O 16  consumes TiO 2 , and thus, no TiO 2  is detected in alloy 1 and alloy 2.  

Ta could play an important role of prohibiting elements diffu- sion in alloys due to its large atomic radius. Thus, for alloy 3, the growth rate of oxide scale is slow. It can be inferred that only very small amount of Cr 2 O 3 , TiO 2 , and NiO form on the outer layer dur- ing the ﬁrst 20 h corrosion in alloy 3. This is insufﬁcient to form the complex oxides such as Na 2 Cr 2 Ti 6 O 16  and NiCr 2 O 4  that was observed in alloy 1 and alloy 2. The formation of these complex oxides may be detrimental and accelerate the hot corrosion. Simi- lar observation has been reported in previous work where Na 2 Cr 2- Ti 6 O 16  was detected in the surface of alloys suffered serious hot corrosion  [26,27] .  

After 60 h, the hot corrosion process of alloy 1 and alloy 2 trans- forms from an initiation stage to a propagation stage where the attack occurs at a very rapid rate according to  Fig. 2 . Different cor- rosion products formed on the surface of alloys with different Ta content indicate that the hot corrosion mechanism is changed by the increasing of Ta. The amount of Ta 2 O 5  formed on top of Al 2 O 3 layer is increased with the increasing of exposure time  [21] . It was reported that Cr 2 O 3  and TiO 2  would react with Ta 2 O 5  to form CrTaO 4  and TiTaO 4  (or (Cr, Ti)TaO 4 )  [28,29] . The continuously com- pact (Cr, Ti)TaO 4  layer may remarkably reduce the rate of ions dif- fusion, and is beneﬁcial to the hot corrosion resistance according to our present results.  

For alloy 1, the rapid outward diffusion of cations like Ni +   and Co +   results in the formation of (Ni, Co)O. As the outer (Ni, Co)O lay- er grows into the metal, it encounters islands of Cr 2 O 3  and TiO 2 , and the result is the formation of NiCr 2 O 4 , CoCr 2 O 4 , NiTiO 3 , or Ni 3- TiO 5  (see in  Fig. 8 a)  [30] . Ni-containing oxides are less protective than Cr 2 O 3 , and thus, considerable amounts of NiO and spinel com- pounds form before the more protective Cr 2 O 3  layer forms  [30] . Because they are less protective due to high solubility in molten salt  [31] , corrosion scales containing higher amounts of Ni and Co are thicker over time. The volume change of corrosion product leads to cracking, and sulphur is able to penetrate through the scale quickly to form inner sulphides to accelerate the hot corro- sion process  [32] .  

While for alloy 3, the protective effect of (Cr, Ti)TaO 4  layer is observed besides the formation of the well-known protective layer of Cr 2 O 3  at the external surface. Cr 2 O 3  and TiO 2  formed on the outer surface could tie up more Ta 2 O 5  to form relatively continuous and compact (Cr, Ti)TaO 4  layer due to the high Ta content, see Figs.  7  and  8 b. # 5. Conclusions  

The hot corrosion behaviour of a directionally solidiﬁed super- alloy with different Ta content coated with Na 2 SO 4  was studied in air at 900   C. The main conclusions were as follows:  

1. The hot corrosion kinetics of alloy 1 and alloy 2 with lower Ta content deviated from parabolic law after 60 h corrosion test, whereas the corrosion kinetics of alloy 3 with high Ta content followed the parabolic law before 60 h and with less mass change afterwards.

 2. Besides the Cr 2 O 3  layer, a relatively continuous and compact (Cr, Ti)TaO 4  layer presented on top of Al 2 O 3  layer was observed with increasing of Ta addition during hot corrosion. It is believed that both of the Cr 2 O 3  and (Cr, Ti)TaO 4  layers acted as a protective scale, and thus improved the hot corrosion resistance.
