ABSTRACT:  The T-cell receptor (TCR) is a crucial molecule in cellular immunity. The single-chain T-cell receptor (scTCR) is a potential format in TCR therapeutics because it eliminates the possibility of  αβ -TCR mispairing. However, its poor stability and solubility impede the in vitro study and manufacturing of therapeutic applications. In this study, some conserved structural motifs are identified in variable domains regardless of germlines and species. Theoretical analysis helps to identify those unfavored factors and leads to a general strategy for stabilizing scTCRs by substituting residues at exact IMGT positions with beneficial propensities on the consensus sequence of germlines. # INTRODUCTION  

T-cell receptors (TCRs) have emerged as a formidable force in the arena of cancer immunotherapies. ,   Notably, the effective- ness of TCR-T in clinical trials, particularly in targeting solid tumors, has garnered significant attention. ,   The recent approval of KIMMTRAK, the first high-affinity soluble  αβ - TCR fused with an anti-CD3 single-chain variable fragment (scFv), represents a landmark achievement in treating unresectable or metastatic uveal melanoma. ,   However, native αβ -TCRs often face challenges such as limited stability, solubility, and low binding affinity, − 9   leading to issues like reduced protein yield in vitro 10   and even graft-versus-host disease (GVHD) in vivo.   Various engineering strategies have been employed to mitigate these issues, including the introduction of disulfide bonds in constant domains (Boulter disulfide bond),   the development of three-domain TCRs by removing the C α  domain,   and modifications to the extracellular constant domains.  

In parallel, single-chain TCRs (scTCRs) have risen as a promising alternative, effectively eliminating mispairing risks, demonstrating higher in vitro protein yields,   and structurally resembling soluble single-chain Fv (scFv).   This advancement lays a solid foundation for affinity maturation, ,   scTCR-based CAR-T cell therapies, ,   and the development of bispecific T- cell engagers.   Nonetheless, scTCRs still encounter challenges related to stability and solubility, even after refolding from inclusion bodies. ,

# RESULTS  

Identification of Conserved Structural Motifs.  Our statistical analysis of hundreds of  αβ -TCR crystal structures (refer to  Table S1 ) revealed five key conserved structural motifs: β AB ,  β CC ′ ,  α -helix, hydrophobic covariation, and the V − C interface (illustrated in  Scheme 1 ). These motifs, situated away from the complementarity-determining regions (CDRs), primarily function to uphold the structural framework rather than directly influence binding affinity and specificity. Thus, optimizing these motifs is expected to improve stability without adversely affecting the binding characteristics.  

Analysis of Sequential Residues Forming  β -Turns.  We observed that sequential residues form two distinct  β -turns,  β AB and  β CC ′ . The Ramachandran plots for residues 15 and 46 fall within  − 60  ±  30 and 120  ±  30 ° , suggesting their role as the  i  + 1th residues in  β -turns. Similarly, residues 16 and 47, with Ramachandran plots in the ranges 80  ±  30 and 0  ±  30 ° , are identified as the  i  + 2th residues of  β -turns ( Figure 1 b). The observed hydrogen bonding patterns ( Figure 1 a) and Ramachandran plot distributions confirm that  β AB  and  β CC ′ are classified as type II  β -turns.  

Formation and Significance of Short  α -Helix.  A trio of residues, 96, 97, and 98, fold into a notably short  α -helix that bridges strands E and F, influenced by covariation interaction patterns and cap effects ( Figure 1 c,d). Despite its insufficient length for forming backbone hydrogen bonds and its near absence, the conservation of this helix suggests a need for additional stability through nonlocal interactions, highlighting its importance in local stability optimization.  

V − C Interface Dynamics in the scTCR Construction.  The construction of a scTCR necessitates the removal of both C α and C β  domains, thereby isolating the V α  and V β  domains. This process significantly impacts the mutual stabilization typically provided by the interactions between the variable and constant domains (V − C interaction).   In particular, residues 96 and 127 play crucial roles in bridging the V α  and C α  domains. Moreover, in the V β  domain, several residues, including 9, 10, 12, 96, 125, and 127, are deeply embedded within the C β  domain, indicating a stronger interaction between V β  and C β  ( Figure 1 e,f). Optimizing the contact residues at the V − C interface could therefore enhance local entropy effects.

Structure-Based Consensus Sequence Alignment and Stability Optimization of Structural Motifs.  Utilizing sequence LOGO,   we aligned TRAV, TRAJ, TRBV, and TRBJ germlines from six species 31 ,   based on IMGT numbering.   This alignment shows that the identified structural motifs are consistently present in both the V α  and V β  domains across different species, underscoring their importance in stability and folding. The scTCRs’ suboptimal stability and solubility suggest that certain residues might be detrimental to their folding. By mapping these structural motifs onto the primary sequence, as annotated in the sequence LOGO ( Figure 2 ), we can directly observe discrepancies between the primary sequence and the conserved structural motifs. For instance, the β AB  motif (residues 14 − 17AA) exhibits a propensity for certain residues that may not be favorable. At residue 14, a key position in a type II  β -turn, Pro, Phe, and His are structurally preferred, yet Gln predominates. These observations provide insights into stability optimization through the alignment of consensus residues and structural propensities. We have developed a potential substitution table ( Table 1 ) to align the residues at the structural motif sites with their propensities. This strategy is detailed as follows:  

Optimization of  β AB  Turns.  In the  β AB  motif, Pro is recommended for residue 14 due to its structural propensity, while Gly is consistently found at residue 16 across all germlines.   To circumvent potential steric hindrance from Pro at residue 14, Ala is sometimes preferred at residue 17.  

β CC ′  Motif Adjustments.  For the  β CC ′  motif, Asp, Tyr, and Asn are favored at residue 45, accommodating both consensus and nonlocal interactions. If residue 43 is Arg or Lys, Asp is preferred to form a salt bridge, while Tyr or Asn is a suitable alternative when residue 43 is neither Arg nor Lys. These substitutions facilitate hydrogen bonding patterns akin to the C-cap effect. ,   Residues 46 and 47 are constrained to the dipeptide Pro − Gly, in line with their propensities and sequence consensus.  

V − C Interface and  α -Helix Optimization.  Upon removal of the constant domain, the interface becomes exposed, rendering the previously buried residues potentially unsuitable for the variable domains. In the V α  domain, residues 96 and 127 are now exposed to a solvent environment without the C α  domain. In the V β  domain, residues 9, 10, 12, 96, 125, and 127, which previously interacted closely with the C β  domain, require reevaluation. Specifically, residue 96, forming part of the short  α - helix at its N-terminus ( Figure 1 c), is ideally substituted with Pro for its N-cap effect on the  α -helix 36   and to mitigate potential interactions with constant domains. The other residues, which fold into  β -strands, are selected for substitution with residues having medium to high  β -sheet propensity and hydrophilic side chains.  

Hydrophobic Covariation Site Optimization.  In the absence of constant domains, the naturally occurring covariation among strands A − G requires further refinement. Considering hydro- phobic side chains,  β -strand propensity, and sequence consensus, residues 11, 13, 19, 21, 124, and 126 are evaluated for optimization. Val, Ile, and Leu emerged as potential candidates. At position 94, Val or Leu could enhance stability through hydrophobic interactions with residues 11, 19, and 126, while Ser or Thr might offer an alternative by forming hydrogen bonds with the  β AB  motif.

# DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS  

T cells, serving as a pivotal platform for drug development, have seen exciting progress in both basic research and clinical translation. ,   The pharmacological modification of TCRs, thereby giving rise to therapies like TCR-T and bispecific T-cell engager, , ,   has emerged as a vital avenue in the quest to treat solid tumors effectively.   However, the prerequisite for functional TCR optimization is its stable performance, enabling it to support a range of biochemical and cellular experiments and extensions.  

Significant research has focused on modifying natural  αβ - TCRs, ,   with one drug already on the market and others in advanced stages of clinical development.   However, this field faces persistent challenges, particularly the heterodimeric nature of the  αβ -TCRs. In vitro protein expression and production, as well as the functional aspects of in vivo TCR-T, are complicated by the risk of reduced target protein yield and increased erroneous proteins due to mispairing of the  α  and  β  chains. ,  

Kranz et al. developed the scTCR format,   which has been reported to nearly eliminate mispairing issues.   However, scTCRs, like  αβ -TCRs, face challenges in stability and expression.   Wild-type scTCRs are often difficult to express. , Recent research efforts have aimed to address these issues in specific scTCR systems, − 22   but the diversity of germline sequences limits the development of a universal strategy for optimizing scTCR stability, despite its importance in affinity maturation 8 , ,   and therapeutic application development.

# EXPERIMENTAL SECTION  

Statistical Analysis of Conserved Structural Motifs.  We utilized the TCR3d database 50   (as of 2022/10/19) to gather statistical information on TCR crystal structures. A total of 516  α -chain and 534 β -chain crystal structures were downloaded from the PDB database 51 and manually separated, resulting in 689  α -chains and 779  β -chains. To refine our data set, we applied a resolution filter of less than 3 Å and used the cd-hit tool 52   to eliminate sequences with 100% redundancy based on sequence similarity. This process yielded 113 human  α -chains, 37 mouse  α -chains, 136 human  β -chains, and 40 mouse  β -chains for further analysis. The phi − psi dihedral angles and solvent-accessible surface area (SASA) were calculated using the dssp software.   For hydrophobic covariation and hydrogen bond analysis, we employed the GetContacts software.   We set criteria for hydrogen bonds as having a length of less than 3.5 Å and a bond angle between 70 and 180 ° . All of the collected data were then subjected to further analysis using our in- house script.  

MD Simulations.  We employed AlphaFold2 v2.3 55   to predict the structures of the V α /V β  domains. Among the five modeled structures provided by AlphaFold2, we selected the structure with the highest predicted local distance difference test (PLDDT) score for further analysis. The chosen initial structure was placed in a TIP3P water box, ensuring a minimum distance of 8 Å between the protein surface and the periodic box boundary. The system was supplemented with 0.15 M NaCl. We also redistributed the hydrogen mass.   The system underwent energy minimization, followed by a heating phase. It was then maintained at 300 K for 1 ns before proceeding with the production simulation. We utilized the RSFF2C force field 57   for the simulations. Four parallel trajectories of 250 ns each were run at 330 K to sample CDRs, and two parallel trajectories of 500 ns each were conducted at 500 K for stability characterization, all under the  NVT (constant number of particles, volume, and temperature) ensemble. The simulations were set to record every 2 fs with data collection every 2000 steps. The simulated trajectories were analyzed using AMBER’s cpptraj tool.   rmsd calculations were performed using only C α  atoms. For cluster analysis, we applied the hierarchical clustering algorithm with an epsilon value set to 1.5. In PCA, only C α  atoms were considered, and the first two principal components out of five were selected as representative components.  

Protein Preparation and Purification.  The DNA sequences encoding the  α -chains,  β -chains, and scTCRs were synthesized and cloned into the pET28a vector by using the Nco I/Xho I cleavage sites. These constructs were then transformed into  Escherichia coli  BL21 (DE3) cells. Monoclonal bacterial colonies were cultured in LB medium at 37  ° C until the OD  reached 0.6 − 0.8. Isopropyl  β - D -1- 600 thi oga lac to pyr a no side (IPTG) was added to achieve a final concentration of 0.8 mM, and the culture was continued at 37  ° C for 3 h. The cells were then harvested by centrifugation at 6000 rpm for 10 min.  

Lysis and Inclusion Were Carried Out by Body Purification.  The bacterial pellets were resuspended in a lysis solution (PBS containing 0.5% TritonX-100) and lysed using ultrasonication. The lysate was centrifuged at 12,000 rpm for 20 min, and the supernatant was discarded. The pellet was resuspended in lysis solution until no visible particles remained and then centrifuged at high speed for 10 min. This process was repeated two to three times. The pellet was then dissolved in 6 M guanidine hydrochloride solution and centrifuged at high speed for 10 min to collect the supernatant containing the purified inclusion bodies.  

Refolding of the TCR  α  and  β  Chains.  The TCR  α  and  β  chains were diluted in 5 mL of a 6 M guanidine hydrochloride solution at a ratio of 1.5:1, respectively. They were then gradually added to precooled refolding buffer 7 ,   and mixed with continuous stirring at 4  ° C for 30 min. The mixture was transferred to a dialysis bag and dialyzed against 10 times the volume of precooled deionized water with stirring for 8 − 12 h. Subsequent dialysis was performed in precooled external dialysate (pH 8.1, 20 mM Tris − HCl) at 4  ° C for 8 h, repeated two to three times. The solution from the dialysis bag was collected and centrifuged at high speed for 10 min to remove any precipitates and bubbles.  

Chromatography and Protein Purification.  Anion exchange chromatography was performed using a HiTrap Q HP (5 mL) column, with a linear elution gradient of 0 − 2 M NaCl in 20 mM Tris (pH 8.1). The elution peaks containing the target protein were collected, combined, and concentrated. The concentrated protein samples were then subjected to size-exclusion chromatography using a Superdex 75 10/300 column.  

Phage Display.  Library Construction and Screening.  The engineered scTCR gene was cloned into a phage display vector by using Nco I/Not I restriction sites to create a template for the phage display library. Discontinuous mutant primers were utilized to construct the CDR3 α /CDR3 β  variable region library. This library was then electroporated into TG1-competent cells for two to three rounds of phage screening. During library screening, the library bacteria were grown in a 2 ×  YT medium with 100  μ g/mL ampicillin and 2% glucose at 37  ° C until the OD 600  reached 0.4 − 0.5. Helper phage was then added, mixed, and incubated at 37  ° C for 30 min without agitation. The culture was centrifuged at low speed, and the pellet was resuspended in 2 ×  YT medium containing 100  μ g/mL ampicillin and 50  μ g/mL kanamycin, followed by overnight incubation at 26  ° C.  

Phage Precipitation and pMHC Complex Formation.  The phage- containing supernatant was collected and mixed with PEG/NaCl solution in an ice bath for 1 h. After three rounds of centrifugation, the phage particles were resuspended in PBS. A 3% milk solution was added to the phage solution for blocking, followed by the addition of biotinylated pMHC solution and incubation at room temperature for 1 h to form the phage-pMHC complex. Streptavidin magnetic beads were then added to form the magnetic bead-phage-pMHC complex, which was washed three times by using magnet adsorption. Trypsin was added and reacted at room temperature for 30 min. Postreaction, the supernatant was collected using magnet adsorption and used to infect preactivated TG1 cells. The infected bacteria were plated and cultured upside down at 30  ° C overnight.  

Phage ELISA Assays.  Single clones were cultured in 2 ×  YT medium with 100  μ g/mL ampicillin and 2% glucose at 37  ° C overnight. Each culture was then transferred to a 96-well plate and incubated with helper phage at 37  ° C for 1 h. After centrifugation, the pellet was resuspended in 200  μ L of 2 ×  YT medium with 100  μ g/mL ampicillin and 50  μ g/mL kanamycin and incubated at 26  ° C overnight. A 96-well ELISA plate was coated with streptavidin, followed by the addition of biotinylated pMHC for 30 min. The plate was blocked with PBS containing 6% milk and washed three to five times. M13-HRP antibody and color-substrate solution were added to each well, and the absorbance (OD 450 ) was measured by using a microplate reader. Clones with OD 450  values greater than 0.45 were selected for DNA sequencing to identify the corresponding mutants.  

Biolayer Interferometry.  The binding kinetics between TCRs and pMHC were analyzed using the Octet Red384 instrument (ForteBio), which employs biofilm-based optical interference technology on a fiber optic biosensor. This method allows for the calculation of binding dissociation constants by monitoring the kinetic parameters of the interacting molecules. In our experiments, biotinylated pMHC molecules were immobilized on an SA (Streptavidin) sensor. The binding dissociation constants ( K D  values) between the biotinylated pMHC and various TCRs were calculated using multipoint steady-state fitting. This process involved measuring the interaction kinetics, which provided the necessary data to determine the affinity of the TCRs for their respective pMHC targets.  

DSF for Determining  T m  Values.  The melting temperatures ( T m ) of scTCRs and their corresponding  αβ -TCRs were determined by using DSF. For this analysis, scTCR samples were prepared at a concentration of 5  μ M. We used 10 ×  SYPRO Orange Protein Gel Stain as the dye, with an excitation wavelength of 470 nm and an emission wavelength of 570 nm. The buffer composition for the samples included 25 mM Tris and 150 mM NaCl at pH 8.0.  

Sample Preparation and the DSF Procedure.  A total volume of 200 μ L of each sample was prepared for testing. Four aliquots of 40  μ L each were taken for parallel experiments. The samples were then placed in a Q5 fluorescence quantitative PCR instrument (manufactured by the ABI Company). The DSF process began with cooling from room temperature to 10.0  ° C (the initial temperature for the experiment), followed by a 1 min stabilization period at 10.0  ° C. The samples were then heated at a rate of 0.10  ° C/s, with the temperature range set from 10.00 to 95.00  ° C. Finally, the annealing rate was set at 0.15  ° C/s, bringing the temperature down from 95.00 to 4.00  ° C.  

Data Analysis.  The raw fluorescence data were smoothed by using the analysis software. The  T m  values were determined by identifying the temperature at which the upward slope of the fluorescence versus temperature curve was steepest. This method provided precise measurements of the thermal stability of scTCRs and  αβ -TCRs.
