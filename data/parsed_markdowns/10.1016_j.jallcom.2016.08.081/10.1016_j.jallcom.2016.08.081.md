Overheating exposures of turbine blades during service increased the risk of service safety in aircraft engines. Nevertheless, limited investigations about microstructural degradation induced by the over- heating temperature and the effects on mechanical property of wrought superalloys were reported. In st   this paper, wrought superalloy GH4037 sectioned from an un-used 1 stage turbine blade of an aircraft engine was adopted to investigate the microstructural degradation caused by short-time thermal exposure at very high temperature (higher than normal service temperature) and the microstructural evolution during the mechanical tests after the short-time thermal exposures. The effects of micro- structural degradation on mechanical properties including hardness, high temperature tensile properties and creep properties were also analyzed. The results indicate that gradual dissolutions of  g 0   phase and grain boundary (GB) carbides were the typical manifestation of microstructural degradation in GH4037 alloy during the short-time thermal exposures at 1000 e 1140    C for 3 min and 5 min. The dissolved  g 0 phase and GB carbides re-precipitated in a very short time (less than 12 min) as ultra- ﬁ ne particles and continuous/cellular GB carbides during 850    C tensile tests and creep tests at 850    C/196 MPa. Due to the re-precipitated ultra- ﬁ ne  g 0   phase, the tensile strength at 850    C after exposing to 1140    C for 3 min was analogous to those of specimens without the thermal exposure. But the re-precipitated continuous/ cellular GB carbides would cause the signi ﬁ cant decrease of tensile ductility at 850    C and the creep elongation at 850    C/196 MPa. On the other hand, the dissolution of  g 0   phase and GB carbides during the short-time thermal exposures reduced the creep properties at 700  C/471 MPa signi ﬁ cantly, as it took much longer time (more than 18.1 h) for the re-precipitation of  g 0   phase when the overheated alloys were exposed at 700  C/471 MPa. The work provides the guidance for overheating-inspection and diagnosis of service safety for blades made of wrought superalloys.

# 1. Introduction

Ni-based superalloys are widely used as turbine blades for aviation and power plant applications, which are expected to withstand aggressive service conditions, such as high temperature, complex stress combination and oxidation environment  [1,2] . The deformation induced by creep is one of the dominant failure modes in turbine blades  [3,4] . For the sake of safety and economy, the diagnosis of service-induced damage and the prognosis of service life are of interests to users, manufacturers and designers. Since  

Under some abnormal circumstances, such as the emergency regimes of One Engine Inoperative (OEI) and the blocking of cooling passages, the turbine blades in turbine engines would be exposed to the excessive elevated temperature by over 150    C higher than the designed service temperature of the alloy, resulting in the so- called overheating exposure  [6,7] . The overheating exposure of turbine blades was strictly controlled to limited time periods dur- ing service  [2] . The corresponding microstructural degradation of turbine blades could be introduced instantly in terms of the coarsening and dissolution of  g 0   precipitates as well as the disso- lution of carbides, even local incipient melting  [8 e 12] . Li et al. re- ported that the coarsening and the dissolution of  g 0   precipitates st   occurred in the 1 stage turbine blades during overheating service, which were made of DZ22 alloy, and caused the signi ﬁ cant reduction of creep strength  [13] . When the service temperature exceeded the solidus temperature of blade alloys, the incipient melting was introduced and catastrophically decreased the creep strength, resulting in cracks in the serviced blades  [11] .  

In the late 1950s ’ , a research program about the effects of overheating exposure on creep properties of polycrystalline su- peralloys was implemented by University of Michigan and NASA [14,15] . It was indicated that the creep properties were unaffected or increased by overheating exposures, and the resistance to overheating exposures increased with the volume fraction of  g 0 phase. Nevertheless, investigations about overheating exposures to superalloys were limitedly published after this program. Recently, a speci ﬁ c test bench (STB) has been established for overheating simulation of in- ﬂ ight conditions by Cormier et al.  [7,16] . The in- ﬂ uence of overheating exposure on the creep behavior of single crystal Ni-based superalloy was investigated by using this STB [17,18] . In previous researches on polycrystalline superalloys, the overheating exposures were introduced during creep and the attention was mainly paid on creep properties. The micro- strucutural degradation was not characterized in detail due to the limitation of microstructural characterization techniques at that time. As a consequence, the impacts of overheating temperature on microstructural degradation in polycrystalline superalloys during overheating exposure may not be revealed, and the in ﬂ uence of microstructural degradation on mechanical properties during and after overheating exposure has not been elucidated, either.  

In this study, GH4037 wrought superalloy was sectioned from st   an un-used 1 stage turbine blade for an aircraft engine. Special short-time thermal exposures were carried out at temperatures much higher than the normal service temperatures of this alloy to simulate the temperature environment during overheating service condition. The objectives of this paper are: 1) to study the effects of temperature and time on micro s tru cut ural degradation during short-time thermal exposures; 2) to investigate the microstructural evolution in the thermal-exposed alloy during mechanical tests; 3) to understand the in ﬂ uence of microstructural degradation on mechanical properties of the alloy.  

# 2. Experimental  

# 2.1. Materials and heat treatment  

The microstructures of GH4037 alloy bar and blades made of GH4037 alloy might differ from each other due to the manufacture processes. To avoid this difference, materials in this research was st   obtained from an un-used forged 1 stage turbine blade for an aircraft engine, which was made of GH4037 wrought superalloy (Ni-13.5Cr-6W-3Mo-2Al-2Ti-0.3V-0.06C, in weight percent). The blade had been subjected to a standard heat treatment (SHT, 1180  C/2 h/air cooling (AC)  þ  1050    C/4 h/AC  þ  800  C/16 h/AC) [19] . The specimens without short-time thermal exposure were hereafter referred as SHT alloy, which contained 20.2 wt%  g 0   pre- cipitates, 0.5 wt% Cr 23 C 6 , 0.3 wt% TiC and M 3 B 2  borides based on the physicochemical phase analyses by NCS Testing Technology Co., Ltd.. The grain boundaries were mainly  ﬁ lled with Cr 23 C 6  carbides [20] . The solvus temperature of  g 0   phase in SHT alloy was deter- mined to be about 935    C by DSC using the heating curve.  

To simulate microstructural evolution caused by overheating temperature and its effect on mechanical properties of the blades, specimens for microstructural observation, tensile and creep tests were sectioned from the blade. Special short-time thermal expo- sures listed in  Table 1  were conducted in laboratory and followed by water-quenching. The specimens exposed to the short-time ther- mal exposures at 1000 e 1140    C for 3 min and 5 min are named as A1\~A4 and B1\~B4, respectively, as shown in  Table 1 .  

# 2.2. Mechanical property tests  

To gain insight into the strength of  g ’ - g  matrix within the grains, the micro-hardness was measured at locations away from grain boundaries. A Vickers indentor with a load of 3 kg was adopted to measure the micro-hardness at 6 locations for each specimen.  

Because of the complex geometry of turbine blades  [20] , plate- type specimens were sectioned from the blade for the investigation of tensile and creep properties. The geometry and dimensions of the plate-type specimens are shown in  Fig. 1 (a). Tensile tests were carried out to gain the tensile strength of SHT alloy at 850    C, 1000    C and 1100    C. To understand the in ﬂ uence of microstruc- tural degradation caused by the short-time thermal exposures on the strength of the alloy at normal service temperature range, an A4 specimen was also subjected to the tensile test at 850    C. The tensile test procedure was modi ﬁ ed based on Chinese Standard GB/T 4338- 2006 (ISO 783: 1999, MOD) to gain the microstructure in the samples at 850    C, 1000    C and 1100    C for 5 min. It took about 30 min to heat the specimens rapidly from room temperature to test temperatures, and then the specimens were hold for 5 min before loading. The tests were strain controlled by a stain rate of 3.3E-4 per second. The ruptured specimens were water quenched to room temperature.  

To investigate the effects of the short-time thermal exposures on creep properties of GH4037 alloy, creep tests were conducted in air at 700  C/471 MPa and 850  C/196 MPa. Extra stress rupture tests at 850  C/196 MPa were conducted. One creep test was carried out for each microstructure condition, while at least 2 stress rupture test were carried out for each microstructure condition. The creep/ stress rupture test conditions were selected with the consideration of Chinese Aeronautical Industry Standard HB/Z 91 e 1985. The testing procedures were compliant with Chinese Standard GB/T 2039-2009, which is similar to International Standard ISO 204: 2009. It took about 3 h to heat the specimens from room temper- ature to the test temperatures. The specimens were hold for 2 h before loading during creep tests. The creep elongations were monitored continuously by using an extensometer attached to the ridges of special  ﬁ xtures, which were made of single crystal Ni- based superalloy and designed to hold the non-standard plate- type specimen during creep tests, as shown in  Fig. 1 (b). To get the microstructural evolution of the specimens (SHT alloy and B4 specimens) during creep tests at 850  C/196 MPa, creep tests were interrupted after holding at 850    C for 2 h before loading and at strains ranging from 0.8% to 1.0%. Then the specimens were water quenched to observe the microstructure.

# 4. Discussion  

The risks of overheating service are the deformation or fracture of turbine blades during overheating exposures and the detri- mental effects on service performance of the blades during the normal service afterwards. Since the mechanical properties of wrought superalloys are affected by  g 0   precipitates in the matrix, morphologies of GB carbides and grain size  [22 e 24] , The rela- tionship between microstructure evolution and the mechanical properties during and after the short-time thermal exposures should be analyzed.  

# 4.1. Microstructure evolution during the thermal exposures and mechanical property tests  

GH4037 alloy was designed for turbine blades operated at 800 e 850    C, and the microstructure of standard heat-treated alloy consists of about 20.0 wt%  g 0   precipitates, 0.9 wt% carbides  [21] . Grain growth was observed in GH4037 alloy after heat treatment at 1200    C for 30min  [25] . Since grain growth resulted from grain boundary migration, which could be retarded by GB carbides, it was implied that GB carbides were dissolved at 1200    C in this alloy. The dissolved GB carbides in turbine blades could re-precipitate along grain boundaries as cellular carbides during service procedure  [6] , which was detrimental to mechanical properties. On the other hand, the precipitation of  g 0   phase was barely observed at 650 e 750    C in GH4037 alloy, but abundant  g 0   precipitates were found in the matrix after aging at 800    C  [25] . According to previous study  [26] , mechanical properties of wrought superalloys increased with the reduction of  g 0   size. However, it was reported that the optimized size of  g 0   precipitates was about 100 nm in GH4037 alloy [27] . Signi ﬁ cant reduction of the notched stress rupture life at 700    C was introduced by  g 0   precipitates with an average size of smaller than 50 nm. The volume fraction of  g 0   phase was also crucial. Generally, mechanical properties of superalloys increased with the volume fraction of  g 0   phase  [24] .  

# 4.1.1. During the thermal exposures  

In the current study, the microstructural degradation of GH4037 alloy caused by the short-time thermal exposures could be classi- ﬁ ed by four types: partial dissolution of  g 0   precipitates and no obvious change in GB carbides (Type I, in A1, B1 and A2 specimens), complete dissolution of  g 0   precipitates and no obvious change in GB carbides (Type II, in B2 specimen), complete dissolution of  g 0   pre- cipitates and obvious dissolution of GB carbides (Type III, in A3 and B3 specimens), complete dissolution of both  g 0   precipitates and GB carbides (Type IV, in A4 and B4 specimens).  Fig. 3 ,  Table 3 ,  Fig. 4  and Table 4  indicate that the dissolution of GB carbides and  g 0   pre- cipitates was accelerated by exposure temperature. With elevating the exposure temperature, the solubility of alloying elements in the matrix increased, and the diffusion coef ﬁ cient of each alloying element increased. Therefore, the driving force and the dynamic factors for the dissolution of GB carbides and  g 0   phase were improved.  

4.1.2. During mechanical property tests after the thermal exposures  

The microstructural degradation would cause supersaturated  g matrix at normal service temperature range, resulting in micro- structural evolution during tensile test at 850    C, creep tests at 850  C/196 MPa and 700  C/471 MPa. It is shown in  Fig. 6 (a e c) as well as  Table 6  that  g 0   phase and GB carbides re-precipitated in a short time (within 12min, including 5min without loading and 7min with loading) at 850    C as ultra- ﬁ ne spherical  g 0   precipitates and continuous/cellular carbides, respectively. It is implied that the nuclei of  g 0   phase occurred in a short time at 850    C in the over- heated specimens.  

The microstructure evolution in specimens with Type I and Type IV microstructural degradation during creep tests at 850/196 MPa and 700/471 MPa was summarized in  Fig. 11  based on results shown in  Figs. 8 e 10 . It is indicated that bimodal distribution of  g 0 precipitates and  ﬁ ne  g 0   precipitates with the uniform size were formed in the specimens with Type I and Type IV microstructures

However, it is presented that it took more than 18.1 h for  g 0 phase to re-precipitate in the overheated specimens at 700    C, as no g 0   precipitates were observed in the ruptured B3 specimen after creep test for 16.1 h at 700  C/471 MPa and the specimen had been hold at 700    C for 2 h before loading. It should also be noted that the morphologies of GB carbides mainly stayed to be similar to those in the specimens before creep tests at 700  C/471 MPa.  

# 4.2. Mechanical properties during the thermal exposures  

# 4.2.1. Hardness  

Osada's work has proved that the hardness of polycrystalline superalloys was attributed to the  g 0   precipitates, carbides, grain boundaries and solid strengthening  [28] . Previous study indicated that the hardness of wrought superalloys containing low volume fraction of  g 0   phase was predominantly dependent on  g 0   pre- cipitates in the matrix  [27] . The hardness was improved by the increase of  g 0   volume fraction, and increased initially with the coarsening of  g 0   precipitates and then decreased by the overaging of  g 0   precipitates  [1] . The coarsening and dissolution of  g 0   precipi- tate caused by overheating exposure of turbine blades made of superalloys containing low volume fraction of  g 0   phase were not able to rejuvenate during cooling procedure in the following ser- vice exposure  [10,25,26] . Therefore, hardness tests were usually conducted on these blades for overheating inspection.  

In this study, specimens after the thermal exposures were water quenched to preserve the microstructure at very high temperature for hardness tests.  Table 5  indicates that the hardness of GH4037 alloy was decreased by increasing the exposure temperature and time. It should be noted that the hardness indentations were remote from grain boundaries. The sizes of indentations were about 80  m m and smaller than the average grain sizes listed in  Table 2 . Therefore, the in ﬂ uences of grain size and GB carbides on hardness were neglected. The hardness was affected by the degradation of  g 0 precipitates in the matrix.  Table 4  indicates that  g 0   precipitates started to dissolve at 1000    C and disappeared when the GH4037 alloy was overheated at 1050    C for 5min and   1100    C for 3min. The corresponding hardness decreased with the reduction of  g 0 volume fraction. When the dissolution of  g 0   phase was completed, the hardness stayed in the range of 181Hv and 201Hv.  

# 4.2.2. Tensile strength at high temperature  

It is generally accepted that the major contribution to the strength of superalloys was provided by  g 0   phase in the matrix, while the carbides provided little additional strengthening  [1] . The high temperature strength was improved by increasing the volume fraction of  g 0   phase. For a constant volume fraction of  g 0   phase, the increase of  g 0   size by over-aging reduced the strength of superalloys [29] .  

In the current study, the tensile strength of GH4037 alloy decreased from 478 MPa at 850    C to 146 MPa and 39 MPa at 1000    C and 1100    C, respectively. However, A4 specimen with the complete dissolution of  g 0   phase and GB carbides ( Tables 3 and 4 ) exhibits the strength of about 484 MPa at 850    C ( Fig. 5 ), which was similar to the strength of SHT alloy at 850    C.  Fig. 6 (c) shows that ultra- ﬁ ne  g 0   phase re-precipitated at 850    C within about 12min in A4 specimen with the average size of 26 nm ( Table 6 ), which was much smaller than the average size of  g 0   precipitates in SHT alloys (80 nm). The decrease in the size of  g 0   precipitates would increase the force for dislocations to bypass  g 0   precipitates  [1]  and increase the strength of the matrix. On the other hand, the ductility of A4 specimen decreased remarkably at 850    C, as shown in  Fig. 5 . Fig. 6 (d) indicates that cracks initiated and propagated along grain boundaries during the tensile test. The continuous/cellular carbides re-precipitated along grain boundaries ( Fig. 6 (a, b)) provided the sites for crack initiation and propagation, and should account for the reduction in the ductility of A4 specimen at 850    C.  

Based on discussion above, it is indicated that the hardness and tensile strength of GH4037 alloy were signi ﬁ cantly reduced by the dissolution of  g 0   phase during the short-time thermal exposures. However, the ultra- ﬁ ne  g 0   precipitates and continuous/cellular GB carbides re-formed instantly during mechanical tests at 850    C. As a result, the specimen after the thermal treatment at 1140    C for 3min exhibited an analogous strength and much lower ductility at 850    C in comparison with SHT alloy ( Fig. 5 ).  

# 4.3. Creep properties after the thermal exposures  

The creep resistance of wrought superalloys was also in ﬂ uenced by  g 0   precipitates, GB carbides and grain size. The creep properties was enhanced by increasing the volume fraction of  g 0   phase, and the smaller size of  g 0   precipitates also improved creep resistance of wrought superalloys  [24] . The cellular and continuous  ﬁ lm-like GB carbides were believed to contribute to the initiation and propa- gation of cavities and cracks  [1,24,30] . Fine and discrete GB pre- cipitates promoted the creep strength  [31,32] . It is generally believed that the creep life can be enhanced by increasing the grain size  [24] .  

# 4.3.1. Creep properties at 850  C/196 MPa  

Table 7  indicates that the stress rupture lives of A1, B1 and A2 specimens (Type I microstructural degradation, as shown in Tables 3 and 4 ), were prolonged to about 101 h at 850  C/196 MPa. Fig. 9 (a) and  Fig. 11  reveal that the  ﬁ ne  g 0   phase precipitated among the un-dissolved  g 0   precipitates in these specimens during creep tests. The bimodal  g 0   distribution contributed to the improvement of the creep lives, which are in consistent with the previous study [33] . In the specimens without  g 0   precipitates before creep tests, such as B2, A3, B3, A4 and B4 specimens, creep lives at 850  C/ 196 MPa decreased with the reduction of GB carbide densities, as presented in  Table 3 ,  Tables 4 and 7 . For the specimens with the obvious dissolution of GB carbides, the dissolved GB carbides re- precipitated as cellular GB carbides in a very short time during mechanical tests at 850    C. The re-precipitation of cellular GB car- bides during stress rupture tests at 850  C/196 MPa would accel- erate the rupture failure. Therefore, the lower the GB carbide density was, the more cellular GB carbides would be formed, and the creep lives would  ﬁ nally decrease.  

Fig. 7 (a) and  Table 8  show that the minimum creep rate of B4 specimen was slightly lower than that of SHT alloy, and the rupture strain was remarkably reduced with the similar creep life to SHT alloy. According to the strengthening mechanism of  g 0   precipitates, the Orowan stress for the movements of dislocations was inversely proportional to the size of  g 0   precipitates  [1] . It is indicated in Table 6  that the  g 0   phase re-precipitated during creep test at 850  C/ 196 MPa in B4 specimen, the size of  g 0   precipitates was much  ﬁ ner than that in SHT alloy. Therefore, the movement of dislocation in B4 specimen became more dif ﬁ cult than that in SHT alloy, resulting in the reduction of the minimum creep rate. In the meanwhile, the cellular GB carbides precipitated during creep tests at 850  C/ 196 MPa ( Fig. 8 (b) and  Fig. 11 ) provided the sites for crack initiation and paths for crack propagation, resulting in the premature of cracks ( Fig. 8 (d)) and the reduction of the rupture strain.  

# 4.3.2. Creep properties at 700  C/471 MPa  

In this study, the re-precipitation of  g 0   phase took more than 18.1 h at 700  C/471 MPa, as presented in  Fig. 11 . Since the volume fractions of  g 0   precipitates in SHT alloy, A2 and B2 specimens were 19.1%, 14.2% and 0, respectively, it can be deduced that the contents of  g 0   precipitates in these specimens at the beginning of creep procedure were in the order of SHT alloy, A2 and B2 specimens. With the decrease of  g 0   volume fraction, the stress for dislocation movement was reduced. Then it was easier for dislocations to move toward grain boundaries and pile up, leading to the stress con- centration for crack initiation. Thus, the increase in minimum creep rate and the reduction in creep lives were introduced into A2 and B2 specimens at 700  C/471 MPa ( Fig. 7 (b) and  Table 8 ).  

As only intergranular cracks were observed in crept specimens ( Fig. 9 (d)), the GB carbides should be also analyzed to account for the reduction of creep properties at 700  C/471 MPa. It is revealed in Fig.10  (b e d) and  Fig.11  that the re-precipitation of GB carbides was insigni ﬁ cant during creep test under this condition. The grain boundaries free of carbides were not immune from GB sliding and the linkage of creep gravities into cracks, as shown in  Fig. 9 (d). Therefore, the creep lives of B2, B3 and B4 specimens at 700  C/ 471 MPa decreased from 30.6 h to 3.1 h with the reduction of linear density of GB carbides ( Tables 3 and 8 ).  

The results of this study indicate that: 1) the mechanical prop- erty results, including the hardness and stress rupture lives at 850  C/196 MPa, were not suf ﬁ cient enough to reveal the degra- dation in microstructure and service performance induced by overheating service exposure in blades made of GH4037 alloy; 2) if the cooling rate is relatively slow after overheating service expo- sure, or the normal service temperature is about 850    C, the re- precipitation of  g 0   phase and GB carbides might occur, then the reduction of tensile strength at 850    C and creep life at 850  C/ 196 MPa might not be observed; 3) if the temperature of blades is cooled to less than 700    C in a short time after the overheating exposure, the risk of service safety for turbine blades made of GH4037 alloy is much higher when the normal service temperature is 700    C instead of 850    C. The results and indications of this study also provide the guidance for overheating-inspection and diagnosis of service safety for blades made of wrought superalloys.  

# 5. Conclusions  

The microstructural degradation and its effects on the me- chanical properties of wrought superalloy GH4037 were investi- gated during and after the short-time thermal exposures at 1000 e 1140    C for 3min and 5min. The conclusions can be sum- marized as follows:  

(1) The microstructural degradation during the thermal expo- sures was typi ﬁ ed by the dissolution of  g 0   precipitates and GB carbides. The  g 0   precipitates started to dissolve at 1000    C, and was accelerated by elevating the temperature. All of  g 0 phase was dissolved at 1050    C for 5min and at   1100    C for 3min. Obvious disappearance of GB carbides were observed when the exposure temperature was 1100  C. Complete dissolution of GB carbides was accomplished at 1140    C for 3min, and the grain growth was evoked simultaneously. (2) Re-precipitation of  g 0   phase was identi ﬁ ed at 700    C and 850    C as ultra- ﬁ ne precipitates. It took less than 12min for  g 0 precipitates to reform at 850    C. But the re-precipitation of  g 0 phase with the size suitable for SEM observation was not found in about 18.1 h at 700    C. The dissolved GB carbides re- precipitated along grain boundaries as continuous/cellular carbides at 850  C within 12min, while no GB carbides re- precipitated at 700    C within 5.1 h. (3) The hardness and high temperature strength of GH4037 alloy decreased predominantly with the dissolution of  g 0   pre- cipitates. Due to the re-precipitation of ultra- ﬁ ne  g 0   pre- cipitates and continuous/cellular GB carbides, the tensile strength of GH4037 alloy at 850  C with the complete dissolution of  g 0   precipitates and GB carbides after the thermal exposure at 1140    C for 3min was close to that of SHT alloy at 850  C, but the tensile ductility was reduced signi ﬁ cantly. (4) Due to the re-precipitation of ultra- ﬁ ne  g 0   phase in the ma- trix during the creep at 850  C/196 MPa, the drastic reduction in the creep lives did not occurred in the alloys after the thermal exposures. However, the formation of continuous/ cellular GB carbides during the creep tests in the exposed alloys with obvious dissolution of GB carbides decreased the creep life slightly. The creep strain rate and rupture strain were reduced obviously. (5) The creep properties of GH4037 alloy at 700  C/471 MPa after different thermal exposures deteriorated dramatically by the dissolution of  g 0   phase and GB carbides, which were not able to re-precipitate in a short time at 700    C.
