# Co Effect on As-cast and Heat-Treated Microstructures in Ru-Containing Single-Crystal Superalloys  

QIANYING SHI, XIANFEI DING, MEILING WANG, YUNRONG ZHENG, JIANPING HE, SAMMY TIN, and QIANG FENG  

The eﬀect of Co on the as-cast and heat-treated microstructures was investigated in two experimental Ni-based single-crystal superalloys containing low levels of Re and Ru. The experimental results indicated that increasing the Co content from 7.9 to 15.8 wt pct decreased the volume fraction of ( c  +  c ¢ ) eutectic and the solidiﬁcation segregation ratio of W. High levels of Co additions were also found to decrease the solvus temperatures of the  c ¢  phase and ( c  +  c ¢ ) eutectic as well as the solidus temperature. During the long-term thermal exposure at 1373 K (1100   C), no TCP phases precipitated in either alloy. However, the coarsening and coalescence of  c ¢  precipitates in the alloy containing 15.8 wt pct Co was slower than that in the other alloy with 7.9 wt pct Co. In the current study, high levels of Co additions decreased the equilibrium volume fraction of  c ¢  phase, leading to a change in the partitioning ratios of TCP-forming elements Cr, Mo, Re, and W between the  c  and  c ¢  phases. This change resulted in a lower degree of elemental supersaturation in the  c  matrix and improved the phase stability of the  c / c ¢ microstructure. These experimental results were then compared with those obtained from multi- component thermodynamic calculations, and good agreement was observed.

# I. INTRODUCTION  

Ni- BASED  single-crystal superalloys with superior properties at high temperature are widely used as turbine blades in aircraft engines. In the past 30 years, various alloying approaches were employed to develop multiple classes or generations of Ni-based superalloys with 1 ]   improved performance. To enhance the degree of solid solution strengthening in the  c  matrix and control the coarsening kinetics of the  c ¢  precipitates, the level of Re content was continuously increased, from 0 wt pct in the ﬁrst-generation single-crystal superalloys to 5 to 6 wt pct 2 , ]   in the third and fourth generation superalloys. Since Re additions exhibit limited solubility in the  c ¢  phase, it partitions preferentially to the  c  phase. The overall composition of single-crystal Ni-based superalloys needs to be carefully controlled as supersaturation of the  c  phase with Re, W, Mo, and Cr will lead to the microstructural instabilities and the formation of detrimental topologi- cally close-packed (TCP) phases during exposures at elevated temperature. Ru additions have recently been identiﬁed as an eﬀective suppressor of TCP phases and are characteristic additions in the fourth-generation single- 4 – 7 ]   crystal superalloys. Since alloys containing high levels of Re and Ru additions tend to have relatively high densities and cost, the overall levels of these alloying additions need to be reduced to meet the practical demands of aircraft engines.  

Co is present in the large majority of commercially available multi-component Ni-based superalloys. As an alloying addition, Co increases the strength at elevated temperature by reducing the stacking fault energy of  c 8 ]   matrix. It has been shown that Co inﬂuences the formation of  c ¢  phase, carbides, and TCP phases in Ni- 9 – 13 ]   based superalloys. Previous studies had shown that increasing the level of Co in wrought Ni-based super- alloys lowered the solvus temperatures of  c ¢  phase and 9 ]   carbides. The volume fraction, mean size, and com- position of  c ¢  phase in the cast polycrystalline superalloy MAR-M247 was also inﬂuenced by Co additions. Moreover, increasing the level of Co additions was reported to be beneﬁcial for microstructural stability by preventing the transformation of discrete carbides into 10 continuous carbide ﬁlms along grain boundaries. Additionally, previous reports showed that Co also aﬀected the formation of TCP phases, such as  r  and  l phases. Co additions were found to prevent the forma- [ 11 ]   tion of  r  phase and  l  phase in MAR-M421 and 12 ]   single-crystal MAR-M247, respectively. On the other hand, Bu ¨ rgel’s study on the directional solidiﬁed super- alloys showed that Co-free alloy had a comparatively more stable microstructure with respect to TCP phase formation than a nominally similar alloy containing 13 ] 4 wt pct Co.  

Currently, only a limited number of studies on the eﬀect of Co on microstructural stability in single-crystal superalloys have been reported and there is some evidence to suggest that Co could play a role similar 6 ]   to Ru. Walston  et al. investigated a series of Ni-based single-crystal superalloys and suggested that high levels of Co additions could induce the reverse partitioning of alloying elements and then improve the resistance to 14 ]   TCP formation. Wang’s  et al. work also demon- strated that TCP formation was suppressed by promot- ing the diﬀusion process due to more Co additions. However, there are some contradictory viewpoints about Co eﬀect on microstructure in single superalloys. Erickson claimed that lower Co concentrations in a third-generation alloy CMSX-10 led to attainment of 15 ] acceptable alloy stability. In addition, previous reports on the inﬂuence of Co on solidiﬁcation segrega- tion, phase transformation temperatures, and other microstructural characteristics of the as-cast and heat- [ 6 , , – 18 ]   treated Ni-based single-crystal superalloys are limited and inconsistent.  

In the current study, two experimental Ni-based single-crystal superalloys containing relatively low con- centrations of Re, Ru, and diﬀerent levels of Co additions were investigated. The inﬂuence of Co addi- tions on the as-cast and heat-treated microstructures was studied through the characterization of the solidiﬁcation segregation behavior, phase transformation tempera- tures, the evolution of  c ¢  phase during the thermal exposure, the elemental partitioning behavior, and the lattice misﬁt. In addition, thermodynamic calculations were performed for these two alloys using  PANDAT software with the PanNi thermodynamic database from CompuTherm LLC (CompuTherm LLC, Madison, WI). The experimental results were compared with the ther- modynamic predictions to provide an in-depth under- standing of the two alloys studied in this work and the eﬀect of Co in these experimental alloys.  

# II. EXPERIMENTAL  

Two experimental Ni-based single-crystal superalloys with compositions listed in Table  I  were directionally 19 ] solidiﬁed using a liquid metal cooling (LMC) process. The compositions of two alloys are nominally identical with the only diﬀerence being the level of Co addition. Alloy A contains 7.9 wt pct Co while alloy B contains 15.8 wt pct Co.  

The solidus ( T S ), liquidus ( T L ), and  c ¢  solvus temper- atures ( T c ¢ ) were determined using NETZSCH STA 449C diﬀerential scanning calorimeter (DSC) with a heating rate of 10 K (10   C)/min between 1273 K and 1773 K (1000   C and 1500   C) under ﬂowing argon gas to minimize oxidation.  T S  and  T L  were deﬁned as the intersection temperature of the baseline and the tangent line with the maximum slope at the endothermic peak in the range of 1573 K to 1723 K (1300   C to 1450   C), respectively. To investigate the eﬀect of Co on phase transformation temperatures, the two alloys were heat- treated at high temperature in the range of 1543 K to 1613 K (1270   C to 1340   C) for 4 hours followed by a water quench. Due to the alloy composition diﬀerence, complete solution treatments for alloys A and B were carried out at 1603 K (1330   C) for 8 hours and 1593 K (1320   C) for 8 hours, respectively. Subsequently, the thermal exposure at 1373 K (1100   C) in the range of 8 and 800 hours was performed in both alloys.  

The cross-sectional microstructures perpendicular to h 001 i  were examined. Samples were prepared using standard metallographic techniques and etched using a solution of 1 pct HF, 33 pct CH 3 COOH, 33 pct HNO 3 and 33 pct H 2 O. The volume fractions of ( c  +  c ¢ ) eutectic and  c ¢  precipitates were determined using the standard point count method. The  c ¢  size was measured using Image-Pro Plus software. To obtain statistically signif- icant results, three to ﬁve images were used for deter- mining the volume fractions of ( c  +  c ¢ ) eutectic and  c precipitates as well as the size of  c ¢  phase in each alloy.

# IV. DISCUSSION  

# A.  Solidiﬁcation Segregation  

During the solidiﬁcation of multi-component Ni-based single-crystal superalloys,  c  phase-forming elements and  c ¢  phase-forming elements show a prefer- ence to segregate into dendrite cores and interdendritic regions, respectively. The strong tendency of refractory alloying elements to partition to dendrite cores provides a driving force for the precipitation of detrimental TCP phases after extended periods of elevated temperature   21 , ] ¢ exposure. The presence of the ( c  +  c ) eutectic within the as-cast structure indicates that substantial 23 ] dendritic segregation occurred during solidiﬁcation. In this study, the reduction in the volume fraction of the ( c  +  c ¢ ) eutectic suggested that the higher concentration of Co in alloy B lowered the overall degree of solidiﬁ- cation segregation. These observations are consistent with the solidiﬁcation segregation ratios measured for alloys A and B. Those results in Table  II  indicate that higher level of Co addition in alloy B reduced the segregation tendency of W during solidiﬁcation. How- ever, the segregation ratio of Re, which is the most strongly segregated element in single-crystal superalloys, was aﬀected slightly by Co addition. This result is 16 , ] inconsistent with previous reports.  

Currently, there are conﬂicting reports on the inﬂu- ence of Co on Re segregation during the solidiﬁcation of 16 single-crystal Ni-based superalloys. Caldwell  et al. investigated the eﬀect of Co addition (4.0 to 12.2 wt pct) on the elemental segregation in a series of single-crystal superalloys containing 5.9 wt pct Re and no Ru addi- tion, and reported that Re exhibited a signiﬁcant decrease in the segregation tendency with increasing 18 ]   the Co content. However, Wang  et al. suggested that the extent of Re segregation increased along with increases of Co addition (from 0 to 12 wt pct) in alloys containing 4.0 wt pct Re and no Ru. Diﬀerent from the above-mentioned reports, both experimental alloys in this study contain low levels of Re (2.1 wt pct) and Ru (2.0 wt pct). The overall content of Re itself was lower and then the eﬀect of diﬀerent Co content on its segregation behavior was not obvious. It was reported that Ru could also inﬂuence the segregation behavior of 16 , – 26 ]   Re to varying degrees. In addition, the diﬀerent techniques for quantifying the degree of segregation between the dendritic and interdendritic regions in the current and other studies might also contribute to the discrepancies associated with the inﬂuence of Co on the segregation ratio of Re. It should be also noted that two investigated experimental alloys were directionally solid- iﬁed  via  a LMC process. Compared with the conven- tional Bridgman process, the LMC process reduced primary dendrite arm spacing (PDAS) and eutectic fraction, and achieved a more homogeneously distrib-   ¢ 27 , ] uted  c  phase. As a result, the PDAS of the alloys investigated in this study are relatively small ( \~ 110  l m) when compared to other single-crystal Ni-based super- alloys with PDAS on the order of 300  l m. The reﬁnement of the dendritic structure and resulting short diﬀusion distances associated with the LMC process may also contribute to the observed eﬀects of Co additions on the segregation ratio of Re.  

# B.  Phase Transformation Temperatures  

To increase the strength of single-crystal superalloys, the coarse  c ¢  phase within the interdendritic regions and the eutectic of the as-cast alloys should be completely dissolved during solution treatment to allow for the reprecipitation of ﬁne  c ¢  particles when cooled below the   ¢ 29 ] c  solvus temperature. Compositional eﬀects on the phase transformation temperatures should be consid- ered in order to develop an optimized solution treatment process. It is well known that the  c ¢  solvus temperature is strongly inﬂuenced by Co additions. Previous investiga- tions on Ni-based wrought, cast polycrystalline, direc- tionally solidiﬁed, and single-crystal superalloys indicated that increasing the Co content signiﬁcantly   ¢ 6 , , , , ] decreased  c  solvus temperature. This was 13 clearly conﬁrmed in the current study. Bu ¨ rgel  et al. pointed that the reason was that Co increased the solubility of Al and possibly also of Ta in the  c -Ni solid solution matrix. Early work suggested that Ru additions induced a reverse parti- tioning behavior of alloying elements between the  c  and   ¢ 4 ] c  phases. However, later studies provided contradic- tory results on whether Ru additions aﬀect the elemental 6 , – 34 ]   partitioning behavior. Besides Ru, the eﬀect of Co on microstructural stability has also attracted much attention. In general, decreasing the level of Co in Ni- based superalloys should have a beneﬁcial eﬀect on microstructural stability, since the electron vacancy number ( N V ) of Co (1.61) is higher than that of Ni 13 ]   [ 15 ]   (0.66). Erickson investigated the third-generation single-crystal superalloy CMSX-10, and suggested that the tendency to form TCP phases was reduced by decreasing the Co content to 3 wt pct. However, with the development of the fourth-generation single-crystal 6 ]   superalloy EPM-102, Walston  et al. suggested that higher levels of Co additions (12 to 20 wt pct) restricted TCP phase precipitation during thermal exposures and neural network models indicated that the reverse par- titioning induced by increasing the Co content was helpful to improve microstructural stability. The changes in the partitioning behavior of alloying elements due to higher levels of Co were also observed in the current experimental study. As shown in Table  V , the experimental partitioning ratios of Re, Mo, W, and Cr decreased as a result of the increase in the Co concen- tration. It should be noted that changes of  c ¢  volume fraction can also aﬀect the partitioning behavior of alloying elements. In Hobbs’ work, the reduction of  c ¢ volume fraction was considered as one of the reasons as to why Ru additions decreased the tendency to form 35 ]   TCP phases in Ni-based single-crystal superalloys. In this study, the  c ¢  solvus temperature and volume fraction of  c ¢  phase decreased as Co additions increased from 7.9 to 15.8 wt pct (Figure  3  and Table  IV ). However, it should also be noted that the Al content in alloy B was slightly lower than that in alloy A (Table  I ), which could contribute to the decreased volume fraction of  c ¢ phase.  

Another important aspect that aﬀects the microstruc- tural stability in single-crystal superalloys is the evolu- tion of c ¢ precipitates, including the coarsening, coalescence, and rafting. One main driving force for the evolution of  c ¢  precipitates in the morphology and size is the coherency strain energy, which is caused by the lattice misﬁt between  c  and  c ¢  phases. The lattice misﬁt is inﬂuenced by the elemental partitioning behav- ior, and alloying elements with a larger atomic radius (Cr, Mo, W, and Re) in comparison with Ni can 36 , ]   signiﬁcantly change  a c  and  a c ¢ . A lower degree of the misﬁt corresponds to a decreased coherency strain energy between  c  and  c ¢  phases, and reduces the rate of coarsening or rafting of  c ¢  precipitates. In this study, alloy A with 7.9 wt pct Co content had a larger lattice misﬁt (  0.14 pct) than that (  0.06 pct) in alloy B with 15.8 wt pct Co. Although the magnitude of the lattice parameters were determined at room temperature, the

This showed that the lattice misﬁt tended to decrease with an increasing Co content, which was consistent with the diﬀerent  c ¢  morphologies observed in the two experi- mental alloys. Since  c ¢  precipitates tend to be more   38 ] ¢ cuboidal in alloys with larger lattice misﬁt, the  c precipitates in alloy A were more cuboidal than those in alloy B, as shown in Figures  5 (a1), (a2) and (b1), (b2). Regarding the above-mentioned positive eﬀects on elemental partitioning and microstructural stability, Co additions appear to be a potential microstructural stabilizer in Ni-based superalloys. Based on the appro- priate volume fraction of  c ¢  phase and microstructural stability, more refractory alloying elements may be accommodated in Ni-based single-crystal superalloys containing high levels of Co without forming TCP phases. The beneﬁcial eﬀects on the microstructural stability and negative eﬀects on  c ¢  volume fraction due to high level of Co addition need to be balanced for the further alloy development.  

# V. CONCLUSIONS  

Two Ni-based single-crystal superalloys containing relatively low levels of Re (2.1 wt pct) and Ru (2.0 wt pct) were cast by the LMC method, and their as-cast and heat-treated microstructures were investi- gated. The eﬀect of Co additions on the solidiﬁcation segregation, phase transformation temperatures, and the microstructural stability was summarized as follows:  

1. The volume fraction of ( c  +  c ¢ ) eutectic in the as- cast Ni-based superalloys decreased as Co additions increased from 7.9 to 15.8 wt pct. The extent of W segregation during solidification was determined to decrease with the increase of Co content.

 2. High levels of Co additions resulted in the decrease of solvus temperatures of  c ¢  phase and ( c  +  c ¢ ) eutectic as well as the solidus temperature. The solution treatment window was enlarged by higher Co concentrations.

 3. During the long-term thermal exposure at 1373 K (1100   C), no TCP phases precipitated in either of the experimental alloys. The  c ¢  precipitates in alloy A with 7.9 wt pct Co coarsened, coalesced, and formed nondirectional rafting earlier than those in alloy B with 15.8 wt pct Co.

 4. As the level of Co increased, the measured parti- tioning ratios of TCP-forming elements Cr, Mo, Re, and W decreased to varying degrees. This could be attributed to the decreased  c ¢  volume fraction associated with the higher overall concentration of Co. This change in the elemental partitioning behavior was responsible for the smaller  c / c ¢  lattice misfit and better microstructural stability in alloy with high Co content.  

5. Experimental determinations of the phase transfor- mation temperatures and the compositions of  c  and c ¢  phases agreed favorably with the  PANDAT  pre- dictions as the observed effect of Co on the two alloys was consistent.
