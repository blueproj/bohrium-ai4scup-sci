The  γ  precipitation in the primary  γ΄  of a new Ni – Co-base disc superalloy, TMW-4M3, has been investigated. It was found that 5 nm  γ  precipitates nucleated homogeneously in the primary  γ΄  after solution heat treatment. The size of these tiny particles was unlikely to change after two steps aging treatment, but signi ﬁ cant growth was detected after creep rupture at 725  1 C under various stresses. The growth and/or coarsening process of  γ  precipitates can be described with the Lifshitz – Slyozov – Wagner (LSW) equation. The phase stability of  γ  precipitates and the in ﬂ uence of  γ  precipitation on the deformation of primary  γ΄  are discussed.

TMW-4M3 is a new promising Ni – Co-base disc superalloy developed at National Institute for Materials Science (NIMS), Japan. This new alloy can be prepared by cast and wrought processing and exhibits good overall mechanical properties at temperatures in excess of 700  1 C  [21] . Typical tri-modal distribu- tion of  γ΄  precipitates, i.e. primary, secondary and tertiary  γ΄ , has been attained in TMW-4M3 when subsolvus heat treatment is employed  [22] . Our results show that the volume fraction of primary  γ΄  is more than 10% if TMW-4M3 is solution treated in the temperature range of 1100 – 1130  1 C followed by two steps aging treatment  [23] .  

# 1. Introduction  

Ni-base superalloys are hardened by  γ΄  precipitates that dis- tributed uniformly in the  γ  matrix. The former has an ordered L1 2 structure, while the latter is a disordered fcc phase  [1] . This unique γ / γ΄  two-phase structure enables Ni-base superalloys to have excellent mechanical properties at high temperatures. Therefore, commercial hot gas path components, for example, turbine blades, discs and nozzle guide vanes, in aero-engines and industrial (land- base) gas turbines, are made of Ni-base superalloys. For the mechanical performance of Ni-base superalloys, the  γ΄  precipitate size, distribution and volume fraction are of great importance. Thus, a great deal of research has been devoted to the  γ΄ precipitation  [2 – 10 ]. In order to optimize the process of  γ΄ precipitation, the hot working parameters and heat treatments have to be extensively explored  [11 – 14 ] for a speci ﬁ c disc alloy.  

In the present study, the  γ  precipitation in the primary  γ΄  of TMW-4M3 has been investigated. The phase stability of  γ  pre- cipitates and the in ﬂ uence of  γ  precipitation on the deformation of primary  γ΄  during creep are discussed.  

In contrast, only few studies paid attention to the  γ  precipitation within  γ΄  phase and its associated effects on deformation  [15 – 19 ], which is indispensable to fully understand the microstructure stability and mechanical behaviors of superalloys. The primary  γ΄  is generally retained to pin the grain boundary during annealing heat treatment and control the grain size of disc superalloys. Undoubtedly, the deformation behavior of primary  γ΄  affects the mechanical perfor- mance of superalloys. It was reported that intergranular damage initiated at primary  γ΄  interfaces might degrade the alloy  [20] .  

# 2. Experiment  

The pancake shaped disc of TMW-4M3 was prepared by cast and wrought (C&W) processing, which has been reported else- where  [24] . The specimens were cut from the pancake shaped disc and heat treated as follows: 1130  1 C/4 h/air cooling (AC) + 650  1 C/ 24 h/AC + 760  1 C/16 h/AC. After heat treatment, cylindrical samples, 4 mm in diameter with a gage length of 22 mm, were machined for creep testing. Tensile creep tests were conducted at 725  1 C under various stresses, i.e. 320 MPa, 480 MPa and 630 MPa. The microstructures after solution heat treatment, two steps aging treatment and creep rupture were observed with transmission electron microscopy (TEM). TEM discs cut perpendicular to stress axis were manually ground to 50  μ m and perforated by a twin-jet electro-polisher at 40 V/18 mA and  − 10  1 C. The electrolyte con- sisted of 225 ml acetic acid, 225 ml butylcellosolve and 50 ml perchloric acid. A Tecnai 20 microscope operating at 200 kV was used.  

# 3. Results and discussion  

# 3.1.  γ  precipitation in the primary  γ΄  

After solution heat treatment at 1130  1 C for 4 h, the microstruc- ture of primary  γ΄  in TMW-4M3 is shown in  Fig. 1 (a) and (b). Selected area diffraction (SAD) along [001],  Fig. 1 (e), presents no extra re ﬂ ections except those originated from ordered L1 2  structure. SAD along  〈 012 〉 ,  〈 013 〉 ,  〈 112 〉 and  〈 101 〉 also implies the L1 2  structure (not shown here). However, the tiny particles of around 5 nm could be clearly imaged using  g /3 g  (or 4 g ) weak beam conditions with operating vector {200}, {220} or {111}.  Fig. 1 (f) shows the diffraction condition for weak beam image  Fig. 1 (a), and only (200) re ﬂ ection is selected for imaging. This is because of the shape effect in reciprocal space and coherent strain between the precipitates and the matrix. The spherical particles have a very small size; thus the spots of reciprocal lattice enlarge to shells with a considerable diameter  [25] . In addition, coherent strain induces a local lattice distortion. Conse- quently, even the deviation vector (away from exact Bragg diffrac- tion) under weak beam conditions is large; the shells may intersect with the Ewald sphere, which makes the tiny particles bright in weak beam image, as seen in  Fig. 1 (a). In bright  ﬁ eld image,  Fig. 1 (b), these small particles are dif ﬁ cult to discern.  

After aging treatment, applying the same diffraction condition, the similar morphology of the tiny particles in primary  γ΄  is illustrated in Fig. 1 (c) and (d). A typical microstructure in a low magni ﬁ cation is shown in  Fig. 1 (g), and primary  γ΄  in which  γ  precipitation occurred (see  Fig. 1 (d)) locates at grain boundary. Comparing  Fig. 1 (a) with (c), one can  ﬁ nd that after aging treatment the size of these particles is unlikely to change, but the number density appears to increase.

The phenomenon of precipitation in primary  γ΄  had been reported by several authors  [15 – 20 ]. It was argued that these particles were either  γ  phase or  β -NiAl formed only after aging treatment. Guo et al. demonstrated that  ﬁ ne  β -NiAl precipitates had no orientation rela- tionship with the  γ  matrix, and diffraction rings originated from β -NiAl were observed  [17] . In our case, there were no diffraction rings in the SAD patterns, and no re ﬂ ections could be indexed with  β -NiAl. Thus, the possibility is excluded that these particles are  β -NiAl. For SAD, all the spots of  γ  phase overlap with those of  γ΄  phase. As also indicated later by fast Fourier transform (FFT) of high resolution TEM image, the precipitates in primary  γ΄  do not have superlattice spots, which implies an fcc structure. It is therefore reasonable to interpret that these particles are  γ  phase. However, at least two different points emerge in the present case. First, the small particles were formed after solution treatment and continued to precipitate during aging treatment. All previous studies concluded that the  γ  precipitation occurred only after aging treatment. Primary  γ΄  is formed at high temperatures and likely super-saturates during cooling. Therefore,  γ phase nucleates homogeneously in the primary  γ΄  if the super- saturation reaches a critical value. Due to the short time for diffusion, the chemical composition of primary  γ΄  is still not in equilibrium. The γ  precipitation continues to occur during aging treatment. Second, no precipitate free zone in primary  γ΄  was observed at this stage. The diffusion distance during heat treatment may be very negligible, which means that adequate diffusion cannot be achieved. Thus, no precipitate free zone is formed.  

The solvus temperature of  γ΄  in TMW-4M3 is around 1162  1 C [23] . Primary  γ΄  is formed during hot working process at tempera- tures below 1162  1 C. The super-saturation in primary  γ΄  increases with decreasing temperature during the cooling of hot working. Thus, it is also possible to precipitate  γ . However, the  γ  precipitates during the cooling of hot working may resolve back into the primary  γ΄  at solution condition 1130  1 C/4 h. After homogenization, γ  precipitation occurs again during the cooling of solution treat- ment, which is the case observed in the present experiments.  

# 3.2. Thermal stability of the  γ  precipitates  

The thermal stability of  γ  precipitates in primary  γ΄  is of great concern for mechanical properties at service temperatures. Cadel et al. suggested that these tiny  γ  precipitates might be meta-stable and disappear after isothermal aging and creep  [19] . The morphol- ogy of  γ  precipitates was examined after creep rupture at 725  1 C and various applied stresses. As predicted using the Thermo-Calc program, the equilibrium volume fraction of  γ  and  γ΄  phase at 725  1 C is 47.7% and 49.4%, respectively. Actually, the real volume fraction of  γ  in TMW-4M3 is slightly higher than 47.7% because there are no  μ  and  s  phases in TMW-4M3, which is different from the calculation using the Thermo-Calc. The precipitation process such as nucleation, growth and coarsening may be affected by internal or external stresses. For example, dislocations generated during plastic deformation are easy diffusion paths that enhance the material exchange between coarsen- ing particles. In the present case, the effects of creep stress on the growth and/or coarsening process of  γ  precipitates are still unclear. However, negligible effects are expected in general for slow deforma- tion during materials service, in particular creep  [28] .

Though it seems that the applied stress of 320 – 630 MPa and  γ precipitate size of 6.9 – 19.9 nm have little impact on deformation mechanisms of primary  γ΄  at 725  1 C, the presence of  γ  precipitate may strengthen primary  γ΄ . Tian et al.  [29]  studied the  γ  precipita- tion behavior in Ni – Al – Ti alloys, and homogeneous distribution of γ  precipitates in the  γ΄  matrix by solution and aging heat treatment at suitable temperatures was attained. The Vickers hardness as a function of aging time at 1073 K showed a peak in the alloys with ﬁ ne  γ  precipitates, which implies that the  γ΄ -L1 2  ordered Ni 3 (AI,Ti) hardens appreciably by  ﬁ ne precipitation of disordered  γ . Accord- ing to this result, it is believed that the  γ  precipitation in primary  γ΄ of TMW-4M3 may strengthen primary  γ΄ . The hardened primary  γ΄ is more effective to impede dislocation motion, therefore improv- ing the total creep resistance.  

Flageolet et al. analyzed the role of  γ  particles within primary  γ΄ on damage accumulation in the powder metallurgy Ni-base disc superalloy N18  [20] . SFs were the main deformation mechanism of primary  γ΄  in the standard heat treated specimen, whereas ( a /2) 〈 110 〉 type dislocations were dominant in the specimen after standard heat treatment plus additional aging treatment. Our results are different from theirs. They also suggested that  γ precipitate acted as dislocation sources in the sample with addi- tional aging treatment. In our case, we did not observe such kind of evidence. This may mainly arise from two factors. First, the loading mode is different. Flageolet et al. conducted trapezoidal cycle testing, i.e. creep-fatigue condition. We carried out isother- mal tensile creep tests. Second, the size of  γ  particles is different. The maximum diameter of  γ  precipitates in N18 after additional aging treatment is 400 nm, much larger than that (20 nm) observed in TMW-4M3.  

# 4. Conclusion  

The  γ  precipitation in the primary  γ΄  of a new Ni – Co-base disc superalloy was studied. These nanoscale dispersive  γ  precipitates are thermally stable at 725  1 C. The signi ﬁ cant growth and/or coarsening of  γ  precipitates occurred after thermal exposure at 725  1 C under various stresses, which can be roughly estimated using LSW equation. SFs and deformation twins were observed to be the dominant deformation mechanisms in the primary  γ΄ . In our case, the precipitate size and the applied stress seem to have no signi ﬁ cant effect on the deformation mechanism of primary  γ΄ . However, it is believed that the presence of uniformly distributed  γ precipitates will harden the  γ΄  matrix, and therefore may con- tribute additional strength to the alloy.
