# Eﬀect of Mo additions on microstructure and tensile behavior of a Co–Al–W–Ta–B alloy at room temperature  

G. Feng, H. Li, S.S. Li and J.B. Sha ⇑  

School of Materials Science and Engineering, Beihang University, Beijing 100191, China  

Received 21 March 2012; revised 11 June 2012; accepted 11 June 2012 Available online 16 June 2012  

Phase constitutions, microstructures and room-temperature tensile behavior of Mo-doped Co–9Al–(9    x )W–2Ta–0.02B– x Mo alloys (where  x  = 0, 2, 4, 6, 9 at.%) are investigated. After aging treatment, the microstructure is composed of  c  matrix and nano- scale cuboidal  c 0   precipitates, with Mo contents of 0–4 at.%; however, a needle-like cellular  j -Co 3 Mo phase is additionally found in the  c 0 / c  microstructure, with Mo contents of 6–9 at.%. The pure  c 0 / c  microstructure exhibits better room-temperature tensile strength and elongation than the  c / c 0 / j  microstructure.

Traditional Co-based alloys have been widely used inturbineguide blades because of its high-temperaturecor- rosion resistance, thermal fatigue resistance and ease of welding  [1] . The microstructures of the conventional Co- based alloys mainly consist of continuous Co solid solution matrix  c  phase with a face-centered cubic crystal structure and one or more carbides, in which the carbides are the main strengthening phases  [2,3] . There is no coherent inter- face strengthening mechanism because the conventional Co-basedalloys do not have a coherent  c / c 0   microstructure like that of Ni-based alloys  [4,5] .  

Recently, cuboidal L1 2 -type  c 0   phase, an intermetallic compound Co 3 (Al, W), has been found for the ﬁrst time in the  c  matrix of a Co–Al–W ternary  [6] . Wide concerns about the phase stabilization, coherent microstructural evolution and mechanical behavior of the ternary have been generated since then by multi-alloying  [7–12] . The development of strength and ductility in Co–Al–W-based alloys at room temperature and high temperatures is now a primary issue. The high-temperature strength is positively dependent on the stabilization of the  c 0   phase, i.e. the  c 0 solvus temperature  [7–9] . In the Co–Al–W ternary, the  c 0 solvus temperature can be improved by increasing the nom- inal Al and W contents  [7] . First-principles calculations have revealed that Mo, Ta, Rh, Ir and Pt can all stabilize the  c 0   phase by dissolving in it, because all of these elements interact with the nearest neighboring Co, Al and/or W atoms  [10,11] . With a 2 at.% Ta addition, for example, the solvus temperature of  c 0   phase in the Co–9Al–9W al- loy is increased from 1000 to 1079   C and the compres- sive strength at 800   C is increased from 360 to 580 MPa  [7,8] , which is superior to the conventional Co-based alloys  [12] . B was found the most eﬀective alloying element to improve the ductility of the Co–Al– W ternary. A considerable increment in elongation of

 7.6% results, i.e. from 4.7% to 12.3%, in Co–9.2Al–

 9.5W alloy when doped with 0.02 at.% B  [12] . This mech- anism of ductility enhancement is explained by suppres- sion of any intergranular fracture with B, discovered in the Ni 3 Al single-phase polycrystalline alloys with very brittle behavior and grain boundary failure  [13,14] .  

In the Co–9Al–9W ternary, 9 at.% W content increases density of the alloy to 9.543 g cm  3 . The eﬀect of substi- tuting W with light elements on the microstructural evo- lution and mechanical behaviors of Co–9Al–9W-based alloys has not been shown systematically. In this research, Co–9Al–9W–2Ta–0.02B was used as the basic composition, and 2, 4, 6 and 9 at.% Mo (10.28 g cm  3   in density) was added to replace the same amount of W. A detailed study to determine the phase constitutions, microstructures and room-temperature tensile behaviors of the Co–9Al–(9    x )W–2Ta–0.02B– x Mo alloys (where  x  = 0, 2, 4, 6, 9 at.%, hereafter referred to 0Mo, 2Mo, 4Mo, 6Mo and 9Mo alloys, respectively) has been performed in the present research.  

Button ingots of the Co–9Al–(9    x )W–2Ta–0.02B– x Mo alloys, each with a weight of 150 g, were prepared by arc melting ﬁve or six times in an argon atmosphere. The purity of all raw elements was 99.99 mass% or higher. The ingots were solution-treated at 1350   C for 8 h in a vacuum, followed by furnace cooling, before being sealed in an evacuated quartz capsule back-ﬁlled with argon and aged at 800   C for 100 h followed by air cooling. The back-scattered microstructures were observed using a scanning electron microscope (SEM; JEOL JXA–8100). The selected area electron diﬀraction patterns and en- ergy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy spectra were obtained 2   in an FEI Tecnai G F20 transmission electron micro- scope (TEM), to conﬁrm the phase crystal structure and composition, respectively. TEM samples, 0.5 mm in thickness, were cut, then mechanically ground/thinned to 50  l m. Foils 3 mm in diameter were gotten from the mechanically thinned samples by punching and then thinned using a GL-696F ion-milled instrument at an an- gle of 20  , with a voltage of 6 kV and a current of 0.15 mA applied to perforate the samples. Finally, the perforated foils were ion-milled again for 30–40 min at an angle of 15   to remove residual contamination and/or to amelio- rate diﬀerential thinning eﬀects. Dog-bone-shaped plates were used to measure room-temperature tensile behav- iors. Tensile tests were conducted on an Instron 8810 ma- chine at a strain rate of 3    10  4   s  1 . The dimensions of the parallel part of the dog-bone-shaped plates were 3 mm  1.8 mm in cross-section and 16 mm in length.  

The back-scattered SEM micrographs of the aged al- loys represent remarkable changes, as seen in  Figure 1 . For the Mo-free sample (0Mo alloy), a coherent  c / c 0 microstructure similar to that of the typical Ni-based superalloys  [4,5]  is found ( Fig. 1 (a)), in which the regu- larly aligned cuboidal precipitates are the  c 0 -Co 3 (Al, W) phase, with an L1 2  structure, and the matrix  c  phase, which is a Co solid solution, where W may be partly replaced by Ta. The cuboidal  c 0   precipitates are 200– 300 nm in cube edge length and are uniformly distrib- uted in the  c  phase. For the 2Mo and 4Mo alloys, the c / c 0   microstructure ( Fig. 1 (b) and (c) and insets) is pre- cisely the same as that of the Mo-free sample shown in  Figure 1 (a). A signiﬁcant change in morphology takes place when 6 at.% W is replaced by 6 at.% Mo (i.e. 6Mo alloy), with the presence of a bright  j -Co 3 Mo phase with a DO 19  structure in the grey matrix ( Fig. 1 (d)), where Mo can be partly replaced by Ta, Al and W. These bright  j -Co 3 Mo phases are formed by solid–solid transformation during aging, are cellular (see the insert in  Fig. 1 (d)) and are distributed continuously on the grain boundaries of the matrix ( Fig. 1 (d)). The average compositions of the grain interiors and cellular Co 3 Mo are 80.5Co–9.7Al–3.3W–4.9Mo–1.6Ta and 71.3Co– 1.8Al–0.9W–17.1Mo–8.9Ta, respectively. An enlarged SEM image in  Figure 1 (e) reveals that needle-like pre- cipitates are present in the matrix; these are 40–70 nm in width and develop throughout the matrix along two directions with cross angles of about 50–75  . The matrix of the 6Mo alloy in  Figure 1 (e) is also observed to con- tain the  c  and  c 0   phases, though the  c 0   phase is not so dense or regularly aligned under SEM observation, as shown in  Figure 1 (a)–(c). The 9Mo alloy still contains the  c ,  c 0   and cellular/needle-like Co 3 Mo phases, and its microstructure is the same as that of the 6Mo alloy ( Fig. 1 (f)). The average compositions of the grain interi- ors and the cellular Co 3 Mo for 9Mo alloy are 81.8Co– 9.4Al–7.5Mo–1.3Ta and 69.4Co–2.3Al–19.7Mo–8.6Ta, respectively. Quantitative metallographic analysis re- veals that the fraction of the cellular Co 3 Mo phase in the 9Mo alloy is about 6.4%, which is higher than the 4.2% in the 6Mo alloy. To determine whether the 4Mo alloy has the interdendritic segregation of Mo or not, a thorough microstructural analysis of the 4Mo sample was performed at diﬀerent magniﬁcations, and it was found that the alloy does not contain any Mo segrega- tion. For further evaluation, a 5Mo alloy (5 at.% W was replaced by 5 at.% Mo) was also designed and micro- structurally analyzed in an aged condition. It was ob- served that only a few Mo segregated regions (less than 1.0% fraction) appeared in the microstructure. This suggests that the 5Mo alloy is probably the critical com- position at which the Mo segregation initiates.

The tensile true stress–strain curves of the aged alloys at room temperature are shown in  Figure 3 . For the 0Mo, 2Mo and 4Mo alloys, with the pure  c / c 0   microstructure, the stress quickly reaches the peak value after yielding, then increases slowly with strain until the total plastic elongation is greater than 11%. The 6Mo and 9Mo alloys, with the  c / c 0 / j  microstructure, have little elongation and fail immediately after yielding. Data of the average 0.2% yield tensile strength ( r 0.2 ) and the elongation ( d ) of the ﬁve alloys are summarized in  Table 1 , where properties of the Co–9.2Al–9.5W  [12] , Co–9.2Al–9.5W–0.02B  [12] and Co–8.8Al–9.8W–2Ta  [8]  alloys are also listed for comparison. The 0Mo, 2Mo and 4Mo alloys have almost the same tensile strength levels, in the range of 755– 780 MPa, and also show excellent ductility, of about 11.51–14.7% in elongation. Their strength and elonga- tion are higher than those of the three alloys (0Mo, 2Mo and 4Mo) with the pure  c / c 0   microstructure. With the additional cellular and needle-like  j -Co 3 Mo phases, the 6Mo and 9Mo alloys show a strength about 100 MPa lower than the 0–4Mo alloys and their elonga- tion is signiﬁcantly low, at about 0.25–0.31%. It is evident that the  j -Co 3 Mo phase plays a negative role in both room-temperature ductility and strength.

It is well known that B additions improve the elonga- tion of either the single Ni 3 Al intermetallic  [13,14]  or the pure coherent  c / c 0   microstructure in Co–Al–W ternary alloy  [12] . The ductile mechanisms of the L1 2  intermetal- lics produced by the B additions have been explained as increasing the grain boundary strength, eliminating impurities from grain boundaries and reducing the stress concentration on grain boundaries by dislocation slip, etc.  [13,14] . In the Co–9Al–9W alloy with a combined addition of 2Ta and 0.02B (the 0Mo alloy), the elongation enhancement by the B addition is retained (see  Table 1 ). Interestingly, further replacement of W with 2 and 4 at.% Mo increases the strength by about 65–90 and 130–155 MPa, respectively, when compared to the Co–

 9.2Al–9.5W–0.02B  [12]  and Co–8.8Al–9.8W–2Ta alloys

 [8] ; at the same time, degradation of the elongation does not occur with the increased strength (see  Table 1 ). When 6 at.% and all the W are replaced by Mo, both the strength and elongation decrease signiﬁcantly, which is related to brittleness and cleavage fracture (see  Fig. 4 (c) and (d)). These ﬁnding, therefore, strongly suggest that the regularly aligned pure coherent  c / c 0   microstructure, accompanied with 2 at.% Ta, 0.02 at.% B and   4 at.% Mo additions, is thought to be the optimum composition and microstructure to produce outstanding room-tem- perature mechanical properties, which are superior to those of the Co–Al–W-based alloys that have been re- ported so far  [8,12] . It has been pointed out that the mazy c  channel in  Figure 1 (a)–(c) supplies the movement zone for dislocation, which is beneﬁcial to the bulk ductility, while the aligned cuboidal  c 0   precipitates restrict disloca- tion sliding within the  c  channel, resulting in the forma- tion of a dislocation network on the  c / c 0   coherent interfaces and the enhancement of strength  [16,17] . As the Mo content is increased up to 6–9 at.%, the brittle j -Co 3 Mo phase forms ( Fig. 4 (d)), which supplies sites for the crack initiation and fast propagation ( Fig. 4 (c) and (d)) that reduce the elongation to an insigniﬁcant le- vel ( Table 1 ), while the  c / c 0   matrix shows no evident plas- tic deformation in time before failure. This indirectly shows that B cannot improve the ductility of the  j - Co 3 Mo phase; on the other hand, the enhancement of ductility with the addition of B is dependent on the crys- tal structure of the intermetallics. The same is true even for those intermetallics with an L1 2  structure; for exam- ple, an improvement in ductility with the addition of B is obtained in the Ni 3 Al and Co 3 (Al,W) alloys  [12–14] but is not achieved in the L1 2 -Co 3 Ti alloy  [18] .  

In summary, the microstructure of the Co–9Al–9W– 2Ta–0.02B alloy is composed of a  c  matrix and cuboidal c 0   precipitates with a size of 200–300 nm when 0–4 at.% W are replaced by Mo. In addition, a cellular and needle-like j -Co 3 Mo phase arises when 6 at.% and whole W are re- placed by Mo. The  j -Co 3 Mo phase is harmful to the al- loy’s strength and ductility. The pure coherent  c / c 0 microstructure exhibits ductile failure with higher room- temperature tensile strength and elongation than the  c / c 0 / j  microstructure, which fractures in a mixed brittle and cleavage mode.
