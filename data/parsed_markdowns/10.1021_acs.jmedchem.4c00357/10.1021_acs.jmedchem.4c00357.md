ABSTRACT:  The NLRP3 inflammasome has been recognized as a promising therapeutic target in drug discovery for inflammatory diseases. Our initial research identified a natural sesquiterpene iso alan to lac tone (IAL) as the active scaffold targeting NLRP3 inflammasome. To improve its activity and metabolic stability, a total of 64 IAL derivatives were designed and synthesized. Among them, compound  49  emerged as the optimal lead, displaying the most potent inhibitory efficacy on nigericin-induced IL-1 β  release in THP-1 cells, with an IC 50  value of 0.29  μ M, approximately 27- fold more potent than that of IAL (IC 50 : 7.86  μ M), and exhibiting higher metabolic stability. # INTRODUCTION  

As an important pattern recognition receptor within the innate immune system, the NOD-like receptor family pyrin domain- containing protein 3 (NLRP3) inflammasome is capable of detecting damage/pathogen-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs and PAMPs) in response to cellular stress, damage, or infection. It can be triggered by a diverse range of stimuli, including viral, bacterial and fungal pathogens, uric acid crystal, and adenosine triphosphate (ATP), resulting in the activation of caspase-1 and the secretion of proinflammatory cytokines IL-1 β and IL-18. − 3   The anomalous activation of NLRP3 inflamma- some is intricately associated with the development of a wide range of inflammatory diseases and metabolic disorders, including inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), cryopyrin- associated periodic syndrome (CAPS), COVID-19, atheroscle- rosis, arthritis, gout, nonalcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH), Alzheimer’s disease, diabetes mellitus, obesity, and aging. − 8  

With the growing understanding of the pathogenic role of NLRP3 inflammasome in a multitude of human diseases with chronic inflammation, it has emerged as a promising therapeutic target for these inflammatory conditions. − 11   Initially, the design of NLRP3 inflammasome inhibitors primarily drew inspiration from the structural framework of MCC950, a well- known compound widely utilized in NLRP3 inflammasome research. ,   In recent years, there has been a growing trend in the development and exploration of NLRP3 inflammasome inhibitors with structural diversity. Currently, numerous pharmaceutical and biotech enterprises have made substantial investment in the development of drugs that target NLRP3 inflammasome pathway. Several promising NLRP3 inhibitors, such as ZYIL1 from Zydus (phase II), RG-6418 from Roche (phase I), IZD-174 from IFM (phase I), and DFV890 from Novartis (phase II, structure undisclosed) have entered clinical trials ( Figure 1 ).   Nevertheless, there are currently no drugs that have been approved for market. Therefore, novel anti- NLRP3 inflammasome agents are urgently needed.  

The realm of natural products has presented vast prospects for the unearthing of innovative lead compounds in the field of pharmaceutical research.   Currently, several natural NLRP3 inhibitors such as oridonin from  Rabdosia rubescens , costunolide from  Saussrurea lappa ,   berberine from  Berberis silva-taroucana ,   velutone F from  Millettia velutina ,   and so on, have been investigated for their potential in treating NLRP3

Our laboratory has been steadfastly devoted to the isolation, structural modification, and mechanism elucidation of bioactive natural products. − 19 Inula racemose  Hook. f. has long been used in traditional Chinese medicine to treat acute and chronic colitis, gastrointestinal disorders, intercostal neuralgia, chest wall contusion and pain, and irregular fetal movement.   Our initial investigations evinced that the  I. racemose  extracts (IRE) exhibited promising potential in suppressing the activation of NLRP3 inflamma- some, with the abundant sesquiterpene lactone iso alan to lac tone (IAL) being the principal bioactive constituent ( Scheme 1  and Figure S175 ). In addition, this naturally occurring and exceptional eudesmane-type sesquiterpene lactone was identi- fied as the active scaffold functioning as a NLRP3 inflammasome inhibitor, exhibiting inhibitory potency against nigericin- induced IL-1 β  release in THP-1 cells with an IC 50  value of 7.86  μ M. However, the weak activity, poor metabolic stability, and limited solubility of IAL have hindered its further exploration and development in the field of drug research. Structurally, IAL shares the same  α , β -unsaturated carbonyl moiety with the natural NLRP3 inhibitors oridonin and costunolide, which function as Michael acceptors and covalently bind to the NACHT domain of NLRP3, indicating it might have the similar binding mode to inhibit the NLRP3 inflammasome activation. ,   Building upon the aforementioned findings, we aimed to delve deeper into the development of more efficacious NLRP3 inhibitors based on this scaffold, while simultaneously unraveling their underlying mechanisms. In this work, we described the design and preparation of a novel series of IAL- based derivatives, and the bioactivity evaluation against NLRP3 inflammasome, as well as the underlying mechanism.

To explore the impact of the  α -methylene- γ -butyrolactone motif on the NLRP3 inhibitory activity, compounds  1 − 3  were prepared with various modifications to this moiety. As expected, none of these compounds exhibited inhibitory activity against nigericin-induced IL-1 β  release in THP-1 cells, suggesting that the entire  α -methylene- γ -butyrolactone motif was crucial for inhibiting the NLRP3 inflammasome activation. In support of this, commercially available tulipalin A was tested to evaluate the activity of this motif, and it was found to be effective against nigericin-induced IL-1 β  release in THP-1 cells ( Table 1 ).  

After identifying the influence of the  α -methylene- γ - butyrolactone motif on the inhibitory efficacy of the NLRP3 inflammasome, further modifications were carried out on other positions of the IAL molecule while preserving the  α -methylene- γ -butyrolactone moiety. The results showed that the terminal alkene group ( 4  and  6 ) was beneficial to the inhibitory activity, while modifications at the C-3 position in ring A ( 5 ,  7 , and  8 ) were well-tolerated. Metabolic site analysis revealed that C-3 of IAL is an important metabolic site ( Figure S180 ), and it was speculated that the introduction of substituents at this position might increase the metabolic stability. Through computer-aided drug design analysis, it was found that the hydroxyl group at C-3 of compound  5  fits well into the hydrophobic binding cavity of the NLRP3 NACHT domain, and the sufficient space around this hydroxyl group suggests the possibility of introducing additional functional groups to establish further interactions with neighboring amino acids ( Figure 2 ). Therefore, the structural modification at C-3 of compound  5  has the potential to improve its interaction with the NLRP3 NACHT domain. In the subsequent modification of compound  5 , the carbonyl group, a crucial pharmaceutical chemical fragment,   will be introduced to investigate its impact on the inhibitory activity against the NLRP3 inflammasome.

Metabolic Stability Study.  The  in vitro  metabolic stability of compounds ( 48 − 50 , and  54 − 56 ) with the IC 50  values below 1  μ M was assessed in mouse and human liver microsomes. As presented in  Table 7 , the tested compounds in mouse liver microsomes generally exhibited shorter or comparable half-lives compared to those in human liver microsomes and compound 49  demonstrated acceptable metabolic stability in both mouse and human liver microsomes with the  T 1/2  value of 65.4 and 182.4 min, respectively.  

Drug-like Properties Evaluation of Compound 49.  To explore any potential for cardiotoxicity, we examined the effect of  49  on the human ether-a -go-go (hERG) potassium channel. In this assay the IC 50  for hERG was 10.63  μ M, which suggests  49 has 36.6-fold safety window (IC 50  for NLRP3 = 290 nM) ( Figure S183 ). We also investigated the potential of  49  to induce DNA breaks using a comet assay. The findings revealed that treatment with  49  (1  μ M) did not cause a significant increase in % DNA in the comet tail in THP-1 and mouse bone marrow- derived macrophages (BMDMs), indicating no significant genotoxicity of  49  ( Figure S184 ). The inhibition and induction of CYPs are major mechanisms causing pharmacokinetic drug − drug interactions. Subsequently, we assessed the inhibitory potency of compound  49  against 6 human cytochrome P450 (CYP) enzymes. The results demonstrated that compound  49 could not exhibit inhibitory effects on CYP1A2, CYP2B6, CYP2D6, CYP2E1, and CYP3A4 (IC 50  > 100  μ M). However, it showed a moderate inhibition of CYP2C9 with an IC 50  of 5.803 μ M, suggesting a potential risk of drug interactions for medications metabolized through CYP2C9.

Compound 49 Covalently Targets the Cys 279 in NLRP3 NACHT Domain.  NLRP3 consists three domains: a PYD domain binding to ASC, a NACHT domain with ATPase activity, and an LRR domain inducing autorepression.   SAR studies demonstrated the crucial role of  α , β -unsaturated carbonyl moiety in IAL for potent NLRP3 inflammasome inhibition. Given that oridonin and costunolide share a structurally similar  α , β -unsaturated carbonyl moiety functioning as a Michael acceptor, covalently binding to NACHT domain of NLRP3, and thus inhibiting NLRP3 inflammasome activa- tion, ,   we speculated that compound  49  might interact similarly with NLRP3. # CONCLUSIONS  

In many senses, NLRP3 inflammasome is emerging as the ideal drug target considering its aberrant activation in a variety of diseases with chronic inflammation. As the path o physiological role of NLRP3 inflammasome in diverse human inflammatory diseases is increasing recognized, the drug research targeting the NLRP3 inflammasome has become an exploding field of research. In our continuing endeavor to develop novel inhibitors targeting the NLRP3 inflammasome, a naturally occurring unique eudesmane-type sesquiterpene lactone IAL was identified to be the active scaffold as a NLRP3 inflammasome inhibitor, and a series of new compounds ( 1 − 64 ) based on this scaffold were designed and synthesized. The SAR character- ization highlighted that compound  49  proved the most promising anti-NLRP3 agent endowed with improved activity, higher metabolic stability, and lower toxicity. Compound  49 exhibited the most potent inhibitory efficacy on nigericin- induced IL-1 β  release in THP-1 cells with the IC 50  value of 0.29 μ M, which was approximately 27-fold more potent than IAL (IC 50 : 7.86  μ M). Importantly,  49  remarkably improved DSS- induced ulcerative colitis  in vivo . The mechanism of action study revealed that  49  could directly target the NLRP3 protein by covalently binding to Cys 279 in NACHT domain of NLRP3, thereby inhibiting the assembly and activation of the NLRP3 inflammasome. Pharmacokinetic study in mice have indicated that compound  49  exhibits low plasma concentrations due to its rapid distribution to tissues Further research should focus on how to improve its colon exposure. However, it can be “cautiously” used as a tool compound for NLRP3 inflammasome inhibitor to explore the therapeutic potential for NLRP3-related diseases.

General Procedure for the Synthesis of Compounds 21 − 26. The synthetic procedure was similar to the procedure for the synthesis of compounds  9 − 20  and  27 − 58 . A solution of compound  5  (100 mg, 0.4 mmol, 1.0 equiv), different Boc-amino acids (1.2 mmol, 3.0 equiv), and DMAP (20 mg, 0.16 mmol, 0.4 equiv) in 10 mL DCM was added to DCC (198 mg, 0.96 mmol, 2.4 equiv), and stirred at room temperature for 16 − 24 h. After reaction completion, the precipitates were removed by filtration, and the filtrate was collected under reduced pressure to get a brown solid. The brown solid was dissolved in DCM and trifluoroacetic acid was slowly added. After reaction at room temperature for 2 h, the mixture was washed with NaHCO 3  saturated solution, and extracted with DCM three times. The combined DCM phases were washed with saturated salt water, dried with sodium sulfate, concentrated and purified by column chromatography on silica gel, to afford the products  21 − 26 , respectively.  

General Procedure for the Synthesis of Compounds 59 − 64. The products  59 − 64  were synthesized from compound  8  following the synthetic procedure for compounds  9 − 20 .

ASC Oligomerization Assay.  The supernatants were removed, and the cells were subsequently washed three times with PBS, which were then cells were lysed by NP-40 for 30 min. Immuno precipitation Analysis.  For the endogenous IP assay, the supernatants were removed, THP-1 cells were washed three times with PBS, and then, cells were lysed by NP-40 for 30 min. Drug Affinity Responsive Target Stability (DARTS).  PMA- differentiated THP-1 cells were plated at a density of 5  ×  10 5 /mL in 10 cm dishes in full media. HEK-293T cells were seeded in a 6-well plate at a density of 5  ×  10 5   cells/mL and transfected with plasmids for 24 h. Cells were washed by PBS, and then lysed by NP-40 for 30 min. Lysates were centrifuged at 13000 rpm for 15 min at 4  ° C. After centrifugation, 1  μ L of compound  49  or DMSO and 99  μ L supernatant or purified protein were incubated 50 min at room temperature. Protein concentration was determined using Quick Start Bradford 1 ×  Dye Reagent (5000205, BIO-RAD). After 50 min, Pronase was added to the above mixture for 30 min at room temperature. The reaction was stopped by addition of 20 ×  protease inhibitor cocktail and incubated at room temperature for 10 min. Protein samples were examined by immunoblotting.  

Thermal Shift Assay.  PMA-differentiated THP-1 cells were plated at a density of 5  ×  10 5 /mL in 10 cm dishes in full media. The next day, media were removed and replaced with Opti-MEM supplemented with LPS, and cells were primed with LPS for 3 h. Cells were subsequently washed by PBS, and lysed by three rounds of freezing and thawing using liquid nitrogen and room temperature water, and centrifugated at 13000 rpm for 15 min at 4  ° C. Lysates or purified NLRP3 Δ LRR protein was divided into two groups in PCR tubes. One group was mixed with 1 μ M compound  49 , and the other group was mixed with DMSO as a negative control. The two groups of proteins were heated at 45 to 63 or 78  ° C under the same conditions using a thermal cycler. Each sample was heated at a single temperature for 2 min. Then protein samples were examined by immunoblotting.

DSS-Induced Colitis Mice Model.  All animal experiments were approved by the Committee on Animal Experimentation of Sichuan University (IACUC number: 2022110203), and performed in compliance with the University’s Guidelines for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals. All male C57BL/6 mice (6 to 8 weeks, n = 6/ group) were raised at room temperature with free access to food and water. The normal group was treated with drinking water for 11 days. The ulcerative colitis was induced by the administration of 3.25% DSS in distilled water for 6 consecutive days and returned to sterile water for 5 days. Treatment groups were given compound  49  (15 and 30 mg/kg) or IAL (30 mg/kg) daily by  i.p . Colitis was quantified using DAI (disease activity index) scores based on three parameters: body weight, rectal bleeding, and stool consistency. After the treatment, the colons of mice were collected for determination of colon length. A part of the colon was directly frozen for subsequent biochemical assays, and a part of the mouse colon was fixed with 4% formaldehyde for H&E staining.  

PK Study.  The PK experiment was approved by the Committee on Animal Experimentation of Sichuan University (IACUC number: 2024031117), and performed in compliance with the University’s Guidelines for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals. C57BL/6 male mice (n = 3 for each group) received  i.p . injections of IAL or compound 49  at a dosage of 30 mg/kg. The solution of compound  49  or IAL was prepared in 5% DMSO and 95% saline to a concentration of 3 mg/mL. The injected volume was individually calculated for each mouse (200 μ L of injected volume per 20 g of mouse body weight). Plasma samples were obtained at 5, 15, 30, 60, 120, 240, 360, 480, 600, and 1440 min after dosing (150 − 200  μ L blood for each time point) and the concentration of  49  and IAL in plasma was determined by LC-MS/MS analysis. The specific information about the LC-MS/MS method are supplemented in the  Supporting Information . Pharmacokinetic parameters were computed using DAS 2.0 software. The detailed procedures for determining the concentration of  49  and IAL in plasma and colon tissues are provided in the  Supporting Information .  

Molecular Docking.  The molecular docking of compound  49  to the NACHT domain was performed using Schro dinger 2018 − 1. The crystal structure of the human NLRP3 NACHT domain (PDB ID: 7ALV) was obtained from the Protein Data Bank (PDB,  http://www. rcsb.org/ ). Using the Protein Preparation Wizard, the protein was prepared by adding hydrogen, deleting water, filling in missing side chains, optimizing H-bond assignment, and minimizing energy in OPLS3 force field. Then the molecule was imported into the workplace for conformational optimization. Covalent docking of Glide moiety was employed for docking. The grid box was centered at residue Cys 279 with a size of 20  ×  20  ×  20 Å. The reaction type chosen was Michael addition. The docking mode “Pose prediction” was selected from the Docking tab for the most thorough treatment. The cutoff of 2.5 kcal/ mol was applied to select Glide poses for both covalent bond formation and subsequent refinement, and up to 200 poses were retained for this molecular. The optimal pose for the ligand reaction site was outputted, and the docking results were subsequently analyzed using Pymol software.
