# a r t i c l e i n f o  

The effect of initial alloy state on hot deformation and dynamic recrystallization (DRX) of GH984G alloy, used to manufacture large scale forgings for 700    C advanced ultra-supercritical (A-USC) coal- ﬁ red power plants, was studied by isothermal compression test. The experimental results show that the apparent activation energy of hot deformation for the as-cast alloy is larger than that of the hot-rolled alloy. The reasonable ranges of processing parameters for the as-cast and hot-rolled alloys are 1100 e 1200    C/0.01 e 0.5 s  1   and 1100 e 1200    C/1.0 e 10 s  1 , respectively, based on the processing maps. The  ﬂ ow instability domain of the as-cast alloy is larger than that of the hot-rolled alloy. The different hot deformation behaviors of the as-cast and hot-rolled alloys result from the various initial grain sizes and DRX mechanisms. The main DRX nucleation mode of the as-cast alloy with large grains is continuous dynamic recrystallization (CDRX). However, for the hot-rolled alloy with  ﬁ ne grains, the discontinuous dynamic recrystallization (DDRX) is dominant.

# 1. Introduction  

Coal- ﬁ red power generation is one of the most important en- ergies for economic development at present. But coal combustion can result in serious environmental pollution. It is imperative to increase the steam temperature and pressure of coal- ﬁ red power plants to rise the energy ef ﬁ ciency and protect the environment

 [ 1 e 4 ]. The next generation of 700    C advanced ultra-supercritical

 (A-USC) coal- ﬁ red power plants could operate at the service tem- perature of 700 e 720    C with the steam pressure up to 35 MPa [ 5 , 6 ]. Only Ni-based or Ni-Fe based heat resistant alloys with better high- temperature creep strength and excellent oxidation/hot corrosion resistance can meet the requirement of the hottest boiler and tur- bine components [ 7 e 11 ].  

Many researchers have simulated the forging process by isothermal compression test to obtain the optimal deformation parameters and avoid macro cracks as well as inhomogeneous microstructures [ 20 e 28 ]. The constitutive equation and processing map have been accepted as powerful tools to evaluate the hot deformation behavior of the materials during isothermal compression. The constitutive equation proposed by Tegart and Sellars [ 29 ] can describe the variation of  ﬂ ow stress under different deformation temperature, strain rate and strain [ 21 , 30 e 33 ]. The processing map (overlap power dissipation map on instability parameter map) according to the dynamic materials model (DMM) put forward by Rao and Prasad et al. [ 34 , 35 ] can be used to guide the hot working processing and control the deformed microstruc- ture without expensive trial-and-error and time-consuming method. The constitutive equation and processing map have been successfully applied in many alloys [ 16 , 17 , 20 , 23 , 26 , 36 e 44 ].  

Recently, a new Ni-Fe based heat resistant alloy GH984G with low cost, excellent high-temperature performance and high ther- mal conductivity was used to manufacture large scale forgings for the 700    C A-USC power plants in China [ 5 , 7 , 12 e 14 ]. The manu- facture of large scale forgings for the Ni-Fe based heat resistant alloy is unmanageable due to the following two reasons. Firstly, a  

In general, the initial alloy state before forging is as-cast alloy. However, the in ﬂ uence of initial alloy state during the isothermal compression test is often neglected. Most of the studies took forged or hot-rolled alloys as the experimental materials in the isothermal compression test [ 16 , 20 , 26 , 36 e 44 ], which probably resulted in a deviation from the actual forging process. This study investigated the hot deformation behaviors and DRX mechanisms of as-cast and hot-rolled alloys in order to establish the correlation between the initial alloy state and hot deformation behavior. The experimental results can optimize the isothermal compression test and provide more reliable forging parameters for manufacturing the large scale forgings of the Ni-Fe based alloy GH984G.

# 2.2. Hot compression  

Cylindrical specimens of 6 mm in diameter and 11 mm in height were machined from the as-cast and hot-rolled alloys. Isothermal compression test was conducted on a Gleeble-1500 thermo-me- chanical simulator over the deformation temperature range of 900 e 1200    C at a 50    C interval and the strain rates of 0.01, 0.1, 1.0, or 10 s  1   with the nominal strain of 50%. According to the calcu- lated result in  Fig. 2 ,  g 0   phase can hardly in ﬂ uence the hot defor- mation since it has dissolved into the matrix when the temperature is higher than 900    C. The specimens were heated up to the deformation temperature at a rate of 10    C/s and held for 30 s before deformation to eliminate thermal gradient. The specimens were water quenched immediately after deformation to preserve the deformation microstructure.

The higher dislocation density in the as-cast alloy can lead to heavier working hardening. Hence, the  ﬂ ow stress of the as-cast alloy is greater than that of the hot-rolled alloy at high strain as shown in  Fig. 3 . However, the hot-rolled alloy exhibits greater  ﬂ ow stress in the early stage of deformation at low temperature and high strain rate as shown in  Fig. 3 (c) and (d), which is related to the different grain size in the as-cast and hot-rolled alloys. At low temperature and high strain rate, the migration of grain boundary is slow and the dislocation multiplication is rapid. In hot-rolled alloy with ﬁ ne grains, more grain boundaries lead to the increasing of dislocation density and the  ﬂ ow stress increase rapidly in the early stage of deformation. Nevertheless, in as-cast alloy with large grains, the dislocation density and the  ﬂ ow stress increase slowly because of the limited grain boundaries and dislocation source. Therefore, the  ﬂ ow stress of hot-rolled alloy is larger than that in as-cast alloy in the early stage of deformation at low temperature and high strain rate. As the strain increases, the high dislocation density promotes the occurring of DRX driven by the decrease of stored energy in hot-rolled alloy, which results in the decrease of dislocation density and  ﬂ ow stress at high strain. But the low rate of dislocation multiplication retards the process of DRX in as-cast alloy and the grain boundaries act as the obstacles to the dislocation motion continuously. The dislocation density and ﬂ ow stress remain unchanged even increase with further defor- mation. Hence, the  ﬂ ow stress of hot-rolled alloy is lower than that in as-cast alloy at high strain.

# 4.4. Dynamic recrystallization mechanism  

# 4.4.1. Effect of initial alloy state on DRX  

The sub-grains and DRX nuclei within the deformed grains demonstrate that the DRX nucleation in the as-cast alloy mainly depends on the continuous dynamic recrystallization (CDRX) [ 73 , 74 ]. The poor deformation compatibility of the coarsening grains in the as-cast alloy leads to the dislocation pile-up near the grain boundaries and within the deformed grains as shown in Fig. 18 (a). To reduce the stored energy, the DRV occurs by disloca- tions annihilation and rearrangement when the dislocation density reaches a threshold, and then the dislocation cells form as indicated in  Fig. 18 (b). The dislocation cells transform into sub-grains and then DRX nuclei by absorbing dislocations as exhibited in  Fig. 18 (c and d) [ 75 ].  

The bulging grain boundary in the hot-rolled alloy indicates that the discontinuous dynamic recrystallization is dominant [ 74 , 76 ]. The process of DDRX is indicated in  Fig. 19 . Driven by the non- uniform dislocation density, the bulging of original grain bound- aries appears as shown in  Fig. 19 (a). The sub-grain boundary evolving from the accumulated dislocations separates the bulging part from the origin grain with further deformation as exhibited in Fig. 19 (b and c). Then the sub-grain boundary transforms into HAGBs by dislocation accumulation and rearrangement as exhibi- ted in  Fig. 19 (d).  

It can be seen from the above analysis that CDRX takes place when the dislocation motion is inhibited continuously, while the good mobility of HAGB is signi ﬁ cant to DDRX [ 74 ]. The poor deformation compatibility caused by huge grains in as-cast alloy

# 5. Conclusions  

The hot deformation behaviors and DRX mechanisms of the Ni- Fe based alloy GH984G with different initial states (as-cast and hot- rolled) were investigated. The following conclusions can be drawn from the research:  

(1) The  ﬂ ow stress of the as-cast alloy is greater compared with that of the hot-rolled alloy at high strain. The apparent activation energy of the as-cast alloy (416 kJ/mol) is larger than that of the hot-rolled alloy (360 kJ/mol). (2) The initial alloy state has an evident in ﬂ uence on the pro- cessing map. The optimal processing domain of the as-cast alloy locates in the strain rate range of 0.01 e 0.5 s  1   and temperature range of 1100 e 1200    C, while 1.0 e 10 s  1   and 1100 e 1200    C for the hot-rolled alloy. The  ﬂ ow instability domain of the as-cast alloy is signi ﬁ cantly larger than that of the hot-rolled alloy. (3) The different deformation behaviors of the as-cast and hot- rolled alloys result from the discrepant initial grain sizes and DRX mechanisms. In the as-cast alloy with the grain size of 400  m m, the poor deformation compatibility results in dislocation pile-up and compels the CDRX to activate only when the dislocation density exceeds a threshold. In the hot- rolled alloy with the grain size of 5  m m, the good deformation compatibility makes the grain boundary migration easier and promotes the occurrence of DDRX. (4) The main DRX nucleation modes in optimal processing and ﬂ ow instability domains are DDRX and CDRX, respectively. The dislocation density in the optimal processing domain is lower an order of magnitude than that in the  ﬂ ow instability domain. The DDRX and low dislocation density are desirable in the alloy during hot deformation. (5) The hot deformation behaviors and DRX mechanisms of the as-cast and hot-rolled alloys are different. The as-cast alloy should be taken as the experimental material in the isothermal compression test, which is consistent with the actual forging process and can provide more reliable forging parameters for manufacturing the large scale forgings.
