# A R T I C L E I N F O  

# A B S T R A C T  

Two FeCrAl alloy coatings with different Cr and Al contents were deposited on Zr-alloy substrates via the cold spray deposition method to study the efficacy of these coatings to improve the accident tolerance of Zr-alloy fuel cladding in light water reactors (LWRs). First, the coatings were tested in a 400 °C steam autoclave for 72 h as an accelerated method to simulate the normal LWR operating conditions. In these tests, both coatings resulted in notable improvements in oxidation resistance compared to the Zr-alloy, with the higher Cr containing coating exhibiting a thinner oxide layer. Both coatings provided good oxidation resistance when tested in 1200 °C ambient air environment; however, the formation of a low melting point eutectic (\~928 °C) between Fe and Zr resulted in significant melting associated with inter-diffusion between Fe in the coating and the Zr in the sub- strate. Therefore, to take advantage of the superior oxidation resistance that FeCrAl coatings can provide, a Mo interlayer was deposited, also by the cold spray process, in-between the protective FeCrAl coating and the Zr- alloy substrate, thus creating a dual cold spray layer accident tolerant coated cladding concept. The wear re- sistance of the FeCrAl coating was superior to the Zr-alloy substrate based on pin-on-disk wear tests, providing an indication of the benefits of such coatings to reduce grit-to-rod fretting (GTRF) damage. The feedstock powders, microstructure, phases, hardness of coatings, as well as oxide and inter-diffusion layers of oxidized coatings were examined using SEM, XRD, and XPS characterization techniques.

# 1. Introduction  

Zirconium-alloys (referred to henceforth as Zr-alloys) have been successfully used as cladding materials for light water reactors (LWRs) for many decades due to their good corrosion resistance at operating temperatures and low absorption cross section of thermal neutrons  [1] . However, during accident conditions, including a loss-of-coolant acci- dent (LOCA), the temperature in the reactor can rise due to decay heat, causing the Zr-alloy cladding to readily oxidize in reaction with the water coolant or steam  [2] . This can result in temperatures rising up to 1200 °C and higher and cause the Zr-alloy cladding to react exother- mically with steam to produce combustible hydrogen gas. This situation is currently being addressed by nuclear industries and research in- stitutions through development of accident tolerant fuel (ATF) con- cepts. The general goal of all ATF concepts is to tolerate an accidental loss of active cooling in the reactor core while at the same time main- taining or even improving fuel performance during normal operating conditions  [3] . ATF concepts being investigated include complete re- placement of Zr-alloy cladding with more oxidation-resistant materials  

As a more near-term option, deposition of thin oxidation-resistant coatings on the conventional Zr-alloy cladding are being considered, as this will not warrant significant changes in reactor design and opera- tion. Several coating processes have been developed to deposit such oxidation-resistant alloys onto structural substrates  [8–12] . In the present study we have investigated the cold spray process for the de- position of FeCrAl alloys with two different compositions on Zr-alloy substrates for enhanced oxidation resistance. An outermost coating may even improve the mechanical properties of the cladding. Research has shown that coatings such as TiN, CrN, Mo, Cr, and others can improve wear resistance, which may be beneficial from the standpoint of redu- cing damage due to grid-to-rod fretting (GTRF) wear or other types of wear in LWRs [ 13 , 14 ]. Few studies have shown that FeCrAl alloys have superior wear resistance compared to conventional Zr-alloys [ 15 , 16 ], so it is worthwhile to assess the wear resistance of cold sprayed FeCrAl coatings in addition to their oxidation resistance.  

In the cold spray process, the feedstock powder is propelled by the pressurized gas (usually nitrogen, helium, or a combination thereof) at supersonic velocities onto the substrate to form a coating  [17] . The particle temperature is low and the deposition occurs in solid-state. Coating formation occurs by severe plastic deformation of particles and an associated adiabatic shear process  [17] . The deposition rates are high, making the process is amenable to large scale manufacturing. The challenge of using FeCrAl/Zr coated cladding system is the formation of a low melting point eutectic between Fe and Zr at temperatures as low as 928 °C [ 18 , 19 ], which in effect renders this coated cladding concept unfeasible for the ATF application. Therefore, in this study, we have developed a Mo interlayer coating, also deposited by the cold spray process, to serve as a diffusion barrier between the outer FeCrAl coating and underlying Zr-alloy substrate. Optimization of this Mo barrier layer has since been completed, and detailed characterization and testing can be found in the authors’ previous paper  [20] .  

# 2. Experimental  

Gas-atomized FeCrAl powders of three compositions were procured from Sandvik Osprey Ltd. (UK). The general powder compositions in- vestigated, Fe-10Cr-6Al, Fe-20Cr-5Al, and Fe-22Cr-6Al-Y, will be re- ferred to henceforth as Low-Alloy (LA) FeCrAl, High-Alloy (HA) FeCrAl, and FeCrAlY, respectively. The as-received powder particle size was below 44 µm (−325 mesh). These powders were mechanically sieved to retain particles below 25 µm (−500 mesh) for this research. LA FeCrAl and HA FeCrAl powders were cold sprayed onto Zircaloy-4 (Zr-4) flat substrates purchased from ATI Wah Chang (Albany OR, USA). The compositions provided by the respective powder and substrate suppliers are shown in  Table 1 . Also included is the composition of a bulk commercial FeCrAl alloy (Kanthal AF, Sandvik, Sweden), which was used to benchmark the oxidation resistance of the coatings developed in this study.  

Development of the FeCrAl/Mo coating system was performed in- dependently from the FeCrAl/Zr work, where different powders and substrates were used  [20] . All work with the FeCrAl/Mo dual layered coating was performed on Optimized ZIRLO™fuel cladding tubes sup- plied by Westinghouse Electric Company (Hopkins SC, USA). Com- mercial Mo powder (99.8% purity) with particle size range of 7–23 µm was procured from Powder Alloy Corp. (Loveland OH, USA). The outer coating on top of the Mo coating was FeCrAlY. Compositions of these powders and substrates are also listed in  Table 1 .  

Technologies (CGT) Kinetiks 4000/34 unit high-pressure cold spray system at the University of Wisconsin. The system provides control over parameters such as the gas preheat temperature, process gas flow rate, and powder feed rate. The cold spray gun in this system is mounted on six-axis robot arm allowing for precise deposition control. Prior to de- position, the surfaces of the Zr-4 and ZIRLO™substrates were ground with 320 grit abrasive SiC paper to slightly roughen the surface and to remove any remnants of surface oxides from early processing steps. This was followed by cleaning with acetone and ethanol to ensure a clean surface. Both FeCrAl and FeCrAlY powders were deposited using N 2  gas (99.998% minimum purity) preheated to 750 °C at a pressure of 40 bar, with the nozzle being cooled using N 2  gas. The transverse speed of the nozzle was 400 mm/s using one pass. The Mo powder was deposited using N 2  gas preheated to 500 °C at a pressure of 40 bar with the gun traverse speed set at 100 mm/s, using one pass.  

Metallographic preparation of samples mounted in a conductive resin involved grinding with SiC abrasive paper successively up to 1200 grit and polishing using diamond suspensions and colloidal silica up to a mirror-finish. The examination and analysis of microstructures and phase purity of the powders, coatings, and oxide layers, as well as in- terface adhesion and wear tracks were performed using a Zeiss LEO 1530 scanning electron microscope (SEM) in conjunction with energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS). X-ray diffraction (XRD) was per- formed using a Bruker D8 Discover Diffractometer with Cu-Kα radiation spanning a 2θ range of 15°–90° Nano-indentation was performed on cross-sections of the powder particles and cold spray coatings using a Bruker Hysitron TI-950 Triboindenter with a Berkovich tip. This method is displacement-controlled with a maximum displacement of 175 nm.  

A pin-on-disk wear testing apparatus was used to qualitatively the wear resistance of the LA and HA FeCrAl coatings as compared to un- coated Zr-4. Sample coupon surfaces were polished up to 1200 grit to create a smooth testing surface. The test was performed in an ambient environment, using a 3 mm diameter ruby (Al 2 O 3 ) ball stylus counter surface rotating at 140 rpm with an impressed load of 25 g for a total of 120 min to create a circular wear track with a radius of 4.5–5.5 mm. The wear track surface was observed using SEM, and the wear track depth was quantified using a white light Zygo interferometer. While these wear test conditions do not represent fretting under prototypical conditions, they nonetheless provide an initial assessment of the wear resistance of the cold spray coatings.

# 3. Results and discussion  

# 3.1. FeCrAl powders and cold spray coatings  

The size distribution and spherical morphology of the HA FeCrAl powder after sieving to below 25 µm in size is shown in  Fig. 1 a. The cross-sectional image of the microstructure of individual particles showed large equiaxed grains ( Fig. 1 b). No major differences in particle size, morphology, and microstructure between HA FeCrAl powder and LA FeCrAl powder (not shown) were observed. An SEM image of the Mo powder depicting its morphology, as well as XRD of the powder, are shown in  Fig. 2 . Though the process is not explicitly known, the powder appears to be large clusters of agglomerated smaller particles ( Fig. 2 a). XRD patterns show the powder to be phase pure Mo with no oxides or other extraneous elements ( Fig. 2 b).  

Cold spray deposition of the two FeCrAl powders was performed on flat Zircaloy-4 coupons at a constant transverse spray speed and a constant powder feed rate. Over 100 µm thick coatings were deposited in less than one minute for both LA and HA FeCrAl powders, testifying to the high deposition rate of the cold spray process. X-ray diffraction results ( Fig. 3 ) revealed that both the powders and the coatings contained peaks corresponding to pure body-centered cubic (BCC) Fe- CrAl with no additional detectable peaks; thus, no oxidation or sec- ondary phase changes occurred during deposition, indicating that phases present in the feedstock powders were retained in the coatings. There was noticeable peak broadening in the resultant coating that occurred after deposition of the feedstock powders ( Fig. 3 b) due to non- uniform microstrain in the matrix [ 22 , 23 ]. Since particles undergo se- vere plastic deformation and work hardening during deposition, cold spray coatings contain residual compressive stresses, and these effects are exhibited in the broadening of the diffraction peaks.  

Additionally, a small peak shift of about 0.02° between the HA and LA FeCrAl coatings was observed for the (110) peak. This is possibly due to a change in lattice parameter associated with difference in the concentration of alloying elements between the two types of FeCrAl alloy powders investigated in this study. In particular, the role of Al concentration in increasing the lattice parameter of FeCrAl alloy has been reported earlier  [24–26] , and supported by our data shown in Fig. 3 b. Further investigation will be done at higher 2θ values, where peak broadening and peak shift are more pronounced.  

An initial investigation of the FeCrAl coating microstructures was performed by observing the coatings in cross-section using SEM ( Fig. 4 ). Both the low- and high-alloy FeCrAl coatings have a dense micro- structure, but contained some regions of porosity  [27] , which can be reduced or even eliminated by further optimization of cold spray parameters (e.g., propellant gas temperature, pressure, and gun tra- verse speed). The micrographs of the coating-substrate interface in- dicated strong adhesion with no cracking/delamination and negligible deformation of the Zr-alloy substrate due to the cold spray deposition process. The high magnification images of the coating microstructure shown in  Fig. 4 c and f are suggestive of a very dense coating with a swirled-type structure indicative of severe plastic deformation, and regions of dynamic recrystallization inherent to the cold spray process. # 3.2. High temperature air oxidation test of coated and uncoated Zr-alloy  

As an initial screening test, coated and uncoated samples were ex- posed to a 1200 °C ambient air environment for 20 min to examine their oxidation resistance. This test provided a well-defined exposure time window where samples experienced rapid heating and cooling rates, allowing for a qualitative assessment of both the thermal shock re- sistance and mechanical integrity of the coatings in comparison to uncoated Zr-4 alloy cladding.  

In order to reduce the neutron penalty associated with FeCrAl coatings, the total thickness of the coatings is expected to be relatively thin with respect to the total wall thickness of the Zr-4 alloy cladding. However, in this first experiment, the HA and LA FeCrAl coatings were polished down until the coatings were about 80 µm thick, to remove the top surface roughness until the coating surfaces were topographically smooth. Before oxidation test, the coated and uncoated samples had smooth surfaces (equivalent to Ra \~265 nm) after polishing with 600 grit SiC paper. Photographs of the three samples before and after 1200 °C oxidation are shown in  Fig. 5 . After oxidation tests, preliminary visual inspection revealed that melting had occurred on the surface of the HA and LA FeCrAl samples due to the formation of a Fe-Zr eutectic at 928 °C at the coating-substrate interface, as indicated by the rough and wavy surface of the coated samples ( Fig. 5 b). The uncoated Zr-4 sample exhibited a dark uniform oxidized surface with a few white nodules.  

After 20 min of oxidation, the Zr-4 sample formed a reasonably thick (\~80 µm) zirconium oxide layer on the surface ( Fig. 6 ). The zir- conium oxide layer was confirmed using XRD to be mostly monoclinic ZrO 2  and some tetragonal ZrO 2 , which is typical in high temperature air environments. It can be expected that the oxidative environment during a LOCA condition would accelerate the growth of the oxide layer, leading to the degradation of the mechanical robustness of the Zr-alloy cladding  [28] .  

Upon heating of the FeCrAl coated Zr-alloy samples, the Fe in the coating begins to diffuse into the Zr-alloy substrate. While Fe has low solubility in α-Zr, the small fraction that is dissolved has extremely fast diffusivity in α-Zr  [29] . As the Zr-alloy substrate is heated further and transformed into β-Zr at about 863 °C, Fe solubility and diffusivity of Fe in β-Zr both increases rapidly  [30] . Once the sample reaches the tem- perature range of 928–948 °C, melting at either the Fe/Zr eutectic or at the liquidus in the L+S region occurs in the Fe-rich side of the Zr-Fe phase diagram  [18] . This is shown macroscopically in  Fig. 5 b, as the flat, smooth surface that was present before 1200 °C exposure turned into a rough, wavy surface indicative of melting.

An attempt was made to explain the various regions present in the LA FeCrAl/Zr-4 system after high-temperature ambient air exposure ( Fig. 7 a). Above 928 °C, Zr in Fe/Zr liquid preferentially reacted with oxygen to form ZrO 2  layer at the surface, most likely due to its higher affinity for oxygen than Fe at these temperatures  [31] . A thinner oxygen-stabilized alpha-Zr region is formed below the oxide layer, which is typical for the Zr-alloy systems and was also noted to be present in the uncoated Zr sample  [32–34] . Below this region, a large diffusion zone formed with varying compositions of a Fe-Zr-Cr ternary system. However, there were some observable differences between the LA and HA FeCrAl samples. The LA FeCrAl sample had a thinner ZrO 2  layer and a thicker reacted zone on the “coated” side of the sample, as confirmed by EDS analysis ( Fig. 7 b). This was most likely because the LA FeCrAl contained more Fe that was able to react with the Zr, thus forming more of the low melting point eutectic and leaving less Zr to form the surface oxide layer. Since the HA FeCrAl contained less Fe, there was more Zr to react to form the eutectic and other phases, as well as more Zr to migrate to the surface and form the oxide layer ( Fig. 7 d).  

Additionally, an investigation was made into the reaction region of the HA FeCrAl sample ( Fig. 8 ). This complicated microstructure is highlighted in  Fig. 8 a, which shows a multitude of phases contained in one small region located below the α-Zr(O) region. This zone ( Fig. 8 b) contained a mixed eutectic-like microstructure with larger Zr-rich re- gions, Fe-rich regions, and smaller regions with mixed Zr/Fe/Cr com- positions. EDS spectral imaging maps ( Fig. 8 c) highlight the various phases contained in this area. In the Fe-Zr binary system, EDS showed mostly FeZr 3  and FeZr 2  phases, though it is possible that the Fe 23 Zr 6 phase was present as well  [19] . In the Fe-Zr-Cr ternary system, the stable phases most likely formed at this temperature were β(Fe,Cr,Zr) and Laves phase forms of (Fe,Cr) 2 Zr in the Fe-rich system. While re- search has been done to quantify the various phases that can form in the Fe-rich side of the Fe-Zr-Cr ternary [ 35 , 36 ], more work must be done in the future to investigate the phases particularly those coming from the Zr-rich side of the ternary that are contained within this region formed after high-temperature oxidation of a cold sprayed FeCrAl coating.  

In order to more clearly observe and establish the protective nature of the FeCrAl coating, a HA FeCrAl coated sample with a much thicker coating (\~750 µm) was prepared and subjected to high-temperature ambient air oxidation at the same conditions (1200 °C, 20 min) as the previous samples. After exposure, it was clear that the coating did not exhibit any delamination or cracking, and the surface remained smooth and adhered to the substrate. The X-ray diffraction pattern ( Fig. 9 ) confirmed that no Zr had reached the surface, and revealed that a thin, protective Al 2 O 3  layer had formed on the surface.  

After examining the microstructure ( Fig. 10 ), it was estimated that about 200 µm of coating were consumed after 20 min of oxidation, which resulted in a diffusion layer 850 µm thick ( Fig. 10 b). However, since the coating was thick enough, it was able to form a thin protective oxide layer about 400–700 nm thick ( Fig. 10 c) to protect the rest of the coating and Zr-4 substrate, which is illuminated by the EDS element maps shown in  Fig. 10 d. Additionally, the coating itself experienced grain growth from the high temperature annealing. The highly plasti- cally deformed microstructure shown in  Fig. 4  could no longer be seen, and significant recrystallization and grain growth occurred with an average size of 8 µm. In addition, the protective aluminum-oxide layer on the surface of the coating prevented significant oxygen ingress into the coating and substrate.

3.4. Pressurized steam corrosion testing of cold spray FeCrAl coatings  

Immediately following the 400 °C steam autoclave test for 72 h, the surfaces of the samples were examined in the plan-view under the SEM to observe the morphologies of the oxide layers. The top surface of the LA FeCrAl sample ( Fig. 12 a) showed a densely packed layer of medium- to large-sized (300 to 700 nm) oxide particles that were octahedral in shape. This sample also had an oxide layer slightly greater than 1 µm in thickness ( Fig. 12 b). In contrast, the HA FeCrAl sample showed very few octahedral-shaped iron oxide particles ( Fig. 12 d). Additionally, this sample showed a much thinner oxide layer after exposure (hundreds of nanometers) as compared to the LA FeCrAl (greater than 1 µm). As a comparison, the Zr-4 control sample exhibited a different oxide mor- phology altogether after steam exposure ( Fig. 12 g), showing a smoother, continuous oxide layer with an average thickness of greater than 1 µm. According to Terrani et al.  [37] , the FeCrAl should form a uniform Cr-rich spinel layer adjacent to the metal and Fe-rich spinel crystals on top of that layer after exposure to high-temperature water. Even though this test was much shorter in duration, the oxide particles that formed were very similar in structure to those that formed during one year of exposure reported in this earlier study  [37] .  

XPS was used to characterize composition of the oxide layers for the LA and HA FeCrAl samples, as shown in  Fig. 12 c and f, respectively. To begin, the LA FeCrAl sample shows an oxide layer rich in Fe toward the outer surface and very little to no Cr content. Since the iron oxide crystals appear to be very large ( Fig. 12 a), XPS did not sputter through the entire layer, which is why no Cr-rich spinel layer appeared in Fig. 12 c. It is estimated that the depth of material sputtered during sputtered during XPS analysis was 1 µm for all samples. There was also Al in the oxide layer (\~15–20 at%). This is not likely α–Al 2 O 3  as it is not stable at temperatures below 900 °C. Al may have been contained in the oxide in the form FeAl 2 O 4 , though an in-depth analysis is needed to confirm this phase  [37] . In the HA FeCrAl sample, the surface oxide layer also contained a significant amount of Al, but unlike LA FeCrAl, Cr was present and the Fe content was low.  

XPS results for the HA FeCrAl sample in  Fig. 12 f showed an Fe-rich oxide layer extremely close to the surface, and then a Cr-rich oxide layer further into the sample, as indicated by the increase in Cr content. This could be explained by the sparse oxide particles ( Fig. 12 d) fol- lowed by the uniformly thin oxide layer ( Fig. 12 e), which must be the Cr-rich spinel layer. Additionally, the HA FeCrAl oxide layer was thin enough (less than 500 nm thick) that XPS was able to sputter through the entire oxide layer and into the original HA FeCrAl coating. The rapid diffusion of Fe towards the sample surface may leave an Fe-de- pleted region at the oxide/coating interface  [37] , which appeared to be evident in the HA FeCrAl sample between 600 and 700 s of sputter time.

# 3.5. Molybdenum diﬀusion barrier between FeCrAl coating and Zr-alloy substrate  

It is clear that while FeCrAl alloys show potential for providing oxidation resistance as coatings atop Zr-alloy substrates, they are not a viable candidate during beyond design basis accident conditions. It was postulated that adding a diffusion barrier in-between the cold spray FeCrAl coating and the Zr-alloy substrate would limit diffusion at high temperatures and allow the FeCrAl coating to remain intact while protecting the Zr-alloy at elevated temperatures. After careful in- vestigation, molybdenum (Mo) was chosen as the diffusion-barrier material due to its high melting point (2632 °C), high eutectic points with Fe and Zr (1449 °C in Fe-Mo system  [40]  and 1550 °C in Zr-Mo system  [41] ), and high-temperature creep resistance  [42] .  

Mo powder was sprayed onto the ZIRLO™substrate using the cold spray conditions previously specified. Subsequently, a layer of FeCrAl- alloy was cold spray deposited onto the existing cold spray deposited Mo. The resulting FeCrAl/Mo coating is shown in  Fig. 13 . While the Mo interlayer showed exceptional bonding to the ZIRLO™substrate, the lack of adhesion of the top FeCrAl layer after deposition was likely due to the limited plastic deformation capacity in the as-sprayed Mo in- terlayer. Further work to optimize this dual-layer coating concept – defining the desired Mo interlayer thickness and enhancing adhesion to Zr-alloy substrate and FeCrAl cold spray coating – has been performed

At 1200 °C, high temperature promoted inter-diffusion at the FeCrAl-Mo interface, as well as at the Mo-Zr interface ( Fig. 14 b). Preliminary phase identifi- cation was performed  [20] , but more advanced techniques such as TEM may be required to confirm these phases. Based on the experimental results from these high temperature tests, Mo proves to be an effective diffusion barrier at these extreme temperatures  [20] .  

# 3.6. Pin-on-disk wear testing of cold spray FeCrAl coatings  

The results from pin-on-disk wear tests for LA FeCrAl, HA FeCrAl, and Zircaloy-4 (with the same testing conditions for all three samples) are shown in  Fig. 15 . The LA and HA FeCrAl samples had wear track widths of about 200 and 230 µm, respectively, and a maximum wear track depth of about 5 µm. Meanwhile, the Zr-4 sample had a wear track width of about 575 µm and a maximum wear depth of approximately 18 µm, which is 3–4 times larger than the depths for either FeCrAl coated samples. Smaller wear track sizes under abrasive wear condi- tions typically indicate higher wear resistance. Longer tests may be required to obtain discernable differences between the LA and HA Fe- CrAl coatings. Overall these tests reflect the good mechanical durability of the cold spray coatings but fretting wear testing must be performed under prototypical LWR environment to get an accurate evaluation of the efficacy of these coatings to mitigate GTRF.
