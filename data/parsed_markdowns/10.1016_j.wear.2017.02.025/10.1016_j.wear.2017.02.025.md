# Abstract  

Machining of Inconel 718 is a challenging process because of high hot strength and hardness,  high reactivity with the tool materials and low thermal conductivity of Inconel 718 alloy. Although  SiAlON ceramics in the form of indexable inserts are utilized successfully in turning and milling  operations of Inconel 718, application of solid SiAlON milling cutters on high speed milling of  superalloys is rare. In this study, SiAlON ceramics were manufactured in the form of solid milling  cutters and tested in high speed milling of Inconel 718 in order to investigate the wear mechanisms  during machining process. The wear based on the severe adhesion of the work-piece to the tool flank  face and repeated formation and removal of an Al and Ti-rich diffusion layer was observed at the flank  faces of the tools.  

Keywords:  Engineering ceramics; Cutting tools; Non-ferrous metals; Electron microscopy; Mapping  

# 1.   Introduction  

Inconel 718 is a nickel-based superalloy with superior mechanical properties at high  temperatures and high corrosion resistance which make it a suitable structural material for gas  turbine engine parts such as disks, blades and shafts. This material is considered as one of the  most difficult to cut materials due to its high shear strength, work-hardening tendency, highly  abrasive carbide particles available in the microstructure, strong tendency to form built up  edge (BUE), and low thermal conductivity [1,2]. Therefore, there are limited number of tool  materials which can withstand against aggressive cutting conditions during machining of  Inconel 718.  

In recent years, machining at high speeds has received more attention due to high  material removal rate and short product cycle time [3]. However, there is lack of a certain  definition for high speed machining (HSM) in the literature. Ng et al. [4] defined high-speed  machining (HSM) as end milling operation at rotational speeds up to 100,000 rpm; however,  the authors indicated that 45,000 rpm is a more realistic upper limit when the work-piece  material and the diameter of the cutting tool are taken into consideration. Uncoated solid WC- Co-based tools are widely used in superalloy machining, and can be operated at relatively low  cutting speeds in the range of 10-50 m/min. depending on the carbide grade. Although coating  of WC-Co-based tools with hard ceramic layers is an effective method to increase the cutting  speed, higher cutting speeds than 200 m/min have not been reported for coated carbide tools  in many investigations [5-10]. Sharman et al. [8] tested TiAlN and CrN coated tools at 90 and  150 m/min. and concluded that TiAlN coated tools had the longest tool life at 90 m/min.  Similarly, Jawaid et al. [5] used 100 m/min. as a maximum cutting speed for testing of PVD  coated carbide tools. Kadirgama et al. [11] investigated the wear behavior of PVD and CVD  coated carbide tools at cutting speed in the range of 100-180 m/min on machining of  Hastelloy C-22HS. They stated adhesion/attrition, built-up edge (BUE) and oxidation as the  dominant wear mechanism during machining of Hastelloy C-22HS and suggested the cutting  speeds lower than 100 m/min for optimum tool life. The cutting speeds exceeding 200 m/min.  were defined as ultra-high-speeds for nickel-based super alloys by Zheng et al. [12] who  investigated the cutting performance, failure types, and mechanisms of the graded round  ceramic tools via ultra-high-speed milling of Inconel 718. At these machining conditions,  only ceramics and ultra-hard tools can be utilized due to their superior thermo-mechanical  properties compared to coated and uncoated carbide counterparts.  

Although indexable ceramic cutting tools have been widely investigated in high-speed  turning of Inconel 718 [13-17], there are only a few publications on the utilization of ceramic  inserts in high-speed milling of superalloys in the machining literature. Zheng et al. [12]  investigated relative cutting performance of the graded nano-ceramic round inserts based on  Si 3 N 4 /TiCN and commercial Al 2 O 3 -SiCw ceramic tools on ultra-high speed milling of Inconel  718. They observed micro-chipping, microcracking, abrasion and adhesion as the main wear  mechanisms on the cutting edges of the tools. They also stated that the graded nano-composite  tools showed better wear resistance due to their layered structure which provides higher  thermal and mechanical resistance. Tian et al. [18] studied high-speed face-milling of Inconel  718 with SiAlON ceramic inserts attached to a holder with 25 mm diameter under dry cutting  conditions. While the notch wear was found to be the dominant mechanism when the cutting  speed is relatively low (600-1400 m/min.), it was the adhesion wear observed at the cutting  edges of the tools at higher cutting speeds in the range of 1800-3000 m/min. In a recent study,  Akhtar et al. [19] analyzed the surface integrity of Inconel 718 alloy high speed milled by  coated carbide and whisker reinforced alumina ceramic inserts. According to author’s  findings, finish surface roughness of the work-piece alloy machined with whisker reinforced  alumina tool was lower than that of the coated carbide tool due to the adherence of material  debris at higher cutting speeds at which the ceramic tool was tested.  

Main reason for the limited use of ceramic tools in milling operations is related to the  heavy interrupted cutting action of the tool edge which requires high resistance to micro- chipping and fracture from the tool material. Therefore, brittle ceramic tools were only  preferred for turning and milling operations in the form of indexable inserts with strong  geometries. However, solid ceramic milling tools have recently been subjected to the  machining literature. Wang and Liu [20] prepared Al 2 O 3 -(W,Ti)C and Si 3 N 4 -TiCN based  solid ceramic milling tools and investigated the relative performance of ceramic tools on  milling of hardened AISI H13 steel at a constant cutting speed of 100 m/min. The authors  concluded that the solid ceramic milling tools have considerably advantageous in terms of  tool life, resultant cutting forces and machined surface quality over WC-Co based milling  tools. Moreover, commercial solid SiAlON milling tools have now been introduced to the  machining market, thanks to advances in precise controlling of microstructure of the SiAlON  based ceramics and also manufacturing techniques (wheel grinding, gas pressure sintering,  etc) which give possibility to obtain more reliable tools for high speed machining (600-1000  m/min) of superalloys. It was stated by Tian et al. [18] that using of such high cutting speeds  brings an advantage to ceramic tools due to high temperature of the cutting zone above  1000°C at which the superalloy starts to soften and therefore the forces acting on the cutting  edge of the tool reduces reasonably. Our previous investigation on the high speed milling of  Inconel 625 alloy by SiAlON-TiN ceramic milling cutters with two different geometries [21]  also showed that refractory SiAlON tools can successfully be operated without any fracturing  and considerable micro-chipping under such high speed conditions.  

In the present work, an α/β-SiAlON ceramic and its TiN reinforced composite were  manufactured in the form of solid milling cutter with a diameter of 12mm. SiAlON based  milling cutters were tested on the side milling operation of Inconel 718 alloy and wear  mechanism of the tools was investigated. The detailed analysis on the diffusion between  SiAlON and Inconel 718 alloy at machining temperature were performed with a novel sample  preparation technique based on the welding of SiAlON ceramics and Inconel 718 alloy by  spark plasma sintering. The diffusion couple results were correlated with the findings of  actual machining tests.  

# 2.   Experimental Procedure  

# 2.1.   Manufacturing of SiAlON milling tools  

A SiAlON based ceramic (S1) and its TiN reinforced composite (S2) were prepared by  wet mixing of proper amount of high purity raw powders (Si 3 N 4  (UBE-SE10/Japan), AlN 

 (Tokuyama/Japan),  Al 2 O 3   (Sumitomo/Japan),  Y 2 O 3   (Treibacher/  Austria),  Sm 2 O 3  

 (Treibacher/Austria), CaO (in the form of CaCO 3 ), TiN (HC. Stark/ Germany)) in an attrition  mill (Union Process/USA). Then, the slurry with \~60 wt. % of solid loading was mixed with  organic additives as pressing aids for subsequent powder consolidation. The granulation of the  slurry was performed by a spray dryer (Nubilosa/Germany) and nearly spherical granules with  a good flowability were obtained. SiAlON granules were cold isostatically pressed into  cylindrical rods (Ø15 mm×100 mm) in a flexible polyurethane die under 200 MPa pressure.  After removal of binder with 1 hour heat treatment at 600°C, the compacts were sintered in a  gas pressure sintering (GPS) furnace (FCT-Anlagenbau GmbH, Germany) at a maximum  temperature of \~1950°C for 1 h under 100 bar N 2  gas pressure.  

After sintering, microstructural analysis of the SiAlON based ceramics was performed  via a scanning electron microscope (SEM) (Zeiss-Supra 50 VP/Germany). Phases formed  after sintering were examined by X-ray diffractometer (Rigaku Rint 2200/Japan) and α- SiAlON/β-SiAlON ratios were calculated by XRD patterns using the integrated intensities of  (102) and (210) reflections of α-SiAlON and (101) and (210) reflections of β-SiAlON. The  calculations were performed using the equations developed by Liddle [22] who studied X-ray  analysis of nitrogen-based ceramic phases.  z  values of the SiAlON ceramics were also  determined by means of dimensional change of hexagonal β-Si 3 N 4  unit cell in  a  and  c   direction. Hardness and fracture toughness values were measured by the Vickers indentation  method and fracture toughness of the materials was calculated according to the equation  developed by Evans and Charles [23]  

The sintered SiAlON rods were ground based on a six flute milling tool geometry with  a diameter of 12 mm which was patented by Davis et al. [24] (Fig. 1). D64 grit grinding  wheels (Wendt-Germany) attached to a CNC controlled grinding machine were used to give  the desired geometry to the cylindrical SiAlON rods.

# 2.2.Diffusion couple tests  

Since the cutting temperature is expected to be higher than 1000°C during high speed  milling of Inconel 718 alloy, the diffusion between SiAlON and Inconel 718 alloy are  considered as an effective parameter which may alter the cutting performance of the tools and  thus needs to be clarified. The diffusion couples of SiAlON-Inconel 718 were prepared by  spark plasma sintering (SPS) technique under vacuum conditions. An Inconel 718 specimen  with a dimensions of 5 mm×5 mm×3 mm was sandwiched between two cylindrical SiAlON  pellets which were cut from the sintered SiAlON rods with a thickness and a diameter of 5  and 12 mm, respectively. The sandwiched SiAlON and Inconel 718 samples were heated in  the graphite mold setup of SPS furnace up to 1200°C with a heating rate of 20°C/min. A 30  min. dwell time was applied in order to let the diffusion of the atoms in SiAlON and Inconel  718 alloy takes place. The schematic illustration of the heating setup and the operating  conditions are given in Fig. 2. Then, the diffusion couples were cut from the cross-section and  mirror polished for the subsequent microstructural characterization. The interface of SiAlON  and Inconel 718 was examined via SEM equipped with an energy dispersive X-ray  spectrometer (EDX). The average diffusion zone thickness of each diffusion couple was  calculated by the average of 20 measurements taken along the interface shown in SEM  micrographs.

# 2.3.Machining tests  

A representative photograph of the SiAlON milling tools during high speed dry  milling tests performed on (Makino-GF8/Japan) 5-axis CNC machining center is given in Fig.  3. The workpiece material used in milling tests was a cylindrical Inconel 718 superalloy part  with a diameter of 100 mm. The composition of the Inconel 718 workpiece obtained by EDX  analysis and the machining parameters used in the machining tests are given in Tables 1 and  2, respectively. The cutting speed was selected as over 500 m/min in order to obtain cutting  zone temperature higher than 1000°C at which Inconel 718 alloys starts to soften as suggested  in [24]. Almost the total length of the flutes was used as axial depth of cut. Each tool was used  for side milling of cylindrical workpiece and each test was stopped when the diameter of the  part was reduced from 100 to 20 mm. The cutting time of this operation was measured as 6  min. and 6 progressive cycle were applied for each tool, meaning that the total machining  time was 36 min. The worn flank faces of the SiAlON based tools were characterized in every  2 cycle (12 min. cutting time) by SEM.

# 3.   Results and Discussion  

# 3.1.   Characterization of the SiAlON Milling Tools  

The properties and the back scattered electron image (BEI) SEM micrographs of the  SiAlON based ceramics are given in Table 3 and Fig. 4, respectively. While β-SiAlON phase  appears as rod-like dark grains, α-SiAlON phase is seen as more equiaxed light gray grains.  Inter-granular phase (IGP) due to sintering additives is seen as white pockets distributed in the  microstructures (Fig. 4). This phase can be found either crystalline or amorphous and is  considered as the weakest part of SiAlON ceramics due to its insufficient thermo-mechanical  resistance compared to the individual α and β-SiAlON grains. According to XRD analysis  results, no particular X-ray reflections belonging to a crystalline IGP was observed for the  SiAlON based ceramics. Apart from α, β and IGP phases, the white equiaxed grains  distributed homogeneously in the microstructure of S2 are TiN particulates (Fig. 4 (b)).

The mechanical properties of the SiAlON based ceramics are directly related to the z  value of β-SiAlON grains, indicating the solubility of Al 2 O 3  in Si 3 N 4  crystal structure, amount  of phases and the microstructural features such as size, shape and distribution of the grains. S1  and S2 ceramics prepared in this study have a slight difference in microstructural properties,  amount of β-phase and z value (Table 3 and Fig. 4). However, it is obvious that slightly  higher hardness and lower fracture toughness of S2 compared to S1 was the result of higher α- SiAlON content in this material. Moreover, the increase in  z  value provided higher interfacial  bond strength which caused less debonding and crack deflection between SiAlON grains. This  is in well agreement with the results of Becher et al. [25] and Sun et al. [26] who performed a  detailed investigation on the fracture behavior of silicon nitride ceramics with different  microstructural characteristics.  

# 3.2.   Diffusion Couple Tests  

The diffusion couple samples were manufactured by simultaneous pressing and  heating of the sandwiched SiAlON-Inconel 718 sample in a SPS furnace as shown in Fig. 2,  schematically. The displacement of the Cu-Be pistons of SPS furnace depending on the  thickness of the sample as a function of temperature is given in Fig. 5. It is seen that the  sandwiched SiAlON-Inconel 718 sample showed some thermal expansion up to \~900°C due  to the increase in temperature of the samples. After a flat period between 900-1060°C, the  pistons started to move toward each other due to the flow of Inconel 718 between two  SiAlON samples. It is obvious that the adhesion of SiAlON and Inconel 718 alloy became  significant above this temperature depending on the chemically bonding of the atoms of the  two parts.

The BEI-SEM micrographs showing the diffusion zone between Inconel 718 alloy and  SiAlON ceramics are given in Fig. 6(a) and 6(b). The average of 20 measurements in  thickness of the diffusion zone and their standard deviations are also presented in Fig. 6(c). As  seen in Fig. 6, while the average thicknesses of the diffusion zones of S1 and S2 are measured  similar to each other, the scatter in the length of the diffusion zone (standard deviation) of S2  (Fig. 6 (c)) is considerably high due to different chemical reactivity of SiAlON and TiN  grains with Inconel 718 alloy which promoted inhomogeneity on the smoothness of the  interface.

Fig. 6. BEI-SEM micrographs of the diffusion layers of (a) S1, (b) S2 and (c) the thicknesses of the diffusion zones of S1 and S2  

In order to investigate the chemical composition of the diffusion zones in detail, the  diffusion zones of the Inconel 718 and SiAlON based ceramics were analyzed by energy  dispersive x-ray spectroscopy (EDX) in mapping mode. The distribution of the elements in  the diffusion zones of S1 and S2 are given in Fig. 7 (a) and (b), respectively. As seen in Fig.  

6, while the diffusion zones of S1 and S2 were formed mainly by Cr, they also contain some  other elements from Inconel 718 side and distributed in distinguished regions of the diffusion  zones. S1 has a continuous brighter region neighboring the Inconel 718 side in which the  atoms of Nb and Mo are concentrated (Fig. 7 (a)). The migration of Nb and Mo atoms from  the inner parts of the Inconel 718 alloys to the interface occurred during heat treatment  process. The migration of these atoms towards the diffusion zone was also observed for S2,  however, its uniformity is low in comparison to S1 (Fig. 7 (b)). Therefore, the Nb and Mo- rich regions were observed locally in the diffusion zone of S2. Furthermore, Al-rich grains  encapsulated by Cr-Mo-Nb matrix along the zone were observed next to the Nb-Mo-rich  zone. It should be remembered that Al exists in both SiAlON and Inconel 718 side and  amount of Al in the SiAlON compositions is expected to be effective on the formation of Al- rich grains in the diffusion zones. It is also important to note that both diffusion zones of S1  and S2 contain no Si atoms indicating that most of the Si atoms were dissolved by Inconel  718 alloy. In case of S2, TiN grains also appeared in the zone without showing any  considerable change in their size and morphology compared to ones in ceramic side (see Figs.  6 (b) and 7 (b)). It could be concluded that TiN particulates have a better chemical stability  than that of SiAlON grains against the attack of the atoms from the Inconel 718 side. This is  probably the reason for irregular border of the diffusion zone on SiAlON side.

# 3.3.   Machining Tests  

The BEI-SEM micrographs of the worn cutting flank faces of the SiAlON milling  tools after 12, 24 and 36 min. cutting tests are given in Fig. 8. It is clear that the rake and the  flank faces of all the SiAlON based tools are significantly covered by the Inconel 718 alloy  due to the high interface temperature at the cutting zone. Although Trend and Wright [27]  reported that the chips have been seen to leave the tool as red hot with a temperature over  650°C during machining of nickel based alloys at high cutting speeds, the interface  temperature during cutting tests was expected to be much higher than the softening  temperature of the Inconel 718 which was measured as \~1060°C during manufacturing of  diffusion couples (Fig. 5). The adhesion of the workpiece material on the tool faces due to  high cutting temperatures when high speed turning of Inconel 718 alloy with SiAlON-Si 3 N 4   and whisker reinforced alumina tools were reported by Zheng et al. [13] and Bushlya et al.  [28], respectively. The adhesion based wear was also reported by Tian et al. [18] as a  dominant wear mechanism when the cutting speed is higher than 1,400 m/min during high  speed milling of Inconel 718 alloy with SiAlON tools. After first 12 min. cutting test, a  characteristic adhesion zone with a depth of 50-100 µm was formed at the flank face of S1.  On the other hand, a crater along the flank face was observed as a result of the instantaneous  removal of adhered Inconel 718 layer from the flank face of S2 within this stage of the tests.  Although the zone thickness of S2-Inconel 718 diffusion couple was lower than that of S1  according to the results of diffusion couple experiments (Fig. 6), the removal of diffusion  based products from the flank face of S2 was started earlier. This was attributed to the weak  interface strength of SiAlON-TiN substrate and diffusion scale on which the thermal  expansion behavior of the corresponding phases is effective. The thermal expansion  coefficients of TiN and Si 3 N 4  were reported in a paper on the manufacturing and mechanical  and physical characterization of Si 3 N 4 /(SiAlON-TiN) layered composite by Lences et al. [29]  as 9.4  10 -6  K -1  and 3.2  10 -6  K -1 , respectively. The differences in thermal expansion  coefficients of SiAlON/SiAlON-TiN substrate and diffusion scale resulted in the formation of  thermal cracks and therefore, weakening of the interface strength during cutting process. The  amount of adhesion of the workpiece and the size of the craters at the flank face of S2  increased with further cutting (after 24 and 36 min.) as presented in Fig. 8. It is obvious that  the tool is expected to have a sudden failure when the size of these craters approaches a  critical level after this point. On the other hand, the wear at the tool surface showed more  uniform progress with only negligible cratering in case of S1.  

![](images/f7902506a7d5ace1259d89a80ef9362dfbf6b13a7f408836b46f9de7981dcfe2.jpg)  
Fig. 8.  BEI-SEM micrographs of the flank faces of the S1 and S2 tools after 12, 24 and 36 min side  milling tests  

In order to explain the wear mechanism of SiAlON tools during high speed milling of  Inconel 718 alloy, one part of worn surface of S1 milling tool after 12 min. cutting tests  (Section A in Fig. 8) was analyzed in detail with a higher magnification BEI-SEM image (Fig.  9). A severe adhesion of workpiece and the formation of a diffusion scale due to the chemical  interaction of the tool flank face and the work-piece were clearly seen. It is clear that the  cutting edge of the tool was exposed to a very high temperature which is believed to be higher  than 1060°C. Therefore, microcracks on the diffusion scale were observed during interrupted  cutting due to the difference in thermal expansion coefficients of SiAlON substrate and  diffusion scale. The milling operation is a highly intermittent cutting process in which the  tools are subjected to severe alternating thermal and mechanical stresses. Once the stress  exceeds the tensile strength of the tool material, microcracks begin to initiate and propagate at  the interface between the SiAlON tool and the scale. When the number of microcracks  increased and they connected to each other, the bonding between the diffusion scale and  SiAlON became insufficient to protect edge integrity. As a consequence, some part of the  scale detached from the surface and left a crater-like grooves, as observed in Fig. 9. The  repeated adhesion and removal of the workpiece material leads to the loss of tool material  from the worn surface. Moreover, cooling of the tool surface from cutting to room  temperature in every part of the test (every 6 min.) was believed to have a contribution on the  formation of thermal microcracks. It should be highlighted that the wear at the flank face of  the tools which was occurred as partial removal of the diffusion layer in the form of fragments  is believed to be instantaneous since it is dependent on multiple variables such as  compatibility of the diffusion scale with the substrate (SiAlON), mechanical and thermal  properties of the diffusion layer, etc. Therefore, a measurable correlation between machining  time and tool wear was not obtained.  

![](images/91e24863040d9f03ef409c9542f0d9ff4407bbe4a38fc4247b1239d6bc5b9ada.jpg)  
Fig. 9.  BSE SEM image of the Section A in Fig. 7 with a high magnification showing the worn flank  face of S1 milling tool.  

Figures 10 and 11 show the distribution of the elements at the worn flank faces of S1  and S2 tools after 12 min test, respectively. It is obvious that the composition of the diffusion  scale and its compatibility with the SiAlON substrate have a considerable effect on the  formation of thermal cracks. According to the EDX maps given in Figs. 10 and 11, the  diffusion scale on the flank faces of the tool cutting edges is mainly composed of Al and Ti.  The formation of Al-rich worn surface of a SiAlON tool as a result of the diffusion process  during high speed cutting experiments was also reported by Tian et al. [18]. It should be  remembered from the diffusion couple experiments that while Al was clearly detected in the  diffusion zones of S1 and S2 in the form of irregularly shaped grains, limited migration of Ti  towards to the interface was observed as opposite to the actual cutting experiments. Moreover,  oxygen was also detected in the diffusion scale since the cutting experiments were conducted  in air as opposite to the diffusion couple experiments which were performed by SPS under  vacuum conditions. Therefore, it is clear that the oxidation of the diffused Al and Ti elements  took place during the high speed cutting of Inconel 718 alloy. Depending on the presence of  TiN particulates, Ti concentration at the diffusion zone of the worn surface of S2 was  observed higher compared to S1 under atmospheric conditions during machining. It was  expected that the Al-Ti-O based diffusion layer crystalized at the tool faces in the form of  Al 2 TiO 5 , the thermal expansion coefficient of which was reported by Kim et al. [30] as  0.68  10 -6  K -1  between 25-1350°C. Since this value is much smaller than that of SiAlON and  TiN, more thermal cracks was observed in case of S2 due to the increase in thermal expansion  coefficient mismatch between diffusion layer and substrate in comparison to S1. Therefore, it  is obvious from the actual test results that increase in Ti concentration of the diffusion layer  accelerated the wear based on the formation and removal of diffusion layer.

# 4.   Conclusions  

In this study, the wear mechanisms of new SiAlON based ceramic milling tools (an  α/β-SiAlON and its TiN reinforced composite) during high speed milling of Inconel 718 alloy  were investigated in a scientific point of view for the first time in the relevant literature. Two  different SiAlON based ceramics were manufactured in the form of a milling cutter with the  same geometry and tested on milling process of Inconel 718 alloy. The machining tests were  performed at a high machining time (36 min. in total) and speed (585 m/min.) which are about  10 times higher than that of carbide tools can be operated. Moreover, in order to explain  chemical wear mechanism of the tools, representative diffusion couples were manufactured  and the structure and the elemental composition of the diffusion interface were analyzed in  detail by microscopic techniques. Accordingly, following conclusions were drawn:  

1.   According to diffusion couple experiments, the addition of TiN particulates into  SiAlON matrix had a positive effect on the resistance to the formation of diffusion  zones when it was in contact with Inconel 718 alloy at high temperatures  (>1000°C). However, the smoothness of the zone thorough the interface  diminished due to different chemical reactivity of TiN and SiAlON grains with  Inconel 718 alloy.  

2.   It was observed that the interfaces of the SiAlON-Inconel 718 diffusion couples  contain mainly Cr, Nb, Mo and Al. TiN particulates observed at the diffusion  scale without showing any considerable morphology change indicated that the  chemical resistance of TiN particles against to the attack of the atoms from  Inconel side is higher than that of SiAlON grains under vacuum conditions.   3.   SiAlON based solid milling cutters can be utilized successfully for high speed  milling of Inconel 718 alloy without any abrasive wear and fracturing. The  diffusion based wear of the tools was observed as the dominant wear mechanism.  

A severe adhesion of the work-piece to the flank and rake faces of the SiAlON  tools was seen due to the high operating temperature (>1000°C) during actual  cutting tests. After adhesion process, a diffusion scale was formed as a result of  the migration and subsequent oxidation of Al and Ti atoms. Since the milling is a  highly intermittent cutting process in which the tool is subjected to severe  alternating thermal and mechanical stresses, an easy removal of the diffusion  layer was occurred. The increase in Ti concentration of the diffusion scale in case  of S2 was found to be detrimental for the wear progress of the milling tools due to  low thermal expansion coefficient of possibly formed Al 2 TiO 5 .

# Highlights  

   The wear of SiAlON tools on high speed milling of Inconel 718 were investigated.     A severe adhesion of Inconel 718 on the tool faces was observed during machining.      Diffusion was the main wear mechanism of the SiAlON tools at high cutting speeds.     The diffusion layer formed on the cutting edge contains Al, Ti and O elements.     The increase in Ti concentration in diffusion scale of the tools accelerated wear.
