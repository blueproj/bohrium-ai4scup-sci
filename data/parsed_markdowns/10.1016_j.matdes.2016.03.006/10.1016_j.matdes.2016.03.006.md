Article history: Received 11 November 2015 Received in revised form 29 February 2016 Accepted 2 March 2016 Available online 4 March 2016  

As precipitation hardening nickel-based super-alloy, the mechanical properties of Inconel 718 (IN718) formed by high deposition-rate LMD (HDR-LMD) are far lower than the relevant speci ﬁ cations, making it unable to be used in practical industry applications. With the purpose of improving its material performance, the current study has been carried out. The microstructure characteristics of the as-deposited IN718 have been analysed, and the mechanical properties of it have been tested. Based on which, reasons that result in its poor mechanical proper- ties have been identi ﬁ ed. Furthermore, aiming to solve these problems the methods in order to improve material performance have been studies. It is found: the poor mechanical properties of the as-deposited IN718 could be signi ﬁ cantly improved by HIPing (Hot Isostatic Pressing) and proper heat treatments. In addition, the mechanical properties of the heat-treated material are superior to AMS speci ﬁ cations for IN718 fabricated by conventional manufacturing processes. It concluded: HIP can dramatically reduce the porosity of the as-deposited material; the columnar grains can be transformed to equiaxed grains by homogenization, eliminating material anisotropy; Laves phase could be dissolved by solution treatment, transforming to needle-like  δ  phase; strengthening phases will be precipitated by double aging, therefore strengthening/hardening the material.

# 1. Introductions  

Laser metal deposition (LMD) is a laser cladding based, free form laser additive manufacturing (LAM) technology, which could be used to fabricate functional, three-dimensional components. During LMD, a melt pool on the surface of the substrate or a previous layer is generated by high power laser radiation. Simultaneously, metal powder is injected into the melt pool by a powder feeding nozzle and melted completely. By moving the working table and/or the laser head, a metallurgical fused bonding is formed. Because of the various advantages of LMD there has been a growing interest in its development in recent years in  ﬁ elds such as process development/optimization  [1 – 3] , modelling/ numerical analysis  [4 – 6] , microstructure and mechanical properties [7 – 10]  and so on.  

In this  ﬁ eld, C. Zhong, et al from Fraunhofer ILT have done quite some basic researches with the funding of the European Commission in the AMAZE Project (Additive Manufacturing Aiming towards Zero Waste & Ef ﬁ cient Production of High-Tech Metal Products)  [20 – 23] . Initially, they have developed a novel method using mixed process parameters for process window development for HDR-LMD of IN718  [20] . After that, as it is found the material defects, especially porosity, in HDR- LMD of IN718 is more serious than that of conventional LMD of IN718, the study on porosity reduction has been carried out  [21] . Then, they have studied the effects of main process parameters on deposition properties  [22] . All these works are quite signi ﬁ cant for the develop- ment of HDR-LMD of IN718. Based on the developed process, C. Zhong et al. have done research on microstructures and tensile properties of IN718 formed by HDR-LMD. They have investigated the microstructures and tensile properties of the as-deposited and heat-treated material,  

Inconel 718 (IN718), as a thermal resistant nickel-based super-alloy has high strengths and high creep-rupture resistance at elevated temperatures to about 700 °C, and it is widely used in the  ﬁ eld of aircraft industries  [11,12] . Moreover, it is also commonly used as critical rotating parts, airfoils, supporting structures, and pressure vessels, and it is one of the most widely used nickel-based superalloy in the aircraft

Firstly, the relatively high porosity shown in the micrographs of the as-deposited material has not been handled, the porosity should be reduced, otherwise, it would affect material mechanical properties. Secondly, the effects of different heat treatments as well as the microstructure evaluations, which indicates the material strengthening mechanisms, are not clear. Thirdly, only tensile test results have been presented. In order to improve this previous work, namely to solve these mentioned problems and performing as a supplement, trying to push an advance in this area the current study has been carried out.  

Based on this idea, the material defects and microstructure proper- ties of the as-deposited material, the effects of HIP (Hot Isostatic Pressing) on porosity, the methods for microstructure modi ﬁ cations, the microstructure evolutions during heat treatments and the material mechanical properties have been studied and presented in this current paper.  

# 2. Experimental  

# 2.1. Materials  

Inconel 718 (IN718) gas atomized (GA) powder has been used. The chemical composition speci ﬁ cation (AMS 5596B) for IN718 and the main elements of the used powder, measured via ICP (inductively coupled plasma) method, are listed in  Table 1 .  

As can be seen from  Table 1  that the used powder meets the AMS requirements. In addition, this alloy is rich in Ni, Cr and Fe elements, and moreover, it contains a relative large fraction of Nb element.  

The nominal particle size of the used powder is 45  μ m-90  μ m. Metallographic prepared cross-sections and SEM (scanning electron microscope) micrograph are shown in  Fig. 1 .  

As can be seen from  Fig. 1 : a large fraction of the powder particles feature satellites, which are formed during atomization of the powder, as smaller molten particles are solidi ﬁ ed faster and adhered to still semi ﬂ uid, larger particles; a few particles with enclosed pores which are also formed during the manufacturing process, can be located (hollow particles); a certain amount of irregular shaped particles can be found.  

# 2.2. Experimental setup and procedure  

The experimental setup consists of a collimator, a standard optic, a zoom optic and a coaxial powder nozzle. The movement of the lenses in the zoom optic and the tool axis are controlled by the NC-control of a 4-axes tool machine. A 12 kW high power diode laser source is linked via a glass  ﬁ bre to the optic. The powder is fed by the use of helium (4 nl/min) with a powder feeder. In order to protect melt pool from oxidation the process is local shielded with argon (12 nl/min). Other process parameters are: laser power P L =2950 W; scanning speed v=1500 mm/min; Powder mass  ﬂ ow rate   m  ¼  2 : 1 kg = h; laser spot diameter d L =4 mm.  

Applying the above process parameters, 3D structures for micro- structure analysis, hardness test and tensile test specimen producing have been deposited, as shown in  Fig. 2 .  

As shown in  Fig. 2 (b), these 3D pyramids are deposited with single direction scanning strategy. The metallographic analysis regarding material defects, especially porosity, as well as microstructures such as grain orientations, segregations and phase precipitations of the as- deposited material has been carried out using optical microscopes, SEM and EDX (Energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy). Aiming the identi ﬁ ed problems based on this analysis, HIP and heat treatments have been performed on the as-deposited material to improve its material performance. In order to verify these theoretical analysis, mechanical property tests, including hardness test and tensile strength test, have been carried out, and relevant results have been evaluated.  

# 3. Metallographic analysis of as-deposited material  

# 3.1. High porosity  

After grinding and polishing, the optical micrograph of the cross- sectioned as-deposited blocks showing material defects is shown in Fig. 3 .  

It can be clearly observed from  Fig. 3  that the as-deposited 3D structure has high porosity. The porosity of as-deposited material, measured by the integrated image processing software of the optical microscope, is 0.41%. Porosity as the main material defects of LMD is detrimental to material mechanical properties. In order to improve material performance, measurement should be taken to reduce it.  

# 3.2. Anisotropy  

In order to observe the microstructures, deposited material has been etched, and the optical micrograph showing the columnar grains of the as-deposited material is presented in  Fig. 4 .  

Mainly 2 kinds of regularly orientated columnar grains, namely co- lumnar grains oriented along deposition direction and columnar grains growing from track edges to track top, can be seen in  Fig. 4 . This indicates that there were 2 dominating solidi ﬁ cation directions which are: along deposition direction and pointing from track edges to track top. Therefore, the directions along which the heat generated in the process were dissipated could be derived because the heat  ﬂ ow directions should be exactly opposite to the solidi ﬁ cation directions. The orientation of columnar grains (grain growing) is dependence on the heat dissipation directions. Since along the substrate and previous deposited layers are the main heat dissipation directions, the 2 dominating solidi ﬁ cation directions were observed in  Fig. 4 . In addition to this, there are some columnar grains grow through several layers, which shows that with the deposition of new layers, these grains have furtherly grown.  

Because of these regularly orientated grains, it is expected that the material has strong anisotropy. Anisotropy of the material is in most cases not desired since it would result in the anisotropic material properties. Therefore, in order to get isotropic material, the microstruc- ture needs to be modi ﬁ ed.

In order to analyse more detailed the microstructures, the as- deposited material has been observed using SEM, and the correspond- ing SEM micrograph is shown in  Fig. 5 .  

It can been seen from  Fig. 5  that there are quite some irregular shaped precipitations in the inter-dendrite area of the as-deposited

According to previous works, the solidi ﬁ cation process of IN718 alloy is often associated with segregation, resulting in the formation of Mo- and Nb-rich brittle intermetallic compound Laves phase that is located at the inter-dendritic regions, represented as (Ni,Cr,Fe)2(Nb,Mo,Ti) [15,23,24,25] , therefore, we suppose that this precipitated phase is Laves phase. It would undermine the material strength, making it more brittle and more easily failed under load.  

On the one hand, IN718 is a precipitation hardening material by  γ ' (Ni 3 Nb) phase and  γ '' (Ni 3 (Al,Ti)) in the  γ  matrix after a full heat treat- ment  [15,29 – 32] . Since both of these phases are too small in size and they cannot even be observed with SEM, tensile test and hardness test were used to indirectly examine the precipitation of these strengthen- ing phases. The results show that: the 0.2% yield strength of the as- deposited material is approx. 525 MPa, which is far less than AMS (Aerospace Material Speci ﬁ cation) 5662 for wrought IN718, which is 1034 MPa, and even much lower than the value of AMS5383 for casting IN718, which is 758 MPa; Vickers hardness of the as-deposited material is approx. 300 HV, and this is as also much lower than the industry re- quirement which is around 350 HV (331HB). On the other hand, the cooling rate of LMD is quite high, therefore there would be not enough time for the precipitation of  γ ' phase and  γ '' phase. This has also been found in some of the previous works on conventional LMD  [15,17] . Hence, it can be derived that there is no or at least not enough precipi- tations of strengthen phases in the as-deposited material.  

According to the above discussions, in order to improve material performance, the following two works need to be done to modify the microstructure of the as-deposited material: dissolve the detrimental phase (Laves phase) and precipitate strengthening phases ( γ ' phase and  γ '' phase).  

# 3.4. Problems of the as-deposited material  

Through metallographic analysis regarding material defects as well as microstructures, the following problems have been identi ﬁ ed, which would affect material performance, resulting in its poor mechan- ical properties.  

Firstly, the as-deposited material has high porosity, as the main ma- terial defects, it undermines the material mechanical properties, so measurement should be taken to reduce it. Secondly, the anisotropy microstructure has been observed which would cause the anisotropic material properties. In order to get homogeneous material, the micro- structure needs to be modi ﬁ ed. Thirdly, in addition to a strong segrega- tion (Nb and Mo elements) in the inner-dendrite area, there is a large fraction of detrimental phase (Laves phase) precipitation, and it would make the material more brittle and easily failed. Therefore, the as- deposited material should be heat treated with the purpose to dissolve this detrimental phase. Finally, the as-deposited material has not been strengthened since there is no or at least not enough strengthening phases. Thus, heat treatment that could realize the precipitation of strengthening phases should be applied to the as-deposited material.

# 4. Improvement of material performance  

4.1. Reduction of porosity  

HIP is the abbreviation for Hot Isostatic Pressing, which is a manufacturing process that can be used to reduce the porosity of metals, and therefore increase the material density in order to improve the mechanical properties and workability of the material. As can be seen in  Section 3.1 , the as-deposited material has a relative high porosity, hence HIP was used to treat the material with the purpose of reducing the porosity. The HIPed samples were treated by argon with a pressure of 1000 bar and a temperature of 1150 °C for 4 h. The optical micrograph showing the cross-sectioned HIPed blocks is shown in  Fig. 6  

Nearly no pores could be observed in  Fig. 6 . In compare with  Fig. 3 , it could be clearly seen that pores in the as-deposited material has been pressed out by HIP, the porosity of the as-material has signi ﬁ cantly been reduced. Porosity is detrimental for mechanical properties, therefore we would expect that the elimination of porosity is bene ﬁ cial for improving the mechanical properties.  

# 4.2. Homogenization of the material  

Aiming to solve the second problem mentioned in  Section 3.2 , namely to eliminate the anisotropy of the material, the samples have been homogenization heat treated. Referring to AMS (Aerospace Material Speci ﬁ cation) 5583D, which states the heat treatment method for casted IN718, the homogenization treatment was carried out as follows:  ﬁ rstly heat the material to 1093 ∓ 14 °C; then hold for 2 h; at last cool it down in air. The optical micrograph showing the recrystal- lized grains of the homogenized material is shown in  Fig. 7 .  

Unlike the microstructure of as-deposited material that is shown in Fig. 4 , equiaxed grains can be observed in  Fig. 7 . The results showed that the columnar grains have been transformed to equiaxed grains, which should be due to the recrystallization happened during

# 4.3. Solution of detrimental phase  

In order to dissolve the Laves phase, solution heat treatment by referring AMS5583D has been performed. Samples have been  ﬁ rstly heated to 954 °C – 982 °C, hold for about 1 h, after that cool down in the air. SEM micrograph recording the microstructure of the material after solution heat treatment is presented in  Fig. 8 .  

As shown in  Fig. 8 , the irregular shaped Laves phase that observed in the inner-dendrite area of the as-deposited material ( Fig. 4 ) could not been found anymore in the solution treated material. It means that the Laves phase has been dissolve by solution heat treatment. Since Laves phase is detrimental for mechanical properties, the elimination of it is bene ﬁ cial for improving material performance. In addition to this, it can be also seen in  Fig. 8  that there is a new precipitation, a needle-like phase that distributed in the grain boundaries. We believe that this needle-like precipitates are  δ  phase. On the one hand,  δ phase, which has a composition of Ni 3 Nb and an orthorhombic (D0 a ) crystal structure, precipitates in between 700 °C and 1000 °C  [26] . The temperature (954 °C – 982 °C) of solution heat treatment is just the temperature for  δ  phase precipitation. On the other hand,  δ  phase is mostly found as plates growing on the (111) planes or found on the grain boundaries  [18,27,28] , the morphology and locations of these new precipitates satis ﬁ es this. Therefore, this new precipitated needle- like phase should be  δ  phase. The solution heat treatment has dissolved the detrimental Laves phase, and transforming it to needle-like  δ  phase. Moreover, small particles that uniformly distributed in the material can be observed in  Fig. 8 . These new precipitations are NbC particles. Because of the size, crystal structure and location of it, it has no obvious effects on mechanical properties.

4.4. Precipitation of strengthening phases  

γ ' phase and  γ ″  phase are the strengthening phases of IN718, which precipitate between 600 °C and 900 °C, and are uniformly distributed small particles, forming a basis for the precipitation hardening of the alloy  [29 – 32] . They have distinctly different morphologies which help in their identi ﬁ cation;  γ ″   phases are round shaped and about 20 nm in size, whereas  γ ' precipitates are disc like with an aspect ratio of 5 – 6 [18,27,28] . In order to strengthen the material, double aging heat treatment referring AMS5583D for cast IN718 with the precipitation temperatures of  γ ' phase and  γ ″  phase has been carried out. The  ﬁ rst aging: heat the material to 718 ∓ 8 °C, holding for 8 h, and then cool it down to 621 ∓ 8 °C in furnace. The second aging: after  ﬁ rst aging, hold the sample for 10 h under 621 ∓ 8 °C, and then cool it down in air to room temperature. SEM micrograph showing the microstructure of the material after double aging heat treatment is illustrated in  Fig. 9 .  

Similar microstructure as solution treated material ( Fig. 8 ) has been observed in  Fig. 9 . The strengthening phases should be precipitated by double aging heat treatment, but they could not be seen in SEM micro- graph. It is believed that this is because the size of the  γ ′  phase and  γ ″ phase are too small. In order to verify this idea, hardness test has been carried out. The Vickers hardness of the solution treated material is around 209 HV, and it is reasonable since all the precipitations that could contribute for hardening the material should have been dissolved in the  γ  matrix. The Vickers hardness of the material after double aging is approx. 467 HV, which is nearly 125% higher than that of solution treated material though the SEM micrographs of them are similar. Therefore, it is believed that strengthening phases  γ ′  and  γ ″  have been precipitated.

# 5. Mechanical properties  

Hardness test for as-deposited material, HIPed material and heat- treated material have been performed, and the relevant results are illustrated in  Fig. 10 .  

As can be seen from  Fig. 10  the hardness values from as-deposited material to heat-treated material is signi ﬁ cantly increased by approx. 53%, and the hardness of HIPed material lies in between, around 27% higher than that of as-deposited material. As mentioned in  Section 4.4 , the hardness of the solution treated material is about 209 HV. Compared to this value, it is believed that the relative high hardness of the as- deposited material is because of the existence Laves phase and MC phase. The increase of the hardness from as-deposited material to HIPed material is supposed mainly as the results of that the HIPed material has a higher material density. The highest hardness of the heat-treated material should attribute mainly to the strengthening phases. It should be noticing that, the hardness of the heat-treated IN718 formed by HDR-LMD, which is approx. 467 HV, is much higher than the industry requirement for the hardness of IN718 which is around 350 HV.

# 6. Summary and conclusions  

Trying to push an advance in the development of HDR-LMD of IN718, the current study has been carried out. The aim of the work is to improve the material performance of IN718 formed by HDR-LMD. The following works have been done and presented in this current paper. Initially, metallographic properties regarding material defects, especially porosity, as well as microstructures such as grain orienta- tions, elements segregations and phase precipitations of the as- deposited material have been analysed using optical microscopes, SEM (scanning electron microscope) and EDX (Energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy). Based on the results, the problems that would affect material performance (mechanical properties) have been identi ﬁ ed. After that, aiming to solve the identi ﬁ ed problems, the effects of HIP (Hot Isostatic Pressing) on porosity reduction, and heat treatments on microstructure modi ﬁ cation have been studied. In order to improve mechanical properties, the as-deposited material was HIPed and heat treated, and the microstructure evolutions have been investigated. Finally, in order to verify all the theoretical analysis and discussions, the mechanical properties, including hardness and tensile properties, of both as-deposited material and heat-treated material were tested, and relevant results have been evaluated. Moreover, the mechanical property test results were compared with AMS (Aerospace Material Speci ﬁ cation) speci ﬁ cations for IN718 fabricated by conventional manufacturing processes (casting and forging).  

According to the achieved results, the following conclusions regarding HDR-LMD of IN718 have been drawn from our work: HIP can signi ﬁ cantly reduce the porosity of the as-deposited IN718, improv- ing material density; the columnar grains of the as-deposited material are regularly oriented, which would results in the material anisotropy, but this could be eliminated by homogenization heat treatment, with which the columnar grains could be transformed to equiaxed grains; the precipitated Laves phase, which has been found mainly at inner-
