# Investigation on remelting solution heat treatment for nickel-based single crystal superalloys  

Yanbin Zhang, Lin Liu  ⁎ , Taiwen Huang, Yafeng Li, Ziqi Jie, Jun Zhang, Wenchao Yang, Hengzhi Fu

To increase the peak temperature during solution heat treatment of a single crystal superalloy over the solidus, a remelting solution heat treatment is investigated. It contains two parts: a) homogenizing at mushy zone temper- atures, and ignoring partial incipient melting; b) eliminating incipient melting structure by secondary solution heat treatment. After remelting solution heat treatment the re ﬁ ning  γ ′  particles uniformly distributed and the re- sidual segregations are less than those after traditional solution heat treatment. Therefore, the rupture life at 1100 °C/150 MPa is improved from 105.3 h after traditional solution heat treatment to 141.1 h.

During the development of nickel based single crystal (SC) superal- loys the content of refractory elements are increasingly added to pursue high mechanical properties. However, due to the low diffusivities of these refractory elements  [1,2] , homogenizing them during solution heat treatment (SHT) is dif ﬁ cult. After 3rd generation SC superalloys, the hardest task of SHT changed from eliminating eutectics to reducing residual segregations which is detrimental  [3 – 9] . To reduce the residual segregations, the solution temperature was elevated. However, undesir- able incipient melting occurred at high solution temperature.  

The major objective of this study was to develop the remelting SHT. A secondary SHT was performed to eliminate the incipient melting structures. Furthermore, to take the advantages of remelting SHT, the dwelling of peak temperature was prolonged to 15 h to reduce the re- sidual segregation. The structures of samples after remelting SHT and secondary SHT were compared with that after standard SHT through the use of scanning electron microscopy (SEM). In addition, their resid- ual segregations were contrasted by the simulation of DICTRA software with the TCNI7 database. Finally, their creep properties were tested. It should be note that the remelting SHT was applicable to other superalloys.  

In order to resolve this contradiction, some investigations were per- formed. Fuchs et al.  [7]  proposed a step wise SHT to increase the solution temperature from 1315 °C to 1365 °C step by step. In this case, the incip- ient melt point increased after each step, and the incipient melting was avoided. After that, some considerable researches  [3,5,10]  have been re- ported to shorten the ramp time before the peak temperature without incipient melting. Furthermore, Rettig et al.  [2]  used an optimization al- gorithm to calculate the incipient melt point each time, and trace it to obtain the fastest possible SHT. These investigations increased the solu- tion temperature and reduced the solution time to some extent. Howev- er, the solution temperature was limited by the solidus after all.

As stated in the introduction section, Hedge and coworkers  [11]  pro- posed the remelting SHT. They found the incipient melting structure could not be eliminated by a series of step wise SHTs of which peak tem- perature is 1310 °C. Without suf ﬁ cient homogenization, another incipi- ent melting occurred when they increased the peak temperature to 1320 °C. In the present investigation, the incipient melting structures were eliminated by HT3 of which the peak temperature is 1320 °C. It is indicated that the incipient melting structures were similar to the cast structures which could be eliminated by proper SHT. The coarse γ ′  in incipient melting structures is hard to dissolve. To dissolve them, a relatively high temperature like that in HT3 is necessary in SHT.  

The cast segregation and residual segregations after HT1 and HT2 are shown in  Table 3 . It could been seen that after HT1 the segregation was reduced. The residual segregation ratios of Al, Ta and W were close to one. However, the residual segregation ratio of Re remained as high as 2.15. This means the peak temperature of HT1 (1330 °C) is insuf ﬁ cient for the homogenization of Re. After HT2, the residual segregation of Re was reduced farther, due to the higher peak temperature of HT2 (1360 °C). This results further support the investigation of Fuchs  [7] that the segregation of Re would not decrease until the solution temper- ature reached 1360 °C.  

The signi ﬁ cantly high solution temperature of HT2 is demonstrated to be the reason for the less residual segregation after HT2 than that after HT1, as is shown in  Table 3 . As stated in the introduction section, some researches enhanced the solution temperature or shorten the time before reaching the peak temperature. However, the solution tem- perature is still lower than incipient melting temperature, and further lower than solidus. The remelting SHT ignored the incipient melting. Its solution temperature exceeded the solidus. This resolve the con ﬂ ict between solution temperature and incipient melting. Samples after HT1 and HT2 followed with HT3 were processed age- ing treatment in  Table 2 . The subsequent creep strain tests were per- formed at 1100 °C and 150 MPa. As is shown in  Fig. 4 , the rupture life of sample after HT1 was 105.3 h, and that after HT2 and HT3 was 141.1 h.  

Lower residual segregations after HT2 are suggested to be the main reason for the longer rupture life. Residual segregation in ﬂ uences the microstructure of  γ ′  precipitate after ageing treatment and the structure stability  [12,13]  during thermal exposure. The refractory elements seg- regating in dendrite core, even in small amount, strongly enhanced the driving force of TCP phases  [14] . The TCP phases were highly undesir- able in superalloys for the reduction of solution strengthening effect in the matrix and their action as crack initiation. Though none TCP phase was found in current work, the in ﬂ uence of residual segregations on TCP phases still need to be noted during longer thermal exposure or in other alloys. It has been reported that pores, in ﬂ uenced by SHT  [17] , strongly de- teriorate the creep life  [18] .The effects of different types of heat treat- ments on porosity were investigated in the current work. SEM micrographs, as is shown in  Fig. 3 , were used to measure the area frac- tion of pores. The area fraction of pores is regarded as the volume frac- tion of pores. The results showed that the porosities after HT1 and HT2 were 0.41% and 0.47%, respectively. More porosity after HT2 is due to the molar volume variation during incipient melting. After HT2 and sub- sequent HT3, the porosity reduced to 0.34%. The reduction of porosity is due to the resolving of incipient melting structures, as is shown in  Fig. 3 (d). It should be note that the porosity after HT3 is less than that after HT1. The reason could be explained by the model developed by Bokstein et al.  [17] . According to the model, the homogenization pores were produced due to the imbalanced cross diffusion between the den- dritic cores and interdendritic areas during SHT, and the homogeniza- tion porosity increased  ﬁ rst and then decreased after the fast diffusion elements reached their equilibria. In the current work, the fast diffusion elements segregated in interdendritic areas, like Al and Ta, reached their equilibria before HT1  ﬁ nish. Therefore, the segregation ratios of Al and Ta after HT1 almost equaled to that after HT3, see  Table 3 . However, the segregation ratios of refractory elements segregated in dendritic core, like Re and W, after HT3 were remarkably less than that after HT1. In this case, the homogenization porosity caused by the imbal- anced cross diffusion decreased due to the compensation through diffu- sion of refractory elements. Without prolonging the solution time, the remelting SHT enhanced the solution temperature, reduced the residual segregation and prolonged the high temperature rupture life. The following conclusions could be drawn:  

1. The eutectics and coarse  γ ′  were dissolved after HT1 with the peak temperature of 1330 °C. Incipient melting occurred during HT2 with the peak temperature of 1360 °C. The incipient melting struc- tures were similar to the cast structures. They could be eliminated by proper SHT like HT3.

 2. After HT1, the residual segregation ratios of Al, Ta and W were close to 1. However, the residual segregation ratio of Re remained as high as 2.15. The segregation ratio of Re reduced to 2.01 after HT2 due to the 30 °C higher solution temperature.

 3. The porosity after HT1 was 0.41%. After HT2, the porosity raised to 0.47% due to the incipient melting. After subsequent HT3, the poros- ity reduced to 0.34% due to the vanishing of incipient melting
