# A R T I C L E I N F O  

# A B S T R A C T  

MCrAlY (M = Ni, Co) coatings are commonly used on gas-turbine components as oxidation resistant overlay coatings and bondcoats for thermal barrier systems. The present work focuses on the e ﬀ ect of the aluminizing process on the CoNiCrAlY coating microstructure. In the as-received condition the outer part of the coating consisted mostly of  β -(Ni,Co)Al with interspersed precipitates of Cr-rich carbide and Cr-rich boride precipitates. Formation of  σ -CoCr was observed at the interface between the  β -layer and the inner initial CoNiCrAlY mi- crostructure. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) combined with energy and wavelength-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX/WDX) was employed to characterize the aluminized CoNiCrAlY coating. Phases were then identi ﬁ ed by electron backscatter di ﬀ raction (EBSD). Detailed microstructural studies of the coating were cor- roborated with the help of coupled thermodynamic-kinetic calculations to model the aluminizing process. The calculations were performed with the in-house developed code employing the commercially available thermo- dynamic and kinetic databases (ThermoCalc). The mechanisms of the observed microstructural changes were elucidated with the help of the modelling results.

# 1. Introduction  

To protect base materials of turbine components used in land based gas turbines against high temperature oxidation and corrosion [ 1 – 7 ] MCrAlY type coatings are commonly applied as overlay coatings and bond coats underneath ceramic thermal barrier coatings (TBC). The Al content typically of 8 – 12 wt. % provides a suitable reservoir for the formation of protective alumina based surface scales (commonly termed as thermally grown oxide, TGO) during high temperature ex- posure. Typical microstructure of MCrAlY coatings consists of  γ -Ni and β -NiAl phases. However, depending on the coating composition the microstructure of the coatings may be more complex and contain sub- stantial amounts of ≤  σ -(Cr, Co),  γ ′ -Ni 3 Al and/or  α -Cr phases especially at T 1000 °C [ 8 – 10 ]. Aluminizing is gaining increased attention as a cost- e ective way to coat low cost materials to compensate for the increased rate of oxidation of these materials. The aluminide coating is aimed to provide the oxidation resistance required at high temperatures without resorting to more expensive materials. Chemical vapour deposition (CVD) or slurry aluminizing [ 11 – 13 ] are often used on the MCrAlY coated components in order to further improve the oxidation resistance  

Given the wide range of alloys (or chemical compositions) employed in high temperature applications, there is a need to understand the microstructural evolution of di ﬀ usion coatings on these alloys as a function of process parameters (time, temperature, Al- ﬂ ux) and sub- strate chemistry. Previous numerical simulations of coating deposition processes during aliminizing worth mentioning are by Wierzba et al. [ 17 ] and Forslund [ 18 ]. The former work predicted the phase evolution in the NiAl coating considering a binary (Ni, Al) and a ternary (Ni, Cr, Al) base alloy system but no discussion was provided for the phase evolution in the interdi ﬀ usion zone (IDZ) or the role of other alloying elements. The more recent work by Forslund et al. employed the homogenization model in DICTRA to predict the phase evolution in the coating and in the IDZ for two systems previously investigated in the literature. An acceptable agreement was achieved between the mea- sured and predicted concentration pro ﬁ les.  

The aim of the presented work is to show the applicability of a previously developed modelling procedure [ 19 ] to predict the phase formations and compositional evolutions occurring during aluminizing. In previously published works by the authors [ 19 – 21 ], the focus was on degradation of oxidation resistant coatings during high temperature exposures. The present work demonstrates the applicability of the model to predict phase evolution during aluminizing of a pre-coated (thermally sprayed CoNiCrAlY-coating) Ni-base superalloy IN 792 and subsequent heat treatment taking all the alloying elements and relevant phases into account. Given the good agreement between experimental observations and modelling results, it enhances the con ﬁ dence in the employed thermodynamic and kinetic data. Furthermore, considering that aluminising of an already coated (MCrAlY) was modelled which is a complex coating system, it shows that the approach hold promise in being employed as a general tool to optimize di ﬀ usion coating pro- cesses for other Ni-base alloys.  

# 2. Experimental procedure  

Cylindrical specimens of a cast Ni-base alloy IN 792CC of 6.8 mm in diameter and 20 mm in length were used as substrates. The commercial CoNiCrAlY bondcoat of approximately 150  μ m in thickness was applied to the cylindrical specimens by low-pressure plasma spraying (LPPS). Subsequently, the surface of the Co-base coating was aluminized (using a proprietary process). The aluminising process comprised of 2 h alu- minising at 1050 °C followed by heat treatment at 850 °C for 2 h to stabilise the  γ - γ ’  microstructure of the base alloy. Finally, a 300- μ m- thick yttria stabilized zirconia TBC (YSZ-TBC) was deposited, using a conventional electron beam physical vapour deposition (EB-PVD). For the EB-PVD process the surfaces of the bondcoats were additionally smoothened prior to the TBC deposition [ 4 ]. The nominal composition of the substrate and the CoNiCrAlY material can be found in  Table 1 . Polished metallographic cross-section was analysed using scanning electron microscopy (SEM; Zeiss Merlin and Supra 50VP) equipped with energy and wave length dispersive energy detectors (EDX, WDX; X- Max, Oxford Instruments). Also, electron backscatter di ﬀ raction (EBSD; Zeiss Merlin) was performed.  

# 3. Modelling procedure  

# 3.1. Homogenization model  

The coupled thermodynamic-kinetic approach to modelling the si- multaneously occurring oxidation, di ﬀ usion and dissolution processes occurring in the substrate-coating system investigated here was based on the homogenisation model presented by Larsson et al. [ 22 ]. The homogenisation model was implemented in a FORTRAN program using the thermodynamic calculation interface of ThermoCalc [ 23 ]. The modelling procedure was modi ﬁ ed further to allow the calculations to be run on parallel computing cores [ 20 ]. The in-house developed modelling procedure has been described in detail elsewhere [ 19 ]. However the governing  ﬂ ux equation will be mentioned here.

# 3.2. Numerical parameters  

A geometric mesh was generated with a  ﬁ ner mesh at the alloy surface (left boundary/aluminized surface) and gradually increasing cell size towards the specimen center (right boundary). The mesh was adapted at each time step since the domain would grow due to addition of Al to the base alloy. The mesh adaption procedure was explained in an earlier work [ 19 ]. An inward Al  ﬂ ux was employed as a boundary condition at the alloy surface to simulate the aluminizing process. Since the exact value of the Al  ﬂ ux at the alloy surface is not known, this was derived from the literature [ 29 ]. Levine et al. evaluated the in ﬂ uence of pack activators, compositions, times and temperatures on the thickness and microstructure of aluminide coatings formed on the Ni base su- peralloy IN-100. They estimated the Al deposition rate from the mea- sured weight gains and assuming parabolic weight change kinetics. In the present work, a value for the Al-surface  ﬂ ux corresponding to a chloride activator during aluminizing at 1050 °C was employed. Al- though the actual value during aluminizing of the alloy in the present work might be di ﬀ erent from the assumed value, the modelling pro- cedure would help understand the mechanisms of phase formations in the aluminide coating. The assumption of symmetry at the center of the sample provided the second spatial boundary condition of zero  ﬂ ux for all elements. In summary, on one side of the 1D domain, a constant pre- calculated inward  ﬂ ux of Al was prescribed while the system was closed for all other elements. On the other side of the domain the system was closed for all elements. The nominal compositions of the base alloy in the as-received state (1) was used as the initial composition at time t = 0. The model provided average element concentrations and phase fractions as a function of time and distance.

# 4. Results and discussion  

# 4.1. Microstructure in as-coated condition  

Fig. 1  shows the bondcoat microstructure of the studied TBC system in the as-received condition. The chemical compositions of the di ﬀ erent phases analysed by EDX are given in  Table 2 . As expected the outer aluminide layer consists of a  β -(Ni,Co)Al layer. The inner part of the bondcoat, corresponding to the non-aluminized CoNiCrAlY-coating, consists of the  γ - and  β -phase. However, the light precipitates observed at the interface between the aluminide layer and the CoNiCrAlY-coating were found to be  σ -(Cr, Co) phase (point 3 in  Table 2 ). The di ﬀ er- entiation between  γ  and  σ  phases in the SEM back scattered electron images appeared to be di ﬃ cult because of the similarity in average atomic numbers of both phases. However, quantitative EDX measure- ments clearly con ﬁ rmed the existence of a binary  β -(Ni,Co)Al +  σ -(Cr, Co) layer between the aluminide layer and the inner CoNiCrAlY-coating ( Table 2 ).  

Additionally, high amounts of Cr-rich precipitates were identi ﬁ ed within the outer aluminide layer ( Fig. 2 ). The compositions of these precipitates derived by EDX point analysis (with typical concentrations of the metallic constituents 22.3Al-50.4Cr-16.9Co-10.4Ni in at.%) cannot be correlated with phase compositions known from existing Ni- Cr-Al or Co-Cr-Al phase diagrams. The precipitates may consist of  α -Cr with high solubility for Al. Despite meticulous e ﬀ orts to identify the single phase composition, interference of the matrix during EDS mea- surements (in this case  β -phase) cannot be completely excluded. However, thermodynamic equilibrium calculations with TCNI8 for a Ni- 10Cr-32Al-20Co (wt.%) system at 1050 °C did con ﬁ rm a high solubility for Al in  α -Cr (about 15 wt.% or 26 at.%).

Interestingly, Cr-rich carbide precipitates were also found within the  β -layer. Both phases were present only in the aluminized layer; they were not found in the original CoNiCrAlY layer. Carbides of the type M 23 C 6  were identi ﬁ ed as one of the Cr-rich precipitates whereas the intermetallic phases were found to correspond to  α -Cr.  

# 4.2. Microstructural evolution during aluminising  

To understand the mechanisms of the observed microstructural evolution during aluminising, the modelling approach discussed in the section Modelling procedure was employed. The calculated phase dis- tribution after the 2 h aluminising at 1050 °C and 2 h heat treatment at 850 °C is shown in  Fig. 4 . A comparison with the EBSD phase map in Fig. 3  indicates that the model was able to predict the observed phase distribution in the following order moving from the surface of the newly formed NiAl coating towards the existing MCrAlY overlay coating:  

•   Formation of  β -NiAl on the MCrAlY overlay coating

 •   Precipitation of  α -Cr close to the coating surface of the NiAl coating

 •   Precipitation of Cr-rich carbides (M 23 C 6 )

 •   Formation of  σ -phase at the interface between the NiAl-MCrAlY coatings  

The width of the NiAl-coating was slightly underestimated by the model by about 5  μ m which can be attributed to process parameters such as heating and cooling rates, surface variations in the chemistry of the aluminizing medium. Furthermore, the model assumes one di- mensional di ﬀ usion and homogenity of the inward Al- ﬂ ux which could lead to the observed deviation from observed coating thickness. However, the observed deviation is deemed acceptable, given the as- sumptions of the model and the in ﬂ uence of the aforementioned process parameters.

# 4.3. Mechanisms of phase formation  

The precipitation of the observed phases can be explained by the temporal and spatial evolution of the chemical activities of the alloying elements C, Cr and Al. The di ﬀ usion velocities of these elements is relatively higher than the slow di ﬀ using elements such as Ta, W and Mo [ 35 – 37 ]. Hence, these alloying elements are expected to govern the kinetics of phase evolution at the MCrAlY coating surface which is exposed to the aluminising environment.  

The temporal evolution of the phase distribution on the surface of the existing MCrAlY coating (corresponds to the  ﬁ rst node in the computational mesh) is shown in  Fig. 5 . The Al  ﬂ ux on the surface leads to the dissolution of the  γ -phase and the formation of  β -NiAl phase. The chemical potential gradient of C from the substrate to the MCrAlY coating ( Fig. 6 ) at t = 0 drives C from the substrate to the coating. The signi ﬁ cantly higher di ﬀ usional velocity of C (almost three orders of magnitude higher than Cr and Al) means that C di ﬀ uses fast towards the aluminising surface. This combined with the high Cr-content in the bond coat leads to the precipitation of Cr-rich carbides in the newly formed  β -NiAl phase on the MCrAlY coating surface. Continuing rapid di ﬀ usion of C from the substrate towards the interface between the newly formed NiAl layer and the bond coat supports the formation of more carbides at this interface.  

For an aluminized Ni-base alloy it was shown by Pillai et al. [ 38 ]. that increasing the Al concentration at the coating/substrate interface during aluminising provided the driving force for carbon to di ﬀ use from the specimen (base alloy) core to the coating/substrate interface. The supply of C from the substrate increases the activity of C at the surface ( Fig. 6 ). The C activity gradient reduces with increasing ex- posure time. The increasing Al and decreasing Ni concentration at the surface further contributes to the increase in C activity (reduced solu- bility). Once the C activity at the surface is higher than that in the substrate, C begins to di ﬀ use back towards the substrate. This desta- bilises and dissolves the Cr-rich carbides in the outer aluminised layer. The signi ﬁ cantly higher di ﬀ usional velocity of C than Cr results in a high concentration of Cr at the surface thereby leading to the pre- cipitation of  α -Cr.  

The presence of  σ -phase at the interface between the NiAl layer and the bond coat is primarily a result of the heat treatment at the lower temperature of 850 °C. The  σ -phase is stable at this lower temperature and the high Cr concentration (Cr present in the bondcoat) at the in- terface leads to the formation of the  σ -phase. # 5. Summary and conclusions  

The microstructural evolution during over-aluminising of an MCrAlY-coating and subsequent heat treatment on the nickel base su- peralloy IN-792 was investigated. The as-received state of the coating layers was evaluated and the phases present in each of the layers were identi ﬁ ed by EDS and EBSD analyses. A  γ + β  microstructure was identi ﬁ ed in the NiCoCrAlY layer, which is commonly observed in such overlay bond coats.  

The increasing Al-concentration on the initial bondcoat surface during aluminising leads to the dissolution of the  γ -phase and pre- cipitation of  β -NiAl. The increasing Al concentration at the surface re- duces the C activity and induces the di ﬀ usion of C from the substrate toward the coating surface. The signi ﬁ cantly higher di ﬀ usion velocity of C compared to other alloying elements leads to an enrichment of C at the surface and this combined with the high Cr concentration in the bondcoat leads to the precipitation of Cr-rich carbides. Once the C ac- tivity at the surface is higher than in the substrate, C begins to di ﬀ use back to the substrate which destabilises the carbides. The excess Cr left − after dissolution of the carbides precipitates as α Cr  at the coating surface. The subsequent heat treatment at the lower temperature of 850 °C stabilises the  σ -phase at the interface between the aluminide coating and the bondcoat.
