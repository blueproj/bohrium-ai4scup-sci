# A B S T R A C T  

# A R T I C L E I N F O  

Powder metallurgy superalloys are widely used to produce critical rotating parts in turbine engines. However, the issues like what is the driving force of precipitation on prior particle boundary (PPB) during consolidation and how the PPB structure evolves during plastic deformation still remain to be resolved. In this study, with the characterization of the microstructure evolution from powder, consolidation, to plastic deformation, the seg- regation of alloying elements on the particle boundaries at elevated temperature was analyzed by equilibrium thermodynamic calculations, and the distortion of PPB structure under large plastic deformation was quanti- tively simulated by  ﬁ nite element (FE) methods. The results indicated that surface energy serves as the main driving force of PPB precipitation and hot extrusion could distort the PPB structure and yield more uniformly re ﬁ ned grains.

Some results also indicated that there were some optional ways to relatively reduce the detrimental e ﬀ ects of PPB precipitation, such as HIPping at higher temperature and subsequent supersolvus heat treatment, but these could induce excessive grain coarsening [ 18 , 25 , 33 – 35 ]. # 1. Introduction  

Due to the excellent comprehensive properties at elevated tem- peratures, superalloys have been widely adopted as high temperature materials for aircraft engines [ 1 – 8 ]. In terms of polycrystalline super- alloys, powder metallurgy (P/M) is used to produce critical turbine discs with complex compositions and less elemental macro segregation [ 3 , 9 – 11 ]. Generally, typical powder metallurgy routes include powder preparation and consolidation, some plastic deformation methods like hot extrusion and forging are also employed at speci ﬁ c conditions [ 12 , 13 ]. Among them, atomization process like argon atomization (AA) and plasma rotation electric pole (PREP) are the common techniques to yield clean pre-alloyed powders [ 14 ]; hot isostatic pressing (HIPping) is the a widely used method to consolidate superalloy powder [ 15 – 18 ]; plastic deformation after HIPping, such as hot extrusion and isothermal forging, are mostly utilized to obtain disc parts with desired micro- structure [ 19 ], for example, hot extrusion after consolidation is used to break up ceramic inclusions which can adversely impact low cycle fa- tigue (LCF). There is no doubt that direct hot isostatic pressing (as-HIP) would dramatically save costs of producing PM superalloys, but the frequent precipitation on prior particle boundary (PPB) during HIPping detriments the mechanical properties [ 17 , 20 – 26 ].  

In this study, the characterization of the microstructure evolution from powder, HIPping, to plastic deformation was performed, and we tried to deal with the abovementioned two issues experimentally and theoretically. In speci ﬁ c, the segregation of alloying elements on the powder surface at elevated temperature was analyzed by equilibrium thermodynamic, and  ﬁ nite element (FE) simulation was performed to estimate the distortion of PPB structure under large plastic deformation.

# 3. Results  

# 3.1. The Element Distribution of Powders  

The results of AES analysis on a 50 × 50  μ m 2   PREP powder surface are shown in  Fig. 1 . Signi ﬁ cant Ni, O, and C signals were detected at the powder surface prior to sputtering. As the powder was sputtered away layer by layer, other elements, such as Ti, Al, Cr, Mo, etc., started to be detected gradually. From  Fig. 1 , it can be judged that the changes of element concentrations, especially for C, O, and Ti, distinctly varied with the sputtering depth within 10 nm. C and O segregated at the surface or near-surface within 1.5 nm depth of the PREP powder were thought caused by surface adsorption and non-equilibrium phases.  

Since AES can only present average element concentrations at a relatively wide area, the ToF-SIMS was used as a complement to characterize the element distributions on powder surface and interior, which are presented in  Figs. 2 and 3  respectively. ToF-SIMS is not very sensitive to the light element ions (like O and C ions) and heavy element ions (like W and Ta ions), hence we just showed some selected alloying elements in this work. Considering the fact that edge e ﬀ ect may cause some inaccuracy as the powder surface was not  ﬂ at enough, it is more conclusive to state that Ti, Zr, and Nb were segregated at interdendrite or intercellular regions in FGH96 powder, based on ToF-SIMS analysis of powder section, as shown in  Fig. 3 .  

# 3.2. The Microstructure After HIPping  

The microstructure of PPB networks in HIPped FGH96 alloys are shown in  Fig. 4 , the average grain sizes of HIPped 1# and 2# FGH96 were around 4.8  μ m (ASTM 12.5) and 10  μ m (ASTM 11.2), corre- spondingly. PPB was obvious in both of the HIPped alloys, and it was denser in 1# specimen consolidated by smaller AA powders.  

The ToF-SIMS results indicating element distribution at PPB in HIPped 1# FGH96 are shown in  Fig. 5 , which illustrates that Al, Ti, Zr, and some Nb were segregated along PPB.  

TEM was adopted to further identify the kinds of precipitates along PPB. As indicated in  Fig. 6 , ZrO 2 , Al 2 O 3 , MC-type carbide, and  γ ' were found existed along PPB in HIPped 2# specimen. Speci ﬁ cally, the oxides and carbides were much smaller than  γ ' precipitates, which was large enough to envelop the tiny ones. In consistent with this result, the presence of oxides, carbides, and  γ ' were also identi ﬁ ed in HIPped 1# FGH96 [ 41 ].  

# 3.3. The Microstructure After Hot Extrusion  

As a complement to above part, the grain structure after HIPping and hot extrusion are presented in  Fig. 7 . It can be judged from  Fig. 7 (a) that some part of PPB acts as grain boundary after HIPping, particularly for the large PPB with high sphericity outlining the shape of initial powder, and a series of tiny grains were located along PPB. After ex- trusion, the PPB structure were distorted. Speci ﬁ cally, the PPB was elongated along extrusion direction to be rugby-ball like or oval shaped. Moreover, the grain size distribution was more homogeneous and smaller comparing with that of HIPped one.  

To further identify the existence of distorted PPB structure, the distribution of alloying elements in 1# FGH96 after hot extrusion was characterized by ToF-SIMS, as illustrated in  Fig. 8 . Generally, the ele- ments, such as Al, Cr, Ni, Ti, and Nb were randomly dispersed, but the Zr corresponding to stable ZrO 2  still remained at the distorted PPB.  

# 4. Discussions  

# 4.1. Thermodynamics of Precipitation on PPB  

The atomized powders consist of non-equilibrium precipitates formed at extreme undercooling conditions, as the powder atomization is a process with very fast cooling rate approximate to 10 4 – 10 5   °C/s [ 42 ]. There exists segregation of element at dendrite-interdendrite, cellular-intercellular, or surface-interior regions in a single powder particle. Speci ﬁ cally, the detected O indicates the presence of oxides formed by reaction with the oxygen during atomization or oxygen ab- sorbed from external atmosphere, the C element is related to the car- bides in consistence with other similar works [ 31 , 42 , 43 ]. In addition, Ti, Nb, Zr are preferred to stay at interdendrite or intercellular area, as the  γ  phase with higher undercooling degree solidi ﬁ es as dendrite or cellular  ﬁ rstly, while the left liquid with amount of  γ '-forming elements Ti, Nb, and carbide or oxide forming elements Zr starts to solidify thereafter.  

Even though there were some C, O and their compounds accumu- lated at powder surface, but the degree of the segregation after HIPping was much higher than the original powder statues, regarding the characterization of precipitates at PPB in HIPped. For example, the Zr existed in powder majorly migrate onto the PPB during HIPping. What is the driving force of this segregation? In order to explain that, let us try to solve it based on thermodynamic, mainly according to the methods developed by Luthra and Briant [ 44 ].

# 4.2. The Evolution of PPB During Hot Extrusion  

Extrusion serves to breakup any ceramic inclusions which are in- troduced during the atomization process from the atomizing nozzle, and it yields large strain to re ﬁ ne grains. In addition, hot extrusion for powder metallurgy superalloy is also used to distort the PPB structure and redistribute thereon precipitates. To quanti ﬁ cationally evaluate the e ﬀ ects of extrusion on the PPB structures regardless of their interac- tions,  ﬁ nite element (FE) method is used to simulate the distortion of powder-like (circle in two-dimension view) element during extrusion with di ﬀ erent area reduction ratios.  

Considering a cylinder billet extruded through a circular tube die, as shown in  Fig. 9 , three kinds of circle grids at center, middle, and rim regions of the billet are set up during the FE analysis. Assuming the billet is well lubricated, the friction coe ﬃ cient of billet-die interface is set as 0.12. The semiaxis length of the grid along extrusion direction,  c , and that perpendicular to extrusion direction,  a , are changed after the plastic deformation. Because of the friction between the billet and the die, the  ﬂ ow behaviors at di ﬀ erent areas perpendicular to extrusion direction are slightly di ﬀ erent, the grids at rim region are longer and thinner than these at center area. Moreover, with the increase of area reduction ratio,  θ , de ﬁ ned as the ratio of section areas perpendicular to extrusion before and after going through die, the elongated grid turns more gracile. Additionally, the distribution of the precipitates on PPB is changed with the distortion of the PPB structure. As the  A / A 0  ratio increased, the surface densities of the PPB precipitates will correspondingly drop if these precipitates were unbroken at PPB. ( Fig. 10 .) Dynamic re- crystallization during the thermo mechanical process is the main me- chanism to re ﬁ ne grains, as extrusion with high strain rate generally yields large strain on the billet, the distribution of the grain size at two sides of the PPB turns more homogenous.
