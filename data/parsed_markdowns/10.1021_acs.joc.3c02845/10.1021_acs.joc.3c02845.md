# Benzoxaborole Catalyst Embedded with a Lewis Base: A Highly Active and Selective Catalyst for  cis -1,2-diol Modification  

Shuhei Kusano, \*  Yuji Yamada, and Shinya Hagihara  

Cite This:  J. Org. Chem.  2024, 89, 6714−6722  

![](images/a02a106b1cb76cf29238e37a5f40367824cb183775daa14a8b29b1a171a2e558.jpg)  

ABSTRACT:  The regioselective modification of polyols allows rapid access to their derivatives, thereby accelerating the polyol- related biology and drug discovery. We previously reported that benzoxaborole is a potent catalyst for the regioselective modification of polyols containing a  cis -1,2-diol structure. In this study, we developed a bifunctional benzoxaborole catalyst embedded with a Lewis base. Benzoxaborole and Lewis base groups were designed to cooperatively activate a substrate ( cis -1,2-diol) and reactant (electrophile), respectively, hence lowering the reaction barrier for the  cis -1,2-diol moiety. The bifunctional catalyst indeed exhibited a significantly higher catalytic activity and selectivity for  cis -1,2-diol modifications rather than a benzoxaborole catalyst without a Lewis base group. Mechanistic analyses, using both experimental and theoretical methods, supported the design of our catalyst. The bifunctional catalyst reported herein would be a new tool for the straightforward synthesis of polyol derivatives.  

# INTRODUCTION  

Over 50 polyol derivatives of natural products and carbohydrates have been approved as drugs and clinical diagnostic agents since 2000.   In their development processes, diverse derivatives of polyols have been synthesized to explore new and promising candidates for pharmaceutical agents. However, the chemical modification of polyols is a laborious task since they have multiple hydroxyl groups, showing a similar reactivity. Protecting groups are typically required to modify a specific site and suppress the side reactions. The synthetic strategy depending on protecting groups has an inherent drawback: tedious protection − deprotection sequen- ces result in lengthy and inefficient synthetic processes. ,  

A promising approach for rapid access to polyol derivatives is a catalyst-controlled reaction that enables the direct and regioselective modification of polyols without protecting groups (or with a minute usage). − 7   Organotin catalysts (Me 2 SnCl 2 , Bu 2 SnCl 2 , and Bu 2 SnO) regio selectively activate a hydroxyl group on carbohydrates via the stannylene acetal formation, thereby enabling the direct acylation, sulfonylation, and alkylation. ,   Transition metal catalysts with chiral ligands also can mediate regioselective modifications of carbohy- drates. ,   Various organic catalysts, including chiral phos- phoric acids,   chiral carbenes,   chiral Lewis bases, − 16   and peptides, − 19   have been successfully applied for the direct and regioselective modification of polyols.  

Organoboron acids are also potent catalysts for the regioselective modification of polyols. − 23   A common mechanism involves the selective recognition and activation of the  cis -1,2-diol structure of polyols  via tetra -coordinated boronate formation, which is a catalytically active intermediate ( Figure 1 ). This concept of diol activation was pioneeringly established by Aoyama and co-workers with a stoichiometric amount of boronic acids ( Figure 1 A). ,   Currently, this reaction strategy has been elaborated as a catalytic reaction ( Figure 1 B). Taylor and co-workers devised borinic acids that catalyzed the regioselective acylation, alkylation, sulfonylation, sulfamoylation, sulfation, and glycosylation of polyols. − 30

The outstanding  cis -1,2-diol affinity of BO 35 ,   offers good activity and regio selectivity in the catalyst-controlled mod- ifications of carbohydrates. In the recent study by Gademann et al., they employed an organoboron acid catalyst and concisely prepared derivatives of fidaxomicin antibiotics via regioselective acylations. They successfully discovered a new drug candidate with promising antibiotic activity from the derivatives.  

Improvements in both catalytic activity and selectivity are required for further application of organoboron acids in polyol modifications. We previously showed that the electronic perturbation of the boron atom center of BO improved the catalytic activity and selectivity ( Figure 2 A).   The installation of electron-withdrawing groups, such as fluoro- and trifluor- omethyl groups, was effective in improving the catalytic performance. Seeking a more potent BO catalyst, we envisaged the bi function aliz ation of BO and designed a Lewis base (LB) group-attached BO − LB catalyst ( Figure 2 B). BO and LB, two independent catalytic sites, are supposed to activate the substrate via boronate formation and electrophiles via nucleophilic addition, respectively. We anticipated that the cooperative activation should lower the required free energy for modification of the  cis -1,2-diol moiety and hence confer improved catalytic activity and selectivity. Enzymes in nature utilize the concerted catalytic machinery and achieve the site- selective modification of densely functionalized natural products, including a polyol.   Again, the bi function aliz ation of organocatalysts has been broadly accepted as an effective approach for increasing the catalytic potential.   The newly developed bifunctional BO − LB catalyst exhibited significantly improved catalytic activity and selectivity in  cis -1,2-diol modification reactions. Mechanistic studies were supportive for the design of our catalyst. Herein, we present the development, scope, limitations, and mechanistic details of BO − LB catalysts.

Next, we optimized the reaction conditions, including the solvent and the catalyst amount. Among the five examined solvents ( Table S1 ),  2a  showed the high yield and  cis - selectivity in CH 3 CN and CH 2 Cl 2 .  

The catalytic activity and the selectivity decreased in THF and 1,4-dioxane. The product yield increased up to 79% with high  cis- selectivity by removing water using 3 Å molecular sieves (MS3Å) from the reaction medium (CH 3 CN) ( Table S1 ). Notably, the  2a -catalyzed benzoylation of  cis - 5  was remarkedly rapid ( Table S2 ). The benzoylation was completed within 15 min in the presence of 5 mol % catalyst. Only 1 mol % catalyst was sufficient to obtain over 80% yield of  cis - 6a within 60 min.  

We explored the tolerance of electrophiles under the optimized condition using 1 mol % catalyst in CD 3 CN and MS3Å ( Table 2  and  Figures S2 − S5 ). Acylation reactions with various aryl- and alkyl acyl chlorides proceeded in good yields ( 6a − 6e , 83 − 98%) and short reaction times (13 − 65 min), except pivaloyl chloride ( 6f ) ( Table 2 A). Carbonate formation with chloroformates also proceeded well and produced the corresponding carbonates  6g  and  6h  in good yields ( Table 2 B). Thiocarbonate formation with phenyl chlo roth i of or mate was negligible ( 6i ). These  2a -catalyzed acylation and carbonate formation proceeded in a short time and with good yields on a large scale ( Table 2 E). Catalyst  2a  accelerated tosylation ( 6j ), but the reaction rate was comparable with that of  4  without an LB group ( Table 2 C). Catalyst  2a  showed inferior catalytic activity than  4  for the carbamate formation with phenyl isocyanate ( 6k ) ( Table 2 D). Given those tolerances of electrophiles, the coincidence between the nucleophilicity of the  N,N- dimethylamino group and the inherent reactivity of electrophiles might be crucial for  2a  to exert high catalytic activity. The electrophiles, Piv − Cl, Ts − Cl, and Ph − NCO, were presumably incompatible for  2a  owing to their steric or electronic natures. ,   Indeed, we observed that Piv − Cl remained intact in the reaction mixture ( Figure S3 ), suggestive of the steric hindrance of Piv − Cl, preventing the activation by the  N,N- dimethylamino group.  

Our catalyst system was tolerated for many other diol compounds ( Table 3  and  Figures S6 − S9 ). Catalyst  2a accelerated the benzoylation of five- and eight-membered cyclic  cis -1,2-diols and afforded mono-benzoylated adducts  8a and  8b  in good yields. Likewise,  2a  showed excellent catalytic activity for both linear 1,2- and 1,3-diol and provided  8c  and 8d  in good yields. Intriguingly, the benzoylation of 1,4-linear diol  7e  was also promoted in the presence of  2a . Since the seven-membered boronate formation is thermodynamic ally disfavored,   we speculate that the benzoylation of  7e  was mostly mediated via the monodentate coordination of  7e ( Figure S10 ). This would be in line with the formation of bisbenzoylated  8e ′  (4%). We further explored the substrate scope of  2a  by using other electrophiles ( Table 3 B). For all of the tested reactions, including acylation and carbonate formation, mono functional i zed products ( 9a − 9d ) were obtained in rapid and good yields. Catalyst  2a  was also tolerated on the large-scale synthesis and provided the products ( 9c  and  9d ) in high yields ( Table 3 C). However, when Troc-Cl was used as an electrophile,  9b  was obtained in a moderate yield. This is due to the generation of byproduct 9b’ , presumably formed via the intramolecular cyclization of 9b .

We then turned our attention to elucidate the underlying catalytic mechanism of  2a  and studied how the  N,N - dimethylamino group participates in the catalytic cycle and endows the remarkable catalytic activity and selectivity. At the outset, we confirmed that  2a  forms the  tetra -coordinated boronate with  cis -1,2-diol that is postulated to be an intermediate for diol activation. In the   11 B NMR spectra of 2a  ( Figure S11 ), we observed a new signal at 11 ppm upon the addition of  cis - 5 . This upfield shift of   11 B resonance in the presence of  cis - 5  indicates the tetra-coordinated boronate formation between  2a  and  cis - 5 .   We then assessed the possibility that the  N,N -dimethylamino group nucleophilic ally activates the electrophiles in our initial catalyst design. We prepared  13  as a non-nucleophilic control catalyst and compared the catalytic activities of  2a  and  13  ( Figure 3 ).

As an alternative possibility, we evaluated whether the  N,N -diemthylamino group facilitates catalytically active boronate formation. To this end, we compared the association constants ( K a ) of  2a  and 4  for  cis - 5 . The  K a  values of  2a − cis - 5  and  4 − cis - 5  complexes were acquired from   1 H NMR titrations as 14 and 29 M − 1 , respectively ( Figures S12 and S13 ). Catalyst  2a  exhibited a substrate (diol) affinity lower than that of  4 . Collectively, the N,N -dimethylamino group presumably modulates the nucleo- philic step rather than facilitating the catalytically active intermediate formation.

# CONCLUSIONS  

In conclusion, this study presents the development of BO − LB catalysts  1  and  2  to improve the catalytic activity and selectivity of BO. The key to our success in developing the significantly active catalyst  2a  was the simple introduction of the  N,N - dimethylamino group via the methylene linkage at the 7- position of the BO skeleton. Mechanistic studies were supportive for our catalyst design that BO and LB groups cooperatively activate the substrate and reactant, respectively. Catalyst  2a  would be a new avenue for facilitating polyol derivatizations. We also showed that the bi function aliz ation of BO is a promising approach for expanding the catalytic properties of BO. Led by this success, we are currently enthusiastic about developing novel bifunctional BO catalysts that can enable a variety of diol modification reactions that are difficult via diol activation by BO.
