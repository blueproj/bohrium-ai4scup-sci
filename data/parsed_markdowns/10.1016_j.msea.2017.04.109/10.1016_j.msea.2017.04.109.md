# Abstract  

This paper reports the hardening behaviour of nickel base superalloy Alloy 693. The alloy is  designed to derive its strength primarily from the precipitation of coherent γ' phase particles ,  induced by the addition of Al and Ti, in a face centered cubic matrix of Ni-Cr-Fe solid  solution. The alloy possesses excellent combination of mechanical strength, corrosion and  oxidation resistance properties at elevated temperatures. In the present work, uni-modal  distribution of  γ' particles is  precipitated out during isothermal annealing of the alloy in the  temperatures range 800 °C to 950 °C for time periods ranging from 0.5 h to 100 h. Variations  in room temperature hardness and tensile properties of aged alloys have been correlated with  precipitation behaviour of the  γ' phase.  These studies have been carried out in an Instron  tensile testing machine in conjunction with microstructural characterization using a field  emission scanning electron microscope. Mechanisms responsible for hardening of the alloy  under different microstructural conditions have been identified on the basis of minimum   critical resolved shear stress computed theoretically for competing mechanisms.

Alloy 693; Isothermal Ageing;  g¢  precipitation; Hardness; Tensile properties; Strengthening  mechanism.  

# 1. Introduction  

Ni based superalloy Alloy 693 is designed to derive its strength primarily from the  precipitation of coherent  γ' phase particles , induced by the addition of Al and Ti, in a face  centered cubic matrix of Ni-Cr-Fe solid solution. The  γ' -phase has an ordered  L1 2  type  crystal structure with Ni 3 (Al,Ti) stoichiometry. The alloy offers an excellent combination of  mechanical strength and corrosion / oxidation / sulfidation resistance properties at elevated  temperatures [1]. It also exhibits good surface stability than that of its predecessor, Alloy 690,  due to the addition of Al that enhances the stability of Cr 2 O 3  scales and improves corrosion  resistance under high temperature oxidizing and sulfidizing conditions [2].  The alloy finds  applications in places, such as, management of high level nuclear waste [3] and  petrochemical processing industry [1]. It is also a candidate material for high temperature  waste and biomass incinerators and for high temperature fuel cells involved in synthesis gas  production (e.g., fuel cells to power automobiles) and with potential to induce metal dusting 

 [1].  

Size, morphology, volume fraction and distribution of the second phase particles are  important parameters that govern their hardening contributions in an alloy. To understand  precipitation behaviour of γ' particles in Alloy 693 and its effect s on mechanical properties of  the alloy, an exhaustive study is being carried out on the physical metallurgy and mechanical  behaviour of Alloy 693 in order to predict its behaviour under service and processing  conditions. Parts of this study pertaining to precipitation behaviour of γ'  and other phases in  Alloy 693 at elevated temperatures have already been published recently [3-5]. In an earlier  paper [6], it has been shown that it is difficult to suppress the formation of  g¢  precipitates in  Alloy 693 by solid solution treatment. This property has been exploited to induce multi- modal distribution of  g¢  precipitates within the nickel matrix by tailoring heat treatment  procedures. The alloy exhibits a uni-modal distribution of  g¢  precipitates when water  quenched after an isothermal annealing treatment at temperatures 800 ºC and above [3].  

Precipitation hardening at room temperature, in general, is governed by means of two  mechanisms depending upon the size of precipitates and average spacing between them: (i)  by shearing of particles by the movement of dislocations, and (ii) by Orowan looping or bye  passing of particles. The former is usually active when particles are of small sizes and  contributes to strengthening due to the involvement of chemical coherency, modulus  mismatch and energy associated with their shearing. On the other hand, when particles are  large and widely spaced, the latter is usually active where dislocations find it easy to loop  around particles and strengthening arises due to additional stress required to make  dislocations expand and bend between particles.  

In a previous study, it has been shown than the  g¢ -phase particles in Alloy 693  precipitate out in a variety of sizes, volume fractions and distributions depending upon time  and temperatures of ageing [3]. Aim of this work is to study the effect of the precipitation of  g¢  particles on room temperature mechanical properties of Alloy 693 and delineate  mechanisms responsible for precipitation hardening. This study has been carried out in an  Instron tensile testing machine in conjunction with microstructural characterization using a  field emission scanning electron microscope.  

# 2. Experimental Techniques:  

Table 1 gives nominal composition of Alloy 693 and that of the alloy used in the present  study. Chemical analysis of major alloying elements was carried out using inductively  coupled plasma optical emission spectroscopy (ICP-OES) technique while C and S were  analyzed using combustion analysis. Solution treated samples were aged isothermally at  temperatures ranging from 800  ° C -  950 °C for different periods of time (0.5 h - 100 h)  followed by water quenching to induce an uni-modal distribution of  g¢  precipitate within the  disordered nickel matrix. Detailed descriptions of solution treatment and that of the  isothermal heat treatment procedure have been given elsewhere [3]. Microhardness  measurements were carried out using a  Vicker’s hardness tester with a load of 1kgf for dwell  time of 10 s. Reported microhardness values are averages of 10 independent readings for  each measurement. Room temperature tensile properties were evaluated using M8 x 1.25  round tensile specimens of 4 mm diameter and about 20 mm gauge length as per ASTM E8  -4 standard in an Instron (Model-1185) machine, using a constant strain rate of 0.98x10 .  Reported tensile properties are averages for two independent tensile tests for each sample.  Microstructural characterization was carried out using a Carl Zeiss make SIGMA field- emission electron microscope operated at 20 kV. Preparation of samples for microstructural  characterization was carried out by polishing samples on different grades of SiC papers (up to  2400 grit size) followed by a final surface polishing using oxide polishing suspension.  Polished surfaces were electro chemically etched at room temperature using a solution  containing 8 g CrO 3  and 5 ml H 2 SO 4  in 85ml H 3 PO 4  acid to reveal  γ' phase particles . Sizes of  particles were measured using the free ware image analysis software ImageJ [7]. Fine  γ'  particles in the solution treated samples were revealed by transmission electron microscopy,  details of which are given in reference [3]. Lattice parameters of  γ and γ' phases  of samples  were obtained by Rietveld refinement of x-ray data, obtained using a Cu K a  radiation source,  utilizing the knowledge of lattice parameter of the  γ'  phase reported earlier [4].  

# 3. Results:  

# 3.1. Microhardness studies  

The solution treated alloy exhibited Vicker's hardness of about 253.8 ± 7.4 VHN. Aged alloy  exhibited higher hardness values with respect to that of the solution treated alloy at all  temperatures. Table 2 gives hardness values of samples aged for different periods of time.  The alloy initially exhibited an increase in hardness with ageing up to 0.5 h at all  temperatures studied. Further change in hardness values could be categorized into two broad  categories with respect to ageing temperatures, namely, (i) a monotonous increase in hardness  till it reached a plateau, as exhibited by samples aged at 800°C and 850°C temperatures; (ii) a  monotonous decrease in hardness values after 30 min of ageing with a plateau during  prolonged ageing, as exhibited by samples aged at temperatures above 850°C. Fig. 1 shows  variations in the hardness of aged alloys with ageing time at different temperatures studied.  

# 3.2. Microstructure of Aged Alloys  

Scanning electron imaging of solution treated sample showed single phase microstructure,  though transmission electron microscopy exhibited the presence of fine particles (\~ 10 nm  size) of  g¢  phase distributed homogeneously within the  g  matrix (Fig.2). Phase of precipitating  particles was confirmed by superlattice reflections at positions characteristic of the  g¢  phase  (inset of Fig.2). These particles have been reported to precipitate out during water quenching  of the solution treated alloy [6].  

Aged samples exhibited a uniform distribution of  g¢ particles at all temperatures. Fig. 3  shows sizes and distributions of  γ' precipitates in samples aged for 30 min, 2 h and 100 h at  temperatures in the range 800 °C - 950 °C. Since the solution treated alloy inherited fine  particles of the  γ' -phase (of size less than about 5 nm) [6], isothermal annealing resulted in  their further growth and/or coarsening depending upon annealing temperature. Samples aged  at 800°C exhibited the maximum density of particles at all annealing times (Fig. 3(a)  –  (c)).  The  γ' particles  appeared to have grown continuously with time as their volume fractions and  sizes increased monotonically (Tables 3 and 4). At higher temperatures, the alloy exhibited  an increase in size of  γ'  particles and a decrease in their volume fractions with increasing time  and temperatures. At 875°C, the alloy exhibited almost constant volume fraction (within  experimental errors) of particles though their sizes increased with time, due to faster  coarsening kinetics at the higher temperature. Further increase of temperature decreased  number density of  γ'  particles with concomitant increase their size. This suggested that, at  temperatures above 875°C, transformation was dominated by faster coarsening kinetics.  Interestingly, at 900 and 950 °C temperatures, the alloy exhibited a larger volume fractions of  the γ' -phase during initial ageing times which reduced during prolonged ageing. This  behaviour was found to be due to under-saturated state of the  g  matrix (with respect to  g¢   forming solutes) at 900 and 950 °C temperatures. This caused dissolution of already formed  g¢  phase particles till composition of the  g  matrix saturates at these temperatures. This was  confirmed on the basis of variation in lattice parameter of  g  matrix during isothermal  annealing at 950  ° C (Table 5). Lattice parameter values were estimated by Rietveld  refinement of x-ray diffraction data (not presented here) corresponding to different heat  treatment. Detailed description of this study is beyond the scope of present work and a part of  it is published elsewhere [4]. From Table 5, it was clear that lattice parameter of the  g  matrix  in the sample aged for 30 min at 950  ° C was more than that in the solution treated sample,  indicating an increase in solute concentration in the matrix after 30 min of ageing. The  observed decrease in the lattice parameter of the  g  matrix after 100 h was due to the  precipitation of a chromium-rich  a -phase, which has already been discussed earlier [4].  During prolonged ageing particles exhibited a tendency to transform from a spherical  morphology to cuboidal morphology. The  γ' particles observed in the present study  always  remained coherent with the matrix during growth and coarsening. Evolution behaviour of  these precipitates has already been discussed elsewhere [3].

# 4.2. Strengthening of alloy  due to the precipitation of γ' -phase  

The degree of strengthening caused by a second-phase hardening particles depend up on their  distribution in the ductile matrix. Further, distribution of particles is described by their  volume fraction, average particle size and mean interparticle spacing, which are inter-related  such that a change in one factor affects the others. For instance, for a given volume fraction  of the second phase, an increase in particle size increases their inter-particle spacing.  Difference in the strengths of the alloy aged for different periods of time and temperature  could be attributed to combinations of one or more of these factors.  Fig.6 shows variations in  the precipitates size as well as yield strengths plotted against ageing time for samples aged at  different temperatures. The observed variation in their strengths was consistent with their  hardness behaviour (Fig.1).  

In an earlier paper [3], it has been shown that the alloy under study, in solution treated  condition, contains fine distribution of γ' precipitates.  The coefficient of strain hardening (i.e.,  n ) exhibited by its stress-strain curve was highest ( n  \~ 0.84) among all the samples studied  here. This value was also close to values reported for other nickel base superalloys hardened  by fine coherent particles and are characteristic by a mechanism involving shearing of  particles by dislocations (see for example, [10, 11]). Monotonous increase in hardness as well  as strengths (both YS and UTS) with ageing at 800 ºC was consistent with increase in size  and volume fraction  of γ' particles  at this temperature (Tables 3 and 4). Precipitation of the  g¢ - phase particles during ageing at 850 °C appeared to have followed a similar nucleation and  growth trend, albeit at a faster rate, as reflected in the hardening behaviour during ageing - its  peak hardness had reached within about 20 h. A small drop in hardness beyond 20 h could be  attributed to the coarsening stage.  On the other hand, samples annealed in the temperature  regime 875°C - 950°C, exhibited drop in their YS (similar to those observed in their hardness  values) while size of particles therein increased with ageing time, suggesting of the  coarsening of  g¢ -phase particles beyond a certain period. Samples aged at 875°C, exhibited  little variation in its strength and strain hardening rate up to 2 h (Fig.4b) though some drop in  its yield strength could be noticed during prolonged ageing. Similar behaviour was observed  for samples annealed at 900°C and 950°C, though they exhibited drop in their strengths after  0.5 h of ageing. The time beyond which the strength started decreasing could be associated  with the beginning of coarsening in this temperature range (Figs. 4c and 4d). This was also  consistent with monotonous decrease in hardness after 30 min in this regime, suggesting that  peak in their hardness values appeared before 30 min (Fig. 1).

Though several factors, such as coherency strain, ordered structure, modulus effect,  stacking fault energy, interfacial energy and morphology, and lattice friction stress, etc. may  act as obstacles, strengthening of  g¢ -phase hardened Ni base alloys is mainly attributed to an  ordered structure and coherency strain associated with  γ' -phase particles [13, 14]. Energy  associated with the formation of anti-phase boundaries (APBs) within ordered precipitates  during their shearing also contributes to the strength. Further, shearing of ordered  g¢  particles  necessitates the passage of pairs of dislocations to restore their L1 2  order [15, 16] - passage of  the leading dislocation of the pair destroys the L1 2  crystalline order and that of the trailing  dislocation restores the order. Two mechanisms for shearing of ordered particles are proposed  depending upon the spacing maintained between the leading and trailing dislocations relative  to size of particles ( d ). Among the aforementioned precipitation hardening mechanisms, there would always  be a competition among them and the one with the least CRSS value would dominate. For a  given volume fraction of precipitates, the stress required to move dislocations through  precipitates would increase with increasing size of precipitates when shearing by weakly  coupled dislocations is active, while it would decrease when either shearing by strongly  coupled dislocations or by Orowan mechanism is active.  

As mentioned in Section 4.2, value of the strain hardening coefficient in solution  treated alloy was close to that reported for other nickel base superalloys exhibiting a  hardening mechanism involving shearing of particles by dislocations [10, 11]. Further, during  shearing of  g¢  particles pairs of dislocations maintained a separation of about 10 to 50 nm [18,  21-23]. Since the size of particles in the solution treated alloy (< 10 nm) was much smaller  than typical separation between two dislocations of the pair, it was not unreasonable to  attribute the active hardening mechanism to weakly coupled pairs of dislocations. All aged  samples exhibited values of coefficient of strain hardening lower than that of the solution  treated sample. This suggested of hardening mechanisms active in aged samples were  different than that in the solution treated alloy.

From Fig.9 it was evident that, for lower volume  fractions of precipitates (i.e.,  f   ≤ 0.2) shearing by weakly coupled pair of dislocations is the  main operative mechanism till particles grow up to an average size of 50 nm beyond which  the Orowan bowing would dominate (Fig.9a). However, for  f  > 0.2, shearing by strongly  coupled dislocations would dominate till Orowan bowing becomes operative when particles  become very large. The size of particles beyond which the Orowan mechanism would  dominate was quite sensitive of the volume fraction. For  f  = 0.5, the Orowan would dominate  only when the size of particles  d  > 250 nm (plot not shown here), while it would dominate for  d  > 350 nm if the volume fraction is increased to 0.55 (Fig.9b). On the basis of this analysis,  it could be concluded that, when the volume fraction is low, for example,  f  < 0.2 (Fig.9a), the  operative mechanism of shearing by weakly coupled dislocations for small particles would be  directly taken over by the Orowan looping when the particles would grow beyond 50 nm.  However, when the volume fraction is large, shearing by strongly coupled dislocations would  dominate mostly. The minimum size of particles beyond which the Orowan mechanism  would dominate over the shearing by strongly coupled dislocations would increase with  increase in volume fraction of particles (Fig.9 b and c), though for very larger volume  fraction it would be practically always governed by strongly coupled dislocations. # 5. Conclusions  

On the basis of the present study, following conclusions can be made on hardening behaviour  of Alloy 693:  

i.   Precipitation of the  γ' -phase significantly improves the strength of Alloy 693   ii.   γ' precipitates  evolve homogenously throughout the matrix with spherical morphology  which tend to change to cuboidal during prolonged ageing.  iii.   Precipitates always maintained coherency with the matrix.  iv.   Up to 850°C, the exhibits a monotonous increase in strength till a plateau is reached.  v.   In temperature range 875°C-950°C, the alloy exhibited a monotonous decrease in  strength after 30 min of ageing with a plateau during prolonged ageing.  vi.   Ageing treatment for 30 minutes at 875°C gives best combination of strength and  ductility.  

vii.   Hardening mechanisms active in different microstructural conditions have been  identified. When the volume fraction of γ' particles is low, the operative mechanism of  shearing by weakly coupled dislocations for small particles is directly taken over by the  

Orowan looping beyond a certain size of particles. Shearing by strongly coupled  dislocations dominates mostly when the volume fraction of particles is large.
