Coupons of a Ni base super alloy, Inconel alloy 718 (IN718) were laser shock peened with and without an ablative layer and resulting microstructure and residual stress state were studied. Laser shock peening without a protective overlay results in both mechanical and thermal loading of the material, leading to melting and re-solidiﬁcation on the surface along with deformation due to laser induced shock wave. The surface shows presence of a non-uniform recast layer which increases the roughness of the surface and also results in a tensile state of residual stresses on surface. The recast layer has areas with modiﬁed chemistry and shows presence of nano particles deposited on top of the matrix. In this study, recast layer formed in Inconel alloy 718 (IN718) as a result of laser shock peening without protective overlay was characterized and compared with surface condition of a sample peened with a protective overlay. These results are presented and discussed in relation with those of previous studies.

Laser shock peening uses short duration laser pulses of nano seconds order to introduce a shock wave into material which plastically deforms the surface of the material and introduces com- pressive residual stresses on surface. A typical application of the process utilizes Q-switched Nd: Glass laser (   = 1.054   m) to irra- diate a target with a very short (<30 ns) and intense (Power density of several GW/cm 2 ) laser pulse. Such pulses can instantaneously ablate the surface layer into a high temperature (about 10,000   ◦ C) and high pressure (several GPa) plasma ( Peyre et al., 1996 ). This plasma is conﬁned by the use of a transparent overlay, usually glass or water. This conﬁning media prevents the plasma from expand- ing away from the surface as soon as it is generated allowing for it to absorb more energy. An overlay, opaque to the laser is applied to the surface of the component being peened. Typically black paint, vinyl tape or a variety of metallic tapes have been used. This sacri- ﬁcial layer is known as protective coating or ablative material. This protective coating protects the sample surface from thermal effects ( Forget et al., 1990 ) and helps in creating a pure mechanical effect in the material, introducing deep compressive residual stresses in  

In absence of a protective coating, the surface of the treated zone is expanded by the thermal effects. After the laser pulse is over, the affected zone cools and tries to contract, it is resisted by the surrounding material and the sub surface. The resulting sur- face residual stresses are consequently tensile stresses ( Masse and Barreau, 1995 ) although the subsurface will still have compressive residual stresses due to propagation of shock wave and consequent non-uniform plastic strain introduced by it. The resultant treat- ment is a combination of thermal and mechanical effects. While the ablative overlay protects the surface and helps avoid the gen- eration of tensile stresses, it is a time consuming affair as the overlay gets damaged during peening and needs to be changed frequently, making it slow and expensive in an industrial conﬁguration. Also, in certain applications the parts being peened might not be acces- sible for application of ablative overlay. Hence, a lot of attention is being given to Laser peening without protective coating ( Patrice et al., 2002 ). This method uses a frequency doubled (second har- monic) of Q-switched Nd:YAG laser with a wavelength of 532 nm and has been applied in various applications ( Sano et al., 2008 ). In available literature about laser shock peening without a protec- tive overlay, the focus of studies has been on evolution of residual stress ﬁelds ( Obata et al., 1999 ). Very few comprehensive studies have been conducted on the microstructure evolution. Hence, it is

Fairhand et al. (1977)  peened the weld zone in two aluminum alloys: a wrought alloy, 5086-H32 and 6061-T6 age hardened alloy without a protective coating, with fused quartz as a conﬁning media and studied the surface effects of peening. In both alloys, they found considerable melting and a irregularly re-solidiﬁed layer, around 10–20   m thick consisting of numerous holes. Also, shrinkage cracks were observed in this re-solidiﬁed layer. Similar structure was noticed in case of laser peened Fe-3 wt.% Si alloy by  Clauer et al. (1977) , when peened with long duration pulses (200 ns). A rough estimate of the depth of thermally affected was given by: x  =  2 √ kt ,  k  being thermal diffusivity and  t  is the pulse duration. It was estimated that due to rapid cooling the thermal effects extend to only around 10   m from the surface. Some re-solidiﬁed droplets were also observed on the surface.  

Forget et al. (1990)  performed direct ablation on monocrys- talline CMSX-2, a Ni-based superalloy. Peening was done using a Nd:YAG laser (   = 1.06   m) and a very short pulse duration (600 ps). Even direct ablation in unconﬁned mode generated high pressures shock wave which deformed the material. TEM foils parallel to sur- face were extracted from 25   m below the surface. The results showed pairs of dislocations shearing the matrix. No TEM was performed on the surface. The surface morphology of the sam- ple showed a wavy proﬁle with concentric circles. No melting was reported on the surface.  

Bugayev et al. (2006)  studied the surface morphology of IN600 coupons peened with a Nd:YAG laser using water as conﬁne- ment mode. A patch was created with 80% overlap on polished IN600 coupons. Studies done using atomic force microscopy (AFM) revealed features which were tilted at an angle to the base mate- rial. They also studied the ablated material which is taken away by water during peening and found presence of nano particles of 60 nm sizes.  

Some direct comparisons have been made between laser peen- ing done with and without ablative media in a water conﬁned regime.  Peyre et al. (1996)  conducted laser peening on 316 L stain- less steel samples with two different kind of treatments: peening with a Nd:Glass laser with an ablative layer and peening with Nd:YAG laser to peen without ablative layer. Comparisons were made on surface topography, mechanical properties and composi- tion changes brought about by these two treatments.  

It is important to study the microstructure changes in conjunc- tion with residual stress state, and surface morphology to develop a better understanding of treatments done with and without an abla- tive layer. None of the studies found in literature had TEM analysis directly from the surface region. Hence, for this study it was decided to make thin foils from the recast layer to conduct a localized study. Ni based superalloys are used in wide variety of components in a gas turbine engine. Inconel 718 (IN718) is the most widely used Ni- based superalloy with uses in compressor and turbine disks, shafts, blades, stators, seals, supports, tubes, and fasteners. Hence, this alloy was used for the present study.

2.2. Laser shock peening conditions  

A Nd:Glass laser with a wavelength of 1.054   m was used to peen the samples in a water conﬁnement mode with fol- lowing parameters: Laser beam energies: 3.9, 3.5, 2.5, and 1.5 J, pulse width: 20 ns, Beam diameter 1.8 mm (power densities: 7.7 GW/cm 2 , 6.9 GW/cm 2 , 4.9 GW/cm 2   and 2.9 GW/cm 2 ). Two sets of samples were made: with protective coating (called Taped sam- ples) and without protective coating (called naked samples). A black vinyl tape was used as protective coating. Peening was per- formed in two ways: (A) Single impact samples-rows of single impacts were made on coupons ( Fig. 2 (a,b)). (B) Patch samples- A patch of 20 mm 2   (in center of the coupon was laser shock peened with a 50% overlap between laser impacts ( Fig. 2 (c,d)). All samples were peened on only one surface.  

The appearance of impacts peened with protective coating was smooth and surface still retained its polished looks. The sample peened without protective coating has a dull and rough appear- ance. The resulting dimples obtained were also slightly larger than the ones obtained with protective coating. Same is true in case of samples with peened patch. Some discoloration is clearly visible in case of sample peened without coating.  

# 2.3. Microstructure  

Samples for metallography were prepared by grinding with suc- cessively ﬁner SiC (Silicon Carbide) grit papers. Final polishing was performed using colloidal silica on a vibratory polisher. For optical microscopy, the sample was etched using a solution of 100 ml HCL and 0.5 ml H 2 O 2 . The recast layer was studied by analyzing the sur- face as well as the cross section. A small piece (10 mm  ×  3 mm) was sectioned from the patch samples and mounted in cross section. These samples were carefully polished for conducting microscopy. A scanning electron microscope (SEM) was used to look at the uniformity and thickness of the recast layer. Localized variations in chemical composition were studied by energy dispersive spec- troscopy (EDS). An attempt was made to analyze the microstructure by using orientation imaging with electron backscattered diffrac- tion (EBSD). Both EDS and EBSD were performed using EDAX systems.  

For transmission electron microscopy (TEM) of the recast layer, a thin slice was sectioned ( ∼ 250   m) parallel to the peened surface from patch samples. This slice was then thinned from the rear to a thickness of  ∼ 100   m. The recast layer was too brittle to be able to punch a disc from thin section. So a 3 mm disc was obtained using an abrasive slurry disc cutter (South Bay Technologies). The surface of thin slice was covered with a tape to avoid any contamination from abrasives. The disc so obtained was dimpled to a thickness of  ∼ 15   m and then ion milled at a low angle (12 ◦ ) to electron transparency. All the thinning was done from the non-peened side to keep the recast layer intact, enabling observation of recast layer microstructure. Similarly, a thin foil was obtained from a sample with a protective overlay to study the microstructure at the very surface.

# 3. Results and discussion  

# 3.1. Surface topography characterization  

Topographical studies were done to study the spatial proﬁle (the dimensions, depth, and area) of the dimples introduced by the peening. Studies were conducted by an optical interferometer (Wyko NT1100) using vertical step interferometry. Patch samples were used to study the roughness of the peened surface. A total of 5 measurements were made at different areas and average value of roughness was reported in Ra (  m).  

Irrespective of the energy used, the appearance of the single impacts and patch samples was similar in both kinds of laser treat- ments.  Fig. 3 (a) shows the dimple produced by single impact when peening is done with a protective coating. The peened area in dim- ple retains the look of original surface of the material.  

Fig. 3 (b) shows a representative SEM micrograph of surface a single impact sample peened without any protective coating. Here, the dimple is clearly visible and has an uneven surface character- ized by presence of one ring in center. This could be caused by the shock wave which comes after ablation and melting, causing a splash. High magniﬁcation images shown in  Fig. 3 (c, d) indi- cate a non-uniform recast layer is formed on top of sample. This shows that there is melting and re-solidiﬁcation of the top layer of the material characterized by formation of pores ( Fig. 3 (c)), which are distributed non-uniformly. No cracks were seen on the sur- face layer.  Fairhand et al. (1977)  have reported seeing shrinkage cracks in their study of aluminum alloys. The surface is also marked by presence of re-solidiﬁed droplets of varying sizes ( Fig. 3 (d)). Droplets of much smaller size were also observed but could not be properly characterized by SEM.  

# 3.2. Single impact and roughness characterization  

Studies were done to study the spatial proﬁle (dimensions, depth, and area) as a function of peening conditions of the dim- ples introduced by the peening as well the roughness introduced in the overlay samples. Studies were conducted by an optical inter- ferometer (Wyko NT1100) using vertical step interferometry.  Fig. 4 compares results from single impact samples with an energy = 3.9 J with and without a protective overlay. Sample with protective overlay has dimple dimensions close to 2 mm in both  X  and  Y  direc- tion with a depth of about 5.5   m while sample without protective overlay has a diameter of  ∼ 2.4   m with a depth of 8.5   m. It is clear from the spatial proﬁle that protective overlay gives a much smoother surface while the sample without overlay has a wavy proﬁle indicating roughness. Also in taped samples, there is a clear evidence of pile up at the edges of dimples, indicating that the high mechanical impact generated when peening is done with an abla- tive overlay. The enhanced pressure pulse getting transferred to the material is the consequence of acoustic impedance mismatch between two materials at the interface (ablative layer and the mate- rial being peened) i.e., when shock wave passes from material 1 to material 2, the pressure increases if Z 1  < Z 2  (Z-Acoustic impedance). Peyre et al. (1998)  reported a 30–50% enhancement is peak pres- sure, when a Al coating was used compared to no coating being used on 316 L steel samples, which was attributed to impedance mismatch effect.  

Fig. 5  shows the proﬁles from patch samples with different energy conditions. The unpeened sample is a smooth surface. In samples peened with overlay, rows of laser peening impacts are clearly discernible with undulating surface. No real deterioration of surface was observed. There are regions at the edges of impacts where the material gets piled up. In case of naked samples ( Fig. 5 (d), rough areas can be clearly seen. LSP introduces some roughness in the peened regions.  

In case of samples peened with ablative overlay (taped samples), the roughness (Ra) of samples increase only by a small magnitude, from 120 nm for as-received sample to 1.87   m for 3.9 J-taped sam- ple. The magnitude of roughness increased with increase in laser energy. But in case of specimen peened without an overlay (naked specimen), the roughness is high, reaching up to 19   m in case of 3.9 J naked conditions. As expected, the naked condition showed much higher roughness than taped conditions.

During laser peening the surface is subjected to intense pressure and temperature which can change surface chemical composition. EDS was done performed on different areas of the peened surface as shown in  Fig. 6 . In case of sample peened without protective overlay, composition varied in different regions. The surface layer is not is not uniform. At some places it is characterized by high density of pores ( Fig. 6 a) and at other places it has a smooth appear- ance ( Fig. 6 b). In case of sample peened with protective overlay, no change was seen.  

EDS data was collected from both regions shown in  Fig. 6  and compared in  Table 2 . There is clearly higher amount oxygen and chromium and much lower Ni content in the layer where there is high density of pores. There are also some variations in Al and Ti content. This suggests that both of these regions (high and low density of pores) are thermally affected, but to a different degree. Peyre et al. (2002)  found two kinds of heat affected layers on a steel sample peened without a protective overlay. These layers also underwent changes in chemistry. They reported a 1–2   m thick chromium oxide layer and 5–15   m thick melted and thermally affected layer. A similar oxide layer was reported by  Obata et al. (1999)  in laser peening of 304 steel by frequency doubled Nd:YAG laser. From the previous study and results from current studies, it is proposed that regions with high density of pores are the oxide layer and the smoother areas on the surface are the melted and resolidiﬁed layer. The non-uniformity of the surface could be due to some of the oxide layer getting blown off during the propagation of pressure pulse, which comes after the thermal loading has ended.  

# 3.4. Cross section characterization  

Cross section analysis ( Fig. 7 ) of the recast layer shows the depth of recast layer is about 5–10   m and that cracks are present in this recast layer. The cracks ran parallel to the surface and did not extend into the unmelted substrate as had been the case in a previous study ( Clauer et al., 1977 ).  Fig. 7 (a) shows the cross section of a patch sample peened with protective overlay. The edge is smooth and no change to microstructure or the surface topography is visible. In case of patch sample, without overlay ( Fig. 7 (b)) an irregular recast layer is clearly visible and extends to an average of 5   m micron.  

The higher magniﬁcation micrograph in Fig. 7(c) clearly showsthat apart from the small recast layer, no modiﬁcation has been made to the original grain structure of the substrate and indeed the same grains extend to the surface. This agrees with the fact that the thermal loading during laser peening lasts for a very short time and hence its effects do not extend very deep.  

An effort was made to use orientation imaging microscopy using electron backscattered diffraction (EBSD) to study any localized changes taking place in and near the recast layer. EBSD is an ideal tool to study localized response of the material. Kikuchi patterns were collected from different locations: Recast layer, close to recast layer and slightly far from recast layer.  Fig. 8  shows these locations and corresponding kikuchi patterns. Kikuchi patterns vary in qual- ity as a function of distance from surface. Kikuchi pattern from the recast layer has a very weak contrast. Kikuchi patterns can be weak due to couple for reasons. It can indicate presence of plastic strain creating regions with deviation from Bragg’s condition and hence resulting in a weak pattern. This seems unlikely considering this region is subjected to high thermal loads and the fact that the data from EBSD comes from a very ﬁne surface layer ( ∼ 20 nm), hence the contribution to the pattern is limited to the region from the very surface and not sub surface which is known to be plastically deformed by the shock wave. Weak pattern could also indicate presence of small grains/particles oriented in different directions hence producing a kikuchi pattern which is average of all the con- tributing grains. But from cross section results ( Fig. 7 ), we know that there is no grain reﬁnement. This indicates presence of a structure which could not be resolved by SEM. Hence it was decided to make a TEM foils out of the recast layer. Kikuchi pattern obtained from 5   m away from surface i.e., about 1   m away from visible recast layer, the quality of pattern becomes better and at 10   m from the surface, we obtain a high quality Kikuchi pattern indicating that at this distance from surface the substrate microstructure has not been affected in any signiﬁcant way.

# 3.6. Diffraction peak broadening analysis  

Fig. 9 (b) shows the FWHM of diffraction peaks as a function of depth for various peening conditions. In case of sample peened with protective overlay, the FWHM is highest at surface (2.5 ◦  ). This is expected as the shock wave generates maximum pressure (and hence deformation) at the surface and then it attenuates rapidly. After  ∼ 50   m, FWHM drops sharply (to  ∼ 1 ◦  ) and stays constant. This steep drop after  ∼ 50   m is similar to what was observed in residual stress proﬁle.  

In case of sample peened without a protective overlay, FWHM was higher. In this case, since the surface is subjected to both ther- mal and mechanical loading, the higher FWHM at surface could be due to plastic strain generated by shock wave or due to any grain size reﬁnement because of high and rapid thermal load. Here also FWHM dropped sharply to  ∼ 1.5 ◦  . But surprisingly, after 100   m or so, the FWHM of both samples did not approach similar values. Indeed FWHM of the samples peened without overlay stayed higher throughout the measurement depths by a signiﬁcant margin. This is surprising considering the fact that residual stress proﬁles of both samples are very similar after 100   m or so. Also, thermal load- ing certainly does not affect the material at such depths and could not be the reason behind higher FWHM. It would be expected that shock waves generated in both samples would attenuate similarly, and if indeed shock pressures in depth are higher in sample with- out overlay causing higher deformation(and higher FWHM), then it should have manifested in residual stress proﬁle too, showing higher subsurface residual stresses. At this point reasons for this discrepancy are not known.

# 4. Conclusions  

Two kinds of laser treatments were compared in term of microstructure and surface aspects. LSP treatment with protective overlay, which subjects the metal to only mechanical loading pro- duces a smooth ﬁnish and produces no changes in the chemistry of surface. On the other hand samples peened without protective overlay is exposed to both thermal and mechanical loads and pro- duces a rough surface consisting of non-uniform recast layer which has re-solidiﬁed droplets of varying size deposited on it. This recast layer also has also has a different chemistry than the baseline mate- rial.  

The effects of laser peening without overlay can be divided into three layers: a thin oxide layer on the surface, a thermally affected layer which is subjected to both thermal and mechanical load- ing and can extend up to  ∼ 70–80   m below the surface and has some changes in chemistry and a plastically deformed which is only affected by mechanical loading and has no modiﬁcations in chemistry.  

The treatment with ablative overlay, with the given laser con- ditions, creates a residual stress proﬁle with high compressive residual stresses on the surface and compressive stresses extending up to a depth of  ∼ 500   m. Treatment without an overlay, creates a proﬁle with a wide range of stresses: form slightly compressive stresses on the surface (due to presence of volume mismatch of the oxide layer), changing to highly tensile region (due to melting subsequent re-solidiﬁcation) extending  ∼ 80   m from the surface and then compressive region similar to sample with overlay to a depth of  ∼ 450   m (due to plastic deformation caused by mechan- ical impact of the shock wave).  

The surface of samples peened with an ablative overlay is only subjected to mechanical loading. Hence the only change in microstructure is a high density of dislocations. The surface of sam- ples peened without ablative layer is subjected to high temperature and pressure. As a result of this interaction, the resultant recast layer has nano particles as well as a high density of dislocations. But there is no evidence of grain reﬁnement in near surface regions.
