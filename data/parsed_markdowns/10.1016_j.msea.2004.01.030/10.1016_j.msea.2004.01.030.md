# Effect of carbon additions on the as-cast microstructure and defect formation of a single crystal Ni-based superalloy  

K.A. Al-Jarba, G.E. Fuchs ∗  

Materials Science  &  Engineering Department, University of Florida, P.O. Box 116400, Gainesville, FL 32611-6400, USA  

Received 25 September 2003; received in revised form 20 January 2004  

# Abstract  

In an effort to reduce grain defects in large single crystal Ni-base superalloy components, carbon is intentionally added. In this study, the effect of carbon additions on the microstructure and solidiﬁcation defect formation of a model Ni-based superalloy, LMSX-1, was examined. The results show that the tendency of the alloy to form all types of solidiﬁcation defects decreased as the carbon content increased. The as-cast microstructures also exhibited a decrease in the amount of  γ – γ ′   eutectic structure and an increase in the volume fraction of carbides and porosity, as the carbon content was increased. The carbides formed in these alloys were mostly of script-type MC carbides which formed continuous, dendritic networks in the interdendritic region. Microprobe analysis of the as-cast structures showed that the partitioning coefﬁcients did not change with carbon additions. Therefore, the reduction in defect formation with increasing carbon content could not be attributed to changes in segregation behavior of alloying elements. Instead, the presence of these carbides in the interdendritic regions of the alloy appeared to have prevented the thermosolutal ﬂuid ﬂow.

# 1. Introduction  

Nickel-based superalloys are complex alloys that are widely used for gas turbine components that are exposed to both high temperatures and high stresses, namely, turbine blades and vanes  [1] . Single crystal nickel-based superal- loys, which exhibit increased creep strength in comparison to conventionally cast and directionally solidiﬁed superal- loys, are used for these components in order to increase the performance and efﬁciency of aircraft and power generating gas turbines. Industrial gas turbines have traditionally used turbine blades fabricated from equiaxed polycrystalline cast nickel based superalloys due to lower operating tempera- tures compared to aircraft gas turbines. However, the need to increase the efﬁciency of industrial gas turbines requires increasing the turbine inlet temperature and, consequently, the use of single crystal nickel base superalloys for the turbine vanes and blades.  

For large single crystals, though, it has proven difﬁcult to avoid casting defects, such as freckles and misoriented grains, which have been attributed to thermal solutal convec- tion  [3,4] . Furthermore, the frequency of defect formation has been reported to increase as the size of the component increases  [2] . However, carbon additions have been shown to signiﬁcantly reduce the frequency of solidiﬁcation defects [3,4]  and to reduce the formation of surface scale on single crystal nickel base superalloy castings  [5] .  

The purpose of this study was to further evaluate the effect of carbon additions on the as-cast microstructure of a model single crystal Ni-base superalloy. The effects of the carbon addition, and the resulting carbides, on the segregation of the different elements in the alloys and the formation of defects, such as freckles, were evaluated.

The effect of carbon additions on the microstructure of the as-cast single crystal Ni-base superalloys was evaluated by utilizing 10–12 kg charges of the master alloy with carbon additions, if any, made just prior to pouring. The microstructure of the as-cast alloys was examined using standard metallographic sample preparation. The samples were mounted, polished and then etched with the Pratt and Whitney Etch # 17 (100 ml H 2 O  +  100 ml HCl +  100 ml HNO 3  +  3 g MoO 3 ) which dissolves the  γ ′ . The microstructures were analyzed using light optical metallog- raphy (LOM) and SEM metallographic techniques. A JEOL Model 6400 scanning electron microscope was operated at 15 kV for metallographic inspection.  

In order to reveal the carbide morphology, a deep etch- ing of the samples was performed using a 70% HCl and 30% peroxide solution. The solution removes the  γ  matrix and the  γ ′   phase to a signiﬁcant depth and, as a result, the morphology of the carbides, which were not effected by the etchant, was revealed.  

The composition of various constituents and phases, such as the carbides, was determined on polished and un-etched samples, using a JEOL Model Superprobe 733 operated at 15 kV with a beam size of 1   m. Two techniques were used to characterize the solidiﬁcation segregation behavior of al- loying elements. The ﬁrst technique was to examine the composition of each element at discrete locations, which represented the extremes of segregation behavior. The com- position of at least three dendrite cores and three interden- dritic regions were determined for each alloy. The effective partitioning coefﬁcient ( k ′ ) was then calculated by dividing the value of the wt.% composition of the core by the wt.% composition of the interdendritic region for each element. The second technique was to perform a line scan through the dendrite core starting at the interdendritic region at one edge of the dendrite, traversing the center of the dendrite core and ending at the opposite edge of the dendrite. The results were plotted as the measured weight percent (wt.%) composition versus distance along the line. The curves had a maximum or minimum value for each element depending on the element’s segregation behavior. The partitioning co- efﬁcient ( k ) was then determined by dividing the measured maximum or minimum wt.% measured by the nominal wt.% composition of each element in the alloy.  

Standard TEM sample preparation and analytical tech- niques were used to determine phase compositions, mor- phologies, crystal structures and orientation relationships. Three (3) mm diameter discs for transmission electron mi- croscopy were obtained from the single crystal bars perpen- dicular to the  ⟨ 0 0 1 ⟩ growth direction. Foils were prepared by jet polishing the discs with 10% perchloric acid  +  70% methyl alcohol  +  20% glycerol at 25–40 V maintaining tem- perature at 0–10   ◦ C using liquid N 2 . The foils were exam- ined at 200 kV in a Philips EM420 microscope.  

Differential thermal analysis was performed to determine the solidus, liquidus,  γ ′ -solvus and carbide dissolution tem- peratures as a function of carbon content. Samples of both the as-cast condition and after a solution heat treatment were evaluated. The solution heat treatment used in this study, was similar to that of the CMSX-10 solution heat treatment with the maximum temperature adjusted for the solidus of the model alloy, LMSX-1. Prior to testing the samples, the DTA unit was calibrated with high purity Ni at a scan rate of 20   ◦ C/min. Due to the severe segregation that occurs dur- ing solidiﬁcation, samples of at least 150 mg in size were used for the DTA analysis. Data was only acquired during the heating cycle of the DTA test in order to avoid the ef- fects of super cooling. All of the DTA samples were heated at a constant rate of 20   ◦ C/min.  

# 3. Results  

# 3.1. Casting defects  

Carbon additions to a model alloy were observed to result in a reduction in the formation of all types of defects ( Fig. 1 and  Table 3 ). However, only one mold was processed for each of the carbon containing alloys and two molds were prepared for the baseline (0 wt.% carbon addition) alloy. Al- though each mold had multiple bars, it is difﬁcult to deter- mine if a small change in the number of defects was due to heat-to-heat variations or is due only to the carbon addition. In general, very similar types and numbers of defects were observed in the two molds of the master alloy without car- bon additions (baseline), which may indicate mold-to-mold variations were limited. However, additional heats of each of the alloys would need to be processed to truly establish any statistical correlation. Therefore, all of the evaluation of defects in this study should be considered as trends. No evidence of freckles was observed in any of the alloys and only a few instances of high angle boundaries (HAB’s), mis- orientation and multiple grains emanating from the selector (multi-starts) occurred. The most common defects were low angle boundaries (LAB’s) and slivers which are generally not correlated with thermal solutal convection and carbon additions are not considered a method to prevent these types of defects.

Both molds of the master alloy (baseline) contained sig- niﬁcant numbers of LAB’s (5 of 14) and slivers (5 of 14), as seen in  Table 3 . The baseline alloy bars only exhibited one HAB, one mis-oriented bar and one multi-start bar. The addition of 0.01 wt.% carbon resulted in a signiﬁcant reduc- tion in the number of slivers (5 or 6 versus 1). The lowest level of carbon additions, though, did not appear to have any signiﬁcant impact on the number of LAB’s, misorientation or multi-start defects since similar number of these types of defects were observed in the mold of 0.01 wt.% samples and the baseline alloy samples. A slight increase in the number of HAB’s (1 versus 2) was noted. Increasing the carbon con- tent further to 0.050 wt.%, resulted in the complete elimina- tion of slivers. Although only a few instances of misorien- tation and multi-start defects were observed in the baseline alloy and the 0.010 wt.% carbon alloy, no evidence of these types of defects was observed in the 0.050 wt.% carbon al- loy. The number of LAB’s did not appear to be reduced by the carbon addition in this alloy, and the number of HAB’s (3) was observed to increase in the 0.050 wt.% carbon alloy.  

The addition of 0.10 wt.% carbon to the baseline alloy, resulted in a nearly complete elimination of all defects. Only one LAB was observed in one single crystal bar in this alloy. Further increasing the carbon addition to 0.150 wt.% resulted in a slight increase in the number of slivers, while the number of LAB’s remained unchanged.  

The effect of carbon on the number of solidiﬁcation de- fects, as a function of bar size, was also evaluated ( Table 3 ). Since the number of bar processed in each mold was not the same for each bar size, the number of defects that occurred in the bars were examined on the basis of percentages. The percentage of the bars with defects was observed to increase with increasing bar diameter. The effectiveness of the car- bon additions to reduce defects appeared to be greater in the larger diameter bars, since the percentage of defects in the larger diameter single crystal bars decreased with increas- ing carbon content more rapidly than the bars with small diameters.  

Although no freckles were formed in the alloys used in this study, Tin  [4]  showed that, in similar alloys, freckle for- mation does decrease with increasing carbon content in sin- gle crystal nickel base superalloys. According to Tin  [4] , the carbon addition altered the segregation behavior of alloying elements and results in reduced segregation. As a result, the driving force for liquid circulation during casting, or ther- mal solutal convection, in the mushy zone was suppressed and, consequently, the frequency of freckle formation was reduced.

# 3.3. Solidiﬁcation segregation and partitioning  

The model alloy, LMSX-1, used in this study has a number of different elements which segregate during solidiﬁcation either to the dendrite core or to the interdendritic regions.  

The partitioning coefﬁcient,  k ′ , deﬁned as the composi- tion (wt.%) of the dendrite core divided by the composi- tion (wt.%) of the interdendritic region, was measured for each element using the discrete point measurement tech- nique ( Fig. 4 ). The partitioning coefﬁcient,  k ′ , equals unity (1.0) when there was no segregation observed. In addition, the degree of segregation increased as the calculated value for  k ′   became either larger or smaller than unity. Elements such as rhenium, tungsten, cobalt, and chromium segregated to the dendrite core, therefore, the partitioning coefﬁcient ( k ′ ) for these elements were greater than 1.0. It can also be seen that Re segregated more strongly than W since the k ′ (Re) was signiﬁcantly greater than the measured  k ′ (W). El- ements such as tantalum, aluminum, and nickel segregated to the interdendritic region and therefore exhibited  k ′   values less than 1.0. Note, that Ta segregated to a greater degree to the interdendritic region than any other element since it ex- hibited the lowest  k ′   value. In general, apart from rhenium, the effective partitioning coefﬁcient of the elements did not change signiﬁcantly with the addition of carbon to the alloy.  

![](images/2443d166e644f0df87fa5db6e3cb98834e072acc326d580321ff05eb3fb2a69a.jpg)  
Fig. 5. Microprobe line scan measurements of tantalum for 0.0, 0.01, 0.05, 0.10, and 0.15 wt.% carbon, respectively. Dendrite core is in the middle of the line scan.  

However, although it has been reported  [4]  that carbon ad- ditions reduced the degree of Re segregation in other model alloys, the Re segregation measured in the LMSX-1 alloys in this study did not systematically decrease for increased car- bon contents. In the LMSX-1 alloys, as carbon was added, the Re segregation ﬁrst decreased slightly, then increased slightly and then returned to a level similar to the 0 wt.% car- bon baseline alloy. However, no systematic variations in the Re portioning coefﬁcients were observed. The variations that were observed may be from experimental or alloy-to-alloy variations.  

The other technique that was used to measure the par- titioning coefﬁcient utilized a line scan from through the dendrite. The result was a series of curves with minimums or maximums occurring in the center of the dendrite, de- pending on the segregation behavior of the element. The line scan results for only tantalum, in each of the 5 alloys, are shown in  Fig. 5 , where the concentration of Ta is at a minimum at the dendrite core, and the resulting  k ′   is less than one. For each of the elements in these alloys, the parti- tioning coefﬁcients were calculated by dividing the average minimum/maximum value of the line scan by the nominal weight percent composition of that element in the alloy. The results of these measurements are shown in  Fig. 6 , as a func- tion of carbon content. Note, that elements that segregate to the dendrite core, will exhibit a maximum  k ′   at the center of the dendrite, so  k ′   >  1 for these elements. Conversely, ele- ments that segregate to the interdendritic region will exhibit a minimum in the partitioning coefﬁcient (k ′ ) at the dendrite core. These results are consistent with the segregation be- havior measured by the discrete point counting techniques. However, the values of the partition coefﬁcient measured by the line scan are closer to unity than those predicted by the discrete point measurement because the line scan values are normalized by the nominal composition values, not the com- position at the interdendritic region. Note here also that the segregation behavior of all of the elements in the LMSX-1 alloy did not change signiﬁcantly with carbon addition, in- cluding rhenium.  

# 3.4. Carbides  

The baseline alloy, without any intentional carbon ad- ditions, did not exhibit any evidence of carbides in the microstructure ( Table 4 ). With the addition of carbon to the alloy, MC-type carbide formed in the interdendritic re- gion. The carbides were observed in all of the alloys with intentional carbon additions and their volume fraction in- creased dramatically as the carbon level was increased. SEM micrographs of etched, transverse as-cast samples of 0.00 and 0.10 wt.% carbon are shown in  Fig. 7 . The baseline (0.00 wt.%) carbon sample exhibited large pools of  γ / γ ′ eutectic, a limited amount of porosity and no evidence of carbides. On the other hand, the 0.10 wt.% carbon samples exhibited a carbide network in the interdendritic region of the microstructure, signiﬁcant levels of porosity and no eu- tectic  γ / γ ′   regions. In general, as the carbon content was increased, the carbide volume fraction and porosity volume fraction increased, while the amount of  γ / γ ′   eutectic de- creased ( Tables 4 and 5 ). Frequently, the carbide network appeared to exhibit morphologies similar to the  γ / γ ′   eutectic.  

Polished but un-etched samples, examined using backsca- ttered SEM electron micrographs techniques ( Fig. 8 ), in- dicated that the carbide networks occupied most of the interdendritic area, particularly, at the higher carbon levels. Similar to the etched samples, the carbide networks in the interdendritic regions exhibited an appearance very similar to the   /  ′ -eutectic in the high carbon alloys. In addition, there appears to be an orientation relationship between the carbide network and the dendrite matrix since the carbides were observed to run parallel to the  γ  matrix dendrite arms of the Ni-base alloy matrix.

# 3.5. Differential thermal analysis

The  γ ′ -solvus temperature was observed at about 1300   ◦ C in the baseline (0 wt.%) alloy following solution heat treatment and decreased with in- creasing carbon content. Similar to the as-cast samples, no carbide solvus temperature was observed in the baseline (0 wt.%) alloy following heat treatment. All alloys with in- tentional carbon additions, including the 0.01 wt.% carbon alloy, exhibited a carbide solvus temperature that generally increased with increasing carbon content.  

# 4. Discussions  

# 4.1. Partitioning and segregation  

Some solidiﬁcation defects (e.g., slivers and freckles) ob- served in directionally solidiﬁed alloys have been attributed to thermal solutal convection due to elemental partitioning. During solidiﬁcation, the segregation of the solute elements, such as Al, and Ti, which are rejected to the interdendritic region and elements, such as W and Re, which segregated to the dendrite core resulting in the interdendritic liquid having an average density lower than that of the liquid alloy above it. This density difference causes the lower density liquid at the bottom of the interdendritic region to ﬂow up to the top of the mushy zone, breaking off or melting off dendrite tips in this process. The fractured/melted dendrite tips can then act as nuclei for chains of equiaxed grains, known as freck- les  [2,8] . Tantalum additions to the alloy offset the density difference and suppress the thermal solutal convections as it segregates to the interdendritic region during solidiﬁcation.  

Casting defects including freckles are affected by the so- lidiﬁcation conditions and sample size. It was reported that the number of freckle line defects usually increases with in- creasing bar diameter  [2] , and decreased thermal gradient and growth rate  [1] . The tendency to form these types of so- lidiﬁcation defects also depends on the difference between solidus and liquidus temperatures of the alloy, the local so- lidiﬁcation time, and the gradient in the mushy zone. No evidence of freckles was observed in any of the samples uti- lized in this study. Perhaps the thermal gradient used during the single crystal processing in this study was great enough to suppress the formation of freckles.  

Additions of carbon to the model alloy used in this study resulted in a reduction of all types of casting defects in agree- ment with other studies  [4,5] . While the current model alloy did not suffer from freckle formation at all carbon levels in- cluding 0.00 wt.% carbon, Tin  [4]  have reported a drop in freckle formation with carbon additions. This reduction in freckle formation was linked with a change in segregation behavior of alloying elements in the material. Tin et al.  [3] reported that the addition of the carbon to the alloy had re- sulted in the modiﬁcation of the segregation coefﬁcients of alloying elements to values closer to one, which indicated a reduction in the solidiﬁcation partitioning. This reduced segregation would result in a reduction in the density differ- ence between the interdendritic liquid and the liquid above it during directional solidiﬁcation and, consequently, reduced freckle formation.  

# 4.2. Carbides formation  

Additions of carbon to the model alloy used in this study resulted in the formation of a carbide phase in the interden- dritic region of the structure. Microprobe and TEM analysis revealed that this phase was a Ta-rich MC-type carbide ( Fig. 12 ). Similar results have been reported in other model alloys  [4] . Backscattered SEM images of the carbide phase ( Fig. 8 ) in cross-sectional samples of the alloy indicated that these carbides form ﬁne dendritic-like structures. Although the equilibrium morphology of carbides is the octahedron, or blocky-type shape, based on the minimum surface en- ergy  [6] , most of the carbides formed in the model alloy were of script-like morphology. The thermal gradient dur- ing processing of the single crystals was calculated to be 30–40   ◦ C/cm and the withdrawal rate was 20 cm/h. Consid- ering the relatively high withdrawal rate and high thermal gradient during solidiﬁcation, the depth of the mushy zone and the time for solidiﬁcation would be expected to be relatively small. As the matrix of  γ  and  γ ′   formed in the dendrite, the carbon and tantalum were rejected to the liquid and enriched the liquid in the carbide forming elements. The accumulation of carbide forming elements along with the high withdrawal rate and directional solidiﬁcation resulted in the formation of a complicated carbide network in the alloy. The morphology of these types of carbides has also been reported to be affected by the carbide precipitation temperature with respect to the liquidus and solidus tem- peratures of the alloy  [6] . Script-like carbides of TaC were also formed in other nickel base superalloys at a withdrawal rate of 18 cm/h  [7] . In addition, the volume fraction of script-like carbides was reported to be greater than faceted carbides at a growth rates of 18 cm/h and higher  [6] . How- ever, in this study, the withdrawal rate was not varied, but kept constant at approximately 20 cm/h, which would be expected to result in greater amounts of script carbides.  

Deep etching of a cross-sectional and longitudinal sam- ples of the high carbon alloys revealed the complicated, continuous network of dendritic carbides ( Fig. 10 ) that had formed in the interdendritic area. These carbides grew par- allel to the primary dendrites of the matrix and, as solidiﬁ- cation proceeded, these carbides grew laterally between the secondary dendrites into four directions forming the cubic dendritic carbides ( Fig. 9 ). Viewing the carbide network in the longitudinal section illustrates the spacing between these carbide sheets ( Fig. 14 ). In a SEM image of a transverse

The method used by Tin  [4]  was the 15  ×  15 point matrix measurement technique reported by Gungor [9] , while the techniques used in this study were by discrete point measurements and by line scans through the dendrite core. Although the 15  ×  15 point matrix scan method offers a concentration versus solid fraction relationship, which is obtained by arranging the points in an either ascending or descending order, based on segregation behavior, mislead- ing results could arise. Since the resulting 225 points ob- tained in the 15 × 15 point matrix are random, compositions obtained from the points do not necessarily represent all stages of solidiﬁcation (e.g., the dendrite core and the inter- dendritic  γ / γ ′   eutectic structure). In addition, point to point ﬂuctuations because of the coarse  γ ′   precipitate that formed during cooling after directional solidiﬁcation can cause dis- crepancies  [10] . Line scans and discrete point measurements do not experience such problems, since the analyses were conﬁned to areas of the microstructure that are intentionally selected to include the compositions that represent all stages of solidiﬁcation and all degrees of segregation.  

Additional possible causes for this disagreement in the partitioning coefﬁcient results from this study and those pre- viously reported  [4]  include the spot size used for the micro- probe analysis and the chemical analysis technique. If the spot size is similar to or smaller than that of the  γ ′   size then misleading results of elements concentration can be mea- sured. For example, if the composition of only the  γ ′   within the  γ / γ ′   microstructure were measured, an artiﬁcially high level of Ta, Al, Ti and Ni would be reported. A larger size beam that simultaneously covers both  γ  and  γ ′   phases would give an average composition. Lastly, the differences in par- titioning results may be due to the use of different chemical analysis techniques (i.e., wavelength dispersive spectroscopy (WDS) versus energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS)) used in these studies. WDS typically offers better resolution in terms of quantitative analysis over EDS  [11] . The work by Tin  [4]  was based on EDS system while the current work utilizes WDS for measuring elemental concentrations.  

The mechanism for the reduction in solidiﬁcation defects in the single crystals with increasing carbon content is not well understood. It has been reported that there is a change of solidiﬁcation behavior of elements, such as Ta and Re, occurs at the point of solidiﬁcation where carbides start to precipitate  [4] . While the precipitation of TaC from the Ta-enriched interdendritic liquid will certainly effect the composition of the liquid, the segregation of the Ta, as well as the other elements in the alloys, during the initial stages of solidiﬁcation to the liquid does not appear to be effected ( Figs. 4–6 ). Therefore, the reduction in the solidiﬁcation defects does not appear to be due to a reduction in the segregation behavior of the elements in the LMSX-1 al- loys. Furthermore, the precipitation of the TaC also results in an effective reduction of tantalum in the interdendritic liquid, as the carbide phase forms. As solidiﬁcation pro- ceeds, the concentration of Ta in the interdendritic region initially increases as the Ta is rejected into the liquid. As the temperature decreases, TaC carbides form and the con- centration of Ta in the mushy zone will decrease, which would be expected to result in a decrease in the density of the interdendritic liquid. Since the Ta segregation to the interdendritic liquid has been reported to result in the re- duction of solidiﬁcation defects  [12–14] , the precipitation of TaC carbide, which results in a reduction in Ta content, would be expected to result in a greater tendency for ther- mal solutal convection. Auburtin et al.  [15]  also reported that the precipitation of carbides (TaC) in the interdendritic area would result in increased density inversion due to re- moval of Ta from the interdendritic liquid. Therefore, the formation of the TaC would be expected to result in an increase in the tendency for thermal solutal convection and the resulting defects. However, in this study, a decrease in the number of defects formed was observed with increasing carbon content, which is similar to reports in the literature [3–5] .  

Since the carbon addition to the LMSX-1 alloy did not sig- niﬁcantly change the segregation behavior of the elements, and the precipitation of the TaC would not be expected to decrease the density inversion driving force for thermal so- lutal convection, another mechanism is needed to describe the decrease in solidiﬁcation defects observed in the carbon containing alloys in the study. The reduction in solidiﬁca- tion defect formation due to carbon addition may be mainly attributed to the physical presence of intricate networks of tantalum carbide in the interdendritic region. The TaC sig- niﬁcantly reduced the permeability of the interdendritic area which results in a suppression the ﬂuid ﬂow inversion caused by the density inversion. For this mechanism to be effective, the precipitation of carbides must occur earlier, during so- lidiﬁcation, than the development of the density inversion, which causes freckle formation. Tin  [4]  also brieﬂy dis- cussed this mechanism for reduced freckle formation in car- bon containing alloys. Since no change in segregation and a decrease in the solidiﬁcation defects was observed with the carbon addition in the LMSX-1 alloy, the presence of the carbides must be restricting the ﬂuid ﬂow in the interden- dritic region and essentially reducing the “effective” primary dendrite arm spacing. A reduction in the primary dendrite arm spacing has been reported to result in a decrease in the tendency to form thermal solutal convection related defects [3,4,12,13,14,16] . In addition, further support for the car- bides obstruction of ﬂuid ﬂow in the interdendritic channels can be seen in the increased amount of porosity observed in the interdendritic region with increasing carbon content ( Table 5 ). Since the carbide networks prevent liquid ﬂow, the interdendritic liquid is unable to ﬁll in the shrinkage porosity as the last liquid solidiﬁes in the interdendritic re- gions. Although some porosity is observed in all of the in- terdendritic regions of the samples in this study, the volume fraction clearly increased with increasing carbon content, and, therefore, increased carbide volume fraction ( Tables 4 and 5 ).  

# 5. Conclusions  

•  Addition of carbon to Ni-based single crystal model alloy

 (LMSX-1) reduces the solidiﬁcation defect formation in the single crystals. •  Carbon addition does not alter the segregation behavior of alloying elements in the cast alloy, as indicated by the partitioning coefﬁcient measurements. •  TaC, carbides form an orientation relationship with the   matrix as revealed by the TEM diffraction pattern. This relationship is not unexpected as both of the phases have FCC structure. •  The physical presence of continuous carbide network in the interdendritic region prevents the ﬂuid ﬂow instability caused by difference in the density between liquid in the mushy zone and the liquid above it. This will result in a reduction in the solidiﬁcation defects formed and increase the level of porosity in the single crystal samples.
