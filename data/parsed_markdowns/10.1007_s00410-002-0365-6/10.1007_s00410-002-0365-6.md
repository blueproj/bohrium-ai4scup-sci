# Water solubility in orthopyroxene  

Received: 3 October 2001 / Accepted: 15 March 2002 / Published online: 9 May 2002   Springer-Verlag 2002  

Abstract  The solubility of water in pure enstatite was measured on samples synthesized at 1,100   C and pres- sures to 100 kbar. Enstatite crystals were grown under water-saturated conditions from a stoichiometric mix- ture of high-purity SiO 2  and Mg(OH) 2 . Water contents were calculated from polarized FTIR spectra measured on oriented single crystals. The water solubility in ort- hoenstatite increases with pressure to 867±35 ppm H 2 O by weight at 75 kbar. At 100 kbar, in the stability ﬁeld of high-clinoenstatite, a water solubility of 714±35 ppm was observed. The water solubility in enstatite at 1,100   C can be described by the equation c H2O = Af H2O  exp(–P D V/RT), where  f H2O  is water fugacity,  D 3 A=0.0204 ppm/bar and V=12.3 cm /mol. The infra- red spectra of the hydrous enstatite crystals show a –1   sharp, intense band at 3,363 cm and a broad, weaker –1 band at 3,064 cm . Both bands are strongly polarized parallel c. Most likely, pairs of protons attached to non- 2+ bridging oxygen atoms substitute for Mg . In order to investigate the eﬀect of chemical impurities on water solubility in enstatite, an additional series of experiments was carried out with gels doped with Al, B, or Li as starting material. Whereas, the presence of Li and B had no detectable eﬀect on water solubility, the addition of about 1 wt% Al 2 O 3  increased water solubility in ensta- tite from 199 to 1,100 ppm at 1,100  C and 15 kbar. In the infrared spectra of these aluminous samples, addi- –1 tional bands occur in the range from 3,450 to 3,650 cm . Similar bands are also observed in natural, aluminous orthopyroxenes and are most likely caused by protons coupled with Al according to the substitution of 3+ +   4+ Al +H for Si . A series of hydrous annealing ex- periments on a natural, gem-quality aluminous enstatite from Tanzania yielded water solubilities generally con- sistent with the results from the synthetic model systems. The results presented here imply that pure enstatite has a similar storage capacity for water as olivine; however, aluminous orthopyroxenes in the mantle may dissolve much larger amounts of water comparable with the en- tire mass of the present hydrosphere. Moreover, the mechanism of aluminum substitution in orthopyroxenes, i.e., the distribution of Al between tetrahedral and octahedral sites, may be a potential probe of water fugacity.  

# Introduction  

Nominally anhydrous minerals from mantle xenoliths always contain traces of water detectable by infrared spectroscopy (Bell and Rossman 1992; Rossman 1996; Ingrin and Skogby 2000). According to several careful studies, at least part of this water is dissolved as hy- droxyl point defect in the host minerals (e.g., Wilkins and Sabine 1973; Bell and Rossman 1992; Rossman 1996). While natural samples usually contain water only at the ppm level, experimental studies have demon- strated solubilities up to 0.1 wt% in olivine and up to several wt% in wadsleyite and ringwoodite, the high-pressure polymorphs of Mg 2 SiO 4  (Smyth 1994; Kohlstedt et al. 1996).  

The observation of a signiﬁcant storage capacity for water in mantle minerals has several important impli- cations. Firstly, the mantle might provide a large reser- voir of water, with water being exchanged over geologic time between this reservoir and the surface of the Earth (Lu and Keppler 1997). Secondly, the physical proper- ties of mantle minerals and the bulk mantle may be af- fected by the trace amounts of water dissolved in the crystal structure of minerals. In particular, transport properties such as viscosity and electrical conductivity may be very sensitive even to traces of OH (Mackwell et al. 1985; Karato 1990).  

The investigation of mantle xenoliths can only pro- vide lower limits of the actual water content of the mantle because large fractions of water may have es- caped upon decompression (Ingrin and Skogby 2000). Estimates of water concentrations from seismic data should, in principle, be possible (Wood 1995), but they are hampered by the dependence of seismic velocities on numerous physical and chemical parameters that cannot be controlled independently. In this situation, experi- mental measurements of maximum water solubilities in major mantle minerals are of fundamental importance as they provide an upper limit of the water storage ca- pacity of the mantle. Moreover, data on water parti- tioning between mantle minerals can be extracted from solubility data. These partition coeﬃcients allow the calculation of the equilibrium distribution of water be- tween various mantle reservoirs (Bolfan-Casanova et al. 2000).  

Next to olivine, pyroxenes are the most important constituents of the upper mantle. Investigations of mantle xenoliths usually show much higher water con- centrations in pyroxenes than in olivine or garnet (Bell and Rossman 1992; Rossman 1996; Ingrin and Skogby 2000). However, there are virtually no experimental data on water solubility in pyroxenes. This may partially be because pyroxenes with their numerous possible chemi- cal substitutions are much more complicated systems than, for example, olivine solid solutions. Accordingly, reliable estimates of water solubility in pyroxenes cannot be obtained from simple model systems alone, but they require a systematic study of the eﬀect of chemical im- purities on defect chemistry, water solubility and water speciation.  

The present study focuses on orthopyroxenes. Mantle orthopyroxenes are usually rich in enstatite components, with FeO and Al 2 O 3  being important minor constituents (Boyd and Meyer 1979). Models for the location of OH in orthopyroxenes have been derived from polarized infrared absorption measurements (Beran 1976; Beran and Zemann 1986). Here we report experimental data on water solubility in the pure MgSiO 3 –H 2 O system as well as on the eﬀect of Al, Li and B on water solubility. In these experiments, pyroxenes were grown under water- saturated conditions from oxide and hydroxide mixtures or gels. The investigation of systems containing light elements such as Li and B was motivated by the obser- vation of unusually high water contents in natural B-rich olivines, which are probably caused by the substitution 3+ +   4+   of B +H for Si (Sykes et al. 1994). Moreover, annealing experiments on natural, gem-quality enstatite crystals were carried out under water-saturated condi- tions.

Three diﬀerent types of starting materials were used: oxide/hy- droxide mixtures, gels, and natural, gem-quality enstatite crystals from Tanzania.

In most experiments with these natural enstatite crystals, the oxygen fugacity was buﬀered by adding Ni foil and some NiO to the capsule. Capsules were checked for leaks before and after the experiments. Capsules with unusual weight losses were discarded. Additional experimental details are given in Rauch (2000).  

# Investigation of run products  

After the experiments with gels or oxide-hydroxide mixtures, the samples were cut lengthwise. One halve of the sample was polished down to a doubly polished slab of 100- to 200- l m thickness. This slab was used for unpolarized FTIR measurements as well as for microprobe analyses and for phase identiﬁcation by microscopy. The other halve of the sample was used for X-ray powder diﬀrac- tion and as a source of single crystals. Usually, it was possible to obtain some clear single crystals up to 100  l m in size from this part of the sample and to orient them under the microscope. After an- nealing experiments with natural enstatite, the single crystals were recovered from the capsules and repolished. Microprobe analyses were carried out using a Cameca SX50 electron microprobe oper- ating at 15 kV and 15 nA. Synthetic enstatite was used as standard for Mg and Si, MgAl 2 O 4  spinel was used as a standard for Al.  

# Infrared spectroscopy  

Near infrared spectra were measured using a Bruker IFS 120 HR Fourier-transform infrared spectrometer coupled to an all-reﬂect- ing infrared microscope (Bruker IRscope I). This spectrometer contains a permanently aligned Michelson-type interferometer with 30   angle of incidence on the beam-splitter. A tungsten light source was used together with a Si-coated CaF 2  beam-splitter and a nar- row-band MCT (mercury cadmium telluride) detector. Four hun- dred scans were usually acquired for one spectrum. Polarized spectra were recorded using a wire-strip type polarizor on a KRS-5 substrate. Samples were usually placed on a CaF 2  plate and im- mersed in poly-trich l oro u oro ethylene oil in order to reduce in- terference fringes. During the measurements, the optics of the spectrometer was kept under vacuum and the microscope was purged with a continuous stream of puriﬁed air in order to avoid absorption bands caused by water vapor.  

Water contents were calculated from the infrared spectra, using both the frequency-dependent calibration of extinction coeﬃcients by Paterson (1982) and the independently calibrated extinction coeﬃcients of orthopyroxene by Bell et al. (1995). When the former calibration was used, the entire infrared absorbance spectrum A( m ) was divided by the frequency dependent extinction coeﬃcient   ( m ) as deﬁned by Paterson and the water content was then determined by numerical integration of the resulting function A( m )/  ( m ) over the entire frequency range where absorption caused by OH was ob- served. The calibration by Libowitzky and Rossman (1997) yields similar values. Whenever possible, polarized spectra measured parallel to the three main axes of the indicatrix were used to de- termine water contents. In a few cases, such measurements were not possible because of the poor quality of the crystals in the run products. Water contents were then estimated from averages of several unpolarized measurements on various grains of random orientation using an orientation factor of 1/3 (Paterson 1982). Model calculations (Rauch 2000) show that major errors in the unpolarized measurements as a result of saturation of absorption in one direction of polarization (Libowitzky and Rossman 1996) are not to be expected under the conditions of this study because of the small absorbances observed and because most OH bands in orth- opyroxenes have components in more than one direction of the indicatrix. Indeed, polarized and unpolarized measurements on the same sample always yielded comparable water concentrations.  

The infrared microscope proved to be essential for producing meaningful measurements. Apertures in the rear focal plane of the 15 X Cassegranian objective allowed spectra to be taken from spots with diameters of typically 20–60  l m. The spectra were very dif- ferent depending on whether they were taken from optically clear single crystals or from milky parts of the sample (Fig. 1). Although clear single crystals yielded spectra with sharp and strongly po- larized absorption bands, the same bands were superimposed on broad, isotropic absorption bands when spectra were taken from milky parts of the sample. Accordingly, measurements through milky spots always yielded much higher water contents than those observed on clear single crystals. This excess water is most likely a result of ﬂuid inclusions and water on grain boundaries, disloca- tions, and planar defects (Keppler and Rauch 2000). In order to determine the true point defect solubility, only measurements on optically perfectly clear single crystals were used.

One possible solution mechanism that would generate OH groups in the pyroxene structure would be the direct 2+   replacement of Mg by two protons. In order to test this possibility, two samples of hydrous enstatite with diﬀerent water contents were carefully analyzed by electron microprobe. A sample with only 55 ppm water synthesized at 2 kbar (ET5) yielded a nearly perfectly stoichiometric composition Mg 2.007±0.008 Si 1.996±0.004 O 6 . However, a sample containing 637 ppm water pre- pared at 40 kbar (En11) yielded a composition of Mg 1.988±0.002 Si 2.006±0.001 O 6  with a slight deﬁciency in magnesium. This would be consistent with a direct 2+   + substitution of Mg by 2H , because 637 ppm water correspond to 0.014 atoms H per formula unit.  

The position and orientation of the OH groups in the pyroxene structure is further constrained by the fre- quency and polarization of the OH bands. According to Nakamoto et al. (1955) and to Libowitzky (1999) the frequency of the main –1   OH band observed at 3,363 cm corresponds to an OH...O distance of 2.8 A. # Effect of Li, B, and Al on the water solubility in enstatite  

A series of experiments was carried out to study the eﬀect of chemical impurities, such as Li, B, and Al, on water solubility in enstatite. Experimental results are compiled in Table 6 and Fig. 8. Because of the relatively poor quality of the crystals obtained, polarized measurements on oriented single crystals were not possible. Water contents, therefore, were calculated from unpolarized measurements, which yield somewhat larger errors.  

Doping the starting material with B or Li has little apparent eﬀect on water solubility. There is no system- atic correlation of water content with dopant concen- tration and within the relatively large errors of the unpolarized measurements, the water contents are comparable with those observed in undoped material. This result may be partially because Li and B did not enter the pyroxene structure, but remained in the ﬂuid phase during crystallization. Support for this hypothesis comes from some ICP-AES analyses of the run prod- ucts. # Water solubility in a natural enstatite crystal  

Some hydrous annealing experiments were carried out with a natural single crystal of aluminous, iron-bearing enstatite from Tanzania. In most runs, the oxygen fu- gacity was buﬀered by the Ni–NiO buﬀer. The results are compiled in Table 8. Although the crystals were found to be homogeneous in water content after the experiments, it cannot be ruled out that in some runs equilibrium was not yet completely reached. However, the water solubilities reported in Table 8 are generally comparable with those observed in the synthetic model systems. Moreover, the infrared spectra in Fig. 10 are generally similar to those observed for synthetic, alu- minous enstatite samples. The presence of iron, there- fore, does not appear to have a major eﬀect on water solubility.  

In order to investigate the eﬀect of oxygen fugacity on water solubility, one experiment was carried out in an iron capsule at Fe–FeO buﬀer conditions. Compared to the run at Ni–NiO buﬀer conditions at the same P and T, only a slight increase in water solubility was observed (Table 8, runs Ke2 and Kfee1). Some small diﬀerences are also visible in the infrared spectrum (Fig. 10b), most –1 notably an increase of the band close to 3,400 cm . This is entirely consistent with the previously discussed band –1   assignments. The band close to 3,400 cm should be a result of protons located near a vacant Mg-site. Because 3+   Fe is most likely also located on the Mg-site, reduc- 3+   2+   tion of Fe to Fe by the Fe–FeO buﬀer should lead to the incorporation of additional hydrogen atoms on the same site for charge compensation. In general, however, the eﬀect oxygen fugacity on water solubility appears to be only minor.  

# Geological implications  

The water solubility in pure MgSiO 3  enstatite is roughly comparable to the water solubility in olivine (Kohlstedt et al. 1996). However, as the solubility increases drasti- cally with aluminum content, aluminous orthopyroxene is likely to be the major host of water in the upper mantle, despite the fact that the modal abundance of orthopyroxene is lower than the abundance of olivine. In the present study, only compositions up to about 1 wt% Al 2 O 3  were investigated. In a fertile mantle peridotite, the aluminum content in the pyroxenes is controlled by the equilibrium with an aluminous phase, such as plagioclase, spinel, or garnet. Because alumina activity indirectly controls water solu- bility, the transitions from plagioclase to spinel to garnet peridotite should also involve discontinuities in water content of the pyroxenes. The water storage capacity, therefore, may be distinctly layered, even within the upper mantle.  

Wood (1995) estimated the water content of the up- per mantle from the seismic data on the width of the 410-km discontinuity. This discontinuity is believed to arise from the transformation of olivine to wadsleyite. As water is known to partition strongly into wadsleyite (Kohlstedt et al. 1996), it aﬀects the equilibrium between wadsleyite and olivine and, therefore, the width of the discontinuity. Wood (1995) estimated a water content of 200 ppm in olivine close to the discontinuity and a water content of 0.2 wt% in the coexisting wadsleyite. These data were believed to be representative for the bulk water content of the upper mantle and the transition zone close to the phase boundary. However, if alumi- nous orthopyroxene strongly concentrates water in equilibrium with olivine, much higher bulk water con- tents would be consistent with the seismic data.  

Measurements of mantle xenoliths probably give the most direct information on water contents in the mantle. However, because of the fast diﬀusion of hydrous spe- cies, it is likely that many of these xenoliths have lost a signiﬁcant part of their original water during the de- compression upon ascent to the surface (Ingrin and Skogby 2000). The results presented in this study suggest that the distribution of Al between diﬀerent positions in the pyroxene lattice may be a sensitive probe of the original water content of a pyroxene and of the water fugacity prevailing during its crystallization. This can be seen in the following way. Normally, orthopyroxene contains aluminum as Tschermak’s component Mg- Al Al SiO 6 , where Al substitutes in equal amounts on the tetrahedral and octahedral sites.
