# The effect of crystallographic texture on columnar grain growth in commercial pure iron during directional annealing

a  Joint Laboratory of Nanostructured Materials and Technology, Nanjing University of Science and Technology, Nanjing 210094, PR China b  State Key Lab for Advanced Metals and Materials, USTB, Beijing 100083, PR China

# Abstract  

The inﬂuence of crystallographic texture on the columnar grain growth in commercial pure iron during directional annealing has been investigated. The crystallographic texture hinders the column arg rain growth due to the high propensity to form low angle or twin boundaries between the column ar grains and the main recrystallized textures at the front of the columnar grains when the columnar grains grow along the direction of the temperature gradient in highly textured materials.

# 1. Introduction  

Directional annealing can be used to produce columnar grain structures that have grain boundaries roughly parallel to the direction of temperature gradient  [1–10] . Previous studies [11–13]  have shown that the microstructural evolution during directional annealing is affected dramatically by the process- ing parameters, such as hot zone velocity/drawing velocity, hot zone temperature, temperature gradient, degree of deformation of the materials and crystallographic textures. And a variety of modeling techniques have been applied to the simulation of microstructural evolution during directional annealing. Godfrey and Martin  [14]  used the Monte-Carlo method (Potts model) to simulate zone annealing of highly textured materials. Using the front-tracking model, Badmos et al.  [15]  simulated the columnar grain growth during directional annealing. The effects of the ini- tial microstructure, hot zone velocity, and hot zone width were investigated for a single-phase system without a texture. Using the same model, they have also investigated the microstruc- tural evolution during directional annealing by varying the grain boundary energy and mobility  [16] .  

The crystallographic textures, however, has a different effect on the microstructural evolution for different materials. Li and Baker  [17]  reported that during directional annealing of 90% cold-rolled polycrystalline nickel, columnar grains or single crystals could be produced by directional secondary recrystal- lization under suitable processing parameters and the columnar grains typically have (1 2 4)[2,1, − 1] orientations, the preferred texture for secondary recrystallization. They suggested that the columnar grains were produced by secondary recrystallization via oriented grain growth because the (1 2 4)[2,1, − 1]-oriented grains formed high-angle, random boundaries, and thus, high- mobility boundaries. However, there are some disagreements as to the inﬂuence of the texture on the microstructure evolu- tion. In the study of this process in commercial pure iron  [18] , a striking feature has been observed. The columnar grains with the random texture have the larger aspect ratio than those with preferred orientations. Using Monte-Carlo simulation, Holm et al.  [19]  showed that while the presence of crystallographic tex- ture may enhance columnar growth, a texture is not necessary for columnar grain growth when the processing parameters are properly chosen. Badmos et al.  [16] , using the front-tracking model, drew the conclusion that anisotropy boundary properties hinder development of columnar grain structures in randomly textured, two-dimensional polycrystalline systems.  

Recently, we have reported on the effects of the process- ing parameters on the microstructural evolution of directionally recrystallized, cold-rolled commercial pure iron and drew the conclusion that the columnar grain structure was developed by abnormal grain growth of the grains which had undergone the primary recrystallization  [18] . Based on the experimental evidence, we suggested that the columnar grain growth is deter- mined by grain boundary mobility anisotropy, which resulted from the temperature gradient during directional annealing. The selective growth of columnar grains during directional anneal- ing was explained by the mobility advantage of the high-angle coincidence site lattice (CSL) boundaries over the general high- angle boundaries and low angle or twin boundaries. In this paper, we investigate the effect of crystallographic texture on the microstructural evolution of commercial pure iron during directional annealing.

A grain boundary can be categorized as a low angle boundary (LAB), a coincidence site lattice (CSL) boundary, a twin bound- ary (CSL with  Σ  = 3) or, if none of these, a general or random boundary. Twins, LABs and some other CSLs have markedly different mobilities from general boundaries. Therefore, they affect grain growth differently via the well-know relationship of V  =  PM , where  V  is the grain boundary migration rate,  P  the driv- ing force and  M  is the boundary mobility. In our previous paper [18] , we observed that columnar grain growth is determined by grain boundary mobility anisotropy, which results from the tem- perature gradient during directional annealing. The preferential migration of high-angle CSL boundaries along the direction of temperature gradient is attributed to their higher mobility than general high-angle boundaries because they are less inhibited by impurities. The most striking feature in the growth of columnar grains is the formation of twin and low angle boundaries between the island grains and the matrix columnar grains. These special grain boundaries hardly move because of their small mobility.  

Our working hypothesis of the block of the crystallographic texture on the columnar grain growth is based on the high propensity of forming low angle or twin boundary during the columnar grains growing along the direction of the tempera- ture gradient. When the hot zone was applied to the end of the specimen, the cold-rolled commercial pure iron underwent primary recrystallization to a ﬁne grain structure. For the spec- imen cold-rolled for 85% of thickness reduction, this ﬁne grain structure has a recrystallized texture including (1 1 0)[ − 1,1,2], (1 1 0)[1, − 1,0] components. At the commencement of the grain growth, these ﬁne grains started to grow by normal grain growth. At this stage, grains have a rather equiaxed shape as is normal for normal grain growth in this type of material. After normal grain growth some grains in the main texture components are larger than average. Hutchinson and Nes  [21]  reported the sim- ilar results in steel, Cu and Al. Therefore, with the movement of the specimen, this kind of grains with low angle boundaries or twin boundaries with respect to their neighbors also have an opportunity to grow and form a columnar grain along the direc- tion of temperature gradient due to the size advantage, although mostofthegrains,which can grow and form column ar grains,are those with high-angle CSL boundaries by growth selection. In a certain range, the stronger the recrystallized texture the higher the frequency of this kind of grains growing. With the columnar grains propagating along the direction of the temperature gra- dient, at the front of columnar grains, the tips of the columnar grains grow abnormally at the expense of their small neighbors. It is obvious that there is a much higher probability for these columnar grains, which developed in the main recrystallized texture component, to encounter the small grains having twin or low angle boundaries because this kind of columnar grains have the similar orientation with the main recrystallized texture. As a result, the columnar grain growth was hindered.  

# 4. Conclusion  

The aspect ratio of columnar grains produced at the optimum drawing velocity decreases with increasing degree of deforma- tion during directionally annealing commercial pure iron. The inﬂuence of the degree of deformation on the columnar grain growth is attributed to the crystallographic texture. For commer- cial pure iron, the presence of crystallographic texture hinders the columnar grain growth. In highly textured commercial pure iron, there is a much higher probability of forming low angle or twin boundaries during the columnar grains growing along the direction of the temperature gradient. In consequence, the columnar grain growth was hindered.
