# INTRODUCTION  

The mammalian target of rapamycin (mTOR) is a highly conserved eukaryotic serine/threonine protein kinase belonging to the ph os phat idyl in osi to l 3-kinase-related kinases (PIKKs) family, which plays an essential role in regulating cell growth, proliferation, metabolism, migration, autophagy, and apopto- sis. ,   mTOR is located downstream of receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs) and the ph os phat idyl in osi to l 3-kinase (PI3K) signaling pathway. When RTKs are coupled with the corresponding ligands (insulin and insulin-like growth factor (IGF-1)), PI3K is activated to catalyze the phosphorylation of ph os phat idyl in osi- tol (4,5)-biphosphate into phosphatidylinositol (3,4,5)-triphos- phate, which functions as the second messenger to further activate protein kinase B (Akt) and ph os phat idyl in osi to l- dependent kinase 1 (PDK1). Activated Akt phosphorylates tuberous sclerosis complex 1/2 and ultimately activates mTOR. mTOR is recognized to form two distinct polyprotein complexes: mTOR complex 1 (mTORC1) and mTOR complex 2 (mTORC2). mTORC1 is also known as a rapamycin-sensitive protein, coordinating with the auxiliary protein Raptor to phosphorylate diverse substrates, including ribosomal protein S6 kinase (S6K) and eukaryotic translation initiation factor 4E binding protein 1 (4EBP1).   mTORC2 is characterized as a rapamycin-insensitive protein, consisting of mTOR, Protor, Rictor, Deptor, mLST8, and mSin1, which phosphorylates and activates Akt in response to cellular stimulation.   In general, mTORC1 is a key regulator of cellular precancerous effects, whereas mTORC2 plays a crucial role in regulating the Akt survival pathway. The overactivation or overexpression of mTOR has been proven to be in close relation to the occurrence and development of many diseases, particularly in various malignant tumors. ,   Therefore, the development of mTOR inhibitors has long been of interest to the pharmaceutical industry.  

Heretofore, structurally diverse mTOR inhibitors have been developed and evaluated, including allosteric mTOR inhibitors (rapamycin and its derivatives, rapalogs), ATP-competitive mTOR inhibitors, and new double-target mTOR inhibitors. − 9

The most in-depth studied ATP-competitive mTOR inhibitors can potently inhibit mTORC1 and mTORC2 simultaneously, showing a higher kinase inhibitory activity than rapalogs, and several candidates, such as MLN-0128, ATG-008, BZE-235, PQR-309, and PF-05212384 (gedatolisib,  PT-1  in-house), have been propelled into phase 2 or phase 3 clinical trials ( Figure 1 ). Because of the continued interest in developing highly active and isoform-selective inhibitors based on the PI3K-Akt-mTOR signaling pathway, , − 13   our recent efforts on structure-based drug development using  PT-1  as a lead facilitated the discovery of a highly selective mTOR inhibitor  24  ( PT-88  in-house).

# RESULTS AND DISCUSSION  

Molecular Design.  The mTOR catalytic site can be divided into four distinct regions ( Figure 2 A), namely, the hinge region

 (orange), the affinity pocket (cyan), the N-lobe region

 (magenta), and the solvent-exposed area. The binding affinity pocket has been confirmed to play a key role in determining selectivity profiles in diverse proteins and lipid kinases. Notably, the hinge region has also been proven to be an important area for mTOR kinase specificity over other lipid kinases.  

To develop highly selective mTOR inhibitors,  PT-1  ( Figure 1 ), a highly potent pan-PI3K/mTOR inhibitor with a dimorpholino-substituted diaryl urea structural motif, was selected as the tool molecule to initiate a medicinal campaign. Because of the poor isoform selectivity profiles of  PT-1  among PIKKs,   a high frequency of adverse events (approximately 26%) was noticed in clinical trials, including stomatitis, transaminitis, and hyperglycemia.   To begin our structure- based development, molecular docking was carried out to investigate the interaction between  PT-1  and the p110 γ  subunit (PDB ID: 7JWE). As shown in  Figure 2 B, five key hydrogen bonds formed between the  PT-1  carbamido group and residues Asp841 and Asp836 and  PT-1  morpholine substituent and residues Val882 and Thr887. The terminal dimethylaminopi- peridine fragment in  PT-1  is oriented toward the solvent- exposed area and showed no crucial interaction with PI3K α  and mTOR proteins ( Figure 2 C,D). The sequence alignment pointed out two key residues, Gln2194 and Leu2354, located in mTOR, being distinct from the corresponding residues in PI3K α  (Asp805 and Phe930) ( Figure 2 E). In particular, the F930L replacement in the hinge region led to a deeper pocket in mTOR in comparison to PI3Ks,   which may bear a bulky substituent in this area. Based on this analysis, we speculated that an alternative structural motif occupied the solvent-exposed area and a bulky heterocycle substituent interacted with the hinge, which could favorably increase the mTOR selectivity. Therefore, two series of trisubstituted triazine derivatives were designed and synthesized.

The second round of structure − activity relationship (SAR) investigation based on compound  15g  was performed to improve further the isoform-selective inhibition of mTOR ( Table 2 ). Maintaining the diaryl urea building block and terminal iso benz of ur an one substituent, diverse  N -containing heterocycles attached to C-4 and C-6 positions have been evaluated. In general, various morpholinyl and its analogue- substituted derivatives exhibited comparable kinase inhibitory activity on mTOR with compound  15g , whereas derivatives with piperazinyl and piperidyl substituents showed a remarkable decrease in potency ( 20e − g ). Notably, the dimethylaminopi- peridine-substituted derivative  20g  showed a 35.2-fold decrease in activity against mTOR compared with  15g  (IC 50  values: 116.0 vs 3.3 nM). The chiral conformation substituents indicated no obvious influence on the kinase inhibition ( 20b  and  20c ). To our delight, bulky groups attached to the C-4/C-6 position led to an increase in isoform selectivity for mTOR ( 20a − c ,  Table 3 ), which was consistent with the assumption resulting from molecular docking analysis ( Figure 4 ). In particular, the dibridged morpholine-substituted derivative  24  ( PT-88 ) exhibited the most potent inhibitory efficiency against mTOR with an IC 50  value of 1.2 nM, which was 1.8-fold more potent than that of the control  PT-1 . Compound  PT-88  shows no obvious inhibition on other PI3K subtypes ( Table 3 ), which indicates that  PT-88  is a highly potent and isoform-selective mTOR inhibitor. According to the sequence alignment results ( Figure 2 E), the F930L replacement in the hinge region of the ATP-binding area generates a deeper pocket in mTOR in comparison to PI3Ks, which is perfect to accommodate the bulky substituent dibridged morpholine, thereby leading to a high kinase selectivity to mTOR over PI3Ks.  

To examine further the kinome-wide specificity of  PT-88 , we investigated the kinase profiling of  PT-88  at a concentration of 1.0  μ M against a panel of 196 human kinases using the Eurofins Kinase Profiling platform (KINOMEscan). As shown in  Figure 4 ,  PT-88  proved to be a highly selective mTOR inhibitor and showed no binding activity to this broad panel of kinases, including mTOR homologous family members  PIK3CA , PIK3C2B ,  PIK3CG ,  PIK3CD , and other PIKK members. The complete profiling data of  PT-88  can be found in  Table S1  of the Supporting Information.  

In Vitro Cell Viability.  To find the most promising lead compound among all of the synthesized derivatives in accordance with their kinase inhibitory activities, we detected the cellular cytotoxicity of selected compounds on six cancer cell lines (MCF-7, MDA-MB-231, HepG2, Hela, HCT-116, and CNE2) by CCK8 assays. ,   As shown in  Table 4 ,  PT-1  was used as a positive control; all tested compounds showed similar cytotoxicity against the six cancer cell lines with IC 50  at micromolar and submicromolar levels. Though nanomolar level kinase inhibitory activities were revealed, compounds  15g ,  16b , and  20b  showed no detectable anti pro life rat ive potency in most cell lines, which may be caused by their poor membrane permeability and solubility, and a large amount of solid precipitated out during the test process. Notably, the bridged morpholine-substituted derivatives  20i  and  PT-88  showed more potent anti pro life rat ive activities against various cell lines than other tested compounds. In particular, compound  PT-88 exhibited an inhibitory IC 50  value of 0.74  μ M against MCF-7, which was 2.0-fold more potent than that of positive control  PT- 1 . In addition, to evaluate the he pato to xi cities of the synthesized mTOR inhibitors, normal human hepatocyte LO2 was used to investigate the selectivity over HepG2 cells.  Table 4  shows that compounds  20i  and  PT-88  exhibited SI values of 35.3 and 3.3, respectively, which were higher than that of  PT-1  (SI value: 1.2). Considering the high occurrence of mTOR mutation or amplification in human breast cancer, the highly mTOR subtype-selective inhibitor  PT-88  was selected to conduct the following antitumor mechanism study in MCF-7 cell lines.

Molecular Dynamics Study.  To investigate the possible binding modes and selectivity of  PT-88  in mTOR, the protein crystal structures of PI3K α  (PDB: 6OAC) and mTOR (PDB: 4JT6) were used for molecular dynamics (MD) simulations, which were performed using Amber 16.  Figure 11  shows the root-mean-square-deviation (rmsd) analysis of compounds  PT- 1  and  PT-88  with PI3K α  and mTOR, and the results indicated that the simulated systems were well equilibrated. The binding free energies of  PT-1  and  PT-88  with PI3K α  and mTOR have also been calculated using mechanics-generalized Born surface area (MM-GBSA). As shown in  Table 5 , the  Δ G  values for  PT- 1 -PI3K α  and  PT-88 -PI3K α  are  − 80.91 and  − 69.52 kcal/mol, respectively, and the  Δ G  values for  PT-1 -mTOR and  PT-88 - mTOR are  − 89.84 and  − 91.05 kcal/mol, respectively. # CONCLUSIONS  

mTOR is a central signal transduction kinase located in the PI3K-Akt-mTOR signaling pathway. It plays a crucial role in the process of oncogenesis. The high homology of the protein sequence in the ATP-binding pocket of PIKKs leads to the poor isoform selectivity of current kinase inhibitors, which causes significant safety issues. In this study, a highly potent mTOR inhibitor  PT-88  was developed via a structure-based drug design strategy by using the clinical candidate gedatolisib ( PT-1 ) as the tool molecule. Compared with  PT-1 ,  PT-88  exerted a more potent antitumor effect in MCF-7 cells by inducing cell autophagy, and a higher isoform selectivity profile which was confirmed by kinase inhibitory/binding profiling assays. Notably,  PT-88  revealed a higher SI over the normal hepatocyte, suggestive of its good biosafety profile. The drug delivery of  PT- 1  and  PT-88  was further improved after encapsulation on a lipodisc-based formulation, whose discoidal morphology provides a practical solution to avoid the PEG dilemma of PEGylated nanocarriers. The formulation significantly improved the pharmacokinetic properties of  PT-1  and  PT-88 , and the

The injectable doses were 10, 20, and 40 mg/kg. Mice were observed for body weight and health status for 7 days. MTD was defined as the concentration of the drug that did not cause unacceptable toxicity, including death, during the experimental period and that did not result in a loss of body weight of more than 15% of the body weight of the mice or any other pathological condition in the body of the mice.  

In Vivo Anticancer Assay.  The in vivo antitumor evaluation was performed with the approval of the Guizhou Medical University (SYXK-2023-0002). The antitumor efficacy of  PT-88  was evaluated in the xenograft mouse model implanted with MCF-7 cells. We first evaluated the MTD of  PT-1  and  PT-88  (4.6 mg compounds were dissolved with 0.33 mL of  N , N -dimethylacetamide, 0.66 mL of PEG400, and 1.32 mL of ddH 2 O), sND/ PT-1 , and sND/ PT-88 (formulated in saline) by gradually increasing the administered dose by tail vein injection into 7 week-old female BALB/c mice ( n  = 3 in each group). Female BALB/c mice (20 − 24 g) were purchased from Shanghai SLAC Laboratory Animal Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China) and kept under SPF conditions.  

Six-week-old female BALB/c nude mice were purchased from Shanghai SLAC Laboratory Animal Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China) and fed for 1 week to acclimatize and used to assess the antitumor effect of PT-88  on MCF-7 xenograft mouse models. MCF-7 at the logarithmic growth phase (4  ×  10 7   cells per milliliter) was harvested and resuspended in phenyl red-free, FBS-free DMEM medium (Invitro- gen), mixed with Corning HC matrigel (cat.354248, 1:1, v/v), and then injected subcutaneously at 100  μ L into the right axillary blood vessel- rich area of each BALB/c female nude mouse. When the tumor size reached an average of 100 mm 3 , the mice were randomly divided into five groups: vehicle ( N , N -dimethylacetamide/PEG400/ddH 2 O = 1:2:4, v/v), sND/PT-88 (20 mg/kg), sND/PT-1 (20 mg/kg), PT-88  (20 mg/kg), and  PT-1  (20 mg/kg).  PT-88  and  PT-1  were dissolved in the vehicle to the desired concentration. Body weight and tumor size were measured every other day by a platform scale and calipers, respectively. Tumor volume was calculated from the equation  a  ×  b 2   × 0.5, where a and b represent larger and smaller diameters, respectively.  

In Vivo Safety Evaluation.  After 2 h of the 10th administration, blood samples were collected to test blood biochemical indicators. Mice were sacrificed after ten times administration, and tumors were dissected carefully for further weighing. Tissues and tumors were dissected and fixed in 4% paraffin solution for H&E staining.  

Statistical Analysis.  All experimental data are expressed as mean  ± standard deviation (SD,  n  = 3). Statistical differences between two groups were determined using Student’s  t -test or Conover’s non- parametric test, and statistical analysis was performed using one-way ANOVA using GraphPad Prism. A value of  P  < 0.05 was considered to be statistically significant.  \* p  < 0.05,  \*\* p  < 0.01,  \*\*\* p  < 0.001, and   # p  < 0.0001.
