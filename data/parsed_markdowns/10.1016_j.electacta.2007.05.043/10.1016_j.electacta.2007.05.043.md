# Inﬂuence of micro-structure on corrosion behavior of a Ni-based superalloy in 3.5% NaCl  

Li Liu, Ying Li   ∗ , Fuhui Wang  

State Key Laboratory for Corrosion and Protection, Institute of Metal Research, Chinese Academy of Sciences, Wencui Rd 62, Shenyang 110016, China  

Received 31 January 2007; received in revised form 14 May 2007; accepted 16 May 2007 Available online 24 May 2007  

# Abstract  

The electrochemical corrosion behavior of a Ni-based superalloy with polycrystalline (cast alloy), single-crystalline (SC (2 0 0)) and nanocrys- talline (NC) micro-structures has been studied in 3.5% NaCl solution. The results indicated that among the three materials, the corrosion resistance increased in the order cast alloy < SC (2 0 0) alloy < NC coating. XPS analyses revealed that the composition of passive ﬁlm on the three materials was different. In addition to Cr 2 O 3  and TiO 2 , some NiO was detected in the passive ﬁlm on the cast alloy, little in that of the SC (2 0 0) alloy and none in that of the NC coating. The double-log plots showed the compact property of the passive ﬁlm formed on the samples also varied in the order cast alloy < SC (2 0 0) alloy < NC coating, with the cast alloy displaying the worst compact property and the NC coating the best. The micro-structure inﬂuenced both the composition of passive ﬁlm as well as the initial growth of passive ﬁlm, which determined the compact property of the ﬁlm and resulted in the observed differences in the corrosion behavior of the three materials.

# 1. Introduction  

Ni-based superalloys, which have both mechanical strength and oxidation resistance, are extensively used in turbine blades of industrial gas turbines and jet engines  [1–3] . When the Ni-based superalloys are employed in jet engines, resistance to pitting corrosion is another important property which can also inﬂuence the durability and service life of the engine. Therefore, besides the oxidation resistance of superalloys at high-temperature, the resistance to pitting corrosion at normal temperature is another important requirement for this mate- rial.  

In order to improve the high-temperature properties of the superalloy, it is increasingly necessary to develop and use single- crystal (SC) Ni-based superalloy. This alloy possesses not only improved mechanical properties, such as stress rupture  [4,5]  and fatigue resistance  [6,7] , but also better hot corrosion resistance [8–10] . However, despite the several possible applications of this SC alloy, the electrochemical corrosion behavior is yet to be thoroughly investigated.  

The properties of alloys, such as corrosion resistance depend not only on the alloy composition but also on their micro- structures. When the compositions are ﬁxed, the micro-structure is very important to the corrosion behavior of materials. The nano crystallization of Ni-based superalloys can obviously enhance their high-temperature oxidation resistance, compared with cast alloys  [11–14] . Also, some investigations have shown that nano crystallization can signiﬁcantly enhance pitting resis- tance at normal temperature. A superior localized corrosion resistance was found on sputtered 304 stainless steel nanocrys- talline coating  [15] . Wang and Li  [16]  also reported improved corrosion resistance of surface nano crystallized 304 SS in 3.5% NaCl solution which was obtained by a sandblasting and annealing process. Ye et al. found that sputtered 309 SS nanocrystalline coating had a higher pitting resistance com- pared to its bulk material  [17] . Results from an earlier study [18]  revealed that nano crystallization signiﬁcantly improved the resistance of the Ni-based superalloy to pitting corrosion in 0.5 M NaCl + 0.05 M H 2 SO 4  solution. However, the corrosion behavior of this nanocrystalline (NC) coating is yet to be studied

# 4. Discussion  

# 4.1. The inﬂuence of micro-structure on formation mechanism of passive ﬁlm  

Differences in the micro-structure of the Ni-based superal- loy inﬂuenced formation mechanism of the passive ﬁlm, which in turn resulted in differences in passive ﬁlm composition. The specimens exhibited passive behavior within certain potential range. Metal surface atoms, exposed to the aqueous solution were in contact with adsorbed water molecules to form oxides. After a continuous oxide layer formed in two dimensions, oxides layer grew in three dimensions  [26] . For the SC (2 0 0) alloy, the formed oxides depended on the distribution of atoms, which means different oxides were formed in different elemental distri- bution area. From EPMA results, it is clear that the distribution of Cr is mainly at dendritic areas and that of Ti is mainly at interdendritic area. The distribution of Ni is full-scale and the concentration is higher at interdendritic area than that at den- dritic areas. Because formation energy of Cr and Ti oxides is lower than that of Ni oxides, the Cr and Ti oxides should form more easily. Thus, Ni oxides could only form at areas without Cr and Ti oxides.  

For the cast alloy, the concentration of Ni is higher at bound- aries. During passivation, Ti and Cr oxides were ﬁrst formed but the passive ﬁlm could not form a continuous layer at once because of the large grain size of the cast alloy. At sufﬁciently high potentials, Ni was oxidized. Although the distribution of Ni on the SC alloy is higher at interdendritic areas than that at dendritic areas, the scale of dendritic structure is lager than the grain size of the cast alloy. Thus, the concentration of Ni per unit surface area of the cast alloy is higher than that of the SC alloy and as such a larger amount of Ni oxides formed on the cast alloy.  

For the NC coating, the small grain sizes promote uniform element distribution which facilitates rapid formation of the oxide layer. Because Cr and Ti are apt to be oxidized and small grain size is in favor of a fast formation of a continuous layer and Ni oxidation is subdued. As a result a continuous Cr and Ti oxide layer rapidly formed on the NC coating without Ni oxides. Because the element distribution is uniform on the whole surface, the content of element should be lower than that on non- uniform surface in unit area. Thus, the formed oxides layer of the NC coating should be thinner than others. XPS results testiﬁed this deduction, which means above analysis is valid.  

# 4.2. The inﬂuence of micro-structure on structure of passive ﬁlm  

The micro-structure not only inﬂuences the composition but also the initial growth of passive ﬁlm, which determines the compact property. The compact property of the passive ﬁlm will in turn inﬂuence the corrosion behavior. The initial oxide layer formation is accompanied by the gradual growth of the oxide ﬁlm. Oxide growth by cation diffusion over vacancies requires the annihilation of the vacancies by the climb of either misﬁt or misorientation interfacial dislocations  [27] . For the SC (2 0 0) alloy, the annihilation of the vacancies by these dislocations occurred during the passivation. In addition, since the content of Ni oxides in the passive ﬁlm was low, it would not inﬂuence on the composition of the passive ﬁlm to any great extent. Thus, the passive ﬁlm would be a more or less compact oxide layer.  

Although the cast alloy possesses grain boundaries with more dislocations, the grain boundary could inhibit the movement of dislocation at normal temperature  [28] . Thus, the annihilation of the vacancies in the interface of scale/metal was inhibited by the grain boundary, leading to an increase in the amount of cation vacancies in the interface. After the vacancies pile to some extent, hole or pore would be formed and even the initially formed oxide layer would be fractured. Thus, a passive ﬁlm with poor compact property should form. Apart from this effect of micro-structure, the composition also inﬂuenced the compact property of passive ﬁlm. The large amount of Ni oxide increased the ill-match in oxide layer and increased the stress between the different oxides, which may decrease the compact property of passive ﬁlm. The poor compact property of the passive ﬁlm should inﬂuence its protective ability of ﬁlm and decrease the corrosion resistance of the material.  

There are a lot of grain boundaries on the NC coating, which will also adversely affect the compact property of the oxide layer as earlier described for the cast alloy. Nevertheless, the small grain size of the NC coating ensures rapid reformation of the oxide layer and thus repairs passive ﬁlm. Therefore, the latter passive ﬁlm will have superior compact property, which would increase the corrosion resistance of the NC coating.

# 4.3. Micro-structure and corrosion behavior  

The composition and compact property of the passive ﬁlms formed on the different micro-structures of the Ni-based super- alloy, their corrosion behavior can be explained. Composition is one of the important factors to inﬂuence the properties of passive ﬁlms. Generally speaking, compared with Cr 2 O 3  and TiO 2 , NiO is more porous and has less protective ability to corrosion. The passive ﬁlms at the place where NiO formed exhibit weak region for localized corrosion, which decreased resistance to corrosion. XPS results show that there was a little NiO in the passive ﬁlm on the SC (2 0 0) alloy, which decreased the protective ability of ﬁlm. However, the content of NiO in the passive ﬁlm on the cast alloy was more than that of the SC (2 0 0) alloy. Thus, the protective ability of the passive ﬁlm on the cast alloy was worse than that of the SC alloy. There was no NiO in the passive ﬁlm on the NC coating; therefore, the integrity was not compromised. Therefore, the resistance to corrosion of the samples increased in the order cast alloy < SC (2 0 0) alloy < NC coating.  

Apart from the composition, the compact property is another factor that plays an important role in protective ability of passive ﬁlms. The oxide ﬁlm on the cast alloy, with poor compact prop- erty, augmented the dissolution of passive ﬁlm, which greatly increased the current density. Although the ion conductivity of the oxides on the cast alloy was low, the oxide layer with a large amount of defects facilitated ion transport in the oxide layer and allowed chloride ions to penetrate the ﬁlm from the solu- tion  [32] . The entry of chloride ions promoted the formation of pits, which increased the current density and decreased the protective ability of passive ﬁlm. Therefore, the passive current density of cast alloy was the largest among three materials. Pre- vious research results have suggested that once the pits formed, the current density would shift to higher values as the applied potential increased [33] .Therefore,the passive current density of the cast alloy slightly increased with potential. When the pitting corrosion enlarged to some extent, the breakdown of passive ﬁlm occurred and the current density increased sharply with potential.  

A more compact passive ﬁlm formed on the SC (2 0 0) alloy, which could effectively resist the attack of chloride ions leading to a lower anodic current. However, with the increase of potential chloride ions gradually penetrate the ﬁlm and above  ∼ 0.2 V, pitting corrosion occurred and current density sharply increased with potential, indicating a break down of the passive ﬁlm.  

For the NC coating, the passive ﬁlm changed from an initially porous layer to a largely compact ﬁlm. Therefore, the current density of the NC coating was smallest. The passive ﬁlm on the NC coating was most stable among three materials and there was no obvious pitting corrosion on the surface.  

# 5. Conclusions  

(1) The composition and compact property of passive ﬁlm inﬂu- en ce the corrosion resistance of material.The porous passive ﬁlm with much of NiO on the cast Ni-based superalloy in 3.5% NaCl has the lowest corrosion resistance. The rela- tive compact passive ﬁlm with a little NiO formed on the SC (2 0 0) alloy assisted the alloy to resist corrosion to some degree. The most compact passive ﬁlm without NiO formed on the NC coating had the highest resistance to pitting cor- rosion.

 (2) The micro-structure inﬂuences the composition of passive ﬁlm and initial growth of passive ﬁlm, both of which inﬂu- enced the corrosion behaviors of three materials.
