# Optimization of Nonaqueous Electrolytes for Primary Lithium/ Air Batteries Operated in Ambient Environment  

Wu Xu, \* ,   Jie Xiao, \*\*   Jian Zhang, Deyu Wang, \*\*   and Ji-Guang Zhang \*  

Paciﬁc Northwest National Laboratory, Energy and Environment Directorate, Richland, Washington 99354, USA  

The selection and optimization of nonaqueous electrolytes for ambient operations of lithium/air batteries have been studied. Organic solvents with a low volatility and low moisture absorption are necessary to minimize the change in electrolyte compo- sitions and the reaction between lithium anode and water during the discharge process. It is also critical to make the electrolyte with a high polarity so that it can reduce wetting and ﬂooding of the carbon-based air electrode and lead to improved battery performance. For ambient operations, the polarity of solvents outweighs the viscosity, ion conductivity, and oxygen solubility once these latter properties attain a certain reasonable level. Hence, a propylene carbonate/ethylene carbonate   PC/EC   mixture is the most feasible solvent system and lithium bis  triﬂuoromethanesulfonyl  imide the best salt for ambient operations of Li/air batteries, whose performance is relatively insensitive to PC/EC ratio and salt concentration. © 2009 The Electrochemical Society.  DOI: 10.1149/1.3168564   All rights reserved.

Metal/air batteries have a much higher theoretical speciﬁc energy than most other primary and rechargeable batteries.   These batteries are unique in that the cathode active material, oxygen, is not stored in the battery. During the battery discharge process, oxygen from the environment diffuses into the air electrode and is reduced at the surface in the air electrode, forming either peroxide or oxide ion that further reacts with cationic species in the electrolyte. Among the metal/air batteries studied so far, lithium/air battery has the highest theoretical speciﬁc energy at about 11 kWh/kg due to the light weight of lithium metal and the fact that cathode material   O 2   is not stored in the battery. Although most studies are warranted for the operation of Li/air batteries in ambient environments, there are very few systematic studies on nonaqueous electrolytes for Li/air batteries directly oper- ated in ambient environment.  

In this work, we investigated organic electrolytes for Li/air bat- teries operated at an oxygen partial pressure of 0.21 atm under dry conditions. The selection and optimization of nonaqueous electro- lytes for practical Li/air batteries are discussed on the basis of com- paring a series of properties, which include evaporation rate, mois- ture absorption rate by solvents and electrolytes, chemical stability of solvents with Li metal, viscosity, ionic conductivity and O 2  solu- bility in the electrolytes, and contact angle of electrolyte on the air electrode. Among those properties, emphasis was placed especially on low vapor pressure and low hygroscope. The effect of solvent ratio and salt concentration on cell performance is also discussed.

Contact angle measurement .— The contact angles of electro- lytes on both the carbon surface and the Teﬂon surface of the air cathode were measured on an NRL C. A. goniometer model 100-00- 115 at room temperature in open air atmosphere.

Evaporation rate of organic solvents .— Comparing the struc- tural difference with those of conventional Li primary and secondary batteries, Li/air batteries are actually a system open to the environ- ment while the others are sealed. Thus, the evaporation rate of sol- vents used in electrolytes for Li/air batteries is a critical parameter to be considered, with a low solvent evaporation rate preferred.  

Figure  1a  and  b  shows, respectively, the solvent evaporation rate in pure solvents and in electrolytes with 10% LiTFSI tested inside the dry box at room temperature   about 29°C  . The solvent evapo- ration rates are very similar in each case, and the existence of 10% LiTFSI in electrolyte slightly suppresses the evaporation rate of sol- vents. However, the neat solvent DPG shows a higher loss rate  75.0% loss in 56 days   than its 10% LiTFSI electrolyte   49.1% loss in 57 days  . The reason can be explained as below with DG as a comparison. DPG has a slightly lower polarity than DG. In a pure solvent system, the intermolecular attraction of DPG molecules is hence weaker, so the energy needed for an individual molecule to escape the liquid surface attraction and to enter the gas phase is lower, meaning that DPG molecules can evaporate faster than DG molecules. However, when the salt is added, the lower polarity DPG molecules encourage more ion pairs to form, which in turn increases the electrolyte viscosity   as shown in Table  II   and reduces the num- ber of solvent molecules that escape from the liquid state. Thus, the solvent evaporation in DPG electrolyte is slower than in DG elec- trolyte. Because the evaporation rates of DG in pure solvent and its 10% LiTFSI electrolyte are very similar, the evaporation rate of DPG in pure solvent is then much faster than that in the electrolyte.  

Solvent DME evaporates quickly and it loses 87% and 77% of the initial weight from pure solvent and the electrolyte, respectively, just by 2 days’ storage inside the dry box at about 29°C. When excluding the weight of 10% LiTFSI in electrolyte, the solvent loss rate is 86%, nearly the same as for the neat DME sample. Similarly, solvents DEE, DEC, and BEE also show a high evaporation rate.  

On the contrary, BDG and PC show much lower evaporation rates and they only lose 3.8% and 4.8% from the pure solvent samples in 63 days, respectively. In between the above two cases, solvents DPG, DG, and EDG can be considered to have moderate evaporation rates.

However, the aprotic solvent having a higher boiling point nor- mally has a higher viscosity, as also shown in Fig.  2 , where the viscosity data were taken from Ref.  8 . A highly viscous solvent leads to a highly viscous electrolyte with a low ionic conductivity, which usually results in poor battery kinetics. Mixing a high viscosity solvent with a low viscosity solvent in appropriate ratios can lead to an electrolyte with high conductivity and better battery performance. Extensive studies need to be conducted to ﬁnd an appropriate elec- trolyte formulation to achieve an optimal balance between the con- siderations of low evaporation rate and high conductivity.  

Moisture absorption rate by neat solvents and electrolytes .— Because Li/air batteries are exposed to the environment, it is ben- eﬁcial if the electrolyte is either hydrophobic or has a low water solubility. These properties reduce the possibility of reaction be- tween the lithium anode and moisture from the environment. The moisture absorption rate of neat solvents and electrolytes with 10% LiTFSI was tested for four low volatility solvents   BDG, PC, EDG, and DPG   in desiccators with a 96–98% RH humid air condition at room temperature   ca. 23°C  .  

The plots in Fig.  3  show that the water content in the studied solvents and electrolytes increases monotonously with the time of exposure. The electrolyte sample absorbs more water than does the neat solvent because the salt LiTFSI is hygroscopic itself. DPG, EDG, and their electrolytes containing 10% LiTFSI have both high moisture absorption rates and high water content at the end of ex- posure experiment, while neat BDG shows the least moisture ab- sorption in such a high humidity environment. BDG and its electro- lyte with 10% LiTFSI show the best results with a negligible 4–7% increase in water content after 30 days. PC and its LiTFSI electro- lyte do not show too high a moisture absorption rate, although PC itself is hygroscopic.

Effect of solvent compositions .— Table  II  lists the conductivity, oxygen solubility, and viscosity of the electrolytes of 1.0 M LiTFSI in different solvent mixtures at 25°C, the contact angles of such electrolytes at the surface of the carbon side of the air electrode at room temperature, and the average values of discharge performance of Li/air coin cells containing these electrolytes tested in dry air condition at room temperature.  

Although most of the electrolytes containing solvent mixtures of PC and ethers or glymes have a lower viscosity, higher conductivity, and higher oxygen solubility than that containing PC/EC, they show a much lower discharge capacity and speciﬁc energy. The main rea- son is probably their low contact angle at the carbon surface of the air electrode. As it is known that the carbon surface is of low polar- ity, hence so is the air electrode made from carbon materials and nonpolar hydrophobic Teﬂon binder. Therefore, ether- and glyme- containing electrolytes would have a better afﬁnity toward such sur- faces, where the porosity is better accessed by the liquid electrolyte. The consequence of this better wetting ability of ether- and glyme- based electrolytes toward the carbon electrode’s porosity structure is the blockage of air pathways, which is key in air electrode perfor- mance. Because the diffusion of oxygen in liquid electrolyte is slow, the quantity of the solid/liquid/gas triphase regions of carbon/ electrolyte/oxygen is limited; thus, the discharge performance of Li/ air batteries with electrolytes of good wetting ability to the air elec- trode is poor. This speculation is demonstrated by the phenomenon that such electrolyte always slowly leaked out during cell testing through the small holes on the coin cell pans, which are designed for air to pass because the low polarity electrolytes can also slowly wet the Teﬂon diffusion membrane. In this case, the solvents in electro- lyte of especially high volatility   normally low boiling point   easily evaporate and the electrolyte compositions also change.  

The electrolyte based on PC/EC has a high contact angle on the surface of the air electrode. Because PC and EC are highly polar aprotic solvents, so are the electrolytes based on them. The electro- lytes cannot easily and completely access the pore surface inside the air electrode. Therefore, many gaps must exist between the liquid electrolyte and the solid carbon/Teﬂon surface inside the air elec- trode. Thus, the oxygen can quickly diffuse into the deep air elec- trode through these gaps and be reduced at the surface of the triphases of carbon/electrolyte/oxygen, which results in a much higher discharge performance. It is therefore critical to increase the polarity of electrolytes so they cannot easily wet the air electrode and block the air pathways inside the air electrode.  

As for ether and glyme solvents, the smaller molecular solvent showed a slightly higher capacity and speciﬁc energy, i.e., DME   DEE    BEE    DG    DPG    EDG    BDG. Actually, this or- der follows the increase in solvent viscosity. Higher solvent viscos- ity leads to higher viscosity of the electrolyte and induces higher resistance for oxygen to diffuse through. The poor performance of cells using BDG-based electrolyte can be attributed to its higher viscosity and lower oxygen solubility, although BDG has a very low evaporation rate and low moisture absorption rate.  

Effect of lithium salts .— Table  III  lists the conductivity, oxygen solubility, and viscosity of the electrolytes of 1.0 M different lithium salts in PC/EC   1:1 wt   at 25°C, the contact angles of such electro- lytes at the surface of carbon side of the air electrode at room tem- perature, and the average values of discharge performance of Li/air coin cells containing these electrolytes tested in dry air condition at room temperature.  

All these lithium salts in the same PC/EC solvent mixture show a relatively high discharge performance because all their contact angles at the surface of the electrode carbon side are higher than 40°, which is beneﬁcial to the formation of triphase points of carbon/electrolyte/oxygen inside the air electrode as discussed above.  

The discharge capacity and speciﬁc energy of these electrolytes with different Li salts show the following order from high to low: LiClO 4    LiBr    LiTFSI    LiPF 6      LiSO 3 CF 3    LiBOB   LiI. The three salts of LiBr, LiBOB, and LiI also show a bit lower discharge power than other salts, although the discharge rate was the same for all electrolytes tested. Because the solvent system for these different lithium salts is the same, i.e., PC/EC, with high polarity, the difference in discharge performance for these electrolytes is mainly associated with the salts and the basic properties of the elec- trolytes such as conductivity, oxygen solubility, and viscosity. LiTFSI electrolyte has the highest oxygen solubility content among all these different salt electrolytes and a slightly lower viscosity and higher contact angle than LiPF 6 - and LiClO 4 -containing electro- lytes. When further considering the moisture sensitivity and thermal instability of LiPF 6  and the highly oxidizing LiClO 4 , both LiPF 6  and LiClO 4  are excluded for Li/air batteries.  

Electrolytes containing LiI, LiCF 3 SO 3 , and LiBOB have either lower oxygen solubility, lower conductivity, or higher viscosity; thus, the Li/air cells containing such electrolytes would show a slightly poorer discharge performance compared with LiTFSI. The LiBr-containing electrolyte shows a similar discharge performance as LiTFSI electrolyte at the discharge rate of 0.05 mA / cm 2 , but it exhibits a low conductivity and low oxygen solubility, which ham- pers its application in high rate discharge conditions. Considering all of these factors, LiTFSI is the best choice of lithium salt for ambient operation of Li/air batteries.

One of the main reasons is that ether- or glyme- based electrolytes have a higher accessibility to the porosity struc- ture of the air electrode so they easily block air pathways, while the cyclic carbonate-based electrolytes have a high polarity and their low afﬁnity to carbon pores creates triphase reaction sites for Li/air chemistry. For ambient operations of Li/air batteries, low electrode wettability and low vaporization pressure are the most important selection criteria of the electrolytes. PC/EC mixture is the most ap- propriate solvent system and LiTFSI is the best salt for ambient operations of Li/air batteries using Darco G-60 activated carbon as the air-electrode material, but this principle should also be applied to all other carbon materials that can be used in air electrodes. For the electrolyte system of LiTFSI in PC/EC, there is a ﬂat maximum discharge capacity and speciﬁc energy for wide PC/EC composi- tions and LiTFSI concentrations, which is beneﬁcial for stable bat- tery performance.
