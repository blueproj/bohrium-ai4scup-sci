# Abstract:  

The prediction of the viscoplastic behavior of nickel-based single crystal superalloys remains  a challenging issue due to the complex loadings encountered in aeronautical engine  components such as high pressure turbine blades. Under particular in-service conditions, these  materials may experience temperature cycles which promote the dissolution of the  strengthening γ’ phase of the material on (over)heating, and subsequent precipitation on  cooling, leading to a transient viscoplastic behavior.  

Within this context, a model was recently developed by Cormier and Cailletaud (Cormier and  Cailletaud, 2010) to fulfill the effects of fast microstructure evolutions occurring upon high  temperature non-isothermal loadings. New internal variables were introduced in the crystal  plasticity framework to take into account microstructure evolutions such as γ’  dissolution/precipitation and dislocation recovery processes which are known to control the  creep behavior and life. Nevertheless, this model did not consider the   ’ directional  coarsening, one of the main microstructural evolutions occurring specifically at high  temperature. In addition, no kinematic hardening was considered to describe the mechanical  behavior, leading to a poor description of cyclic loadings.  

This paper details the development of a new model by introducing new internal variables for  both modeling the   ’ directional coarsening and the evolutions of isotropic and kinematic  hardening under complex loading paths.  

This model was calibrated using monotonous and cyclic experiments performed on [001]  oriented single-crystal samples and both under isothermal and non-isothermal conditions.  Thereby, it is able to predict microstructural evolutions for complex thermal and mechanical  loadings as well as internal stress evolutions whatever the thermo mechanical history. The  model efficiency was highlighted by comparing FEM simulation and experimental results of a  non-isothermal creep test on a notched sample (i.e. under complex mechanical stress state).  

Keywords: Nickel base single crystal superalloy, Non-isothermal loadings, Microstructural  evolutions; γ’ rafting, Crystal plasticity model, FEM simulation, Dissolution/Precipitation,  Creep, Dwell-Fatigue.

# 1.   Introduction  

Monocrystalline nickel base superalloys are widely used in the hottest parts of aeroengines or  industrial gas turbines (Caron and Khan, 1983). Blades made of these alloys operate for  thousands of hours at temperatures as high as 1100°C (Reed, 2006). These alloys are chosen  for their superior mechanical performances at high temperatures, in particular their creep  resistance for uncooled components such as high pressure turbine blades of turboshaft engines  for helicopters or small industrial gas turbines. These interesting properties result from the  precipitation of a high volume fraction (close to 70 %) of the long-range ordered L1 2  γ’ phase  which appears as cubes coherently embedded in a face-centered cubic (fcc) solid solution γ  matrix.  

Furthermore, a single crystal superalloy with a negative (respectively positive)   /  ’ lattice  mismatch (coherency stress due to the difference between the lattice parameters of the γ and  γ’ phases) exhibits a directional coarsening of the γ’ precipitates perpendicularly (respectively  parallel) to the applied tensile stress axis (N-type coarsening, respectively P-type coarsening).  This morphological evolution, often known under the “  ’ rafting” denomination, usually takes  place during the high temperature (T>800˚C) primary creep stage when the γ’ phase has  entirely coalesced and the γ channels have become wider along the applied stress axis  (Henderson et al., 1999; Shui et al., 2006). Moreover, during severe thermo mechanical  histories, thermal loadings can also induce microstructural changes, especially fast ones such  as γ’ dissolution. These changes modify the internal stress state, as le Graverend et  al.  (le  Graverend et al., 2012) have already shown by X-ray synchrotron study for temperature peaks  representative of helicopter in-operation emergency ratings (Cormier et al., 2007b; Cormier et  al., 2008; le Graverend et al., 2010). Under such circumstances, the material’s microstructure  is out of equilibrium, so that the properties for a given temperature differ from the steady state   (Cailletaud, 1979; Cormier et al., 2008; le Graverend et al., 2010).  

The recent development of microstructure-sensitive models (i.e. models in which internal  variables representing microstructure are added) (Cormier and Cailletaud, 2010; Fedelich et  al., 2012; Khan and Liu, 2012; Shenoy et al., 2008; Zhu et al., 2012) has been motivated by  the requirement of a better prediction of the mechanical behavior under conditions where  microstructure is likely to evolve during thermo mechanical loadings, as it is observed during  in-service operations (Sujata et al., 2010) or during the severe certification procedures of aero- engines (Mauget et al., 2012). In fact, under such conditions, classical constitutive modeling  approaches where the temperature dependence is only taken into account by the material’s  parameters fails to predict transient mechanical responses of alloys during thermo mechanical  loadings (Cailletaud, 1979). For instance, such a microstructure-sensitive modeling approach  is undertaken to account for the impact of the γ’ morphological evolution under directional  coarsening conditions on the mechanical properties of nickel-based single crystal superalloys  (Fedelich et al., 2012; Tinga et al., 2009).  

This article presents a set of viscoplastic constitutive equations developed in the crystal  plasticity framework (Cailletaud, 2009; Fedelich, 2002; Méric et al., 1991; Nouailhas and  Cailletaud, 1995). This model accounts for the thermo mechanical history  via  the  consideration of both the   ’ rafting kinetics under isothermal and non-isothermal loadings and  the dissolution/precipitation of the γ’ phase developed by Cormier et  al.  (Cormier and  Cailletaud, 2010; Ghighi et al., 2012). Thus, specially tailored internal variables have been  introduced to describe microstructure evolutions, without requiring an intensive  microstructural analysis.  

The paper is then organized as follows: section 2   describes both the microstructure evolutions  occurring during isothermal and non-isothermal loadings and their consequences on the  mechanical behavior. Section   3 depicts the constitutive equations of the model focusing on  the newly introduced internal variables. Section   4   illustrates the capabilities of the model  based on pre-existing experimental results for the prediction of isothermal and non-isothermal  creep, low cycle fatigue (LCF) and dwell-fatigue behaviors. Section 5 compares experimental  and numerical creep elongation and microstructure evolutions of a notched specimen  submitted to a non-isothermal creep test. Finally, section 6 is devoted to the limitations of the  model in its present form.  

# 2.   Microstructural evolutions occurring during thermo-mechanical loadings and  their consequences on the mechanical behavior  

This section outlines the main microstructural evolutions happening during high temperature  inelastic deformations of nickel-based single crystal superalloys. Their consequences on the  mechanical behavior both under isothermal and non-isothermal conditions are then presented.  The aim of this section is hence to exhibit some basic microstructural parameters included in  constitutive equations through new internal variables.  

# 2.1   Isothermal condition  

The main microstructural evolution observed during high temperature exposure is the  modification of the γ’ volume fraction. This one quickly decreases above 950˚C. The γ’  volume fraction has a strong influence on mechanical properties, as shown by Murakumo et  al . (Murakumo et al., 2004) in the case of the TMS-75 alloy. It is important to note that  volume fractions are here considered at thermodynamical equilibrium. They are obtained for  long thermal hold times at high temperature as opposed to those involving rapid temperature  changes which are subsequently considered in this article.  

The other classical microstructure evolution observed during prolonged thermal exposure  under load is the γ’ rafting whose kinetics depend on temperature/stress conditions (Li et al.,  2007; Pollock and Argon, 1994). Two categories of tests can then be differentiated:  

   short tests (tensile and fast cyclic tests) or low temperature creep tests (T<800˚C)  during which it can be considered that almost no significant metallurgical  microstructural evolutions occur (Espié, 1996; Hanriot, 1993), except a possible  rounding of   ’ precipitates at their corners due to a higher density of dislocations  caused by thermo-elastic stresses between the two phases (Ganghoffer et al., 1991;  Müller et al., 1992)  

   long tests (creep and low strain rate cyclic tests) during which γ’ phase coalesces along  an orientation depending on the principal stress axes (Arrell and Vallés, 1996;  Basoalto et al., 2000) and the crystallographic orientation of the sample (Tian et al.,  2012; Yue and Lu, 1999) (see Fig. 1).  

This kind of microstructural evolution is also observed during fatigue tests. The γ/γ’  morphology evolves as a function of the orientation and the maximum stress value. For  example, a microstructure oriented at 45 degrees of the stress axis is observed during a slow  strain controlled test at high temperature for [001] oriented samples (R ε =-1,   =10 -5  s -1 )  (Frenz et al., 1996; Gaubert, 2009; Mughrabi et al., 1996).  

The   ’ directional coarsening has been shown to have an impact on tensile and cyclic  mechanical behaviors. For instance, a pre-compressive creep strain which forms a γ’ rafted  microstructure parallel to the [001] oriented axis sample (for a negative misfit superalloy) is  detrimental to the subsequent remaining tensile creep life at 1050°C (Ayrault, 1989; Tetzlaff  and Mughrabi, 2000) and benifical below 1000°C (Li et al., 2007; Tetzlaff and Mughrabi,  2000). The softer mechanical behavior observed for γ’ pre-rafted samples at temperature in  excess of 1050°C has to be correlated with the decrease of the yield stress noticed on rafted  specimens during monotonous tensile tests on γ’-rafted specimens (Espié, 1996; MacKay and  Ebert, 1985). The same result was also noticed for cyclic tests due to the formation of γ’-rafts  (Espié, 1996; Frenz et al., 1996; Gaubert, 2009).  

Even if a γ’-rafted microstructure can be considered as detrimental for the remaining creep  life, it is not the case for fatigue tests on [001] oriented samples which exhibit a better LCF  life when γ’ rafting is parallel to the axis sample (Ott and Mughrabi, 1999). This results from  the γ/γ’ interfaces which impede more efficiently crack propagation under mode I taking  preferentially place in γ channels. This effect will not be taken into account in the following  since our attention will be mainly focused on the inelastic mechanical behavior, with little  attention to the damage modeling (except under isothermal creep conditions).  

# 2.2   Non-isothermal condition  

Contrary to long temperature exposures, short thermal regimes do not allow the dissolution  process to reach the thermodynamic equilibrium. The material is then in a metastable transient  state. Cormier et  al . studied the temporal evolution of the γ’ volume fraction for a given  temperature and showed that a massive dissolution of γ’ precipitates is effective for short hold  times for temperatures above 1050˚C (Cormier et al., 2007a). The authors also showed that  the higher the mechanical load is, the higher the γ’ dissolution kinetics is. This phenomenon is  due to a modification of the strain energy state of the γ/γ’ system which modified the kinetics  of dissolution. This observation is similar to the precipitation mechanism assisted by  dislocations and better known as "pipeline diffusion" (Embury et al., 2003; Giraud et al.,  2013).  

As for isothermal experiments, microstructure evolutions have an impact on the mechanical  behavior during non-isothermal conditions especially when the dissolution/precipitation of the  strengthening γ’ phase occurs. An example of this microstructure evolution is presented in  Fig. 2 where it can be observed wider    channels as well ultrafine   ’ particles therein after an  overheating at 1200°C on MC2 alloy.  

The impact of the   ’ morphology on the non-isothermal creep properties was already  characterized for two kinds of experiments on MC2 and CMSX-4 alloys (Cormier et al.,  2007b; Giraud et al., 2012): creep tests under a nominal temperature of 1050°C containing  one single overheating close to the   ’-solvus at 1200°C (for 30, 90 or 150 seconds) and under  thermal cycling conditions (repeated over-temperatures from a 1050°C reference temperature  level). Under such conditions, it was observed a decrease of the non-isothermal creep life with  a N-type   ’ rafted microstructure compared to an initially cuboidal   ’ precipitation. This result  comes from an increased viscoplastic strain rate for   ’ rafted microstructure.  

In addition, under such non-isothermal conditions, the dissolution/precipitation processes play  also a prominent role on the mechanical behavior. le Graverend et  al.  have shown that an  overheating with no loading from 1050°C up to 1200°C during dwell-fatigue tests (R σ =0,  σ max =230 MPa) leads to a transient decrease of the subsequent strain rate (le Graverend et al.,  2010) whose duration depends on the level of plastic strain upon the overheating. Moreover,  this hardening effect depends on the crystalline orientation of the sample, what seems to be  linked to a critical resolved shear stress below which tertiary particles provide a  strengthening. The same hardening effect had already been observed by Cailletaud on the  polycrystalline superalloy IN100 during non-isothermal stress-controlled cyclic tests  (Cailletaud, 1979).  

This strengthening mechanism is linked to the precipitation of fine tertiary γ’ precipitates in  the γ matrix channels after the over-temperature, due to the supersaturation of the matrix in  γ’-elements (Al, Ti, etc.). This particles block then the dislocation motion at the lowest  temperature as long as they are not dissolved or not re-coalesced with the largest   ’ particles  (Kakehi, 1999), a process that leads to wider γ channels that favor an easier dislocation  motion. Alternatively, this tertiary γ’ particles are likely to form “ micro-rafts” in the center of  the    channels (Cormier et al., 2010; le Graverend et al., 2010) which lead to a size allowing  easier dislocation shearing (Mohles et al., 1999).  

# 2.3   Recovery mechanisms during high temperature exposure  

Rapid recovery processes (dynamic and static) of internal stresses appear at high temperature  (decrease of hardening). There were already identified for isothermal tests and came from  underlying deformation mechanisms such as the shearing, the annihilation, and the climb of  dislocations along the γ’-rafts. γ’ terminations are then preferred locations for deformation  recovery processes due to the largest local dislocation density (Carroll et al., 2004; Srinivasan  et al., 2000; Svoboda and Lukas, 1997). These processes were recently observed and analyzed  by le Graverend et  al . (le Graverend et al., 2012; le Graverend et al., 2014b) during non- isothermal creep tests followed by  in situ  X-ray diffraction under synchrotron radiation. The  authors observed that the dislocation density in the γ’ phase became higher than in the γ phase  during overheating. They explained this by the motion of dislocations no longer blocked at the  γ/γ’ interfaces during overheating at temperatures above 1130˚C. This dislocation motion  results from the massive dissolution of the γ’ phase at such temperatures, which would allow  the release of dislocations in γ channels and their entrance in γ’ particles. Then, upon cooling  (from the overheating temperature to the nominal one), dislocations present in the γ’ phase  remain trapped which justifies the slowest recovery process observed for this phase.  

# 2.4   Basic ingredients to model the impact of microstructure evolutions on the  mechanical behavior and life  

The main consequences of fast and short temperature changes during a creep loading are the  following (Cormier et al., 2007b; le Graverend et al., 2014c):  

   A creep strain jump is observed during an overheating due to both a γ ’   dissolution and  a lower dislocation density at the γ/γ ’  interfaces when the overheating is performed  above a temperature threshold (see figure 3),  

   A fine tertiary γ ’   precipitation occurs on fast cooling in the γ   channels between the  coarse secondary γ ’   particles not fully dissolved during the overheating (see Figure 2).  These two kinds of γ ’   particles are respectively denoted in the model as “small” and  “large” γ ’   precipitates and have to be taken into account through their respective  volume fractions ( f   and  f  ) to reproduce possible transient strengthening effects,  

   A new primary stage during the first hours of post-overheating creep due to the  recovery of both the γ ’   volume fraction of coarse precipitates ( f  ) and the dislocation  density at the γ/γ ’   interfaces (see figure 3). The recovery of  f   arises from the  coalescence of the fine γ’ precipitation with the coarser ones during post-overheating  creep,  

   A post-overheating creep rate depending of the level of stress: creep tests on <001>  oriented samples performed at 1050°C and stresses below \~ 150 MPa lead to an  

increase of the post-overheating creep rate whereas no modification is noticed for  higher stresses.  

Moreover, there is a link between overheating duration and creep life. Actually, the longer the  overheating is, the higher the amplitude of the creep deformation during this one is.  Nevertheless, this implies a greater amplitude of the post-overheating primary stage leading to  a lower post-overheating minimum creep rate and, hence, a longer post-overheating creep life.  According to dwell-fatigue tests, fine γ’ precipitates may transiently impede the dislocation  motion as long as they are not dissolved (le Graverend et al., 2010). Indeed, the authors have  observed that a 180 second overheating at 1200˚C performed during dwell-fatigue tests at  1050˚C on <111> oriented samples led to a decrease of the plastic strain rate. However, this  one does not modify the material viscosity has shown during two monotonous tensile tests  under varying strain rate on γ/γ’ microstructures containing (or not) tertiary γ’ particles (a  result detailed later in the present article in figure 16) exhibiting similar stress relaxations  when the strain rate decreased from 10 -3  s -1  to 10 -5  s -1 .  

Based on all these microstructural characterizations, additional variables accounting for the  effects of both γ ’  particle evolutions and dislocation activity are introduced in a crystal  plasticity model (Méric et al., 1991). Contrary to several models in which the γ/γ ’   microstructure is explicitly modeled (Busso et al., 2000; Glatzel and Feller-Kniepmeier, 1989;  Preussner et al., 2009) and need a micro-macro transition to capture the mechanical behavior  of the “composite”, an homogeneous field approach based on the model developed by  Cormier and Cailletaud (Cormier and Cailletaud, 2010) is undertaken to achieve cost- effective finite element (FE) simulations with complex thermo mechanical paths. It is  therefore emphasized that no attempt is made to model and to mesh the actual γ/γ ’   microstructure of the alloy and its explicit morphological evolution during thermo mechanical  loading. Thus, all microstructural variables required to account for the non-isothermal  mechanical behavior (γ ’   volume fraction of large and small precipitates, dislocation recovery  variables, the mean width of γ channels) are directly introduced in the constitutive equations.

# 3.2   Microstructural evolutions of the γ’ phase

# 4.1.1   Creep and relaxation  

Creep tests at 1050°C were simulated with the coupled behavior/damage model whose the  parameters of the Rabotnov-Kachanov’s law were identified thanks to X-tomography scans of  porosity during creep tests under various temperature/stress conditions in the (1000-1100°C)  temperature range (le Graverend et al., 2014a). During this study, it was highlighted that there  is a non-linear accumulation of damage as a function of the stress level at high temperature.  Nevertheless, for sake of simplicity, it is assumed a linear accumulation which does not  drastically degrade the prediction of tertiary stage, whatever the applied load (Fig. 5). This  figure includes experiments and simulations for different levels of stress and shows that the  longer the duration of the test is, the more pronounced the isotropic hardening recovery is,  thought the decrease of the Orowan stress (Fig. (5f)).  

Furthermore, figure (5f) exhibits that the Orowan stress decreases even more quickly with the  plastic strain under low applied stress, due to longer tests. As far as, the γ’ rafting mechanism  is a diffusion controlled process and this phenomenon is time dependent, as Fedelich et  al.   have been observed (Fedelich et al., 2012). Contrary to observations made for CMSX-4 ®   alloy containing heavy elements, like rhenium, and which therefore lead to a rafting kinetics  much slower than the MC2 alloy for an equivalent plastic strain (Epishin et al., 2008), the  modeled Orowan stress quickly decreases to reach a plateau at 1% of plastic strain for low  stress levels. The kinetics of modeled microstructural evolutions is thus inherently dependent  of the alloy composition.  

In addition, the prediction of the stress levels reached during a relaxation test (figure 6) is  quite consistent with the experience. Actually, the model well describes the viscosity of the  material given by the strong decrease of the stress at the beginning of the test as well as the  final internal stress value given by the asymptote.

# 4.2.    Non-isothermal tests  

# 4.2.1 Parameters for non-isothermal tests  

The tensile-cycling-relaxation test at 1050°C with an overheating up to 1200°C with no  applied load during 30 or 150 seconds provides the model parameters which describe the  thermal history of the material. As shown in figure 8, the apparent yield stress  R \*  at 1050°C  measured consecutively to an overheating was increased by about 160 MPa compared to the  reference test without any initial overheating. This is due to a decrease in the mean free path  for the motion of dislocations because of the reduction of the effective γ channel width linked  to the precipitation of tertiary γ’ precipitates upon cooling. Consequently, these ones increase  the Orowan stress ( w 001 decreases).  

Three other conclusions can be pointed out from figure 8. Firstly, the stress level at 1% of  strain is exactly the same for both tests which implies that an overheating decreases the  overall hardening mainly through an increase of the kinematic hardening evolution kinetics  ( D \* ). Thus, the presence of tertiary γ’ precipitates decreases the kinematic hardening  amplitude ( C \* /D \* ) and increases both the kinematic hardening evolution kinetics ( D \* ) and the  isotropic hardening amplitude. This change entails an almost perfect plastic behavior of the  overheated material from 0.5% to 1% of total strain. Secondly, the first strain rate variation  leads to the same stress decrease, leading to the conclusion that the presence of tertiary γ’  precipitates has no influence on the viscosity. Last, the two curves are perfectly superimposed  during the second loading at 10 -3  s -1  (from 1.5% and 2% of strain) which means that the  hardening behavior observed at the beginning is transient. So tertiary γ’ precipitates have no  more influence on the monotonous mechanical response.  

In figure (10a), experiences and simulations of the tests presented in the figure 9 are  compared. It is also reported changes with time of  w 001 ,  C \* ,  D \* ,  C \* /D \*  variables and of the  isotropic hardening ( r 0  r Q   Orowan ) (see figures 17b, c and d). As shown in figure (10d), this is due to the decrease of the γ channel width from 82 nm to 50  nm. This decrease is due to the nucleation of tertiary γ’ precipitates in the γ channel after  cooling. In addition, after reaching the plateau, the evolution of  D \*  is consistent with what is  observed for the first strain rate change (from 10 -3  s -1  to 10 -5  s -1 ), namely a fast decrease to  recover its equilibrium value (figure (10c)). # 5.    Capability of the model to predict the non-isothermal creep behavior of a  single-crystalline structure  

# 5.1    Aim, experimental and numerical boundary conditions  

A specimen was designed to generate a non-uniform and multiaxial complex mechanical field  by the presence of two asymmetrical notches (see figure 14). Thus, the γ/γ’ microstructure  should evolve according to the local mechanical field of this sample which was tested under  non-isothermal conditions with a reference level at 1050°C/F=410 N. An overheating of 20  seconds at 1200°C under load was performed after 24 hours of creep to obtain the influence of  the plastic strain on the kinetics of both dissolution/precipitation and γ’ rafting development.  After the overheating, the specimen was crept for 30 more minutes at 1050°C.  The heating and the overheating were performed in a typical creep test bench with a radiant  furnace (Cormier et al., 2007b). The rapid rise in temperature from the nominal temperature  of 1050°C to 1200°C was performed in 70 seconds while the return to the nominal  temperature lasted 10 seconds. The temperature was directly controlled on the sample using a  spot welded S-type thermocouple.  

During testing, the overall displacement of the specimen was recorded by a laser  extensometer whose measurement is given in figure 15. The strain rate after the overheating is  significantly higher than before (Fig. 15), respectively equal to 4.31 10 -2  mm.h -1  ( u after ) and  2.57 10 -3  mm.h -1  ( u before ) (factor equal to 17).  

The mesh used for FEM simulations is shown in figure 14. Tetragonal linear elements were  used with a full integration and the mesh was refined in the area of high multiaxiality  corresponding to the path between the two notches. The boundary conditions applied are  those highlighted in the figure 19 which avoided rigid body motion.  

The specimen macroscopic loading axis is oriented along the [001] crystallographic  orientation and [100] and [010] directions are parallel to the sides and slices of the specimen  (N.B. the slight misorientation of the loading axis with the [001] crystallographic direction of  about of 2 degrees has been neglected here during finite element simulations).  

# 5.2   Microstructure and image analysis  

Microstructural observations were performed on a longitudinal section of the specimen to  determine microstructure gradients generated by the notches and the overheating. Quantitative  microstructural analyzes have been performed using the Visilog ®  software and analysis  methods specifically developed at Pprime Institute (Vaunois et al., 2010). Figure 16 highlights  that γ’ rafts in the center of the specimen, where the stress field has the highest multiaxiality,  have a strong misorientation with respect to the macroscopic loading axis and this  misorientation progressively fades away from the center along the loading direction. Image  processing revealed that the misorientation of γ/γ’ interfaces compared to the initial  mm from the center of the specimen, this misorientation is almost zero.  Figures 17 (c) and (d) show that the volume fraction of secondary and tertiary γ’ precipitates  are correlated to the plastic strain level. Actually, the plastic deformation is the most  important in the center of the specimen where the volume fraction of tertiary γ’ precipitates is  the highest, which reduces even more the γ channel width (Fig. (17a)). This is the result of a  higher strain rate in the shear band which induced accelerated γ’ dissolution during the  overheating. Furthermore, we observed at the end of the test a lower volume fraction of  secondary γ’ precipitates. The model explicitly takes into account the link between the  dissolution/precipitation kinetics of secondary γ’ precipitates and plastic strain, which is not  the case for tertiary γ’ precipitates although these two volume fractions are correlated during  the nucleation of tertiary γ’ precipitates, according to Eq. 14 (Cormier and Cailletaud, 2010;  Ghighi et al., 2012). As Embury et  al.  have shown, dislocations promote precipitation as  preferential nucleation sites (Embury et al., 2003). Similarly, dissolution is also assisted by  dislocations (Giraud et al., 2012). The question is whether the level of plasticity has a direct or  indirect impact on the tertiary γ’ precipitation given that plasticity already promotes a greater  dissolution of secondary γ’ precipitates. This issue is out of the scope of this article and it will  be assumed that the plastic strain has an indirect influence on tertiary γ’ precipitates though its  influence on the secondary γ’ particles dissolution/precipitation kinetics.  

Qualitative and quantitative analyzes of the microstructure were made for a transverse path  from the notch to the bulk (Fig. 18). The misorientation of γ/γ’ interfaces (relative to the  normal to the axis of the specimen) increases from the notch to the center of the specimen  (Fig. (18b)). This misorientation will be analyzed in a subsequent section.  Figure 19 shows areas where the γ’ rafting process had occurred along a path at 45° to the  axis of the specimen, away from the notch, revealing a stress gradient along this path. A  magnification of one of these areas along the right notch (notch A in figure (18a)) shows a  microstructure at ±45° of the [001] direction. Therefore, strong gradients of microstructure  exist within the sample highlighting the non-uniformity of the mechanical field.  The most surprising observation is in figure 19 where it can be still observed a cuboidal γ/γ’  microstructure above the left notch (notch B in figure (18a)). Such a microstructure is due to a  very low plastic strain, the creep strain being the prominent driving force for the raft  development (Paris et al., 1997; Pollock and Argon, 1994; Véron et al., 1996). The numerical  simulation of the test will explain this microstructural state analyzing the local mechanical  field in terms of equivalent Von Mises stress and inelastic strain.

# 5.4.2   Mechanical response prediction  

Despite of a relatively good agreement between the simulated and experimental volume  fractions for each class of precipitates and a good prediction of microstructural state, a poor  description of the elongation is observed, both before and during the overheating (see figure  (22d)). Two main reasons are responsible of this result. First of all, the model was written for  the [001] orientation which means that the multiaxiality of stresses generated at the center of  the sample is intrinsically not well described. Indeed, the creep rate dependence with the  orientation was already shown by several authors for different superalloys and they found that  samples oriented close to the [111] direction exhibit low creep strain rates (Ghosh et al., 1990;  Yu et al., 2013). This means that the experimental Schmid factor in the areas of maximum of  triaxiality have changed and is not equal to 0.408 (Schmid factor on octahedral slip systems  for the [001] orientation) anymore. It is probably the reason why the creep elongation rate is  experimentally lower than numerically (Fig. (22d)).  

There is also a large difference of elongation evolution during the overheating: 0.71 mm  experimentally versus 0.35 mm numerically. The main reason could be due to the crystal  rotation which has not been considered here. This phenomena was already observed during  isothermal creep tests on the SRR99 alloy (Ardakani et al., 1998; Ghosh et al., 1990; Gunturi  et al., 2000; Inoue et al., 2009; Kakehi, 1999) or on the MC2 alloy at high temperature  (Ghighi et al., 2012). Thus, the simulation is no longer situated in the approximation of small  deformations. One way to improve such a simulation would be to consider the orientation  tensor dependent on the accumulated plastic strain and to be in the context of large strains.  

# 6.   Limitations  

One of the present limitations of the model detailed in this paper is the viscosity formulation  that is used. Indeed, dwell-fatigue tests have shown that the Norton’s law is not suitable for  very fast strain rate changes (  >2.5 10 -6  s -1 ). Indeed, the hysteresis loops are experimentally  p opened and the yield stress is less than 110 MPa (see figure (13d)). However, there is no  numerical saturation of the viscous stress at high plastic strain rate which leads to an  overestimation of the yield stress during fast loadings ( R \*  160 MPa >> 110 MPa) (see  figure (13d)). Therefore, the model provides hysteresis loops with a reduced plastic amplitude  compared to the ones observed under such stress rates. That means that the Ratchetting effect  (accumulation of plastic strain at each loop) observed during experiences is not well  predicted. A modification of the viscosity law could be undertaken to solve this problem,  using a hyperbolic sine flow rule that models intrinsic saturation of the viscosity at high  plastic strain rate and gives a better description of the viscous stress for very slow tests  (   10  10   s -1 ) (Miller, 1976).  

The Kachanov-Rabotnov’s law predicts correctly lifetimes for isothermal tests (Fig. 11),  which is not the case for non-isothermal tests. In fact, such a formulation whose driving force  is controlled by the invariants of the stress tensor cannot account for the nonlinear  accumulation effect of damage under complex loading with thermal history, especially under  non-isothermal creep cycle (Cormier et al., 2007b; Giraud et al., 2012; Goti et al., 2012;  Raffaitin et al., 2007): for a given applied stress, the faster the thermal cycling is, the greater  the decrease of the creep lifetime or the longer the overheating is, the higher the residual creep  life is. This cannot be predicted by the damage model given that a simulated overheating at  1200°C under a load of 120, 140 or 160 MPa leads to a divergence of the simulation due to a  rapid increase of the total strain. The Kachanov-Rabotnov’s law is mainly based on a failure  constant time (  c  ) which depends on the stress value and three other material parameters  depending on the temperature. This means that  t   decreases when the temperature increases  for a given applied stress which means that the remaining time to failure is shorter when  longer overheatings are introduced in the creep life of the alloy. This trend is clearly not  observed for MC2 alloy (Cormier et al., 2007b). The recovery mechanisms of the γ/γ’  microstructure or of the dislocations substructures occurring at very high temperature (le     Graverend et al., 2012) and leading to increased creep lives are not taken into account. A  formulation of creep damage dependent on the accumulated plastic strain, as Cormier et al.  have already proposed (Cormier and Cailletaud, 2010), seems more relevant in the case of  non-isothermal tests. The study has also shown that the crystal lattice rotation has a large  influence on the mechanical behavior and then on microstructural evolutions. This was  observed for a [001] sample whose lattice is not supposed to rotate with the increase of the  plastic deformation since such an orientation is stable (this is also true for a [111] orientation).  However, a complex geometry which entails complex mechanical fields could lead to lattice  rotation under creep conditions, even for a [001] orientation, as shown in section 5. Hence, a  large strain formulation has to be employed in a future formulation of the model to allow an  evolution of the orientation tensor. With such an approach, the evolution of γ channel along a  <001> orientation would also require a 3D formulation to get an accurate description of the γ’  rafting process as a function of the loading direction.  

In spite of all these limitations, the model presented in this paper is successful to predict many  complex thermo mechanical loadings for both elongation predictions and microstructure  evolutions (see Table 6).  

# Conclusions  

The prediction of thermo mechanical response of nickel-based single crystal superalloys, as  well as microstructural evolutions like γ’ rafting or dissolution/precipitation of the γ’ phase,  for monotonous, cyclic and complex tests are real issues for aero-engine manufacturers. To be  able to model the mechanical answer of the material when a severe thermo mechanical  loadings is applied, the developed model accounts for internal stresses and microstructure  evolutions through several observations made during isothermal or non-isothermal tests like  low cycle fatigue, isothermal creep, relaxation and complex tests (fatigue-relaxation or  tensile-cycling-relaxation). The decrease of the yield stress size at high temperature due to the  γ’ rafting process of secondary γ’ precipitates, the hardening after an overheating which  increases the yield stress size and/or the evolution of the structural hardening due to the  precipitation of tertiary γ’ precipitates in the γ channels, and the effect of the thermal history  on the creep rate after an overheating are some examples of new contributions that have been  considered in the present model.  

Indeed, internal stresses, microstructure evolutions and their consequences under  thermo mechanical complex loading at high temperature were modeled to well describe low  cycle fatigue, isothermal creep, relaxation and complex tests like fatigue-relaxation or tensile- cycling-relaxation.  

The model performances were also tested comparing finite element simulations and  experimental results on a dual-notched specimen generating strong multiaxial strain/stress  fields. This specimen allowed the comparison between the calculated and experimentally  observed microstructures through the volume fractions of secondary and tertiary γ’  precipitates as well as the width of γ channels. A rather good agreement of the microstructure  model’s predictions with the observation was obtained, especially, the impact of the plastic  deformation on the microstructural evolutions kinetics. The current model is then able to  model the case of complex geometries and loads at high temperatures with thermal history by  taking into account the microstructural evolutions and their effects on the internal stresses.   Thus, the new γ’ rafting model developed, as well as the new formulation of a non-linear  kinematic hardening, are able to take into account the mechanical and thermal history through  new internal variables. Moreover, both the homothetic growth, the directional coarsening and  the changes induced by thermal loadings are taken into account what makes this model one of  the only ones to be able to simulate a wide variety of tests for complex mechanical and  thermal conditions.
