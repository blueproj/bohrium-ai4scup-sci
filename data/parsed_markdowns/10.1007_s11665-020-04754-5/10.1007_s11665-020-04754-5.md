# Effects of Heat Treatment on Microstructural Evolution and Stress Rupture Properties of a Directionally Solidiﬁed Nickel-Based Superalloy during Long-Term Thermal Exposure  

X.W. Jiang, J.J. Wu, P. Song, B. Yin, D. Wang, Y. Wang, J.S. Dong, J. Shen, H. Li, and L.H. Lou (Submitted November 24, 2019; in revised form March 16, 2020)  

Effects of a low-temperature solution heat treatment (SHT) below  c ¢  solvus temperature and a high- temperature partial solution heat treatment (SHT) above  c ¢  solvus temperature on microstructural evo- lution and stress rupture properties of a directionally solidiﬁed nickel-based superalloy during long-term thermal exposure at 900   C were investigated in detail. The two heat treatments generated different microstructures, e.g., different  c ¢  sizes and surface fractions of residual  c – c ¢  eutectic phase. Carbides in the two heat-treated samples exhibited different microstructural stability during the subsequent long-term thermal exposure. The creep lifetime at 870   C/310 MPa of the samples treated by the high-temperature SHT is more than two times of that treated by the low-temperature SHT, whereas the creep lifetime of the samples treated by the high-temperature partial SHT exhibited a sharp reduction after 1000-h exposure and then decreased by a steady rate up to 10,000-h exposure. In comparison, the creep lifetime of the samples treated by the low-temperature SHT exhibits a slower reduction rate from 0- up to 10,000-h exposure.  

Keywords heat treatment, microstructure, stress rupture, thermal exposure  

# 1. Introduction  

Ni-based superalloys are widely used in power industries, such as for making gas turbine components, due to their excellent high-temperature properties (Ref  1 ). Extensive studies have shown that various microstructural changes in the gas turbine blades during long-term thermal exposure (LTTE) or service have signiﬁcant effects on the mechanical properties (Ref  2 – 8 ). Therefore, microstructural stability of gas turbine blade materials is signiﬁcantly important to the reliability of the gas turbine. The mechanical properties of nickel-based super- alloys are strongly dependent upon microstructural factors such as morphology, particle size, surface fraction, coarsening rate and composition of L1 2 -type ordered  c ¢  [Ni 3 (Al, Ti)] phase precipitated coherently with the  c  matrix (Ref  9 – 12 ) and carbides. The blades in current advanced gas turbines are exposed to extremely high temperature and stress which give rise to signiﬁcant microstructural degradation, such as MC carbide decomposition, agglomeration of  c ¢  phase and forma- tion of minor phases. MC carbides usually form in superalloys during freezing, and they are a major source of carbon for the alloy (Ref  13 ). MC carbides generally decompose into M 23 C 6 and  g  phase during thermal exposure, especially for the alloys with high contents of Ti, Ta or Hf (Ref  14 ). It is well known that microstructure of nickel-based superalloys highly depends on applied heat treatments. Therefore, it becomes crucial to optimize the microstructure of superalloys through appropriate heat treatments. High-temperature homogenization treatments are usually conducted for dissolving the  c – c ¢  eutectics, dissolving the  c ¢  phase for subsequent re-precipitation and reducing the chemical segregation of the elements (Ref  15 – 17 ). Nevertheless, recrystallization, as one of the major difﬁculties in producing and rejuvenation process of directionally solidi- ﬁed and single-crystal blades with large size and complex structure, may be induced during high-temperature homoge- nization treatments. Therefore, several attempts have been made to avoid recrystallization and reduce the cost of solution heat treatment (SHT) of single-crystal alloys by reducing both the SHT temperatures and hold times at each temperature (Ref 18 – 20 ). Effects of various homogenization treatments on the microstructure and mechanical properties have been exten- sively studied. However, effects of heat treatments on microstructural evolution including c ¢ and carbides and mechanical properties during LTTE have not been reported too much previously (Ref  21 ).  

In the present study, the low-temperature SHT below  c ¢ solvus temperature and a high-temperature partial SHT above  c ¢ solvus temperature were conducted on a hot-corrosion-resistant directionally solidiﬁed nickel-based superalloy and effects of the two homogenization treatments on microstructural evolu- tion and stress rupture properties during LTTE up to 10,000 h were investigated.

# 4.2 Effects of SHT on Creep Rupture Properties During the LTTE  

The high-temperature solution heat treatment as SHT 2 signiﬁcantly improves creep rupture lifetime of the DS Ni- based superalloy in comparison with the samples heat treated by SHT 1 mainly through the reduced levels of the  c / c ¢ eutectics and the dissolution re-precipitation of  c ¢  phase. Formation of the rafted  c ¢  structure was found in the SHT 2 rupture samples so that dislocations can no longer easily cut the lamellar  c ¢  structure (Ref  27 ). The driving force for  c ¢  rafting is proportional to the misﬁt strain. The differences in  c ¢  rafting behavior between the original samples treated by SHT 1 and 2 have been mainly attributed to dissolution of the  c / c ¢  eutectics, increase in  c ¢  surface fraction as well as the variation of lattice misﬁt. High-temperature solution treatment as SHT 2 has dissolved most primary  c ¢  phase in the DCs and partial  c ¢  phase in the IRs and decreased surface fraction of the  c / c ¢  eutectics in the IRs, which may induce a change in  c ¢  distribution and generate a higher  c – c ¢  misﬁt. After the extended exposure, the c ¢  coarsening and partial loss of coherency at the  c / c ¢  interface reduced the driving force for the rafted structure of  c / c ¢  and reduced critical stress of dislocation cutting  c ¢  precipitates, which lead to the sharp decrease in creep rupture lifetime for both the thermally exposed samples treated by SHT 1 and 2. # 5. Conclusion  

1. The low-temperature solution treatment below the  c ¢  sol- vus was mainly performed to adjust the size and mor- phology of c ¢ precipitates. In comparison, high- temperature partial solution treatment above the  c ¢  solvus temperature was mainly performed to dissolve  c ¢  precipi- tates in the dendrite cores and dissolve partially  c – c ¢ eutectics in the interdendritic regions, so the residual  c ¢ precipitates in the IRs were subjected to much more seri- ous coarsening process during cooling and thermal expo- sure process due to the diffusion of  c ¢ -forming elements in the solution heat treatment.

 2. In comparison with the samples treated by the low-tem- perature solution treatment, MC carbides were subjected to more serious decomposition and a larger amount of M 23 C 6  carbides precipitated in the thermally exposed samples treated by the high-temperature partial solution heat treatment during thermal exposure.

 3. Compared to the low-temperature solution treatment, the high-temperature partial solution treatment signiﬁcantly improves creep rupture strength of the heat-treated sam- ples by partial dissolution of  c ¢  and  c – c ¢  eutectics disso- lution. But the samples treated by the high-temperature partial solution treatment exhibit a much sharper reduc- tion of creep properties after a subsequent thermal expo- sure.
