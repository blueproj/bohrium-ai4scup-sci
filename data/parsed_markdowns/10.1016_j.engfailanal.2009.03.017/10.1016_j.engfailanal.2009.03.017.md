# a b s t r a c t  

The fracture on the blade in an aircraft gas turbine has been studied to see the cause of crack initiation. The turbine blades were made of Ni-base superalloy of MAR 200Hf grade and fabricated by DS investment casting. From the visual examination of the fractured sur- face, the turbine blade had initially cracked by a fatigue mechanism over a period of time and then fractured by the overload at the last moment. On further examination, the crack initiated at the trailing edge of the blade, and propagated by the fatigue under the cyclic loading experienced by the blade during service. The pores and segregated area of Hf and Ti at the trailing edge of the blade, caused by DS investment casting, is found by the microstructure and EDX analysis of the blade. The crack initiated at this area and was due to the stress concentration at the pores and segregation of Hf and Ti.

# 1. Introduction  

During the past few decades, the operating temperatures of gas turbine engines have been on the rise to achieve higher and higher engine power and efﬁciency. This has necessitated a continuing advancement in the temperature withstanding capabilities of materials used in their construction  [1] . To take advantage of their high temperature mechanical strength, Ni-base superalloy is traditionally used in the hot section of gas turbine engines and the turbine blades are directionally solidiﬁed (DS) with the grains oriented along the blade axis. Recently, Ni-base superalloy of MAR series is widely used for the manufacture of an aircraft gas turbine blade  [2,3] . Gas turbine blades work mostly at high temperature gradient and are subjected to high rotational velocity. High speed results in large centrifugal forces in the blades and simultaneous high temperature reduces the blade material strength. The service life of the blades is governed by the modes of degradation and failure such as fatigue, fracture, yielding, creep, corrosion and wear. Also, the casting defects such as porosity or freckle, which are resulted by a manufacturing process, can cause the failure of the in-service blade  [4,5] .  

The turbine section of F100-PW-220 engine consists of four stages of turbine stator and rotor. The last stage rotor blade was fractured during service. The ﬁrst inspection interval of the blade was 2900 h and the blade was fractured on one fourth of that. From the visual examination of the fractured surface, the blade had initially cracked by a fatigue mechanism over a period of time and then fractured by overload at the last moment. In this paper, it has been studied to see the cause and process of the fracture on the blade. The fractured blade is sampled and studied using an optical microscope, an inductively coupled plasma spectrometer (ICP) and a scanning electron microscopy (SEM). The ultimate goal of this study is to improve the safety of the aircraft and the maintenance practice.

# 3. Results and discussion  

# 3.1. Visual examination  

The fractured blade is presented in  Fig. 1 . The blade was fractured at the 7.62 mm distance from the blade root platform. The fracture path is observed to be perpendicular to the direction of maximum tensile stress. The fracture surface has three clearly distinguishable regions. Near the trailing edge, the fracture surface shows the presence of ﬂat fracture features. A lit- tle interior towards the leading edge, the fracture surface has a smooth appearance. The remaining fracture surface is very rough, which indicates the instant fracture region by overload. It is inferred that the blade fractured instantly after the pro- gress of the fatigue crack up to 30% of the cross section area.  

# 3.2. Material veriﬁcation  

Using ICP, the chemical composition of the blade was analyzed. The chemical analysis shows that the blade material is Ni- base superalloy MAR-M200Hf. The quantitative chemical compositions are summarized in  Table 1  for comparison. The mea- sured composition seems to be in agreement with the nominal composition of the Ni-base superalloy.  

# 3.3. Analysis of the fractured surface  

Fig. 2  shows the SEM fractographs of the fracture surface. Macro fra c to graphic feature is indicative of the progressive fa- tigue crack and overload fracture. At higher magniﬁcations, typical fatigue striations are observed. The fatigue crack had propagated over a distance of about 13.46 mm. From the ﬁgure, it can be inferred that the crack initiated at the trailing edge of the blade and propagated by fatigue. The remaining fracture surface shows an intergranular fracture morphology as pre- sented in  Fig. 3 , which indicates the typical feature by overload at the last moment  [6,7] .

Fig. 4  shows the SEM fractographs of the crack initiation area at the trailing edge of the blade. The defect of porosity net- work such as ﬁve isolated pores is found at the area. The regularly spaced fatigue striations are observed as shown in  Fig. 4 a and b. Fatigue striations often bow out in the direction of crack-propagation and generally tend to align in the perpendicular to the principal crack-propagation direction  [8] . Following the orientation of the striations, the fatigue crack origin is iden- tiﬁed. It is conﬁrmed that the fatigue crack appeared to have initiated at the trailing edge. Details are presented in  Fig. 5  for the SEM fractographs of the pores in the crack initiation area. The fractographic features indicate the pores, which have the cracks and segregation. It is inferred that the cracks in the pore were the secondary cracks created by ﬁnal rupture. # 3.4. Metallographic analysis  

The common strengthening mechanism of Ni-base superalloy is precipitation hardening through gamma prime ( c 0 ) pre- cipitation in the matrix  [9] . The ideal microstructure with a ﬁne ordered quasi-cubic precipitation of the  c 0   phase should be obtained during manufacture to optimize high temperature strength. This superalloy contained Hf to improve the high temperature mechanical properties and ductility  [10] . However, the  c 0   phase in the matrix was coarsened at the trailing edge from  Fig. 7 , as the distribution of Hf was not homogeneous and formed into segregation during a manufacturing process. It is inferred that the grain boundaries at the trailing edge were fragile to cyclic loading. So the small cracks could easily propagate along the segregation grain boundaries from the defect at the trailing edge, as presented in  Fig. 8 . It is suggested that the origin of the crack was the casting defect of the pores and segregation of Hf and Ti at the trailing edge of the blade and the small cracks initiated there according to vibration, thermal and mechanical stresses induced by an aircraft engine operation.  

# 4. Conclusions  

It has been studied to see the cause and process of the fracture on the blade in an aircraft gas turbine. The pores and seg- regated area of Hf and Ti at the trailing edge of the blade, caused by DS investment casting, are found by the microstructure and EDX analyses of the blade. It is shown that the grain boundaries at this area were fragile to cyclic loading. The crack ini- tiated at this area and was due to the stress concentration at the pores and segregation of Hf and Ti. The crack propagated by the fatigue under the cyclic loading experienced by the blade during service and then fractured by the overload at the last moment. It is concluded that the fracture was caused by the mechanism of fatigue-crack initiation and growth from the cast- ing defect of the pores and segregation of Hf and Ti at the trailing edge of the blade.
