# 1. Introduction  

Nickel aluminide intermetallic alloys (NiAl and Ni 3 Al) have received considerable attention for high-temperature structural and coating applications, such as ﬁrst-row vanes in industry gas turbines and heat shield for combustion chambers, due to their low density, high melting point, good thermal conductivity and excellent oxidation resistance [1–5] . However, its poor high- temperature strength and low-temperature ductility and fracture toughness limit its industry application. Therefore in order to con- quer these shortcomings, many measures have been taken [6–11] . Among the developed alloys, the NiAl-based multiphase alloy has been thought as one of most promising alloy, owing to its relative good ductility [12,13] .    The plasticity of the NiAl-base multiphase alloy can be improved signiﬁcantly by introduction of ductile phases, such as  phase and  -Cr phase which, however, decrease its high temperature mechanical properties. In addition, the previ- ous researches [14–18] reveal that the strengthening phase, such as Heusler phase and Laves phase can improve high temperature mechanical properties of the NiAl-based alloy obviously. # 2. Experiments  

Master alloy with a composition of Ni–Al26–Cr15–Co6– Ti3–Mo2–Nb0.5–Ho0.1 (at.%) (NiAl-based multiphase alloy for short) were prepared by vacuum induction melting with starting materials of 99.99% Ni, 99.9% Al, 99.9% Cr, 99.9% Co, 99.9% Ti, 99.9% Nb and 99.9% Ho respectively. The melted alloy was casted into rods and some were investigated at as-cast state. The specimens for heat treatment were cut by electron discharge machining (EDM) from the as-cast alloy. # 3.2. Microstructure evolution during solid solution treatments  

The solid solution heat treatments with different cooling rate have been applied on the NiAl-based multiphase alloy. Microstruc- ture observations on the solid solution heat-treated alloys reveal that the cooling rate has great effect on the alloy. As shown in Fig. 3 (a), the water quenching after the solid solution treatment leads to many micro-cracks in the alloy and most micro-cracks start from the interface of NiAl and the precipitating phase. Based on the XRD pattern, it can be concluded that the precipitating phases left in the HT1 alloy should be  phase. In addition, the amount of precipitating phase decreases obviously. The solid solution effect breaks the near net frame formed by the precipitating phases and spheres the precipitating phases. However, the microstructure of the alloy with the solid solution treatment followed by air-cooling is a little better, as shown in Fig. 3 (b). Clearly, no micro-crack gener- ates in the alloy, but the block precipitating phases have the sharp edges, which is harmful to the room temperature mechanical prop- erties. The small precipitating phase almost changes into the sphere shape. The XRD pattern shows that  phase increases but  phase decreases in the HT2 alloy. In addition, it also reveals that the rel- ative slow cooling rate results in more  -Cr precipitates and Ni 3 Al in the alloy. According to the researches [24,28] , the W and Mo is the elements that promotes the formation of  phase which forms and grows on the  phase. In the HT2 alloy, the  -Cr pre- cipitates expense a lot of Cr element, which restrain the growth of  phase. However the redundant Ni and Co elements promote the growth of  and Ni 3 Al phases at the NiAl phase boundary. In gen- eral, the alloys treated through solid solution process, such as water quenching or air-cooling, have a tendency that the continuously distributed gray phases likely break into semi-continuously or dis- continuously distributed ones. # 3.4. Compressive properties  

The mechanical properties of the as-cast and heat-treated alloys obtained from compression test are shown in Table 2 . The mechan- ical properties of alloys with solid solution treatment are not higher than those of the as-cast alloy, though the solid solution treatment results in great microstructure changes. Especially the HT1 alloy, its mechanical properties are much worse than those of the as- cast alloy, which should be attributed to the micro-crack generated during the water quenching. The alloys treated by solid solution treatment and short-term aging and long-term aging both have better mechanical properties. Especially the alloy with solid solu- tion treatment and short-term aging treatment, its yield strength and compressive strength are approximate two times of the as-cast alloy, and moreover the compressive ductility increases more than seven times.  

The improvement of compressive properties of the NiAl-based multiphase alloy should be ascribed to the microstructure opti- mization caused by the solid solution treatment and short-term aging. According to the former investigations [17,34] , the hard strengthening phases can well improve the high temperature strength of the NiAl alloy, but they are also detrimental to the room temperature mechanical properties. Because the refractory elements prefer to segregate on the grain boundary and form block phases during the solidiﬁcation, which increases the possibility of cracks generating during deformation. In the present study, the micro-cracks in HT1 alloy generated during water quenching well demonstrate the harmful effect of the strengthening phase. In addition, the big difference in lattice parameters between NiAl and strengthening phase increases the interfacial stress, which decreases the cohesion between phases. Therefore it is easy to understand why the alloy only with solid solution treatments possesses bad mechanical properties. With the solid solution treat- ment and short-term aging, block and segregated strengthening phases are substituted by ﬁne and homogeneous ones, and more- over ﬁne Ni 3 Al laths and  -Cr particles precipitate in NiAl matrix. These microstructure evolutions are beneﬁcial to both room tem- perature and high temperature mechanical properties. It is certain that the HT4 alloy with short-term aging treatment owns the best mechanical properties, due to its well-optimized microstructure. The long-term aging treatment reveals that the coarsening of  -Cr particle is so signiﬁcant, but the amount of Ni 3 Al lath decreases. The dispersion-strengthening effect from  -Cr particle and Ni 3 Al lath decrease a lot. Moreover the coarsening of  -Cr particle consumes so many Cr elements, which reduces the solution strengthening on NiAl matrix. Additionally   and  phases still exist in the alloy, but the quantity becomes less, which may be helpful to the room tem- perature mechanical properties. So the alloy with long-term aging treatment has better ductility than as-cast alloy, but its strength is almost the same as the as-cast alloy. Therefore it can be concluded that to such a NiAl-based multiphase alloy, solid solution treatment and short-term aging is more appropriate.  

# 4. Conclusions  

(1) The as-cast Ni–Al26–Cr15–Co6–Ti3–Mo2–Nb0.5–Ho0.1 (at.%) alloy is mainly composed of NiAl,  phase, and some  phase. In addition, Ni 2 AlTi, Cr 2 Nb and Ni 2 Al 3 Ho small particle precipitate along NiAl phase boundary. Moreover, most of the precipitat- ing phases distribute along the NiAl phase boundaries and form into a near-net-shape structure.  

(2) With increase of the cooling rate after solid solution treatment, micro-cracks are generated inside of the alloy, and most of the micro-cracks start from the precipitating phase boundary. Intermediate cooling rate changes the precipitating phases into discontinuous distribution and lower cooling rate lead to  -Cr precipitates and needle-like Ni 3 Al phase.  

(3) Lath-shaped Ni 3 Al phase and small spherical  -Cr parti- cles precipitate in the NiAl matrix after the heat treatment of 1523 K/16 h/AC + 1123 K/24 h/FC. A Nishiyama–Wassermann (NW) orientation relationship such as [0 0  1]  || [ 1 1 0]  ′  and (1 1 0)  || (1 1 1)  ′  is found between lath-shaped Ni 3 Al phase and NiAl matrix. In addition, the  phase is substituted by  and some Cr 3 Ni 2  phases, during the short-term aging treatment. The long-term aging treatment coarsens the globule-shaped  -Cr particle and decreases the lath-shaped Ni 3 Al phase a lit- tle. Moreover, the orientation relationship of [1 0 0]  || [1 0 0]  ; [1 1 2]  || [1 1 3]  ′  is found between the Ni 3 Al lath and NiAl phase.

 (4) The solid solution treatment subsequent with short-term aging improves mechanical properties of the NiAl-based multiphase alloy signiﬁcantly.
