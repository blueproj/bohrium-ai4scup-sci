# Factors contributing to the strength of a polycrystalline nickel–cobalt base superalloy  

Toshio Osada, ⇑ Nobuo Nagashima, Yuefeng Gu, Yong Yuan, Tadaharu Yokokawa and Hiroshi Harada  

High Temperature Materials Center, National Institute for Materials Science, Sengen 1-2-1, Tsukuba 305-0047, Japan Received 15 October 2010; revised 13 January 2011; accepted 17 January 2011 Available online 22 January 2011  

The strengthening behaviors of a new polycrystalline Ni–Co base superalloy are analyzed by measuring its Vickers hardness over a range of structural scales, from nano to micro. The results clearly show, for the ﬁrst time, the contributions of boundaries (grain, annealing twin and primary  c 0 ) and secondary and tertiary  c 0   precipitates in solid solutions to the total strength of such polycrys- talline superalloys.

Keywords:  Nickel–cobalt base superalloys; Precipitation; Hardness test; Nanoindentation; Atomic force microscopy (AFM)  

Polycrystalline Ni base superalloys with a two- phase structure consisting of  c -phase matrix (face- centered cubic) and intermetallic  c 0 -phase precipitates (L1 2 ) have been widely used as turbine disk materials in advanced aircraft engines owing to their excellent properties such as high-temperature strength, creep and fatigue resistance, hot corrosion resistance, and oxida- tion resistance  [1] . Superalloys develop high-temperature strength through solid solutions  [2] , grain boundaries  [3] and precipitate strengthening  [4,5] . However, the contri- bution of each strengthening factor to the total strength of polycrystalline superalloys is not clearly understood because of their complicated microstructures, which nor- mally consist of ﬁne grains (in the 10–100  l m size range) and  c 0   phases of variable sizes and volume fractions [6–10] . It is well known that trimodal distribution of the  c 0   precipitates in a matrix provides a large degree of strengthening and that the distribution varies depending on the heat-treatment temperature and cooling rate [8,10] . The primary-generation  c 0 -phase precipitates that remain undissolved during solution heat treatment at high temperature are more than approximately 1  l m in size and can be located on both the grain boundaries and within the grains themselves. The secondary-genera- tion  c 0 -phase precipitates are medium sized, ranging from approximately 100 nm to 1  l m, and they nucleate during cooling from solution temperature, and grow dur- ing aging heat treatment at an intermediate temperature.  

The tertiary-generation  c 0 -precipitates have the smallest size, i.e. less than approximately 100 nm. Therefore, it is diﬃcult to analyze the contributions of such small pre- cipitates to the superalloy strength.  

Several attempts have beenmadeto model the contribu- tion of each strengthening factor to the total strength of a typical polycrystalline Ni base superalloy  [9–11] . However, most of these models have been consistently constructed using quantitative calculation with limited experimental results  [9,10]  or using alloys with simple microstructures [11] . Therefore, these models cannot divide or indicate clearly the contribution of each generation of precipitate to the total strength. Therefore, it would be time consum- ing and costly to explore the optimum combination of  c 0 phases in a polycrystalline superalloy.  

An indentation test is one of the most common tech- niques used to extract the local material properties at the nano-, sub-micron- or micron-scale  [12–15] . In particu- lar, nanoindentation combined with atomic force microscopy (AFM) is useful for evaluating the hardness of a speciﬁc nanoscale site in a material with complex microstructures, because an in situ AFM image enables the accurate setting of a Berkovich indenter with a trian- gular pyramidal shape at the desired site  [12,13] . The National Institute for Materials Science (NIMS), Japan, has developed an empirical approach for converting indentation force–penetration depth ( F – h ) curves into the corresponding Vickers hardness  [13–15] , making it possible to obtain Vickers hardness values at scales ranging from nano- to micro-levels. Tests conducted using a micron-sized quadrangular diamond indenter have shown that its ultimate tensile strength ( r UTS ) is proportional to its Vickers hardness:  r uts    3.27 Hv  for steel  [16]  and Ni base superalloys  [10] .  

The purpose of this study was to clarify the contribu- tion of each generation of  c 0   precipitates combined with the hardening of various boundaries and a solid solution to the strength of a polycrystalline Ni–Co base superal- loy, TMW-4M3  [7] . The hardness of TMW-4M3 was measured in the nano- to micro-scale range. On the basis of the results, the strengthening mechanisms of the alloy with a complex microstructure are discussed.  

The Ni–Co base superalloy, TMW-4M3, used in this study was cut from pancake-shaped disks forged by Mits- ubishi Materials Co. Ltd.  [7] . The material fabrication processes have been described in detail else- where  [7] , and the processes for TMW-4M3 are outlined brieﬂy here. Ingots with a diameter of 500 mm were pre- pared by triple melting. The ingots were forged at 1100   C into 200 mm diameter billets. The billets were then isothermally forged at 1100   C into a 440 mm diam- eter pancake-shaped disk. Next, 5    5    2 mm specimens were cut out of the pancake-shaped disk for hardness test- ing. Heat treatments were carried out at a solution tem- perature of 1100   C for 4 h, followed by air cooling

 (AC). Similar to the aging conditions used for U720Li

 [10] , the air-cooled sample was primary aged at 650   C/ 24 h/AC and then secondary aged at 760   C/16 h/AC.  

The heat-treated samples were electrolytic ally polished in a solution of 8% perchloric acid, 70% ethanol, 10% but- oxy-ethanol and 12% H 2 O at a voltage of 40 V for 10 s. The microstructures of the specimens were observed by ﬁeld emission scanning electron microscopy (FE-SEM) using a microscope operating at 15 kV. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) was performed using a Tec- nai instrument operating at 200 kV. The specimens for TEM observation were ground to a thickness of 50  l m, and then twin-jet electropolished using a chemical solu- tion of 50 ml perchloric acid, 100 ml ethanol and 300 ml 2-butoxyethanol at 0   C and 40 V. From the obtained FE-SEM and TEM dark-ﬁeld images, the sizes of the grain and  c 0   precipitations were measured using a linear intercept method. The volume fraction of the precipita- tion was measured using a point counting method.  

Ultra-micro- (indentation force: 0.1–1 mN), micro- (1–1000 mN) and Vickers (0.196–490 mN) hardness tests were performed on the polished specimens at room temperature. For the Vickers hardness test, a quadrangu- lar diamond indenter with face angles of 136   (Akashi) was used. For the micro-hardness test, a triangular pyramidal indenter with an apical angle of 115   was used. An AFM ultra micro-hardness tester with special levers developed by NIMS was utilized for testing the hardness at the nanoscale level  [13–15] . In this study, two types of special levers with apical angles of 60   and 115   were used for the triangular pyramidal indenter. In summary, the individual contribution of  c 0   precip- itates, boundaries and solid solution to the strength of a polycrystalline Ni–Co base superalloy disk with a  c / c 0 two-phase structure was clariﬁed using three types of hardness tests including an ultra-micro-, a micro- and a Vickers hardness tests. The results presented in this paper indicate that the overall hardness,  Hv , of the alloy can be divided into  D Hv GB  +  D Hv TB  +  D Hv c 0 I ,  D Hv c 0 II , and D Hv c 0 III  +  D Hv sol , in addition to  Hv base , by investigating the converted Vickers hardness at size scales ranging from the nano to the micro. In particular, secondary  c 0 contributes greatly to the strength increment of the alloy. This implies that the optimization of the aging condition is the key to strengthening such superalloys.
