# Abstract  

In this work, a precipitation strengthened high entropy alloy was subjected to  thermo -mechanical process  in order to homogenize the grain microstructure.  Tensile tests from room temperature to 1000  o C were  conducted; microstructures were observed by scanning electron microscope and transmission electron  microscope.  Formation of cellular precipitate along grain boundaries was observed and could be related to hot  tensile ductility drop at 750  o C (1023 K). Experimental analysis has indicated that driving force for the  formation of cellular precipitates could be resulted from the chemical instability of supersaturation after  annealing and migration of grain boundaries, and this phenomenon could be suppressed either through alloy  design to increase gamma-prime solvus, and to hinder the migration of grain boundaries. This study serves as  a guideline to design composition and  thermo -mechanical process for precipitation strengthened high entropy  alloys.  

Keywords: high-entropy alloy; cellular precipitate; tensile test  

# 1 Introduction  

High Entropy Alloys (HEAs) present unprecedented composition space to design new alloys [1-3], and  several HEAs have shown to possess excellent mechanical properties, such as high hardness [4, 5], high  compressive strength [6, 7], and superior cryogenic to room temperature tensile and compressive properties  [8-10]. High hardness and compressive strength of HEAs can be associated with the formation of hard  intermetallic phases, like σ [4], laves and B2 [11], however, these brittle intermetallic phases can be  detrimental to tensile ductility. On the other hand, CoCrFeMnNi [8, 10] and CoCrFeNi [12] have shown to  possess higher strength and ductility combinations with decreasing temperatures due to nano-twinning, the  strength of these HEAs can drop dramatically at elevated temperatures due to lack of precipitation  strengthening. He  et al.  [13] designed a HEA, (FeCoNiCr) 94 Ti 2 A l4 , with L1 2  γ′ precipitate, and its room  temperature tensile strength can reach 1273 MPa. Glatzel  et al.  [14] investigated several HEAs with FCC γ +  L1 2  γ′ microstructure, and these samples were casted by directional solidification and shown to possess tensile  strength around 800 MPa until 800  o C (1073 K). So, precipitation strengthening in HEAs can be a viable  approach to obtain excellent mechanical properties at elevated temperatures. However, to the best of authors’  knowledge, there is little literatures examining microstructure evolution of precipitation strengthened HEAs  during  thermo -mechanical processing (TMP), especially the effect of  thermo -mechanical processing on tensile  properties of HEAs from room temperature to elevated temperatures has not been reported. The aim of this  article is to provide a guideline to design alloy composition and  thermo -mechanical processing for wrought  

# 2 Experiments  

The composition of the alloy of interest Al 3.31 Co 27 Cr 18 Fe 18 Ni 27.27 Ti 5.78  (JC2) is listed in Table I, for  comparative study, JC2-nCo is included to see the effect of Co substitution by Ni on the microstructure. Ingots  with dimensions of 60 mm x 20 mm x 15 mm were prepared by vacuum arc melting (VAM) under a vacuum  of 2x10 -2  torr. Homogenization heat treatment was conducted on ingots at 1150  o C (1423 K) for 6 h after  VAM. The  homogenized  ingots were reheated to 1100  o C (1373 K) in a furnace before forging by air-hammer  into 60 % reduction in thickness (20 mm to 8 mm) and then air-cooled. The forged samples were then annealed  at 1050  o C (1323 K), which was above the γ′ solvus (1022  o C (1295 K) measured by differential thermal  analysis, Pyris Diamond TG/DTA), for 30 min followed by either water quenching  (designated as JC2-TMP)  or furnace cooling  (designated as JC2-FC) . Precipitation ageing heat treatment was performed at 750  o C (1023  K) for 50 h, which is the peak-ageing condition. A homogenized sample without TMP was aged at 750  o C  (1023 K) for 50 h (designed as JC2-nTMP) and later was tensile tested in order to examine tensile behavior  with and without TMP. The alloy, JC2-nCo, was treated by the same procedure of homogenization and TMP;  due to higher γ′ solvus of JC2-nCo (1094  o C ( 1367 K)  by DTA analysis), it was annealed at 1100  o C (1373 K)  for 30 min followed by water quenching; precipitation ageing at 750  o C (1023 K) for 30 min was performed  and compared with that of JC2-TMP subjected to precipitation ageing for 30 min; the purpose was to examine  the microstructural evolution much shorter than 50 h.

# 3 Results and discussion  

# 3.1 Microstructure of the JC2 (Al 3.31 Co 27 Cr 18 Fe 18 Ni 27.27 Ti 5.78 ) Alloy  

The optical micrographs of samples aged at 750  o C  (1023 K)  for 50 h for JC2-nTMP and JC2-TMP, are  shown in Figure 2. The grain size of JC2-nTMP is over 600 μm (Figure 2(a)), and its grain structure is  anisotropic due to directional solidification by copper mold during VAM process. After hot forging, annealing,  and ageing, the equiaxed grains had been refined down to 80 μm (Figure 2(b)). Figure 3 shows the SEM  microstructure after 750  o C  (1023 K) /50 h ageing for the JC2-TMP sample. Nano size spherical precipitates  that were previously identified as L1 2  γ′ phase [16], can be seen in the matrix. The size of γ′ precipitate is  about 25 nm with volume fraction approximately 34% measured by image analysis, which is close to 33%  predicted by Thermo-Calc (TCNi8 database) [17], indicating that TCNi8 database can be applied to predict  the fractions of precipitates in this HEA. Interestingly, there is cellular precipitate along grain boundaries  (Figure 3 (c)); it appears that these cellular precipitates formed during the TMP process, i.e. during the  evolution and migration of grain boundaries. Figure 4 shows TEM analysis on the intergranular cellular  precipitates.  The bright field image, Figure 4(a), shows the intergranular cellular precipitate.  Selected-area  diffraction pattern (SADP) was conducted to characterize the phases of cellular precipitate  (Figure 4(b) and  4(c)) . SADP at area B shows a FCC diffraction patterns, and the structure of A is with extra superlattice spots  indicating the presence of L1 2  γ′ phase. The constitutes of cellular precipitates are same as those in the grains,  i.e. FCC γ matrix + L1 2  γ′ precipitate. The longitude direction of cellular precipitate is always perpendicular  to the grain boundaries, so it grew during the migration of grain boundaries. We believe that cellular precipitate  in this HEA could be the discontinuous precipitation commonly observed in numerous traditional alloy  systems [18], such as Co-W [19], Ni-Co-Al [20], Fe-Ni-Ti [21], and also in commercial superalloys [22-24].  The STEM bright filed image shown in Figure 4(d) shows better contrast of cellular structure. It can be seen  that the width of the cellular γ ′ precipitate close to the grain boundary is always wider than the part initiating  from the original grain boundary. The narrow width of cellular γ ′  precipitate at the beginning was due to fast  migration of grain boundaries (growth rate of cellular precipitate) in the beginning. γ ′  phase could also  nucleate and grow in the matrix; decreasing the degree of supersaturation at the front. Hence, the migration of  grain boundaries would be retarded by the γ ′  precipitate in matrix as it grew into sufficient size. Thus, the  growth rate of cellular precipitate in perpendicular to the grain boundary would become slower, and then the  width of cellular γ ′ precipitate would become wider (Figure 4(d)).  

The composition of intergranular cellular phases was measured by TEM-EDS and listed in Table II.

Moreover, Co, Cr, and Fe can also be detected in γ′ phase. Co can partially substitute the Ni-site in  Ni 3 Al. Fe and Cr can occupy both Ni-site and Al-site in Ni 3 Al, but the solubility of Fe and Cr in Ni 3 Al is lower  than that of Co [25].  A predicted phase composition of γ′ precipitate at 750  o C  (1023 K)  by Thermo-Calc  software (TCNI8 database) is also listed in Table II. It shows that the composition of this precipitate is very  close to that predicted. This implies that cellular precipitate was formed toward thermal equilibrium state, and  these were not resulted from elemental segregation at grain boundaries. Since these were FCC γ matrix + L1 2   γ′ precipitate in the form of equilibrium cellular precipitate, the driving force for their formation could be  related to the chemical instability of supersaturated solid solution due to water quenching during solution heat  treatment.  

# 3.2 Tensile properties of JC2 aged samples  (750  o C/1023 K for 50 h)  at different temperatures  

The results of yield strength, tensile strength, and ductility at different temperatures are listed in Table 3.  And,  the tensile properties of JC2-TMP are plotted in Figure 5 . The value of room temperature yield strength  and UTS of JC2-nTMP, were 755 MPa and 940 MPa, respectively, and the tensile ductility was around 16 %.  JC2-TMP showed better properties in both strength and ductility at room temperature; the value of yield stress  and tensile strength reached 952 MPa and 1306 MPa, respectively. The strength of JC2-TMP was enhanced  by grain boundary strengthening due to its finer grain size. Interestingly, homogeneous grain size could also  contribute to the ductility increase, because the stress distribution could be more uniform, so the tensile  ductility was 20.5 %. The presence of cellular precipitate did not appear to be detrimental to the tensile  property at room temperature.  

As the temperature increased, both yield strength and UTS decreased, but the degree of decrease were  not significant in both JC2-TMP and JC2-nTMP samples below 650  o C  (923 K),  this could be due to  precipitation strengthening by L1 2  γ′ phase and its temperature-dependent anomalous effect, which is usually  found in γ′-strengthened superalloys. The cross-slip of dislocations can change from the octahedral to the cube  plane typically around 750  o C  (1023 K) , this would cause the formation of Kear–Wilsdorf locks which can  hinder dislocation movement [26, 27]. Above 750  o C  (1023 K) , the cube slip would be easily active and then  the yield strength would drop quickly.  

Interestingly, tensile ductility at 650  o C  (923 K)  in JC2-nTMP, decreased dramatically. By contrast, TMP  processed sample still possessed moderate ductility, i.e. 13.1%, with yield strength and UTS higher than those  without TMP. A hot ductility drop of the sample after TMP occurred at 750  o C  (1023 K) , shown in Figure 5.  It appears that cellular precipitates could be very detrimental at 750  o C  (1023 K)  for HEA, this is analyzed  and discussed in the following sections. At temperature above 750  o C  (1023 K) , tensile ductility could recover  again (around 18.5 %), however the yield strength dropped significantly at 1000  o C  (1273K) , which was  getting close to its γ′ solvus.  

# 3.3 The microstructure of fractured samples  

Figure 6 shows the cross-section of fractured samples, JC2-nTMP and JC2-TMP, after tensile tests at  room temperature. The fracture mode of JC2-nTMP  (Figure 6(a) and 6(b))  is a combination of intergranular  and intragranular cracks, and the fracture surfaces are much rougher than that of JC2-TMP; JC2-TMP  (Figure  6(c) and 6(d))  has a much flatter fracture surface and shows intragranular fracture. Without TMP, coarser  grain size would result stress concentration due to inhomogeneous grain structure. With TMP, homogeneous  and finer grain size would render larger surface area of grains, so the deformation of the sample could be much  more uniform.  

Figure 7 shows the cross-section of fractured surface of samples testing at 650  o C  (923 K) . The crack  was initiated from grain boundaries in both JC2-nTMP (Figure 7(a), 7(c), and 7(e)) and JC2-TMP (Figure  7(b), 7(d), and (7f)). It appears that cellular precipitate would weaken grain boundaries. The fracture mode is  a mixture of intergranular and intragranular fracture, but JC2-TMP has less fractions of area of intergranular  fracture. It’s clear that the intergranular fracture initiated at the cellular precipitate, (Figure 7(d)). The length  of intergranular cracks can be related to the grain size. Cracks could propagate easily along grain boundary  and would be retard at the junctions of grain boundaries, so the JC2-TMP sample with smaller grain size can  possess better resistance to intergranular fracture.  

The cross-sectional micrographs of fractured sample at 750  o C  (1023 K)  are shown in Figure 8(a) and  8(b), it can be seen that cracks also initiated at the cellular precipitate resulting intergranular fracture.  Intergranular fracture was the dominant fracture mode at 750  o C  (1023 K) . Thus, the lost of ductility was  obviously due to the presence of cellular precipitates. As temperature was further increased to 1000  o C  (1273  K) , which was close to the γ ′  solvus of this HEA, the cellular γ ′  precipitates were almost dissolved back to  the matrix, shown in Figure 8(c) and 8(d), thus, the ductility recovered again. At 750  o C ( 1023 K) , the volume  fraction of  γ ′  was at its highest, hence the fraction of the cellular precipitate was also the most pronounced.

# 3.4 The formation mechanism and prevention of cellular precipitation in HEA  

Interestingly, formation of cellular precipitates along grain boundaries in HEA can occur after the  thermo-mechanical processing, although the cellular precipitates were not affecting the strength and ductility  at lower temperatures, especially at room temperature; its detrimental effect at 750  o C  (1023 K)  was obvious.  So, it is important to understand its formation mechanism and prevention measure.  

To determine the underlying mechanism of cellular precipitate formation, we can firstly examine the  state of microstructure in the as-solution heat treated condition (Figure 9), there was no apparent γ′ precipitate  in the microstructure (Figure 9(a)), only small fractions of cellular precipitate could be observed at grain  boundaries. TEM bright field image analysis confirmed that there were nano-scale cellular L1 2  γ′ precipitates   at grain boundaries (Figure 9(b) , which was confirmed by the presence of superlattice reflection (Figure 9(c)).  However, SADP within the grain , Figure 9(d),  showed a single FCC γ with no precipitates due to fast cooling  (water quench) from the annealing temperature (1050  o C,  1323 K ). Comparing the annealing sample with aged  sample (750  o C for 50 h), the cellular γ′ size at grain boundary in the former was far smaller, so the  intergranular precipitate nucleated during the annealing process and continued to grow during the later ageing  process. The reaction of discontinuous precipitation in several superalloys involving the precipitation of TCP  due to the supersaturation of refractory elements [28, 29]:  

# γ 1  + γ′ 1     γ 2  +γ′ 2  + TCP  

However, the reaction of cellular precipitation in JC2 was associated with the supersaturation of γ′-forming  elements in γ matrix due to the HEA composition:  

γ 1     cellular γ 2  + cellular γ′  

There are two conditions for the initiation of discontinuous precipitation [18]; first one is the  supersaturated matrix, the main driving force of discontinuous or cellular precipitation is chemical instability  of supersaturated matrix. Generally, small γ′ precipitates can form in the matrix during continuous cooling  after solution heat treatment, even by water quenching, in commercial Ni-based superalloys [30-32], causing  the degree of supersaturation to drop. However, small γ′ precipitates were not observed within grains of the  HEA by TEM analysis (Figure 9). Thus, the solution treated sample of JC2 alloy has greater driving force for  cellular precipitation due to higher degree of supersaturation. The second one is mobile grain boundaries;  commercial wrought superalloys have well dispersive precipitates, e.g. carbide [33] and cooling γ′ [32] along  the grain boundaries to retard the mobility of grain boundaries. However, the design of JC2 HEA did not  consider the pinning mechanism for grain boundaries, so the mobility of grain boundaries of JC2 could be  higher than that of the commercial superalloys, hence cellular precipitate was easier to form in this HEA.  

By substituting the entire Co content with Ni,  an alloy JC2-nCo was designed for comparative study,  Table 1. TMP was also conducted on JC2-nCo. The annealing temperature of JC2-nCo was increased to 1100  o C  (1373 K)  due to the increase of γ′ solvus. Figure 10(a) and 10(b) showed the microstructure of solution  heat treated sample of JC2-nCo; γ′ precipitates in the matrix could be observed, and several γ′ particles could  be observed at the grain boundary, and there was no cellular precipitate. After ageing at 750  o C  (1023 K)  for  30 min, no cellular precipitate was observed at grain boundaries (Figure 10(c)). Furthermore, the size of γ′  precipitate in the matrix grew to about 11 nm (Figure 10(d)). By contrast, JC2 after ageing showed cellular  precipitates (Figure 10(e) and the γ′ particles in matrix was too small to be resolved in SEM (Figure 10(f)).  The substitution of Co by Ni in JC2-nCo has increased of γ′ solvus due to the mixing enthalpy of Ni-Al and  Ni-Ti can be higher than that of Co-Al or Co-Ti [34]. Higher γ′ solvus implies that γ′ particles can precipitate  during cooling at higher temperature. Thus, JC2-nCo showed more γ′ after annealing than that of JC2. Judging  from the two essential conditions for the reaction of cellular precipitate, i.e. a supersaturated matrix and mobile  grain boundaries, the precipitation of γ′ during quenching in the matrix of JC2-nCo would decrease the degree  of supersaturation and retard the migration of grain boundaries, so the formation of cellular precipitation in  JC2-nCo could be suppressed.  

There is another more direct approach to eliminate cellular precipitate. If the quenching method after  solution heat treatment was changed to furnace cooling, which had slower cooling rate than that of water  quenching, more time would be available during the cooling process for γ′ particles to be developed to pin  grain boundaries and decrease the degree of supersaturation. The microstructure of the JC2-FC sample after  annealing and furnace cooling was shown in Figure 10(g). Since the sample already precipitated large amount  of cooling γ′ phase, which can reduce the driving force for cellular precipitation and pin the grain boundaries,  no cellular precipitates could be detected; Figure 10(h) shows the microstructure of the JC2-FC sample after  ageing at 750  o C  (1023 K)  for 50 h. However, the size the γ′ particles would be larger and might affect the  tensile strength of HEA. Future works are ongoing to conduct elevated temperature tensile testing on HEAs  

# 4 Conclusion  

This work has investigated the tensile properties of a HEA with and without thermos-mechanical process;  the underlying mechanisms and effects of cellular precipitations on tensile properties are presented. Without  thermos-mechanical processing, HEA showed high strength and good ductility at room temperature, but lost  ductility at 650  o C (923K). With thermos-mechanical processing, HEA showed higher strength and better  ductility than the former. However, ductility drop can occur with increasing temperature, and the ductility  reached a minimum at 750  o C  (1023 K)  due to highest fractions of cellular precipitates; the ductility can be  recovered at higher temperature above the solvus of precipitates. The driving force of cellular precipitation is  the chemical instability of supersaturation due to water quenching after annealing, and the cellular  precipitation can be suppressed through the modification of alloy composition to increase γ′ solvus, or slower  cooling rate to precipitate cooling γ′ phase to decrease the degree of supersaturation to reduce the driving force  and retard the movement of grain boundaries.
