# Microstructural evolution during transient liquid phase bonding of Inconel 617 using Ni–Si–B ﬁller metal

# Abstract  

The inﬂuence of process parameters on microstructural characteristics of transient liquid phase (TLP) bonded Inconel 617 alloy was investigated. Experiments were carried out at 1065   ◦ C using nickel based ﬁller metal (Ni–4.5% Si–3% B) with B as the melting point depressant (MPD) element. Two different thickness of interlayer and various holding times were employed. The inﬂuence of these processing parameters on the characteristics of the joint area particularly size, morphology and composition of precipitates was investigated.  

The presence of MoB, Mo 2 B, M 23 C 6 , TiC, M 23 (B, C) 6  and Ni 3 B precipitates in the diffusion layer and Ni 3 B, Ni 3 Si and Ni 5 Si 2  precipitates in the interlayer at the interface between the base metal and interlayer were demonstrated using electron back scattered diffraction (EBSD), energy dispersive spectrometry (EDS) and TEM.

# 1. Introduction  

Inconel 617 is a solid solution strengthened nickel based superalloy widely used in the aerospace, power generation and nuclear industry because of its superior mechanical proper- ties, corrosion and oxidation resistance at elevated temperatures [1–5] . Although, the good weldability of Inconel 617 allows fusion welding techniques, such as gas tungsten arc welding and gas metal arc welding, to be used successfully for joining this alloy; however, problems inherent to fusion welding reduce the life cycle of such components. The important problems of traditional fusion welding are the precipitation of brittle phases, which contribute to cracking problems in the fusion and heat affected zones (HAZ) of the weldments  [6,7] . The major pre- cipitates of Inconel 617 have been reported to be carbides and borides whose characteristics vary as a function of the aging temperature. Mankins et al.  [1]  found M 23 C 6 –TiC and Ni 3 (Al, Ti) or  γ ′   during aging under stress in the temperature range 649–1093   ◦ C and Kimball et al.  [8]  identiﬁed M 23 C 6  and  γ ′   at the grain boundary during aging in the absence of stress for tem- peratures in the range 593–816   ◦ C. Takahashi et al.  [9]  detected M 23 C 6  (Cr 21 Mo 2 C 6 )–TiC–TiN–M 6 C (Mo 3 Cr 2  (Ni, Co) C) after short and long time exposure at 1000   ◦ C and Susukida et al.  [10] reported borides in Inconel 617. Transient liquid phase (TLP) bonding is a solid state joining technique, which has not the common defects and problems associated with fusion welding [6,7,11] . The TLP process does not require high pressure, it is highly tolerant to the presence of faying surface oxide layer, it is possible to make many joints in one pass, to join dissimi- lar materials, and ﬁnally to join complex structures with better joint properties  [12] . All these advantages make TLP a potential alternative joining technique for gas turbine components made of Inconel 617 superalloy.  

In TLP bonding the ﬁller metal containing a melting point depressant (MPD) element is placed between the base metal sur- faces to reduce the bonding time. The joining operation is carried out at a temperature between the liquidus of the ﬁller metal and the solidus of the base metal. Normally, the process comprises three main stages: dissolution of the base metal, isothermal solidiﬁcation and homogenization of the bond region  [6,12] . Essentially, on holding at the bonding temperature, the ﬁller metal melts and ﬁlls the joint clearance and dissolution occurs.  

![Table 1 Nominal composition (wt%) of the base and ﬁller metal ](images/716d66aebce241d3f61c7bae3ccbd7ffdf8dd2e546f95fd864f5b6df6fbb670c.jpg)  

Subsequently, MPD element diffuses into the base metal result- ing in isothermal solidiﬁcation. After isothermal solidiﬁcation is completed, no liquid phases would remain in the bonded joints. The homogenization step is aimed to obtain similar microstruc- tures and chemical compositions of the joint (close) to the bulk of base metal. Ideally, the joints should be maintained for suf- ﬁcient holding times to allow the completion of the isothermal solidiﬁcation thereby avoiding the formation of brittle phases.  

In the present study, TLP bonding of Inconel 617 using Ni–4.5% Si–3% B ﬁller metal was investigated. The inﬂuences of ﬁller metal thickness and isothermal solidiﬁcation holding times on the width of the centerline eutectic phases, morphol- ogy, distribution and composition of second phases are reported. The results are interpreted in the framework of the classical the- ory for nucleation and growth of precipitates.  

# 2. Experimental procedures  

# 2.1. Materials  

The Inconel 617 alloy was received in the form of 1 mm thick sheet that was annealed at 1177   ◦ C for 1 h and air cooled before TLP bonding experiments. The test specimens, 25 mm  ×  5 mm, were machined out of the as received material. A boron con- taining nickel base ﬁller metal (American Welding Society designation BNi-3), with thicknesses of 25.4 and 76.2   m was employed. The ﬁller metal was cut out to the same dimensions of the base metal and placed between them. The composition of the ﬁller metal and the base metal are given in  Table 1 .  

# 2.2. Experimental techniques  

The contacting surfaces of the base metals were polished using 1200 grade SiC paper to remove the surface oxide and ultrasonically cleaned with acetone before the bonding oper- ation. The interlayer was then inserted between the two base metal coupons. A stainless steel ﬁxture was used to ﬁx the parts in order to reduce metal ﬂow during the TLP operation. The joints were placed on a boron nitride tray and held in a tube furnace for periods of time ranging from 5 min to 24 h at 1065   ◦ C under a vacuum of 1  ×  10 − 5   mbar. The eutectic temper- ature of the binary alloy Ni–B was reported to be in the range of 1080–1140   ◦ C  [13] . Therefore, the selected test temperature of 1065   ◦ C was below the eutectic temperature range of Ni–B (as suggested by Gale and Wallach  [14] ). After bonding, the specimens were polished using SiC paper and Al 2 O 3  powder of 0.05   m diameter. The specimens were etched with Kalling’s reagent (5 g CuCl 2  + 100 cm 3   HCl + 100 cm 3   C 2 H 5 OH) for opti- cal and scanning electron microscopy (SEM) examinations. In addition, some specimens were electro-polished in an electrolyte consisting of 20% percloric acid and 80% methanol for TEM examination.  

The joints were examined using light and scanning electron microscopy, ﬁeld emission gun scanning electron microscopy

 (FEG-SEM), variable pressure scanning electron microscopy

 (VP-SEM) equipped with electron backscattered diffraction system (EBSD) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM). Energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) in SEM, FEG-SEM, VP- SEM and TEM was employed for microstructural analysis. # 3.2. Chemical compositions of the precipitates  

The intermetallic compounds were mainly observed in the centerline between the interlayer and the base metal. EDS results indicated that silicon, boron and nickel were present in the liquid phase after short holding times (5 min–2 h). With longer holding times (18–24 h), it was found that only silicon and nickel were present in the interlayer with most of the silicon at the interface between the base metal and the ﬁller.  Fig. 7  shows the map anal- ysis of six different areas from the base metal to the interlayer to analyze the diffusion of boron within the joint area. It is reported that for X-ray microanalysis, the SEM should be operated at energies two or three times the critical ionization energies of the elements of interest  [16] . Thus, the 10 kV accelerating voltage was selected for map analysis, spot and line scan. This accelerat- ing voltage was optimum for light elements such as carbon and boron and heavy elements like molybdenum, chromium, nickel and cobalt in this investigation. The growth of nickel grains started from the interface into the liquid phase and the average diameter increased for longer holding times at the bonding tem- perature. In parallel, the amount of the liquid phase decreased under the same conditions and disappeared completely after the long holding times experiments. The average eutectic width was measured using optical and SEM microscopy and  Fig. 8  shows the effect of holding time at each thickness on eutectic width. Each point is an average of 50 measurements. The presence of boron was not detected after 10 h holding time in the liq- uid phase for the 25.4   m thick interlayer, while it was present for the 76.2   m thick interlayer and disappeared only after 18 h holding time. Microstructural analysis also revealed that only nickel grains remained in the interlayer after 24 h holding time indicating that such long periods are required to attain sufﬁcient homogenization.  

EDS analysis indicated that higher concentrations of Mo, Cr, B and C were present in the precipitates.  Figs. 9 and 10  display the map analysis of one platelike and one blocky precipitate in the diffusion layer after 5 min and 6 h holding times, respec- tively. Although, three types of carbides (M 23 C 6 , M 6 C, TiC) have been previously reported in Inconel 617  [9]  in the present study, the microanalysis showed that only M 23 C 6  and TiC were present in the diffusion layer. # 4. Discussion  

TLP bonding process has three major stages and the isother- mal solidiﬁcation is the most important step in this process. The formation of the intermetallic compounds when insufﬁ- cient holding times are employed (i.e. incomplete isothermal solidiﬁcation) can decrease the mechanical properties of the joints. During this period, some brittle phases are formed at the centerline of the ﬁller metal. As the microstructural exam- inations showed, with increasing holding times the amount of the liquid phase decreased and the rate of boron diffusion (MPD element) into the base metal increased. Based on the amount of boron and silicon in the ﬁller metal used in this investigation and the Ni–Si–B phase diagram, Ni 3 B–Ni 5 Si 2  and Ni 3 Si can be formed in the ﬁller metal  [20] . By increasing the holding time, the amount of boron or Ni 3 B is reduced in the ﬁller metal until complete diffusion occurred in the base metal and a total absence of Ni 3 B in the ﬁller metal. It should be noted that after 24 h and for both thicknesses of interlayer, sil- icon was not observed in the diffusion layer and it was only accumulated as Ni 3 Si and Ni 5 Si 2  precipitates at the interface of the diffusion layer and the ﬁller metal. This is probably because of the difference in diffusion rate of silicon and boron (6.22  ×  10 − 11   m 2   s − 1   for boron and 3.09  ×  10 − 14   m 2   s − 1   for sil- icon in nickel base substrate)  [14,21] . On the other hand, with sufﬁcient holding time (homogenization) the joint area reached a uniform microstructure and composition and  Fig. 3  illustrates

The metallic elements were reported to be Cr, Mo and Ni, which were found in both types of carbides.  

Both MoB and Mo 2 B precipitates were detected in the dif- fusion layer. Orthorhombic and tetrahedral crystal structures were observed for MoB, while Mo 2 B was only found with tetrahedral crystal structure as indicated in  Table 2 [22,23] . Based on the Mo–B binary phase diagram and the amount of boron in the ﬁller metal Mo 2 B precipitates should form in the diffusion layer. However, it is believed that with shorter holding times, the concentration of boron in the base metal would be higher and may lead to the formation of MoB pre- cipitates in the diffusion layer. By contrast, by increasing the holding time at the bonding temperature, the size of the MoB precipitates decreased while that of Mo 2 B increased. Finally, with sufﬁcient holding time (18 h for both thicknesses of inter- layers) only Mo 2 B precipitates were observed. In fact, the maximum solid solubility of boron in nickel is reported to be 3.7 at% at 1082   ◦ C, hence most of this amount has reacted and diffused into the base metal after 18 h holding at 1065   ◦ C [24] . Because of its small atomic size (RB = 0.0079 nm) boron diffuses very fast and segregates easily at grain boundaries [15,25,26] . Molybdenum segregation at the grain boundaries has also been reported in nickel based alloys mainly due to the large difference between the atomic diameters of Ni and Mo [25,27] . The presence of molybdenum borides in this inves- tigation can be related to the simultaneous presence of these two elements at the grain boundary. Also, using the thermo- dynamic software (FACT)  [28] , it was possible to predict that MoB precipitates will form in the ﬁrst 50   m (shorter holding time) of the diffusion layer and Mo 2 B precipitates in the 180   m (longer holding time) thick diffusion layer formed. These pred- ications were conﬁrmed by microstructural examinations as indicated in  Figs. 10 and 11 . The dissolution of precipitates was accompanied by a break-up phenomenon. It was observed that the dissolution process started from inside the grains and continued at the grain boundaries. Microstructural examinations indicated that the break-up started ﬁrst in the platelike precipi- tates leading to their separation and then continued with blocky precipitates. This is probably because platelike or needlelike precipitates are unstable in comparison with blocky precipitates especially with regards to the dissolution time for changing their size.

# 5. Conclusions  

Complete isothermal solidiﬁcation (i.e. absence of liquid phase) was obtained after 2 h holding time with an interlayer of 25.4   m thick and 6 h with 76.2   m thick.  

1- Precipitates were either platelike or blocky. Their growth started at the grain boundaries and continued towards the interior of the grains while their dissolution started from inside the grains with the occurrence of a break-up and sep- aration phenomenon. 2- M 23 C 6 , M 23 (B, C) 6  and TiC were detected inside the grains and at the grain boundaries in the diffusion layer. MoB and Mo 2 B were observed only at the grain boundaries in the diffusion layer. 3- Silicon as Ni 3 Si and Ni 5 Si 2  accumulated at the interface between the base metal and ﬁller metal after 24 h holding time for both thicknesses and was not detected in the diffu- sion layer. 5- Ni 3 B was detected in the ﬁller metal for short holding times but disappeared with increasing holding times.
