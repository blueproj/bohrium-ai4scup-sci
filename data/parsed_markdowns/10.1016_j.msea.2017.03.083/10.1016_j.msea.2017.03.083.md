# Abstract:  

Creep damage by void nucleation and growth limits the lifetime of components subjected to  mechanical loads at high temperatures. For the first time, the porosity of a Ni-based single  crystal superalloy subjected to high temperature creep tests (T ≥ 1000 °C) is followed by  ex- situ  X-ray computed tomography. A large experimental campaign consisting of nine  temperature/stress conditions is carried out to determine the kinetics of the damage  accumulation by voids. It is, indeed, essential to characterize their evolution to create internal  variables describing properly the evolution of damage in a Continuum Damage Mechanics  framework. Nonetheless, it is pointed out that the increase in the plastic strain rate during the  tertiary creep stage is not necessarily related to the increase in the pore volume fraction for the  alloy and temperature range explored (1000-1100°C). Therefore, it seems that the changes in  the microstructure, i.e. precipitation coarsening and   /  ’ topological inversion, and the  shearing of the   ’ particles have to be considered further to properly describe the damage  evolution. So, a transformative paradigm shift from the Continuum Damage Mechanics theory  to the hereby-introduced concept of Quasi-Continuum Damage Mechanics, in which the  microstructural evolution has to be considered, would be then a more suitable way to model  damage.  

Keywords: Nickel-based single crystal superalloy; Void; X-ray tomography; Damage; Creep;  Continuum Damage Mechanics

# 1.   Introduction  

The development and exploitation of gas-turbine blades casted from Ni-based superalloys in  single crystalline form has been one of the most successful industrial and commercial  ventures relating to advanced structural materials over the last twenty years. These  components are used in the hot section of the engine and are therefore subjected to high- temperature creep deformations during in-service operations [1, 2]. These alloys are  particularly efficient to bear high-temperature mechanical loads, and more specifically creep  load, for uncooled component such high pressure turbine blade turboshaft engine for  helicopter or small industrial gas turbines. This interesting property is induced by the  precipitation of a high volume fraction (close to 70 % up to 850°C) of the long-range ordered  L1 2    ’ phase which appears as cubes coherently embedded in a face-centered cubic (fcc) solid  solution γ matrix.  

The high-temperature creep properties of these alloys are often improved by increasing the  amount of refractory elements (e.g. W, Mo, Re ...) due to the solution strengthening effects of  these elements and also their ability to slow down the diffusing processes [3-5]. However, the  counterpart is an increase of the volume fractions of solidification and homogenization pores  [6, 7] which has a detrimental effect on the service lifetime of turbine blades controlled by  creep-induced cavity growth [8-10]. Three sets of pores can be brought out: solidification- pores (S-pores), homogenization-pores (H-pores), and creep deformation pores (D-pores), as  defined by Epishin et al. [11]. S-pores are formed during dendritic growth due to the macro- segregation of alloying elements which implies shrinkage cavities in interdendritic regions  [12, 13]. The formation of these pores is highly dependent on the withdrawal rate [14-18]. H- pores are due to the dendritic segregation which implies the formation of pores by Kirkendall  effect because of the difference in diffusion rates between alloying elements [19, 20]. Finally,  D-pores are created during high temperature creep tests and are highly dependent on the  amount of plastic deformation since their polyhedron shape, unlike S- and H-pores, is due to  their formation mechanism by nucleation of vacancies along slip planes [11]. The three types  of pores (H, D and S-pores) have sizes in the order of 5 to 15 µm (H and D-pore) and of 15 to  50 µm (S-pore) [6]. Porosity has already been investigated either in 3D by X-ray tomography  [7, 21-23] or in 2D by scanning electron microscopy [6, 13, 24, 25]. However, it is the first  time that porosity is characterized for various temperature/stress conditions and, most  importantly, over the lifetime of specimens subjected to high-temperature creep loading. As  such, the aim of this article is to identify the contribution of the increase in porosity during  high-temperature creep to the creep strain rate evolution.  

The first part is dedicated to the presentation of the creep experiments and of the  microstructural characterizations, namely Scanning Electron Microscopy and X-ray computed  tomography analyses (sections 2 and 3). The variations in the pore volume fraction will be  correlated with the plastic strain rate evolutions in the section 4 in order to validate/invalidate  the common Continuum Damage Mechanics (CDM) approach, as developed in [26-29], and  consisting of considering pores as the only source of damage.  

# 2.   Experimental procedure  

# 2.1   Material  

Experiments were performed using the first-generation Ni-based single-crystal superalloy  MC2. Its nominal composition is given in Table 1. Experiments were carried out on  specimens which had received the MC2 standard heat treatment (3h/1300°C/air-quenched  (AQ) + 6h/1080°C/AQ + 20h/870°C/AQ) leading to a 0.4-0.5 µm average γ’   precipitate size  and to a  ≈ 70% precipitate volume fraction (a typical as-received microstructure at the  precipitate scale is shown in Fig. 1) [30-32]  

The testing specimens had a gage length of 14 mm with a prismatic section 2 mm wide and  1.5 mm thick and were oriented close to a <001> crystallographic orientation (deviation less  than 5°). All samples were machined from MC2 rods from the same heat master and were  heat treated simultaneously (solution + aging heat treatments).  

# 2.2   Creep experiment  

Nine isothermal creep tests in tension under uniaxial stress were performed on specimens  having a 2x1.5 mm 2  cross-section (see Table 2 ) . Creep tests were performed under constant  load using a test bench equipped with a radiant furnace. Therefore, the stress increased  continuously during the experiments which can be considered negligible at 1050 and 1100°C,  but not entirely at 1000°C. The temperature was controlled using S-thermocouples spot- welded in the middle of the gage length following the TMF code of practice [33]. A  temperature accuracy of ± 2°C was achieved with such a procedure [34, 35]. Between each  interruption, elongation was recorded using a laser extensometer following the displacement  of two flags positioned on both sides of the gage length. The final creep curves were then  obtained by summing all the plastic elongation and were post-processed using a power  function for the primary and secondary creep stage and an exponential function for the tertiary  creep stage, as shown in Fig. 2. The rectangles in Fig. 3 highlight when the creep experiments  were interrupted to perform  ex-situ  X-ray tomography scans at room temperature.  

# 2.3   Microstructure analyses  

Micro-tomography was performed with a 3.5 µm resolution to get a better estimation of the  scattering in pore distributions and to be statistically representative since a large volume of  material, viz. 4.55x1.5x2.0 mm 3  (13.65 mm 3 ), was analyzed, namely 1/3 of the gage length.  The general principle of the tomography technique was already described in details in  previous papers [36, 37]. As a reminder, X-ray tomography requires an X-ray source, a  rotation stage and a radioscopic detector. A complete analysis lasting for 30 minutes is made  by acquiring a large number (900 in our case) of X-ray absorption radiographs of the same  sample under di ﬀ erent viewing angles (one orientation for each radiograph, i.e. every 0.4°). A  ﬁnal computed reconstruction step is required to produce a three dimensional map of the local  absorption coe ﬃ cients in the material which gives, indirectly, a 3D imaging of the structure.  The source/detector distance is fixed (57 cm in our case) but the resolution (defined here as  the voxel size in the reconstructed image) can be tuned by changing the position of the object  between the source and the detector (due to the conical beam). The images shown in the next  section were obtained with a standard laboratory tomograph ( Phoenix v|tome|x s  from General  Electric Measurement and Control) composed of a microfocus (the focus size is tunable  between 1 and 4 µm) X-ray tube operating at 140 keV with a 1 mm thickness copper ﬁltration  to reduce the beam hardening e ﬀ ect.  

In addition, destructive microstructural observations were also made at the last interruption of  two tests to compare the fractions of pores collected in both characterization techniques. They  were carried out using a JEOL 6100 scanning electron microscope (SEM; JEOL Ltd., Tokyo,  Japan) operating at 25 kV. Each specimen was analyzed by doing 121 images with a x350  magnification. To do so, the samples were cut parallel (surface close to a (100) plane) to the  loading axis and subsequently mechanically polished up to a mirror finish. The specimens  were not etched since the purpose of the observations was to measure the area fraction of  pores. Image processing was performed using the Visilog®   software [38] which enhances the  image features allowing better determination of the different pore sizes and their surface  fractions.  

# 2.4. Processing of the 3D images  

The analyses conducted by using the tomography in absorption contrast mode, i.e. just two  entities were distinguished: the empty space (i.e. pores) and the material made of superalloy.  Because of the limited spatial resolution used compared to the one obtained by synchrotron  radiation, the sharp boundaries between superalloy and air were visualized somewhat smeared  out in the tomograms (see Fig. 4).  

The free software Fiji ®  was used to post-process images [39]. Firstly, the contrast of images  was improved. Then, a segmentation algorithm based on the Random Forest algorithm [40]  was used to separate pores from the alloy (see Fig. 5a). Once the conversion into a binary data  set was completed, each separated object was labelled with a number. During this step,  objects with a volume below 3×3×3 voxels were assumed to be noise and therefore excluded  (see Fig. 5b). Therefore, the minimum volume detected was 1,157.6 μm 3  which corresponds  to a radius of 6.5 μm by considering the diameter of a sphere with the same volume. A  dedicated computer 3D image analysis routine implemented as a plugin in [41] was finally  used to compute the morphology (volume V, surface S, aspect ratio, centroids location, etc.)  of each pore.  

After the image processing, it is possible to identify the primary and secondary dendrite arms  as well as to notice that the pores are mainly located in the interdendritic regions where the S-  and H-pores are formed [6, 20] (Fig. 6a and Fig. 6b). It can be also noticed in Fig. 6c that the  shape of the particles is not well defined compared to what was previously observed by Link  et al. [7] who used a X-ray synchrotron radiation and a spatial resolution of 1 µm. This is due  to the spatial resolution of 3.5 µm which has been chosen in this study in order to have the  best metrological resolution/time to scan/spatial resolution compromise and which does not  allow to have properly defined pore contours.

# 3.2   SEM Analysis  

Two specimens were also characterized by SEM at their final interruption. One corresponds  to the condition 1050 °C/160 MPa that is clearly in the secondary creep stage (see Fig. 3b)  

and the second corresponds to 1100 °C/160 MPa that is in the tertiary creep stage and close to  failure (see Fig. 3c). 8.1 mm 2  and 7.5 mm 2  were analyzed and led to pores area fractions of  0.217 % and 0.262%, respectively. It has to be compared to the results found by tomography,  viz. 0.146 % and 0.224 % (see Table 3). It is worth noticing that no deformation pore was  observed with transversal cracks contrary to large pores, as seen in Fig. 8c, d, e, and f. The  pore size distributions measured by SEM and X-ray tomography are also compared in Fig. 9.  

# 3.3   Plastic Strain Rate and Evolution of the Pore Volume Fraction  

The evolution of the plastic strain rate as a function of time has been plotted at 1000°C (Fig.  10), 1050°C (Fig. 11), and 1100°C (Fig.12) and compared to the volume fraction of pores to  highlight if the increase in the plastic strain rate in the tertiary creep stage is directly related to  an increase in the pore volume fraction.  F v  instead of  ΔF v  has been plotted because the initial  level of porosity has an effect on the secondary plastic strain rate, as shown by Reed  et al.   who compared creep experiments at 1150°C/100 MPa using HIPed and un-HIPed specimens  [9].  

Since the creep experiments at 1050°C did not reach the tertiary creep stage, no noticeable  increase in the plastic strain rate was recorded (see Fig. 11). Also,  F v  seems to increase  linearly and no obvious change in the rate happens. A similar result is found for the condition  1000°C/200 MPa (Fig. 10b). In the case of experiments experiencing a tertiary creep stage,  two cases have to be considered: no increase in  F v  was measured, namely 1000°C/180 MPa  (Fig. 10a) and 1000°C/300 MPa (Fig.10c), and an increase in  F v  was recorded, viz. for  1100°C/160MPa (Fig. 12b) and 1100°C/180MPa (Fig. 12c). We exclude the condition  1100°C/140 MPa (Fig. 12a) from the second case since the increase in the volume fraction is  due to the failure of the specimen.  

# 4.   Discussion  

The first part of the discussion will be dedicated to verify that X-ray computed tomography  can be used to characterize the damage by pores during high-temperature creep tests. Then,  the evolution of the porosity given by the tomographic measurements will be compared to the  evolution of the plastic strain rate for each temperature/stress condition in order to investigate  if pores are the only factor responsible for triggering the tertiary creep stage, as the  Continuum Damage Mechanics theory states it and as proposed by Kachanov [46] and  Rabotnov [47] for modeling creep damage.  

# 4.1   Is the spatial resolution an issue?  

Spatial resolution is an important matter when it comes to characterize porosity. One question  may raise regarding the capability of capturing small pores and therefore nucleation due to the  spatial resolution that has been chosen in this study even if the nucleation of pores is not its  purpose. The discrepancy between the two characterization techniques explored, viz. 2D  characterization using SEM observations and 3D characterizations using XCT, is underlined  by smaller volume fractions with X-ray tomography (0.146% and 0.224%) compared to the  ones with SEM image analysis (0.217% and 0.262%, respectively). Differences of 0.071%  and 0.038% (relative errors of 32.7% and 14.5%, respectively) are therefore achieved. It is  caused by a better spatial resolution with SEM allowing to get smaller pores such as  nucleating (Fig. 8a and b) and homogenization pores (D- and H-pores, respectively).  However, it seems that the relative error will be more or less important depending on the  creep stage characterized: the difference will be smaller in the tertiary creep stage during  which large pores have more important effects mainly due to transversal cracks developing  from their boundaries [48, 49] and the coalescence of pores leading to a larger ratio of big  pores [9, 50]. So, the X-ray measurements underestimate the actual volume fraction of pores  but give similar pore distributions than SEM (see Fig. 9). The purpose of this work is to track  changes in the volume fraction of pores during high-temperature creep testing through the  study of a statistically reliable and representative volume element (R.V.E.) of material.  Because large pores are more important regarding lifetime prediction [48], the spatial  resolution chosen for the tomographic scans is not detrimental with regards to the tracking of  damage leading to failure, even if small but dense pores may have a large effect on creep life  [49]. In the following, it is hence assumed that the porosity-induced increase in creep strain  rate is mainly controlled by the evolution of the larger pores captured by XCT analyzes.  

# 4.2   Evolution of the pore volume fraction and plastic deformation  

Fig. 7 brings to light that the evolution of pore volume fractions depends on time as well as on  the amount of plastic deformation. In fact, pores play a very important role in maintaining the  generalized plastic flow during creep at high temperatures (T > 1000°C): voids are internal  free surface causing dislocation annihilations [51]. Actually, due to the combination of elastic  and osmotic forces, γ/γ’ interfacial dislocations move transversely to the applied stress by a  combined glide-climb process, which produces both a longitudinal plastic deformation and  vacancies [52]. The removal of vacancies by dislocation climb in the framework of plastic  deformation is required. In superalloys, this removal takes place at the surface of the  specimen (external removal) and at the pores located in the interdendritic regions (internal  removal). Since the characteristic distance of dislocation climb to the surface of samples is  much larger than towards pores, vacancies preferentially diffuse there causing then an  increase in pore volume fraction. Then, pores are both generating dislocations by localizing  stresses and fostering dislocation annihilations, as seen by Agudo Jácome   et al.  who have  documented elevated dislocation densities close to cast micro-pores in inderdendritic regions  after only short periods of high temperature and low stress creep periods [53].  

4.3   Toward a better understanding of the damage evolution during high temperature  creep in Ni-based single crystal superalloys  

The nucleation and growth of pores are due to the diffusion of vacancies that is assisted by  dislocations [54-56] via pipe-line diffusion [57, 58]. In the meanwhile, pores generate stress  concentrations that act as sources of dislocations and vacancies [59] in order to relief the  stresses [60, 61]. Therefore, pores need dislocations to grow faster and generate dislocations  which will subsequently support the diffusion of vacancies toward pores. However,  dislocations are not necessary to the pore growth, as shown by the Gaussian-type increase in  the pore volume fraction which was measured during homogenization processes [19, 20]. The  authors explained the peak by a loss of vacancies via annihilation at the specimen surface,  whereas the vacancy generation by cross-diffusion of atoms gradually slows down. Contrary  to a homogenization process, there is no slowdown in the vacancy generation during high- temperature creep tests which leads to a monotonous increase in the pore volume fraction, as  shown in Fig. 13 highlighting a power law relation between the  F v  rate and the secondary  plastic strain rate.  

The CDM theory, on which the effective stress concept is based, considers that damage is a  discontinuity of matter, namely voids and cracks [26-29]. This lies on the fact that the CDM  approach is used for predicting the initiation and propagation of cracks leading to the final  failure of the materials. The Rabotnov-Kachanov (RK) formalism [46, 47], which is usually  used for the modeling of creep damage is based on this concept. However, back in the 60’s,  researchers did not have the present technology for  in situ  or  ex situ  characterization of the  microstructure evolution with high spatial and time resolutions. So, the following questions  are raised: are pores just seeable markers of damage which also affect the damage kinetics? or  are they the underlying cause of the tertiary creep stage leading to the final failure? These two  questions are then similar to: what is damage in Ni-based single crystal superalloys exposed  to high-temperature creep loading? In fact, in one of our previous paper [35], we found that  damage is not just voids and cannot be predicted by RK-type laws based on a stress level.  

Actually, the authors improved the lifetime of a second generation single crystal superalloy by  introducing a 30s thermal jumps at 1200°C under stress when the absolute value of the  effective lattice misfit is maximal. Since the volume fraction of pores cannot be decreased and  was even possibly increased by introducing such a harsh event, the increase in the lifetime is  caused either by a decrease in the damage level or by a long-term decrease in the damage  kinetics. Either way, it cannot be predicted by the RK law which always predicts an increase  in the damage level and in the damage kinetics when the temperature is increased. The same  conclusion can be expressed about the results obtained in [62, 63] in which thermal cycling  led to shorter lifetimes compared to the isothermal condition, which is also in contradiction  with the RK theory since lower temperatures should lead to slower damage kinetics and,  therefore, to better lifetimes.  

For the first time,  ex situ  X-ray tomography characterizations have been done at different  temperature and stress levels in the present article in order to determine if the CDM theory is  missing the fundamental reason of the increase in the plastic strain rate during the tertiary  creep stage. The condition 1100°C/160 MPa (Fig. 12b) brings out that the regime of  F v  is  changed, viz. large increase in  F v , after that the tertiary creep stage is triggered which means  that the tertiary creep stage is not directly due to the increase in the volume fraction of pores.  The same result would have been obtained at 1100°C/180 MPa (Fig.12c), but no X-ray  tomography characterization has been done right after the beginning of the tertiary creep  stage. Therefore, an increase in  F v  happened, but it is not possible to determine if that was  either in the secondary creep stage or in the tertiary creep stage. However, the conditions  1000°C/180 MPa (Fig. 10a) and 1000°C/300 MPa (Fig. 10c) point in the same direction that  Fig. 12b, namely the increase in  F v  starts when the increase in the plastic strain rate is already  well established. Therefore, the increase in the plastic strain rate during the tertiary creep  stage is due to a different process, like the shearing of the   ’ particles for instance, which is  probably fostered by the porosity and other defects, such as intermetallic phases, that are  modifying the microstructure and the state of stresses around them [64-66]. If it is obvious  that at 1000°C (volume fraction higher than 50%) the changes in the microstructural state via  a   /  ’ topological inversion [67, 68], viz. the   ’ phase is surrounding the    phase instead of the  opposite which leads to a negative phase connectivity parameter Na (see Figs. 14, 15, and 16)  as defined by Caron  et al.  [68], will support a higher amount of dislocations entering into the   ’ particles [11]. This parameter obtained by using the Visilog® software as well as specially  tailored algorithms that enhanced image features has even been used by Arnoux  et al.  [69] as  a damage marker in the case of a fourth-generation single crystal superalloy, viz. MCNG. On  the contrary, no   /  ’ topological inversion (Na>0) has been observed at the other temperatures  (see Figs. 17 and 18). It is probably the reason why a higher energy is required to trigger the  tertiary creep stage that will start because of the destabilization of the   /  ’ interfacial  dislocation network that is formed during the primary creep stage and protects against   ’  shearing [70, 71]. The shearing of the   ’ phase has already been demonstrated to be a  “damage mechanism” [72-74] in a sense that it leads to an increase in the creep rate since the  strengthening phase does not fulfill its function of restraining the propagation of dislocations  in the    channels anymore.  

One can argue that hot isostatic pressing (HIP) slightly improves creep lifetimes [9, 75, 76]  which might mean that pores are the main source of damage. However, by almost eliminating  the initial porosity, there are less loci of stress localization which means that there are less  local sources of softening and crack initiation at the largest pores, namely solidification and  homogenization pores [48, 49]. A higher density of defect leads to faster plastic strain rates  [9, 77] and premature failures, as shown by le Graverend  et al.  [49] who investigated the  discrepancy in lifetime at 1050°C/160 MPa and determined that a high density of small pores  can lead to shorter lifetimes as large pores can. Therefore, decreasing the volume fraction and  the density of pores may just delay the massive shearing event of the   ’ particles which leads  to better lifetimes.  

Therefore, a transformative paradigm from continuum damage mechanics to quasi-continuum  damage mechanics approach (“quasi” since the new models will not directly represent the loss  of a load-carrying capacity due to the increase of porosity, as defined in [26-29, 46, 47]) has  been formulated by finding that the increase in the plastic strain rate during the tertiary creep  stage is not directly due to an increase in the volume fraction of pores, but probably due to a  shearing of the   ’ rafts whose   /  ’ interfacial dislocation network is destabilized and whose  regularity is undermined by a topological inversion.  

# 5.   Conclusions  

For the first time, a high-temperature creep campaign at different levels of stress and  temperature has been followed by  ex-situ  X-ray computed tomography. It led to characterize  the evolution of the porosity in terms of volume fraction during creep loading in a first- generation Ni-based single-crystal superalloy. The experimental results have been used to  understand what triggers the tertiary creep stage, i.e. the increase of the plastic strain rate. It  was shown that this increase occurred before the increase in the volume fraction of pores.  Therefore, the decrease of the load carrying capacity, viz. the cross section area, due to the  increase in the volume fraction of pores is not responsible for the onset of the tertiary creep  stage. Thus, it seems that the Continuum Damage Mechanics theory is not adapted to describe  the damage mechanisms that are involved and lead to a fast increase in the volume fraction of  pores. However, pores are still easy-access quantifiable markers of the damage level and  promote a higher amount of dislocations that will shear the   ’ particles. As a result, a  transformative shift needs to be operated toward a Quasi-Continuum Damage Mechanics  (QCDM) theory in which the microstructural state is considered and will help in improving  lifing predictions.
