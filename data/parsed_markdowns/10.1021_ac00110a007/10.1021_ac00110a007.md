# Water Solubility Measurements in Supercritical Fluids and High-Pressure Liquids Using Near-lnfrared Spectroscopy  

Kevin Jackson, Lawrence E. Bowman, and John L. Fulton\*  

Chemical Methods and Separations Group, Chemical Sciences Department, Pacific Northwest Laboratory, Richland,Washington99352  

A small amount of water added to a supercritical fluid can greatly increase the solubility of polar species in nonpolar fluids. These modified supercritical solutions significantly expand the use of the fluids in separations and reactions. In order to successfully utilize these systems, information on the miscibility or solubility of water in the fluid is required. Often solubility data are not available for water in a supercritical fluid under a given set of temperature and pressure conditions, and a costly set of equipment must be assembled in order to make these measure- ments. A relatively fast and inexpensive technique to measure water solubilities using a simple long path length optical cell in an FT-IR spectrometer is described. This techniqueis also applicable tocommon and newly devel- oped refrigerants where water solubilities are often un- known at temperatures much above ambient. In this paper, water solubility data in carbon dioxide and two types of refrigerants (chlo rod i flu oro methane, R22; 1,1,1,2- tetra flu oro ethane, R134a) are presented for temperatures from approximately 40 to 110 °C and pressures from approximately 10 to 344.8 bar.  

For many systems, miscibility data can be determined from thebinary phase equilibria derived from direct visual observation without the problems associated with taking discrete samples from a pressure vessel.1.2 For the binary system of water in a nonpolar supercritical fluid, however, the very low water solubility (<2.0 mol %) usually precludes use of these direct methods since it is difficult to visually detect the very small volumes of the liquid phase. In liquefied gases, at low temperatures and ambient pressures, water is normally quantified using Karl Fischer titration, grav i metric methods(phosphorus pentoxide),orelectric conduc tometric methods.3 At higher temperatures and pressures, these methods become difficult to perform and more elaborate systems have to be devised. Direct fluid injection mass spectrometry DFI- MS)has been suggested as an alternative technique,+butthis method requires expensive equipment.Alternative spectroscopic techniques that are easier to implement include Raman and internal reflectance IR spectroscopy.However,bothof these techniques suffer from a lack of sensitivity when the solute concentrations are very low, as for the case of water solubility in nonpolar solvents.Direct UV spectroscopic measurement is another commonly used solubility tool that is employed for high pressure systems but this technique is not suitable for species suchaswater that lack spectral transitions in theUVregion  

In this paper, we use a simple, high-pressure near-IR technique to measure water solubility. Previously, near-IR spectroscopy was used for measuring the solubility of alkanes in carbon dioxide by Swaid et al.5 through the application of the Beer-Lambert law. Near-IR spectroscopy is also well suited for water solubility measurements in high-pressure solutions. Water bands in the near-IR region are generally free of interfering bands from the supercritical solvents studied, including CO2 and the several refrigerants reported in this work. Also, one can use long path length optical cells (\~5 cm) that are common equipment in most high-pressure laboratories.Incontrast,studiesinthe mid-IR region require special windows in complex cells having short path lengths (\~200 μm).In summary, the near-IR method is preferred over the mid-IR approach since (i) a simple, long path length transmission cell can be used,(ii) water has strong absorbance bands in the near-IR region, and (ii) generally the solvent bands are noninterfering with water bands.  

Our primary interest is to describe a method for water solubility measurements in supercritical fluids, but the method could equally be used for high-pressure liquids. A supercritical fluid is a substance that is above its critical point--thecondition that can be uniquely defined by the critical temperature,T,and critical pressure, Pe, of the substance. The critical point of a pure substancelies attheapexof thevapor/liquid equilibrium curve. In the supercritical region, the high compressibility allows the properties of the solutions to be adjusted over a wide range by making modest changes in the pressure of the system. Another region of interest is the near-critical liquid phase lying just below the critical temperature,where the liquid still exhibits a great deal of compressibility when compared to that of aconventional liquid far below the critical point. The water solubility can be related to the three types of intermolecular interactions:solute/solute(1),solute/solvent(II), and solvent/solvent (lIn). # CONCLUSIONS  

We demonstrate a simple method to obtain solubility informa- tion for water in supercritical fluids and subcritical or near-critical liquids including refrigerants.The method can be implemented with the minimum amount of special equipment: a conventional FT-IR spectrometer is fitted with a simple, longer path length optical cell. The method is particularly well suited for water solubility determination in supercritical fluids since (1) water vibrational bands are generally clear of solvent bands, (2) water bands have strong absorbance in the near-IR region, and (3) the low solubility of water in nonpolar fluids allows the use of long path length cells. The method is based upon identifying the discontinuity in the relationship between the absorbance and overall water concentration at the point of saturation. The technique is, for the most part, free from problems associated with changes in the specific absorbance of the solute due to changes in the solvent environment (through density manipula- tion) or changes in the amount of solute/solute interaction, e.g., intermolecular hydrogenbonding.Therefore,it mayalsobe possible to extend this technique to study other liquid modifiers in supercritical fluids.  

For the systems studied in this paper, a near-linear relationship was observed between the peak area and water concentration up to the saturation point of the fluid. The water solubility was quite low in carbon dioxide, as one would expect for a highly polar solute in a nonpolar solvent. Due to the refrigerants containing repulsive fuorine atoms, the water solubility in these solvents was also quite low even though they have relatively high dipole moments.Increasingthetemperatureorthedensityofthe super critical phase slightly increases the water solubility in the three solvents studied, CO2 ,R22, and R134a.
