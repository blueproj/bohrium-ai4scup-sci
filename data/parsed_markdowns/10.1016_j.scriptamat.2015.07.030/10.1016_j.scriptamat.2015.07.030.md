# Novel wrought  c / c 0   cobalt base superalloys with high strength and improved oxidation resistance  

S. Neumeier   ⇑ , L.P. Freund, M. Göken  

Friedrich-Alexander-Universität Erlangen-Nürnberg (FAU), Materials Science & Engineering, Institute I, Martensstr. 5, 91058 Erlangen, Germany  

# a r t i c l e i n f o  

# a b s t r a c t  

Article history: Received 5 June 2015 Revised 20 July 2015 Accepted 21 July 2015 Available online 30 July 2015  

New polycrystalline Co-base superalloys with  c / c 0   microstructure have been developed, which show excellent mechanical properties and enhanced oxidation behavior. The  c 0   solvus temperature of these alloys is comparatively low giving a large processing window. The yield strength of these alloys is similar to that of wrought Ni-base superalloys. However, the creep strength at 750   C is largely exceeding that of the Ni-base superalloys Waspaloy and Udimet 720Li. Oxidation tests indicate that the oxidation resistance is comparable to commercial Ni-base superalloys.

Co-base superalloys consisting of a fcc  c  Co solid solution hard- ened by  c 0   Co 3 (Al,W) precipitates with L1 2  crystal structure have attracted much attention since their discovery  [1] . In the past, other intermetallic L1 2  phases like Co 3 Ti  [2]  or Co 3 Ta  [3]  were con- sidered as reinforcing phase, but these phases were either not stable at high temperatures  [3,4] , the  c / c 0   lattice misﬁt was too large  [2–4]  or the  c 0   volume fraction was rather low. In some cases also discontinuous precipitation occurred  [2,4,5]  which deterio- rates the creep properties. In contrast, Co 3 (Al,W) hardened Co-base superalloys exhibit identical microstructures as  c / c 0 Ni-base superalloys and show very promising properties. Due to the higher melting point of Co, they have higher solidus and liq- uidus temperatures  [6,7]  and they show less segregation  [7] . The lattice misﬁt is moderate  [1,8,9] , the ﬂow stress is comparable or even higher than in Ni-base superalloys above 900   C  [10]  and the creep properties are similar to ﬁrst generation Ni-base superal- loys  [6,11] . Moreover, the  c 0   solvus temperature in Co-base super- alloys is usually lower compared to that in Ni-base superalloys though a higher  c 0   volume fraction can be achieved at typical appli- cation temperatures of wrought alloys. This makes them very interesting high temperature structural materials with high strength that are suitable to be produced by conventional casting and hot forming. However, the previously investigated  c / c 0 Co-base superalloys have a poor oxidation resistance  [12]  and high density  [13] . The goal of this work was to develop polycrystalline c / c 0   Co-base superalloys with good workability, high strength and  

good oxidation resistance to compete with polycrystalline wrought Ni-base superalloys.  

Therefore, two Co-base superalloys, CoWAlloy1 and CoWAlloy2, are investigated and compared with the commercial wrought Ni-base superalloys Udimet 720Li and Waspaloy ( Table 1 ).  

Both Udimet 720Li and Waspaloy exhibit a polycrystalline two-phase  c / c 0   microstructure and are widely used in gas turbine applications. Udimet 720Li is a high strength alloy due to the high c 0   fraction and Waspaloy has a comparably high creep strength for its rather low  c 0   fraction  [14,15] .  

To improve the properties of ternary Co–Al–W alloys several alloying elements are added: Ni is used to stabilize the Co 3 (Al,W) phase and to widen the narrow  c / c 0   two-phase region  [16] . The ele- ments Ti, Ta and Hf partition to the  c 0   phase  [17–20]  so they enhance the phase stability and increase slightly the  c 0   solvus tem- perature  [5,6,21] . Ti replaces W in  c 0   and is therefore used for low- ering the density  [5] . Ta is beneﬁcial for improving the  c 0   hardness and the yield strength  [10,20] . Cr is essential for oxidation and cor- rosion resistance and partitions preferentially to the  c  matrix where it acts as solid solution hardener. B improves the grain boundary strength, ductility  [22]  and oxidation resistance  [12] . Si reduces the oxide scale thickness  [12]  and supports the formation of chromia oxide scale by the formation of silica  [23,24] . Zr and C are used for grain boundary strengthening and formation of carbides.  

The Co-base superalloys were vacuum induction-melted from raw elements, cast, solution heat treated at 1250   C for 3 h in air, annealed at 1100   C and hot rolled at Vacuumschmelze GmbH & Co. KG, Hanau, Germany. Afterwards a 3-step heat treatment was

performed to recrystallize and age the alloys: (1) 1050   C/4 h (oil quench) for CoWAlloy1 and 1000   C/4 h (oil quench) for CoWAlloy2, (2) 900   C/4 h and (3) 750   C/16 h. Differential scanning calorimetry analysis (DSC) was conducted on disc specimens with 4 mm diameter and a mass of about 110 mg with a heating/cooling rate of 5 K/min using a Netzsch STA 409 CD for determining the phase transformation tempera- tures. The density was measured using Archimedes’ principle. The characterization of the microstructure was carried out in a Zeiss Crossbeam 1540 EsB focused ion beam microscope. Electron backscatter diffraction was performed with a Nordlys2 detector of Oxford Instruments for measuring the grain size. Samples were cut from the hot rolled and heat treated bars, and subsequently ground and polished. Compression tests were performed at RT, 600   C, 750   C, 800   C and 850   C on cylindrical specimens with 3 mm diameter and 4.5 mm length to determine the yield strength. The high temperature creep strength was investigated in compres- sion on cylindrical samples at 750   C/620 MPa. The oxidation behavior was characterized on polished samples by oxidation tests at 750   C for 1000 h and at 900   C for 50 h.

This provides the possibility to produce high strength superal- loys with good processability. The 40   C higher  c 0   solvus tempera- ture of CoWAlloy1 compared to CoWAlloy2 is due to the higher contents of Ti and Ta  [5,6,13,21] .  

After hot rolling and heat treatment both Co-base superalloys show a recrystallized microstructure with ﬁne grains and car- bides/borides at the grain boundaries ( Fig. 2 ). The median grain size of both CoWAlloys is below 8  l m and similar to that of Udimet 720Li, while Waspaloy has much bigger grains of about 33  l m. Primary  c 0   precipitates with a size of 0.5  l m and 0.2  l m, that are useful to pin the grain boundaries during the sub-solvus recrystallization heat treatment, and secondary  c 0   precipitates with a size of about 60 nm are present in CoWAlloy1 and CoWAlloy2. The different  c 0   shapes indicate a larger  c / c 0   lattice misﬁt in CoWAlloy1 than in CoWAlloy2. This is mainly ascribed to the higher content of Ti and Ta in CoWAlloy1 which do not only parti- tion preferentially to  c 0   but also increase strongly its lattice param- eter  [5,9] . The density of both Co-alloys is around 8.8 g/cm 3   which is much lower compared to other  c / c 0   Co-base superalloys  [13] with CoWAlloy1 having a slightly lower density as a consequence of the stronger replacement of W by Ti.  

Fig. 3 a shows the yield strength of all investigated alloys together with literature data  [10,29] .  

CoWAlloy1 and CoWAlloy2 possess a much higher compressive yield strength than the conventional carbide and solid solution hardened Co-alloy Mar-M509  [29]  and  c / c 0   Co-base superalloy C o–8.8Al–9.8W–2Ta  [10]  which is attributed to the presence of  c 0 precipitates and the smaller grain size, respectively. In comparison to Udimet 720Li and Waspaloy a similar strength is achieved although the  c 0   fraction is higher. This could be caused by different deformation mechanisms and generally lower anti-phase bound- ary (APB) and stacking fault (SF) energies, as found in single crys- talline Co-base superalloys  [30] , that reduce the resistance of the  c 0 precipitates against penetration. At temperatures T  P  800   C CoWAlloy1 exhibits an even higher yield strength compared to Udimet 720Li. This shows that the strengthening effect of the  c 0 precipitates in Co-base superalloys is shifted towards higher tem- peratures although the  c 0   solvus is typically lower. Suzuki et al.  [10] observed similar results for a Co–8.8Al–9.8W–2Ta alloy, and con- cluded that this is caused by the high density of stacking faults formed in the  c 0   precipitates above the peak temperature. Knop et al.  [17]  stated that the observed shift of the breakdown of the ﬂow stress anomaly to higher temperatures in their polycrystalline Co–Ni superalloy is possibly caused by the high W content that restricts diffusion and hence thermally activated slip in the  c 0 precipitates.  

The excellent mechanical properties of both  c / c 0   Co-base super- alloys become particularly evident when comparing the creep properties at 750   C ( Fig. 3 b).  

Obviously, both CoWAlloys have higher creep strength than Waspaloy which is most likely due to the higher  c 0   fraction. But they even have a superior creep strength compared to

Udimet 720Li despite containing a similar  c 0 fraction. The minimum creep rate is about 30 (CoWAlloy1) respectively 2 (CoWAlloy2) times lower than that of Udimet 720Li. This means that both CoWAlloys possess even signiﬁcantly higher creep strength when the creep strength is related to the density, as the density of the CoWAlloys is less than 10% higher than that of the investigated Ni-base superalloys. The reason for this high creep strength could be the high content of slowly diffusing W  [31]  that retards diffusion controlled processes, the fact that less primary  c 0 particles are present at the grain boundaries compared to Udimet 720Li, which means that the intragranular  c 0   precipitate fraction is much higher, or different creep deformation mecha- nisms. In comparison with each other, CoWAlloy1 shows a signif- icantly higher strength than CoWAlloy2 which can be a result of the strengthening effect of Ti and Ta  [10,20] , which are also known to increase the APB energies in Ni 3 Al and thus the resistance against shearing  [32] . Further reasons could be the larger lattice misﬁt or other effects such as differences in SF energies.  

At this point it’s also worth to mention that it’s known from Ni-base superalloys that single crystalline  [33]  and polycrystalline [34]  superalloys can exhibit a tension/compression asymmetry in yield and creep strength. Sondhi et al.  [34]  ascribed the tension/- compression asymmetry in the polycrystalline turbine disc superalloy IN 100 to the internal stresses due to the different lattice parameters of  c  and  c 0 . This means that the observed mechanical properties may differ to those under tension, however, since all alloys investigated in this study should have a positive lattice misﬁt, such as Udimet 720Li  [35] , the order of the investi- gated alloys in terms of tensile creep strength should hardly be affected by this factor.  

However, not only superior creep properties are required for high temperature applications, but also a good oxidation resis- tance. Both CoWAlloys exhibit a similar oxide scale morphology ( Fig. 4 a) and a strongly improved oxidation resistance compared to previously investigated  c / c 0   Co-base superalloys  [12,13] . The for- mation of thick Co-oxides at the surface could be suppressed and the upper oxide layer consists of a protective chromium oxide with a thinner silicon oxide underneath. Si therefore seems to con- tribute to the good oxidation resistance which is consistent with literature  [23,24] . The innermost oxide scale consists of an irregu- lar but continuous alumina layer and no phase transformation of the  c / c 0   microstructure has taken place which indicates good phase stability. The oxide scale is more than one order of magnitude thin- ner than that of the ternary  c / c 0   Co-alloy Co–9Al–9W  [12]  and even slightly thinner than that of Udimet 720Li and Waspaloy after annealing for 50 h at 900   C ( Fig. 4 b).  

At lower temperatures of 750   C the oxide scale morphology is identical after heating for 1000 h and an oxide scale thickness of only 10  l m and 6  l m for CoWAlloy1 and CoWAlloy2 is present which shows again their good potential.  

In summary, new  c 0   hardened Co-base superalloys with promis- ing properties have been developed. Due to the very high solidus temperature and low solvus temperature CoWAlloy1 and CoWAlloy2 possess a large processing window. After hot rolling and heat treatment, a microstructure with ﬁne grain size and high  c 0   fraction of more than 50% is generated. The yield strength of both CoWAlloys is quite high and that of CoWAlloy1 even exceeding Waspaloy. At 750   C both Co-alloys show excellent creep strength with CoWAlloy1 being by far superior to the investigated Ni-base superalloys. The oxidation resistance of CoWAlloy1 and CoWAlloy2 is much better than that of previous Co 3 (Al,W) hardened Co-base superalloys due to a high Cr content and additions of Si. These results show that the newly developed Co-base superalloys are promising cast and wrought alloys with a high temperature capability in particular at temperatures above 700   C.
