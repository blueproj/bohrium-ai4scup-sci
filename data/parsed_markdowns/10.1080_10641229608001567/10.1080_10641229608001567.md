# Introduction  

Fullerenes C60 and C7o,the all-carbon molecules withclosed- cage structures, have been found naturally in the Russian meta- anthracite Shungite1 and in a fulgurite2 . They were also found to be associated with an impact pit on an Earth-circling satellite3 , with soot- rich clays and marls at the Cretaceous-Tertiary boundary4-6, and with carbon-rich rocks from the Sudbury impact structure7. More recently, the fullerenes were reported to occur in the Allende carbonaceous meteorite8. In all but one of these cases have the materials either not been in contact with water, or they may have been in contact with water very briefly.The exceptions were the actually studied samples from the Cretaceous-Tertiary (KT) boundary which were all marine sediments4- consolidated rocks had become uplifted above sea level.BecausetheseKT boundary materials were exposed to water,one would like to know the solubilities of C60 and C7o in water in order to evaluate how much of the fullerenes might have been leached from the sediments since their formation about 65 My ago.  

Ruoff et al.9 found that highsolubility offullerenes occurs in solvents with large refractive indices,dielectric constants around 4，and largemolecular volumes.Theindexof refraction(=589.3nm)of water at room temperature is 1.33, which is comparatively small. The dielectric constant of water at room temperature is near 80, which is very large, and the molar volume of 18 ml/mole is exceptionally small. Thus,one expects the solubility of C 6 o in water to be very much smaller than, for example, in ethanol, which was reported as 1 μg/ml9. An exploratory experiment was done by me in which solid C6o was exposed for one month to water, followed by High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) analysis of the C6O content of the water. No fullerene was detected,which,given the detection limit of the analysis, meant a solubility of less than 10 ng/ml.A more precisely controlled subsequent experiment demonstrated that the so lub ili ties of C 60 and C70 in water were less than 0.04 ng/ml (see following sections). Obviously the experimental determination of the so lub ili ties of C 6 o and C 7 o in water is a daunting task  

One can entertain the argument that the study of fullerene so lub ili ties in a homologous series of alcohols may shed more light on solubilitiesinwater.Sizablecavities mustformin anysolvent inorder to accommodate the relatively large and rigid fullerene molecules.Water and alcohols have in common that a substantial number of hydrogen bonds must rupturefor thecavities toform,hence that the so lub ili ties in all of these substances may be inversely proportional to some function of the weremeasured in alcohols of the homologous series of methanol,ethanol, 1-propanol,1-butanol,1-pentanol,1-hexanol, and 1-octanol.  

# Experimental  

C60and C 70 powders were purchased from Bucky USA.TheC60 was99.9%pure;the C 70 contained less than 2%C60.Thealcoholswere atleast99%pure.The water used was HPLC-grade.  

Approximately 1 mg of C 60 or C 70 were placed in 25 mls crew- capped bottles and about 5 ml of the alcohols were added.After the caps were screwed on tightly, the bottles were weighed to monitor any subsequent mass losses through the caps.This was necessary when it was discovered that fullerenes form supersaturated solutions in alcohols quite easily. In every case the losses at the time of analysis were less than O.3%of theoriginal fluid mass.The fluids were stored forone month,but were agitated twice everyday.It was found that this procedure resulted in full equilibration. The exploratory study of the solubility in water was done by this procedure also. For the more precisely controlled experiment, 50 ml of water and fullerenes were placed in stoppered erienmeyers together with magnetic stirring rods. These systems were stirred continuously for one month. Following filtration through 0.2 μm PTFEfilters,the water was evaporated from the flask which was then rinsed repeatedly with a few ml HPLC toluene.The toluene was filtered again and was evaporated down to 0.1 ml.  

Experiments were done atRice University where the temperature of the solvents in contact with the fullerenes was always in the range 24.5±0.5 C and at the Free University of Amsterdam where that temperature range was 25 ± 2 C. lmmediately before the analysis by High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC),the solutions were filtered through O.2 μmPTFE filters.The HPLC facilityat Rice University has been described elsewhere4-6. The detection limit was 0.5 ng injected (injection volumes 25μl).Thesystem at the FreeUniversity wasaHewlett-Packard model 1050 chromatograph with a detection limit of 0.1 ng fullerene injected(injection volumes 10 l),whichmadethis system more suited for the measurements of the low solubilities in methanol and water.Thefiltered alcohols and water samples were directly injected into the mobile phases which were either 60%methanoland 40%tolueneor50%methanoland50%tolueneatflowratesof1 ml/min.The mixing of the injected fluids with the mobile phase caused the release of dissolved air.This was only a minor inconvenience which did not interfere with the measurement of C 60 or C 7 o because stable baselines were reached well before the absorption peaks of the fullerenes appeared in the chromatograms. Hence, fluids were not degassed.  

Concentrations of fullerenes in the solvents were determined by the comparison of peak areas(Rice)or peak heights(Free University)of theunknownswith those obtainedby the injection of standard solutions of 20,000,2000,1000,or 100ng/ml.So lub ili ties were determined in all seven alcohols atleastonce at Rice and once at the Free Universityof Amsterdam.Theresults listed inTable 1are averages of these multiple determinations.
