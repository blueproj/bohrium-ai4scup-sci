# Characterization of Plastic Flow Pertinent to the Evolution of Bulk Residual Stress in Powder- Metallurgy, Nickel-Base Superalloys  

S.L. SEMIATIN, P.N. FAGIN, R.L. GOETZ, D.U. FURRER, and R.E. DUTTON  

The plastic-ﬂow behavior which controls the formation of bulk residual stresses during ﬁnal heat treatment of powder-metallurgy (PM), nickel-base superalloys was quantiﬁed using con- ventional (isothermal) stress-relaxation (SR) tests and a novel approach which simulates  con- current  temperature and strain transients during cooling following solution treatment. The concurrent cooling/straining test involves characterization of the thermal compliance of the test sample. In turn, this information is used to program the ram-displacement- vs -time proﬁle to impose a constant plastic strain rate during cooling. To demonstrate the eﬃcacy of the new approach, SR tests (in both tension and compression) and concurrent cooling/tension-straining experiments were performed on two PM superalloys, LSHR and IN-100. The isothermal SR experiments were conducted at a series of temperatures between 1144 K and 1436 K (871   C and 1163   C) on samples that had been supersolvus solution treated and cooled slowly or rapidly to produce starting microstructures comprising coarse gamma grains and coarse or ﬁne secondary gamma-prime precipitates, respectively. The concurrent cooling/straining tests comprised supersolvus solution treatment and various combinations of subsequent cooling rate and plastic strain rate. Comparison of ﬂow-stress data from the SR and concurrent cooling/ straining tests showed some similarities and some diﬀerences which were explained in the context of the size of the gamma-prime precipitates and the evolution of dislocation substruc- ture. The magnitude of the eﬀect of concurrent deformation during cooling on gamma-prime precipitation was also quantiﬁed experimentally and theoretically.

# I. INTRODUCTION  

T HE  ﬁnal heat treatment of nickel-base superalloys typically comprises high-temperature solution treatment to dissolve all or most of a strengthening phase (such as gamma prime), cooling  via  water or oil quenching or forced convection, and then lower-temperature 1 , ]   aging. In large components of these materials, bulk residual stresses are developed due to small but non- uniform plastic strains associated with non-uniform cooling following the solution treatment step. In such instances, the surface cools rapidly and tends to shrink, but it is constrained by the hotter interior of the component. The tensile stresses thus developed at the surface relax somewhat by creep-like ﬂow at moderate- to-high temperatures. Subsequently, the interior cools and tends to shrink from the surface, thereby tending to give rise to largely compressive stresses at the surface which are balanced by tensile stresses at the interior. The residual stresses so developed may be reduced during the subsequent aging treatment, but usually are not elimi- nated. Furthermore, depending on the location of the ﬁnished component within the heat-treatment envelope, clamping, cutter paths,  etc ., residual-stress patterns may also undergo changes during ﬁnal machining.  

Because of the complex geometry of typical parts and the spatially-non-uniform nature of the temperature transients, stress patterns, and plastic ﬂow, the quanti- tative prediction of residual stresses following solution heat treatment typically relies on ﬁnite-element-method 3 – 9 ]   (FEM) analysis. In addition to part geometry, initial temperature, and orientation relative to the quench medium, inputs to such models include the thermophys- ical properties of the part/quench medium, interface heat transfer coeﬃcients, and the constitutive response of the workpiece, each of which must be input as a function of temperature.  

A number of methods have been utilized to quantify the constitutive behavior used in FEM simulations of the formation of bulk residual stress. These techniques include conventional (isothermal) tension, compression,  

10 – 13 ]   creep, and stress-relaxation (SR) tests. To quantify the often non-equilibrium nature of material behavior 14 ]   and microstructure evolution during cooling, an alternate approach, the so-called on-cooling tension 15 , ]   test, has also been utilized. This latter method consists of preheating to the pertinent solution temper- ature, soaking, cooling to a speciﬁc temperature at a prescribed rate comparable to that experienced in an actual component, holding for a short period of time ( \~ 30 seconds) to equilibrate the temperature of the material, and tension testing. Experiments are thus conducted on material with a possibly metastable microstructure, but are still essentially isothermal in nature.  

The work reported herein was performed in support of a larger program concerned with bulk residual stresses in superalloy components. The overall goal of this program has been to develop methods of predicting (and measuring) residual stresses to a high enough accuracy to enable them to be included in the design of components. Such predictions require accurate consti- tutive models describing material ﬂow during cooling. The speciﬁc objectives of the present research were thus threefold: (1) Develop a new test technique comprising concurrent cooling and straining which mirrors the transient behavior during cool-down following solution treatment, (2) compare and interpret the ﬂow-stress measurements from this new approach to those from conventional (isothermal) SR tests, and (3) assess the eﬀect of concurrent cooling/deformation on microstruc- ture evolution and compare to that occurring during static (no-deformation) conditions. To accomplish these objectives, two nickel-base, gamma-prime-strengthened superalloys, LSHR and IN-100, were used. Both alloys contained a coarse gamma-grain microstructure typical of supersolvus solution heat treatment. The principal microstructural feature of interest was thus the size of the gamma-prime precipitates that evolve during cooling with or without concurrent deformation.  

# II. MATERIALS AND PROCEDURES  

# A.  Materials  

Two nickel-base, powder-metallurgy (PM) superal- loys, LSHR and IN-100, were used to compare the plastic ﬂow behavior in conventional (isothermal) SR tests and a novel concurrent cooling/straining approach. LSHR (denoting ‘‘low-solvus, high refractory’’) was developed by NASA for jet-engine-disk applications. PM IN-100, a proprietary alloy of Pratt & Whitney, is also widely used for the manufacture of jet-engine disks. Both alloys provide an attractive balance of properties at the bore and rim of disks that have been subjected to a graded-microstructure heat treatment in which only the component rim is exposed above the solvus temper- ature to promote local growth of the gamma 17 , ] grains.  

The program materials were identical to those used in several previous investigations of the thermo mechanical 19 – 21 ]   processing of PM superalloys. The LSHR material consisted of 230-mm-diameter extruded billet produced by Special Metals (Princeton, KY). Its composition is given in Table  I ; its processing history is summarized in detail in References  19  and  20 . The microstructure of the as-received LSHR comprised a ﬁne, microduplex struc- ture of gamma grains and gamma-prime precipitates, each of whose average diameter was  \~ 2  l m, and \~ 0.33 vol pct of carbide/boride particles with an average diameter of 315 nm. The gamma-prime-solvus temper- ature,  T c ¢ , of LSHR was 1430 K (1157   C).  

The IN-100 material was received from Pratt & Whitney as 89-mm-diameter bar that had been extracted from the center of a larger-diameter extruded billet. Its nominal composition is also given in Table  I . In the as-received condition, the microstructure consisted pri- marily of ﬁne gamma grains and gamma-prime precip- itates, each of whose average diameter was  \~ 1.5  l m. There was also  \~ 0.35 vol pct of carbide and boride particles whose average diameter was  \~ 230 nm. The gamma-prime-solvus temperature,  T c ¢ , of this alloy was 1458 K (1185   C).  

The present program materials are typically in the extruded-and-isothermally forged condition (and not as- extruded) prior to ﬁnal heat treatment. Because the focus of the present work was on supersolvus-solution- heat-treated material, for which the gamma grain size is 19 ]   similar for both wrought conditions, the eﬀect of diﬀerences in prior subsolvus microstructure on the results is likely minimal.

# 2.  Compression SR tests  

Isothermal compression SR tests were conducted using silicon-nitride platens mounted in a 250 kN servo-hydraulic test system. Samples were not lubricated to avoid the confounding eﬀect of thinning of the lubricant layer. In this regard, a previous analysis of the [ 23 ]   hot-compression test revealed that errors in ﬂow stress for the sample geometry and the small reductions used in the present work would be less than several percent.  

For each experiment, the tooling and sample were induction heated using an iron-chromium-aluminum alloy susceptor to a subsolvus test temperature in approximately 10 minutes. The test temperatures con- sisted of 1144 K, 1283 K, and 1339 K (871   C, 1010   C, and 1066   C) for LSHR and 1144 K, 1255 K, and 1339 K (871   C, 982   C, and 1066   C) for IN-100; selected compression SR tests were also performed at a temperature 22 K (22   C) below the respective solvus temperature of each alloy. Following a soak at temper- ature for an additional 10 minutes, each sample was then compressed to a prestrain of 0.01 using a strain rate of 10  4   or 10  5   s  1 , allowed to relax for 30 minutes, prestrained at the same strain rate to an  additional  strain of 0.02, and allowed to relax again for 30 minutes. During each relaxation stage, the crosshead was locked. The principal measurement during each experiment was the decay of the load ( P )/ ﬂow stress ( r ) with time ( t ) during each of the relaxation stages. # 3.  Tension SR tests  

Isothermal tension SR tests were conducted in the same test frame and in a manner similar to those performed in compression. In these tests, the gage section and approximately 15 mm of each shoulder of the test sample were induction heated to a uniform temperature. The inductor consisted of a helical coil wound from square-cross-section, copper tubing with a ﬂux concentrator at each end, whose purpose was to ensure temperature uniformity. The coil turns were spaced wide enough to allow the placement of two ceramic probes onto the reduced section of the heated test sample. Outside the induction coil, the probes were attached to a 12.7-mm extensometer.  

The test temperatures, soak time at temperature prior to testing, pre-strain strain rate, pre-strains, and relax- ation times for the tension SR tests were identical to those employed in the compression SR tests. The principal measurements consisted of the temporal vari- ations of the instantaneous load/stress and the  total strain rate (measured by the extensometer) during each of the relaxation stages. From these measurements, the plastic strain rate for a number of diﬀerent ﬂow stresses was determined as the diﬀerence between the measured total strain rate and the elastic strain rate. The latter was simply the slope of the stress- vs -time plot divided by Young’s modulus.  

C.  Concurrent Cooling/Straining Tests  

Mechanical tests consisting of concurrent cooling and straining in tension were designed and performed to characterize material behavior that more closely mirrors that occurring during the formation of bulk residual stresses during cool-down following solution heat treat- ment of nickel-base superalloy components. The ten- sion-sample geometry and fabrication methods were identical to those used for the tension SR experiments.  

Test procedures comprised induction heating to a supersolvus solution temperature of 1464 K (1191   C) (LSHR) or 1477 K (1204   C) (IN-100), soaking at temperature for 20 minutes, and cooling to 1144 K (871   C) at a constant rate of 13 K/min (13   C/min) or 153 K/min (153   C/min) and a constant  plastic  strain rate. The plastic strain rates were selected such that a total plastic strain of approximately 0.015 or 0.03 had been imposed upon reaching 1144 K (871   C).  

The concurrent cooling/straining experiments utilized the same servo-hydraulic test frame and extensometer (which provided the control signal for the ram) as those employed for the tension SR tests. Because both program alloys are extremely soft at supersolvus solu- tion temperatures, it was necessary to fabricate exten- sions to enable the extensometer probes to be attached to the cooler (and thus harder) portion of the shoulders at positions lying approximately 40 mm from their respective ends of the gage section. Because such quantities are initially unknown, preliminary estimates based on experience or other tests ( e.g. , SR data) were used for an initial run for each set of cooling-rate/plastic-strain-rate parameters. The measured loads/stresses from these initial trials were then used to calibrate the expected elastic extensions employed to determine  D L total  for a second set of conﬁrmation experiments. Such an iterative process with two experiments for each set of test parameters was found to be suﬃcient to obtain stress- vs -temperature results for a maximum variation of plastic strain rate during cooling in a given test of approximately 5 pct or less.  

The stress- vs -temperature results from the concurrent cooling/straining experiments were compared to the ﬂow stresses from the isothermal SR tests at identical values of plastic strain rate and microstructure.

# IV. DISCUSSION  

Various features of the high-temperature mechanical behavior of LSHR and IN-100 at strains and strain rates typical of those that control the evolution of bulk residual stress were elucidated in the present research. The discussion in the present section focuses on a comparison of the plastic-ﬂow behavior during isother- mal SR and continuous cooling/straining modes of deformation, precipitate strengthening at elevated tem- peratures, the eﬀect of small levels of deformation during cooling on precipitate growth, and the eﬀect of process variables on the tendency for quench cracking.  

# A.  Comparison of Flow Behaviors  

The ﬂow stress dependence on temperature from isothermal SR and concurrent cooling/straining tests showed some similarities and some diﬀerences. The comparison was performed at identical values of plastic strain rate  and  equivalent microstructure in terms of the sizes of the gamma grains and the secondary-gamma- prime precipitates. The gamma grain size was equivalent for all samples in light of the supersolvus exposure used in the fabrication of SR samples and the solution treatment at the beginning of the concurrent cooling/ straining experiments. In addition, the volume fraction of precipitates developed at a given temperature during cooling at the rates used in this work was very close to the corresponding equilibrium value (maintained in the isothermal SR tests) for under-cooling of the order of   22 ] 50 K (50 C) or greater.  

To enable the comparison of SR and concurrent cooling/straining results, however, the precipitate size  at intermediate temperatures  for the concurrent cooling/ straining experiments had to be estimated. This was done  via  precipitation simulations tuned to produce the measured  ﬁnal size at 1144 K (871   C). Moreover, to enable the comparison at identical precipitate size, some degree of extrapolation outside the measured SR ﬂow stresses for the coarse precipitate sizes was necessary. However, the deviation from an actual measurement encumbered by this necessity was relatively small ( £ 5 pct).  

The isothermal and concurrent-cooling/straining behaviors are compared in Figure  13  (LSHR) and 14 (IN-100). At intermediate temperatures, the SR ﬂow stresses for LSHR were higher by a factor of 10 to 100 pct of the concurrent cooling/straining value, for the various combinations of cooling rate and strain rate (Figure  13 ). By contrast, the diﬀerence in measured ﬂow stresses at the lowest test temperature [1144 K (871   C)] was very small in each case. The intermediate-temper- ature trend may indicate the ability to retain a soft, high- temperature substructure or to maintain superplastic ﬂow as the temperature decreased during the concurrent cooling/straining test. However, as the temperature approached 1144 K (871   C), which was characterized by warm/cold-working type conditions ( m  \~ 0.1), dislo- cation substructure evolution was likely similar for both types of mechanical test, and thus comparable ﬂow stresses were required for plastic ﬂow.

# B.  Precipitate Strengthening at Elevated Temperatures  

The broad dependence of the precipitate-strengthen- ing portion of the ﬂow stress on strain rate and temperature (Figure  7 ) appeared to be typical of a thermally-activated process such as climb-limited glide of dislocations. The speciﬁc mechanism of deformation was also considered to be important in interpreting the observations, however. At the lowest test temperature [1144 K (871   C)], for instance, both materials deformed in the low-m-value PLC regime for all strain rates investigated and both precipitate sizes (Figures  3 (a), 5 (a), and  6 (a)). Thus, plastic ﬂow here was likely controlled by the competition between dislocation shearing/bowing between precipitates and climb-assisted bypass, and a noticeable eﬀect of precipitate size on ﬂow was observed for all strain rates.  

At 1339 K (1066   C), on the other hand, LSHR   exhibited low-m PLC at strain rates between  \~ 10  5 and 10  4   s  1   and superplastic ﬂow at strain rates between       \~ 10  6 and 10  5 s  1 (Figures  3 (c) and  6 (c)); this change correlated qualitatively with the observed transition from a ﬁnite to a negligible eﬀect of precipitate size on plastic ﬂow with decreasing strain rate (Figure  7 (a)). For IN-100 deformed at this temperature, a transition from PLC to superplastic conditions was also observed as the strain rate decreased (Figure  5 (c)), but there was no diﬀerence in the ﬂow stress of samples with coarser and ﬁner precipitates perhaps because the latter sizes were also relatively large (Table  II ). The eﬀect of precipitate size on plastic ﬂow at intermediate temper- atures,  viz ., 1283 K (1010   C) (LSHR) or 1144 K (982   C) (IN-100), lay between the behaviors noted at lower and higher temperatures (Figures  7 (a) and (b)).

# C.  Effect of Concurrent Deformation on Precipitate Size  

A plausible explanation for the eﬀect of concurrent deformation on the size of the secondary-gamma-prime particles was derived from ‘‘fast-acting’’ (spreadsheet) model simulations of precipitation in LSHR. For this purpose, an approach identical to that described in detail in Reference  22  was utilized. In brief, the analysis treats the nucleation and growth of secondary gamma- prime precipitates following supersolvus solution treat- ment. For the cooling rates used in the present work, coarsening was not important. Furthermore, all of the material input parameters for LSHR ( e.g. , gamma- gamma prime interface energy, gamma-prime composi- tion, free energy of transformation) were the same as Reference  22  with the sole exception of the ‘‘eﬀective’’ diﬀusivity  D , whose value was varied parametrically. Speciﬁcally, the activation energy for diﬀusion was assumed to be the same as before, but the exponential prefactor was varied to investigate the inﬂuence of its magnitude on precipitate growth.  

Simulation results for the average size of secondary- gamma-prime precipitates at 1144 K (871   C) developed during cooling of LSHR at a rate of 13 or 153 K/min (13 or 153   C/min) following supersolvus solution treatment are summarized in Figure  15 . The predictions are plotted in terms of normalized size as a function of normalized diﬀusivity. The normalization factors con- sisted of the size and diﬀusivity pertaining to the corresponding static case comprising cooling with no deformation. The simulations revealed that the relative size for a given ratio of dynamic and static diﬀusivities was insensitive to cooling rate.

This enhancement is qualitatively similar to the acceleration of carbo-nitride precipitation in high- strength, low-alloy (HSLA) steels due to prior or 33 ] concurrent hot working at conventional strain rates. Hence, it can be hypothesized that small strains/low strain rates produce less dislocation substructure which may control the magnitude of enhanced (pipe) diﬀusion.  

# D.  Effect of Process Variables on Quench Cracking  

The concurrent cooling/tension straining test pro- vided a quantitative measure of the eﬀect of process variables such as cooling rate and plastic strain rate on the tendency for brittle, intergranular cracking reminis- cent of what is usually referred to as ‘‘quench cracking’’ 34 ]   in commercial practice. For the range of parameters investigated herein, such failures were noted only for the higher cooling rate and the highest strain rate for each program alloy. In particular, failure occurred for a combination of cooling/strain rate of 153 K/min  

 (153   C/min) and \~ 2.5  9  10  4 s  1 at \~ 1303 K

 (1030   C) for LSHR and 1180 K (907   C) for IN-100. Comparison of all of the stress- vs -temperature data revealed that other samples could be deformed to lower temperatures without failure while subjected to compa- rable/lower cooling rates or higher levels of strain or stress without failure. Thus, a failure criterion based solely  on maximum applied stress or strain was deemed to be inappropriate. Rather, a criterion which incorpo- rates the rate of plastic straining appears to be pertinent. The EBSD GROD results (Figure  11 ) suggest that such a rate criterion should focus on the competition between ‘‘mantle-like’’ deformation at the gamma grain bound- aries and that within the grain interiors. Further research in this area based on the present observations and experimental method as well as other specialized [ 34 ] test techniques ( e.g. , the constrained-cooling test ) is warranted.  

# V. CONCLUSIONS  

An extensive series of mechanical tests consisting of conventional isothermal, SR experiments and novel non-isothermal trials involving concurrent cooling and deformation at a constant plastic strain rate were performed for the PM superalloys, LSHR and IN-100, each with a coarse gamma grain size. From this work, the following conclusions are drawn:  

1. Log stress–log strain rate data from SR tests are essentially identical irrespective of stress state (compression  vs  tension) and the strain rate used to impose the pre-strain. Such tests at elevated temper- atures reveal deformation behaviors indicative of the ranges of strain rate and temperature over which power law creep (PLC) or superplastic flow is dominant.

 2. SR tests can be used to quantify the eﬀect of pre- cipitate size on plastic ﬂow and the PLC tempera- ture/strain rate regime in which such size eﬀects are important. The activation energy associated with precipitate strengthening in the PLC regime ( \~ 300 kJ/mol) is comparable to that for diﬀusion of various substitutional solutes in nickel. Further- more, the level of strengthening associated with pre- cipitates in PM superalloys deformed in the low- temperature, high-strain-rate regime is of the same order as that predicted by models of precipitate shearing by strongly-coupled pairs of dislocations.

 3. At intermediate temperatures in the range between 1144 K (871   C) and the solvus temperature, ﬂow- stress measurements from the concurrent cooling/ straining test lie  below  corresponding results deter- mined at equivalent plastic strain rate and microstruc- ture from isothermal SR experiments. This trend may be related to the ability to retain a soft, high-tempera- ture dislocation substructure during concurrent cool- ing/straining. By contrast, the two types of tests give rise to similar flow stresses at lower temperatures in the range investigated.

 4. Concurrent cooling/straining gives rise to secondary- gamma-prime precipitates whose size is measurably coarser than those developed during cooling under no-deformation conditions. This trend can be ascribed to an enhancement of diﬀusion by the substruc- ture developed during concurrent cooling/straining. Because of this eﬀect, caution should be exercised in predicting the size of precipitates in commercial-scale components (whose cooling following solution treat- ment has included a small plastic strain) from small lab-scale test coupons cooled under strain-free conditions.

 5. The concurrent cooling/tension straining test can be used to assess the eﬀect of process variables on the tendency for quench cracking. The present results suggests that a criterion incorporating plastic strain rate may be more appropriate than one based solely on maximum stress or accumulated strain, at least for the two PM superalloys investigated in the pre- sent work.
