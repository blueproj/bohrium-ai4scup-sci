# Abstract  

Nowadays, it is challenging to completely eliminate low angle or high angle grain boundaries 

 (LAGBs or HAGBs) from Nickel-based single crystal (SX) superalloys manufactured using the  conventional directional solidification technique. The additions of C, B and Hf have been found to be  an effective measure in improving the damage resistance of grain boundary (GB) defects, and thus  increasing the creep resistance. However, the strengthening mechanism through their additions is still  ACCEPTED unclear. In this study, a double-seed solidification technique with two misorientation levels, i.e., 5° and  20°, was used to produce a series of bicrystal superalloys with different contents of Hf and B. It is the  first report of an alloy with joint Hf and B addition that demonstrates tolerance to GBs with a  misorientation as high as \~20° under all of the creep conditions: 1100 °C/130 MPa, 980 °C/250 MPa  and 760 °C/785 MPa. Interestingly, the effect of individual additions of Hf or B was not as pronounced  as that of the joint Hf and B addition. To understand the influence of these additions on the creep  mechanism in nickel-based superalloys with GB defects, a detailed characterization of the  microstructures in the vicinity of the LAGBs or HAGBs was carried out, and the elemental  distribution at the HAGBs was analyzed with various techniques. This study will be beneficial for  understanding the role of Hf and B additions on improving the GB tolerance, and optimizing the Hf  and B additions in nickel-based single crystal superalloys.  

Keywords : Single crystal superalloys; Microalloying elements; Creep property; Grain boundary;  Microstructural evolution  

# 1. Introduction  

With increasing thermal efficiency requirements, the geometry and size of single crystal turbine  MANUSCRIPT (SX) blades has become more complex. Nowadays, with available directional solidification  technique such as conventional Bridgman casting, it is still difficult to cast these complex or large  single crystals without any casting defects such as low angle or high angle grain boundaries (LAGBs  or HAGBs). In the actual production of blades, grain selection failure or nucleation of spurious grains  around areas of rapidly changing cross-sections often leads to grain boundary (GB) defects, like the  positions of turbine blade platform (Fig. 1a) and the body of guide vane (Fig. 1b) [1]. The existence  of GB defects destroys the integrity of the  deformation, accelerating the formation of creep cracks and drastically degrading the creep properties  of SX superalloys [2, 3].  

In industrial investment castings, the presence of LAGB defects in SX turbine blades can be  ACCEPTED tolerated below a certain misorientation tolerance, allowing such casting to remain as an economically  viable technique. However, some SX turbine blades are scrapped due to the formation of HAGBs,  resulting in lower casting yield and higher production cost. Nowadays, improving the grain boundary  tolerance of SX superalloys is still an important and challenging task for physical metallurgists.  Previous works have reported that the GB misorientation tolerances of most SX superalloys seldom  exceed 10° - for example, 6° for René N4 SX superalloy [4], 6° for CM 186LC SX superalloy [5], and  10° for PWA 1483 and PWA1484 SX superalloys [6]. Significant improvements in GB tolerance of  transverse creep properties have been reported in some SX superalloys with minor alloying additions  of C, B and Hf [1]. For instance, in their study of the alloy René N4, Ross and O’Hara [4] suggested  that the additions of C, B and Hf enhanced the GB tolerance to 12° misorientation. However, the exact  mechanisms for the improvement of the GB tolerance through these minor alloying additions are not  well understood yet.  

Chen et al. [7] and Stinville et al. [8] inferred that the segregation of minor alloying elements  along the GBs may explain the increased tolerance to damage accumulation of SX superalloys with a  GB defect. Kolb et al. [9] suggested that small additions of B to Co-based superalloys lead to  MANUSCRIPT significant grain boundary strengthening due to the B segregation, as well as to a higher strength of  the  γ / γ′  microstructure. As reported by Chen et al. [7], a higher concentration of C caused a larger  density of carbides along the GBs of RR 2072 bicrystal superalloys, which enhanced the creep  performance at 950 °C. However, up to now, there have few reports about the individual and  interactive roles of Hf and B additions on the creep properties of SX superalloys with GB defects. In  addition, to the best of the authors’ knowledge, where Hf and B atoms at GB is still blurry or  controversial. It is already known that Hf atoms can segregate into MC carbides at the GBs [10], but  whether Hf atoms segregate to GBs in a solid solution state is still not reported. It is well known that  B atoms prefer to reside at the GBs due to its different atomic size and electronic properties [11].  ACCEPTED However, Kontis et al. [12] have recently noted that a majority of B atoms in polycrystalline   borides, rather than in bulk solid solution. It is  worth noting that all of the aforementioned studies were carried out at a temperature below 1000 °C.  Nevertheless, with an increase of the operating temperature (760 °C \~ 1100 °C), the diffusion of the  alloying elements at the GBs is expected to be significant. Thus, a detailed understanding of the  creep mechanisms of the GBs in the whole service temperature range, especially during  high-temperature operation, is crucial for achieving an improved GB tolerance by alloy design of  minor alloying elements.  

Accordingly, the aim of the present work is to investigate the effects of Hf and B additions on  the creep properties of four alloys containing GBs with different misorientation angles subjected to a  whole service temperature range (760 °C - 1100 °C). Relationships among Hf and B additions, GB  microstructural evolution characteristics, segregation behavior of alloying elements, and creep  properties are analyzed in detail. This study allows a better understanding of the exact creep  mechanisms of GBs and the role of minor alloying elements, and especially, how the microstructure  and the corresponding property of SX turbine blades changes with GB defects during service.  

# 2. Experimental procedures  

The investigated alloy is a Re-containing SX superalloy DD11, which was developed by the  MANUSCRIPT Beijing Institute of Aeronautical Materials (BIAM), China. Bicrystal slabs of  the base alloy, alloy Hf and alloy B were solution heat treated at 1320 °C for 4 h followed by  air-cooling, while bicrystal slabs of alloy HfB were solution heat treated at 1310 °C for 6 h followed by  air-cooling due to the lower incipient melting temperature. Then, the bicrystal slabs were two-step  aged at 1130 °C for 4 h and at 870 °C for 32 h, both followed by air-cooling. # 4 Discussion  

# 4.1 Effect of bicrystal boundary misorientation on creep properties  

It is well known that GBs serve as obstacles to dislocation movement, resulting in dislocation  ACCEPTED pileups in the vicinity of the GBs [1]. Hence, many important properties of polycrystalline materials  are strongly influenced by the presence of GBs. Based on the Read-Shockley theory [18], the GB  interfacial free energy increases with an increase in the misorientation from 0° to 25°, thus resulting in  a decrease of the cohesive strength between the grains [19]. In this study, it is apparent that the creep  rupture lives decreased with an increasing GB misorientation for all four bicrystal alloys (Fig. 8). In  the interest of simplicity, only the base alloy is shown to demonstrate the role of GB misorientation on  the creep ruptures properties, but it should be noted that similar behaviors were observed for the other  alloys. LAGBs have low interfacial energy, and no secondary phases precipitate at these GBs. Damage,  such as the form of small cracks initiating at the LAGB, was rarely observed in the interrupted-creep  microstructure (Fig. 10a). It is thus evident that the dislocations could slide across the LAGB,  providing a path for uniform, coordinated deformation. The failure behavior in the transverse  specimens with a LAGB was not controlled by GB separation, resulting in the transgranular failure,  thus a good creep property was achieved in the base alloy with a LAGB. However, continuous cracks  propagated along the HAGB during creep (Fig. 10b), suggesting that the dislocations could not pass  through the HAGB by sliding or climbing, and the stress was concentrated at the GB due to dislocation  pile up. Consequently, intergranular failure without the dimples was observed, and the creep rupture  life of these samples was less than 20 h due to the high interfacial free energy and weak GB strength.

# 4.2.1 Effect of Hf and B on the DP colonies along the HAGBs  

It is well-known that diffusion along a GB occurs more rapidly than diffusion through grain  ACCEPTED interiors, thus the GB stability is not as good as that of the matrix in superalloys. In addition, the high  content defects at the grain boundaries, as compared to the bulk lattice, serve as nucleation sites for  secondary phases. DP colonies microstructure is one type of microstructural instability of GBs,   two-phase microstructure into a  γ - γ′  +TCP lamellar structure  in Ni-based superalloys during heat treatment or long-term service [16]. The GB misorientation has a  significant effect on the interfacial free energy and migration of the GB [14] .  Yu et al. [15] reported  that the cellular structure exists along an artificial grain boundary with a misorientation of 25° after  heat treatment at 1100 °C for 50 h; all of the samples in that study were produced by self-diffusion  bonding of the same SX superalloy containing 5 wt.% Re. Nystrom et al. [14] also reported that  cellular DP colonies occurred at GBs with a misorientation higher than 10° at 1093 °C in a bicrystal  superalloy containing 6.7 wt.% Re. It is generally recognized that the nucleation of TCP phases in DP  colonies can be accelerated by supersaturation of refractory alloying elements, and that a higher  misorientation of grain boundaries is required for the DP transformation in more thermodynamic ally  stable superalloys [16].  

The current results clearly suggest that the localized formation of DP colonies occurred along the  MANUSCRIPT GB with a misorientation of about 20° in the base alloy, which contains 3 wt.% Re. This is due to the  high GB interfacial free energy and the diffusion mobility of refractory alloying elements. This  phenomenon is identical to the reports of Nystrom et al. [14] and Yu et al. [20]. DP colonies can serve  as the origin of crack initiation and propagation due to the de-cohesion of the TCP/large   phase of the  DP colonies along the HAGB (Fig. 10b), and can significantly degrade the interface strength of the  crystals. Hence, intergranular fracture mode was observed in the base alloy with a HAGB (Fig. 9b).  

Nevertheless, in this study, the DP colonies along the HAGB were suppressed in all three Hf and  ACCEPTED B modified alloys after full heat treatment. Through comprehensive analysis of Figs. 4, 7 and 10, the  effect on the inhibition potency for DP colonies was in the order of Hf + B  ≫  Hf  ＞  B. As reported  in previous investigations of bicrystal superalloys [7, 8], GBs are commonly located in the final  solidification site of interdendritic regions. Compared to the base alloy, individual Hf or B addition  could reduce the concentrations of Re and W in the HAGBs of the interdendritic region. When both  Hf and B were added into the alloy, the concentrations of Re and W were reduced to the lowest  degree (Table 3). The main driving force for the general DP colonies formation along the GB is  considered to be governed by the supersaturation of refractory alloying elements in the  γ  matrix [16].  In the present work, both the emergence and growth of DP colonies along the HAGBs were reduced  in the Hf and B modified alloys as shown in Fig. 7a. This further verifies that the supersaturation of  refractory alloying elements in the  γ  matrix was considerably reduced by the joint additions of Hf  and B, which was mainly caused by the decreased Re and W macro concentrations at the GBs. Hence,  the Hf or B containing alloys featured a lower driving force for TCP precipitation. As a consequence,  the TCP nucleation was delayed and the DP colony width was decreased.  

In addition, it should be pointed out that the formation of borides in alloy HfB may also play an  important role in the inhibition of DP colonies. LAGBs always have low interfacial energy, and no carbide or boride precipitates form at these  ACCEPTED GBs. However, plenty of secondary phases can precipitate at the HAGBs, which possess higher  interfacial energy [21]. Carbides are always found to precipitate along the GB due to the higher  diffusion of C through the GB [22]. Hf is a strong MC carbide forming element, and it can control the  type and morphology of carbides along the GB [23], resulting in improvement of the mechanical  properties of superalloys. Chen et al. [7] found that minor elements (C, Hf and B) promoted the  formation of MC and M  carbide precipitates, enhancing the creep performance of bicrystal  superalloys by inhibiting GB sliding. In this study, it was confirmed through APT analysis that C  slightly segregated to the GB, while Hf had no segregation to the GB – based on AES, TOF SIMS and  APT analyses. Only a small quantity of discontinuous blocky MC carbides enriched in Hf and Ta were  observed along the HAGB in alloy Hf. The precipitation of MC carbides along the HAGB during creep  was not obvious in the creep-interrupted microstructure (Fig. 10c). Thus, an improvement of creep  properties was not achieved only by the Hf addition, especially at the high temperature conditions.  

Borides can precipitate along the GBs when the solubility limit of B is exceeded in superalloys  [24]. Such GB borides, including M 3 B 2  and M 5 B 3 , also have significant influence in Ni-base  superalloys [12, 23, 25]. Wang et al. [26] found that the precipitation of many nano-size M MANUSCRIPT 5 B 3   borides at GBs can improve the high temperature creep properties in in a directionally solidified  Ni-base superalloy. Kontis et al. [12] noted that the addition of B caused a significant improvement  of creep properties due to the precipitation of Cr-rich borides along the GBs in a polycrystalline  superalloy. Kontis et al. [23] also found these borides were micro structurally stable after creep and  without substantial compositional changes in the above-mentioned superalloy. Generally, the  presence of fine and discrete borides along the GB prevents the boundary migration and contributes  to the creep resistance [22]. In this study, the B segregation was confirmed by AES, TOF-SIMS and  APT analyses, meanwhile, the Cr, Co, Mo and Re segregation to the GB was also observed through  APT analyses. As a result, the borides can be directly formed by a reaction between Cr and Mo with  

The precipitation of initial fine and discrete borides along the GB in alloy HfB (Fig. 3f) could  ACCEPTED prevent GB sliding at the beginning of the creep deformation. Meanwhile, a completely decorated  HAGB with fine and discrete borides was observed in alloy HfB after the creep interrupted at 40 h  (Fig. 10e). Also proved by thermal exposure experiments at 1100 °C, the continuous and stable  precipitation of borides can play an important role in GB strengthening (Figs. 4e and 7b). In  comparison with the poor creep properties of HAGBs in alloy Hf and alloy B, the discrete M 3 B 2 borides are considered to be necessary for obtaining optimum creep properties of alloy HfB.  

# 4.2.3 Effect of elemental segregation  

In many polycrystalline materials, some solute atoms can segregate to planar defects in order to  minimize the Gibbs free energy of the system, thereby affecting the bulk properties of the material  [27]. It is well known that B segregation is beneficial for enhancing GB cohesion and suppressing  intergranular fracture in many metallic and intermetallic alloys [28]. However, at present, it is unclear  whether the improved GB strength in superalloys can be ascribed to the B segregation along the GBs  or alternatively to the formation of borides [12]. In addition, Hf segregation to the GB has not yet been  reported either [29]. In this study, there was no obvious segregation of Hf, but the segregation of B to  the HAGB was confirmed by AES, TOF-SIMS and APT analyses. Additionally, B was also present in  the form of borides.

In summary, we first report that a SX superalloy with combined additions of Hf and B  demonstrates good tolerance to GBs with misorentations as high as about 20° under all of the creep  ACCEPTED conditions: 1100 °C/130 MPa, 980 °C/250 MPa and 760 °C/785 MPa. The interaction effects of Hf  and B on the creep properties should be related to the significant suppression of DP colonies, the  precipitation of fine discrete and hardly growing GB borides and the reduction of interfacial free  energy due to B segregation. Moreover, the joint additions of Hf and B may be helpful for breaking the  technical and economic barriers in transitioning single crystal technology to large industrial gas  turbine (IGT) components. This study is helpful for understanding the role of Hf and B on the  strengthening and fracture mechanisms of GBs and optimizing the minor Hf and B additions in  Ni-base SX superalloys.  

# 5. Conclusions  

The effect of minor alloying elements (Hf and B) and GB misorientations (about 5° and 20°) on  the microstructural evolution, the segregation behavior of alloying elements as well as the transverse  creep properties of bicrystal superalloys were investigated in the current study. The following  conclusions can be drawn:  

1. For a fixed alloy, the creep rupture lives of bicrystal superalloy with a HAGB were  significantly lower than those with a LAGB or as a SX. The alloy with the joint additions of Hf and B  demonstrated high tolerance for a GB with a misorientation as high as about 20° under all of the  creep conditions, i.e., 1100  o C/130 MPa, 980  o  

2. DP colonies along the HAGB formed in the base alloy after full heat treatment, but were  delayed by the individual addition of Hf or B and significantly suppressed by the joint addition of Hf  and B.  

3. The individual addition of Hf promoted the precipitation of blocky carbides along the HAGB,  however, the limited amount of carbides had no obvious influence on the creep performance.  

4. A strong B-enriched zone with a width of approximately 5 nm was formed at the HAGB and  no enrichment of Hf at the HAGB was detected in both Hf and B containing alloy. Meanwhile, the  segregation of C, Cr, Co, Re and Mo was also observed at the HAGB.
