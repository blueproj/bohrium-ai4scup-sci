Segregation and microstructural evolution at interfaces of thermal barrier coatings (TBCs) during thermal cycling were systematically investigated using scanning electron microscopy, transmission electron microscopy and X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy. The as-sprayed TBCs were thermally cycled between 1100    C and ambient temperature up to 3000 cycles. Results show that phosphorus, sulfur, and yttrium co-segregate at the top coat (TC)/bond coat (BC) interface after 3000 cycles. The above co-segregation at the BC/substrate interface was delayed by 1000 cycles. Chemical components of thermally grown oxide (TGO) layers at both the TC/BC and BC/substrate interfaces change from O and Al to O, Al, Cr, Co and Ni as the thermal cycling proceeded. The corresponding oxides in the TGO layers evolved from  a -Al 2 O 3  to  a - Al 2 O 3 , Cr 2 O 3 , NiO and spinel phases. Controlled by the element diffusion rate and selective internal oxidation, evolution of the thickness of the TGO layers followed a natural logarithmic law.

# 1. Introduction  

Hot-zone components in gas-turbine engine are exposed to severe conditions including mechanical/thermal stress, high tem- perature oxidation and hot corrosion. Hot gas of advanced turbine engine is up to \~1700    C which is much higher than the tempera- ture capacity of most metallic materials. In order to reduce the surface temperature of metallic substrate, thermal barrier coatings

 (TBCs) have been successfully developed and applied for decades

 [ 1 ]. The typical TBCs mainly consist of a ceramic top coat (TC) and an underlying metallic bond coat (BC) [ 2 ]. The TC, generally fabri- cated either by air plasma spraying (APS) or electron beam-physical vapor deposition (EB-PVD), is usually composed of yttria partially- stabilized zirconia (YSZ) [ 3 , 4 ]. The TC can reduce the surface  

The TGO layer mainly formed along the TC/BC interface of TBCs prepared by EB-PVD. While, for the sprayed TBCs, the TGO layers likely grow at both TC/BC and BC/substrate interfaces [ 9 ]. Growth of TGO layers will induce out-of-plane stress normal to the interfaces and consequently result in the interfacial crack initiation and propagation [ 10 ]. Cracks and buckle-type delaminations formed parallel to the interface between the mixed oxides zone and columnar TGO will severely deteriorate the performance of TBCs [ 11 , 12 ].  

Dramatic composition change, signi ﬁ cant mismatch in thermal- expansion coef ﬁ cient, and severe stress concentration usually coexist at the interfaces among TGO, BC, TC and substrates [ 13 ], thus making these interfaces the weakest sites within TBCs particularly when sulfur (S) segregation is presented [ 14 , 15 ]. In the case of MCrAlY-type BC, TBCs will be even peeled off when the thickness of the TGO layer reaches around 7 e 8  m m [ 9 ]. Therefore, accurately revealing the dynamics evolution of these interfaces in TBCs is very fundamental to understand the degradation behavior of the TBCs. Until now, most of the knowledge on these interfaces were gained based on scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and X- ray diffraction (XRD) which have a low spatial resolution, limiting researchers ’  deep understanding regarding the degradation of the associated mechanical performance. Additionally, as a complete coating system, interaction always happens among TC, BC and substrate during service. Performance of the TC/BC interface will in ﬂ uence that of the BC/substrate interface. Hence, it is necessary to investigate the interfacial behavior induced by cyclic oxidation, such as element segregation and microstructural evolution both at the TC/BC and BC/substrate interfaces within the TBCs simulta- neously. The systematic study will help us gain a better under- standing of the correlation between interfacial oxidation behavior and the performance variation of the TBCs during service, which will facilitate the designing of more degradation resistant TBCs in the future.  

Previously, we have studied the evolution of the interfacial microstructure of TBCs in the initial stage of isothermal oxidation [ 16 ]. To further simulate the actual service process of turbine blade, in the present work, we mainly investigated the interfacial oxida- tion behavior of plasma sprayed TBCs during thermal cycling using XRD, X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), SEM and trans- mission electron microscopy (TEM). The evolution of all the in- terfaces among the coatings, substrate and TGO layers formed after a series of cyclic oxidation was investigated systematically, which is very critical for a better understanding of the degradation behavior of TBCs.  

# 2. Experiments  

# 2.1. Preparation of the TBCs  

The material used for the substrate is a nickel-based single crystal superalloy. The dimension of the substrate is Ф 40 mm    3 mm. Commercially available NI-130 (Praxair Surface Technologies Inc., Indiana, USA) and AI-1075 (ZrO 2 e 8Y 2 O 3 , wt.%; Praxair Surface Technologies Inc., Indiana, USA) powders were used for fabrication of the BC and TC, respectively. Detailed compositions of the superalloy and NI-130 powder are provided in  Table 1 . The nominal diameters of the NI-130 and AI-1075 powders are 5 e 45  m m as revealed by the typical SEM images shown in  Fig. 1 .  

Before the coating deposition, surfaces of the substrates were grit-blasted at a distance about 100 mm with 60 mesh  a -Al 2 O 3 powders at a pressure of 0.7 MPa using the 8070P-B sandblasting machine. After the sandblasting, surface roughness ( R a ) of the substrate was measured as 6.08  ±  0.32  m m. The grit-blasted sub- strates were subjected to spraying using the Metco 9 M PS machine equipped with a vacuum plasma spray (VPS) system. The BC with a thickness of 50 e 70  m m was deposited on the grit-blasted substrate by low pressure plasma spray (LPPS) technique using an 80 kW F6 plasma gun (F6, 80 kW class, GTV, Germany). The TC with a thick- ness of 100 e 150  m m was sprayed on the BC surface using atmo- spheric plasma spray (APS). The precise spray conditions and plasma spray parameters for both the BC and the TC are given in Table 2 .  

# 2.2. Thermal cycling  

Using a vertical cyclic oxidation rig (RJ3-80-12, Fengda Indus- trial Furnace Co., Ltd., Foshan, China), thermal cycling tests under laboratory air at 1100    C achieved within 10 min were carried out on the specimens coated with TBCs. Within single cycle, the sample was exposed at 1100    C for 50 min and then cooled to ambient temperature within 30 min. During cooling stage, 0.2 Mach  ﬂ ow of clean air was blown on both sides of the specimens. The specimens were removed either when approximately 25% delamination were detected on the surface or at a given number of cycles. Twelve given number of cycles, 100 e 1000 ( D N  ¼  100), 2000 and 3000 cycles were set for the thermal cycling tests. For each given cycle, 10 specimens were tested simultaneously. Although all specimens were studied, only the typical results will be presented here. In the initial stage of the thermal cycling, the specimens were examined visually once in 16 cycles, i.e., once a day. The frequency of examinations will be increased when delamination was observed visually on the surface. The thermal cycling tests will be stopped when the fraction of delamination-failed TBCs reached around 25%.  

# 2.3. Characterization of TBCs  

Before and after thermal cycling tests, XRD analyses of the cross- sectional samples were measured using the Philips PW3040/00 X ’ Pert MPD/MRD diffractometer. SEM analyses were performed in the FEI Inspect F250. High spatial resolution interfacial micro- structural evolution of the TBCs was investigated using FEI Tecnai 2   G F20, which was equipped with a high-angle annular dark-field (HAADF) detector and energy dispersive spectrum (EDS) system and operated at 200 kV. The samples for TEM investigations were prepared by a conventional cross-sectioning method consisting of mechanical thinning and ion milling using the Gatan precision ion polishing system (PIPS). Chemical state of segregated elements around the interfaces of TBCs was detected using an X-ray photo- electron spectroscopy (XPS, ESCALAB250, Thermo VG, Waltham, MA, USA) with an Al K a  anode ( l  ¼  1486.6 eV). The Al  K a  X-ray monochromator was operated with an anode power of 350  W . The normal direction of the sample surface was oriented at 45    to both the X-ray source and photoelectron spectrometer. The growth behavior of TGO was measured using an Image Pro Plus 6 software based on the TEM and SEM images taken from the cross-sectional samples. At each given cycle, at least 10 images were used to measure the TGO thicknesses.

As cycling time increase, the content of [Al] decreases because of the preferential oxidation, which will raise the concentration of [Cr] and [Ni] neighboring the interfaces. Thus, the activities of reset main metallic elements are enhanced. Consequently, minor [O] diffused through the  a -Al 2 O 3  layer reacts with [Cr], [Ni], [Co] and [Al] at the TC/BC interface during stages 2 and 3 of the thermal cycling ( Table 3  and  Fig. 16 ). The Kirkendall voids provide convenient paths for further diffusion of [O] and [N] into the BC/substrate interface and then react with metal elements to form corresponding oxides and nitride in the last stage ( Table 3  and  Fig. 16 ). Therefore, the content of [Al] gradually decreases in the interfacial TGO layer, while the content of other elements gradually increases during the thermal cycling ( Fig. 17 ).  

# 4.2. Segregation of P, S, and Y  

P is a trace element in the substrate. It segregates at the BC/ substrate interface and TC/BC interface after 2000 cycles ( Fig. 11 ) and 3000 cycles ( Figs. 8 and 12 ), respectively. Trace P is bene ﬁ cial for the whole performance of substrate, because its segregation at the TGO/metal interfaces retards the oxidation rate when NiO was formed [ 28 ]. However, it ’ s detrimental for preserving the adhesive strength of interfaces due to embrittling effects on grain boundaries [ 29 ], increasing intergranular stress corrosion susceptibility [ 30 ] and intergranular crack initiation [ 31 ].  

S tends to be captured by reactive element Y. During the cyclic oxidation, S could react with O to form volatile sulfur oxide gases. The volatile sulfur oxide gases might be one of the reasons for formation of the voids and/or pores in the TGO layers ( Figs. 11 and 12 ). Given the [O] gradient gradually decreases from the TC surface down to the substrate, oxygen partial pressure at the TGO layers is much lower than that on the TC surface. Hence, [S] does not react with [O] to form volatile sulfur oxide gases, but co-segregates with [Y].  

A small content of Yadded to metallic materials would segregate to the grain boundaries and/or interfaces, which can facilitate the capture of S and restrain the oxidation of Al so that the adhesive strength of grain boundaries and/or interfaces can be enhanced [ 13 , 32 , 33 ]. However, Y usually occurred in the form of Y 2 O 3  which is extremely stable. Due to the stability of Y 2 O 3 , Y diffuses at a relative low diffusion rate in the TBCs. [Y] have a high af ﬁ nity with [P] and [S]. Once encountered each other, they will co-segregate [ 15 ]. This may explain why their co-segregation was found both at interfaces and in the TC after 3000 cycles ( Figs. 8 and 12 ). As a  “ reactive element ” , Y is also added in the BC based on its role in improving the oxidation resistance by reducing the  a -Al 2 O 3  growth rate and promoting the TGO adhesion, because [Y] can reduce the activities of [P] and [S] in the alloy and thus prevents them from segregating to the interfaces [ 34 ].  

# 4.3. Growth kinetics of the interfacial TGO  

Interfacial oxidation of the TBCs is a complex process, which is roughly divided into 3 stages [ 35 e 37 ]: [O] absorption and diffusion into TC; formation of TGO (thickness of the layer \~200 nm); growth of the TGO layers and their destruction. Generally speaking, in the initial stage of oxidation, TGO mainly consist of  a -Al 2 O 3  ( Figs. 6 and 10 ) and Cr 2 O 3  ( Figs. 6 and 9 ) [ 35 e 37 ]. TGO thickening is controlled by the solid-state diffusion and chemical reaction of [O], [Al], and [Cr] [ 35 e 37 ]. Within the initial 500 cycles, diffusion rates of [O], [Al] and [Cr] are large because the as-sprayed structures of TC and BC can provide enough convenient channels. Accordingly, the growth rate of the TGO is larger in initial stages ( Table 3 ). Migration rate of [O] through TC into the TC/BC interface is faster than the chemical reaction. Thus, uniform [O] concentration was formed, which leads to the fast-homogenous oxidation of the BC surface. It can be speculated that diffusion in the defective grain-boundaries pro- vides an initially high oxidation rate. Oxidation proceeds rapidly at the initial stage on the BC surface and then gradually slows down due to the formation of the TGO layer composed of  a -Al 2 O 3  and Cr 2 O 3  which retards the fast diffusion of [O]. Growth of oxide grains decreases the number of fast-diffusion paths and slows down the oxidation rate. As the fast-diffusion paths are cut off, the oxidation rate is decreased. Therefore, the oxidation of the TC/BC interfaces in the present work deviates from the parabolic rate law and can be better described by the natural logarithmic model. As the thermal cycling test continues, [Al] and [Cr] in the BC are gradually depleted while the activity of [Ni] increases during the number of cycles ranging from 600 to 1000. Thus, spinel oxides ( Fig. 7 ) form in the TGO layers after 1000 cycles. The rapidly growth of spinel phases cause pores and microcracks in the TGO layers between TC and BC, which continually provides diffusion channels and reaction spaces for the oxidation ions. Consequently, the thickness of the TGO layers still increases slowly though the oxidation rate decreased.  

It takes time for [O] to diffuse from atmosphere steadily through the TC and BC into the BC/substrate interface. Therefore, continuous TGO layer does not grow at the BC/substrate interface until 1000 cycles. When the thermal cycles exceed 1000, voids and/or pores formed in the coatings and interfaces ( Figs. 7a and 9 a). The voids and/or pores will facilitate the penetration of [O] and accelerate the diffusion of [Al] and [Cr] to the BC/substrate interface. The voids and/or pores likely promote the cracking. Microcracks ( Fig. 12 a) induced by thermal stress further increase the diffusion of [O], [Al], [Cr], [Co], and [Ni] towards the BC/substrate interface. Furthermore, metallic elements can diffuse from both BC and substrate to the BC/ substrate interface, which will provide more ions for oxidation reaction at the BC/substrate interface than those of the TC/BC interface. Therefore, the oxidation rate of the BC/substrate interface is larger than that of the TC/BC interface ( Table 3  and  Fig. 15 ).  

Thickening of the TGO layers brings about the Al-depleted zone where  b -NiAl phase disappears in BC beneath the TGO layer and  g 0 - Ni 3 Al decreases in substrate close to the TGO/substrate interface. Compared to uncoated alloys, the BC does dramatically improve the oxidation resistance of nickel-based superalloys. For example, the thickness of the TGO layer was about 1000 nm after thermal cycling test at 900    C for 10 cycles, and affected regions of internal oxida- tion beneath the surface (the BC/substrate interface) was \~700 nm [ 38 ]. However, with the protection of the BC, the nickel-based su- peralloys surface is free of TGO after thermal cycling tested at 1100    C for 100 cycles ( Fig. 6 ).  

# 5. Conclusion  

Typical TBCs comprising the Ni23Co17Cr12Al0.5Y BC and 8YSZ TC were deposited onto a grit-blasting Ni-based single crystal su- peralloy. The as-sprayed specimens were subjected to thermal cycling test between 1100    C and ambient temperature up to 3000 cycles. Segregation and microstructural evolution at interfaces of the oxidized specimens have been studied by XRD, XPS, SEM and TEM. The following conclusions can be drawn:  

1) Chemical elements in the substrate and coatings are likely to interdiffuse through the interfaces during the thermal cycling. As the number of thermal cycles increased, [P] and [S] diffuse from the substrate and co-segregate with [Y] at the BC/substrate interface after 2000 cycles and TC/BC interface after 3000 cycles. [P] and [S] exist as the elemental state. Overall, the predominant chemical component of the interfacial TGO changes from O and Al to O, Al and Cr, and to O, Al, Cr, Co and Ni as the thermal cycling proceeds. 2) As the thermal cycling test proceeds, the evolution of oxides induced by internal oxidation at both TC/BC and BC/substrate interfaces obeys the following sequence: original phases  /  a - Al 2 O 3  /  a -Al 2 O 3  þ  Cr 2 O 3  /  a -Al 2 O 3  þ  Cr 2 O 3  þ  NiO  /  a - Al 2 O 3 þ Cr 2 O 3 þ NiO þ Ni(Al,Cr) 2 O 4 / a - Al 2 O 3  þ  Cr 2 O 3  þ  NiO  þ  Ni(Al,Cr) 2 O 4  þ  (Ni,Co)(Al,Cr) 2 O 4 .  g -Ni precipitates in the TGO layer between BC and the substrate after 2000 cycles. Moreover, TiN forms in the vicinity of the TGO/ substrate interface after 3000 thermal cycles due to the internal nitridation. 3) TGO formed at TC/BC and BC/substrate interfaces after 100 cy- cles and 1000 cycles respectively. Oxidation of above two in-  

terfaces follows the natural logarithmic law. During the entire thermal cycling test, although the TGO thickness of the TC/BC interface is larger than that of the BC/substrate interface at the corresponding stage, the oxidation rate constant of the former is smaller than that of the latter. After 3000 cycles of thermal cycling, obvious cracks generate at the BC/TGO interface.
