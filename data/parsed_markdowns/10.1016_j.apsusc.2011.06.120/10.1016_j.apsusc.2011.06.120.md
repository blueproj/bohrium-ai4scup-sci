The    present study   has been conducted in order   to   determine the   inﬂuence of superalloy substrate rough- ness on adhesion and oxidation behavior of    magnetron-sputtered NiCoCrAlY coatings. Six types    of    coating samples with   different substrate roughness were tested. The surface   roughness and real surface area of both the    substrates and coatings were    studied by atomic force    microscopy (AFM) techniques. The    scratch tests performed at progressive loads were employed to evaluate the    adhesion of    the    coatings. Cyclic oxi- dation tests    were    performed at 1100   ◦ C in air for 50    cycles,    each cycle consisting of    1 h    heating in the tube furnace followed by   15 min cooling in the open air. The AFM   measurements exhibit that the   surface roughness of    the    sputtered NiCoCrAlY coating increases with the    increasing of the    superalloy substrate roughness. The NiCoCrAlY coatings present slightly lower   roughness than   the corresponding superalloy substrate. The scratch adhesion tests    indicate that    the    coatings on    substrates with    a    smoother surface possess better adhesion than    on    those with    a rougher surface. Both    the    real    surface area    and    oxidation weight gain    of the    coatings decrease with    the    decreasing of    the superalloy substrate roughness. The    NiC- oCrAlY coating sputtered on the    superalloy substrate with lower roughness provides    relatively higher antioxidant protection than    that    provided by the    coating with    rougher substrate.

Some previous studies about MCrAlY bond coat have been focused on the effects of compositions [12] and preparation technologies [13] on oxidation and corrosion behavior of the coat- ings, which are associated with the formation and evolution of TGO. However, few studies have been concerned with the effect of substrate characteristics, such as substrate roughness, on perfor- mance of MCrAlY coatings. Substrate roughness is a fundamental factor that cannot be ignored for the characteristics of coatings and ﬁlms. Chang et al. [14] conﬁrmed that the magnetic properties of Co/Pt multilayer thin ﬁlms were very sensitive to substrate rough- ness. Lee et al. [15] found that a smooth substrate surface not only provided better adhesion as well as lower friction coefﬁcient of the CrN hard coating but also improved its corrosion resistance. Some other studies concerning the effects of substrate roughness on the characteristics of coatings and ﬁlms have also been carried out.  

# 1. Introduction  

MCrAlY (M = Ni and/or Co) coatings are widely used in gas tur- bines as a bond coat of thermal barrier coatings (TBCs) to protect the superalloy substrate from oxidation and provide adhesion between the superalloy substrate and ceramic top coat [1,2] . The behavior of alloy bond coat has crucial impact on the overall performance of TBCs [3,4] . As is well known, the growth and adherence of thermally grown oxide (TGO) on the MCrAlY coat during oxidation is a major factor determining the lifetime of TBCs. It is generally accepted that the formation of a continuous and homogeneous TGO protects the coating from further oxidation and avoids the formation of mixed oxide protrusions [5] .   The reactive element Y is of premier impor- tance to the adherence of the TGO. The doping of Hf could further enhance the adherence of the TGO and then result in the improved oxidation performance of the MCrAlY coating [6] .  

In the present paper, the surface roughness, real surface area, adhesion and oxidation behavior of magnetron-sputtered NiC- oCrAlY coatings with different superalloy substrate roughness have been studied. Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM) and scratch adhesion test techniques were used to characterize the surface morphology and adhesion strength of the coatings, respectively. The oxidation behaviors of the coatings with different substrate roughness were examined by cyclic oxidation testing. The mechanisms of superal-

# 2. Experimental details  

# 2.1. Pretreatment of superalloy substrate  

Square-shaped GH3128 nickel-based superalloys of 15 mm × 15 mm × 2.5 mm were employed as the substrate materials. The elemental composition was shown in Table 1 . The superalloy substrates were subjected to six different polishing procedures prior to coating deposition as outlined below:  

![](images/d4eeeb460b0e106e7555d8898afd3009cd07687225f02dc00c2164e5a87e6cf1.jpg)  

After polishing process, all substrates were thoroughly cleaned with acetone. All samples were characterized by a NanoScope IIIa Atomic Force Microscope (AFM) in tapping mode with scanning area of 31.372  m × 31.372  m. The mean roughness value R a  of each substrate sample was calculated with over ﬁve tests on differ- ent regions of the substrate surface.  

# 2.2. Deposition and characterization of NiCoCrAlY coatings  

NiCoCrAlY coatings were deposited by means of the DC mag- netron sputtering method on the GH3128 superalloy substrate. The NiCoCrAlY composite target had a diameter of 60 mm and a thick- ness of 3 mm. Both the superalloy substrate and the alloy target were thoroughly cleaned before the coating deposition. The oper- ating conditions for the DC magnetron sputtering system were the same as that introduced in our previous study [10] . The microstruc- ture analysis of the NiCoCrAlY coatings was carried out using an X’ Pert Pro X-ray diffractometer (with Cu-K  ,  = 0.15402 nm). The AFM was also used to study the surface roughness and real surface area of the coatings.  

# 2.3. Scratch adhesion testing  

To determine the adhesion of the coatings, six types of coated samples produced with different substrate roughness were tested using a coating adhesion automatic scratch tester. A progressive constant load was applied by the spherical diamond indenter tip through the surface of the coated sample. The adhesive damage of the substrate-coating interface was brought out by the indenter tip;  

simultaneously an acoustic emission signal was aroused to display the failure of the substrate-coating system. The minimum load at which coating failure occurs is deﬁned as the critical load ( L C ) and can be represented as an indication of the substrate-coating system adhesion. The conditions employed in the tests were as follows: initial load applied, 0.5 N, ﬁnal load applied, 100 N, loading rate, 100 N/min, and scratch speed, 20 mm/min. A KEYENCE VHX-600K digital optical microscope (OM) system and a HITACHI S-3400N scanning electron microscope (SEM) were employed to evaluate the characteristic features of the scratch scar, which allowed the veriﬁcation of the critical loads determined in the scratch tests previously.  

# 2.4. Cyclic oxidation testing  

All samples were thoroughly cleaned with acetone and dried by air blower. Cyclic oxidation experiments of samples were per- formed at 1100   ◦ C in ambient atmosphere. A tube furnace with a temperature accuracy of ± 0.5   ◦ C in the hot zone was used. Six sam- ples with different substrate roughness were put into an alumina crucible and the crucible was placed into the hot zone of the fur- nace. After exposure at high temperature for 1 h, the crucible and samples were removed from the furnace and cooled to room tem- perature. After the cooling procedure for 15 min, the mass changes of samples were measured using an electronic analytical balance with a sensitivity of 0.01 mg. The crucible and samples were then returned into the furnace for next cycle. The oxidation experiments in this study were lasted for 50 cycles.

Furthermore, the sputtered NiCoCrAlY coatings presented slightly lower roughness than the corresponding superalloy substrate. It can be concluded that for this sputtered NiCoCrAlY coating, the memory of the initial substrate roughness was existent and the dynamic growth process closely depended on the initial substrate roughness [16] .

# 3.3. Effect of substrate roughness on cyclic oxidation behavior of NiCoCrAlY coatings  

The weight gains of all the NiCoCrAlY coatings basically followed the parabolic rate law up to the total 50 cycles of oxidation test as can be inferred from the square of weight gain (mg 2 /cm 4 ) versus number of cycle plots shown in Fig. 5 . After the cyclic oxidation testing, the weight gain of the six types of NiCoCrAlY coatings with different substrate roughness (Sample A, B, C, D, E, and F) were about 2.80, 2.62, 2.50, 2.32, 2.15 and 1.99 mg/cm 2 , respectively. Therefore, it can be inferred that the weight gain values for the NiCoCrAlY coatings decreased with the decreasing of the substrate roughness. The NiCoCrAlY coatings on the superalloy substrate with lower surface roughness provided relatively higher protection than that provided by the coating on the substrate with higher sur- face roughness. Table 3 presents the parabolic rate constants of the NiCoCrAlY coatings deposited on superalloy substrate with differ- ent surface roughness during cycling oxidation for 50 cycles. It can be seen that the parabolic rate constants of the NiCoCrAlY coatings decreased with the decreasing of the superalloy substrate rough- ness, which conﬁrmed that the NiCoCrAlY coatings with smoother substrate provided higher protection than that provided by the coating with rougher substrate.

It can be seen that the main phases of the scales after cyclic oxidation test for 50 cycles were Cr 2 O 3 ,  -Al 2 O 3 , NiCr 2 O 4 , CoCr 2 O 4 , and some  ı-Ni 3 Al located below the scales were also detected. Among these oxides,  -Al 2 O 3  was the protective oxida- tion product, whereas NiCr 2 O 4  and CoCr 2 O 4  (spinels) played the detrimental role in causing crack nucleation and growth [21] . For these cyclic oxidation tested NiCoCrAlY coatings sputtered onto superalloy substrates with different roughness, no signiﬁcant dif- ferences in structure and elemental composition were observed. It means that the substrate roughness affected the quantity but not the structure of the scales.  

The magnetron-sputtered nanocrystalline NiCoCrAlY coatings on superalloys possessed excellent oxidation resistance due to the selective oxidation property and nanostructured characteristic of the coatings [10] . Furthermore, the effect of superalloy substrate roughness on the cyclic oxidation resistance of the coatings could not be disregarded as noted above. The roughness of NiCoCrAlY coating increased with the increase of the substrate roughness as mentioned above. # 4. Conclusions  

The surface roughness, real surface area, adhesion strength and cyclic oxidation behavior of the NiCoCrAlY coatings deposited by magnetron sputtering on to GH3128 nickel based superalloys have been studied. The effects of superalloy substrate roughness were analyzed. According to above results and discussions, the following conclusions can be drawn:  

(1) The surface roughness of the magnetron-sputtered NiCoCrAlY coatings increases with the increasing of the superalloy sub- strate roughness. The NiCoCrAlY coatings present slightly lower roughness than the corresponding superalloy substrates.

 (2) The adhesion strength of sputtered NiCoCrAlY coatings increases with the decreasing of superalloy substrate rough- ness. The main causes of this change law are the decrease of internal stress in the coating and ﬂaws at the interface.

 (3) The NiCoCrAlY coating on the superalloy substrate with lower roughness provides relatively higher protection than that pro- vided by the coating with rougher substrate. Both the real surface area and oxidation weight gain of the coatings decrease with the decreasing of the superalloy substrate roughness. All the NiCoCrAlY coatings sputtered on superalloy substrate with different surface roughness basically follow the parabolic rate law up to the total 50 cycles of oxidation test at 1100   ◦ C. Further- more, the parabolic rate constants of the NiCoCrAlY coatings decrease with the decreasing of the superalloy substrate rough- ness.
