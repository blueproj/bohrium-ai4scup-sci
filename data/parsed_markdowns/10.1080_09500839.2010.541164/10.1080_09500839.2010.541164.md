# Enhanced resistance to weld cracking by strain-induced rapid solidification during linear friction welding

( Received 17 June 2010; final version received 14 November 2010 )  

A study of weld cracking resistance during linear friction joining of a difficult-to-weld nickel-based superalloy was performed by Gleeble thermo mechanical simulation coupled with an extensive microstructural analysis. It was found that crack-free welds produced by the supposedly solid-state joining technique of linear friction welding is not due to preclusion of grain boundary liquation as has been commonly assumed and reported. Instead, resistance to cracking can be related to a reduction in liquid stability by the imposed compressive strain during welding.  

Keywords:  rapid solidification; friction welding; nickel alloys; stress- assisted diffusion; weld cracking  

# 1. Introduction  

Nickel-based superalloys that are strengthened mainly by   0 precipitates, such as IN 738, are used for the manufacturing of hot-section components in aero-engines and power generation gas turbines. Fusion welding is an economical joining technique for the fabrication and repair of service-damaged turbine parts. A major problem which, however, limits a wider commercial application of these materials is their poor weldability during component fabrication and repairs [1]. The alloys, particularly, those with high-volume fraction of the ordered intermetallic   Ni 3 (Al,Ti),   0 phase ( 4 40%) are highly susceptible to intergranular microfissuring during conventional welding processes. The fundamental cause of the weldability problem in these alloys is the liquation of grain boundary region, which weakens and embrittles the materials during joining. Developments in welding research have resulted in the use of supposedly exclusive solid-state friction joining processes. These include friction stir welding, friction spot welding, inertia friction welding, continuous drive friction welding and linear friction welding (LFW), which are state- of-the-art in producing crack-free welds in difficult-to-weld structural alloys. Studies have shown that, in particular, LFW is potentially well-suited for joining highly crack-susceptible nickel-based superalloys [2,3]. Currently, there are active on-going research on effective and efficient industrial utilization of this technique in the manufacturing of aero-engine components like turbine discs and blades [4].  

LFW involves the use of heat generated under a reciprocating linear motion of two workpieces against each other in plasticizing and subsequently joining them under the influence of an axial compressive forging force during the terminal stage of the process. The effectiveness of friction welding techniques in producing crack-free welds has been generally attributed to preclusion of grain boundary liquation during joining, since joining essentially occurs below the melting temperature of the bulk material. Recent study by the present authors, however, showed that this is predicated on a significantly flawed premise [5]. A careful microscopic analysis of IN 738 superalloy welded by LFW showed that despite the preclusion of cracking,     0 precipitates liquated and contributed to grain boundary liquation during joining [5]. Therefore, production of crack-free welds during the supposedly exclusive solid-state LFW is not due to preclusion of grain boundary liquation as has been generally assumed and reported. Notwithstanding the occurrence of liquation, the difference between resistance to cracking during LFW and susceptibility to cracking during conventional welding can be related to the compressive stress that is applied during the forging phase of LFW. This concept, which has not been reported in the literature, is the main focus of this communication.  

# 2. Experimental  

The material used in this study was cast plates of IN 738 superalloy with a composition of (wt pct) 0.11 C, 15.84 Cr, 8.5 Co, 2.48 W, 1.88 Mo, 0.92 Nb, 0.07 Fe, 3.46 Al, 3.47 Ti, 1.69 Ta, 0.04 Zr, 0.012 B and balance nickel. Test welding coupons 3   with dimensions 12.8    11.1    17.7 mm were subjected to standard solution heat treatment (SHT) of 2 h at 1120  C followed by air cooling, and subsequently joined by the LFW technique at NRC Aerospace (Montreal, QC). In order to study and decouple the effect of non-equilibrium thermal cycle and imposed compressive force during the joining, physical simulation was performed using Gleeble thermomecha- nical simulation system. The simulation was performed at 150  C/s heating rate to temperatures ranging from 1110  C to 1230  C for different holding times followed by air cooling. As-welded and Gleeble-simulated specimens were analyzed using a JSM 5900 scanning electron microscope (SEM) equipped with an ultra-thin window Oxford energy dispersive spectrometer and a ZEISS Axiovert 25 inverted reflected- light optical microscope.

Aside from back-diffusion, an effective mode through which non-equilibrium grain boundary liquid can rapidly solidify at high temperatures is by intergranular liquid film migration (LFM), which is characterized by a homogeneously alloyed or dealloyed solidified zone produced by the migration process of liquid films [11]. The most widely accepted mechanism that is used in explaining LFM is diffusional coherency strain mechanism [12]. According to this, on introduction of a metastable liquid between two adjacent grains, a rapid equilibration process is set up by lattice solute back-diffusion. This results in composition-dependent variation in lattice parameter between solute rich solid layer in contact with the liquid and the matrix well away from the solid–liquid interface. If there is a sufficient size difference between the solute and matrix atoms, coherency strains could be developed within the grains due to lattice mismatch. The strains would cause shifts in the free energy curve of the liquid–solid interface on either side of the film such that the compositions of the liquid and solid at equilibrium will be different at both solid– liquid interfaces. Ojo et al. [11] have reported that atomic size mismatch between     0 forming elements and matrix atoms in IN 738 can induce intergranular LFM driven by diffusional coherency strain in the HAZ during conventional welding, where no externally applied stress is involved. During LFW, however, imposed compressive strain can significantly influence LFM by (1) aiding the initiation stage, (2) increasing the migration velocity and (3) abetting the sustenance of the process, and these are discussed next.

Fast solidification by LFM process is dependent and controlled by rapid solute diffusional transport within the intergranular liquid film. The liquid state diffusion is primarily driven by solute concentration gradient in the liquid, which is caused by difference in compositions of the liquid in contact with the two adjacent strained grains. The difference in the liquid compositions,  D C , arises due to an existence of a differential strain,  D " , between the adjacent grains, which causes the liquid composition at equilibrium with the strained solids to differ. According to the diffusional coherency strain theory, despite expected generation of similar levels of coherency stress in adjacent grains, owing to symmetrical lattice back-diffusion,  D " does exist between the grains. This is due to difference in the values of crystallographic orientation-dependent modulus of elasticity,  Y , in the two grains [13]. An increase in  D "  will produce a corresponding increase in  D C , which would imply a higher driving force for LFM, and as such, any factor that effectively increases the magnitude of  D "  can significantly accelerate the LFM process.  

For a given set of  Y  values in two adjacent grains, the higher the magnitude of stress experienced by the grains, the higher will be  D " . During conventional welding processes, where no externally applied stress is involved, based on coherency strain mechanism,  D "  is generated mainly by coherency stress produced by change in intragranular chemical composition induced by solute lattice back-diffusion. However, during LFW, in addition to diffusional coherency stress that may be present, an externally imposed compressive stress, which is generally several orders of magnitude higher, is involved. Moreover, even though LFM is possible in the weld HAZ during conventional welding without imposed stress, its mere occurrence does not guarantee resistance to liquation cracking by rapid re-solidification. One major factor that limits the effectiveness of LFM in aiding resistance to weld cracking is significant reduction in its driving force, due to loss of coherency, which could result in immobility of grain boundary film, thus, preventing complete elimination of the intergranular liquid. It has been recognized that coherency strain energy is a function of size of migrated region [14] and Baker and Purdy [9] have suggested that coherency loss in the grain ahead of a migrating liquid film will occur when the effective solute penetration distance,  D s / v , becomes greater than or equal to the critical diffusion distance,  L s , required for the nucleation of misfit dislocation. In situations where an applied stress is involved, such as during LFW, the associated higher migration velocity,  v , could significantly reduce  D s / v , such that the condition of  D s / v  5  L s  may persist for a longer time to sustain the process by coherency energy. Even in an eventual loss of coherency strain driving force, the  D "  induced by the externally applied stress would serve as an extra source of driving energy to sustain rapid re-solidification of the intergranular liquid through LFM process. In addition, Brechet and Purdy [15] made an important comment on LFM process which is applicable to situations involving   discrete liquating particles like     0 precipitates in superalloys. They reported that a complete solidification of grain boundary liquid film would occur only under non- steady state migration process, in which the intergranular liquid is not being constantly supplied with extra solute atoms, such as, from intragranular liquid. The enhanced re-solidification of intragranular liquid under imposed compressive strain, as observed in this study, can shift the process towards such non-steady state condition, where complete solidification of intergranular liquid by LFM is possible.  

Microscopic study of LFM region in this study showed that besides smaller migrated region in the specimen simulated without imposed stress, non-equilibrium re-solidified eutectic products had formed ahead of the migrated zone (Figure 4a).

This implies that without the application of external stress, the LFM was not effective in completely removing the grain boundary liquid during holding at the peak temperature and the residual liquid ahead of the migrated zone transformed to eutectic products by non-equilibrium solidification reaction during cooling. A similar observation of the ineffectiveness of LFM in completely removing intergranular liquid, due to loss of driving energy, resulting in the formation of eutectic products and the attendant microfissuring during conventional welding, has been previously reported by the present authors [11]. In contrast, however, re-solidified eutectic products were not observed in front of migrated zones in Gleeble simulated specimens subjected to an imposed compressive stress in this study (Figure 4b). This, in addition to a larger migrated zone, indicates that a more sustained LFM under imposed stress was effective in enabling a complete solidification of the intergranular liquid prior to cooling to lower temperatures, where eutectic solidification reaction occurs. Therefore, rapid high temperature re-solidification of intergranular liquid through LFM characterized by a combination of enhanced initiation stage, higher migration velocity and better sustenance, constitutes an important factor that could significantly improve resistance to liquation cracking during LFW.  

A careful study and correlation of Gleeble simulated microstructures with those in actual welded sample in this study suggests that the peak temperature reached at the WZ during the LFW of IN 738 was at least 1230  C. This is in agreement with a recently reported study that shows that peak temperature within the WZ during material friction processing can be up to 0.9–0.97  T m , where  T m  is the melting temperature [16]. In addition, temperature measurement by optical pyrometer during friction welding of a nickel-based superalloy, Astroloy, reportedly showed that the WZ temperature was up to 1280  C [17]. Notably, however, the WZ microstructure in the LFWed IN 738 (Figure 5a) is not consistent with what is expected at such high temperatures based exclusively on thermal effect. The formation of this type of microstructure cannot be duly explained without considering and incorporating the phenomenon of strain-induced rapid solidification that is discussed for the first time in this communication. As stated earlier, rapid heating of IN 738 to 1180  C and above produces intergranular and intragranular liquation with the resul- tant as-cooled microstructure consisting of re-solidified eutectic products.  

Nevertheless, such re-solidified eutectic products were not observed within the WZ in the LFWed specimen despite indications that the peak temperature that was reached there was, at least, 1230  C during the joining. This can be attributed to rapid re- solidification of non-equilibrium liquid produced on heating to the peak tempera- ture, facilitated by the considerable compressive strain experienced by the WZ during the welding. A thermo mechanically affected region at about 400  m m away from the weld interface, which experienced marginal strain compared to the WZ, contained the re-solidified eutectic products (Figure 5b). This is at variance with what is typically observed in conventionally welded materials, in that formation of re- solidified eutectic products normally increases towards the WZ and is often accompanied by liquation cracking. The disparity can be explained by the absence of an externally applied stress during conventional welding. It is imperative to add that the effectiveness of the imposed compressive strain in aiding solidification is made possible by the fact that a material containing grain boundary liquid can exhibit significant hot ductility under compression. This is in stark contrast to the case under tensile loading, where intergranular liquation produces zero hot ductility [18].  

# 4. Conclusion  

In contrast to general conception, prevention of weld cracking during LFW is not due to preclusion of liquation during joining. Instead, rapid re-solidification of intergranular and intragranular liquid aided by imposed compressive strain, as established in this communication, is a key factor that cannot be neglected both in understanding cracking resistance as well as in understanding weld microstructure.
