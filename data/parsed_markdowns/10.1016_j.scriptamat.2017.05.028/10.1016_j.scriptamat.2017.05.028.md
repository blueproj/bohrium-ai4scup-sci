# The effect of Nb on grain boundary segregation of B in high refractory Ni-based superalloys

Atom probe tomography was used to quantitatively assess grain boundary phase compositions and determine local segregation along the grain boundary before and after a 1000 h thermal exposure at 800 °C on an experi- mental powder processed Ni-base superalloy containing elevated levels of Nb. Due to high levels of refractory alloying elements at the grain boundary, a complex network of  σ  phase precipitates formed and the interfacial segregation pro ﬁ les were studied. Although elemental B segregates to grain boundaries and secondary phase in- terfaces, insuf ﬁ cient levels are present to result in boride formation due to an enhanced solubility of B in the ma- trix phase.

In many Ni-base su- peralloy systems, boron additions have been observed to form some sort of Cr or the Mo-rich boride precipitate along the grain boundaries [5 – 8,21] . Detailed studies of this grain boundary transformation in mul- tiple alloys have led to the development of a criterion for boride forma- tion in terms of grain size, and B and Cr content  [12] . The present report aims to investigate the presence and state of boron at grain boundaries and phase interfaces of an experimental Ni-based superalloy, RRHT4, containing elevated levels of Nb, both before and after a long term 1000 h exposure at 800 °C. Grain boundary topologically closed packed (TCP) phase compositions were analyzed and compared to thermody- namically predicted compositions. Based on recent studies and Ther- mo-Calc predictions  [13] , boride precipitation should have occurred in alloy RRHT4, but only elemental boron was found along the grain boundaries and interphase interfaces. Clearly, the characteristic segre- gation behavior of boron and precipitation borides in Ni-base superal- loys remains not well understood and  ﬁ ndings from this study contribute to an improved understanding of the complex thermody- namic and kinetics in multicomponent Ni-base superalloys.  

Nickel based superalloys are the material of choice when it comes to the  “ hot section ”  of modern gas turbine engines and power generation applications, thanks to their high strength along with considerable fa- tigue resistance when exposed to corrosive environments, high stress levels, and elevated temperatures  [1,2] . The compositions of modern Ni-base superalloys are rather complex as a variety of major and minor alloying elements are utilized to serve as solid solution strength- eners in the  γ  matrix, promote the formation of  γ ′  precipitates, and/or contribute to strengthen the grain boundaries. It is generally under- stood that minor additions of boron strengthen the grain boundaries and enhance the overall creep properties of polycrystalline Ni-base su- peralloys. A study on Alloy 617, showed that a minor boron addition to the alloy's chemistry, resulted in a 25% higher creep rupture strength over the boron free variant  [3] . In most prior studies reporting on the ef- fect of boron on the properties of Ni-superalloys, the bene ﬁ cial effects are largely attributed to the presence of elemental boron along the grain boundaries and reducing the extent of creep damage  [9 – 11] . As a result, the chemistries of most commercial polycrystalline Ni-base su- peralloys contain minor additions of B, such that a monolayer of B forms along the grain boundaries. Dark  ﬁ eld TEM observations of the grain boundary  σ  phase are pro- vided in  Fig. 2 a with an inset of overlaid SAD patterns for the  γ  and  σ phases. The  σ  phase shows a (001) σ //(1-11) γ  and [110] σ //[011] γ  rela- tionship with the  γ  matrix, which has previously been observed  [16, 17] .  Fig. 2 b shows how the  σ  precipitates are completely encased and surrounded by a  γ ′  layer. As previously noted, growth of the Cr,Mo- rich  σ  phase selectively reduces the concentration of the  γ  forming ele- ments in the vicinity of the precipitate and enriches these depleted re- gions with  γ ′  forming elements that eventually contribute to the formation of a surrounding  γ ′  layer  [13] .  

3D APT tomography of the  σ  phase in the LTHT sample is provided in Fig. 3  and shows a  γ ′ / σ  interface, as well as  σ / σ  interface in an analyzed volume consisting of 86 million atoms. Interestingly, boron segregation was observed not only at the  γ ′ / σ  interface, but also at the  σ / σ  interface. The Ga contamination was estimated to be \~0.02 at.% on average. An inset image of the complex grain boundary  σ  network, after a deep etch using the electrolytic etchant used for the extraction, has been pro- vided and shows a  “ basket-weave ”  like structure surrounding each grain. A higher magni ﬁ cation of this network clearly shows that it con- sists of smaller  σ  precipitates that have nucleated in the grain boundary and have conglomerated together upon coarsening to form a very intri- cate  σ  phase structure surrounding each grain. The APT needle also cap- tures the  γ ′  surrounding layer, which is rich in Al, as  σ  has very low solubility for the  γ ′  forming elements.

A proximity histogram (proxigram), or the pro ﬁ le of the local atomic concentration in proximity of an interface, for the grain boundary region of the starting, as-aged RRHT4 microstructure shown in  Fig.1  is provid- ed in  Fig. 4 a. It clearly reveals a high degree of segregation of elemental boron at the grain boundary, up to 1 at.%. While the  γ ′  forming ele- ments, Al and Ta, show low levels of enrichment at the grain boundary, Nb, Cr, Mo and W are present in higher concentrations, which likely contributes to the eventual nucleation of  σ  phase at the grain bound- aries as the alloy is subject to the thermally exposure. Interestingly, Ni seems to be depleted in that area, while Co levels seem to remain fairly constant. In comparison, an equivalent proxigram of the LTHT sample from  Fig. 3 , shows the  σ / γ ′  phase interface, which is also enriched with elemental boron,  Fig. 4 b. Although the overall boron concentration is much lower, \~0.2 at.%, the B shows high degree of segregation to the interface. Interestingly, the B peak width in the LTHT sample is wider than in the as aged sample. This is likely due to solute segregation and diffusion of B atoms ahead of the mobile interface as the  σ  and  γ ′  precip- itates grow during the thermal exposure at 800 °C, whereas the high angle grain boundary is sessile. Finally,  Fig. 4 c shows the XRD patterns corresponding to the extracted phases from the original and the LTHT samples. In both samples, the NbC, as well as the HfO 2 , peaks can be seen, however the LTHT sample also exhibits diffraction peaks corre- sponding to the  σ  phase, as a result of the thermal exposure. It is inter- esting to note that no  μ  and/or P phase peaks have been observed in these alloys, as the two phases often show intergrowth and have been shown to nucleate off of each other  [16,17] . A closer look at XRD pattern of the as aged condition, reveals very faint  σ  peaks, that contribute \~0.75% of the total powder fraction, as determined by GSAS-II. In addi- tion, no characteristic diffraction peaks for boride were detected, even in the original microstructure, which suggests that borides are not sta- ble in this alloy and elemental boron resides at the grain boundaries.

Each proxigram was computed to at least 50 nm into the phase and the average concen- tration for each element was calculated over the plateau region and away from the interface. It is interesting to note that the  γ  matrix phase composition next to the grain boundary is nominally identical to  γ  phase channels contained inside the grains, as previously reported in  [13] . In addition, the averaged composition of the 30 – 40 nm diameter tertiary  γ ′  observed along the grain boundary was found to be similar to that of the intragranular \~300 nm diameter secondary  γ ′  precipitates, with the exception that the  ﬁ ne tertiary  γ ′  precipitates along the grain boundary contain a modestly higher concentration of Nb and slightly lower concentration of Al and Ta. These differences are likely attributed to the fact that these nano-scale tertiary  γ ′  precipitates most likely nu- cleate and grow during aging at 850 °C, whereas secondary  γ ′  precipi- tates nucleate from the  γ  phase at much higher temperatures during cooling from the solution temperature. Under conditions where the grain boundary tertiary  γ ′  form, the  γ  matrix tends to be enriched and supersaturated with Nb, rather than Al and Ta, thereby resulting in the modestly higher Nb content  [15,18] . In addition, the average grain bound- ary composition was obtained by identifying boundary using the peak po- sition of the B concentration and averaging over a 3 nm wide volume along the boron peak. The corresponding results show that the Ni and Co contents are depleted at the grain boundary by \~7 at.% and \~1 at.%, re- spectively, while Cr and Mo are both enriched by \~2 at.%. The  γ ′  forming elements Al and Ta, seem to be fairly constant to slightly enriched with compositions of 1.5 at.% and 0.2 at.%, respectively. Interestingly, the con- centration of Nb and W along the grain boundary is nearly twice as much as compared to the matrix, with values of 2.1 at.% and 2.2 at.%, re- spectively. Finally, the level of boron seems to peak at about 1.0 at.%, which is about 6 times higher than the level measured in the matrix. Looking at the LTHT sample, the composition of the  γ ′  phase surrounding the  σ  is almost identical to that of the secondary  γ ′  precipitates contained within the grains, reported in  [13] , which suggests that these precipitates approach equilibrium after 4 h of aging at 850 °C and form with the same composition. The  σ  phase composition seems to be enriched in  γ  forming elements, and has limited solubility for the  γ ′  forming elements. From the composition given in  Table 2 , the chemical formula for  σ  can be estimated as (Cr 52 , (Mo, W) 10 ), (Ni 18 , Co 19 ), which is similar to  σ  phase precipitates reported in U720  [19,20] .  

The presence of elemental boron along the grain boundaries and ab- sence of borides in the microstructure of RRHT4 is noteworthy as the cri- terion presented in  [12]  would indicate that they would likely form in the alloy based on the nominal boron concentration and the measured aver- age grain size of 25  μ m  [13] . Moreover, other atom probe tomography studies on a commercial Ni-base superalloy RR1000, which contains half the B content and an average grain size of only 5  μ m, showed the exis- tence of M 5 B 3  borides at grain boundaries, as well as within the grain in- terior  [21] . Comparing the B segregation levels along the grain boundaries in RR1000 and RRHT4, shows that the magnitude of the B concentration is lower by a factor of \~5 in RRHT4. The absence of borides in RRHT4 is sig- ni ﬁ cant and is consistent with observations reported from similar exper- imental high Nb content superalloys, RRHT1-5, as well as commonly used commercial Ni-base superalloys such as IN718 and IN909  [15,22 – 24] . In IN718, a study on the effect of C concentrations on the grain boundary segregation behavior of B revealed that higher overall C contents tended to decrease the concentration of B along the grain boundaries  [24] . Based on the results contained in  Table 2 , RRHT4 exhibits a higher overall solubility for elemental B as a comparatively higher concentration of B content can be observed in the matrix phase when compared to commer- cial alloys such as RR1000, Alloy 617B and Astroloy, which do not contain Nb  [3,21,25] . Consistent with all other reported studies, limited solubility of B was observed in the intermetallic  γ ′  precipitates. These results appear to suggest that Nb plays a role in increasing the solubility of B in the  γ

In addition, detailed APT studies on MC carbides in various Ni-base superalloys have shown that there is a comparatively higher de- gree of B solubility within the primary NbC carbides when compared to primary TaC carbides  [4,21] . The increased solubility of B in the  γ  matrix, as well as the thermodynamic ally stable NbC, serves to reduce the overall degree of B segregation along the grain boundary and contributes to the lack of boride precipitation. This would be consistent with the absence of borides observed within the microstructure of various Ni-base superal- loys, such as the RRHT1-4, IN718 and IN909, that contain suf ﬁ ciently high levels of Nb, that induce NbC formation and increase the overall solubility in the  γ  matrix. It would appear that grain boundary boride phases are largely observed only in Ni-base superalloys in which B solubility in the γ  matrix is limited and primary TaC or TiC carbides form, such as STALS15-CC and RR1000. Considering the chemistry of the grain bound- ary in the as-aged RRHT4 sample prior to the thermal exposure, it is ap- parent that elevated levels of Cr, Mo and W exist along these regions, Fig. 4 a, and contribute to the precipitation of  σ  phase. It is interesting to note that the excessive levels of Cr, Mo and W along the grain boundaries contribute to the formation of  σ  phase rather than M 5 B 3  and M 3 B 2  borides which would suggest that the driving force for  σ  phase formation is higher than that of the borides for this particular alloy, possibly due to an insuf ﬁ cient concentration of elemental B present along the grain boundary. After the  σ  phase forms, both the  σ  and the  γ ′  phases in the LTHT sample exhibit low solubility for B, which seems to be the reason for the boron segregation at the  σ / γ ′  phase interface, peaking at about 0.2 at.%. Although this value is lower than the boron peak measured at the initial  γ / γ  grain boundary, one must consider that the overall interfa- cial area increases during the LTHT as new  γ / γ ′  and  γ ′ / σ  interfaces are created that allows the elemental boron to redistribute and become more dilute, especially as this  σ  phase seems to form immediately during aging. This is also consistent with the B segregation to the  σ / σ  interface, as such segregation will further dilute the boron. Interestingly, B has been found to retard conglomeration of M 23 C 6  carbides, which has been de- scribed to be the same phase as  σ  without the C  [4,26] .  

In summary, the results of this paper show that no borides were present in RRHT4 and elemental boron was heavily segregated along the grain boundaries in the starting microstructure as well as along the  γ ′ / σ  interfaces following the LTHT. These observations contradict multiple recent studies that predict boride formation in these alloys based on composition. This discrepancy has been attributed to the de- pletion of boron and reduction in concentration along grain boundaries due to the higher solubility in the matrix phase as well as the formation of stable NbC carbides, which also have some solubility for B. Without the formation of grain boundary borides to reduce the local concentra- tions of Cr, Mo and W, rapid nucleation and growth of  σ  phase precipi- tates occurs and conglomeration of these precipitates leads to a complex grain boundary  “ basket-weave ”  type network.
