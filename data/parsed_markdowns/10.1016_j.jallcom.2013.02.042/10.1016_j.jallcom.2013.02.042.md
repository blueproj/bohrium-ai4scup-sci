Polycrystalline  c / c 0 – d  eutectic Ni-base superalloys based on the Ni–Al–Nb alloy system were recently demonstrated to possess excellent high temperature strength and creep resistance. Investigations aimed to establish the fundamental relationships between alloy chemistry, solidiﬁcation behavior and cast microstructure in these novel Ni–Al–Nb  c / c 0 – d  alloy systems are currently underway. This particular study is focused on understanding the inﬂuence of Ta additions on the solidiﬁcation sequence, phase vol- ume fraction, distribution coefﬁcient and solid state partitioning parameter of this eutectic alloy system by systematically investigating a series of experimental alloys with nominally constant overall levels of Ta + Nb content but varying Ta/Nb ratios. Although many of the tendencies observed in these multi-com- ponent  c / c 0 – d  eutectic alloys are in agreement with trends observed in lower order model alloy systems, Ta additions were found to signiﬁcantly modify solidiﬁcation characteristics of the alloys. The experimen- tal observations were also used to critically assess the predictive capability of thermodynamic database calculations. Despite the qualitative agreement observed between the experimental results and predic- tions for relatively simple quaternary and quinary model alloys, comparison of the results for higher order, multi-component  c / c 0 – d  eutectic alloys reveals notable differences.

Ni-base superalloys possess an exceptional combination of chemical and mechanical properties at elevated temperature and have been extensively used in structural components in gas tur- bine engines for propulsion and power generation applications [1–3] . Traditional polycrystalline Ni-base superalloys possess a two-phase microstructure consisting of a disordered  c  (FCC) ma- trix and ordered intermetallic precipitates  c 0 -Ni 3 Al (L1 2 ) and/or c 00 -Ni 3 Nb (D0 22 ) to provide order strengthening and/or coherency strengthening, which accounts for the majority of their high tem- perature mechanical properties  [4–6] . Based on the increasingly demanding requirements for high temperature strength and corro- sion resistance, the compositions of these alloys have been highly engineered and reﬁned over the past 60 or so years. Modern poly- crystalline superalloys usually contain more than ten major alloy- ing elements to strengthen both the matrix and the precipitates by solid solution strengthening, as well as minor amounts of carbon, boron and hafnium as grain boundary strengtheners  [3,5,6] . Directionally solidiﬁed  c / c 0 – d  (Ni–Al–Nb) eutectic alloys were originally considered as candidate materials for high pressure gas turbine airfoil structures in the 1970s and 1980s  [7–9] . Fabricated under high thermal gradients and slow growth rates, the direction- ally solidiﬁed  c / c 0 – d  eutectic alloys have a characteristic composite microstructure consisting of alternating  c  (Ni-FCC) and  d  (Ni 3 Nb- D0 a ) lamellae solidiﬁed directly from the liquid  [10] . Possessing this  in situ  composite structure with the strengthening  d  lamellae embedded within a ductile  c (Ni)/ c 0 (Ni 3 Al-L1 2 ) matrix, the  c / c 0 – d eutectic alloys were shown to exhibit superior longitudinal tensile strength and creep resistance when compared to the best perform- ing  c / c 0   Ni-base superalloys available at that time  [11–16] . This eu- tectic system was also considered as advantageous over other systems, such as the refractory-metal-reinforced composites and carbide ﬁber composites, for its compositional ﬂexibility, better resistance to thermal fatigue damage and improved ductility and oxidation resistance  [17–19] .  

In spite of these attractive properties, the application of these directionally solidiﬁed  c / c 0 – d  eutectic alloys for airfoil components has been largely limited by some of their intrinsic deﬁciencies, including relatively low  d c  interfacial strength , limited transverse ductility  [17]  and mismatch of thermal expansion coef- ﬁcient between the constituent  c – d  phases . These issues along with processing difﬁculties contributed to making these alloys unsuitable for the intended applications. However, many of these limitations may potentially be overcome by processing these  c / c 0 – d  eutectic alloys in polycrystalline form for use in applications where resistance to creep deformation is not the primary criteria. This would facilitate harnessing many of the unique attributes of these  c / c 0 – d  eutectic polycrystalline alloys, including high temper- ature yield strength  [7,8,11–16] , long-term microstructural stabil- ity  [10,16,19–21]  and ease of processing, for high performance turbine engine structures where temperatures are limited to below 800   C in the compressor or turbine sections.  

Apart from a limited number of published studies on quater- nary directionally solidiﬁed  c / c 0 – d  eutectic alloys  [22–24]  and ter- nary and quaternary phase equilibria  [25–27] , however, much of our knowledge about the  c / c 0 – d  eutectic alloy system remains qualitative in nature. A fundamental understanding of how various alloy additions inﬂuence the solidiﬁcation characteristics and microstructure of multi-component eutectic alloys is critical to the development of high performance polycrystalline  c / c 0 – d  eutec- tic alloys with optimized properties. As an alloying addition, Ta (Tantalum) has been reported to have a number of positive effects on the solidiﬁcation characteristics and mechanical properties of Ni-base superalloys  [24,28] . Moreover, Ta is one of the few ele- ments that have substantial solubility in the ( d ) Ni 3 Nb phase [29] . Knowledge regarding the inﬂuence of Ta on  c / c 0 – d  eutectic system containing other alloying elements (Cr, Co, Ti, Mo, and W) can provide valuable insight for optimizing the microstructure of these materials. The aim of the present investigation was to elu- cidate the effect of Ta on the solidiﬁcation pathways, elemental segregation behavior and formation of primary microstructures of near eutectic multi-component  c / c 0 – d  alloys. A critical compar- ison between experimental results and thermodynamic calcula- tions generated with a commercially available thermodynamic software package, Pandat (Computherm LLC.) was also performed using the PanNi7 database.  

# 2. Experiment materials and procedure  

In this study, four alloy compositions with constant overall levels of Ta + Nb but increasing Ta/Nb ratios were designed to study the inﬂuence of Ta on this  c / c 0 – d  eu- tectic system,  Table 1 . Each alloy was named by its Ta concentration. Concentra- tions of other elements were kept constant. Pure Ni (99.95%), Cr (99.5%), Co (99.9%), Al (99.9%), Ti (99.9%), W (99.9%), Mo (99.8%), Ta (99.9%) and Nb (99.98%) were melted in a MRF ABJ-900 arc melter under an argon atmosphere. The melting chamber was purged under vacuum to a pressure level of 10  3   mbar and then back- ﬁlled with high purity argon (99.998%) to ensure alloy cleanliness. This purging pro- cedure was repeated three times before melting. Each of the arc melted ingots was melted and remelted up to six times to ensure uniform mixing of the elemental additions. The solidiﬁed button ingots were observed to have approximately the same dimensions (  3 cm in diameter and   1 cm in height) and weight (  60 g). Mass loss during arc melting was determined to be less than 0.1% for all the alloys. Nominally constant solidiﬁcation conditions, as described elsewhere (solidiﬁcation rate    20   C/s, thermal gradient    300   C/cm)  [30] , were ensured by exercising identical melting procedures.  

To better characterize the dendritic microstructure and maintain consistency between alloys, microstructural characterization was performed on specimens ex- cised from the center of the transverse plane of all button ingots, perpendicular to the general dendrite growth direction. Standard grinding, polishing and ultra- sonic cleaning procedures were practiced before etching with a solution of 1:1:1 HCl:HNO 3 :distilled water (by volume). A Nikon ECLIPSE MA200 microscope was used for optical microscopy (OM) and a JEOL JSM-5900LV scanning electron micro- scope (SEM) was used for for detailed analysis of the  c / c 0 – d  microstructure.  

A SETARAM SETSYS differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) unit was used to measure the solidus and liquidus temperatures of these experimental alloys. The DSC unit was calibrated with high purity Ni (99.95%) and silver (99.9%) at scanning rates of 3, 5 and 10   C/min, using a sapphire reference. DSC samples were sectioned near the center of the alloys with a mass of approximately 200 mg to ensure that sure they are representative of the bulk alloys. Samples were ultra-sonically cleaned for 30 min in ethanol and then tested in high purity alumina crucibles. The DSC experiments were conducted under ﬂowing argon (99.998%) to protect both the furnace and the DSC samples from oxidation. Only the heating curve was used to determine the phase transformation temperatures to avoid undercool- ing effects. Derivatives of the heating curves were used for solidus and liquidus determination, as it is more sensitive to phase change. A heating rate of 5   C/min was chosen since it was determined to yield the best combination of peak resolu- tion and temperature accuracy.  

Elemental segregation characteristics and phase compositions were measured using a JEOL JSM-5900LV SEM equipped with Oxford Instruments X-Max energy dispersive spectrometer (EDS) on polished and unetched samples. The EDS system was calibrated using a powder alloy standard of similar composition (Ni–7.2Cr– 6.0Al–0.7Ti–1.0Mo–0.5W–1.0Ta–11.5Nb at.%) to increase the accuracy of measure- ments. To ensure the consistency of the results throughout the study, all the exper- imental parameters were kept constant (accelerating voltage = 20 kV, working distance = 10 mm, spot size = 40, acquisition time = 60 s). A minimum of 10 discrete EDS measurements each occurring over an area measuring approximately 20  l m    50  l m were taken from representative regions of the microstructure to ensure the statistical signiﬁcance and accuracy. Quantitative area fraction measurement of different phases in these experi- mental  c / c 0 – d  eutectic alloys was performed using image analysis software as representative of the phase volume fractions. SEM images were taken at a min- imum of ten randomly selected regions at appropriate magniﬁcations that al- lowed for statistically signiﬁcant measurements of area fractions of  c  and  d . These SEM images were ﬁrst processed in Photoshop 7.0 by manually selecting the phase boundaries using the Pen Tool function. A selected phase was then ﬁlled with white color, while the background was ﬁlled with black color. These contrast-enhanced images with well-deﬁned phase boundary were ﬁnally ana- lyzed digitally using the thresholding function of ImageTool 2.0 to generate the area fraction information. These area fractions will be referred to as volume fractions hereafter. Some of the experimental results were compared with the thermodynamic database predictions from a commercially available thermody- namic software package, Pandat (Computherm LLC.). The PanNi7 database was used for calculations in this study.  

The phase volume fraction and elemental partitioning behavior of these mul- ti-component  c / c 0 – d  eutectic alloys were assessed in both the as-cast condition as well as after homogenization. Samples of X1Ta, X3Ta and X5Ta were homog- enized at 1270   C (above  c 0   solvus) for 1 day, 2 days and 3 days respectively, fol- lowed by water quench, in an attempt to minimize compositional heterogeneities within the microstructure attributed to dendritic segregation. The homogenization temperature was chosen because temperatures above 1270   C were shown to induce incipient melting in these XTa alloys, while longer heat treatment times were chosen for higher Ta-containing alloys because of their higher melting (solidus) temperatures. This heat treatment was intended to convert the dendritic as-cast microstructure into a near-equilibrium micro- structure comprised of coarse  d  particles uniformly embedded within a  c  matrix, in which the degree of residual segregation was minimized. EDS and image anal- ysis were then performed on these homogenized samples in the manner de- scribed above.

# 4. Discussion  

Multi-component polycrystalline  c / c 0 – d  eutectic alloys have been recently demonstrated to possess attractive yield strength and creep resistance at intermediate temperatures, yet the alloying effect of various elements common to traditional  c / c 0   Ni-base superalloys, is still unknown to this high Nb-containing  c / c 0 – d  eu- tectic system. The effect of Ta is of particular interest because it shares many similarities to Nb. Ta and Nb have the same valence and approximately identical atomic radii, making them completely soluble within each other  [36] . Additionally, Nb and Ta are both  d - Ni 3 Nb (D0 a -type structure) forming elements and promote the L  ?  c  +  d  eutectic reaction in their respective Ni-binary systems [37,38] . Within the isothermal section of Ni–Nb–Ta ternary system at 1200   C, a continuous  d  phase ﬁeld extends from the Ni–Nb side to the Ni–Ta side,  Fig. 9 . For these reasons, any increase in either the Nb content or the Ta content in the  c – d  eutectic system can be expected to shift the alloy composition towards the Nb(Ta)-rich side (the hyper-eutectic side) and stabilize the formation of  d phase. However, it is not clear how variations in Ta/Nb ratio will affect the solidiﬁcation characteristics and microstructure of this c / c 0 – d  eutectic system, particularly in the presence of other alloy- ing elements.

Substantial differences in microstructure were observed in the XTa series of alloys with various Ta/Nb ratios, indicating a dis- tinctly different effect of these two elements on the  c – d  eutectic system. Ta additions of greater than 2 at.% in the XTa alloys result in the formation of primary  c  dendrites during solidiﬁcation, shift- ing the initial eutectic composition into the hypo-eutectic  c  phase region,  Fig. 3 . The distinct solidiﬁcation sequence associated with high Ta levels can be qualitatively explained by the proposed eu- tectic trough in the pseudo-ternary Ni–Nb–Ta system, with a line representing the (Ta + Nb) = 13.7 at.% isopleth,  Fig. 10 . Both the eu- tectic trough in the Ni–Nb–Ta ternary system and the proposed eu- tectic trough in the XTa alloy system exhibit a symmetric shape that spans across the Ni–Nb side to the Ni–Ta side. As the phase boundary traverses across the ternary phase diagram, the  c – d  eu- tectic trough exhibits a curvature that extends into the primary  d ﬁeld. This curvature of the  c – d  eutectic trough is consistent with the observed variation in as-cast microstructures associated with the alloys investigated in this study. Since the Ta/Nb ratio varies from 0.08 in alloy X1Ta to 0.57 in alloy X5Ta, the nominal compo- sition of the alloys can be observed to start at the virtual eutectic trough and gradually span into the  c  phase region, leading to a higher level of primary  c  dendrite formation. A similar trend would be expected if the  c – d  eutectic alloy composition were to start from the Ni–Ta side with small amounts of Nb additions substitut- ing for Ta.  

Differences in the inﬂuence of Ta and Nb additions were also re- ﬂected by the noticeable differences of their segregation behavior during solidiﬁcation,  Fig. 6 . While Nb strongly segregates to the c – d  eutectic regions, 0    served to partition equally between the dendritic and interdendrit- ic regions,  k 0     1. This behavior contradicts the well documented segregation behavior of Ta in single crystal Ni-base superalloys, where Ta segregates preferentially into the interdendritic region during solidiﬁcation, resulting in  k 0   > 1  [31–33,40] . This difference in the segregation characteristics of Ta and Nb can be illustrated again by the Ni–Nb–Ta ternary phase diagram,  Fig. 11 . During the initial stages of solidiﬁcation in the X3Ta and X5Ta alloys, li- quid and solid ( c ) coexist within the two-phase region, with the phase composition determined by the S–L tie line. Since the exper- imental alloys investigated in this study contain limited levels of Ta (1–5 at.%), the S–L tie lines that these alloy compositions reside on are close and roughly parallel to the Ni–Nb binary side. Despite these contrasting effects observed between Ta and Nb addition in this system, nominally identical solid-state partitioning tendencies between  c  and  d  ( k  = 0.5) were observed for these alloys with different Ta/Nb ratios at 1270   C,  Fig. 7 . This is not surprising, since elemental Ta and Nb have very similar electronic structures and atomic radii, leading to nominally identical effects on the cor- responding lattice parameters of both  c  and  d  phases . These factors all contribute to inﬂuencing the extent of solid solubility , which governs the equilibrium phase volume fraction of  c and  d  in this eutectic system. Such identical portioning behavior of Ta and Nb between  c  and  d  is in good agreement with the pro- nounced promoting effect of the overall (Ta + Nb) content, as well as the neutral effect of Ta/Nb ratio, on eutectic  d  volume fraction, Table 2 .  

The observed different solidiﬁcation characteristics and the similar solid state partitioning behavior of Ta and Nb have impor- tant implications to the design of cast and wrought  c / c 0 – d  eutectic alloys. To achieve the most desirable mechanical properties of these eutectic alloys, the overall Ta + Nb content could be ﬁnely tuned to generate the optimal volume fraction of the strengthening d  phase. On the other hand, the Ta/Nb ratio could be adjusted inde- pendently of the Ta + Nb content to promote the segregation-less near-eutectic microstructure during solidiﬁcation. Moreover, Ta was reported to strongly partition to the  c 0   precipitates in the  c / c 0   matrix  [1,43–45] . This suggests that equilibrium volume fraction of  c 0   precipitates can be potentially varied independently to the volume fraction of  d  phase by adjusting the Ta/Nb ratio in  c / c 0 – d eutectic alloys.  

As an increasingly popular tool in materials design and selec- tion, thermodynamic database models have been demonstrated to be quite reliable in predicting phase stability and volume frac- tions in traditional  c / c 0   Ni-base superalloys  [46–49] . These data- base models could potentially be extended to the  c / c 0 – d  eutectic system, which has substantially higher Nb content than traditional Ni-base superalloys. In our previous study, it was demonstrated that Pandat was able to predict qualitative trends in Cr-containing quaternary  c / c 0 – d  eutectic system  [30] . For the multi-component c / c 0 – d  eutectic alloys investigated in this study, however, Pandat predictions are comparatively less accurate. Pertaining to the equilibrium phase volume fraction, qualitatively different trends were predicted by Pandat when compared to the measured results, Fig. 5 . These discrepancies can likely be attributed to the limited amount of thermodynamic data that exists in high Nb containing sub-systems, which causes errors in the thermodynamic prediction of  c / c 0 – d  eutectic alloys in general  [50] . To summarize, additional experimental assessment of multi-component  c / c 0 – d  eutectic al- loys are required to validate the existing thermodynamic databases before new  c / c 0 – d  eutectic alloys can be designed solely based on computational methods.  

# 5. Conclusions  

Both similarities and differences between Ta and Nb alloying additions were observed in multi-component  c – d  eutectic system. Based on the results presented in the paper, the following conclu- sions may be drawn:  

1. At high temperature close to the solidus temperature, Ta and Nb tend to have a nominally identical inﬂuence on the phase equi- libria of this  c – d  eutectic system in terms of maintaining a con- stant equilibrium volume fraction of the  d  phase.

 2. During solidiﬁcation, substitution of Nb with Ta tends to pro- mote primary  c  formation, shifting the alloy composition to the  c -rich side (hypo-eutectic). The segregation behavior of Ta also differs from Nb in the cast alloys, as Ta is equally distrib- uted between the dendritic and interdendritic ( c – d  eutectic) regions.

 3. The microstructure of the  c / c 0 – d  eutectic alloys is sensitive to both the overall level of Ta + Nb, as well as the Ta/Nb ratio. Modiﬁcations to the resulting microstructure can be accom- plished by varying the alloy composition.

 4. Thermodynamic software package Pandat coupled with the PanNi7 database is capable of predicting the qualitative trends of solidiﬁcation behavior of multi-component  c / c 0 – d  eutectic alloys, but was unable to correctly characterize all trends of phase volume fraction associated with higher Ta levels at lower temperature. High ﬁdelity data in the high Nb and high Ta regimes is required to validate and reﬁne these databases.
