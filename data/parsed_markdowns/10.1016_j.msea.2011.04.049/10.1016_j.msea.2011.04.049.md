A new method for the investigation of interfacial dislocations in nickel-base superalloys employing SEM has been recently established by the authors. The dislocations become visible because of grooves, which form in the   /  ′ -interface at the dislocation core. The groove formation mechanism was investigated by annealing/cooling experiments and creep experiments with the single-crystal superalloys SRR99 and CMSX-4. Possible forces for groove formation are discussed and their magnitude is estimated. The osmotic force, resulting from the vacancy supersaturation during quenching, was found to be the main reason for groove formation. The correlation between groove shape and Burgers vector of the interfacial dislocation is explained. The conditions required for observation of such grooves using SEM are discussed.

# 1. Introduction  

Nickel base superalloys consist of two phases: the fcc matrix (   phase) and L1 2  precipitates (  ′   phase). After standard heat treatment the precipitates have a cuboidal shape and the   /  ′ - interfaces are planar and coherent. Deformation or overaging at high temperature results in interfacial dislocations forming networks. They relax misﬁt stresses and introduce backstresses, retarding mobile matrix dislocations. Therefore the investiga- tion of interfacial dislocations is of immense interest  [1–7] . Whilst studies have been historically conducted by transmis- sion electron microscopy (TEM), it has recently been shown that such semicoherent interfaces can also be visualized by scanning electron microscope (SEM)  [8] . When the   ′ -phase is removed by deep etching, the   /  ′ -interface becomes visible, and is covered with a net of grooves, formed at the disloca- tions.  

The 2nd question relates to the shape of the groove. The grooves can be divided into two groups: ﬁrstly, grooves in which the   /  ′ - interface on its right side has mirror symmetry to the interface on its left side, subsequently denoted as symmetric grooves. Second, grooves, in which the interface is strongly bent on one side and weakly on the other, or asymmetric grooves. These two groups were described in our previous paper  [8] , in which it was found, that the dislocation at the tip of the asymmetric groove always has a Burgers vector, which is at 45 ◦ to the  { 1 0 0 }   /  ′ -interface. Such dislocations experience an elastic force and can move at high temperature along the interface. It was assumed in  [8]  that such a movement results in a weakly bent interface behind the disloca- tion, and a strongly bent interface ahead: we call this the  movement hypothesis  ( Fig. 1 c). A different explanation for the asymmetry of the groove could be that it develops during cooling. In this case the ori- entation of the half plane relative to the interface would determine the asymmetry of the groove ( Fig. 1 d). This  orientation hypothesis was not considered in  [8] .  

The aim of our new investigations was to determine the mech- anism by which these grooves are formed, allowing both control over groove formation as well as an understanding of the connec- tion between groove shape and dislocation type. # 2. Experimental  

Superalloys in the form of 1st and 2nd generation single crystals, SRR99 and CMSX-4 respectively, were investigated. Heat treatment consisted of a homogenization and a two stage annealing process. SRR99 was annealed at 1080   ◦ C/4 h and 870   ◦ C/16 h; CMSX-4 at 1140   ◦ C/6 h, 870   ◦ C/20 h. This heat treatment results in the super- alloys having a regular cuboidal microstructure with a cube edge length of about 0.4   m. The following experiments were performed in order to answer the four questions described above:  

# 2.1. Annealing/cooling experiments (see 1st question)  

Two step experiments were performed in order to discern between swelling and indentation mechanisms. Specimens of SRR99 were annealed in air at 1100   ◦ C for 300 h in order to partially dissolve the   ′ -phase  [9]  and to generate interfacial dislocations [8]  followed by cooling of the specimens. The crucial factor was the cooling rate: only upon slow cooling of the specimens, the dissolved   ′ -phase re-precipitated at the   /  ′ -interfaces of the pri- mary   ′ -precipitates resulting in swelling, i.e. interface migration. In rapidly quenched specimens, the re-precipitation led to very ﬁne secondary   ′ -precipitates in the matrix channels with no swelling. Thus, where grooves form during cooling, the groove depth should increase with decreasing cooling rate.  

The cooling rates were controlled in temperatures ranging from 1100   ◦ C to 800   ◦ C. These varied from 0.7  ×  10 − 1  ◦ C/s for a cylindrical specimen with a diameter of 25 mm and height of 22 mm employ- ing normal furnace cooling, to 1.4  ×  10 1  ◦ C/s and 2.4  ×  10 2  ◦ C/s for cuboid specimens of size 6 mm  ×  6 mm  ×  12 mm using vertical type furnace with fast quenching provision in air and in water respec- tively. The SEM-investigations were carried out on the annealed specimens cut along  { 0 0 1 }  planes. After polishing, the   ′ -phase was removed by selective deep etching until the surrounding matrix with the topography of the   /  ′ -interface became visible (details given in  [8] ). The   /  ′ -interfaces were studied with a Zeiss, LEO GEMINI 1530 VP SEM equipped with an annular in-lens detec- tor for secondary electrons. For the observation of grooves, the rotational symmetry of this detector relative to the optical axis is important, as it provides certainty that an observed asymmetric contrast really results from groove asymmetry. The resolution of the SEM at 15 kV is about 1 nm ( Fig. 2 ).  

In order to have a comparison between images obtained by the new SEM method and the classical TEM method, foils prepared from the same annealed SRR99 specimens along the (0 0 1) plane were investigated with a TEM Philips CM30.

# 2.2. Creep experiments (see 2nd question)  

Creep tests were performed in order to allow interfacial dis- locations to move in deﬁned directions, allowing differentiation between the movement and orientation hypotheses. A specimen of SRR99 with [0 0 1] orientation was creep tested in air under ten- sion at 980   ◦ C, 200 MPa for 5 h up to 0.06% strain and then cooled down under load. Under such primary creep conditions, disloca- tions propagate in (0 0 1) matrix channels perpendicular to the load axis  [10] . TEM foils were prepared from (1 0 0) longitudinal cuts. This is different from the usual procedure, where creep specimens are cut parallel to the (0 0 1)-interface in order to analyze interfa- cial dislocation networks. However, when both are of interest, the shape of the groove and the Burgers vector of the dislocation located at the tip of  this  groove, the analyzed   /  ′   interface has to be located edge on in the TEM-foil. When such foils are arranged in an untilted position, the grooves are clearly visible. For observation of the dis- locations causing these grooves, the specimen was tilted, allowing a better view onto the dislocations ( Fig. 5 ). The Burgers vectors   ⃗  and line vectors  of these dislocations were analyzed on different zone axes with different reﬂections  ⃗  (diffraction contrast).   ⃗  was deter- mined by the  ⃗  ·  ⃗  =  0 criterion, its sign by imaging the dislocation with  +⃗  and  −⃗  reﬂections according to  [11] .  ⃗  was determined by taking images at different zone axes and using the TOCA software [12] .  

# 2.3. Optimisation of groove development (see 3rd question)  

The dependence of the interface topography upon the test temperature was analyzed in CMSX-4 specimens, which were creep-deformed at 1150, 1100, 1050, 1000, 950, 900 and 850   ◦ C. Additionally we determined whether the stress condition present during cooling (under load respectively without load) has an effect on the grooves. These investigations were performed using SEM and TEM.  

The 4th question, concerning the stresses in the interface, was treated theoretically, based on dislocation theory, thermodynamics and literature data.

During cooling two growth processes were running simultaneously in the   /  ′ -microstructure:  

•  Swelling of primary   ′ -precipitates by extracting the   ′ -forming elements from the   -channels, resulting in SGFZs around the pri- mary   ′   precipitates.

The growth process needs time, thus it depends on the cooling rate. For the fast cooling rate, nucleation of secondary  ′ -precipitates is dominant over the slow   ′ -growth. Therefore small   ′   nuclei formed everywhere in the   -channels ( Fig. 4 c). The interfaces remained stationary in their high temperature positions, leading to smooth interfaces ( Figs. 2c and 4c ). For slower cooling, the   ′ -growth becomes important. It is clear from  Table 1  that the groove depth (equal to the height of the   ′   protrusion), the width of SGFZ and the size of secondary   ′   precipitates increased, when the cooling rate decreased. It follows, that the grooves were not gen- erated during the network formation at 1100   ◦ C, but afterwards during cooling.  

The mesh size of an annealing network depends on the misﬁt. Therefore, in a ﬁrst approximation it should be independent from the cooling rate, as observed in  Figs. 2a–c and 3a–c . The increase in the regularity of the network with the increase in cooling rate shown in  Figs. 2 and 3  could be due to the growth of protrusions through the dislocation meshes. The shape of a single mesh on one side, was determined at high temperature by the minimization of the energies due to dislocations and misﬁt stresses, the anisotropic  ′   growth in  ⟨ 1 1 1 ⟩ directions  [13]  during cooling, on another side, resulted in a “butterﬂy”-morphology as observed in  Fig. 6 a. If these two shapes are incompatible, the regularity of the network is dis- torted.  

The 2nd question concerned the asymmetric shape of grooves. In order to test between the proposed hypotheses viz. movement and direction hypothesis, a well deﬁned dislocation movement was initiated in SRR99 by uniaxial tensile loading in [0 0 1] direction. It is well known that at temperatures high enough for creep, and stresses too low for   ′   cutting, deformation starts by matrix dislo- cations, entering the channels by bowing out. The front part of such a loop glides in the matrix on a  { 1 1 1 }  plane, whereas the trailing parts proceed in the   /  ′ -interfaces by glide/climb  [10,15] . These trailing dislocations in   /  ′ -interfaces were the subject of inter- est in  Fig. 5 . Comparison of the experimental result in  Fig. 5 e and the hypothetical mechanisms in  Fig. 1 c and d leads to the conclu- sion that the orientation hypothesis is correct: the groove notch is oriented along the half plane.

The main properties of these forces are described below; details are provided in  Appendix .  

The surface tension of the interface  F int / L  tries to reduce the surface area, and in the present case to annihilate the groove and restore a planar surface. The force was oriented along the bisectrix of the angle  ϕ  at the groove tip, and directed from the   ′ - phase towards the   -phase. For the most pronounced groove,  ϕ  = 0, resulted a force  F int max   /L  ≈ 0 . 06 N / m. The interface tension force is shown in  Fig. 7  by a dash-dot line.  

The line tension of a dislocation tries to shrink the dislocation loop, wrapped around a   ′ -precipitate. The resulting force on the precipitate  F line / L  lies in the plane of the dislocation curvature, is perpendicular to the dislocation line, and directed from the    phase towards the   ′ -phase. Its magnitude is inverse to the curvature radius, which was taken as 1/20 of the cube size of 500 nm. It is clear from  Fig. 7 , that  F line / L  is temperature dependent, which is due to the temperature dependence of elastic constants.  

Misﬁt stresses are acting on the component of the Burgers vec- tor parallel to the planar interface. The elastic force is normal to the interface and attracts the dislocation towards the interface to com- pensate the misﬁt stress. As soon as the dislocation is placed in the interface, the force becomes zero. So misﬁt stresses are irrelevant for the groove formation.  

When uniaxial applied load acts on a dislocation, the result- ing stress has two properties: ﬁrst, its direction is perpendicular to the stress direction and to the dislocation line, second, its abso- lute value is proportional to the component of the Burgers vector parallel to the stress direction. For a  a /2 ⟨ 0 1 1  ⟩ dislocation with the Burgers vector tilted 45 ◦ to the load axis, the absolute value of the elastic force is 1.6  ×  10 − 2   N/m at 1100   ◦ C and 90 MPa, which is neg- ligible compared to the other forces ( Fig. 7 ). The effect of this force for groove formation is even reduced if the dislocation lies in the  /  ′ -interface perpendicular the load axis, as is the case for   ′ -rafts. Then, the glide component alone can contribute to groove forma- tion. This theoretical conclusion, that the applied load is insufﬁcient for groove formation, was in accordance with our experimental observation: CMSX-4 specimens tested under identical creep con- ditions but cooled down either under load or without load showed no signiﬁcant difference in the topography of their interfaces.  

Osmotic forces result from the vacancy supersaturation caused by quenching. It is well known, that they are much stronger than the forces usually attainable under external stresses  [16] , which ﬁts with the results of the calculations performed ( Fig. 7 ). At the beginning of cooling, the force due to line tension  F line / L  can be stronger than the osmotic force  F os / L , but, in general, the osmotic force has a higher importance for groove formation because it acts along the total dislocation line, whereas  F line / L  acts only where the dislocation bends around the corner of a precipitate. This was in agreement with experimental observation that the grooves extend all over the raft. Thus from the quantitative investigation of forces and the experimental observations of networks, the osmotic force appears to be the main force involved in groove formation.

After having identiﬁed the swelling mechanism and the osmotic force, a 5th question arises: Does the interface move exclusively during swelling and the network remains ﬁxed in the initial posi- tion, or, to a certain extent, do the dislocations follow the expanding interface? If there is such a movement of the network, the dis- tance between interfacial networks on opposite sides of the matrix channel  H  =  w  + 2 h  should depend on the cooling rate, because the osmotic force, which retards the movement of the interfacial dislo- cations, depends on the formation and diffusion of vacancies, both being time dependent processes. It is important to mention here that the condensation of vacan- cies on interfacial dislocations during cooling has practically no effect on groove formation and dislocation position. If we assume that all supersaturated vacancies generated by quenching from 1100   ◦ C, have been absorbed, the shift in position would be less than 1 nm (estimated in  Appendix ).  

In summary therefore, the following conditions are necessary for observation of interfacial dislocations by SEM (3rd question):  

•  The test temperature should be high enough to dissolve enough  ′ -phase for groove formation. For CMSX-4 this temperature was about 950   ◦ C, where about 2 vol.%   ′   was dissolved according to formula  (1) .  

•  The cooling rate should be slow enough to provide sufﬁcient time for groove formation. However, a slow cooling rate not only has a positive effect for clear visualization of the interfacial networks, but also a negative effect of distortion of the networks during cooling. Therefore, cooling should be faster for high test tem- peratures (strong   ′ -re-precipitation) than for lower ones (weak  ′ -re-precipitation).

 •  Cooling the specimen under load condition plays no role in groove formation. Therefore, the routine procedure of freezing in dislo- cation conﬁgurations under load does not disturb the observation of interfacial dislocation networks in present case.  

Groove formation is expected to have an effect on the mechan- ical properties of superalloys under service conditions. It is known, that turbine blades of aircraft engines operate under non- isothermal conditions  [17] , e.g. overheating during take off. Cooling down from this overheating to stationary operating temperatures can result in grooved interfaces, which is expected to support the entering of dislocations into the   ′ -precipitates.  

# 5. Conclusions  

(1) The grooved topography of semicoherent   /  ′ -interfaces forms by a “swelling mechanism’ ′ : the fraction of the   ′ -phase, which was dissolved during annealing or creep, re-precipitates during cooling. The interface expands preferentially between the inter- facial dislocations, because the dislocations are pinned, mostly by strong osmotic forces resulting from the vacancy supersat- uration during quenching.

 (2) The osmotic force acts along the half extra plane of the interfa- cial dislocation. If the shape of the groove is symmetric, the Burgers vector of the dislocation lies in the interface. If the groove is asymmetric, the half plane is oriented in the same direction as the bisecting line of the groove.

 (3) For the observation of interfacial dislocations in SEM, two experimental aspects have to be considered: the test tempera- ture must be high enough to dissolve a part of   ′   sufﬁcient for groove formation (about 2 vol.%), and the cooling rate should be slow enough for the groove to form but fast enough to avoid network distortion.
