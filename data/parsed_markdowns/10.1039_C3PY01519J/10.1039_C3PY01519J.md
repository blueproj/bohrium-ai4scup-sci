# Conjugated polymers based on benz odi thi oph ene and fluorinated  quinoxaline for bulk heterojunction solar cells: thiophene  versus  thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene as π-conjugated spacers  

Desta Gedefaw,  Marta Tessarolo,  Wenliu Zhuang,  Renee Kroon,  Ergang Wang,  Margherita  Bolognesi,  Mirko Seri,\* d  Michele Muccini,  Mats R. Andersson\* a  

Received (in XXX, XXX) XthXXXXXXXXX 20XX, Accepted Xth XXXXXXXXX 20XX  DOI: 10.1039/b000000x  

Two conjugated donor–acceptor copolymers based on a benz odi thi oph ene donor unit and a fluorinated  quinoxaline acceptor unit, spaced with either thiophene or thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene π-bridges, were  10  designed and synthesized. The effect of different π-bridges and of the processing conditions on the  optical, electrical, morphological and photovoltaic properties of the polymer:fullerene blend films were  investigated. The polymer containing the thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene π-bridge ( PBDTFQ-TT ) showed a red- shifted absorption and enhanced charge carrier mobility, as compared to its analogue with the thiophene  π-bridge ( PBDTFQ-T ), due to its narrower optical gap (by \~ 0.1 eV) and stronger inter-chain  15  interactions, favored by the structural planarity and increased linearity of the polymer backbone, as also  supported by DFT calculations. The blend of  PBDTFQ-TT  and PC 61 BM ([6,6]-phenyl-C 61 -butyric acid  methyl ester), compared to the  PBDTFQ-T :PC 61 BM one processed in the same conditions (by blade- coating technique), showed greatly enhanced photovoltaic performance, with more than doubled power  conversion efficiency (PCE up to 5.60% for the best device) due to the increased short-circuit current  20  density and fill factor. However, similar PCEs were also achieved for  PBDTFQ-T :PC 61 BM-based  devices by optimizing the processing conditions through the addition of 1,8-diiodooctane (DIO) as the  solvent additive. Through morphological and electrical analysis of the films, produced with and without  additive, it was observed that the addition of DIO greatly enhances the self-organization, and  consequently the charge mobility, of the thiophene π-bridge-based polymer, while it was detrimental for  25  the nanoscale morphology and photovoltaic performances of the thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene π-bridge-based  polymer in the corresponding blend.  

# 1. Introduction  

Organic photovoltaics (OPV) are a promising technology for  30  manufacturing large-area, lightweight and flexible solar cells  through low-cost and low-temperature solution processing  techniques.  Conjugated polymers and fullerene derivatives are  the typical constituents of the active layer in so-called bulk  heterojunction (BHJ) organic solar cells, in which these two  35  materials, acting as an electron donor and an electron acceptor  respectively, are deposited as a finely intermixed blend film  sandwiched between two electrodes.  Rapidly increasing power  3 conversion efficiencies (PCEs), as high as 8.5%  for conventional  4 single-junction solar cells and 9.35%  for inverted structure ones,  40  have been achieved through the development of new materials, as  well as through device processing and architecture optimization. In particular, the properties of the polymeric photoactive  materials are one of the most determining factors on the overall  performance of the final solar cells.  An ideal polymeric electron  45  donor should present a broad absorption spectrum with a high  absorption coefficient,  a good hole mobility,  suitable energies  9 of the frontier molecular orbitals matching those of the acceptor and appropriate film forming and nano-structuring ability once  blended with the acceptor, to form a photoactive layer with nano- 50  segregated and bicontinuous inter penetrating donor:acceptor  (D:A) domains. A well-known strategy to obtain efficient donor  polymers is to alternate electron-rich and electron-deficient  conjugated moieties in the backbone.  In these push–pull  architectures, both the donor and acceptor units as well as the  55  conjugated π-bridging groups, if used, inserted between them as  spacers, play a fundamental role. Nevertheless, while much effort  has been devoted to the study of new donor and acceptor building  blocks, only recently increased attention has been given to the π- bridges.  Indeed, conjugated π-bridges strongly affect the  60  electronic structure of push–pull polymers, and consequently  their optical and electrical properties, for instance by: (i)  contributing to the π-electron density; (ii) mediating the  

electronic interaction between the push and pull moieties, thus  affecting the spatial distribution of the molecular orbitals over the  polymer backbone. Moreover, π-bridges also affect the structural  conformation of the polymer chains, by introducing rigidity and  5  planarity or torsion angles on the backbone and by favoring or  hindering inter-chain interactions. In push–pull polymers, π- conjugated spacers range from vinylenes,  ethynylenes,  to  aromatic  rings  such  as  thiophenes,   furans   and  selenophenes.  In addition, the introduction of fused aromatic  10  units, such as thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene,  has recently attracted  much interest. In fact, the centrosymmetric, rigid and coplanar  structure of thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene ensures a high delocalization  of the π-electrons over the polymer backbone and also favors  intermolecular π–π stacking, resulting in red-shifted absorption,  15  reduced band gap and enhanced charge mobility.  For these  reasons, this π-conjugated bridge has been successfully applied as  a spacer in push–pull polymers for organic field-effect transistors  (OFET).  In an earlier report, an OFET hole mobility  improvement by one or two orders of magnitude was found when  20  the thiophene bridge was replaced by thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene.   Thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene has also been applied as a π-bridging  group in push–pull semiconducting polymers for photovoltaic  17 applications, such as in carbazole–b enzo thi a diaz ole  and  benz odi thi oph ene–b enzo thi a diaz ole  polymer systems, and  25  comparative studies with analogous polymers containing a single  thiophene ring as spacer have been reported. It has to be noted  that in all these cases the thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene-based oligomers  or polymers showed decreased solubility at room temperature.  However, they exhibited red-shifted light absorption spectra and  30  higher charge carrier mobility (by two orders of magnitude)  compared to the analogous thiophene containing polymers, due to  the band gap narrowing (by \~ 0.1 eV) and stronger intermolecular  interactions favored by the structural planarity and linearity of the  polymer backbone.  35  The easy method of getting high-performing semiconducting  polymers by changing the π-bridging group between the donor  and acceptor moieties prompted us to apply it to the class of  efficient push–pull polymers based on benz odi thi oph ene (4,8- bis(3-butylnonyl)benzo[1,2- b :4,5- b' ]dithiophene, or BDT) and  40  fluorinated  quinoxaline  (6,7-difluoro-2,3-bis(3- (octyloxy)phenyl)quinoxaline, or FQ) units. Herein, we discuss  the synthesis, optical, electrical and OPV properties of two  polymers ( PBDTFQ-T  and  PBDTFQ-TT , Figure 1) having  either thiophene (T) or thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene (TT) groups as π- 45  bridging units inserted between BDT and FQ, respectively. We  also use theoretical calculations to investigate the effect of these  two different π-conjugated spacers on the electronic structure,  chain conformation and related properties of the two polymers. In  this work, differently from other studies on thiophene or  50  thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene spaced polymers, these two polymers  have both identical donor and acceptor building blocks, including  side chains. This allows us to compare the properties of the two  polymers on the basis of the different π–spacer nature. An  unavoidable drawback of this choice is the big difference in the  55  solubility of the two corresponding monomers and of  oligomers/polymers during polymerization, this affecting the  relative molecular masses of the final products. The two polymers  were employed as donor materials in BHJ OPV devices together  

with PC 61 BM ([6,6]-phenyl-C 61 -butyric acid methyl ester) as the  60  acceptor. The polymer with the thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene π-bridge  View Article Online ( PBDTFQ-TT ) showed higher efficiency compared to the  DOI: 10.1039/C3PY01519J thiophene-based analogue ( PBDTFQ-T ) in devices prepared in  the same processing conditions, in accordance with the results  found in literature for similar systems.  However, we found  65  that through an appropriate optimization of the active layer  processing conditions, such as the use of a solvent additive  (DIO), similarly high efficiencies were obtained for  PBDTFQ-T  as well.  

In addition, it is worth emphasizing that all OPV devices  70  investigated in this work were fabricated  via  doctor-blading in  air, which represents an important step for the up-scaling of the  OPV devices production to large areas, with minimized material  losses and low-cost processes.

# 2.2 OPV device fabrication and characterization  

Patterned ITO-coated glasses (Rs \~ 10 Ω/□) were cleaned in  110  sequential sonicating baths (for 15 min) of deionized water,  acetone and isopropanol. After the final sonication step,  substrates were dried with a stream of N 2  gas and then placed in  an oxygen plasma chamber for 10 min. Next, a thin layer (\~ 30  nm) of PEDOT:PSS (Clevios PVP Al 4083) was spun-cast on the  

ITO surface and subsequently annealed at 150 °C for 15 min. The  active layers were prepared using the blade-coating technique.  For both polymers the doctor blade conditions used for the best  devices were: a volume of hot solution of 40 µL, hotplate at 60  5  °C, blade height of 100 µm and speed of 12.5 mm/s. For the  devices, a polymer concentration of 15 mg/mL and 10 mg/mL in  ODCB was used for  PBDTFQ-TT  and  PBDTFQ-T  respectively.  The mixture solutions were stirred overnight at 80 °C. To  complete the device fabrication, LiF and Al (0.6 nm and 100 nm)  −6 10  were deposited sequentially without breaking vacuum (\~3 × 10 Torr) using a thermal evaporator directly connected to the glove  box. The current–voltage ( I – V ) characteristics of all OPV devices  were recorded by a Keithley 236 source-measure unit under  2 simulated AM1.5G illumination of 100 mW/cm  (Abet  15  Technologies Sun 2000 Solar Simulator). The light intensity was  determined by a calibrated silicon solar cell fitted with a KG5  color glass filter to bring spectral mismatch to unity. The active  2 area of the solar cell was exactly 6 mm . During testing, each cell  was carefully masked, by calibrated mask, to prevent an excess  20  photocurrent generated from the parasitic device regions outside  the overlapped electrodes area. All solar cells were tested inside  the glove box in oxygen and moisture free environment.  

External Quantum Efficiency (EQE) was measured with a home- built system on encapsulated devices: monochromatic light was  25  obtained with a Xenon arc lamp from Lot-Oriel (300 Watt power)  coupled with a Spectra-Pro monochromator. The photocurrent  produced by the device passed through a calibrated resistance (50  Ω) and the voltage drop signal was collected through the  resistance with a Lock-In Digital Amplifier-SR830. Signal was  30  pulsed by means of an optical chopper (\~ 300 Hz frequency). A  calibrated silicon photodiode was used as reference.  

# 2.3 Thin-film characterization  

All thin-film characterizations were performed in air. Solution  and thin film optical absorption spectra were recorded on a  35  JASCO V-550 spectrophotometer. The thickness of the various  films was measured by a profilometer (KLA Tencor, P-6). All  active films were characterized on glass/ITO/PEDOT:PSS  substrates and subsequently corrected for the background signal,  in order to reproduce the effective condition and the real  40  nanomorphology of the active layer. Atomic Force Microscopy  (AFM) images were taken with a Solver Pro (NT-934 MDT)  scanning probe microscope in tapping mode. The AFM images  were recorded directly on the tested devices.  

The hole mobility of the photosensitive layers was measured by  45  the space charge limited current (SCLC) method using devices  with structure of ITO/PEDOT:PSS/polymer:PC 61 BM/Au. The  processing conditions used for the active layers were the  optimized ones. Charge mobility was extracted by ﬁtting the  current density–voltage curves, recorded in dark conditions, with  50  the Mott–Gurney equation.

# 3.2 Electrochemical and optical properties  

Square-wave voltammetry (SWV) was employed to examine the  electrochemical properties and estimate the HOMO and LUMO  energy levels of the polymers respectively from the oxidation and  reduction peak values (Table 1 and Fig. 2). The HOMO and  LUMO energy levels of  PBDTFQ-T  were deduced to be −6.00  and −3.63 eV, respectively, while those of  PBDTFQ-TT  −5.90  and −3.52 eV, respectively. Interestingly, both polymers exhibit  deep HOMO energy levels, which would result in devices with a  high  V OC , according to the difference LUMO ACCEPTOR  −  

HOMO DONOR .  Moreover, the measured HOMO energies are in  an ideal range to ensure a good air stability.

Considering that the LUMO level of the typical electron  acceptors PC 61 BM and PC 71 BM are located at −4.03 and −4.13  eV respectively,  the offset between the LUMO of the donor  polymer  and  that  of  the  molecular  acceptor  (LUMO DONOR −LUMO ACCEPTOR ), ranging from 0.4 to 0.6 eV,  provides a driving force to ensure an efficient exciton  dissociation.  

The slightly higher lying HOMO energy of  PBDTFQ-TT   50  compared to  PBDTFQ-T  (by \~ 0.10 eV) is in agreement with the  other cases of thiophene and thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene bridged  donor–acceptor polymers reported in literature. 5  The UV–vis absorption spectra of both  PBDTFQ-T  and  PBDTFQ-TT  (Fig.  3 ) show the same features: a higher-energy  absorption band between 380–480 nm, which can be assigned to  localized π–π\* transitions, and a lower-energy band between  500–700 nm, arising from the intramolecular charge-transfer-like  10  interactions between the electron-rich BDT and electron-poor  11e quinoxaline units  and from the intermolecular interactions  between the polymer chains. The difference in the position of the  absorption bands registered can be ascribed to the different  structures of the π-bridges in the polymers. Indeed,  PBDTFQ-TT 15  shows a broader absorption band with a red-shifted onset  compared to  PBDTFQ-T  (Table 1). This could be due to the  more linear and planar geometry of the backbone of  PBDTFQ- TT , favoring the overlap between the π-orbitals along the  30 polymeric chain and extending the conjugation length  as also  20  confirmed by theoretical studies (reported below). Moreover,  with the enhanced backbone planarity of  PBDTFQ-TT , the  interchain packing, and hence red-shifted absorption onset, is  opt promoted in the solid state. Indeed, the resulting optical gap ( E g of  PBDTFQ-TT  is slightly lower (1.71 eV) compared to  25  PBDTFQ-T  (1.77 eV), as also confirmed by DFT calculations. It  is worth mentioning that the optical gaps of these two polymers  are smaller than their respective electrochemical band gaps (ca.  2.4 eV), in agreement with the literature.  This can be due to  the fact that for conjugated polymers, the electron and hole  30  remain electrostatic ally bound to one another in the excited state  after light absorption (contrary to the ionized state in the  electrochemical measurements).  Also polymer interchain  interactions can generally result in a reduction in optical gaps in  the solid state absorption, as compared to electrochemical band  35  gaps.  In addition, the interface barrier between the polymer  film and the electrode surface can further cause a difference,  which makes the electrochemical band gaps even larger.  

For polymer  PBDTFQ-T , a red shift of the onset (by \~ 40 nm)  and of the absorption maxima (by \~ 12 nm) when passing from  40  solution to thin film is observed, indicating that π–π  

intermolecular interactions occur in the solid state. In addition,  PBDTFQ-T  displays vibronic features both in solution and in  thin film, implying the presence of ordered π–π stacks likely due  to polymeric chains entanglements, difficult to dissolve even in  45  diluted solutions owing to the high molecular mass of the  polymer.  In comparison,  PBDTFQ-TT  shows a smaller red  shift of the absorption onset (by \~ 10 nm), when passing from  solution to film. This could be due to: (i) the lower molecular  mass of  PBDTFQ-TT , compared to  PBDTFQ-T,  which renders  50  easier the polymer solubilization and chains disentanglement s; (ii)  the planar and linear geometry of the thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene π- spacer, compared to thiophene, which imparts a more linear  conformation to the polymer.  

# 3.3 Theoretical calculations  

55  Quantum-chemical calculations by the density functional theory  34 (DFT) at the B3LYP/6-31G(d) level  using the Gaussian 09  35 program  were employed to analyze the electronic structure of  these polymers. For computational simplification, the alkoxy side  chains on the quinoxaline acceptor unit were replaced with  60  methoxy groups, the branched alkyl side chains on the BDT unit  were replaced with methyl groups, and the backbones were  simplified to two repeating units. All the optimized molecular  structures are in a stable local minimum of the ground state  potential energy surface, and analytical second derivatives of the  65  Hessian matrix were calculated at the same level of theory to  ensure this.  

![](images/75ecf11dde1023934bbb68c6c25d203e060aaa40f7b17f2efcfeb14de3c000d5.jpg)  
Fig. 4  Possible conformers of π-bridges flanking FQ (π-A-π) segments.  

In order to obtain more accurate results, we first needed to  70  determine the conformations of the structure units of the two  polymers. As the BDT unit is a centrosymmetric fused unit, we  assume a  trans  conformation between the BDT unit and the  bridging group (i.e. thiophene or thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene), to  minimize the steric hindrance as well as the potential energy of  75  the whole systems.  More attention was given to resolve the  conformation between the quinoxaline unit and the bridging  group. Notably, even if different conformations (i.e.  syn - conformation defects) may be adopted between the BDT unit and  the bridging group, they still result in the same conclusions which  

can be made in our below computational analysis to resolve the  conformation between the quinoxaline unit and the bridging  group (i.e. thiophene or thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene). In the case of  thiophene as a π-bridge, as shown in Fig. 4, there are three  5  possible conformers to be resolved. We performed DFT  calculations for these possible conformations by using the two- repeating-unit model, because there are two joints in the π-bridge  flanking FQ (π-A-π) segment for the BDT units to bond with. The  optimized geometries obtained are depicted in Fig. S21 (ESI).  10  The potential energies of the three conformers of the π-A-π  segments decrease in the order: 121>112>111 for both the  thiophene and the thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene spacers. However, in  the optimized geometries of the corresponding dimers (Fig. S21,  ESI), additional factors could determine the lower conformation  15  energy, other than that of the π-A-π segment, such as the presence  of bulky side chains. For example, the lowering of the potential  energy through the minimization of the steric hindrance between  the bulky side chains on the BDT unit and on the FQ one may  prevail. In fact, the distance between the benzyl carbon on the  20  BDT unit and the closest oxygen atom on the adjacent  quinoxaline units,  L 2 , is 4.0 Å for the dimer with π-A-π  conformations 111 or 112, while it is 9.5 Å for the dimer with the  π-A-π conformation 121.  

We therefore postulate that, for polymer  PBDTFQ-T , the  25  conformation 121 of the π-A-π segment is favored because it  allows a minimum steric hindrance in the presence of adjacent  bulky side chains. Such conformation preference may already  occur in the polymerization of  PBDTFQ-T .  

On the other hand, in the case of the dimers with thieno[3,2- 30  b ]thiophene bridge, the steric hindrance of lateral alkyl chains is  strongly reduced: the distance between the benzyl carbon atom on  the BDT unit and the closest oxygen atom on the adjacent  quinoxaline units,  L 2 , is in any case comparable (9.4 Å). As a  consequence, thanks to the rigidity and centrosymmetry of the  35  thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene unit, the three possible conformations of  the dimers are all linear and have similar spatial orientation of the  side chains. Then for polymer  PBDTFQ-TT  is favored the  conformation where the π-A-π unit has the lowest potential  energy, which is conformation 111. It has to be mentioned that  40  for  PBDTFQ-TT , other possible conformations between the  quinoxaline unit and the π-bridge may exist, as well as between  the BDT unit and the π-bridge, but the polymer backbone  

linearity would be maintained. The linear backbone of  PBDTFQ- TT , compared to the curved backbone of  PBDTFQ-T ,  View Article Online 45  presumably favors inter-chain stacking/packing and leads to  DOI: 10.1039/C3PY01519J improved charge mobility.  

The optimized molecular geometries of the two-repeating-unit  models ( PBDTFQ-T_121  and  PBDTFQ-TT_111 ) and their  frontier molecular orbitals are shown in Fig. S22 (ESI). # 3.4 Photovoltaic performances  

To investigate the effects of  π -conjugated bridges on the  photovoltaic properties of  PBDTFQ-T  and  PBDTFQ-TT , they  10  have been employed as donor (D) materials in BHJ solar cells,  with PC 61 BM as acceptor (A) counterpart, in the conventional  device configuration glass/ITO/PEDOT:PSS/Active Layer/LiF/Al.  Further details for the device fabrication and characterization are  given in the experimental section. The photovoltaic responses  15  including  V OC ,  J SC , FF, and PCE of optimized devices are  summarized in Table 3 while representative current density– voltage ( J – V ) plots, measured under standard illumination  −2 (AM1.5G, 100 mW cm ), are shown in Fig. 5.  

The optimal D:A ratio was 1:1 (w/w) for both materials: upon  20  increasing or decreasing it lower OPV responses were observed  (Table S1, ESI). By comparing the performances of devices  

prepared in the same conditions, the one based on  PBDTFQ- T :PC 61 BM (1:1 w/w) showed a low PCE (2.18%), mainly due to  2 a low  J SC   (−5.46 mA/cm ) and FF (46%) (Table 3). On the other  25  hand, the device based on  PBDTFQ-TT :PC 61 BM (1:1 w/w)  showed more than doubled PCE, as high as 5.29%, (5.60% for  2 the best device), with  V OC  = 0.82 V,  J SC  = −10.87 mA/cm  and FF  = 59%. The doubled  J SC  is explicable through the effect of the  thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene spacer in enhancing the polymer long- 30  wavelength range absorption and charge mobility (see hole  mobility measurements). Moreover, the improvement in FF could  be due to the formation of an optimal blend film morphology,   facilitated by the presence of the thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene which  favors a linear polymer backbone and promotes inter-chain  35  packing, as confirmed by DFT calculations. In order to optimize  the photovoltaic performances of  PBDTFQ-T  through the  optimization of its blend nanomorphology, a 3% (v/v) of 1,8- diiodooctane (DIO) was added to the active layer solution.  As a  result, the  J SC  of the  PBDTFQ-T :PC 61 BM-based device was  2 40  almost doubled (−9.18 mA/cm ) and FF grew up to 67%,  resulting in a PCE of 5.30% (5.68% for the best device).  Presumably, the presence of a solvent additive (i.e. DIO), with  selective solubility of the fullerene,  in the  PBDTFQ-T :PC 61 BM  blend promotes the molecular re-organization by controlling the  45  kinetics of segregation and crystallization of the BHJ components  during the drying process, leading to an overall blend film  morphology with optimal phases intermixing, separation and  continuity.  On the contrary, the addition of 3% of DIO to the  PBDTFQ-TT :PC 61 BM-based blend led to a drastic drop in PCE  50  (3.19%), due to a decrease in  V OC  (0.75 V), FF (45%) and  J SC   2 (−9.53 mA/cm ). This means that an optimal nanomorphology is  obtained without the need of additive in the case of  PBDTFQ-TT thanks to the presence of the thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene π-bridge.  Note that the promising PCEs reached by these polymers in  

combination with the low-absorbing PC 61 BM acceptor allow their  possible application as bottom active layer materials in tandem  devices.  In fact, their partial spectral coverage would enable  some light to pass towards the top active layer which is typically  5  composed of a polymer:PC 71 BM blend.  

Different annealing temperatures and times were experimented on  the polymer:PC 61 BM-based active layers in order to further  enhance the devices performances, but no improvement was  achieved (see ESI). In addition, both polymers were tested in  10  solar cells with PC 71 BM (instead of PC 61 BM) as acceptor, in  order to increase the absorption spectral coverage of the active  layer. Nevertheless, the devices prepared by using the optimized  processing conditions found for PC 61 BM-based cells showed  inferior photovoltaic performances (see Table S1 in ESI).

# 3.5 Thin-film morphology and charge carrier mobility  

To get insight into the influence of the π-bridges and of the  45  processing conditions on the electrical properties of the two  polymers and corresponding blends, the hole mobility ( µ h ) of the  polymer:fullerene films in hole-only devices was extrapolated  through the space charge limited current (SCLC) method. The  charge mobility of the two polymers blended with PC 61 BM and  50  processed in the same conditions (DCB without DIO) are quite  −4  2 −4  2 different: 1.1×10 cm /(V·s) and 3.7×10 cm /(V·s) for  PBDTFQ-T  and  PBDTFQ-TT  respectively. This reflects the  inferior (halved) photovoltaic performance, in particular the  lower  J SC , of the  PBDTFQ-T -based blend compared to the  55  PBDTFQ-TT -based one, in these experimental conditions.  

As expected, the  PBDTFQ-T :PC 61 BM film processed with DIO  −4  2 gives an increase in hole mobility up to 3.5×10 cm /V·s,  reaching almost the same value as the optimized  PBDTFQ-TT - based blend and following the same trend observed for the  60  corresponding photovoltaic performances.

In  order to get a deeper insight on the effects of the conjugated  70  spacer and of the additive on the morphology of the two  polymers-based blend films, tapping-mode AFM images of the  active layers of optimized cells, prepared with and without DIO

By comparing the AFM topography of  PBDTFQ-T :PC 61 BM and  PBDTFQ-TT :PC 61 BM blend films prepared without DIO, it can  be noted that the latter presents a more structured topography  5  with a slightly higher roughness (RMS = 2.2 nm) compared to the  former film, which in turn shows finer and regular unconnected  grains, or small circular islands, accompanied by a lower  roughness (RMS = 1.3 nm) (Fig. 7a and b, respectively). The addition of DIO to the  PBDTFQ-T :PC 61 BM-based blend  leads to an improved morphology by generating more organized  nanostructures and promoting an optimal D:A intermixing,  15  compared to the pristine  PBDTFQ-T :PC 61 BM-based blend film,  indicating that the use of the additive promotes both the phase  separation between the BHJ components and also the formation  of more ordered and aggregated polymeric domains, as discussed  above. This favors charge separation and transport in the  20  corresponding  device,  in  agreement  with  the  doubled  photocurrent (Table 3) and increased hole mobility.  

Diversely, the addition of the additive (DIO) to the  PBDTFQ- TT :PC 61 BM-based blend film leads to the formation of evident  aggregates which suggest a probable higher phase separation  25  between polymer and fullerene. This is likely due to the stronger  tendency to intermolecular π-stacking of  PBDTFQ-TT  compared  to  PBDTFQ-T . This kind of morphology is expected to result in  inefficient exciton separation and lacks the bicontinuous  inter penetrating pathways necessary for the charge transport, in  30  agreement with the relatively low OPV performance.  

# 4. Conclusions  

In conclusion, we have synthesized two easily accessible  conjugated polymers ( PBDTFQ-T  and  PBDTFQ-TT ) consisting  of benz odi thi oph ene electron-rich and fluorinated quinoxaline  35  electron-poor units spaced with either thiophene or thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene π-bridges, respectively. The presence of thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene as π-bridge makes the corresponding polymer  backbone more planar and linear compared to the analogue  polymer with the thiophene π-bridge. This leads  PBDTFQ-TT  to  40  have a lower band gap, a red-shifted absorption and a higher  tendency to form π-aggregates compared to  PBDTFQ-T . On the  other hand, thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene imparts less solubility to the  corresponding monomer and oligomers, thereby limiting  significantly the maximum molar mass obtainable for the  45  resulting polymer, as compared to the analogous polymer with  thiophene as π-bridge. A first comparison between the electrical  and photovoltaic properties of the two synthesized polymers,  blended with PC 61 BM as acceptor and processed in the same  experimental conditions with doctor-blading (without using  50  solvent additives), reveals an improved performance of the  polymer with the thieno[3,2- b ]thiopheneas π-bridge (with a PCE  of 5.29% on average and 5.60% at maximum) compared to the  thiophene π-bridged analogue (with an average PCE of 2.18%).  This can be ascribed to enhanced light absorption, charge  55  mobility and improved blend film nanomorphology, despite the  lower molar mass. Nevertheless, by tuning the processing  conditions through the addition of a solvent additive (DIO) to the  

PBDTFQ-T  based blend, it was possible to improve the blend  film nanostructuring, charge transport properties and light  View Article Online 60  absorption, and finally obtain comparable efficiencies (average  PCE around 5.30% and 5.68% for the best device) for the devices  prepared with the two polymers. However, the facile method for  obtaining highly performing polymers by simply varying the π- spacer in donor–acceptor structures and obtaining efficient BHJ  65  solar cells without the need of any additional processing solvent  or molecule suggests the importance of a further and deeper  investigation on the use of thieno[3,2- b ]thiophene or similar π- bridges inserted in other polymeric structures with different donor  and acceptor units.
