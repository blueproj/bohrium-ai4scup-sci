# 9 Abstract  

The lattice misﬁt and diﬀraction elastic constants in hot rolled polycrystalline Co-7Al-5W-2Ta and Co-6Al-6W-2Ti (at. %) are measured using neutron and synchrotron X-ray diﬀraction. The misﬁt in the two alloys was found to be +0.67 and +0.59 %, respectively, using neutron diﬀraction at HRPD. The misﬁt was found to increase with temperature, as in Ni superalloys. This implies that the amount of coherency strengthening increases with temperature. The diﬀraction elastic constants measured show that the  γ ′   phase is less stiﬀthan the  γ  matrix in all orientations, which means that load shedding will occur to the  γ  phase.

# 12 1. Introduction  

13 Ni base superalloys play an important role in the modern aircraft engine, 14 comprising approximately 40-50 % of its weight [1]. The principal strengthen- 15 ing mechanisms are dependent on the presence of coherent L1 2 - γ ′   precipitates 16 (Ni 3 Al), in an FCC solid solution, A1- γ  matrix. Ni base superalloys are chosen 17 for the hottest sections of gas turbines, due to the  γ ′   precipitates showing an 18 anomalous increase strength with increasing temperature. Therefore, the alloys 19 exhibit superior creep and fatigue properties at elevated temperatures [2, 3, 4]. 20 The increase in the number of aircraft worldwide and high oil prices make 21 fuel economy the biggest challenge for the aircraft industry [5]. Increases in 22 temperature allow for improvements in cycle eﬃciency, and this design criterion 23 is a potent driver for the development of new alloy systems to replace Ni base 24 superalloys. These alloys are now quite mature, with successive generations 25 showing limited improvement in temperature capability.  

26 In 2006, Sato et al. [6] discovered a new L1 2  γ ′   phase, Co 3 (Al,W), in the 27 Co-Al-W ternary system which coexists with the FCC Co matrix. The lattice 28 misﬁt between the  γ  and  γ ′   phases is 0.53 %, which allows cuboidal Co 3 (Al,W)  

29 phase precipitates to exist in the  γ  matrix with a high degree of coherency. The 30 microstructure of these Co-Al-W base alloys is therefore quite similar to Ni base 31 superalloys. 32 Co-Al-W base alloys exhibit the ﬂow stress anomaly [7, 8, 9, 10], with limited 33 or no decrease in the ﬂow stress at temperatures up to 600   ◦ C . The addition 34 of elements such as Ti, Ta and Ir to the base alloy increases the  γ ′   solvus tem- 35 perature and improves strength. Moreover, the addition of boron has a remark- 36 able eﬀect by providing grain boundary strengthening. Further investigation 37 [11, 12, 13, 14] showed that Co 3 (Al,W) is metastable at 1000   ◦ C , decomposing 38 into either  γ , CoAl and Co 7 W 6  or  γ , Co 7 W 6  and Co 3 W. It was also shown 39 that for long aging time at 900   ◦ C , discontinuously coarsened lamellar colonies 40 (regions of lamellar orientation) form at the  γ  grain boundaries. Four phases 41 were found in the colonies:  γ ,  γ ′ , CoAl and Co 3 W. 42 Knowledge of the elastic properties of both  γ  and  γ ′   phases is critical for a 43 fundamental understanding of the mechanical behaviour of the Co-Al-W base 44 alloys. Lattice misﬁt ( δ ) is another very important microstructural parame- 45 ter that determines the mechanical properties in the superalloys, deﬁned as 46 δ  = 2( a γ ′  − a γ ) / ( a γ  +  a γ ′ ) [2]. Positive lattice misﬁt has been observed at both 47 room temperature and elevated temperature during high-energy X-ray diﬀrac- 48 tion experiments [6, 15]. Positive misﬁt would be expected to lead to directional 49 coarsening (rafting) parallel to the applied tensile stress [16]. Microstructural 50 stability is favoured by small lattice misﬁt, i.e. low surface energies that pro- 51 vide the driving force for coarsening. The magnitude of the lattice misﬁt varies 52 strongly with temperature in Ni base superalloys [17, 18]. The reliable pre- 53 diction of these properties in multicomponent alloys is diﬃcult and, given the 54 importance of lattice misﬁt in determining mechanical properties, it is necessary 55 to study this dependence over a range of temperatures up to the  γ ′   solvus in 56 the Co-Al-W system. 57 It is also valuable to understand the load partitioning between the  γ  and 58 γ ′   phases that arises due to stiﬀness diﬀerences. Load partitioning will aﬀect 59 the stresses that evolve in the two phases during creep, both during particle 60 shearing and non-shearing regimes. It will also aﬀect the driving force for rafting 61 in the very high temperature creep regime. Recently, both experimental and 62 theoretical calculations to determine the moduli of the two phases at cryogenic 63 temperatures have been performed [20, 21, 22, 23]. Density functional theory 64 calculations suggest that the shear modulus  G  (at 0 K) of the  γ ′   is rather large, 65 which would increase the resistance to dislocation motion and hence improve the 66 strength. Furthermore, the intrinsic ductility of the intermetallic is determined 67 by the ratio  G/B , where  B  is the bulk modulus. The calculated data suggest 68 that the  γ ′   phase behaves in a brittle manner. However, the experimental data 69 for a polycrystal at 5 K suggest that  G/B<  0 . 5, which would suggest that the 70 γ ′   is intrinsically ductile. It has been shown that in Ni base superalloys the 71 elastic moduli are temperature dependent. To the authors’ knowledge, no data 72 has been published about the  γ  and  γ ′   moduli for these Co-Al-W base alloys at 73 both room and elevated temperatures. 74 In this work, synchrotron and neutron diﬀraction measurements have been 75 performed to study the micromechanics of Co-Al-W-X quaternary alloys. The 76 variation of the lattice misﬁt values and the variation of diﬀraction elastic con- 77 stants (DECs) over a range of temperatures have been measured and are dis- 78 cussed. A comparison is made between the diﬀerent diﬀraction techniques.

110 Neutron diﬀraction experiments were conducted on the HRPD (High Res- 111 olution Powder Diﬀraction) beamline at the ISIS facility, Didcot, Oxon., UK. 112 The aim of this experiment was to determine the evolution of lattice misﬁt with 113 temperature. A schematic representation of the experimental setup is shown in 114 Figure 1a. The HRPD instrument is a high resolution, long ﬂight path, Time- 115 of-Flight (TOF) spallation neutron diﬀraction beamline with detector banks at 116 168 ◦ ,  ± 90 ◦ and 30 ◦ . The 168 ◦ bank is a backscattering detector and was em- 117 ployed in this study to obtain maximum  d -spacing resolution. Specimens 119 were produced from the second batch ( ∼ 6 mm thick) and machined into 40 mm 120 long and 3 mm wide strips by Electrical Discharge Machining (EDM). Several 121 pieces of each alloy were packed inside a neutron-transparent vanadium can and 122 placed in an infra-red vacuum furnace. Room temperature and elevated tem- 123 perature (with 100   ◦ C increments) neutron diﬀraction data were collected for 124 each alloy. Typical neutron count times were  ∼ 20 min for each measurement.  

# 125 2.4. SXRD experiment to determine lattice misﬁt and DECs  

126 SXRD measurements were made using the I12 beamline at the Diamond 127 synchrotron, Didcot, Oxon., UK. Tensile specimens with a gauge section of 19 128 ×  1.5  ×  2 mm were prepared from the batch 1 alloys using EDM with the 129 gauge length parallel to the rolling direction. The aim of this experiment was 130 to determine the lattice misﬁt and DECs at room temperature and elevated 131 temperatures. The experimental set-up is shown in Figure 1b. The dog-bone 132 specimens were tested using a Zwick-Roell 5 kN tensile test rig in the beamline. 133 An infrared furnace was mounted onto the load frame. Specimens were loaded 134 from 0 MPa to 270 MPa under displacement control at a strain rate of 10 − 4 s − 1 . 135 Room temperature diﬀraction data were recorded during loading with an expo- 136 sure time of 1 s. Following unloading, the temperature was ramped to 100   ◦ C, 137 isothermally held, with the loading and diﬀraction data measurement process 138 repeated. Following the 100   ◦ C measurement the temperature was increased in 139 50   ◦ C increments, with the same loading and data measurement procedure. The 140 process was performed until temperatures were reached just below the solvus 141 temperature of each alloy, Table 1. An 79 keV ( λ =0.15675  A), 500  ×  500  μ m 142 monochromated X-ray beam was used during the tensile loading and the diﬀrac- 143 tion data were recorded using a Thales Pixium 2D area detector, 1319 mm from 144 the specimen, in order to obtain full Debye-Scherrer diﬀraction rings. The rings 145 were integrated to provide intensity-2 θ  diﬀraction spectra using the Fit2D soft- 146 ware package [27].

247 3.4. Lattice misﬁt and its dependence on temperature  

248 Figure 6 shows the lattice misﬁt and its evolution with temperature for the 249 2Ta and 2Ti alloys in the unloaded condition, from the Rietveld-reﬁned neutron 250 HRPD data and the I12 data. It should be noted that the samples were from 251 diﬀerent alloy melts, Table 1. Both the HRPD and I12 show that the lattice 252 parameter for  γ ′   is always greater than that for  γ , that is, the misﬁt is positive 253 and increases with temperature. In contrast, commercial Ni base superalloys 254 such as CMSX-4 have a negative misﬁt of around  − 0 . 2 %, which also increases 255 with temperature, becoming nearly zero at service temperatures [34, 35, 18]. 256 In addition to the importance of the sign and magnitude of misﬁt for rafting 257 and coarsening, the misﬁt is also the determinant of the amount of coherency 258 strengthening. Commercial Ni base superalloys derive much of their strength 259 from anti-phase boundary strengthening; that is, the requirement for disloca- 260 tions shearing the  γ ′   to do so in groups, forming dislocation ribbons [36, 37]. 261 Because Ni base superalloys have been engineered to minimise coarsening and 262 hence misﬁt, the contribution from coherency strengthening is often quite low 263 at temperature. In contrast, superalloys exploiting the Co 3 (Al,W) phase should 264 suﬀer minimal coarsening because of the low solubility and diﬀusivity of W in

It shows that the 268 misﬁt increases from  ∼ 0.33 % at RT to 0.51 % at 750   ◦ C, indicating a diﬀerence 269 in thermal expansion coeﬃcient between the two phases of 2  ×  10 − 6   K − 1 . The 270 I12 data for the 2Ta alloy shows a much smaller thermal expansion coeﬃcient 271 diﬀerential between the two phases, of around 0 . 5 × 10 − 6   K − 1 . In addition, both 272 the neutron and X-ray results indicate that the 2Ta alloy has a higher misﬁt, 273 by 0 . 1–0 . 2 %. 274 This can be rationalised by considering the atomic radii of the constituent 275 elements. Metallic Al and W have very similar atomic radii, 1.43 and 1 . 37  A, 276 respectively. Co and Ni both have atomic radii of 1 . 25  A [38]. Appealing to 277 the Hume-Rothery rules [39], it is therefore expected both that Ni-Al solutions 278 would order to form Ni 3 Al and that Al and W could form a sublattice together 279 in the newly-discovered Co 3 (Al,W) phase. Ti has an atomic radius of 1 . 45  280 and Ta, 1 . 43  A. Therefore, is is expected that both Ti and Ta will increase 281 the  γ ′   lattice parameter and misﬁt and that both are strong  γ ′   formers. We 282 hypothesise that Ta segregates even more strongly to the  γ ′   phase than Ti, 283 which is consistent with its eﬀect on the  γ ′   fraction [40, 41], presumably because 284 of its higher valency, and this then provides a rationale for why Ta increases the 285 misﬁt more than Ti. 286 As the temperature increases beyond 700   ◦ C, the HRPD data suggests that 287 the misﬁt began to decrease in both alloys, and by a greater amount in the 2Ti 288 alloy than in 2Ta. This approximately coincides with the temperature at which 289 dissolution of the  γ ′   begins, which is presumably a related phenomenon. 290 Disappointingly, the two techniques give misﬁt values that are quite diﬀer- 291 ent, by around 0.15 % for the 2Ta alloy and over 0.2 % for the 2Ti alloy. It 292 should be noted that diﬀerent alloy batches were used and so some of the dis- 293 crepancy may be due to melt-to-melt variability. The HRPD experiment, (i)  

294 used a diﬀractometer with higher resolution which was good enough to clearly 295 distinguish the  γ  and  γ ′   peaks, and (ii) sampled more material in more orienta- 296 tions. Therefore, of the two data sets we believe that the HRPD data is to be 297 preferred. 298 For comparison, an FEI TITAN 80/300 FEGTEM was used to determine 299 the lattice misﬁt for the 2Ta alloy. Diﬀraction patterns were obtained from the 300 Fast Fourier Transform of lattice images obtained using high resolution TEM, 301 allowing a misﬁt of 1.2 % to be determined. Clearly this was quite diﬀerent to 302 both the diﬀraction measurements, and, given the likely elastic constants, would 303 imply an almost incredible level of misﬁt stress, even given that the interfaces 304 are diﬀuse in these alloys. It is possible that localised heating by the electron 305 beam was responsible for this discrepancy, which only serves to highlight how 306 diﬃcult it can be to accurately determine lattice misﬁt in nm-scale coherent 307 precipitates. 308 Pyczak et al. [42] have previously measured the misﬁt in Co-9Al-9W-0.1B 309 using a doublet ﬁt to the  { 002 }  peak, obtaining a misﬁt of +0 . 8 % at room 310 temperature, 0 . 35 % at 800   ◦ C and 0 . 1 % at 900   ◦ C. In contrast, Sato et al. [6] 311 measured the misﬁt in Co-9.2Al-9W to be 0 . 5 %. Shinagawa et al. # 374 4. Conclusions  

375 The misﬁt and diﬀraction elastic constants in rolled polycrystalline Co-7Al- 376 5W-2Ta and Co-6Al-6W-2Ti (at. %) have been measured using synchrotron X- 377 ray diﬀraction at I12, Diamond as well as by neutron diﬀraction at HRPD, ISIS 378 and Vulcan at SNS. It is found that, in these closely misﬁtting alloys, accurate 379 determination of the lattice parameters is assisted by a resolution that permits 380 the FCC peaks to be observed directly for each phase, rather than relying on 381 constraining the position of the FCC  γ ′   peak position from the corresponding 382 superlattice peak and then ﬁtting a closely-overlapped doublet. 383 Lattice misﬁt values for the -2Ta and -2Ti alloys were found to be around 384 +0.67 and +0.59 %, respectively, using HRPD. The misﬁt was found to rise 385 with temperature, as in Ni base superalloys. This implies that the amount of 386 coherency strengthening increases with temperature. The negatively misﬁtting 387 Ni base superalloys show the opposite behaviour. In Co base superalloys, which 388 are not expected to suﬀer from rapid coarsening due to the low diﬀusivity of W 389 in the matrix phase, this should be a desirable feature. 390 The measured diﬀraction elastic constants show that the  γ ′   phase is less stiﬀ 391 than the  γ  matrix in all orientations, which means that load shedding will occur 392 to the  γ  phase. This is undesired. 393 For the development of engineering diﬀractometers that perform loading 394 experiments in two phase materials where the two phases possess a close crys- 395 tallographic relationship, such as martensites, shape memory alloys, superalloys 396 and many other two phase metallic systems, it is recommended that improved 397 resolution be provided such that closely misﬁtting peaks can be resolved more 398 easily. In many cases such peaks correspond to the interface planes, which are 399 of fundamental interest. Therefore, improvements in instrument resolution are 400 of great importance to the study of advanced alloys.
