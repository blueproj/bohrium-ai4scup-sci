# Abstract  

Depth-profiling of a thermal barrier coating (TBC) system was carried out using femtosecond laser-induced breakdown spectroscopy (fs- LIBS). The TBC system consisted of an outer 7% yttria stabilized zirconia (7YSZ) ceramic coating and an inner Pt-modified bond coat on a Ni- based superalloy single crystal substrate. In the absence of the ceramic layer, it was possible to qualitatively differentiate between the bond coat and the substrate, and also between the two layers of the bond coat. The spatial location of the interface between the two bond coat layers could also be obtained accurately from the spectral profile of W. In presence of the ceramic coating, it was also possible to easily and accurately determine the ceramic/bond coat interface from the spectral profile of Al. Potential use of fs-LIBS in combination with fs laser machining to produce superior quality cooling holes on thermal barrier coated superalloy components is discussed.

# 1. Introduction  

Thermal barrier coatings (TBCs) are widely employed on Ni- based superalloy components of gas turbine engines for protection against extreme thermal, chemical and mechanical service conditions. A thermal barrier coating system consists of a ceramic outer layer, an underlying metallic bond coat and the Ni-based superalloy substrate. Zirconia partially stabilized by 7% yttria, often referred to as 7YSZ, has emerged as the most preferred ceramic coating material for TBC systems  [1] . The YSZ coating is typically deposited by electron beam physical vapor deposition (EB-PVD) technique. A platinum-modified aluminide, also referred to as  β -(Ni,Pt)Al, bond coat  [2,3]  is usually used in combination with the above 7YSZ coating  [1] . While the outer ceramic coating protects against the heat load, the bond coat enhances the oxidation resistance of the substrate alloy. For internal cooling of the hollow thermal barrier coated components, cooling holes that extend from the external surface to the internal surface, need to be machined on these components. Machining of cooling holes using Nd-YAG pulsed lasers has been an accepted process for aero-engine components over past few decades  [4] . Despite their widespread use, the holes produced by Nd-YAG lasers, which employ nanosecond (ns) and microsecond ( μ s) pulse widths, are often associated with deficiencies such as the formation of recast layer, high heat affected zone and micro-cracks  [4,5] . Drilling of holes on thermal barrier coated components additionally causes delami- nation of the ceramic layer  [5,6] .  

During the past decade, the development of chirped-pulse technology has led to the commercial availability of powerful and reliable femtosecond (fs) pulsed lasers. Due to the ultra-short pulse width of fs laser pulses, which is of the order of 100 fs or − 15   100×10 s, negligible heat diffusion to the surroundingmaterial occurs during the laser – material interaction and the material removal occurs mostly by direct ablation from solid to vapor  [7,8] . Using appropriate laser conditions, melting and the consequent formation of the recast layer in the material during machining can

Such advantages of fs lasers can be exploited in machining of superior quality cooling holes on thermal barrier coated components. Despite this potential, only a few studies have been carried out on fs laser machining of TBCs  [10] . Since the threshold fluence for − 2   ablation of the ceramic coating at 1.5 J cm is significantly higher − 2 than that for the bond coat/substrate (0.3 J cm )  [10,11] , a single laser fluence would not be ideal for making a through-thickness hole on a thermal barrier coated component. For example, a − 2   fluence as high as of 46 J cm has been used for machining of the 7YSZ coating without causing any appreciable melting  [10] . However, in case of metallic CMSX-4 superalloy, a much lower − 2   fluence of13J cm has been reported to cause significant melting [11] . Therefore, the fluence used for machining the ceramic YSZ coating needs to be reduced appropriately at the ceramic/bond coat interface for avoiding collateral damage such as excessive melting in the metallic bond coat/substrate. One of the methods to re- cognize the arrival of the interface during the drilling process is to use laser-induced breakdown spectroscopy (LIBS) in conjunction with laser machining, and continuously monitor for the emergence of the spectral signals of elements such as Al and Ni which constitute the bond coat. Once these signals are detected, which ideally should occur at ceramic/bond coat interface, the adjust- ment in the fluence can be carried out. Use of LIBS in combination with laser machining for detecting materials at various depths has been reported in several studies for both machining and bio- medical applications  [12 – 14] .  

Chemical characterization by LIBS typically involves the use of a pulsed laser to ablate a small amount of material from the sample surface. The resulting luminous plasma is then analyzed through optical emission spectroscopy to obtain the compositional information. This technique, which offers a quick method for chemical analysis, is relatively insensitive to sample size/surface conditions, and can beperformedin ambient environment. LIBS is also suitable for on-site/in situ analysis because it is straightfor- ward to transport the laser light to the work site through fiber-optic cables and similarly, fiber-optic delivery of the plasma emission can also be made to the spectrometer for analysis. Apart from surface analysis, LIBS has also been used for depth-profiling or depth-resolved analysis of materials, especially for coated systems [15 – 19] . During depth-profiling, multiple laser pulses are applied at a given location on the sample surface to generate a crater and the spectral signals from various depths are collected during the process by the LIBS set-up. By this method, it is possible to analyze the material ablated by a specific pulse or a set of pulses and correlate the inferred chemical signal to a specific depth in the sample  [18 – 21] . Typically, LIBS characterization is carried out using ns pulsed lasers  [15 – 21] . However, because of the advan- tages of fs lasers, as mentioned above, they are also being consi- dered for LIBS characterization of materials  [17,22,23] . Although depth-resolution in a coated system, i.e. the accuracy with which the spatial location of the coating/substrate interface can be deter- mined, remains a major issue for depth-profile analysis  [15,21] , LIBS has been used quite successfully for qualitative character- ization of layered components  [15 – 19] .  

The objective of the present study has been to use fs-LIBS for carrying out the depth-profile analysis of a thermal barrier coating system which consisted of a 7YSZ ceramic layer, a  β - (Ni,Pt)Al bond coat and a Ni-based superalloy substrate. A major goal was to examine the feasibility of qualitatively differentiate between the coating layers, and between the coating and the substrate based on the spectral signals collected

# 2. Experimental details  

The thermal barrier coated samples used in the present study for depth-profiling consisted of a 7YSZ ceramic outer layer, an MDC 150L  β -(Ni,Pt)Al bond coat and the Ni-based single crystal substrate CMSX-4. The nominal composition (wt.%) of the substrate alloywas 61.7Ni-5.6 Al-1.0 Ti-6.5 Ta-6.5 Cr-0.6Mo-6.0 W-9.0 Co-3.0 Re-0.1 Hf. Depth-profiling of bond coated samples, i.e. without the ceramic layer, was also carried out. The coated samples were provided by Howmet Research Corp., U.S.A. and they were studied in as-coated condition. While the bond coat was prepared through a combination of electroplating, high temperature diffusion and chemical vapor deposition processes [2] , the YSZ layer was deposited by the EB-PVD technique. ATi: sapphire pulsed laser (Clark MXR CPA2001) operating at a wavelength of 780 nm with a pulse duration of 150 fs and having a Gaussian energy distribution was used for material ablation. A pulse repetition rate of 125 Hz was used for probing the coatings and all the experiments were carried out in ambient air. The beam was focused normal to the sample surface using a plano-convex 2   BK-7 glass lens of focal length 17.5 cm. The 1/e spot radius of the beam was approximately 30  μ m. Plasma emission was collected using an achromatic doublet BK-7 glass lens (focal length: 2.5 cm) which focused the light onto a 100  μ m diameter optical fiber head connected to a Meschelle 5000 spectrograph (Andor Inc., U.S.A.). The dispersed light was detected by a intensified charged coupled device (ICCD) detector (iStar, Andor Inc., U.S.A.) which was attached to the spectrometer. The detector delay time (gate delay) and the integration time (gate width) were 500 ns and 800 ns, respectively. The schematic of the experimental set-up used in the present study is shown in  Fig. 1 . Plasma emission from ten equally spaced locations was collected and integrated for each data collection scan. The spacing between any two adjacent locations was 0.2 mm. Spectra were collected from the craters on every tenth laser pulse. Following the depth-profiling, separate craters were produced on the coating surface for various predetermined number of laser pulses. The depths for these craters were measured and used for generating calibration curves for depth vs. number of pulses.  

An optical microscope was used for measuring the depths of the above craters formed on the sample surface. For depths greater than about 50  μ m, cross-sections of the craters were observed in the optical microscope for estimation of the depth. For preparing the cross-sectional samples, care was taken to section the coating perpendicular to the coating surface. The sectioned surface was then metal lo graphically polished and the depth of the craters intermittently measured. The largest depth measured in the process for a given crater was assigned as its actual depth. The cross-sectional microstructure of the coating was also examined by a Hitachi S3200N scanning electron microscope (SEM) in back scattered electron (BSE) mode. The chemical composition across the coating was determined by using a Cameca SX100 electron probe micro-analyzer (EPMA).

While this manner of data presentation is straightforward and simple, it does not directly correlate the intensity variation with actual sampling depth. As evident from  Fig. 5 (b), the normalized intensity, unlike the absolute intensity, remains virtually unchanged over the crater depth. # 3.2.4. Detection of ceramic layer/bond coat interface  

As mentioned previously, the detection of the ceramic coating/bond coat interface is of primary importance for laser machining of thermal barrier coated components. Therefore, depth-profiling of a thermal barrier coated sample was carried out to examine whether the above interface could be detected accurately. The depth-profiling was carried out at a laser fluence − 2 of 37 J cm , which was nearly 25 times the single-pulse threshold fluence for ablation for 7YSZ material  [10]  and 123 times that of the bond coat/substrate alloy  [11] . After about the 75th pulse, a sharp rise in the intensity of the Al 396.15  line could be visually detected in the experimental spectra. This rise in intensity was due to the onset of ablation of Al from the bond coat as the crater reached the 7YSZ/bond coat interface. The spectroscopic information obtained can be presented in terms of a normalized intensity vs. depth plot, as shown in  Fig. 12 , where the abrupt intensity rise at an approximate depth of 120  μ m, the location of the YSZ/bond coat interface, is apparent. As additional pulses were applied and the crater entered further into the bond coat, the intensity of Al 396.15  line expectedly continued to remain at a high value, as evident from  Fig. 12 . The above experiment clearly indicates that it is possible to spectro- scopically identify the 7YSZ/bond coat interface by monitoring the spectral signal of Al during laser machining.  

# 4. Discussion  

Reported studies on the ns laser ablation of bulk materials suggest that an enhancement in plasma intensity occurs with the increase of cavity depth. Because of its long pulse width, a ns laser pulse remains on long after the initiation of the plasma, which typically occurs after about 10 ps  [32,33] . As a result, the laser pulse interacts with the plasma causing a significant rise in its temperature and intensity. This effect is accentuated when the plasma remains confined in a high aspect ratio crater leading to a further increase in temperature  [34 – 36] . Such increase of plasma temperature in a crater is said to be the reason for which an enhancement in plasma intensity with increasing cavity depth occurs in case of ns lasers. In case of fs laser ablation, however, the laser pulse does not heat the generated plasma because the pulse ends before the initiation of the plasma [32,33] . Therefore, the plasma generated by a fs pulse cools and decays extremely fast  [37] . It can be speculated that the decay of such a plasma would be progressively faster as the crater deepens during depth-profiling because of increasingly rapid loss of heat through the crater wall. This is the likely reason for the progressive drop in the spectral intensities with the increase in crater depth, as observed in case of uncoated CMSX-4 alloy ( Fig. 5 (a)). To overcome this, normalized intensity, which re- mained constant over the cavity depth ( Fig. 5 (b)), rather than simple intensity was used for analysis.  

In a conventional overlay coating, where the coating and the substrate remain as two distinctly separate chemical entities, an abrupt concentration change is usually observed across the coating/substrate interface. During depth-profiling by LIBS, such abrupt concentration change causes a corresponding sharp change in the spectral intensity profile which can be used to accurately determine the spatial location of the interface. In case of a diffusion coating such as the present  β -(Ni,Pt)Al bond coat, however, such sharp concentration variations across the inter- faces usually do not exist. The reason for this can be ascribed to the way these coatings are formed. For example, the formation of the  β -(Ni,Pt)Al coating involves extensive interdiffusion between the substrate elements and externally supplied Pt and Al at high temperatures, typically above 1050 °C  [2,3] . As a result, the coating and the substrate become an integral structure with the  γ – γ ' substrate transitioning into the  β -(Ni,Pt)Al matrix of the coating (see  Fig. 2 (b)). Therefore, the change in the chemical compositions for most of the elements such as Al, Ni, Co and Cr across the IDZ/substrate and  β  layer/IDZ interfaces is gradual rather than abrupt (see  Fig. 3 ). Only the concentration of W changes abruptly across the  β  layer/IDZ interface which enabled the accurate determination of the location of this inter- face in the coating from the spectroscopic information.  

In a typical conical or bell-shaped crater produced by a Gaussian laser beam  [28,34] , material ablation and the resultant plasma emission caused by any given laser pulse occur from a range of depths instead of a single depth, i.e. the bottom of the crater. Consequently, mixing of spectral signals from various depths occurs which makes it difficult to accurately correlate the detected signal intensity with the probed depth  [28] . In case of the bond coated sample, since the  β  layer does not contain any W, there would be no effect of signal mixing on the intensity of W 400.87  line as long as the crater remains confined within this layer. However, as the crater extends into the IDZ and subsequently into the substrate, both of which contain considerable amounts of W, the effect of mixing of signals would be significant. This probably is one of the major reasons for which the spatial position of the IDZ/substrate interface could not be determined accurately from the spectral intensity profile of W ( Fig. 6 ).  

As previously mentioned, interaction with laser pulses causes some degree of melting in the bond coated sample during depth- profiling ( Fig. 10 ). The molten metal produced after each pulse is pushed sideways under the pressure exerted by the generated plasma  [38] . The displaced liquid metal from the lower portion of the crater is likely to solidify on the relatively colder top regions of the sidewall. This serves as a mechanism of transport of elements such as W from IDZ and substrate to the crater wall adjoining the  β  layer. Additionally, material transfer through evaporation and subsequent deposition might also have occurred simultaneously. During depth-profiling, the re-ablation of the transferred material, which has a high concentration of W, by the subsequent pulses is expected to introduce additional signal mixing and, hence, inaccuracy in locating the IDZ/substrate interface. This supposition is well supported by the variation of spectral intensity for Wobtained across the coating cross-section ( Fig. 9 ), where the IDZ/substrate interface can be accurately located in the absence of the crater wall. With regard to the spectral intensity variation for Al with depth ( Fig. 11 ), it is affected by both the lack of sharp concentration gradient across the interfaces and the mixing of spectral signals from various depths of the crater. As a result, the position of neither interface in the coating could be determined from  Fig. 11 .  

Easy spectroscopic identification of the ceramic/bond coat interface, as observed in the depth-profiling study of the thermal barrier coated sample ( Fig. 12 ), indicates that LIBS can be potentially used in combination with fs laser machining of actual thermal barrier coated superalloy components. By changing the laser fluence appropriately on arrival of the interface during the machining process, it would be possible to achieve extremely clean and damage-free cooling holes on the coated components. It may be pointed out here that the sharp change in the intensity of the Al 396.15  line observed at the ceramic layer/bond coat interface ( Fig. 12 ) was because of the sharp composition change of Al across this interface from zero in the YSZ layer to about 40 at.% in the bond coat. In contrast, the absence of such sharp concentration changes of Al across the  β  layer/IDZ and IDZ/substrate interfaces in the bond coated sample led to the inability of identifying these interface from the spectral intensity profile of Al ( Fig. 11 ). This underlines the importance of chemically sharp interfaces in enhancing the depth-resolution in laser-induced breakdown spectroscopy.  

# 5. Conclusions  

In the present investigation, depth-profiling of a  β -(Ni,Pt)Al bond coated sample and a thermal barrier coated sample was performed using femtosecond laser-induced breakdown spectro- scopy. The following conclusions can be drawn from this study.  

a) The bond coat can be qualitatively distinguished from the substrate by monitoring the variation of spectral intensity of W. b) The  β  layer/IDZ interface inside the bond coat can be accurately located from the spectral profile of W. c) Mixing of spectral signals from various depths of the conical crater produced by the Gaussian laser pulses during depth- profiling affects the depth-resolution. d) It was possible to accurately locate the 7YSZ ceramic layer/ bond coat interface in a thermal barrier coated sample using fs-LIBS.
