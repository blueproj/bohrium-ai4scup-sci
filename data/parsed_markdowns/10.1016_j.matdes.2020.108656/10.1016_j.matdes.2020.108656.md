# a b s t r a c t  

Design of shape memory alloy based structure in high – frequency or dynamic applications needs to build on a lu- minous comprehension for high speed deformation mechanisms of the corresponding material. In present work, dynamic responses and bulk texture evolutions are studied in martensitic NiTi through multi – scale characteriza- tion efforts. The objective is to quantitatively decompose the macroscopic strain according to deformation mech- anisms and construct a phenom eno logical constitutive model. This study uses quasi – static  in – situ  and dynamic ex – situ  neutron diffraction techniques to follow the evolution of bulk texture, twin volume fraction, and active twinning modes under various strain rates. It is demonstrated that the mechanical responses are strongly rate sensitive. The observation is attributed to the variation in slip activity. A distinct twinning mode change during compression is captured through  in – situ  neutron diffraction, and the shear induced reorientation is well de- scribed. Based on experimental observations, a constitutive model that includes effects of elasticity, reorientation of accommodation twinning, and plasticity is constructed and validated in a wide strain rate range. The overall results provide insights into strain rate dependent mechanical response of martensitic NiTi in both experiment  

# 1. Introduction  

Unique mechanical properties, such as shape memory effect (SME) and superelasticity (SE), are physical foundations to the widespread use of shape memory alloys (SMAs). Recoverable, nonlinear, and large deformation during thermo mechanical cycling is the basic feature of SME and SE. Multiple effects during strain recovery, such as providing driving force/displacement, outputting mechanical work, dissipating energy, etc., can be applied in industry. From the microcosmic aspect, crystallographic reversibility of the deformation and transformation de- termines the strain recoverability of SMA. These classical knowledges have been recognized. However, developments of the application  ﬁ eld and service environment pose new challenges for the construction and design of SMAs. Particularly in high frequency or high speed appli- cations, SMAs are used as dampers in vibration and seismic protection [ 1 – 4 ] (frequency 10 0 \~10 2   Hz), dynamic actuators [ 5 , 6 ] (frequency 10 1 \~10 3   Hz), or impact resistance in composite structures and precision mechanical components [ 7 , 8 ] (sustain shock loading). These new de- mands push investigations for dynamic responses of SMAs.  

SMA is a material category, among it NiTi – based alloys are the most widely used. Up to now, there are several reports on strain rate sensitivity of NiTi alloys. At a strain rate up to 1.7×10 4   s − 1 , the transition stress for stress – induced martensite, the yield stress of resulting martensite, and the yield stress of parent austenite are all rate dependence in austenitic NiTi as Nemat – Nasser observed [ 9 ]. They found that there exists a certain critical strain rate (\~10 4   s − 1 ), above which the transition stress exceeds the yield stress of austen- ite phase. As results, NiTi shows properties similar to ordinary metals. This rate sensitivity is attributed to thermally activated inter- facial motion and drag effect. The dislocation – induced slip displaces motion of the phase interface, and acts as the dominant deformation mechanism, then the stress – induced martensitic transformation is bypassed. Phase transition velocity in NiTi was further presented by Guo et al. [ 10 ], the result from experiment is about 35 m/s which is two orders lower than theoretical estimation. This slow martensitic transition velocity leads a direct slip deformation of par- ent phase at high strain rate. Recently, Zhang et al. also con ﬁ rmed that the stress – induced martensitic transformation is absent under dynamic impact [ 11 ]. They used a magnetically driven  ﬂ yer plate im- pact technique to compress NiTi at 10 4   s − 1   \~10 7   s − 1 , the subsequent diffraction analysis and model simulation revealed that an elastic – plastic transition instead of martensitic transformation take place in austenitic NiTi. On the contrary, some plate impact experiments give other interesting observations. A strong nonlinear Hugoniot (shock velocity – particle velocity) was observed in austenitic NiTi which is in contrast to many other B2 ordered alloys [ 12 ]. The reason was attributed to the reduction of sound speed accompany with cubic to monoclinic structure transformation. The residual transfor- mation product was also observed around the spall region in plate impact specimens [ 13 ]. Besides, the rate – dependent domain spacing behavior was also investigated in NiTi [ 14 ]. Since various heat effects are very complex, thermo – mechanical  ﬁ elds and time scales couple with each other during rate – dependent phase transition [ 15 , 16 ]. An insightful one – demensional martensite domain model was pro- posed to quantify those effect of thermo – mechanical coupling [ 17 ]. Comparing with austenite, dynamic response of martensitic NiTi is not fully investigated. Liu et al. reported rate dependence of defor- mation mechanisms in monoclinic NiTi [ 18 ]. However, the strain rate only reached 3000 s − 1 . In this rate range, the character of stress – strain relation seems insensitive to deformation rate, except for a higher stress level comparing with quasi – static condition. More detailed microstructure analysis was carried out by Qiu et al. [ 19 ]. After dynamic compression, the twin boundary becomes fragmented and dif ﬁ cult to distinguish, and more dislocations are in- troduced in martensitic variants. A higher velocity impact experi- ment was performed by Zhu et al. [ 20 ]. After a projectile shoot at 1.2 km ⋅ s − 1 , martensitic variants lose their ordered morphology, and accumulated dislocations dramatically decrease the shape re- covery ratio of alloy. Given the above, controversies still remain in some basic issues about the dynamic features of NiTi, such as whether the deformation mechanism is identical spanning several orders of strain rate, whether there is a critical transformation/defor- mation strain rate for SMAs, and how to compare the experimental results under various loading techniques. These issues are just cru- cial for design and application of SMAs, hence more detailed investi- gation is needed.  

Until now, experimental researches on strain rate effect of SMAs mainly focus on mechanical test and local microstructural analysis. Con- ventional methods such as X – ray diffraction [ 21 – 23 ], TEM and electron diffraction [ 24 , 25 ] have been employed to investigate microstructural details related to phase transition, texture evolution and deformation mechanism. However, since the limitations of surface effect or statistical ﬂ uctuation, those methods may not well identify the overall behavior of bulk material. Lacking of entire understanding to material structures may be reasons for the above controversies. Neutron diffraction pro- vides a powerful tool to complement this aspect. Neutron beam pene- trates several millimeters into most materials in a nondestructive way and brings out the average microstructural responses of material. The gauge volume is up to 1 cm 3 , thus allows more accurate measurements to avoid surface effects. Researchers have systematically investigated the microstructural evolutions of NiTi – based SMAs by neutron diffrac- tion techniques [ 26 – 33 ]. These works fully revealed the micromechanical behavior of SMA, however, the strain rate effects were not involved. Since the time scale of high strain rate deformation ( b 1 ms) is much shorter than that of neutron data collection (sec\~min), the deformation process is dif ﬁ cult to capture directly. Strain 'frozen' test provides opportunity for this observation. Using the recovered specimens after high velocity impact or high pressure torsion, the evo- lutions of texture, phase/twinning fraction, dislocation density, and micromechanical strain contribution were studied via neutron diffrac- tion in metallic materials such as beryllium [ 34 – 36 ], magnesium [ 37 , 38 ], and aluminum [ 39 ] alloys. These studies give better under- stands on strain rate dependent deformation processes. Thus, neutron diffraction shows unique advantages and ir replace ability in researching the dynamic microstructural evolution of bulk materials.  

With those in mind, we carried out a series of compression tests for martensitic NiTi under wide strain rate range. Meanwhile,  in – situ / ex – situ  neutron diffraction measurements were performed. The goal is to quantify the strain contribution to overall deformation response and investigate the strain rate effect on each microstruc- tural deformation mechanism. Then efforts are utilizing the strain decomposition result to validate a rate dependent constitutive model and simulate the stress – strain relations. Through the present work, we hope to illuminate a way for understanding the rate depen- dent deformation mechanism by neutron diffraction technique and link the micromechanical observation to the constitutive model for reproducing the macromechanical responses. These could guide more explicit design and application of SMAs.  

# 2. Method  

# 2.1. Material and specimens  

Near – equiatomic NiTi alloy used in this work was provided by Gen- eral Research Institute for Nonferrous Metals (GRINM). The alloy was prepared by vacuum induction melting of high purity elements (99.98% Ni and 99.95% Ti). After casting and homogenized treatment, the ingot of NiTi alloy was hot – rolled to a plate of 14 mm thick. The ob- tained NiTi plate was annealed at 850   ∘ C for 30 min, followed by water quench to room temperature (RT). Chemical composition of the exper- imental material is (in wt%) Ni – 55.1, O – 0.031, C – 0.0028, and Ti-balance. Transformation temperatures of the alloy were determined using a dif- ferential scanning calorimeter (DSC). Measurements were carried out at temperatures from  − 70 °C to 140 °C under a controlled heating/cooling rate of 10 °C/min. The results are shown in Fig. S1. The  M s ,  M f ,  A s , and  A f are 45 °C, 30 °C, 53 °C, and 68 °C, respectively. No intermediate phase, such as R – phase or B19 phase, are observed. The martensite fraction is also calculated by integrating the heat  ﬂ ow curves, see dash lines in Fig. S1. The material is fully B19 ′  phase until 47 °C during heating.  

Crystal structure of B19 ′  martensitic NiTi is monoclinic, where the space group is (P 1 1 2 1 /m) with 2 equivalent atom positions, ( x ,  y ,  z ) and ( − x ,  − y ,  z  + 1/2), each with a multiplicity of 4. The lattice con- stants of  a  = 2.893 Å,  b  = 4.630 Å,  c  = 4.111 Å, and  γ = 97.3° were used to index the diffraction patterns [ 31 , 40 ]. The only proposed slip

# 2.2. Mechanical testing  

Uniaxial quasi – static compression tests were performed on an Instron hydraulic test system (Instron 5967). The strain rates were cho- sen as 10 − 4   s − 1   and 10 − 1   s − 1 . The end face displacement of specimen was measured by an LVDT.  Φ 4 mm × 4 mm specimens were used in these tests, and both ends of specimen were covered with moly – disul ﬁ de grease to minimize the end friction with compression platens. No signi ﬁ cant buckling, bulging, or bending was observed during tests.  

Dynamic compression experiments (strain rates \~10 3   s − 1 ) were per- formed using a split Hopkinson pressure bar (SHPB). In the experi- ments, the diameter of each bar was 10 mm, and the length of the striker bar was 400 mm. Specimens were sandwiched between the inci- dent and transmission bars and deformed by the compressive loading. Dynamic stress, strain, and strain rate were calculated from the re ﬂ ected and transmitted pulses according to the one – dimensional stress wave theory [ 19 , 46 ]. Both sizes of specimens were tested in SHPB experi- ments, the smaller ones were used to achieve higher strain rates during compression.  

# 2.3. Neutron diffraction experiments  

# 2.3.1. Instrument and load frame  

Neutron diffraction measurements were carried out on the Residual Stress Neutron Diffractometer (RSND) at China Mianyang Research Re- actor (CMRR) of China Academy of Engineering Physics (CAEP) [ 47 ]. The experimental setup is shown in Fig. S3. Brie ﬂ y, double – focusing silicon perfect crystal monochromator was employed to ensure a monochro- matic neutron beam. To acquire the maximum  ﬂ ux of this diffractome- ter (\~4.7 × 10 6   n ⋅ cm − 2   ⋅ s − 1 ), Si (311) re ﬂ ection and a take – off angle of 58° were chosen. The wavelength in this condition was 1.587 Å. Gadolinium – aluminum slit masked the incident beam to a 10 mm × 10 mm square area. Whole specimen could immerse in the neutron beam during measurements. A two – dimensional position sensitive de- tector with an active area of 200 mm × 200 mm was placed 1.0 m away from the specimen to record diffraction spectra. The detector slit was set to a 10 mm (horizontal) × 40 mm (vertical) cross – section and placed 50 mm away from the sample. The scattering angle (2 θ ) subtended roughly 10° within the detector in this arrangement. A mod- i ﬁ ed Eulerian cradle was used to accomplish texture measurements on RSND, see  Fig. 2 (a). The modi ﬁ ed Eulerian cradle was equipped with a servomotor load frame and a halogen spot heater to produce 30 kN uni- axial loading and RT to 500 °C temperature heating [ 48 ]. The  χ – tilt plane of Eulerian cradle was placed parallel to the diffraction vectors  Q to ensure complete pole  ﬁ gure (PF) could be measured.

# 2.4. PF calculations  

Neutron diffraction data were analyzed using the Stress Tex Calculator (STeCa) software package [ 49 ]. For each PF, 720 two – dimensional pictures were converted into Intensity – 2 θ diffractograms, and then a polynomial background was subtracted from each diffractogram. Without  ﬁ tting, the integral intensity PFs were calculated and outputted with the resolution of  α  = 10° and β  = 5° ( α  and  β  represent polar angle and azimuth angle, the rela- tions are  α  = 90°  − χ  and  β  = 360°  − φ ). Negative intensities on PF were set to zero since there were no physical meaning. After nor- malization, the PFs were imported into MTEX to calculate orientation distribution functions (ODFs) [ 50 ]. By simulating from the ODFs, re- constructed PF data were exported with a grid of 1 ∘ × 1 ∘ for the twin fraction calculation.

# 3.2.1. Phenom eno logical constitutive model  

Various theoretical models have been developed to describe the constitutive behavior of SMAs in recent decades [ 66 , 67 ]. Three broad categories can be classi ﬁ ed by lengthscale: (1) phenomenology based macroscopic models [ 68 , 69 ]; (2) micromechanics based micro – macro models [ 70 , 71 ]; (3) molecular dynamics or quantum mechanics based microscopic models [ 53 , 72 , 73 ]. Among them, the macro – phenom eno logical models have the advantages of simpleness in form and clearness in physical concept. In addition, they can satisfy most of requirements from applications standpoint. To directly use our experi- mental parameters and simulate the stress – strain relations, we chose a phenom eno logical constitutive model in this paper [ 63 ]. # 3.3. Discussion  

It can be seen from the mechanical responses of martensitic NiTi ( Fig. 4 ), strain rate signi ﬁ cantly affects the stress – strain relations, espe- cially in the reorientation/detwinning processes of martensitic variants. Since the mechanical property of SMA is strongly coupled with temper- ature, it should  ﬁ rstly consider the temperature rise during dynamic de- formation when answering the in ﬂ uence of strain rate [ 14 – 17 ]. With the strain rate increases, the heat exchange between specimen and en- vironment converts from isothermal to adiabatic process. If reverse martensitic transition happens, the resulting austenite will promote the stress level in Regime II. Two factors should be considered here. First, the generated heat remains within the specimen at high strain rates, increasing its temperature during deformation. Second, the ap- plied stress stabilizes the martensitic phase, increasing the transition temperature. At high strain rates, most of deformation work dissipates into heat, and only a small portion of work is stored in the metal as elas- tic energy of defects. As illustrated in  Fig. 11 (a), none of signi ﬁ cant difference is found when comparing the texture evolution between dynamic compression and quasi – static condition. This means the main twinning mode during deformation remains the same at high strain rates. However, from the quantitatively calculated results in  Fig. 11 (b), the accommodation twin- ning strain decreases with increasing strain rate. On the complementary aspect, the contribution of slip strain promotes in the early stage of de- formation [ 52 , 64 , 65 ]. The reasonable interpretation is that plenty of dis- locations are introduced into the material under impact loading. The dislocations assist the operation of slip. Besides, the work hardening leads to the strengthening of material, and more grains with disadvan- tage orientations reach the critical resolved shear stress [ 20 ]. As results, the mechanical responses change with different strain rates, especially the stress plateaus become inconspicuous under high strain rate.  

The shape recovery performance for the deformed specimens were also examined. The result is shown in Fig. S8. A suddenly drop of shape recovery ability is observed at 2200 s − 1 . This can be interpreted as more dislocations accumulate in the material during high speed im- pact, thus dislocation induced slip is more active than low speed defor- mation condition. Slip and twinning compete with each other in the early stage of deformation, and the slip is irreversible. As a consequence, the shape memory performance of NiTi alloy degrades after high – speed deformation.  

Based on neutron diffraction results, the evolutions of twin volume fraction versus stress, strain and strain rate are obtained experimentally. As seen in  Fig. 10 , the twinning contribution to the total plastic strain is modest, because the twinning volume is small comparing with the whole specimen. The slip contribution reaches 30% even in early defor- mation stage, which indicates a distinct irreversible character in variant reorientation process. In large deformation stage, the dominated defor- mation mode is slip, and the contribution of twinning is limited. The slip strain grows almost linearly with total strain. Further, a type of additive decomposition model is used to simulate the experimental results both from macroscopic stress – strain relations and microscopic strain partitioning data ( Fig. 13 ). Most of the physically meaningful material parameters are decided from experiments. By changing single variable from a same set of parametric system, the model well reproduces the mechanical response of martensitic NiTi for a wide strain rate range, es- pecially the key features under high strain rates.  

# 4. Conclusions  

1. We perform compressive tests on B19 ′  NiTi to investigate the me- chanical response under strain rates from 10 − 4   s − 1   to 5600 s − 1 . The alloy shows a normal shape memory response at quasi – static conditions. However, the stress plateau in Regime II signi ﬁ cantly el- evates with increasing strain rates. The classical elastic – plastic tran- sition is even observed at 5600 s − 1 . Neutron diffraction measurements are carried out for both quasi – static and dynamic – recovered specimens. It reveals that the dominant twinning mode is invariable with increasing strain rates, but the corresponding twin volume fraction decreases. It is attributed to that the dislocation – induced slip partly replaces the martensitic variant re- orientation/detwinning process in Regime II. By comparing with Schmid factors and axial strain contribution of various twinning modes, the dominated accommodation twinning mode is identi ﬁ ed as Mode D. The macroscopic strain is decomposed according to the active deformation mechanisms.  

2. A phenom eno logical constitutive model is used to describe the me- chanical response for B19 ′  NiTi. The material model bases on micromechanics and just can be validated by the strain partitioning data. The constitutive response is provided according to the additive decomposition of strain rates. The inelastic – strain rates resulting from martensitic variants reorientation and classical plasticity are described using modi ﬁ ed power laws. The reorientation fractions are formulated by transformation functions. Most of the physically meaningful parameters can be determined by the experiments. By changing single variable, the model can well reproduce the strain rate dependent mechanical response including stress – strain rela- tions and micromechanical strain partitioning results. Though the present work, we provide a method to understand and predict the mechanical properties for martensitic SMAs. This could guide the de- sign and application of SMAs, especially in dynamic condition.
