The hot deformation behavior of a powder metallurgy (P/M) Ni-base superalloy FGH96 was investigated at the temperature ( T ) of 1050–1140   ◦ C, strain rate ( ˙ ε ) of 0.002–1.0 s − 1 , and the height reduction of 20–50% via isothermal hot compression experiment. The ﬂow behavior and the microstructural mechanism of the synthesized superalloy were systematically studied. The processing map was constructed based on the experiment data to evaluate the efﬁciency of power dissipation and recognize the instability regimes for forging process determination. The processing map with the strain of 0.15 exhibits a smooth and small variation of    and predicts a little instability regime conﬁned at ( T : 1050   ◦ C, ˙ ε  : 1 . 0 s − 1 ), attributed to the previous particle boundary (PPB) cracks. The map obtained at the strain of 0.65 reveals a large domain with the efﬁciency above 40% and predicts two instability regimes at around (1050   ◦ C, 1 s − 1 ) and located in the regime of ( T : 1080–1140   ◦ C, ˙ ε  : 0 . 002  − 0 . 01 s − 1 ), respectively. The optimum processing condition is identiﬁed as ( T : 1140   ◦ C, ˙ ε  : 1 . 0 s − 1 ), which presented the highest efﬁciency in hot working process, as well as the obtained ﬁne microstructure after the hot processing. Via microstructure characterization and observation, it is concluded that the dynamic recrystallization (DRX) occurred at the PPBs in the P/M superalloy. The DRX and the strain rate sensitivity exponent  m  are both affected by true strain. The local DRX presents a relatively low  m  in a small strain compression. With an increase of strain, the fraction of the recrystallized grains increases with  m . The global DRX, however, presents a relatively high m  in a large strain compression. It is thus indicated that the superplasticity of FGH96 superalloy can be realized at a slow strain rate such as 0.001 s − 1   and the deformation temperature of 1110   ◦ C, near to the  ′ -transus temperature, by using the uniformly ﬁne-grained superalloys as well as the high temperature superplastic forming equipment.

# 1. Introduction  

Powder metallurgy (P/M) Ni-base superalloy is usually used in fabrication of turbine disc  [1–4] , which is one of the most important components in gas advanced engine  [5–7] , owing to the high tem- perature properties and microstructural stability of the superalloy. This kind of superalloy, however, is rather difﬁcult to deform due to its poor hot workability. Investigation on the deformation behavior of the alloy is thus essential for the design and optimization of the hot working process for this alloy.  

In this paper, the isothermal compression of P/M FGH96 super- alloy was conducted at different strain rates and temperatures. The ﬂow behavior, microstructural mechanism and the variation of strain rate sensitivity exponent  m  were investigated. The investi- gations on the above behaviors and mechanisms provide a basis for optimizing the hot working process of the P/M superalloy materials.  

In tandem with this goal, processing map technology was employed in this research. # 2.2. Experimental procedure  

The isothermal compression tests were conducted on a Gleeble- 1500D simulator at the deformation temperature from 1050 to 1140   ◦ C and the strain rate from 0.002 to 1.0 s − 1 . All the specimens were heated with a heating rate of 10   ◦ C/s and soaked for 2.0 min at the deformation temperature before isothermal compression. The deformation temperature was controlled to  ± 2   ◦ C. The specimens were deformed to the height reductions of 15, 35 and 50%, and then cooled down by water spraying to room temperature. The deformed specimens were sectioned parallel to the compression axis and the microstructure examination was conducted by an OLYMPUS-PM3 optical microscope (OM). The grain size of the specimens was mea- sured from the central area of the compressed specimen by the mean-linear intercept method. The error in grain size measurement is within  ± 5%, which is consistent with the related report on this issue  [19] .

# 3.3. Microstructural mechanism  

The domains of the processing maps could be interpreted based on the characteristic efﬁciency variation associated with the microstructural mechanisms. The microstructural mechanisms of the safe processing regimes depend on DRV and DRX, including nucleation and grain growth. # 4. Conclusions  

The hot deformation behavior of P/M Ni-base superalloy FGH96 has been investigated via physical experiment and microstructure characterization and the following conclusions are drawn from this research:  

(1) The processing map obtained at the strain of 0.15 exhibits a smooth and small variation of    and predicts a little instability regime conﬁned at ( T : 1050   ◦ C, ˙ ε  : 1 . 0 s − 1 ), attributed to the previous particle boundary (PPB) cracks.

 (2) The map obtained at the strain of 0.65 reveals a large domain exhibited the efﬁciencies all above 40%, and predicts two insta- bility regimes occurred at ( T : 1050   ◦ C, ˙ ε  : 1 . 0 s − 1 ) and located at the regime of the temperature range of 1080–1140   ◦ C and the strain rate range of 0.002–0.01 s − 1 , respectively. The opti- mum processing condition is deﬁned as ( T : 1140   ◦ C, ˙ ε  : 1 . 0 s − 1 ) where presented the highest efﬁciency in the hot process, as well as the ﬁne microstructure after the hot processing.

 (3) Through microstructure characterization and analysis, it is concluded that DRX preferably occurred at the PPBs of P/M superalloy materials. The DRX and strain rate sensitivity expo- nent  m  are both affected by true strain in such a way that the local DRX presents a relatively low  m  value in a small strain compression. With an increase of strain, the fraction of the recrystallized grains increases with  m . The global DRX, on the other hand, presents a relatively high  m  in the strain large compression. It thus indicated that the superplasticity of FGH96 superalloy can be obtained under a small strain rate and the high deformation temperature of 1110   ◦ C, near to   ′ -transus temperature with the uniform and ﬁne-grained superalloy materials.
