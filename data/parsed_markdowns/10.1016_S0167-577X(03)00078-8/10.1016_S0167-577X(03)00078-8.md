# Preparation of a (Ti,Nb,W)C particulate reinforced nickel-base superalloy via super-high temperature treatment of melt  

F.S. Yin \* , X.F. Sun a , J.G. Li a , H.R. Guan a , Z.Q. Hu a

# Abstract  

A well-dispersed (Ti,Nb,W)C particulate reinforced nickel-base superalloy was prepared via super-high temperature (above 2123 K) treatment of melt. Scanning electron microscope (SEM) observation and energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) results show that most of the (Ti,Nb,W)C particles have a carbonitride core. Gas analysis shows that nitrogen content in the alloy with super-high temperature treatment of melt is much higher than that in the conventional cast superalloy. The well- dispersed (Ti,Nb,W)C particles could be attributed that nitrogen combines with Ti in the alloy melt to produce TiN, which acts as the nucleator to form (Ti,Nb,W)C. This work provided a new way to prepare the TiC particulate strengthened composites. D  2003 Elsevier Science B.V. All rights reserved.  

Keywords:  Ni-base alloy; Melt treatment; Microstructure; TiC  

Titanium carbide reinforced iron or nickel-base matrix composites have found extensive applications in tools, dies and wear and high-temperature oxida- tion-resistant components. Conventional powder met- allurgy techniques have been used to prepare Al 2 O 3 , NbC particulate reinforced iron-base or nickel-base composites for many years. Recently, an in situ liquid metallurgical technique has provided an alternative means of preparing such composites with possible benefits to form near-finished shapes by casting. Kattamis et al.  [1,2]  fabricated TiC particulate rein- forced Ni, 304 stainless steel and IN-100 nickel-base superalloy composites by casting a supersaturated Fe–Ti–C and Ni–Ti–C melt. Hui et al.  [3]  have prepared TiC particulate reinforced Fe–Cr–Ni matrix composites by liquid in situ synthesis of metallic powders in high temperature melt. Galgali et al.  [4] have reported a method of preparing TiC reinforced composites by dissolving a TiC-rich Fe–TiC master alloy in liquid steel. In the present paper, a new method of in situ preparing TiC particulate reinforced nickel-base superalloy composite via superheat tem- perature treatment of melt is reported.  

The master alloy used in this study has the follow- ing composition (wt.%): 0.17C, 8.8Cr, 9.6Co, 10.5W, 1.6Mo, 1.0Nb, 5.8Al, 2.4Ti and rest Ni. The liquidus temperature of the alloy is 1623 K determined by DTA. X-ray diffraction analysis showed that the master alloy is composed of  g ,  g V and (Ti,Nb,W)C carbide phases.

The microstructure was examined on ISM-6301F scanning electron micro- scope (SEM) equipped with energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) for microanalysis. Figs. 1 and 2  show the grain size and micro- structure of the alloy cast with different remelting processes. It can be seen that both the grain size and microstructure are all varied by the super-high temper- ature treatment of melt obviously. In as-cast condition, the conventionally cast superalloy consists of  g  solid solution matrix,  g V  precipitate, ( g  +  g V ) eutectic and (Ti,Nb,W)C carbide  [5] . The grain structure is coarse and columnar  (Fig. 1a) , and the carbide morphology is script like  (Fig. 2a) . It is very unexpected that the super-high temperature (2123 K) treatment of melt produces very fine equiaxed grains  (Fig. 1b)  and blocky (Ti,Nb,W)C carbide particles  (Fig. 2b) . In order to verify this result, the same experiment was repeated and a similar result was obtained. Observa-

The occurrence of the second reaction prompts the ongoing of the first reaction. With the increase of the melt superheating temperature, the above reactions proceed more completely. Therefore, the nitrogen content increases greatly.  

According to the above-mentioned results, the following assumption could be drawn.  

In the conventional remelting process, the (Ti,Nb, W)C carbide formed by a (Ti,Nb,W)C +  g  eutectic reaction after the formation of  g  dendrite; therefore, the carbide had script-like morphology as reported in previous investigations  [7–11] .  

After the super-high temperature (2123 K) treat- ment of the melt, the nitrogen content in the alloy melt was increased greatly. Nitrogen was combined with Ti in the alloy melt to produce TiN, which can act as the nucleator to form (Ti,Nb,W)C carbide. In this con- dition, the (Ti,Nb,W)C carbide would form in the alloy melt and grow in a near-equilibrium mode, and therefore, had an octahedral blocky morphology  [12] .  

In summary, the super-high temperature treatment of melt could produce well-dispersed (Ti,Nb,W)C particles in the Ni-base superalloy. This method would be a new way to prepare TiC particulate reinforced composites.
