# Abstract  

The inﬂuence of 5% prestain on  c 0 precipitation during high temperature deformation of Udimet 720 was studied using stress   relaxation. The occurrence of a stress plateau was associated with  c 0 precipitation and veriﬁed by metallography examination.   PTT diagram was determined for the alloy and related to the diﬀusion coeﬃcient of  c 0 forming elements.

Keywords:  Nickel alloys; Forging; Precipitation; Stress relaxation  

# 1. Introduction  

Udimet 720 (U720) is a nickel-based superalloy with excellent strength and corrosion resistance at high tem- peratures. The alloy is generally used in forged condition and the superior mechanical properties are achieved through the precipitation of  c 0 , which may attain a vol- ume fraction of more than 45% under speciﬁc heat treat-   ment conditions  [1,2] . Precipitation of  c 0 is generally preceded by the development of a supersaturated  c phase solid solution accompanied with appropriate undercooling  [3,4] . However, deformation can aﬀect precipitation by reducing the incubation time for nucle- ation leading to strain induced precipitation.  

Although precipitation of  c 0 phase in superalloys has been extensively studied, most of the investigations have been focused on the inﬂuence of various heat treatments and cooling rates on the characteristics and kinetics of  c precipitation  [5,6] . In spite of the fact that U720 is used under forged condition, very little has been reported, at   least in open literature, on precipitation of  c 0 during deformation, i.e. dynamic precipitation. The objective of the present work was to investigate strain induced   precipitation of  c 0 in U720 alloy using a stress relaxation technique. The technique has the advantage of detecting precipitation start ( P s ) and ﬁnish ( P f ) times with ade- quate accuracy and good repeatability in one single test. It has been already applied and validated for strain in- duced precipitation of Ti(C,N)  [7]  and Nb(C,N)  [8] .  

# 2. Experimental  

The chemical composition of U720 superalloy used in this investigation was as expressed in wt%, 18Cr,

 14.8Co, 3Mo, 1.5W, 4.8Ti, 2.4Al, 0.05C, 0.04B and

 0.03Zr. The material was extracted from the root section of U720 forged blades, which were mechanically dam- aged at their tip after 3000 h of service. To determine  

the solvus temperature of  c 0 which is its upper limit of precipitation, diﬀerential thermal analysis (DTA) was performed. A 77 mg specimen cut from the as received material was examined in the temperature range 800– 1300   C in two cycles of heating and cooling at a rate of 20   C/min in argon atmosphere. Cylindrical speci- mens, 7 mm in diameter and 10 mm in height, were ma- chined by EDM (electro discharge machining) and used for stress relaxation experiments. The specimens were solutionized at 1155   C for 15 min in order to dissolve   all  c 0 precipitates into the matrix. Then, they were cooled down with a rate of 0.2   C/s to the test tempera- ture at which a compressive strain of 0.05 with a con- stant strain rate of 0.1 s  1   was applied after holding isothermally for 1 min ( Fig. 1 ). The strain of 0.05 was chosen to insure that no recrystallization took place dur- ing deformation and the subsequent holding periods. The decrease in stress with time was monitored, while the strain level after deformation was kept constant at the test temperature. Thermal contraction proﬁle of the anvils at each temperature was determined and compensated during the test by changing the anvil level according to the proﬁle. Preliminary tests showed that after 30 min the stress level dropped to about 5 MP. Therefore, for the actual tests, all specimens were held for 30 min after deformation and then water quenched. Some of the specimens tested at 1125   C were also quenched at shorter holding times of 10, 50, 100, 400 and 1000 s in order to study the evolution of the micro- structure at diﬀerent stages of the relaxation period.  

Deformed specimens were prepared for metallo- graphic examinations using regular polishing methods. The specimens were the immersion-etched in 100 ml HCl + 100 ml methanol + 5 g CuCl 2  for optical micro- scopy, and electro-etched using 17 ml H2O + 1 ml gla- cial acetic acid + 2 ml HNO 3  for scanning electron microscopy. Microhardness measurements were carried out, using a 5 kg load and reported values are averages of three measurements.

# 3. Results  

Fig. 2  shows the result of the DTA. The heating cycle   shows heat absorption, an indication of  c 0 dissolution, up to 1120   C at which the curve rebounds till 1150   C. After this temperature, no considerable heat exchange is observed between the specimen and the   ambience, indicating that the  c 0 solvus temperature of the alloy should be about 1150   C. In the cooling cycle,   on the other hand, there is no obvious evidence of  c 0 for- mation, which is expected to appear as an exothermic behavior. These results contrast with the one obtained by Loomis et al. during their DTA study  [9] . It can be concluded that the amount of heat generated as a result   of precipitation of  c 0 during the cooling cycle has not been enough to generate a well deﬁned exothermic peak on the curve. This may be attributed to the low volume   fraction of  c 0 precipitates that are formed at the cooling rate of 20   C/min. This conclusion can be supported by the fact that re-heating the specimen with the same rate (cycle 2 in  Fig. 2 ) did not show a rebound in the curve,   implying that there were not enough  c 0 precipitates to dissolve and absorb enough energy to create a well deﬁned bump on the curve. This clearly shows that a   signiﬁcant amount of  c 0 precipitation cannot be ob- tained solely by thermal process used in the present investigation.  

The stress relaxation curves are shown in  Fig. 3 . It can be seen that, stress relaxes in approximately linear manner down to a point where a plateau, which is not necessarily fully horizontal at all temperatures, appears on the curve. After this period, which is speciﬁed be- tween the two arrows in  Fig. 3 , the stress relaxes almost at the same rate as it did before the plateau. This sug- gests that, a phenomenon such as precipitation could have delayed the stress relaxation process. It is also ob- served in  Fig. 3  that, the stress level of the plateaus increases by decreasing the test temperature. Finally, it should be noted that, as the test temperature is

Isothermal holding experiments, prior to stress relaxa- tion tests, were carried out at 1125 and 1150   C (after solutionizing at 1155   C for 15 min) to check possible   c 0 precipitation during cooling. Samples were water quenched immediately after reaching the test tempera- ture or after 300 s holding. SEM examination of these   samples showed the presence of very ﬁne  c 0 precipitates (average size of 30–40 nm) for all experimental condi- tions mentioned above. The relatively constant average size of particles measured after 0 s or 300 s holding indi- cates that they are probably formed during the quench to room temperature rather than during cooling from solution to the test temperature.

Microstructure evolution during the stress relaxation process was studied by water quenching of the speci- mens at diﬀerent stages of the test at 1125   C. The cor- responding microstructures quenched after 10, 50, 100, 400 and 1000 s holding are presented in  Fig. 4 . As can be seen, no dynamic recrystallization took place during the test. Also, the evidence of precipitation can be ob- served in the microstructures, the extent of which in- creases gradually with increasing time from 10 to 1000 s. SEM micrographs of the specimens reported in   Fig. 6 , show the progress of  c 0 precipitation starting from a very ﬁne and homogenized distribution of parti- cles with an average diameter of around 50 nm after 10 s holding. Increasing the holding time to 50 and 100 s re-   sults in an increase in the  c 0 average particle size to 0.15 and 0.23  l m, respectively. After 100 s, i.e. the end point   of the plateau,  c 0 particles start to coarsen, coalesce and appear to align themselves in preferred directions, as shown in  Fig. 5 . It is interesting to note that, this align- ment of particles was only observed outside the plateau and was associated with regions where a grain boundary movement had occurred. Accordingly, the extent and   number of zones with aligned  c 0 particles increased with the holding time after the plateau. This indicates that, the grain boundary movement is probably responsible for the observed directionality of the growing particles.  

The precipitation start ( P s ) and ﬁnish ( P f ) times determined from the stress relaxation curves are pre- sented in the form of precipitation-time–temperature (PTT) diagram in  Fig. 6 , where a classic C-shape beha- vior is demonstrated. The driving force for this kind of precipitation is controlled by the degree of supersatura- tion of precipitates, i.e. Al, Ti, and Cr. Above the nose (i.e. at deformation temperatures higher than 1120   C), there is less supersaturation and therefore, the nucle- ation rate is low; longer times are then required to start precipitation. Below the nose, on the other hand, the low diﬀusivity of the precipitating elements is the cause of the increase in the start time. The results of microhardness measurement of the specimens shown in  Fig. 4  are plotted in  Fig. 7 . Hard- ness value increases from 350 Hv after 10 s to a peak value of 382 Hv after 100 s holding within the stress pla- teau region. The value drops to around 370 Hv after 400 s and slightly increases after 1000 s holding time.   This indicates that, the critical size of  c 0 beyond which there is little eﬀect on hardness increase, i.e. 230 nm, is   attained after 100 s. A similar  c 0 size to attain maximum hardness in U720 was found during aging heat treat- ment after 36 h holding at 850   C  [2] . The results are in good agreement with the stress–time curve at 1125   C, which indicated that the plateau started after about 10 s and ﬁnished after about 100 s. The above re- sults demonstrate that the eﬀective volume fraction of  c 0 particles is almost attained after 10 s holding following a 5% deformation. This implies that, during forging where much higher strains are employed, dynamic strain in-   duced precipitation of  c 0 takes place and is probably completed at the very early stages of the forging process.  

# 4. Conclusion  

The occurrence of strain-induced precipitation of  c 0 was followed using a stress relaxation based technique. The results showed that, under the experimental   conditions where  c 0 precipitation is expected, the stress relaxation is delayed and a plateau appears on the stress–log(time) curves. Optical and SEM microscopy examinations did not show the occurrence of recrystalli- zation during the test. It also appeared that, dynamic   precipitation of  c 0 is likely to take place during the early stages of the forging process.
