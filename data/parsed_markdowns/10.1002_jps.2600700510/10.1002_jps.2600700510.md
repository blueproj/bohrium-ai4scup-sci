# INTRODUCTION  

Recently it has been shown that the partition coefficient in octanol/water (Pow) is a useful indicator of the bioaccumulation tendency of organicionic and nonionic dye stuffs (Anliker et al., 1981). If the Pow is <1000, it can be confidently predicted that the bio accumulation factor(BF)infishwillbe<10o.So far results of fish bio ac cum u- lation tests with over 75 dyestuffs have confirmed this empirical rule.  

Neelyetal.(1974),andseveral authorssince(for a review see Davies and Dobbs, 1984),have described linear correlations between log bio accumulation factors (BF) and log Pow, but mainly for the highly lipophilic halogenated hydrocarbons. On the basis of any of the established correlation equations bioaccumulation would have been predicted in the case of the relatively lipophilic disperse dyes (log Pow > 3) and of some organic pigments (calculated log Pow up to 9). However, the experimental studies showed that the 23 disperse dyes and pigments investigated did not bioaccu- mulate in fish. For disperse dyes it may be hypothesized that this behavior may be due to their pronounced aggregation tendency making transport across membranes difficult.  

Other factors which might become critical and which must be considered when estimating the bio accumulation potential ofionic and nonionic chemicals have been discussed by Esser and Moser (1982), and in the OECD Guidelines (OECD, 1981). The pigments did not seem to accumulate despite their generally very high calculated Powvalues which would predict bio accumulation factors several orders of magnitude above 10". Due to the extremely low water solubilities of the pigments it was not possible to determine the real BF. In order to work in a practical and experimentally accessible concentration range the pigments had tobe finely dispersed in the water used for the fish bioaccumulation test. No accumulation in the fish as compared with the amount ofpigments dispersed in the test water was observed (Anlikeret al.,1981).  

To investigate this phenomenon further the measurements of the solubilities of a number of organic pigments and disperse dyes in n-octanol for comparison with the solubilities of strongly bioaccumulating compounds, and the calculation of the Pow and the water so lub ili ties seemed desirable.  

It was shown recently (Chiou,1985),that octanol simulates fats in respect to their partition behavior very well, and it can be assumed that this is also the case for its so lub ili zing effects for organic chemicals.Although there was not found a direct cor- relation between logB F and log fat solubility for 10 chemicals(Dobbs and Williams, 1983), the solubilityin octanol is an indicator of the maximum fat storage ofa chemi- cal, as it will be shown in this paper.

# Determination of Oct an ol/Water Partition Coefficients  

n-Octanol-water partition coefficients,log Pow, of the compounds were determined by the flask-shaking method according to established procedures (e.g., OECD Method 107, OECD, 1981), if they were in an experimentally accessible range and if the compounds were soluble enough in both phases. In addition, all partition coeffi- cients were calculated using primarily two schemes, that of Rekker and de Kort (1979) or of Hansch and Leo (1979). The latter was available as a computer program, CLOGP 3.2 (Pomona College, 1983). The calculated log P's given in Tables 1 and 2 are mostly the CLOGP 3 values, corrected, if necessary, by hand for intramoleculan H bonds. For some compounds the results obtained by the Rekker scheme, using some of our own fragment constants,were considered more trustworthy.

This good agreement indicates that the solubility in octanol reflects quite well the solubility of chemicals in natural lipids. This finding forms a parallel to a study (Chiou,1985)whichindicated a close correlation between partition coefficients in triolein/water (Prw) and Pow suggesting that Pow is also an excellent predictor for Ptw , i.e.,forlipid/water partition coefficients and bio concentration factors  

It seems reasonable to assume that thegood correspondence between octanol and lipid solubility is alsovalid in the range oflower solubility so that the measured solu- bilities of the pigments and dyes in octanol (Tables 1 and 2) will be reasonably close to their solubility in lipids-and lipids, as shown clearly in a recent paper (Geyer et al.,1985) are certainly the main,if not the only storage sitefor lipophilic chemicals in fish. Thus, even if the partition coefficients of most pigments (see Table 2) would indicate bio accumulation factors up to several orders of magnitude above10',the uptake of these chemicals will be limited at a ceiling given by the value of their lipid solubility.  

A simple calculation may serve to illustrate this fact: If pigment XV (see Table 2) is taken as a typical example, its (calculated) water solubility of between 2 × 10-6 and 10-′ ppm and its log Pow = 8.10 would theoretically predict a lipid concentration of between 250 and 1200 ppm. However, its measured octanol (= lipid) solubility is only 0.46 ppm, i.e., between 550 and 2700 times less. Based on a typical lipid content of fish of 5% (Geyer et al.,1985), the pigment content of fish on a wet weight basis would be at maximum 0.05 × 0.46 = 0.023 ppm which of course, is almost at the practical limit of analytical detectability andinviewof theverylow order of toxicity of organic pigments (Anliker and Clarke, 1980; NPIRI Raw Materials Handbook, 1983; Webb, 1984; Cowley, 1985) certainly beyond any potential hazard. In addition, this figure will probably represent an upper limit which will not be reached under practical conditions because of the molecular size effects as also suggested for disperse dyes, i.e., a substantial inhibition of permeation through membranes and other bio- logical materialsdue to thelarge molecular size of these organic pigments.Interesting evidence supporting this assumption has recently been published(Opperhuizenet al., 1985). Preliminary data obtained with the CIBA-GEIGY molecular modelling system have confirmed that most of these pigments have van der Waals diameters which by far exceed the 9.5 A units for the effective cross section (second smallest cross section) of the molecules suggested by these authors to be the upper limit for transport through biological membranes.  

Thus, the assessment of the bioaccumulation of very sparingly soluble, extremely hydrophobic chemicals such as the pigments, must be based on a calculation as shown above rather than a log BF vs log Pow correlation equation which does not take into account the solubility of the chemical in lipids or in water nor its very large molecular size and thus may lead to completely wrong conclusions.  

For disperse dyes with substantially higher octanol (lipid) solubilities, the samc arguments lead to the conclusion, that the bioaccumulation (also up to the maximum lipid solubility) may reach much higher levels which may thus warrant the execution of a fish accumulation test—-if it is indicated by the magnitude of log Pow (i.e., >3) andC(i.e.,<2g/liter).  

# CONCLUSIONS  

Colorants (dyestuffs and organic pigments) are not readily biodegradable (Clarke and Anliker, 1980) as defined by the OECD Guidelines for Testing of Chemicals (OECD, 1981). Existing regulations in Japan require in such cases the performance of fish bio accumulation tests.  

The numerical value of n-octanol/water partition coefficient Pow has become an importantfactor to be considered in determining whether to conduct the expensive fish bio accumulation studies  

It has been shown (Anliker et al., 1980) that the Pow provides a useful indicator of bioaccumulation tendency also for ionic and nonionic dyestuffs. If the Pow is less than 1000 it can be confidently predicted that the BF in fish will be less than 100. Consequently there is no need to conduct a fish bioaccumulation study with such substances.  

The experimental determination, however, of the Pow of the practically water-insol- uble pigments may be extremely dificult. In such cases it is suggested to calculate thePow.

The high Pow values would suggest strong bioaccumulation tendencies. But no accumulation in the fish as compared with the amount of pigments (e.g., pigments XXII and XXIII) dispersedin the test-waterwas observed for the pigments tested.  

The reason for this apparent inconsistency is the very limited fat (lipid) storage potential of these pigments indicated by their low solubilities in n-octanol and thein large molecular size.As a consequence the procedure to follow in deciding whether a fish accumulation test for assessing the accumulation potential of pigments is nec es sarywouldbe:  

—-measurement or calculations of the Pow and Cy - determination of the solubility in n-octanol, especially for those with Pow > 103 - determination of the van der Waals diameters, measured perpendicularly to thein longestaxis.  

As shown in the example,the maximumpossible concentration to be expected in fish can be estimated from n-octanol solubility values and the percentage of lipid content infish.Based on the resulting value and the to xico logical profile of thepigment the potential hazard can be assessed. If it is found to be of no concern the performance of fish accumulation tests,which areproblematic anyway due to the extremelylow water solubility of these pigments, is unnecessary. In addition, the findings of Opper- huizen et al.
