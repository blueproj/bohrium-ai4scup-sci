# 1.  

ACCEPTED 2 )-nano precipitates are a promising new  materials class for next generation high-temperature superalloys. Currently, Ni-based superalloys  are the material of choice for high-temperature structural applications; these alloys derive their  favorable properties from highly stable, coherent, ordered intermetallic  γ ’(L1 2 )-nano precipitates  embedded in a disordered  (f.c.c.)-matrix [1-3]. Nickel-based superalloys, as a mature  technology, have been optimized for peak operating temperature and high temperature  performance by multiple alloying additions. It is not unusual for Ni-based superalloys to contain  10 or more elements in an effort to improve overall high-temperature properties [2-8]. The  higher liquidus temperatures of Co-based superalloys make them an attractive alloy class to  compete with modern Ni-based superalloys. Cobalt was previously seen as a possible substitute  for Ni because they are both transition metals and have similar densities, with Co having a higher  melting point than Ni. The potential of Co was not realized due to an apparent lack of a  γ ’(L1 2 )- phase field; instead, legacy Co-based superalloys were strengthened primarily through solid- solution-strengthening and carbide formation and relegated to lower temperature applications  [3]. Sato et al. later demonstrated that the Co-Al-W system contains a  γ (f.c.c) plus  γ ’(L1 2 ) microstructure is achievable in a Co-based system[9].  

Ni-Al, Ni-Al-Cr and some quaternary Ni-Al-Cr-X [10-16] model alloys have been  examined in great detail and have provided important insights into coarsening behavior in the Ni- based superalloys through measurement of kinetic properties, such as coarsening rate constants  and the temporal exponents of the mean precipitate radius, <R(t)>, precipitate number density,  N V (t), and supersaturation s the different alloying elements; and the  coarsening kinetics of Co-10Al-10W (at. %) at 800 and 900 °C, while Sauza et al. The archival  literature on coarsening behavior in Co-based superalloys remains scarce, however. As a new  class of alloys, the majority of the research on Co-based superalloys has focused on the  mechanical behavior at 900-1000 °C for times typically greater than 100 h (because this  temperature regime is relevant to jet engine turbine components) [17-24], long-term stability of  the  γ ’(L1 2 )-nano precipitates [25-29], and the partitioning behavior of all elements in an alloy  [30-33]. At these temperatures and times, the kinetics of the Co-Al-W system are generally too  rapid to infer information on the development of the precipitate composition and microstructure  during the early stages of nucleation, growth, and coarsening. The current research investigates  the temporal evolution of a model ternary Co-8.8Al-7.3W (at. %) superalloy aged at 650 °C and  

750 °C, which permits sufficiently slow kinetics to observe the early stages of precipitation. By  aging at these lower temperatures for different times, the important kinetic properties described  above are established. Our current research also examines the effects of quench-rate on  2 )- precipitation, which is relevant to thermal processing and resulting precipitate size distributions 

 (PSDs), which ultimately influence the high-temperature performance of these alloys.

# 4.1. Effect of quenching medium on microstructure and microhardness  

Cooling rate, especially from solutionizing temperatures, has a significant effect on the  mechanical properties of conventional Ni-based superalloys. Mitchell et al. [44] have  demonstrated that slow cooling rates decrease the microhardness values of Ni-based superalloys,  primarily due to the formation of multimodal microstructures, which contain large primary  γ’(L12)-, intermediate secondary γ’(L12)-, and small tertiary γ’(L12)-nano precipitates. Slow  

cooling of Ni-based superalloys can also lead to the non-uniform formation of  γ ’(L1 2 )- nano precipitates in the matrix, which is also undesirable for mechanical properties [44]. Faster  cooling rates, in Ni-based alloys, suppress the formation of large primary  nano precipitates, which leads to unimodal precipitate size distributions (PSDs) and consequently  an increase in the microhardness values and a general improvement in mechanical properties.  

The quench study presented in this article suggests that Co-based alloys may be more  resistant to forming multimodal microstructures upon cooling. While the slowest cooling rate  (10.4 °C/min)  in the Co-8.8Al-7.3W alloy resulted in a unimodal PSD, Mitchell et al. [44]  observed multi-modal precipitate formation at a slower cooling rate (6 °C/min). Therefore to  fully assess the quenching susceptibility of   γ future studies will need to investigate slower cooling rates (0.1 and 1 °C/min).
