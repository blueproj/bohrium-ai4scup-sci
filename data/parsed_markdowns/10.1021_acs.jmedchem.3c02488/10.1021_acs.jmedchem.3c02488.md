ABSTRACT:  Previously, we demonstrated that linear peptide epoxyketones targeting the immuno proteasome (iP) could ameliorate cognitive deficits in mouse models of Alzheimer’s disease (AD) independently of amyloid deposition. We also reported the first iP- targeting macrocyclic peptide epoxyketones, which exhibit improved metabolic stability compared with their linear counterparts. Here, we prepared additional macrocyclic peptide epoxyketones and compared them with existing macrocyclic iP inhibitors by assessing Caco2 cell- based permeability and microsomal stability, providing the four best macrocyclic iP inhibitors. We then evaluated the four compounds using the Ames test and the potency assays in BV2 cells, selecting compound  5  as our AD drug lead. # INTRODUCTION  

Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is the most common cause of dementia and the leading cause of death and disability in geriatrics. In 2023, an estimated 32 million people worldwide are living with AD, including 6.7 million Americans.   By 2050, the total prevalence of AD is estimated to be 152 million globally, including 14 million Americans.   To date, four symptomatic (donepezil, galantamine, rivastigmine, and memantine) and two disease-modifying (aducanumab and lecanemab) drugs have been approved by the US FDA for AD treatment. While the latter two amyloid-clearing monoclonal antibody drugs have made a breakthrough impact on AD treatment by slowing disease progression, they do not stop or cure AD, leaving significant room for improvement. Increasing evidence suggests that AD is closely associated with an innate immune response that triggers the release of inflammatory cytokines from activated microglia and hyper-reactive astrocytes accumulated around  β -amyloid (A β ) plaques to induce neurotoxic effects. − 5   As a result, inflammation has drawn significant attention to developing AD drugs.   Anti- inflammatory drugs targeting COXs, TNF- α , TREM2, or IL- 1 β , etc., have been investigated in multiple clinical trials on AD 7 − 9   but have not yet yielded effective AD drugs.

# RESULTS AND DISCUSSION  

Chemistry.  Previously, we reported the creation of a macrocyclic peptide epoxyketone library, most of which retained good iP inhibitory activity as predicted by molecular dynamics studies.   We also showed that one of the library family members, DB-60, has promising in vivo efficacy in a mouse model of AD, further demonstrating LMP2-inhibiting macrocyclic peptide epoxyketones as potential AD drug leads. We selected five macrocyclic LMP2 inhibitors from the existing library (DB-60 and  1 − 4 ,  Figure 2 A) with good LMP2 inhibition potency and specificity to screen for a macrocyclic AD drug lead. In addition, we prepared four additional macrocyclic LMP2 inhibitors based on the information obtained from the SAR studies from the macrocyclic library.   To this end, a proline residue at the P3 position with or without 4-monofluoride was kept for all four compounds to maintain the LMP2 inhibition activity ( 5 − 8 ,  Figure 2 B). ,   Two common amino acid groups (leucyl and phenyl) and a cyclopentenyl residue were placed at the P1. At the same time, a macrocyclic ring was created between P2 (Ser, Tyr) and P3 (Pro) groups using a 5- or 7-carbon chain to provide compounds  5 − 8 . The cyclopentenyl group at the P1 position has been reported to give the LMP7 and LMP2 binding.   All four compounds ( 5 − 8 ) were synthesized following a previously reported procedure.   In general, as illustrated in  Scheme 1 , these macrocyclic peptide epoxyke- tones were prepared via a convergent approach, coupling right- hand fragments ( α ′  and  β ′ -epoxy amino acids) with macro- cyclic left-hand fragments. Specifically, for the macrocyclic left- hand fragments, Boc-Ser-O-Bzl ( 9 ) was first converted to the O -alkylated terminal alkene ( 10 ) using the palladium catalyst and allyl methyl carbonate, followed by Boc-deprotection and coupling with Boc-Pro-OH ( 12 ) via a conventional amide coupling reaction to yield a dipeptide intermediate ( 13 ). Compound  13  was then Boc-deprotected and coupled with 4- pentenoic acid to provide dipeptidyl dienes ( 15 ). A ring- closing metathesis of compound  15  using Grubb’s second- generation catalyst provided alkene-bridged macrocyclic peptides ( 16 ). Reduction of the cycloalkenes and deprotection of the benzyl ester were simultaneously achieved via hydro- genation to afford the macrocyclic left fragments ( 17 ), which were then coupled with the epoxyketone right-hand fragments to provide the final macrocyclic peptide epoxyketones ( 18 ).  

Macrocyclic Peptide Epoxyketones Have Good In Vitro Metabolic Stability.  Assessing metabolic stability during drug development is essential since metabolic enzymes present in physiological environments can destroy peptide drugs before reaching the brain.   For example, carfilzomib, a linear peptide epoxyketone inhibitor of cP and iP approved by the FDA for the treatment of multiple myeloma, is rapidly cleared from systemic circulation (half-life  ≤ 1 h) due to enzymatic activities of peptidases and epoxide hydrolases 26 ,  

and is known to be non-brain-permeable. Therefore, early metabolic stability assessment is essential, especially for peptide-based central nervous system (CNS) drug candidates. Before assessing the metabolic stability of selected macro- cyclic compounds, we first verified the iP inhibition potency and specificity of newly prepared macrocyclic compounds ( 5 − 8 ) with previously reported macrocyclic compounds ( 1 − 4  and DB-60) as internal controls and then performed metabolic stability assays. Interestingly, our results revealed that the LMP2 inhibitory potency of macrocyclic peptide epoxyketones widely varies depending on the presence and configuration of the fluoro residue of P3-proline. Based on molecular docking analysis previously reported by us,   we suspect that the  trans - fluoro group as a hydrogen-bond acceptor may contribute to additional interactions with nearby His-114 or His-116, improving the potency of compound  5  (IC 50  = 87 nM) compared to the non-fluoro counterpart (compound  21 , IC 50 = 412 nM) from our previous publication. In addition, it appears that P2-tyrosine provides an additional handle to LMP7 binding, thereby exhibiting LMP7 and LMP2 inhibition activity ( Table S1 ).

Combined results support the fact that macrocyclic peptide epoxyketones lacking P2-tyrosine have superior metabolic stability to linear counterparts.  

Macrocyclic Peptide Epoxyketones Are Minimally Impacted by Efflux Transporters.  The BBB is a major hurdle in developing CNS drugs as it blocks over 98% of all small-molecule drugs from entering the brain. P-glycoprotein (Pgp;  ABCB1 ) and breast cancer resistance protein (BCRP; ABCG1 ) are two major membrane transporters abundantly present in the BBB actively engaged in drug efflux. For example, FDA-approved peptide-based linear proteasome inhibitors such as carfilzomib and, to a lesser extent, ixazomib are substrates of Pgp and BCRP. − 30  

Given this, we wanted to examine whether macrocyclic compounds ( 1 − 8  and DB-60) have the potential to be a BBB- permeable drug by measuring the permeability and efflux ratio (ER) using the Caco2 cell monolayers overexpressing both Pgp and BCRP. Of note, most of the macrocyclic compounds, except for compounds  1  and  2 , show favorable log P and topological polarized surface area (TPSA, <120 Å) values ( Table 2 ), which estimate the passive diffusion of molecules across the BBB.   Remarkably, the Caco2 cell permeability assay demonstrated that macrocyclic iP inhibitors ( 3 − 8  and DB-60) have generally high permeability values ( P app  A − B, >1 ×  10 − 6   cm/s) and low ER (0.4 − 2.6) ( Tables 2  and  S2 ), suggesting the promising potential of macrocyclic peptides to cross the BBB. In contrast, Caco2 cell monolayers treated with 1  or  2  exhibited much higher  P app  B − A transport than the  P app A − B transport ( Table S2 ), indicating that compounds  1  and  2 are likely good substrates of the efflux transporters. This result suggests that macrocyclic compounds containing an extra glycine residue within the macrocyclic ring ( 1  and  2 ) are highly subjective of drug efflux by Pgp and BCRP as opposed to other macrocyclic compounds ( 3 − 8 ), even though the added glycine residue provides extra binding affinity to LMP2 ( Table 1 ).  

To verify this further, we examined which transporter contributes to the efflux of compounds  1  and  2  by measuring the ER with and without efflux transporter inhibitors. The treatment of zosuquidar, a Pgp inhibitor, decreased the ER value of  1  and  2  ( ∼ 22 with no inhibitor vs  ∼ 1.4 with zosuquidar) ( Table 2 ), indicating that  1  and  2  are Pgp substrates. However, the ER value differences between BCRP inhibitor Ko143-treated and untreated groups were insignif- icant for all compounds tested ( Table 2 ), suggesting that they are not substrates of BCRP. Taken together, macrocyclic peptide epoxyketones, except for  1  and  2 , have promising potential to be BBB-permeable drugs.  

Macrocyclic Peptide Epoxyketones Have Favorable In Vitro Toxicology Profiles.  Based on the combined results from the metabolic stability and Caco2 permeability assays, we selected four compounds ( 3 − 5  and  7 ) and performed various in vitro toxicology studies. When normal human cell lines (HMC3, microglia; ARPE19, retinal pigment epithelia) were treated with these compounds for 96 h, no toxic effects were observed up to 30 and 100  μ M in HMC3 and ARPE19, respectively ( Figure 3 A). In comparison, the previously reported linear peptide epoxyketone LMP2 inhibitor, KZR- 504,   displayed mild cytotoxicity at low micromolar concentrations, with an IC 50  value of 24  μ M in HMC3 cells.

Encouraged by the promising in vitro toxicity profile and LMP2 inhibition potency of  5 , as a preliminary drug development step, we explored its pharmacokinetic (PK) properties in healthy BALB/c mice with a single administration intravenously (iv, 40 mg/kg) or orally (po, 150 mg/kg).  

As shown in  Figure 5 , the iv administration showed a fast distribution phase (up to 2 h), followed by a slow elimination phase. To our surprise, when  5  was administered orally, maximum plasma levels of  5  were reached at the first sampling point ( t max , 15 − 30 min), indicating that  5  is rapidly absorbed via the gastric intestinal tract. The bioavailability of  5  was estimated to be  ∼ 10% in healthy BALB/c mice, which suggests promising potential for further optimization of the oral dosage forms. The estimated PK parameters for  5  are summarized in Table 3 . Briefly, compound  5  was rapidly cleared from the blood circulation system with a mean half-life ( t 1/2 ) of 0.42 and 0.79 h after i.v. and po administration, respectively. The systemic clearance (CL) was 4253 mL/min/kg. The apparent volume of distribution at the steady state ( V ss ) of 2.6 L/kg was considerably greater than the total body fluids in mice, suggesting that  5  was rapidly enriched in most tissues. The human plasma protein binding of compound  5  was also investigated by using the equilibrium dialysis method, a frequently used technique for measuring compound-plasma protein binding. The unbound fraction ( f  u ) of  5  was measured as 0.45  ±  0.07, while the unbound clearance (i.e., CL int,u  = CL int / f u ) was estimated as 72.0 mL/min/kg. On the other hand, the plasma stability (78%) and recovery rate (74%) were slightly low for the 4 h equilibration time tested. Considering the probable nonspecific binding effect of  5 , the plasma protein binding may need to be further investigated using alternative approaches, such as ultrafiltration and ultra cent ri fuga tion. Taken together with the intrinsic clearance data ( Table 1 ), this suggests that macrocyclic compounds, including  5 , may still have stability issues in plasma while considerably improved over linear counterparts. # CONCLUSIONS  

We previously reported that inhibition of the iP by linear peptide epoxyketones could improve cognitive function in mouse models of AD by suppressing microglia-mediated inflammation. ,   To identify a lead drug candidate with CNS drug properties, we also reported the creation of a library of macrocyclic peptide epoxyketones targeting the LMP2 subunit.   The present study is a follow-up investigation of the pharmaceutical properties of the macrocyclic peptide epox- yketone iP inhibitors to identify an AD drug lead that may be further investigated for its efficacy in preclinical and clinical settings. In this report, four additional macrocyclic peptide epoxyketones (compounds  5 − 8 ) were created and compared with five macrocyclic compounds (compounds  1 − 4  and DB- 60) selected from the existing library of macrocyclic peptide epoxyketones targeting the LMP2. These nine macrocyclic compounds were initially screened using various in vitro assays such as relevant physicochemical properties, metabolic stability, BBB permeability, and inhibitory potency. To this end, compound  5  was identified as a drug lead. Our preliminary in vivo PK and biodistribution studies in BALB/ c mice suggest the macrocyclic compound  5  can achieve the inhibition of the LMP2 activity in the brain with no apparent toxicity ( Figure 6 ), supporting  5  as a nontoxic LMP2-targeting AD drug lead.  

The brain is the most sophisticated and vital organ in the body. The most challenging endeavor in the design of CNS drugs is to overcome the BBB. Although several linear peptide- based cP inhibitors, including two peptide epoxyketones (carfilzomib-injectable and oprozomib-oral), are in the clinic or under clinical development, they are shown to be marginally brain permeable or impermeable. − 36   Likewise, KZR-504, a linear peptide epoxyketone-based LMP2 inhibitor, is reported to have poor brain permeability in mice.   The poor brain permeability may be attributed to rapid metabolism and the activity of the efflux transporters present in the BBB. This is one of the reasons for designing macrocyclic peptide epoxyketones in our previous work, resisting the action of enzymes responsible for peptide epoxyketone metabolism (peptidases and epoxide hydrolases). − 39   As expected, the macrocyclic backbone considerably improved the metabolic stability and BBB permeability potential ( Tables 1  and  2 ).  

When  5  was administered orally to healthy mice, we observed a modest but promising oral bioavailability of  ∼ 10% ( Table 3 ), which may be due to the first-pass metabolism or/ and nonoptimum delivery formulation. While one can argue that compound  5  may need additional optimization for oral delivery, our current study supports a promising potential of  5 as an AD drug lead that can be administered via the oral route. Since AD patients may require frequent and prolonged drug treatment, orally administrable drugs can provide convenience and dosing flexibility. In summary, we assessed in vitro and in vivo drug properties of newly prepared and existing macro- cyclic peptide epoxyketones that primarily inhibit the iP catalytic subunit LMP2, identifying compound  5  as an AD drug lead that may need further optimization and preclinical investigations. Additional studies regarding the in vivo efficacy and mechanism of compound 5 are ongoing in two distinct AD mouse models (amyloid pathology recapitulating 5xFAD and tau aggregates-forming PS19).

Proteasome Activity Assay.  The 20S proteasome assay was performed as previously described.   Briefly, purified human 20S cP or iP (50 ng) in 20S proteasome assay buffer [AB, 200 mM Tris and 5 mM EDTA (pH 8.0)] was incubated with varied concentrations of test compounds (3, 10, 30, 100, 300, 1000, 3000, and 10,000 nM) or control (DMSO only) for 1 h. For the in cellulo assay, BV2 cells were seeded (60,000 cells/well, 6-well-plate) 1 day before the test compound treatment. The cell pellets were harvested after 24 h of incubation and then lysed using passive lysis buffer (PLB; Promega). After centrifugation (13,000 g  for 5 min at 4  ° C), the resulting supernatant was added to a 96-well plate (10  μ g total protein/well) in AB. For tissue samples collected from mice treated with  5 , they were homogenized in a 4-fold excess volume of ice-cold phosphate buffer saline (PBS) and then further diluted with an equal volume of PLB. After centrifugation (14,000 g  for 15 min at 4  ° C), the resulting supernatant in AB was added to a 96-well plate (300  μ g total protein/ well). For mouse blood, it was directly mixed with four parts volume of PLB, and the resulting blood lysates (2  μ L) in AB were added to a 96-well plate. The individual proteasome subunit activities were monitored by adding respective subunit-selective 7-amino-4-methyl- coumarin (AMC) labeled fluorescence substrates into the 96-well plates, followed by the measurement of fluorescence on a plate reader (excitation/emission = 360/460 nm; SpectraMax M5, Molecular Devices): Ac-PAL-AMC (LMP2; 100  μ M), Suc-LLVY-AMC (chymotrypsin-like; 100  μ M), Ac-ANW-AMC (LMP7; 100  μ M), Ac-nLPnLD-AMC (Y; 50  μ M), Ac-WLA-AMC (X; 50  μ M), and Ac- RLR-AMC (trypsin-like; 25  μ M).  

Metabolic Stability Assays.  Following preincubation at 37  ° C, the pooled human liver microsome was mixed with test compounds (2  μ M) to achieve the final matrix concentration of 0.5 mg/mL (i.e., V  = 2 mL/mg). An aliquot was taken from the microsomal reaction mixture at the predetermined time points (0, 5, 10, 15, 30, and 60 min). The collected aliquots were then immediately mixed with two volumes of acetonitrile containing internal standard chrysin to terminate the reactions. After centrifugation (13,000 g , 10 min, 4 ° C) of the resulting mixtures, the quantity of test compounds was measured with tandem mass spectrometry (LC − MS/MS); specifi- cally, the peak areas observed in LC − MS/MS of each compound were converted to % remaining. The % remaining was plotted against the incubation time using the  t  = 0 peak area values as 100%. The half-life ( T 1/2 ) was calculated as ln 2/slope. Intrinsic clearance (CL int ; mL/min/mg microsome protein) was calculated as  V  ×  slope and then further multiplied with the two scaling factors: 45, microsome protein per gram liver; 20, gram liver per body weight for a human. Equilibrium Dialysis.  The unbound fraction ( f  u ) of compound  5 was determined using the Rapid Equilibrium Dialysis device (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Rockford, IL). Briefly, after loading the spiked  5  in human plasma (2  μ M) and PBS to the sample (300  μ L) and buffer chamber (500  μ L), respectively, it was incubated on an orbital shaker (250 rpm) for 4 h. A 50  μ L aliquot of sample was then collected from each chamber and added to ice-cold precipitation buffer (acetonitrile/ water = 270/30  μ L). After vortexing (20 min) and then centrifuging (12,000 g , 10 min), the supernatant was collected for LC − MS/MS analysis. The unbound fraction was calculated as the concentration ratio of the buffer chamber to the plasma sample chamber.

The plates were then incubated at 37  ° C for 120 min. 10  μ L of the sample aliquot was collected from the donor side and added to a mixture of transport buffer (70  μ L) and ice-cold acetonitrile (240  μ L). 70  μ L of the sample aliquot was also collected from the receiver compartments and added to ice-cold acetonitrile (240  μ L). Quenched samples were vortexed for 5 min and centrifuged (3000 g , 15 min). The supernatant was further diluted 1:1 with water for LC − MS/MS analysis.  

Apparent permeability ( P app ) coefficients (cm/s) were calculated by (d Q /d t )  ×  [1/( A  ×  C 0 )], where d Q /d t  is the amount of product present in the basal (A to B) or apical (B to A) in a function of time (nmol/s);  A  is the surface area of transwell (cm 2 ); and  C 0  is the initial concentration of the compound (nmol/mL) applied in the donor compartment. ER was calculated by the ratio of  P app  in the B − A direction to  P app  in the A − B direction. In general, Pgp- or BCRP- mediated efflux is considered to occur when ER is above 2.  

Cell Viability Assay.  HMC3 and ARPE cells were seeded on 96- well plates (5000 cells/well). After a 24 h incubation, varied concentrations of test compounds were added to the plates and incubated for 96 h. Viable cells were then quantified by the CellTiter 96 AQueous One Solution Cell Proliferation Assay (Promega) using the SpectraMax M5 plate reader.  

AMES Test.  The bacterial strains ( S. typhimurium  TA98 and TA100) were exposed to the test compounds with and without the Sprague − Dawley rat S9 fraction. The compounds (highest concen- tration, 1 mg/mL; 4-fold dilution series) were preincubated with bacteria in 24-well plates for 90 min at 37  ° C with slight agitation followed by 48 h of incubation in 384-well plates at 37  ° C in His- medium. The His-medium contains a pH-sensitive indicator dye, which changes color from purple to yellow in response to the acidification of the medium by mutations induced by test compounds. The color change was detected by visual inspection, and the positive wells were counted (48 wells per concentration, triplicate). For the mutagenicity positive controls, without the S9 fraction, 2-nitro- fluorene (2  μ g/mL)/4-nitroquinolene- N -oxide (0.1  μ g/mL) was used for both TA98 and TA100 strains. With the S9 fraction, 2- aminoanthracene (1  μ g/mL for TA98; 2.5  μ g/mL for TA100) was used. The test compound is classified as a mutagen if the fold induction is greater than 2 compared to the baseline obtained from the vehicle.  

Animal Experiments.  Evaluation of PK properties of  5  was done using healthy BALB/c mice (male, 7 weeks old, 20 − 25 g, Jackson Laboratory). Mice were given intravenous bolus injections of  5  [40 mg/kg, dissolved in 10 mM citrate buffer (pH 3.1), 4 mg/mL] through the tail vein.  5  was also orally administered via a gavage needle [150 mg/kg, dissolved in 20% (w/v) Cremophor EL in PBS, 10 mg/mL]. Blood (20 − 30  μ L) was collected via a tail vein snip into tubes with lithium heparin at designated times (iv, 5, 15, 30, 60, 120, 360, and 1440 min; po, 15, 30, 60, 120, and 360 min). 10  μ L of blood samples was snap-frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at  − 80  ° C until the proteasome activity assay. The remaining blood was processed to plasma by centrifugation (2000 g  for 10 min at 4  ° C) to quantify  5 using LC − MS/MS. The PK parameters were obtained using non compartment al methods with the sparse data option of WinNonlin (ver. 8.1). The mice were sacrificed at a designated time, and then, tissues (brain, liver, lung, kidney, and spleen) were harvested and kept at  − 80  ° C until analysis. Half of the tissue homogenates were subjected to liquid − liquid extraction by mixing with a 9-fold excess volume of cold acetonitrile containing chrysin (internal standard). After centrifugation (3000 g  for 5 min at 4  ° C), the resulting organic phase was transferred to a new tube and evaporated. The samples were reconstituted with acetonitrile and analyzed by LC − MS/MS. Another half of the tissue homogenates were used for the proteasome activity assay.  

Quantitation of Test Compounds.  Test compounds were quantified by an AB Sciex API 5500 (Agilent, Palo Alto, CA) with LC (Waters BEH UPLC). The chromatographic separation was done on a Waters XBridge C18 column (2.1  ×  30 mm i.d., 3.5  μ m). The mobile phase comprised methanol: acetonitrile: water (1:1:2, v/v) containing 0.1% formic acid at the flow rate of 0.8 mL/min. The total run time was 7 min. Quantification was carried out in the selected reaction monitoring mode using the following transition ( m / z ): carfilzomib, 720.0/289.1; DB-60, 590.0/357.1; chrysin, 255.1/153.0; 1 , 509.4/310.1;  2 , 523.2/324.1;  3 , 438.2/154.1;  4 , 488.3/154.2;  5 , 470.0/230.1;  6 , 504.2/230.1;  7 , 494.0/88.1;  8 , 580.0/357.1.
