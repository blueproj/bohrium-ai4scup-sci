A numerical model to predict mechanical properties of Ni-base superalloys for turbine disk  applications is developed by modifying and combining previous models that consider various  microstructural factors. Modifications are made so that as many necessary model parameters as  possible, whose values have been obtained from mechanical measurements and microstructural  observation, can be determined using a thermodynamic calculation and a precipitation kinetics  simulation. The model’s predictions of tensile properties, creep resistance and fatigue damage  tolerance have good correlation with experimental data. The applicability and limits of the present  model as an alloy design tool for new Ni-base disk superalloys are discussed in this paper.

# 1. Introduction  

Ni-base superalloys have good mechanical properties at temperatures 650  ≤   T   ≤  850 °C and  therefore are used for turbine disk applications in aero-engines (Locq and Caron 2011; Zhou et al.,  2011; Crudden et al., 2013; Evans and Saxana, 2013). When new Ni-base superalloys are being  developed for turbine disk applications, several mechanical properties should be considered (e.g.,  such as tensile properties, creep resistance, and fatigue damage tolerance). To improve these  properties, various alloying elements (e.g., B, C, transition metals) have been added to Ni-Al alloys  (Crudden et al., 2013; Reed et al., 2016). However, quantification of these mechanical properties takes  time; an alternative approach is to use a computational technique that predicts these mechanical  properties before the superalloys are fabricated.  

All existing models of these mechanical properties have some limitations. Microstructure-based  physical models based on continuum mechanics can predict mechanical properties in functional forms  of not only stress and temperature but also microstructural factors (Sabnis et al., 2013; Le Graverend  et al., 2014; Ghorbanpour et al., 2017; Barba et al., 2018). These models are practically suitable for  analyses of relevant experimental properties, but require that some model parameter values be  optimized experimentally, so application to new alloys is limited if the necessary experimental  information is not available for those new alloys. Mechanical properties can be also predicted using  material informatics approaches such as statistics and machine learning (Hou et al., 2010; Broderick  ACCEPTED et al., 2016; Conduit et al., 2017; Jiang et al., 2018), but these methods cannot readily represent  changes in mechanical properties that occur when various microstructural factors change. Empirical  models can consider a variety of microstructural factors, and use experimental data to predict  mechanical properties (Kozar et al., 2009; Osada et al., 2013; Ahmadi et al., 2014; Galindo-Nava et al.,  2015; Kassner, 2015). These alloys were fabricated using argon gas atomization, powder sieving (-270 mesh),  and hot isostatic pressing at sub-solvus temperatures and 103 MPa for 4 h. The heat treatment  procedure entailed sub-solvus solution annealing for 1 h, salt quenching or air cooling, and two-step  aging heat treatments at 870 °C for 1 h, air cooling, 650 °C for 24 h, then air cooling.  

For scanning electron microscope (SEM, Hitachi S-4200) investigations, alloys that had been salt  quenched and aged were electro-etched at a voltage of 6 V DC in a reagent composed of 5 ml HClO 4   in 95 ml of ethanol, chemically etched in a reagent composed of 5 g CuCl 2  in 100 ml of HCl and  100 ml of methanol at room temperature. Thin foils were  prepared by electrochemical polishing using a twin-jet polisher and a solution of 90% acetic acid and  10% perchloric acid at 60 V and room temperature. Additional analyses for microstructural  observations were performed using an electron probe microanalyser (EPMA, JXA-8100) and an  energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscope (EDS).  

To investigate the mechanical properties of the alloys, tensile tests based on ASTM E21 were  conducted at 650 °C on specimens with a gauge diameter of 6.25 mm and a gauge length of 32 mm, in  universal testing machines using resistance heating and axial extensometers. The strain rate was  0.005/min before yielding and 0.05/min after yielding. A creep test based on ASTM E139 was  performed for the alloys that underwent a salt quenching at 650 °C, under a stress level of 670 MPa  on specimens that had a gauge diameter of 6.0 mm and a gauge length of 27 mm in uniaxial lever arm  constant-load creep frames using resistance heating furnaces and shoulder-mounted extensometers.  Fatigue crack propagation behavior of 7.6-mm through-thickness cracks was investigated at 650 °C  and 700 °C on compact-tension specimens that were 8 mm thick and 36.4 mm wide. The test was  performed using MTS 810 servo-hydraulic testing machines, based on ASTM E647 with specimens  cycled under controlled stress intensity  K ([minimum load]/[maximum load]) = 0.1.

#  

The present numerical model calculates tensile properties, creep resistance, and fatigue damage  ACCEPTED tolerance by using computational techniques. In most previous numerical models, the model  parameters had to be obtained by fitting relevant experimental data or from new experimental  measurements. Since it was not easy to determine model parameter values for a wide range of alloy  systems, most of the previous models showed limitations when being utilized for alloy design of  unknown alloy compositions. In contrast, in our model all parameters are calculated from  computational techniques or from relevant equations established from already available experimental  data for a wide range of commercial alloys and the present alloys. Accordingly, no further  experimental measurements are required to estimate mechanical properties of new alloy compositions.  This is the biggest difference between our model and previous ones.  

Our model has two limitations: First, it does not use a computational approach to estimate grain  size, which is highly dependent on process conditions such as powder size, hot isostatic pressing  temperature and solutioning temperature (Jackson and Reed, 1999; Locq et al., 2000; Huron et al.,  2004; Qiu et al., 2013). To our best knowledge, no existing model estimates grain size from a wide  range of process conditions with an accuracy level that is suitable for a practical alloy design.  Therefore, grain size is left as an input parameter or a target value when predicting mechanical  properties of new alloys. Second, our model cannot consider microstructural changes that occur  during deformation, and therefore should be applied only at 650  ≤   T   ≤  850 °C, in which  microstructural changes are relatively minor. Furthermore, tensile properties are calculated only at  650 °C, because the model parameters necessary for the calculation were determined from  information that was collected at that temperature.

In this section, we discuss the reliability and applicability of our model by comparing the  MANUSCRIPT calculations and experimental information for the seven designed alloys (Table 1) and the commercial  alloys (Table 3). All model parameters except grain size are obtained using a thermodynamics  calculation and a precipitation kinetics simulation, so the numerical model is highly dependent on the  CALPHAD  thermodynamic  calculation  The  thermodynamics software used in this study were Thermo-Calc (database: Ni-DATA (ver.6) (Thermo  Calc, Saunders et al., 2000)) and Jmatpro (Jmatpro); the precipitation kinetics simulation software  was MatCalc (thermodynamic database: Ni-DATA (ver.6) & diffusion database: MC_Ni ver.2.004)  (Saunders et al., 2000; MC_Ni ver.2.004; Kozeschnik et al., 2004; Svoboda et al., 2004; Radis et al.,  2009). The reliability of the thermodynamics calculation has been demonstrated (Kim et al., 2016). In  the present study, the reliability of the precipitation kinetics simulation was evaluated by comparing   precipitates to relevant experimental observations.  

In the seven new alloys considered in the present work (Table 1), the average grain size was 4 \~  5  µ m (Fig. 8). The first area examined to confirm the reliability of the precipitation kinetics simulation was the   precipitates (Fig. 9). In the MatCalc simulation, the dislocation  density was 10 , the number of size classes was 250, and the nucleation site was bulk considering  the homogeneous nucleation. The interfacial energy was estimated from the generalized broken bond  (GBB) approach while taking into account interfacial curvature size effects (Sonderegger and  Kozeschnik, 2009; 2009). The precipitate growth kinetics was simulated with the SFFK mean-field  model (Kozeschnik et al., 2004; Svoboda et al., 2004) for multi-component multi-phase systems. In  addition, the volume misfit was ignored due to the very small volume misfit between  γ  and  γ′ . The  precipitation kinetics simulation reproduced the size and fraction of secondary  γ′  precipitates well.  

To further evaluate the reliability of the precipitation kinetics simulation, experimental  measurements of the size of tertiary  γ′  precipitates was compared with the MatCalc calculation (Fig.  10). In alloy 1, measured size of tertiary  γ′  precipitates was 50 nm to 100 nm, whereas the calculated  mean size was 63.7 nm (Fig. 10a). In alloy 7, the measured sizes of tertiary  γ′  precipitates were 30 nm  to 60 nm, whereas the calculated mean size was 66.4 nm (Fig. 10b). These results confirm that the  calculations agree with the experimental measurements within an error range, and that the  precipitation kinetics simulation can be used for further prediction of tensile properties, creep  resistance, and fatigue damage tolerance.

For the fatigue tests that include dwell time, the fatigue damage tolerance is mostly affected by the  ACCEPTED stress-assisted grain boundary oxidation and the stress-relaxation effect. In experiments, RR1000 had  better fatigue damage tolerance than U720Li (Everitt et al., 2008) (Fig. 15d). Fatigue damage tolerance decreases as grain size decreases, so if the grain size of N18 were the  same as that of SMO48, the fatigue damage tolerance of N18 might be better than that of SMO48  (Telesman et al., 2004; Gao et al., 2005).  

These results confirm that the fatigue damage tolerance model reproduces the relative trend in  fatigue crack growth rate among disk superalloys well, even though this model can not estimate  fatigue crack growth rate directly. It should be noted here that the present model does not consider  grain boundary engineering such as the formation of special boundaries (Gao et al., 2005) and  serration of grain boundaries (Kearsey et al., 2000); this point should be remembered during  application of this model.  

Although our model still has limitations, the present results show that the proposed numerical  model reproduces tensile properties, creep resistance, and fatigue damage tolerance in Ni-base disk  superalloys reasonably well. The numerical model can be used to guide design of new disk  superalloys that have improved mechanical properties. This numerical model has already been used to  develop several disk superalloys, and related patent application and registration are in progress.

Title:  A numerical model to predict mechanical properties for Ni-base disk superalloys  

Authors:  Young-Kwang Kim, Donghoon Kim, Hong-Kyu Kim, Eun-Yoo Yoon, Youngseon Lee, Chang- Seok Oh and Byeong-Joo Lee  

# Highlights  

   We propose a numerical model to predict tensile properties, creep resistance and fatigue damage  tolerance.     The model has been achieved by combining earlier empirical models with modifications.     The model considers various microstructural factors based on deformation mechanisms.     The model enables to predict mechanical properties without any further experimental  measurement due to calculating all model parameters from computational approaches.
