The   behavior   of   phosphorous   and   its   eﬀect   on   the   microstructure   of   a   powder-processed,   high   refractory   content, Ni-based   superalloy   was   studied   and   compared   to   a   P-free   variant.   Transmission   electron   microscopy   and   atom probe   tomography   were   used   to   identify   the   crystal   structure   of   the   ﬁve   topologically   close   packed   (TCP)   and geometrically   close   packed   (GCP)   phases   that   formed,   assess   phase   compositions,   and   determine   any   elemen- tal   segregation   to   phase   boundaries.   The   results   revealed   strong   phosphorous   segregation   to   grain   boundaries, moderate   solubility   in   TCP   and   MC   phases,   limited   solubility   in   the   𝛾 matrix   and   no   solubility   in   the   𝛾 ′   phase or   any   other   Ni 3 X   type   GCP   phases.   The   phosphorus   segregation   to   the   grain   boundaries   of   the   P-doped   alloy lowered   the   incipient   melting   temperature,   thereby   eliminating   the   processing   window,   and   led   to   the   formation of   (Ni,Co,Cr,P) 2 (Nb,Mo)   laves   phases   along   grain   boundaries   during   supersolvus   heat   treatment.   Interestingly, APT   revealed   segregation   of   P   to   Al 2 O 3   oxide   surfaces,   explaining   the   frequent   observation   of   laves   precipitates nucleating   on   such   impurities.   Due   to   the   low   stability   of   the   C14–Ni 2 Nb   laves   phase,   it   was   prone   to   phase separation,   and   served   as   the   nucleation   point   for   𝜇 ,   intergrown   𝜂 / 𝛿 ,   and   Nb 3 P   phosphide   phases.

# 1.   Introduction  

Nickel   based   superalloys   are   the   ideal   material   for   hot   section components   of   modern   gas   turbine   engines   owing   to   their   excellent creep   properties,   high   strength,   and   considerable   fatigue   resistance. These   materials   are   often   subjected   to   corrosive   environments,   high stress   levels,   and   elevated   temperatures   during   service   [1,2] .   As   such, the   composition   of   modern   Ni-based   superalloys   tends   to   be   complex   in order   to   enhance   each   property   and   provide   adequate   resistance   against diﬀerent   failure   modes.   To   achieve   this,   a   variety   of   major   and   minor alloying   elements   are   utilized   to   either   increase   𝛾 matrix   solid-solution strength,   promote   stable   𝛾 ′   phase   formation,   and   suppress   undesired secondary   phase   formation   [3] .   For   polycrystalline   superalloys,   used as   turbine   disks   or   rotors,   grain   boundary   strength   and   cohesion   are tremendously   important,   and   minor   trace   elements,   e.g.,   B,   C   and   Zr, are   utilized   as   these   typically   segregate   to   grain   boundaries   and   have beneﬁcial   eﬀects   [4–6] .  

With   aim   to   achieve   better   properties,   scientists   have   tried   alloying with   a   variety   of   other   elements,   one   of   which   is   phosphorous   [3,7] . The   eﬀect   of   trace   P   amounts   on   the   microstructure   and   resulting material   properties   has   been   somewhat   ambiguous   in   the   past.   Early studies   suggest   that   P   is   a   detrimental   element   causing   grain   boundary

As   newer   generation   polycrystalline   superalloys   typically   utilize higher   levels   of   refractory   elements   to   improve   creep   resistance, fabrication   via   conventional   cast   and   wrought   processing   techniques produces   highly   heterogeneous   microstructures   [1] .   Furthermore, the   processing   window   between   𝛾 ′   formation   and   incipient   melting becomes   signiﬁcantly   reduced,   and   manufacturing   of   components becomes   increasingly   diﬃcult   [21] .   Powder   processing   of   alloys   utilizes rapid   solidiﬁcation   via   spray   atomization,   combined   with   densiﬁcation via   techniques   such   as   hot   isostatic   pressing   and   forging,   leading   to minimized   dendritic   segregation.   This   processing   route   thereby   allows for   a   higher   degree   of   compositional   and   microstructural   homogene- ity   when   compared   to   cast   and   wrought   processing   [22] .   Chemical segregation   still   occurs   despite   the   relatively   shorter   length   scales related   with   solid   state   diﬀusion,   and   excess   interstitial   solutes   that have   limited   solubility   in   the   general   𝛾 / 𝛾 ′   microstructure   segregate   to and   accumulate   along   grain   boundaries.   Since   no   solidiﬁcation   events occur   during   consolidation   of   the   powder   into   the   ﬁnished   forging,   the beneﬁcial   eﬀects   of   phosphorous   additions   might   be   exploited   mean- while   avoiding   the   formation   of   undesired   secondary   phases.   However, there   are   no   studies   on   the   behavior   of   P   in   powder-processed   Ni-based superalloys.   Moreover,   accurate   compositional   and   structural   data   for the   characteristic   P   segregation   behavior   in   cast   and   wrought   alloys   is lacking.   This   knowledge   gap   prevents   eﬀective   utilization   of   phospho- rous   additions   for   creep   property   improvements   despite   studies   showing a   clear   beneﬁt   in   the   cast   and   wrought   alloys.   Furthermore,   thermo- dynamic   software   databases   based   on   the   CALPHAD   approach,   such   as those   of   Thermo-Calc   and   Pandat,   currently   do   not   include   P   due   to   the limited   information   for   this   element.   This   study   aims   to   contribute   to   an improved   understanding   of   the   complex   thermodynamics   and   kinetics in   multicomponent   Ni-based   superalloys   by   elucidating   the   partitioning behavior   of   phosphorous   and   investigating   the   microstructural   eﬀects of   its   addition   to   powder-processed   Ni-based   superalloys.  

# 2.   Experimental   procedures  

Two   experimental   alloys   with   nominally   identical   compositions, except   for   400   ppm   phosphorous   addition   to   one   of   them,   were   used   in this   study,   and   their   compositions   are   provided   in   Table   1 .   For   simplic- ity,   the   phosphorous-free   and   phosphorous-containing   alloys   will   also be   referred   to   as   P0   and   P4   throughout   the   manuscript.   The   diﬀerence in   their   composition   is   also   provided   in   Table   1 .   Both   experimental alloys   were   fabricated   using   nominally   identical   powder   metallurgy processing   parameters,   where   spray   atomized   powder   was   sieved, canned   and   consolidated   via   hot   isostatic   pressing   (HIP).   Following densiﬁcation to form a ∼10 kg billet, the material was isothermallyforged   at   1100   °C   to   form   a   pancake.   Subsequently,   specimens   were cut   from   the   forged   pancake   and   supersolvus   solution   heat   treated at   1150   °C   for   1   h,   followed   by   a   controlled   cool   at   1   °C/s,   in   an   MTI KSL-1500X   muﬄe   furnace.   Additional   samples   from   P4   were   also subsolvus   heat   treated   at   1120   °C   for   1   h,   followed   by   a   controlled   cool at   1   °C/s.   All   samples   were   then   aged   at   850   °C   for   4   h   and   air-cooled.  

Metallographic   samples   were   prepared   using   standard   grinding   and polishing   techniques   to   a   ﬁnal   polish   with   a   solution   of   0.06   μm   colloidal silica   and   observed   under   back-scattered   electron   (BSE)   contrast   in either   a   JEOL   JSM-6701F   ﬁeld   emission   scanning   electron   microscope (FESEM)   or   a   Zeiss   Supra   55   FESEM,   both   operating   at   an   accelerat- ing   voltage   of   15   kV.   The   Zeiss   Supra   55   FESEM   is   equipped   with   a Thermo-Fisher   x-ray   energy   dispersive   spectroscopy   (EDS)   detector and   an   Oxford   Instruments   electron   back-scattered   diﬀraction   detector

 (EBSD).   This   set-up   was   also   used   for   transmission   kikuchi   diﬀraction

 (TKD)   on   transmission   electron   microscopy   (TEM)   samples   with   a   30   kV accelerating   voltage   in   10   μm   ×  6   μm   regions   and   a   step   size   of   0.025   μm.  

For   phase   identiﬁcation,   thin   foils   for   TEM   observation   were prepared   by   twin-jet   thinning   in   a   solution   of   7%   perchloric   acid and 93% ethanol at 248 K ( − 25 °C) and 40 V. A FEI Technai G 2   F30                             TEM   operating   at   300   kV   was   used   for   observation   and   selective   area diﬀraction   (SAD),   while   a   JEOL2010F   TEM   operating   at   200   kV   was used   for   elemental   mapping,   line   scans   and   point   analysis.   Finally,   local phase   chemistries   were   evaluated   using   location   speciﬁc   atom   probe tomography   (APT)   samples   fabricated   with   a   FEI   Helios   Nanolabs   600 microscope   equipped   with   an   electron   gun   and   a   Ga   focused   ion   beam (FIB). The microtips were polished with Ga +   ions down to a ∼ 50 nm                       radius   using   a   ﬁnal   energy   of   5   kV   and   a   current   of   41   pA.   The   APT samples   were   analyzed   using   a   LEAP® 4000X   Si   under   a   base   tempera- ture   of   34   K,   laser   pulse   energy   of   25   pJ,   250   kHz   laser   pulse   repetition rate,   and   detection   rates   of   up   to   1.5%.   The   data   were   reconstructed and   analyzed   using   IVAS   3.8.12   from   Cameca   Instruments   Inc.

# 3.2.   Atom   probe   tomography  

Atom   probe   tomography   was   used   to   study   the   characteristic   parti- tioning   of   elements   in   the   subsolvus   and   supersolvus   heat   treated   P4   al- loy,   and   representative   3D   reconstructions   are   provided   in   Fig.   4 a   and   b, respectively.   Only   Cr   (blue)   and   Al   (red)   atoms   are   displayed   due   to   their strong   partitioning   to   the   𝛾 and   𝛾 ′   phase,   respectively,   along   with   a   14 at.%   Cr   iso concentration   surface   delineating   the   𝛾 ′   precipitates   from   the 𝛾 matrix.   Three   distinct   populations   of   𝛾 ′ ,   based   on   size,   exist   in   the   sub- solvus   heat   treated   P4   sample,   i.e.,   primary   𝛾 ′ ,   secondary   𝛾 ′   and   tertiary 𝛾 ′ ,   while   only   secondary   𝛾 ′   and   tertiary   𝛾 ′   exist   in   the   supersolvus   heat treated   P4.   Additionally,   some   𝛾 phase   precipitates   were   also   observed inside   larger   𝛾 ′   regardless   of   heat   treatment,   which   is   related   to   the   fast 𝛾 ′   growth   kinetics   and   slow   diﬀusion   of   certain   elements   [28,29] .  

Proximity   histograms   were   created   for   each   interface,   and   rep- resentative   plots   for   secondary   𝛾 ′ / 𝛾 interfaces   of   the   subsolvus   and supersolvus   treated   P4   samples   are   given   in   Fig.   5 a   and   b,   respectively.   It should   be   noted   that   mass-to-charge   ratio   peak   overlaps   were   corrected for   based   on   the   natural   abundancies   of   each   isotope   of   an   element   [30] . Typical   partitioning   behavior   can   be   observed,   where   Ni,   Al,   Nb   and   Ta partition   to   the   𝛾 ′ ,   while   Cr,   Co,   Mo   and   W   partition   to   the   𝛾 matrix. Meanwhile,   P   has   a   slight   partitioning   tendency   towards   the   𝛾 phase in   both   cases.   Comparing   the   two   heat   treatment   conditions,   not   much diﬀerence   can   be   observed   between   the   subsolvus   and   supersolvus   in terms   of   partitioning   behavior   of   elements,   besides   the   slightly   higher enrichment   of   W,   Al,   P   and   B   at   the   𝛾 / 𝛾 ′   interface   in   the   subsolvus sample.  

The   composition   of   each   phase   in   the   APT   sharp   specimens   was obtained   by   averaging   over   the   plateau   regions   away   from   the   interface and   excluding   the   tertiary   𝛾 ′ ;   the   results   are   tabulated   in   Table   2 . For   the   three   𝛾 ′   populations   only   minor   diﬀerences   can   be   observed, primarily   in   the   lower   Cr   content   in   the   secondary   𝛾 ′   – likely   due   to the   formation   of   secondary   𝛾 precipitates   – and   slightly   higher   Nb content   in   the   tertiary   𝛾 ′ .   It   has   previously   been   reported   that   these alloys   have   a   higher   Nb   concentration   in   the   𝛾 matrix   [23] ,   hence   the higher   Al   and   Nb   content   in   these   tertiary   𝛾 ′   precipitates,   which   form from   the   supersaturated   matrix   during   aging   [31,32] . # 4.   Discussion  

Trace   elements   are   often   added   to   Ni-based   superalloys   in   hope   of improving   the   creep   resistance   and/or   microstructural   stability   of   the alloy   [4,5,36] .   However,   more   often   than   not,   limited   information   is available   on   the   exact   behavior   of   these   minor   alloying   additions,   e.g.   B, C,   P,   and   engineers   typically   rely   on   best   practice   principles   rather   than thermodynamic   calculations   to   determine   the   content   added.   With   the gaining   popularity   of   Integrated   Computational   Materials   Engineering (ICME),   the   knowledge   pertaining   to   the   behavior   of   trace   elements and   the   compositional/structural   characteristics   of   various   phases needs   to   be   improved   for   proper   microstructural   simulation.   Comparing thermodynamic   calculations   from   packages   such   as   Pandat,   Fig.   1 ,   to experimentally   measured   results,   Table   2 ,   suggests   that   these   software are   relatively   reliable   for   alloy   development,   but   signiﬁcant   diﬀerences arise   when   there   is   a   lack   of   thermodynamic   and   chemical   data, especially   in   the   compositional   range   of   novel   high   refractory   content superalloys.   This   creates   a   need   for   high   quality   data,   which   can   popu- late   databases   and   enable   improved   modeling   of   phase   stability   [37] .   As compositional   data   related   to   phosphorous   additions   and   the   subsequent formation   of   phases   associated   with   its   presence   is   limited,   this   element is   typically   excluded   from   databases.   In   hopes   of   elucidating   the   behav- ior   of   P   additions   in   powder   processed   high   refractory   content   Ni-based superalloys,   a   detailed   investigation   was   carried   out   on   the   eﬀect   of 0.04   wt.%   phosphorus   additions   to   an   experimental   Ni-based   superalloy.

This   enrichment is   likely   due   to   the   incoherent   interface   between   the   oxide   particle   and matrix/grain   boundary,   which   will   act   as   preferred   nucleation   site,   as the   laves   phases   exhibit   high   solubility   for   phosphorous,   Table   4 ,   and   act as   a   sink   for   the   nearby   P   atoms. It   is   generally   known   that   they   exist   in   the   form   of (Ni,   Cr,   Fe) 2 (Mo,   Nb)   and   have   an   increased   solubility   of   P,   however, it   is   important   to   note   that   the   Ni 2 Nb   is   not   a   stable   phase   in   the Ni–Nb   or   Ni–Cr–Nb   systems   [47,48] .   As   such,   this   phase   is   rarely reported   in   the   literature   of   superalloys   that   do   not   contain   Fe,   which stabilizes   the   C14–Fe 2 Nb   variant   in   alloys   such   as   IN718,   718Plus,   and GH417   [49] .   In   a   previous   report   aimed   at   elucidating   the   laves   phase formation   in   the   P4   alloy,   it   was   shown   using   density   functional   theory calculations   that   phosphorous   can   in   fact   stabilize   the   C14   laves   phase in   the   absence   of   Fe   [25] .   The   calculations   further   suggested   that   Ni anti-sites   and   Al   additions   can   also   stabilize   this   phase,   which   is   at   ﬁrst counter intuitive   -   Al   has   no   solubility   in   the   phase,   Table   4 ,   and   Ni 2 Nb is   not   a   stable   phase   in   the   absence   of   Fe   as   discussed   above.   Taking a   closer   look   at   the   Ni–Nb   and   Ni–Nb–Cr   phase   diagrams   it   becomes more   obvious   that   such   additions   would   stabilize   and   transform   the Ni 2 Nb   laves   phase   to   the   more   stable   Ni 3 Nb   or   Ni 6 AlNb   phases.   From Figs.   9   and   11   it   becomes   obvious   that   this   is   a   viable   transformation pathway   as   intergrown   Ni 6 AlNb/Ni 3 Nb   can   be   seen   to   form   from   larger laves   precipitates.   Krakow   et   al.   [35]   performed   detailed   transmission electron   microscopy   study   of   TCP   phases   forming   in   the   Fe-containing commercial   ATI718Plus®,   and   found   that   C14   laves   and   𝜎 phase nucleate   from   existing   𝜂 phase   precipitates;   they   further   character- ized   all   of   the   crystallographic   orientation   relationships   between   the phases.  

In   the   present   study,   it   was   observed   that   the   𝜂 and   𝜇 (similar   to   𝜎 ) form   from   the   C14   laves,   as   it   forms   ﬁrst.   This   intergrowth   of   phases suggests   that   this   laves   phase   has   a   relatively   low   stability   and   is   subject to   form   regions   of   diﬀerent   composition   as   it   grows,   as   shown   in   Fig.   2 , and   due   to   its   high   refractory   content   can   act   as   a   nucleation   site   for various   TCP   and   GCP   phases.   As   the   Ni-rich   phases   form   in   the   Ni 2 Nb laves   phase,   the   phosphorous   content   is   likely   further   concentrated   due to   its   low   solubility   in   𝜂 and   𝛿 ,   Fig.   10 ,   leading   to   P   rich   zones   in   the vicinity   of   these   phases   and   the   eventual   formation   of   Nb 3 P   phosphides, Fig.   11 .   This   phosphide   phase   was   also   observed   in   laves   precipitates with   no   Ni 6 AlNb/Ni 3 Nb   needle   precipitates,   suggesting   that   in   a   P   and Nb   rich   environment   it   may   be   more   stable   than   the   low   stability   laves phase.   This   sort   of   environment   is   easily   created   by   the   laves   phase which   initially   acts   as   a   sink   for   P   and   Nb   atoms,   but   subsequently   starts to   enhance   local   concentration   ﬂuctuations   due   to   its   low   stability, Fig.   2 .   It   should   be   noted   that   up   to   now   this   phosphide   phase   has inaccurately   been   identiﬁed   as   Nb   rich   Ni 3 P   [12,13] ,   or   just   reported as   M 3 P   with   no   crystal   structure   measurement   [15,18] ,   in   cast   and wrought   alloys.   Detailed   TEM   characterization   reveals   that   this   phase   is rather   a   Ni-rich   Nb 3 P   with   a   tetragonal   crystal   structure   – present   in   the Ni–Nb–P   phase   diagram   [50] .   To   the   best   of   the   author’s   knowledge, this   is   the   ﬁrst   report   of   such   phase   in   a   Ni-based   superalloy.   This phase   could   potentially   be   used   to   improve   mechanical   the   properties of   a   Ni–Fe–Nb–P   type   laves   phase   system   in   a   similar   manner   to   the reported   improvement   of   fracture   toughness   of   the   C14   Fe 2 (Ti,   Mo) laves   phase   by   the   precipitation   of   ductile   A2   (Mo,   Ti)   precipitates   [51] .  

Experimental   observations   and   DFT   calculations   for   simple   alloy systems   show   the   stability   of   C14- 𝜇 phase   intergrowth,   which   is reported   to   have   very   detrimental   eﬀects   on   creep   properties   [46,52] . This   sort   of   compositional   and   structural   non-uniformity   can   be   seen   in Fig.   2 ,   and   C14- 𝜇 phase   intergrowth   can   be   observed   in   Fig.   6 .   The   C14 laves   and   𝜇 phase   form   using   similar   elements,   however   they   possess diﬀerent   crystal   structures.   Table   4   reveals   that   the   main   diﬀerence   in composition   between   the   two   is   the   higher   Mo   and   W   content   in   the 𝜇 phase   in   lieu   of   Co   and   Cr.   This   transformation   makes   sense   as   it transitions   from   the   A 2 B   laves   chemistry   to   A 7 B 6 𝜇 chemistry,   where   A   is   typically   Co,   Cr,   Ni,   Fe,   and   B   is   Mo,   W,   Nb,   respectively.   Since   both Ni 7 Mo 6 and   Ni 7 Nb 6 are   stable   𝜇 phases   in   the   Ni–Mo–Cr   [53]   and   Ni–     Nb–Cr   [48,54]   phase   diagrams,   respectively,   it   is   not   surprising   that   the Nb   content   did   not   decrease,   but   instead   the   Mo   increases,   substituting for   Co   and   Cr,   i.e.,   A   type   atoms,   Table   4 .   The   A 7 B 6 chemistry   would   give   a   54:46   ratio   for   the   ideal   structure,   while   calculating   the   actual ratio   from   Table   4 ,   assuming   a   (Ni,Co,Cr) x (Mo,Nb,W,Ta) y site   occupa-   tion,   gives   a   55:42   ratio,   with   the   remaining   elements   unaccounted   for. This   close   agreement   between   the   ideal   and   calculated   ratio   provides a   high   degree   of   certainty   that   this   is   in   fact   the   rhombohedral   𝜇 phase,   with   the   diﬀerence   accounting   for   the   non-equilibrium   state   of the   structure.   Interestingly,   there   seems   to   be   no   segregation   to   the interface,   suggesting   at   least   semi-coherency   between   the   two   phases. Finally,   when   compared   to   the   𝛾 ′ ,   𝜂 and   𝛿 phases,   this   phase   also   has solubility   for   phosphorous,   although   lower   than   that   of   the   C14   laves. From   this   observation   it   can   be   deduced   that   Ni 3 X   type   GCP   phases   have no   solubility   for   phosphorous   in   contrast   to   TCP   phases   such   as   𝜇 and laves.   TCP   phases   have   a   much   higher   atomic   packing   density   than   GCP phases,   and   are   typically   characterized   by   distorted   atoms   in   distorted lattices,   rather   than   as   arranged   “hard   spheres ” of   the   latter   [55,56] . Introducing   a   small   atom   like   phosphorous   in   the   lattice   can   alleviate some   the   lattice   strains,   and   would   be   favorable   as   long   as   it   does   not push   the   composition   outside   of   the   preferred   atomic   volume   ratio range   of   the   TCP   phase.   This   can   also   explain   the   diﬀerent   solubility   of P   in   the   laves   and   𝜇 phases,   as   the   former   has   an   even   higher   packing density   and   preference   for   much   bigger   atomic   volume   mismatch.  

# 4.3.   Implications   for   mechanical   properties  

The   formation   of   TCP   phases   depletes   the   𝛾 ′   forming   elements   such as   Nb,   as   well   as   𝛾 solid   solution   elements   such   as   Mo   -   leading   to   matrix softening,   which   is   considered   deleterious   to   properties.   The   brittle   na- ture   of   the   these   phases   can   often   lead   to   increased   crack   initiation   sites, reduced   ductility   and   toughness,   and   poor   fatigue   and   tensile   properties [57] .   Although   mechanical   properties   were   not   in   the   scope   of   this study,   a   few   remarks   should   be   made   about   the   formation   of   secondary TCP   and   GCP   phases   in   superalloys   and   their   detrimental   nature,   which would   potentially   outweigh   the   beneﬁt   of   the   phosphorous   grain   bound- ary   strengthening.   As   novel   powder   metallurgy   processed   superalloys contain   high   levels   of   refractory   elements,   often   close   to   the   solubility limits,   these   alloys   are   prone   to   micro segregation   and   formation   of   TCP phases   due   to   the   high   alloying   content   present   in   the   𝛾 – 𝛾 ′   microstruc- ture,   typically   close   to   the   solubility   limits.   Furthermore,   their   high content   of   𝛾 ′   forming   elements   pushes   their   𝛾 ′   solvus   temperature   close to   the   solidus   of   the   alloy,   Table   2 ,   minimizing   the   processing   window. Any   further   reduction   of   this   processing   window   should   be   carefully   con- sidered.   Adding   phosphorous   to   superalloys   has   been   reported   to   lead   to improved   creep   properties   due   to   better   grain   boundary   cohesion,   how- ever,   cast   and   wrought   alloys   typically   have   larger   processing   windows and   a   slight   sacriﬁces   in   lowering   the   incipient   melting   temperature   can be   justiﬁed   by   the   improved   properties.   Furthermore,   these   alloys   have low   content   of   refractory   element   and   the   propensity   to   form   TCP   phases is   low   due   to   the   relatively   high   stability   of   the   general   microstructure. In   summary,   the   eﬀect   of   400   ppm   (by   weight)   phosphorous   ad- ditions   to   an   experimental   powder   processed   superalloy   with   high refractory   content   was   systematically   investigated.   It   was   found   that   P only   slightly   aﬀects   the   general   𝛾 – 𝛾 ′   microstructure   – mainly   a   slight reduction   in   the   𝛾 liquidus   was   observed,   and   tends   to   segregate   to   grain boundaries.   The   reduction   of   the   incipient   melting   temperature,   due   to the   grain   boundary   enrichment   of   phosphorous,   leads   to   a   nonexistent processing   window,   and   the   grain   boundary   eutectic   formed   during supersolvus   annealing   leads   to   laves   phase   formation.   Due   to   the   low stability   of   the   laves   phase,   it   further   promotes   the   formation   of   local compositional   heterogeneity   within   is   as   it   becomes   bigger   and   serves as   the   origination   point   of   various   TCP   and   GCP   phases,   which   were characterized   via   atom   probe   tomography   and   transmission   electron microscopy.   One   of   these   secondary   phases   is   the   Ni   rich   Nb 3 P,   which   is reported   for   the   ﬁrst   time   in   Ni-based   superalloys.   These   ﬁndings   suggest that   caution   should   be   used   when   considering   phosphorous   additions   to powder-processed   superalloys,   as   these   alloys   have   relatively   lower   sta- bility   than   their   cast   and   wrought   counterparts,   leading   to   the   propen- sity   to   form   deleterious   phases,   that   can   outweigh   the   beneﬁcial   eﬀects.  

# 5.   Conclusions  

An   experimental   powder   processed   Ni-based   superalloy   was   doped with   400   ppm   of   phosphorus,   due   to   the   reported   creep   property improvements   associated   with   this   trace   element   in   cast   and   wrought superalloys.   The   partitioning   behavior   and   eﬀect   on   the   overall   mi- crostructural   stability   was   systematically   characterized   at   the   atom-scale via   atom   probe   tomography   and   electron   microscopy.   Based   on   the   ob- servations   and   analysis   of   data,   the   following   conclusions   can   be   drawn:  

1.   In   the   general   𝛾 – 𝛾 ′   microstructure,   phosphorous   has   slight   preferen- tial   partitioning   to   the   𝛾 matrix   and   no   solubility   in   the   𝛾 ′   phase.   This leads   to   a   slight   decrease   in   the   liquidus   temperature   and   no   change in   the   𝛾 ′   solvus.   Negligible   segregation   can   be   observed   to   the   𝛾 - secondary   𝛾 ′   interface   only   in   the   subsolvus   heat   treated   sample. Finally,   some   P   also   partitions   to   the   NbC,   and   increases   its   stability.

 2.   Due   to   the   limited   solubility   inside   the   𝛾 – 𝛾 ′   grains,   phosphorous mainly   segregates   to   the   grain   boundary,   particularly   triple   junc- tions   and   impurity   interfaces   – due   to   the   higher   space   availability. This   signiﬁcantly   reduces   the   incipient   melting   temperature,   causing a   decrease   in   the   processing   window.  

 3.   Low   stability   laves   phase   forms   from   the   eutectic   during   supersolvus heat   treatment,   attracting   the   phosphorous   atoms   in   the   vicinity. The   laves   precipitates   further   phase   decomposes   into   various   TCP and   GCP   phases,   such   as   𝜂 ,   𝛿 ,   and   𝜇 .   The   GCP   Ni 3 X   phases   exhibit no   solubility   for   phosphorous,   while   the   TCP   phase   P   solubility   is dependent   on   the   atomic   packing.  

 4.   A   Ni-rich   Nb 3 P   phase   with   P4 2 /n   tetragonal   space   group,   where a   =   10.98   Å and   c   =   5.55   Å,   was   characterized   and   reported   for   the ﬁrst   time   in   superalloys.   This   phase   forms   inside   low   stability   laves precipitates,   which   provide   a   P   and   Nb   rich   environment.  

5.   Phosphorous   segregates   to   the   interface   of   Al 2 O 3 inclusions,   likely   formed   during   VIM   processing,   which   can   then   act   as   laves nucleation   sites.  

6.   The   400   ppm   P   additions   to   an   experimental   powder-processed superalloys   reduce   its   processing   window   to   unrealistic   levels   and led   to   the   formation   of   5   additional   phases.   The   positive   eﬀect   of phosphorous   on   the   creep   resistance   of   the   alloy   is   likely   outweighed by   the   brittle   nature   of   these   TCP   and   GCP   phases.
