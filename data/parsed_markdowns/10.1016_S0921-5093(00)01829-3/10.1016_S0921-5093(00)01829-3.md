# Abstract  

This paper is concerned with modelling and prediction of the stress rupture properties of several single crystal superalloys. The stress rupture theory is based on a modiﬁed form of the damage mechanics equations of Kachanov and Rabotnov. The ultimate tensile strength (UTS) is included and acts as an upper bound in these equations, which corrects for the fact that failure should occur when the effective stress reaches the UTS, rather than inﬁnity, and allows the curvature in stress rupture data to be modelled effectively. The interpretation of damage on a microscopic scale is discussed. The time–temperature behaviour of these alloys is shown to obey a stress modiﬁed Sherby–Dorn parameter. A consideration is made for the changing strength of the material with temperature by comparing rupture data at constant fractions of the UTS rather than at constant stress. The activation energy used in the Sherby–Dorn parameter is similar to that for self-diffusion of nickel. By basing the rupture model on physical properties (namely the UTS and activation energy for diffusion), predictions of rupture behaviour of new alloys can be made when these corresponding physical properties are determined. However, such predictions of behaviour can only be made accurately between alloys of relatively similar composition. A major distinction is shown to exist between non-rhenium containing ﬁrst generation alloys, and alloys containing rhenium, due to the increased rupture strength and different rafting kinetics of the latter. © 2001

# 1. Introduction  

Single crystal Ni base superalloys have been in use for nearly 40 years as materials for turbine blades in jet engines. The development of these alloys has been such that we have now reached a third generation in their evolution. Different alloy generations are convention- ally discriminated by their rhenium content, ﬁrst gener- ation alloys having no rhenium whilst second and third generation alloys have approximately 3 and 6 wt.% rhenium, respectively.  

Superalloys in the form of single crystals contain a regular embedded phase of cuboidal precipitates of the g %  L1 2  structure within an FCC matrix, this two-phase microstructure results in complex deformation mecha- nisms [1–5]. There are additional complexities in ex- plaining and modelling the properties of single crystal superalloys. For example, macroscopic deformation and microscopic mechanisms are effected by relatively small changes in creep conditions in CMSX-4 [6,7]. Transient creep yielding strains of up to 6% occurs at relatively low temperature (750°C) and high stresses, although as the temperature is raised by only 100– 200°C or the stress lowered these transients disappear [6,7]. Furthermore, the microstructure is stable below approximately 900–950°C (depending on the speciﬁc alloy), but above these temperatures it can form a rafted structure of  g %  platelets. The effect of such mor- phological changes can either increase or decrease creep resistance depending on the test conditions [8–12].  

As a result of these issues, as well as the anisotropy and inherently complex nature of creep and dislocation motion in the  g / g %  structure, the mechanical properties of single crystal superalloys have been the subject of numerous scientiﬁc investigations. Such works have made signiﬁcant progress in rationalising and modelling

However, many of these models are highly speciﬁc to one alloy and mech- anism and have not reached a stage where they can be used for component modelling and design where differ- ent conditions and mechanisms are encountered. # 3. Analysis and results  

# 3.1.  Stress rupture and damage mechanics  

The theory of damage mechanics was developed by Kachanov and Rabotnov in the 1960s and has since found widespread application in the analysis of stress rupture and creep of a range of materials and structures [15]. At a macroscopic level damage due to creep or stress rupture includes all effects occurring during creep loading that result in the material changing from its initial condition. This includes macroscopic effects such as cracking and microscopic effects such as deformation and microstructural changes. Different mechanisms of damage are differentiated both in the function used to describe how they evolve and the function used to describe how they effect material properties.  

Kachanov [16] proposed a function for brittle vis- coplastic damage occurring during creep of polycrys- talline alloys. The evolution of damage is such that the damage rate was a function of the damage state vari- able  v , and the test conditions.  

If damage is manifested as a loss of section due to the growth of cracks and cavities it can be quantiﬁed as v = A v / A , where  A  and  A v  are the original cross sectional area and the area lost due to damage, respec- tively. The stress acting on the effective remaining area ( A eff = A − A v ) is called the effective stress and is given by  s eff = F / A eff = s / (1 − v ). The principal of strain equivalence assumes that the effect of stress acting on material at a certain damage state is equivalent to the effective stress acting on undamaged material. Using this principle, damage can be associated with a change in material properties, which may be due to loss of sectional area, but may also be due to weakening of the material, such that the effect of the change in properties is equivalent to the effect of increasing the nominal stress on virgin material to the level of effective stress on damaged material.

# 3.2.  Mechanisms of damage in modern single crystal superalloys  

The most appropriate and widespread description of damage during high temperature creep of metals is the growth of cavities on grain boundaries, this represents a truly continuous process, i.e. on a very small scale and distributed evenly throughout the material. However, such a mechanism implies that the grain boundaries are responsible for failure. Developments in casting tech- nology over the past three decades have led to the possibility for removal of grain boundaries and the introduction of single crystal alloys into service. A resultant re-deﬁnition of damage at the macroscopic level is needed for certain applications such as single crystal turbine blades.

Internal cracking form casting porosity has previ- ously been used as a damage mechanism in the mod- elling of creep of single crystal superalloys [18]. Whilst this is clearly a damaging process, it occurs very late in life, and it is not obvious if it is responsible for the increase in strain rate occurring during tertiary creep, or a result of the high strains and strain rates encoun- tered. Additionally, the distribution and size of casting pores in a single crystal, and subsequently of microc- racks, is not on the same ﬁne scale associated with truly continuous damage processes such as grain boundary cavitation. For these reasons it is thought that internal cracking from casting porosity should not be consid- ered a primary cause of damage at the microstructural level.  

To determine how such alloys are damaged by strain accumulation it is necessary to consider the mechanisms of creep ﬂow and its effect on the microstructure. Whilst neither phase of the  g / g %  microstructure is partic- ularly resistant to creep deformation at high tempera- tures [1], the  g %  phase is signiﬁcantly stronger than the  g phase in plastic deformation. The interface between the two phases also presents a major obstacle to dislocation motion during creep. As a consequence of these proper- ties, on application of load at high temperature creep ﬂow is initiated in the matrix phase but is unable to extend into the precipitates. As deformation progresses in the matrix a network of interfacial dislocations forms between the two phases [4,19], this interface is resistant to further deformation and helps prevent matrix dislo- cations penetrating the precipitates.

Such two phase ﬂow has been analysed in detail by Mu ¨ller et al. [20] who used a ﬁnite element model to analyse ﬂow of a matrix undergoing isotropic creep around elastic / perfect plastic precipitates. They showed that as the system is allowed to deform the von Mises stresses in the channels relax at the expense of those in the precipitate. The yield strength of the precipitate was ﬁrst reached, at a point close to the top and bottom edges of the precipitate and near a vertical channel. Two situations were studied, in one the stressing was sufﬁcient to cause yielding of the whole  g %  precipitate and in the other yielding only occurred in distinct regions of the precipitate. Their work clearly shows that in certain situations, load shedding is sufﬁcient to cause precipitate yield, and does not provide the strengthen- ing associated with load shedding from the weaker to the stronger phase in other composite structures. Sev- eral studies (e.g. 4, 7) have shown that at high tempera- tures ( \ 950°C) the precipitates are resistant to deformation up to high strains and prior to tertiary creep, whilst the onset of tertiary creep itself may be associated with precipitate shear. It is likely that, as well as diffusional processes, the load shedding dis- cussed above plays a signiﬁcant contribution to the onset of precipitate shear and tertiary creep. Additional stresses may also build up in the precipitate close to the interface due to constraint and the fact that after the formation of a stable dislocation network at the precip- itate boundary the interface is effectively non-sliding.  

This gives a macroscopic view of how, during stable creep deformation, the matrix channels stop carrying load, and effectively become damaged or weakened whilst stresses build up in the precipitates eventually leading to precipitate shear. The mechanisms causing damage, tertiary creep and failure in modern single crystal superalloys can be thus summarised.  

1. Load redistribution occurs from the creeping matrix onto the initially non-deforming precipitates.

 2. Local stresses build up due to constraint at the interface.

 3. The stress distribution resulting from these pro- cesses eventually leads to precipitate shear.

 4. Internal cracking from casting porosity, which is very evident in failed specimens, may be a contribut- ing factor to creep damage late in life or may simply be a secondary consequence of the large strains encountered before failure.

# 4.2.  Effect of rafting  

As temperature is increased the cuboidal precipitates become morphologically unstable and, for alloys with negative misﬁt, when a tensile load is applied in a  001   crystallographic direction they raft to form platelets perpendicular to the load axis. Depending on conditions of stress and temperature, rafting may have either a detrimental or beneﬁcial effect on creep be- haviour [8–12]. These conditions can broadly be classiﬁed as those under which the precipitates are sheared and under they are not sheared, respectively [12]. Under conditions of constant load and tempera- ture where the rupture life is sufﬁciently long to allow rafting, the morphological change appears to increase creep resistance [8,9]. This is necessarily because during constant load and temperature testing if sufﬁcient time exists for rafting to occur the stress must be relatively low which reduces the possibility of precipitate shear. If, however, a specimen is crept at high temperature and low stress, until fully rafted, and then the tempera- ture reduced and load increased to conditions where the precipitates are sheared, the lower temperature resis- tance is usually reduced compared with the cuboidal structure [11,12,25].  

The results presented here fall into the constant load and temperature category where the precipitates are not sheared and where rafting seems to strengthen the material. Most situations where rafting reduces creep properties have been seen during stress / temperature change experiments as described above, it is clear that such a weakening phenomenon would need to be in- cluded in any model capable of describing such multi- stage creep tests.  

It was discussed earlier that a signiﬁcant source of creep strain and damage in single crystal superalloys arises due to ﬂow of the weaker matrix phase between and around the precipitates (for loading conditions where the precipitates are not sheared). As a direct result of rafting this damage process is hindered and the material strengthens. Our analysis shows that the rela- tive creep resistance of the rafted structure increases above that of the non-rafted structure at lower temper- atures. Fig. 9 shows optimised values of the parameter C  for CMSX-4 at individual temperatures. As discussed in the previous section  C  follows a Boltzmann tempera- ture function, but only up to approximately 1000– 1050°C (for CMSX-4) where rafting becomes signiﬁcant. Above this temperature the parameter  C decreases due to the strengthening effect of rafting. This strengthening mechanism can be viewed in a number of ways. The number of vertical gamma channels has been reduced by a certain fraction, as has the ability for ﬂow and load redistribution, similarly the average horizontal distance a matrix dislocation must travel in order to reach a vertical channel has increased [8,9].

It is interesting to comment on the difference be- tween the two parameters sets obtained for ﬁrst genera- tion alloys, and second and third generation alloys. The two parameter sets used for RR2000 and SRR99, and CMSX-4 and CMSX-10 are given in Table 3. The activation energy for damage accumulation ( D H 1  in Eq. (15)), is the same for both (278 kJ mol − 1 ), and is very similar to the activation energy for self-diffusion of nickel, suggesting plastic ﬂow due to dislocation motion is controlled by diffusion and dislocation climb. The pre-exponential constant,  A , is larger for the second and third generation alloys which results in their in- creased strength over the ﬁrst generation alloys (i.e. lower damage accumulation and creep rates). Also, the activation energy for attenuation of matrix ﬂow is quite low for the ﬁrst generation alloys   190 kJ mol − 1 whilst for the second generation alloys it is the same as that for damage accumulation (278 kJ mol − 1 ). It should be emphasised that rhenium has two effects in the microstructure, one is to increase overall creep resistance through increasing the solid solution resis- tance of the matrix and the other is through changing the rafting kinetics.  

The reason that  D H 2  is lower than  D H 1  in non-rhe- nium containing alloys may be because the rate of rafting and, therefore, attenuation of creep rate is con- trolled by diffusion along the interface rather than diffusion through bulk material. It is known that dur- ing the rafting process rhenium, in second and third generation alloys, sits near the interface [26], which is a possible reason that  D H 2  is higher for rhenium contain- ing alloys than for ﬁrst generation alloys.  

In this study, we have analysed the tensile and stress rupture properties of four different single crystal super- alloys of similar composition; these are RR2000, SRR99, CMSX-4 and CMSX-10. The ﬁrst two are ﬁrst generation alloys, whilst the latter two are second and third generation, respectively. The current turnaround time for the development of new alloys is often pro- hibitively large. This is largely due to the iterative process involved in selecting and reﬁning alloy compo- sitions, casting a certain amount of the selected compo- sition and performing sequences of stability and mechanical tests. Depending on the results of such tests the composition may be further reﬁned or, if the initial tests are encouraging, further tests, analysis and quan- tiﬁcation of properties are performed. This procedure typically goes through ﬁve iterations in the develop- ment of a single new alloy.  

Obviously the simple requirement of time makes creep and stress rupture testing difﬁcult to tie in with the above procedure. Various techniques are available for predicting thermal and kinetic stabilities from initial compositions, but there are very few reliable techniques for predicting creep strength from compositional infor- mation. In the stress rupture model presented herein the stress rupture properties are based on physical alloy properties with the objective of enabling the correlation and prediction of stress rupture behaviour of different alloys and compositions. The physical properties that have been used, are the tensile strength at temperature and activation energies for diffusion in nickel and pre- exponential constants. Obviously a certain amount of testing is required to determine tensile strengths but this is minimal in comparison to creep testing. The predic- tion of tensile strength is a subject which has received considerably more attention than predicting rupture strength, furthermore models exist which successfully provide estimates of tensile strength on the basis of composition alone [27,28]. By combining such models for predicting tensile strength with models like that developed herein it is hoped to extend the range of material properties which can be estimated from poten- tial alloy compositions, thus optimising the design process.  

# 6. Conclusions  

A modiﬁed damage mechanics approach has been developed to model the stress rupture properties of modern single crystal superalloys. Damage occurs due to ﬂow of the weaker matrix phase around the  g % precipitates. As this occurs stress is re-distributed onto the precipitates and stress gradients build up due to constraint between the two phases. Eventually stresses build up sufﬁciently to alloy  g %  shear, which ultimately leads to tertiary creep and failure.  

By including the tensile strength in the damage me- chanics equations it has been shown how the curvature in stress rupture data can be modelled. Failure occurs when either the effective stress or the applied stress reach the UTS, which enables the continuity between tensile and creep strength to be modelled. This is con- sidered appropriate as failure in both cases is primarily a result of precipitate shear.  

The time–temperature rupture behaviour of single crystal superalloy does not follow conventional time– temperature parameters such as Larson–Miller or Sherby–Dorn. However, if a consideration is made for the temperature dependence of the tensile strength and rupture lives are compared at different fraction of this strength then an approach similar to Sherby–Dorn can be used.  

By basing rupture life calculations on physical prop- erties such as tensile strength and activation energies correlations and predictions of different alloys can be made. Signiﬁcant success has been made in predicting alloy rupture by basing rupture properties on the tensile strength of the alloy and activation energies for diffu- sion. Different ranges of single crystal superalloy com- position within which correlations and predictions of rupture behaviour can be made are broadly classiﬁed as rhenium and non-rhenium containing alloys.
