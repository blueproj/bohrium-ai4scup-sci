# Abstract  

Cast single crystal Ni-base superalloys exhibit severe solidiﬁcation segregation that must be subsequently removed by solution heat treatment. In order to understand how some of the elements in these alloys effect the solidiﬁcation partitioning, the as-cast microstructure of the third generation single crystal Ni-base superalloy, CMSX-10, was examined. In addition, the solidiﬁcation partitioning was calculated using Thermo-Calc™. The solidus, liquidus and    -solvus were also calculated for the compositions of the base alloy, and determined compositions of the dendrite cores and the interdendritic regions. All of the calculated values were compared to the experimentally determined results of this study and areas for future work will be discussed. © 2002 Published by Elsevier Science B.V.  

Keywords : Ni-base superalloys; Single crystals; Solidiﬁcation; Microstructure; Computational materials science; Calphad  

# 1. Introduction  

Dramatic improvements in the performance of gas turbines have resulted from the development of direc- tional solidiﬁcation and the production of single crystal turbine blades and vanes [1–3]. During solidiﬁcation, some elements partition to the dendrite core, while other elements tend to accumulate in the interdendritic liquid and then solidify as the interdendritic and eutec- tic regions. For example, W and Re are reported to partition to the dendrite core, whereas, Ta, Al and Ti partition to the interdendritic region [4]. The resulting segregation must be eliminated and all of the     and  /    eutectic must be put into solution by subsequent heat treatments.  

As new alloys have been developed, the refractory metal alloy additions have continued to rise. In particu- lar, the W, Re and Ta levels have increased steadily in the ﬁrst three generations of single crystal Ni-base superalloys [5]. For example, the refractory metal (i.e. W + Mo + Re + Ta) contents of representative ﬁrst

 (CMSX-2), second (CMSX-4) and third generation

 (CMSX-10) alloys has increased (Table 1) from about 14 to   16.5 wt% and then to   20 wt%, respectively [5]. These increased refractory elements result in alloys with increased strength and creep resistance due to increased solid solution strengthening, strengthening of the     and slowed diffusion rates.  

The increased refractory metal contents which result in slowed diffusion and creep rates, also result in slowed diffusion during solution heat treatments and increased levels of elemental segregation. Therefore, the second and third generation alloys require increased solution heat treatment temperatures and times to fully solution all of the     and eliminate segregation. Typical commercial heat treatment cycles for the three genera- tions of single crystals are presented in Table 2. Note the dramatic increases in both the temperatures and times required for the solution heat treatments of the second (CMSX-4) and, in particular, the third genera- tion alloy (CMSX-10), in comparison to the ﬁrst gener- ation alloy (CMSX-2 / 3). This increased heat treatment temperatures and times result in signiﬁcant increases in the cost of processing of these alloys [6]. In order to reduce the cost of solution heat treatments of single crystal alloys by reducing both the heat treatment temperatures and hold times at each temperature, it is necessary to understand the effects of the alloy compo- sition on partitioning and phase transformation tem- peratures. Understanding the effect of composition on the partitioning will provide an indication if chemistry modiﬁcations to the base alloy may reduce the parti- tioning during solidiﬁcation which would result in a decreased solution heat treatment. Therefore, a detailed study of the as-cast microstructure and the calculation of partitioning coefﬁcients and phase transformation temperatures of CMSX-10 was undertaken.

# 2.1.  Differential thermal analysis  

Differential thermal analysis (DTA) was performed in a DuPont 9000-series DTA on samples to determine the solidus, liquidus,    -solvus and eutectic melting point after each heat treatment step and in the as-cast material. Prior to the testing of the samples, the DTA unit was calibrated with high purity Ni at a scan rate of 20 °C / min. All tests were performed in a purged high- purity argon atmosphere with a ﬂow rate of   50 cc / min. High-purity alumina crucibles were used for all samples. Due to the severe segregation that occurs in these alloys [11], cylindrical samples with a minimum size of 250 mg (  4 mm diameter × 4 mm) were used. Data was acquired only on-heating to better character- ize the effect of the thermal processing and to avoid the effects of supercooling. All samples were heated at a constant 20 °C / min rate and duplicate samples were evaluated for each condition.  

# 2.2.  Thermo - Calc calculations  

Thermo-Calc simulations were performed using the Thermo-Tech Ni-Data database. For the equilibrium calculations, the alloy composition of interest was input to the program and the simulations were started at a temperature above the liquidus temperature. The pro- gram proceeded to step down in temperature in 5 °C increments until solidiﬁcation was complete and     pre- cipitation had occurred. At each temperature step, Thermo-Calc would determine the phase equilibria state that produced the lowest Gibb’s free energy value for the provided alloy composition.  

The Thermo-Calc Scheil–Gulliver simulations were also performed in a similar fashion to the equilibrium calculations. In the Scheil method, the desired alloy composition is given to the program along with a temperature above the liquidus point. Thermo-Calc again begins to step down in temperature until the liquidus is reached. At the liquidus, the formed solid will have the composition  C s1  and the liquid  C l1  is equal to the alloy composition  C 0 . Thermo-Calc will then begin using the liquid composition for the purposes of the Gibb’s free energy minimization calculations in- stead of the bulk alloy composition at each subsequent temperature step until solidiﬁcation is complete. There- fore, at temperature,  T 2 , Thermo-Calc will use the liquid composition,  C l1 , for the Gibb’s free energy calculation and at  T 3 , Thermo-Calc will use the liquid composition,  C l2  for the minimization calculation. This allows Thermo-Calc to simulate segregation as the alloy solidiﬁes. It should be noted that since Thermo-Calc is a thermodynamic software program it does not account for back diffusion occurring in the solid and also assumes that the liquid phase undergoes complete mixing.

# 3. Results  

For clarity, the results are discussed in terms of processing (i.e. thermal) history. Therefore, the com- plete sample characterization is presented ﬁrst for the as-cast material and then for the heat treated samples.  

# 3.1.  As - cast material  

The as-cast CMSX-10 sample exhibited a two-phase  /    microstructure with a dendritic segregation pattern and a limited amount of   /    eutectic solidiﬁcation occurring in some interdendritic areas (Fig. 1). The average primary and secondary dendrite arm spacings were 253 and 62   m, respectively, in the as-cast sam- ples. The average volume fraction of the   /    eutectic in the as-cast microstructure was   12%.  

In comparison to the bulk alloy composition, the dendrite cores were signiﬁcantly enriched in W and Re and depleted in Ta, Al and Ti (Table 4). Conversely, the interdendritic region of the as-cast microstructure was enriched in Al, Ti and Ta, while lower levels of W and Re were observed. The eutectic   /    regions were further enriched in Ta, Al and Ti, and depleted in W and Re. The partitioning coefﬁcients ( k ) of these ele- ments are presented in Table 4. Note that the elements which segregated to the dendrite cores during solidiﬁca- tion have partitioning elements greater than unity. The greater the partitioning coefﬁcient, the greater the de- gree of segregation. A partitioning coefﬁcient less than unity, indicates that the element segregated to the liquid during solidiﬁcation, ultimately solidifying in the inter- dendritic region. The smaller the partitioning coefﬁ- cient, the stronger the degree of segregation during solidiﬁcation. Elements with partitioning coefﬁcients close to unity, for example, Co, Cr and Mo, do not exhibit any signiﬁcant degree of segregation to either the dendrite core or the interdendritic regions during solidiﬁcation. These results are similar to the partition- ing coefﬁcients reported for the model, third-generation single crystal alloy, SX-1 [12].  

The DTA testing of the as-cast samples indicated that the liquidus was 1414 °C and the solidus was 1366 °C. Note that the measured solidus in the as-cast condition is approximately equal to the maximum heat treatment in the standard solution heat treatment (Table 4). No evidence of the    -solvus was observed in the DTA data from the as-cast material.

# 4. Discussion  

The development of directional solidiﬁcation to pro- duce single crystals, allowed for the removal of the grain boundary strengthening elements, which also in- creased the solidus of the alloy [1,2]. This increased solidus temperature allowed for a heat treatment win- dow to be established in the single crystal alloys deﬁned as the difference between the solidus (maximum) and    solvus (minimum) temperatures. The heat treatment window, resulting from the increased solidus tempera- ture, allowed for improved microstructural control since complete dissolution of the     could now be achieved during heat treatment. The     could then be re-precipitated and grown to the optimal size during either cool-down from the solution anneal or during subsequent aging heat treatment. The single crystal alloys presented the unique opportunity to control the size and morphology of the     to produce the optimal balance of properties. The solution heat treatment cy- cles were initially developed to produce complete disso- lution of    , including the eutectic   /   . However, continued development of the single crystal alloys re- sulted in alloys with increasing refractory metal con- tents. In particular, the levels of W and Re increased during the development of second and third generation alloys. The increased refractory metal contents resulted in more highly segregated microstructures in the as-cast condition, due to the partitioning of these elements to the dendrite cores. Therefore, the solution heat treat- ment not only was intended to solution the    , but also to reduce the chemical segregation present in the as-cast microstructure. In fact, for the third generation alloys, such as CMSX-10, the solution anneal heat treatment now appears to be controlled by the need to reduce chemical segregation, instead of the dissolution of the    [18].

Although all of the     is solutioned at temperatures above 1340 °C, the chemical segregation from the par- titioning of elements to the dendrite core or to the interdendritic areas persists to much high temperatures [18]. Some of the elements which have lower tempera- ture melting points, such as Al, exhibit more rapid diffusion and much more uniform distributions at tem- peratures above 1350 °C. However, the refractory metal elements W and Re, which have a somewhat higher melting point than Ta, do not exhibit apprecia- ble homogenization until the heat treatment tempera- tures reach 1360 °C or above. The local enrichment of Re and W that occurs in the dendrite core, due to the solidiﬁcation partitioning, must be reduced or elimi- nated by the solution heat treatment. These elements participate in the formation of topologically close packed phases, such as    and  P , in alloys that are not micro structurally stable and would be expected to result in degraded properties [14,16,17].  

The local enrichment of refractory metals, such as Re and W, in the dendrite core and ﬁne interdendritic region is also responsible for the changes in the Thermo-Calc liquidus, solidus, and     solvus results. The calculations indicate that as the refractory metal content increases, the liquidus and solidus temperatures increase. The high refractory content dendrite core and ﬁne interdendritic regions also show a decrease in Al, Ti, and Ta levels and a consequent decrease in calcu- lated     solvus temperature. The calculation results of the coarse interdendritic region closely approximate the results for the bulk alloy compositions. The coarse interdendritic region has a composition that is similar to the bulk alloy with the exception of higher Ta and lower Cr content. The similar composition levels would explain why the coarse interdendritic region reaction temperatures closely approximate the reaction tempera- tures calculated for the bulk alloy.  

The Thermo-Calc results show good agreement with the experimentally measured results listed in Tables 4 and 5. However, to achieve this good agreement with the experimental DTA measurements, one must look at the     solvus from the Scheil simulation and the solidus temperature from the equilibrium calculation. There- fore, one must use discretion and perform some experi- mental veriﬁcation before accepting the results from the simulation.  

The Scheil calculation using the base alloy composi- tion accurately predicted the liquidus and the     solvus temperatures, but calculates an extremely low solidus temperature. The inability to simulate back diffusion in the solid phase means that the Scheil simulation will over-estimate the amount of segregation that occurs in the liquid. This produces an extremely low ﬁnal solidiﬁ- cation temperature and is the cause of the miscalcula- tion of the solidus temperature when compared to the experimental data.  

The equilibrium calculation produces a more accu- rate solidus value compared to the Scheil calculation since it assumes that the system reaches equilibrium at each temperature step. This produces a much more conservative estimate of segregation during solidiﬁca- tion and yields a better solidus value. The conservative segregation estimate is also the reason that the     solvus temperature is a bit high compared to the DTA data. It should be noted that the liquidus value agrees with that of the Scheil simulation since no segregation has oc- curred when the ﬁrst solid forms.  

# 5. Summary  

The solution heat treatment of the third generation, single crystal Ni-base superalloys, CMSX-10 requires  45 h and temperatures up to 1365 °C. Therefore, the cost of this solution heat treatment is signiﬁcant. In order to determine if the heat treatment could be simpliﬁed and / or shortened to reduce the cost, a de- tailed study was completed of as-cast structures, ele- mental partitioning and the ability to calculate partitioning and phase transformation temperatures. In the case of cast, single crystal Ni-base superalloys, the solution heat treatment reduces the segregated mi- crostructure that occurs due to the partition of the elements to either the dendrite core or to the interden- dritic region. The most strongly partitioning elements were W, Re, Ta and Al. The W and Re partitioned to the dendrite core, while the interdendritic regions were found to be highly enriched in Ta and Al. This high level of segregation results in the need for long term, high-temperature solution heat treatments. The remain- ing elements did not demonstrate a very strong ten- dency to segregate to either the dendrite or interdendritic regions.  

Thermo-Calc calculations were able to predict the liquidus, solidus and     solvus temperatures for the CMSX-10 alloy composition. The Scheil calculation over-estimates the amount of segregation that occurs during solidiﬁcation and predicts an extremely low- solidus value but does yield liquidus and     solvus temperatures that agrees well with experimentally mea- sured values. The predicted partitioning coefﬁcients for the Scheil simulation agree well with the experimentally measured partitioning values. The equilibrium base al- loy calculation produces a much better value for the solidus temperature since it uses more conservative estimates of solidiﬁcation segregation. The Thermo- Calc simulations also allowed the authors to explore the effects of the experimentally determined dendrite and interdendritic compositions on the solidiﬁcation reactions. The dendrite core and the ﬁne interdendritic compositions showed increased values of liquidus and solidus temperatures due to high levels of Re and W.  

The dendrite core also showed decreased     solvus temperature due to the reduced levels of Al, Ti and Ta. The coarse interdendritic compositions showed liq- uidus, solidus and     solvus temperatures closer to those of the experimentally determined base alloy, since the decreased degree of segregation assumed in the calcula- tion was closer to the actual base alloy composition.
