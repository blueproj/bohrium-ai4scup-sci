# Solubility measurements of methane and ethane in water at and near hydrate conditions  

K.Y. Song, G. Feneyrou, F. Fleyfel ', R. Martin, John Lievois ?, R. Kobayashi

# Abstract  

The isobaric solubility of pure methane and ethane gases in liquid water was measured under different conditions, including hydrate formation points, using a ramping method in which measurements are made through the entire and continuous hydrate formation/decomposition cycle. The work was conducted for methane at 3.45 MPa (500 psia) and temperatures ranging from 17.0 to 0.0°C (290.2 to 273.2 K), and for ethane at 0.66 MPa (95 psia) and a temperature range of 17.0 to 0.0°C (290.2 to 273.2 K). The isobaric solubilities obtained show a significant divergence from (normal) Henry's law solubility as the temperature is lowered. The increase in the solubility is presumably the result of the onset of a sorption process that traps the gas molecules in the water structure. Several sub-processes were observed as the hydrate formation was taking place: (1) the dissolution of the gas in the liquid water, or interstitial solubility, (2) the onset of the sorption sub-process and build-up of the hydrate precursors, (3) the catastrophic formation and the existence of the solid phase with its slushy characteristics, and (4) the solidification of the hydrates, therefore causing the total plugging of the experimental cell.  

Keywords:Solubility;Methane;Ethane;Water;Hydrates  

# 1. Introduction  

The solubility of non-polar gases, e.g. CH4, C2H6, C§Hg, C4Hio, in liquid water has been extensively studied by Culberson and McKetta (1950, 1951), Kobayashi (1951), Battino (1986, 1987) and found to be extremely low. It is argued that the dissolution of a hydrocarbon, R, is considered as a two-step process: (1) the transfer to water phase, and (2) the solvation. The solvation is regarded as a relaxation to the equilibrium R + nH2O = R(H2O),. The increase in the solubility with decreasing temperature is explained by displacement of solvation equilibrium with changes in temperature (△Hsolv < o), and because the solute introduces low entropy structures in water, as stated by Frank and Evans (1945), Kauzmann (1959), Nemethy and Scheraga (1962a,b,c), and Stillinger (1980). The same argument suggests that the solubility of hydrocarbons in water is promoted as the temperature decreases because of the formation of an ice-like structure, i.e. pentagonal dodecahedra, in the solvent (Klotz, 1960; Patterson and Barbe, 1976). In fact, Shinoda and Fujihara (1968) reported that the entropy and the enthalpy of solution of hydrocarbons in water showed a striking divergence from the normal behavior established for regular solution because of the structure involved, even though thein operating pressure (14.7 psia) was much lower than the pressure required to form any hydrates.  

Recent studies (Fleyfel et al., 1993a,b) on the incorporation of CH4 and C3H: in water at moderate temperatures 6.0-9.0°C (279-282 K) and moderate pressures at 1.38 MPa (200 psia) indicate that the solubilities of these gases increase much more rapidly with decreasing temperature, qualitatively agreeing with what is reported in the literature. The hypothesis developed obeyed the geometry of the basic water structure, first introduced by Pauling (1959). He stated that in order to account for the density of liquid water (\~ 1.0 g cm-3), the liquid must form its own "buckyballs". The resulting water "buckyball" includes twenty one water molecules, twenty of which form a pentagonal dodecahedra and one H2O molecule in the middle to add stability to the cage. It was stated by Bernal and Fowler (1933), Bernal (1957), and Nemethy and Scheraga (1962a,b,c) that at ambient tempera- tures these pentagonal dodecahedra are metastable and flicker in and out of existence.Sloan and Fleyfel (1991) reported a kinetic model suggesting that during the nucleation period a great amount of metastability occurs because of the forming and breaking of structures. Lekvam and Ruoff (1993) reported a kinetic model for the formation of the methane hydrate from liquid water and methane gas. In their work they propose several stages prior to hydrate formation. From our experiments it was possible to detect almost all of the steps they suggested. It is also reported in Kohl and Riesenfeld (1974) that the solubility of CO2 in water increases dramatically as the temperature is decreased near hydrate conditions. The data near the hydrate region were an extrapolation of the curves reported and not actual measurements. These results, however, could lead to a possible generalization about the behavior ofgasesnear thehydratepoint.  

Englezos and Bishnoi (1988) reported experimental values of the mole fraction of methane in water in the primary hydrate nucleation region at temperatures ranging from 274.0 to 282.0 K and pressures from 3.29 to 8.36 MPa (477 to 1212 psia) as well as the limits of stability of methane in liquid water estimated by using a cubic equation proposed by Trebble and Bishnoi (1986). They concluded that the increase in solubility of the gas in water is due to the cluster formation in the liquid state prior to the appearance of the stable solid phase.  

In this paper, the solubility of methane and ethane in liquid water is reported at and near hydrate conditions, and a comparison with Henry's law solubility is obtained.

# 2.1. Rocking cell  

A rocking Jerguson-type visible cell was used, with 1/2-in. type-440 stainless steel balls which roll back and forth along a 5/8-in. bore. A magnetic pump is also incorporated with the visual cell to recirculate the gas. Two small beryllium springs are mounted to cushion and protect the platinum resistance thermometer (PRT) located at the end of the cell and immersed in water. The visual cell allows us to correlate the events occurring within the cell with the measurements, the solubility of gas into water, followed by the loss of clarity of the water-rich liquid, identify the small hydrate crystals forming on the glass, and finally the freezing of the balls into place by hydrate crystals. The motion of the balls and their collision with the springs do not affect the recorded signal at all. The three thermometers are placed on the cell wall, in the air bath, and in the liquid water. All PRTs are calibrated to 0.003°C vs. a NIST-certified PRT. The pressure is measured by a Ruska system pressure transducer. The experimental apparatus is shown in Fig. 5. The signals from the PRTs and the pressure transducers are collected by a PC for later recall.  

# 2.2. Air bath  

The air bath houses a visible cell, a magnetic pump and a reservoir bottle added in-line as a preconditioner. It has evolved in our laboratory over a decade. The temperature of the bath is controlled by both heating and refrigeration simultaneously. Through careful design it has become possible to develop and build air baths that fluctuate in the millidegree range. As the occasion demands, it is possible to acquire samples of the phases for analysis elsewhere, i.e. by chromatogra ph y or spectroscopy.  

# 2.3. Ruska digital positive displacement pump  

A Ruska digital positive displacement pump played a very important role in our measurements. It is digital-controlled, which allows pump operations to be programmed through the on-board mem- brane keypad or via a computer interface. Some of the standard operations of the pump include absolute and incremental discharges at preset rates, variable speed forward and reverse jog, which allows rates to be discharged on-the-fly, and a home function that sets the absolute position to zero. Depending on the maximum pressure requirements, the digital control system provides variable rates min -'. This pump has a constant pressure option whereby the speed of the pump is varied to maintain the desired pressure. The accuracy of the controlled pressure depends largely on the transducer that closes the control loop. For standard systems the pressure transducer is rated at 0.o7% full-scale accuracy.

# 4. Discussion  

High pressure solubility data are available at various temperatures ranging down to 283 K, and low temperature data are available at 1 atm. Since hydrate regions typically occur at high pressures and low temperatures, extrapolation of the data available in the literature was needed to provide a background toour measurements.This background will represent the baseline that willbe the reference to measure the divergence/increaseinthe solubilityof themethane and ethane in water. Three observations are noted from the results shown as follows:  

1. Onset of "sorption" resulting in the solubility increase as the temperature is lowered, as shown in Fig. 6 and Fig. 7,

 2. No liberation of gas is detected until 2 deg after the heating process started, shown in Fig. 3 and Fig.4,

 3. A large amount of gas is still in the water when the temperature comes back to its initial state, because it is noted that the volume did not reach its initial value, also clearly represented in Fig. 3. Each observation will be analyzed separately.  

# 4.1. "Sorption" effect  

The large ratio of mole fraction between the measured values and the predicted ones, and therefore the increase in solubility shows that there must be a physical phenomenon ("sorption") present besides the normal solubility as it is known regarding hydrocarbons in water which draws the gases into the liquid water as the hydrate formation region is approached. That "sorption" phenomenon is found to occur at temperatures even higher than the catastrophic temperature, T., as represented in Fig. 6 and Fig. 7. At these temperatures the water is still in its clear and transparent state. In the case of methane, T. is at 2.5°C (278.2 K), and the divergence in solubility 0ccurs at 15.0°C (288.2 K). For ethane, however, T. is at 1.8°C (286.2 K) and the divergence in solubility is at 13.0°C (288.2 K).  

The most reasonable explanationfor thisphenomenon is the ordering ofwater molecules and the ice-like characteristic that the water adapts around a hydrocarbon molecule.According to Pauling

 (1959), Bernal and Fowler (1933), Klotz (1960), Frank and Wen (1957), and Shinoda and Fujihara

 (1968), the water molecules in the liquid state can be considered to be short-lived clusters put together by hydrogen bonds, the clusters continually breaking and forming with the whole system in dynamic equilibrium, i.e. metastable equilibrium. Nemethy and Scheraga (1962a,b,c) statistically describe the system as containing molecules with different hydrogen bond distributions: some molecules with all the four possible bonds forming a three-dimensional tetrahedral shape (pentagonal dodecahedral cages), some on the surface of the cluster with 3, 2, 1 hydrogen bonds, and some free molecules not engaged in hydrogen bonding at all. The 4-hydrogen-bond network occupies the lowest energy level, then followed by the 3, 2, 1, and 0 networks residing in higher levels, respectively, because of the degrees of freedom assigned to each species. The introduction of a hydrocarbon molecule to the water causes non-hydrogen-bonding water molecules, having dipole-dipole interactions, to interact with the gas and form a weaker hydrocarbon-water interaction such as dipole-induced dipole. And since the presence of the hydrocarbon will not affect the ground state level, i.e. the geometry of the pentagonal dodecahedral cages, the only way it will dissolve is through a "sorption" process leading to an increase of the total ice-likeness of the solution. Such behavior was also discussed by Frank and Evans (1945). The number of water molecules affected by the interaction with the hydrocarbon solute is directly related to the size of the guest molecule, and thus the contact surface of the guest molecule. Therefore the ice-likeness characteristics will increase with the size of the hydrocarbon, in agreement with Frank and Wen's observations on the size effect (Frank and Wen, 1957).  

# 4.2.Nogasreleased uponheating thesystema fewdegrees  

Heating the system 2.0 degrees resulted in no gas release, as represented in Fig. 3 and Fig. 4. This is caused by the fact that the system was undercooled. As the temperature of water reached 2.2°C (275.4 K) for the ethane run and 2.3°C (275.5 K) for the methane-water system, the system started releasingasubstantialamountofgas.  

4.3.Gas volume not returning to its initial position  

This behavior suggests that the gas is still trapped in the water and the pentagonal dodecahedra are resisting decomposition even though the water was back to its clear state. This behavior was detected spectroscopic ally by Fleyfel et al. (1993a,b) with NMR spectroscopy.  

# 5. Conclusion  

We find it astonishing that with such a simple P-V--T method seven regions are detected as gas hydrates form and decompose from liquid water. Also, three distinct characteristics are clearly shown: (1) the onset of "sorption" that will increase the solubility by almost two to three factors of magnitude will cause divergence from Henry's Law behavior, (2) the hydrates start dissociating when the H-Lw-V temperature is surpassed; this is noticed by the change in gas volume, (3) the cages resist dissociation since the volume of the gas does not return to its initial value, particularly for the methane-water system. Future work will consist of studying hydrates of gas mixtures at constant temperatures as well as probing the effect of inhibitors on the " sorption" phenomenon.
