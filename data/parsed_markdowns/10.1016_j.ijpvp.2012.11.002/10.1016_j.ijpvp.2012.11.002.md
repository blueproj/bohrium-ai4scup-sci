This paper reports the use of non-invasive and semi-invasive techniques to measure the residual stresses in a large dissimilar weldment. This took the form of a butt weld between two sections of a P92 steel pipe, joined using an In625 welding consumable. Residual stress measurements have been carried out on the 30 mm thick welded pipe using the deep-hole drilling technique to characterise the through wall section residual stress distribution for the weld metal, HAZ and parent material. In addition, neutron diffraction measurements have been carried out within the weld zone. Diffraction patterns presented a high intensity and sharp peaks for the base P92 steel material. However measurements in the weld superalloy material were proven problematic as very weak diffraction patterns were observed. A thor- ough examination of the weld material suggested that the likely cause of this phenomenon was texture in the weld material created during the solidi ﬁ cation phase of the welding procedure. This paper discusses the challenges in the execution and interpretation of the neutron diffraction results and demonstrates that realistic measurements of residual stresses can be achieved, in complex dissimilar metal weldments.

Residual stresses are categorised based on the length scale over which they equilibrate  [3] . Macro- stresses, Type I, vary over large distances and over several grain in a polycrystalline material. # 1. Introduction  

Weldments in safety critical plants are subject to structural integrity assessments where residual stresses may play a key part [1,2] . In the case of engineering components operating at elevated temperature, the presence of tensile residual stresses can increase the likelihood of time dependent failure by acting as a driving force for the initiation and growth of cracks. There is a need to be able to measure the residual stresses arising from such a joining technique. Dissimilar welds are often used in service without heat treatment which means a knowledge of residual stress is even more impor- tant. Residual stress measurements provide both an improved understanding of the magnitude and origins of residual stresses in these complex dissimilar metal weldments as well as an improved basis for undertaking integrity assessments.  

A wide range of techniques is available for measuring stresses over different length scales. These techniques range from non- invasive to invasive and are capable of measuring stresses from the macro-scale down to the micro-scale. Examples of non-invasive techniques are X-ray diffraction and neutron diffraction  [8] . In semi-invasive methods, analysis requires only small quantities of material to be removed, allowing further testing on the component. Examples of semi-invasive methods include center hole drilling  [9] , slotting  [10]  and the Sachs method  [11] . Previous studies  [13,14]  have successfully assessed the presence of residual stresses in similar metal welds by both measurements and modelling. However for dissimilar weldments the magnitude and distribution of the residual stresses for the as-welded condition have not been investigated rigorously. These weldments have a complex microstructure. In addition, diffusion effects between the weldment and parent material should be taken into account. These phenomena are of increased importance as dissimilar weldments are often used in service in the as-welded condition so there is little opportunity to mitigate the build up of signi ﬁ cant residual stress. Usually, a dissimilar weld material, such as In625, is used in power plant pipelines to connect pipes made of different steels, such as joining martensitic to austenitic steel.  

In this work, residual stress distributions in a P92/In625 welded pipe have been obtained using neutron diffraction and deep-hole drilling techniques. The neutron diffraction measurements were undertaken using the ENGIN-X instrument at the ISIS neutron source at the Rutherford Appleton Laboratory, UK. The deep-holedrilling measurements were carried out at the University of Bristol residual stress Laboratory, UK. Measurements have been made at the parent, heat affected and weld regions of the pipe, to establish a comprehensive distribution of the residual stresses.

# 2.4. Neutron diffraction (ND)  

Diffraction methods fall in the non-invasive category of tech- niques  [8] . The main strength of neutron methods, and the reason they have been used here, is the very long path length that is possible which makes measurements in thick samples feasible. While some X-ray methods, notably energy-dispersive diffraction using high-energy synchrotron X-rays, can achieve signi ﬁ cant penetration depths in steel, they are generally limited to the measurement of in-plane components of strain due to geometry restrictions  [20] . The manufacturing and welding processes introduce three-dimensional spatial stress variation. The DHD and ND techniques have been used to measure the through wall section distribution of stresses along the weld centreline in the hoop and axial directions.

The set denoted DHD 1 was a measurement at a non-start/stop welding positionwhile the set DHD 2 was located at a start/stop position 135    further around the pipe. The two measurements sets display good agreement, particularly at the outer surface of the pipe, although there is greater variation at the inner surface. Both indicate hoop stresses of a predominantly tensile character with a tendency to reduce to zero close to the inner surface of the pipe. The ND measurements of the hoop stress are in excellent agreement with both DHD measure- ments across the full pro ﬁ le. Since all three measurements occurred at different locations around the weld ( Fig. 5 ) it would seem that the transient conditions at start/stop locations do not signi ﬁ cantly in ﬂ uence the hoop stress component within the weld metal. The same cannot be said for the axial component which shows greater discrepancies between the data sets, especially for the ND measurements which are systematically more compressive than the DHD measurements. If the residual stress state is axisymmetric then the axial stresses integrated over the cross-section of the pipe would be expected to balance. This is not observed in the current case. However, the DHD measurements are more tensile while the ND measurements are more compressive which implies the lack of balance may be due to a variation of residual stress around the circumference of the weld. A systematic error due to an incorrect choice of elastic constants and  d 0   values cannot be neglected but would be dif ﬁ cult to reconcile with the good agreement in the hoop direction. It is also important to note that as shown by previous studies  [26] , residual stress distribution in components can be optimally resolved by using the DHD technique for depths greater than 1 mm.  

# 3.3. Heat affected zone measurements  

Only a single DHD measurement was made in the HAZ, which represents a non-start/stop location at a circumferential position between the weld centreline DHD and ND measurements. The data is presented in  Fig. 13  along with the ND measurement which was at the same axial location but 20    around the circumference. In comparison with the weld centreline measurements it can be seen that the axial stress is greater than the hoop stress over the entire thickness of the weld. Both components are tensile at the outer surface but become compressive at the inner surface. The transition from tension to compression occurs at approximately 7 mm below the pipe surface for the axial component and 15 mm below the surface for the hoop stress component. As with the weld centreline measurements, the ND measurements neutron data has points of agreement and disagreement with the DHD pro ﬁ le. In this case the axial component of stress shows better agreement while the hoop stress is systematically more tensile than the neutron data. In both cases the neutron diffraction data fails to re ﬂ ect the DHD data ’ s tendency towards zero stress (both components) at the inner surface.  

A simpli ﬁ ed model of the distortion induced by the circumfer- ential weld can be visualised as a pinching of the pipe as the weld metal cools. This would be expected to lead to bending stresses in the axial direction with high tension on the upper surface and compression on the lower. This is clear in the HAZ measurements, where the bending would be most severe, and can be seen to some extent in the weld zone measurements.

# 3.4. Limitations of DHD and ND measurements  

Neither the DHD nor the ND techniques are able to measure stress at a precise location but rather measure average values over a sampling volume. This sampling volume is notably different in size and shape for the two techniques. The DHD technique works by measuring changes in the diameter of a small hole when a 10 mm diameter core is trepanned around the hole. The sampling volume is roughly spherical with a diameter equal to the diameter of the trepanned core. The ND technique samples a roughly cuboid volume approximately 4 mm    4 mm    6 mm for the measure- ments presented here. The resolution is slightly poorer for the radial direction.  

ND measurements are sensitive to strains acting at several length scales. Normally it can be expected that when using large gauge volumes the short range strains tend to average out and do not in ﬂ uence the measured strain. This may not be true in the presence of texture or large grain sizes. Importantly, diffraction measurements are also sensitive to various types of elastic and plastic anisotropy effects  [24] . In some cases this may be advanta- geous but can be a problem when the focus is on the macroscopic stress state and will lead to offsets between the two sets of data.

# 4. Conclusions  

Measurements of residual stresses have been undertaken in a circumferential dissimilar weldment of a P92 steel pipe using an In625 superalloy weld  ﬁ ller. The deep-hole drilling and neutron diffraction techniques were employed in order to provide through thickness measurements of residual stresses within the weld metal and heat affected region. It may be concluded that:  

I. It is possible to undertake realistic residual stress measure- ments using both the DHD and ND techniques. II. The two techniques provide generally consistent measure- ment of welding-induced residual stresses for the weld metal and heat affected zone of the welded pipe. III. The transient conditions at start/stop locations do not signi ﬁ cantly in ﬂ uence the hoop stress distribution within the weld. IV. The highly textured nature of the solidi ﬁ ed In625 resulted in the low intensity counts of the diffraction peaks during the ND measurements making these measurements dif ﬁ cult and time consuming to obtain.
