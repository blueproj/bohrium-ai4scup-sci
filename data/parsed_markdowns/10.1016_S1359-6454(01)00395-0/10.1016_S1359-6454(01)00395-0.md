# Abstract  

Microstructural features of nickel base superalloys N18 have been investigated by transmission electron microscopy (TEM), ﬁeld emission gun-scanning electron microscopy (FEG-SEM), ﬁeld ion microscopy (FIM), one-dimensional atom probe (APFIM) and three-dimensional atom probe (3DAP). Heat treatments conducted at 700 and 800 ° C lead to the precipitation of a large volume fraction of  g   precipitates (57%) that are Al and Ti enriched. Cr, Co and Mo preferentially partition to the  g  solid solution. Small Cr-enriched particles ( q ) were discovered in the core of largest  g  particles. These contain more Cr and less Mo than the  g  solid solution but are thought to be tiny metastable  g  particles that were formed in the inner region of  g   particles during cooling from 800 ° C. Intergranular Ni-depleted borides arranged in “clusters” of particles very close to each other were observed using 3DAP. Their stoichiometry is M 2 B with M    Cr, Mo. One nanometer-thick Ni and Co enriched ﬁlms (compared to borides) separate adjoining boride particles. Grain boundary (GB) segregation of B, Mo and to some extent Cr has been observed. The thickness of the segregated layer wetting GB is close to 1 nm.   2002 Acta Materialia Inc. Published by Elsevier Science Ltd. All rights reserved.  

Keywords:  Atom probe; Nickel base superalloys; Microstructure; Grain boundaries; Segregation  

# 1. Introduction  

Nickel base superalloys are key materials in the aerospace industry for turbine blades and disks. These engine components are submitted to very high stresses under temperatures in the range of 700–1000 ° C. Due to the addition of numerous elements (Al, Cr, Ti, Mo…), nickel base superal- loys are able to combine high creep performance at high temperatures and excellent oxidation (Cr) and corrosion resistance. These materials derive their impressive creep resistance from the presence of a high volume fraction (  50–70%) of ordered Ni 3  (Al,Ti) type precipitates ( g  ) which form rafts under stress. Boron, added in small proportion (less than 1000 at. ppm) to polycrystalline superalloys segregate to grain boundaries (GBs) and improve ductility and mechanical resistance.  

The ﬁne-scale microstructure as well as the microchemistry of superalloys N18 was investi- gated using transmission electron microscopy (TEM), scanning electron microscope equipped with a ﬁeld emission gun (FEG-SEM), ﬁeld ionmicroscopy (FIM) and the three-dimensional atom probe techniques (3DAP). These polycrystalline superalloys were developed by Ofﬁce National d’Etude et de Recherches en Ae ´ronautique (ONERA, Cha ˆtillon, France) and are implemented by SNECMA in turbine disks of last generation air- crafts.  

Experiments reported in this paper were carried out mainly using 3DAP. This instrument is an ultra high resolution analytical microscope [1–3] which has unique three-dimensional (3D) imaging capa- bilities. This analytical microscope makes it poss- ible to image the distribution of atoms in the small volume that is analysed in the material (typically 10 × 10 × 100 nm 3 ). The spatial resolution is less than 0.1 nm in depth (normal to the sample surface) so that atomic planes can be imaged and analysed individually. Therefore, both composition and order ﬁelds can be imaged and the composition of a small selected region in the reconstructed image can be measured quantitatively. Contrarily to depth resolution, the lateral resolution (i.e. parallel to the specimen surface), is far from reaching the atomic scale in general (0.5 nm) because of ion trajectory aberrations. This means that in most cases, the lat- tice cannot be imaged in 3D.  

# 2. Experimental  

The material (N18) was prepared by powder metallurgy at ONERA. The nominal composition of this superalloy is given in Table 1. Numerous addition elements are present in N18 superalloys (Al, Ti, Cr, Mo, B, C…), some of them being speciﬁcally added to avoid intergranular fracture (B) and improving ductility.  

The grain size in N18 is close to 10  µ m. The thermal heat treatment applied to samples consists of a homogenisation for 4 h at 1165 ° C followed by a 50–100 ° C/min cooling step and two ﬁnal annealing treatments (24 h at 700 ° C + 4 h at 800 ° C) leading to the precipitation of a large volume frac- tion of  g   particles (more than 50%) within the fcc g  solid solution (Fig. 1). Four particle populations were identiﬁed in both FEG-SEM and TEM images. The largest particles (  1  µ m) probably originate from the primary precipitation during sol- idiﬁcation. The second population (200 nm) is likely to form during the partial homogenisation held at 1165 ° C. This temperature was chosen a little bit under the solvus line of  g   so that primary g   formed during solidiﬁcation cannot be com- pletely dissolved. Higher temperatures would lead to a detrimental increase in the grain size. Particles have both the typical cuboidal and “butterﬂy” shapes often observed for relatively large particles for which the morphological evolution during growth is mainly controlled by the mismatch elas- tic energy (misﬁt between  g  and  g   phases). The third population (30–50 nm) has probably formed during the two-step precipitation heat treatment held at 700 and 800 ° C whereas the ﬁnest spherical precipitates (  10 nm) are thought to nucleate dur- ing cooling from 800 ° C. Note that precipitates are smaller close to the GB. This is thought to be caused by the very large primary  g   precipitates which form at GBs, leading therefore to an Al- depleted zone close to boundaries.  

3DAP is a destructive microanalysis instrument, based on the ﬁeld evaporation and ionisation of surface atoms of the material and time of ﬂight mass spectrometry [1]. Samples were prepared in the form of sharply pointed needles by standard electro polishing techniques. The pulse fraction was 19%, the repetition frequency 1700 Hz. Specimens were maintained at 70 K and under high vacuum conditions (10  7   Pa) during analysis.

# 5. The precipitation of Cr-enriched particles within  g   particles  

The distribution of solutes in  g   particles is also of great concern. As shown in the Cr map provided in Fig. 5, small Cr-enriched particles (denominated q  hereafter), 5 nm in diameter, are present within the  g   phase of N18. These tiny particles appeared as dark zones in the FIM micrograph, similarly to the  g  matrix. The number density of such  q  par- ticles is rather high (Fig. 5). Similar particles were observed by TEM and 3DAP in a single crystal superalloy SC16 with very similar Cr and Mo contents (Table 4). The presence of such small Cr-enriched precipi- tates ( q ) in the  g   phase of superalloys is rather unusual. However, a similar observation was made by Miller et al. a few years ago in single crystal superalloys PWA1480; they reported similar com- position and particle sizes [5,8]. q particles appeared in dark contrast in FIM images, similarly to  g  phase ( g   is brightly imaged in FIM at rather high tip temperature   70 K). TEM images indi- cated that these particles were located preferen- tially in the interior of  g   precipitates but not in the outer periphery. No extra spots were observed in the electron diffraction patterns. Although these particles were found to have a Cr content (33 at.%) higher than the Cr concentration in the  g  matrix (  25 at.%), these regions were interpreted as  g  pre- cipitates [5].  

The precise nature of such tiny  q  particles within g   precipitates is intriguing and is still a subject of debate. The stoichiometry being close to that of the ordered phase Ni 2 Cr (Table 4), it has been tempt- ing to interpret these particles as small Ni 2 Cr nuclei. However, materials were treated at tem- peratures well above the critical order–disorder temperature of this phase ( T c    590 ° C for the binary system Ni 2 Cr) where only short range ordering is expected to occur. For instance PWA1480 superalloys were subjected to a two- step precipitation treatment: 4 h at 1079 ° C and 32 h at 871 ° C followed by air cooling. The last treat- ment applied to N18 was made at 800 ° C which is also well above  T c .  

Nevertheless, it is quite possible that the pres- ence of additional elements such as Ti, Al increases the critical temperature. The other eventuality is that Ni 2 Cr ordering might take place during cooling as well. The second interpretation is that these Cr- enriched particles are actually  g  particles that pre- cipitated within  g   particles (Table 3). One possible driving force for the nucleation of such  g  clusters within  g   could be the development of a supersatu- ration in Cr (and other  g -like elements) in the  g  ordered phase, most likely during cooling from 800 ° C. Following this scenario, the solubility limit of Al in the  g   phase of this complex material would thus increase with decreasing temperatures whereas it would be the reverse for Cr.  g   would have thus to admit more Al and reject Cr from  g  particles during cooling, provided that diffusion rate is sufﬁcient. It is clear that the sample is rap- idly cooled from 800 ° C, only the outer region of g   particles will be able to reject  g -like elements like Cr (resp. incorporate more  g  -like elements) and precipitation of tiny  g  precipitates will there- fore proceed in the interior. The question of the stability of such tiny meta- stable  g  precipitates thought to form during cooling is of great concern for mechanical properties at high temperatures. The formation of a given vol- ume fraction of such  g  particles is indeed very energy consuming as compared to a partial dissol- ution of  g   precipitates (i.e. a decrease of the vol- ume of large  g  ). Isothermal ageing or creep is therefore very likely to dissolve these Cr-enriched q  particles, leading to the rejection of Cr out of g   and the incorporation of more Al in  g  . As a consequence, these  q  particles are not likely to con- tribute to a large extent to creep performance of turbine disks at temperatures in the range of 700–800 ° C.

The thin ﬁlm (1 nm) that separates borides is rather hard to be interpreted into  g  regions since it contains much less Ni than  g  (25% versus 38%) and a lot of boron (8.74% versus 0.083% for  g ), as well as more than twice the amount of Mo (17.37%) as  g  (8.36%). This ﬁlm is thought to accommodate the possible disorientation between neighbouring boride particles. M 2 B particles have probably nucleated on various habit planes of the fcc and are therefore disoriented to each other so that coalescence (coagulation) of neighbouring borides was difﬁcult during their growth because of the formation of high energy phase interfaces. This ﬁlm could therefore act as a wetting media.  

It is worth noting that GBs are not only preferen- tial sites for the precipitation of borides. Boron seg- regation has also been observed all along GBs in the form of a continuous ﬁlm (Fig. 10). The Swed- ish APFIM group has been a pioneer in the investi- gation of interfacial segregation, notably in nickel base alloys (alloys 600 and 690) [13]. The GB shown in the elemental maps given in Fig. 10 was identiﬁed and fully characterised using TEM and Kikuchi lines before its analysis with 3DAP [14,15]. Large Al-rich  g   particles are present on each side of the GB (their size exceeds that of the volume that is represented). Note the presence of fringe contrast along   011   on the left side of the GB. This contrast is related to the concentration wave which exists in the  g   ordered phase (Ni 3 Al) along the   011   superstructure direction. The dis- orientation between the grains prohibits the ordered planes from being resolved in both grains in this view.  

3DAP investigations reveal that not only boron segregates to GBs but also Mo and Cr. 15% of Cr, almost 6% of Mo and 1.5% of B were detected in the core of GBs, which were also found to be Al depleted. The presence of a high Cr content at the GBs is likely to contribute to improved corrosion properties. Similar results were observed in ASTROLOY [16,17]. Enrichment of boron ( + phosphorus and carbon) was also measured in alloy 718 [18]. A more detailed discussion of interfacial segregation in N18 is provided else- where [19].  

# 7. Conclusions  

TEM and 3DAP investigations have revealed the rather complex microstructure of Nl8 superalloys. Heat treatments conducted at 700 and 800 ° C lead to the precipitation of a large volume fraction of g   precipitates (57%), in good agreement with Gue- dou et al. [20]. Four populations were identiﬁed. Small Cr-enriched particles ( q ) were discovered in large  g   particles. They contain more Cr and Mo than the  g  solid solution. TEM diffraction did not show any extra spots, suggesting therefore that these particles have the fcc structure of the  g  phase. The most convincing interpretation is that these  q particles are nothing but tiny metastable  g  particles that were formed during cooling in the inner region of  g   particles.  

Intergranular borides arranged in the form of grapes were observed using 3DAP. Their stoichi- ometry is M 2 B with M    Mo, Cr. Mo-enriched Ni- depleted ﬁlms, 1 nm thick (e.g. compared to  g matrix), separate boride particles within the cluster, suggesting therefore that these particles have nucleated in the form of various orientation vari- ants with respect to the fcc lattice of  g . This can explain why boride particles have not coalesced to form a single larger particle. The thin ﬁlm observed between particles could then act as a wet- ting ﬁlm accommodating the disorientation between orientation variants.
