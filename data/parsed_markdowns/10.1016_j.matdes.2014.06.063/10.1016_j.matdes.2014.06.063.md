# 1. Introduction  

GH4133B alloy (NiŌĆō20.0CrŌĆō2.5TiŌĆō1.5NbŌĆō1.0Al) is a traditional Ni-base superalloy, and it has good microstructure stability, excel- lent mechanical properties, good ultimate strength and creep per- formance at elevated temperature. This alloy has been widely applied for manufacturing turbine engine blades and other bearing components under a high temperature  [1] . This alloy as a tradi- tional superalloy has been used for a number of years, and its suit- able service temperature is about 700   C. With the continuous development of modern aviation industry, the working tempera- ture for aircraft engine disk increased, the traditional Ni-based superalloy cannot meet the necessary for high temperature struc- tural components.

Because of the high alloy content in superalloy, the high tem- perature deformation becomes a key problem. In particular, for the FGH4096-GH4133B dual alloys, they have different micro- structures, different hot deformation behavior and different opti- mum process parameters. Therefore, it is very important to investigate and con’¼ürm the quantitative relationships between the deformation resistance and the deformation temperature and the strain rate for the hot working. The constitutive equation has been used to research the ’¼éow stress of many other alloys [10ŌĆō14] . In the present work, the mechanical behaviors of the FGH4096-GH4133B dual alloy during high temperature deforma- tion were investigated by using hot compression tests. The consti- tutive model, activation energy, and the relationship between the ’¼éow stress and the deformation temperature and strain rate were determined. The constitutive equation represented as a function of temperature, strain rate and true strain was developed and pro- vided an accurate and precise estimate of ’¼éow stress.  

# 2. Experimental materials and procedures  

FGH4096 superalloy used in this work is a powder metallurgy superalloy produced by hot isostatic press (HIP). It has the follow- ing chemical composition (wt, %): C, 0.03; Cr, 16.00; Co, 12.70; W, 4.00; Al, 2.20; Ti, 3.70; Ni to balance. The diameter of original Pow- der particles range from 20 to 50  l m, and they are procured by the way of argon atomization. The original Powder particles were encapsulated in a stainless steel capsule, and then produced by HIP. The HIPed bar was prepared after the heat treatment with the hardness of about 60ŌĆō62 HRC.  Fig. 1  presents the typical optical microstructure of as-received PM FGH4096 superalloy.  

GH4133B superalloy is a traditional Ni-base superalloy. It has the following chemical composition (wt, %): Cr, 20.00; Nb, 1.50; Mg, 0.005; Al, 0.90; Ti, 2.80; Zr, 0.004; Ni to balance. Fig. 2 presentsthe typical optical microstructure of as-received GH4133B superalloy.  

The superalloy FGH4096 and GH4133B were joined by the elec- tron beam welding. The parameters of electron beam welding are shown in  Table 1 . The electron beam welding was conducted, and the cylindrical compression specimens were machined from the central part of the electron beam weldments.  Fig. 3  presents the typical microstructure of the welding seam.  

The cylindrical compression specimens with the dimension of ┬Ż 8    12 mm were machined from the post forged bar. The end surfaces of the cylindrical samples were grooved for holding the glass lubricant in hot compression process.  

Isothermal hot compression tests were conducted on a Gleeble- 1500D thermo-simulation machine at deformation temperatures of 1020   C, 1050   C, 1080   C, 1110   C and 1140   C (the nominal c 0 -transus temperature is about 1080   C), and constant strain rates of 0.001, 0.01, 0.1 and 1.0 s  1 , respectively (The test standard is ASTM: E209). The height reduction for all the specimens was 50%. All the specimens were heated at a heating rate of 10   C/s and soaked for 5 min at deformation temperature in order to obtain a uniform temperature distribution in the entire specimen. Deformation temperature was controlled to ┬▒2   C. The loadŌĆōstroke curves obtained from the compression tests were recorded and converted into true stressŌĆōtrue strain curves using standard method. The whole process of heating, heat preservation and hot deformation was protected by the argon. In order to analyze the effects of the forming processing parameters on the microstruc- tures, the deformed specimens were sectioned along the perpen- dicular electron beam weldments, the grain structures of the deformed specimens were observed by an Olympus PM3 optical microscope (OM).
