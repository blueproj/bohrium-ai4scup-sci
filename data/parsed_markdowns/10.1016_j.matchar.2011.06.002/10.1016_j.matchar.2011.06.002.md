# A R T I C L E D A T A  

# A B S T R A C T  

The compositional and microstructural evolution of different generations of  γ ′  precipitates during the continuous cooling of a commercial nickel base superalloy, Rene88DT, has been characterized by three dimensional atom probe tomography coupled with energy-filtered transmission electron microscopy studies. After solutionizing in the single  γ  phase field, continuous cooling at a very high rate results in a monomodal size distribution of  γ ′  precipitates with a high nucleation density and non-equilibrium compositions. In contrast, a relatively slower cooling rate (\~24 °C/min) results in a multi-modal size distribution of  γ ′  precipitates with the larger first generation primary precipitates exhibiting close to equilibrium composition, along with the smaller scale secondary  γ ′  precipitates, exhibiting non-equilibrium composition (excess of Co and Cr, depleted in Al and Ti). The composition of the  γ  matrix near these precipitates also exhibits similar trends with the composition being closer to equilibrium near the primary precipitates as compared to the secondary precipitates.

# 1. Introduction  

Commercial superalloys like Rene88DT are used in high temperature applications like turbine disk in aircraft jet engines due to their excellent high temperature properties, including strength, ductility, improved fracture toughness and fatigue resistance, as well as enhanced creep and oxidation resistance [1,2] . Typically this alloy has a microstructure containing a disordered face-centered cubic matrix  γ  phase with dispersed precipitates of the L1 2 -ordered  γ ′  phase  [2,3] . The morphology and distribution of these ordered  γ ′  precipitates inside the disordered  γ  matrix determine the mechanical properties  

Over the years there have been a number of reports in the literature where three-dimensional atom probe tomography and atom probe field ion microscopy have been employed to characterize the atomic scale microstructure of Ni-based super- alloys  [7 – 10] . In order to observe the compositional changes between  γ  and  γ ′  phases, integrated concentration profiles (so- called ladder diagrams) have been plotted using more traditional conventional atom probe field ion microscopy  [11] . Compared to atom probe field ion microscopy, more recent atom probe systems, such as those incorporating a local electrode (e.g. the LEAP system by Cameca Inc.) allow for both substantially larger field-of-view as well as faster data acquisition. This leads to data generation from larger volumes over the same time period  [12] , thereby yielding more quantitative information from and across, nanoscale precipitates from these large volumes. The morphol- ogy of precipitates can be visualized using isoconcentation surface (or iso surface) in IVAS ™ 3.0, a commercial reconstruc- tion software, in three dimensions  [13] . This analysis method enables integration of chemical information with three- dimensional position information  [14,15] . Thus, atom probe analysis is often useful to show the change in composition of  γ ′ and  γ  matrix and morphology for relatively smaller sized precipitates. This, when coupled with conventional and analyt- ical transmission electron microscope (TEM) analyses, can give us very useful information regarding the structure, morphology and composition of the  γ ′  precipitates along with the  γ  matrix [16,17] . Furthermore, energy filtered transmission electron microscopy techniques have been used quite successfully in order to distinguish these precipitates from the matrix, based on the partitioning behavior of different elements  [18] .  

Precipitation of  γ ′  during continuous cooling of a nickel base superalloy from the high temperature single  γ  phase-field is a rather complex process critically dependent on the cooling rates employed. Faster cooling rates, such as those encountered by water quenching the alloy from the high-temperature single  γ phase field, typically lead to the formation of a monomodal size distribution of refined  γ ′  precipitates  [3,19 – 22] . These precipi- tates have close to spherical morphology and their density is quite high. In contrast, relatively slower cooling rates lead to the formation of  γ ′  precipitates of two or even more different size ranges due to multiple nucleation events occurring at different undercoolings below the  γ ′  solvus  [19 – 22] . This typically results in a bimodal size distribution of  γ ′  precipitates due to the coupling of multiple thermodynamic and kinetic factors  [20] . These multiple nucleation bursts result from the complex interplay of continuously increasing thermodynamic driving force for nucleation (chemical free energy difference) due to increasing undercooling, reduction in this driving force due to previous nucleation events, and the rapidly declining diffusivity of alloying elements with decreasing temperature. The first burst of nucleation, occurring at lower undercoolings (relatively higher temperatures, just below the  γ ′  solvus temperature), leads to the formation of the first generation of  γ ′  precipitates, often referred to as primary  γ ′  precipitates. Similarly, a second burst of nucleation, at higher undercoolings, leads to the formation of secondary  γ ′  precipitates. Here it has to be noted that the second burst the undercooling depends on the new solubility limit of the matrix, due to the presence of primary  γ ′ precipitates and this means that the solvus temperature of the secondary  γ ′  precipitates is different from the primary ones. Indeed it has been observed that similar nucleation bursts can occur at even lower temperatures leading to successive generations of  γ ′  precipitates  [20,21,23] . The nucleation density and size-scale of these different generations of  γ ′  precipitates depend on the diffusion mediated growth rates associated with different undercoolings. In addition to that, the effect of compositional partitioning at different undercoolings makes the evolution more complex. Some studies have been con- ducted in the recent past in order to understand the evolution of different generations of  γ ′  precipitates formed at different cooling rates  [9,23] . In a recent study, Hwang et al.  [3]  have discussed the partitioning of constituent alloying elements between the  γ  and  γ ′  phases in water-quenched Rene88DT with a monomodal size distribution of precipitates. However there is a lack of systematic investigation of the effect of cooling rate on the size and morphology of different generations of  γ ′  pre- cipitates and the compositional partitioning between these precipitates and the matrix in complex engineering superalloys, which typically contain a large number of alloying elements. Thus the present study focuses on these aspects in the Rene88DT alloy, which is subjected to a super-solvus treatment in the single  γ  phase field, followed by cooling to room temperature under three different cooling rates.  

# 2. Experimental Procedure  

The Rene88DT alloy used in the present study has a composition of 55.63Ni – 18.02Cr – 13.00Co – 4.74Ti – 4.45Al – 2.48Mo – 1.21W – 0.46Nb, all in at.%. Small samples (1 mm×1 mm×2.5 mm) of this alloy were solution treated at a super-solvus temperature of 1150 °C for 30 min ( γ ′  solvus for this alloy is 1124 °C  [2] ). This was followed by cooling the samples at three different rates of >300 °C/min, 280 °C/min and 24 °C/min, as measured by a thermocouple attached to the center of each specimen. These cooling rates correspond to the water quenched, fast cooled and slow cooled conditions respectively. For convenience, these samples will be subsequently referred by the names WQ0, FC0 and SC0 where the first two letters refer to the cooling rate of the particular specimen and the last digit indicates that no subsequent annealing treatments were employed. Transmission electron microscopy samples were prepared using conventional electro polishing techniques followed by argon ion milling using a Fishione 1010 system. Images were obtained using the Cr M-edge (at 42 eV) in the energy-filtered transmission electron microscopy mode on an FEI Tecnai F20 microscope operating at 200 kV, as described elsewhere  [18] . Sharp needle-like specimens (with a tip radius of 100 nm) for atom probe tomography (APT) studies in the LEAP were prepared using standard electro polishing techniques  [3] . The APT experiments were carried out using a LEAP 3000 local electrode atom probe (LEAP ™ ) system from Cameca Inc. in the electric-field evaporation mode at a temperature of 60 K, with the evaporation rate varying from 0.5% to 1.0% and a pulsing voltage at 20% of the steady-state applied voltage. Data analysis was performed using the IVAS 3.0 software from Cameca Inc.  

Pandat ™ , a commercial CALPHAD based thermodynamic software that conducts phase diagram calculations, was used to compute the equilibrium composition of  γ ′  phase in Rene88DT as Ni – 10%Al – 2.4%Co – 3.1%Cr – 0.13%Mo – 1%Nb – 10.7%Ti – 0.02%W and that of  γ  matrix as Ni – 0.05%Al – 21.4%Co – 29.9%Cr – 4.4%Mo – 2.2%W, all in at.%. The software also predicted the equilibrium volume fraction of  γ ′  phase to be 0.44.

Similar to the primary  γ ′  precipitates, the secondary  γ ′ particles were also analyzed from the EFTEM data in  Fig. 1 . It isto be noted that smaller secondary  γ ′  precipitates are substantially greater innumber as theynucleate at a larger under cooling. Also they exhibit a nearly spherical morphology due to diffusion- limited growth of precipitates at low temperatures. The lower diffusivity at larger undercoolings is also responsible for the formation of  “ γ ′ -free depletion zones ”  around larger primary  γ ′ precipitates ( Fig. 1 (b) and (c)), formed due to the lack of long- range equilibration of the  γ  matrix composition away from a growing primary  γ ′  precipitates during continuous cooling. Also, one important thing to note is that the growth of these precipitates would also depend on the change in composition of the matrix during cooling, which may be different in SC0 and FC0 conditions. A more elaborate discussion on this will be taken up later. In addition to the difference in size of the primary  γ ′  precipitates in FC0 and SC0 samples, there is also a variation in the thickness of the  “ depletion zone ”  around them. Recent simulations, based on classical nucleation and growth theory, have predicted the formation of such trimodal microstructures  [23] .

Based on the APT composition data and energy-filtered transmission electron microscopy analysis, the effect of cooling rate on the overall  γ ′  precipitation mechanism can be explained via the schematic drawn in  Fig. 6 . For all three cooling rates, as the sample is cooled below the solutionizing temperature, with increasing undercooling, the volumetric free energy term,  Δ G v , continues to increase until the critical nucleation barrier  Δ G\* is sufficiently low, permitting the first burst of nucleation to take place. In the case of water quenching treatment (WQ0), due to the faster cooling rates (>300 °C/min), the under-cooling is maximum when the above-mentioned nucleation event occurs (leftmost curve in Fig. 6 ). Due to very high nucleation rate at this undercooling the density of the  γ ′  precipitates is very high (calculated as 759 particles/ μ m 2 ). Also at this high undercooling (lower temperature), diffusion is rather limited, which coupled with the superimposed fast quench rate allows only very limited

These precipitates also develop necked regions due the overlap of their diffusion fields. This along with the above factors of very high nucleation density and low diffusivity, restricts the growth of  γ ′  precipitates, causing a spherical morphology to be maintained. The high cooling rate during water-quenching prevents any further nucleation events despite the fact that non-equilibrium conditions exist. So a monomodal size distribution of  γ ′  precipitates is observed ( Fig. 1 (a)).  

On the other hand, the scenario changes substantially during slow-cooling (SC0 sample), in terms of nucleation and growth of γ ′  precipitates. At the slow cooling rates (\~24 °C/min), the first generation of  γ ′  precipitates (primary  γ ′ ) is nucleated at relatively higher temperatures (lower undercooling), where diffusion rates are sufficiently rapid to equilibrate the  γ ′  and  γ compositions near the interface, thus resulting in equilibrium precipitate compositions (rightmost curve in  Fig. 6 ). This is evident from the composition of the primary  γ ′  precipitates in Table 1(a)  for the SC0 sample. Also at these relatively elevated temperatures, the nucleation rate is quite low, resulting in a low number density of precipitates (8 particles/ μ m 2 ), which are separated by large distances (refer  Fig. 6 ). These particles grow due to diffusion-mediated transport of elements like Al and Ti into the precipitate and rejection of  γ -stabilizing elements like Co and Cr. While local  γ ′– γ  equilibrium (end points of the thermodynamic tie line in the  γ + γ ′  two-phase field) is easily established across the precipitate/matrix interface, the limited time at that temperature reduces the diffusion distances and consequently the long-range  γ  composition does not reach equilibrium. As soon as the long-range diffusion fields from two adjacent primary  γ ′  precipitates overlap (often referred to as soft impingement), there is not sufficient driving force for new nucleation between these adjacent  γ ′  precipitates at that temperature. Nevertheless, the  γ  matrix region between the primary  γ ′  precipitates, away from the growing  γ ′ / γ  interfaces, retains a far-from equilibrium composition. With decreasing temperature during continuous cooling, there is a continual increase in the driving force of nucleation with the maximum being in the  γ  regions in between the primary  γ ′  precipitates. Eventually, the critical nucleation barrier  Δ G\* at a lower temperature is low enough to allow a second burst of nucleation, thus forming a very fine distribution of secondary γ ′  precipitates in the regions between the primary  γ ′  pre- cipitates, decorated by  “ depletion zone ”  (rightmost curve in Fig. 6 ). These secondary precipitates appear to be forming at temperatures evenlower than those at which themonomodal γ ′ precipitates nucleated during water-quenching (WQ0 sample) (refer  Fig. 6 ). Similar to the monomodal  γ ′  precipitates, these secondary precipitates are typically spherical and exhibit a large nucleation density ( Fig. 5 ). They are substantially smaller in size as compared to the monomodal  γ ′  precipitates, and are further away from equilibrium in terms of composition, as shown in Table 1(a) . It should be noted that on further cooling, depending on the supersaturation remaining in the  γ  matrix, additional nucleation bursts can occur resulting in multimodal size distributions of  γ ′  precipitates as seen in the microstructure.  

Finally, during fast cooling (FC0 sample) an intermediate cooling rate is encountered by the sample, and this case also exhibits the presence of multimodal size distribution of precipitates similar to the SC0 sample (middle curve in Fig. 6 ). In case of the FC0 sample too, near-equilibrium composition primary  γ ′  precipitates and far-from equilibrium secondary  γ ′  precipitates are formed. However, due to the complexity of the different competing thermodynamic and kinetic factors like increasing driving force with decreasing temperature, formation of depletion zones and supersatura- tion of matrix, change in equilibrium  γ ′  phase composition and volume fraction with reducing temperature and decreas- ing diffusion mobilities, it is difficult to determine the exact mechanism leading to the differences in the size and composition for secondary  γ ′  particles in FC0 and SC0 heat- treated conditions.  

# 4. Summary  

The experimental atom probe tomography results and energy- filtered transmission electron microscopy analysis presented in this paper indicate that there is a strong influence of the cooling rate on the formation of different generations of  γ ′ precipitates, their size, morphology and composition, along with the partitioning of elements across the  γ / γ ′  interface. With an increase in cooling rate, the size of the primary and secondary  γ ′  precipitates decreases due to the limited time available for diffusion and reduction in mobility of elements, thereby affecting the growth of the  γ ′  phase. These kinetic factors also result in non-equilibrium compositions of the secondary (and in some cases tertiary)  γ ′  precipitates formed at high undercoolings. More detailed investigations of the influence of cooling rate on the temperature as well as mechanism of formation of different generations of  γ ′  is currently underway.
