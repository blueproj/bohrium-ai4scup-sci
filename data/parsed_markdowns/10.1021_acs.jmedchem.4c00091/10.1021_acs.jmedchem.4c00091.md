ABSTRACT:  Artificial intelligence (AI)  de novo  molecular generation is a highly promising strategy in the drug discovery, with deep reinforcement learning (RL) models emerging as powerful tools. This study introduces a fragment-by-fragment growth RL forward molecular generation and optimization strategy based on a low activity lead compound. This process integrates fragment growth-based reaction templates, while target docking and drug-likeness prediction were simultaneously performed. This comprehensive approach considers molecular similarity, internal diversity, syn the s iz ability, and effectiveness, thereby enhancing the quality and efficiency of molecular generation. Finally, a series of tyrosinase inhibitors were generated and synthesized. Most  

compounds exhibited more improved activity than lead, with an optimal candidate compound surpassing the effects of kojic acid and demonstrating significant anti pigmentation activity in a zebrafish model. Furthermore, metabolic stability studies indicated susceptibility to hepatic metabolism. The proposed AI structural optimization strategies will play a promising role in accelerating the drug discovery and improving traditional efficiency.  

# INTRODUCTION  

The development and progress of machine learning (ML) and deep learning (DL) technologies in the field of artificial intelligence (AI) have brought revolutionary changes to drug discovery.   These technologies demonstrate immense potential in various stages of drug discovery and development, including target identification,  de novo  molecule design, drug screening, chemical synthesis, multi pharmacology, drug repurposing, preclinical research, approval, and postmarket analysis. − 3 They have made significant contributions to enhancing efficiency, reducing costs, and accelerating the discovery of novel drugs. In the case of target validation,  de novo  drug molecular design and generation, in particular, hold promise as pivotal starting points in drug discovery.   Numerous successful applications of AI in drug discovery have been reported with the help of a wide range of algorithm models. ,  

Reinforcement learning (RL) is a learning approach within ML/DL.   It differs from supervised and unsupervised learning by its reduced reliance on pre-existing model data. Moreover, RL dynamically receives feedback in the form of rewards from the environment for its actions.   This acquired learning information is then used to update model parameters, maximizing the anticipated benefits. Additionally, RL is a continuous decision- making process that emulates the acquisition of knowledge and the enhancement of solutions to adapt to the environment, much like the human brain.   Due to its remarkable capacity for exploration and learning, RL has gained popularity in the field of multiobjective optimization for drug molecular design. However, previous RL models based on “atom and bond” growth often produce molecules with unrealistic structures that lack syn the s iz ability and reasonable drug-like properties and therefore are often unable to verify their biological activity.   To overcome these limitations, researchers have put forward fragment-based algorithms. − 14  

Following the methodology proposed in ref  14 , we have developed a fragment-by-fragment RL forward synthesis algorithm for generating molecules based on the lead compound and reaction templates. This algorithm leverages our metic- ulously curated reaction templates and zinc’s fragment library. Its objective is to generate structurally innovative, chemically synthesizable, and more potent small molecule candidates that effectively inhibit hyper pigmentation.

Skin hyper pigmentation disorders are characterized by excessive skin pigmentation resulting from an increase in the number or activity of melanocytes.   These disorders can be classified based on their etiology, including genetic, endocrine- related, inflammatory, metabolic, drug-induced, tumor-related, physical, and artificial pigmentations.   Examples of these disorders include freckles, melasma, sunspots, Riehl’s melanosis, drug-induced eruptions, melanoma, frictional melanosis, and tattoos.   Excessive pigmentation not only has an impact on aesthetics but also poses risks for skin allergies, inflammation, metabolic disorders, neuro degenerative diseases, and tumors, leading to a significant psychological burden. − 22   Conse- quently, there has been significant attention focused on the prevention and reduction of pigmentation within the medical and cosmetic industries.  

The inhibition of melanin synthesis and the reduction of melanin concentration are crucial and commonly used strategies in the treatment of hyper pigmentation disorders.   Melanin,

Dopaquinone then undergoes further con- versions, leading to the formation of pheomelanin (red/yellow and soluble) or eumelanin (brown/black and insoluble) ( Figure 1 B).   TYR is classified as a type 3 binuclear copper-containing oxidoreductase enzyme characterized by a conserved active site composed of six histidine residues (HIS61, HIS85, HIS94, HIS259, HIS263, and HIS296) that coordinate two copper ions. In clinical practice, commonly utilized TYR inhibitors, including hydroquinone, kojic acid, arbutin, azelaic acid, and L -ascorbic acid, possess certain inhibitory activity and whitening effects. However, they are characterized by inherent drawbacks such as high water solubility, poor penetration, and inadequate stability, which can potentially result in adverse reactions like skin irritation, allergies, and toxicity. − 34   As a result, researchers have been striving to identify safer and more effective TYR inhibitors. Currently, the piperazine group, due to its basicity, flexibility, potential hydrophobic interactions, and broad pharmacological activity, has emerged as a critical component in the design of structurally innovative and highly efficacious TYR inhibitors. − 41  

In this study, we utilized a previously reported piperazine butyramide low activity TYR inhibitor as a lead compound ( T0 , IC 50  = 652.9  μ M for  L -tyrosine). This compound was fragmented to obtain a set of query fragments. A similar fragment search was performed to establish a similar fragment building block library used for molecular generation. Specifically targeting the TYR protein, appropriate fragments are selected by our algorithm in conjunction with a reaction template library to generate novel molecules with synthetic feasibility. The generated molecules underwent a series of evaluations, including attribute validation, filtering, and molecular docking. Sub- sequently, we visualized the synthesizable  de novo  molecularly generated molecules that exhibited potential TYR inhibitory activity. These molecules were further artificially screened and selected based on their structural similarity to compound  T0 , synthetic feasibility, and structural rationality. Finally, the selected candidate compounds were synthesized and evaluated for their  in vitro  and  in vivo  activities ( Figure 2 ).  

# RESULTS AND DISCUSSION  

RL Model and  De Novo  Molecular Generation.  This paper utilizes an RL model to tackle the molecular generation problem discussed earlier. It conceptualizes the process of molecular generation as an interactive exchange between an agent and the environment. At each step, the agent selects appropriate fragments and connects them to the already generated molecules based on reaction templates, gradually expanding the molecular structure. Throughout this process, the agent aims to maximize cumulative rewards by engaging with the environment. The reward function evaluates the physical and chemical properties of the generated molecules as well as their docking scores, providing feedback to the agent. The agent then selects the optimal action based on the current state representation, which includes choosing suitable fragments and reaction templates. These actions influence the subsequent state of the agent and further determine the selection of the next action. By acquiring the optimal strategy through learning, the algorithm is capable of automatically adjusting the generation process to obtain optimal molecular structures.

The RL model used in this article is a TD3 (Twin Delayed Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient) model.   TD3 is an improved version of the Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (DDPG) algorithm, specifically designed for continuous action spaces. It improves the algorithm’s performance and stability through three fundamental techniques: Twin Q-Networks, delayed policy updates, and target policy smoothing. The specific structure of the RL model is illustrated in  Figure 3 . The reaction template set utilized in this study consists of the 300 most frequently used templates from the USPTO 1K TPL data set,   comprising 68 single-molecular reaction templates and 232 dual-molecular reaction templates. Zinc’s building block library serves as the source for the molecular building block library.  

Specifically, our algorithm employs a fragment-based growth strategy. It begins by decomposing the reference molecule into multiple query fragments. These query fragments are then used to construct a collection of similar fragments, from which new molecules are generated using predefined reaction templates. The reference molecule is utilized as a target guide to direct the growth of molecules, which are selected from known active molecules. Through comparison and evaluation of similarity with the reference molecule, our algorithm can generate candidates with similar structures and properties. Training and optimization for RL are achieved by utilizing zinc’s library, which offers a diverse range of chemical fragments. At each step of reactant selection, synthetic fragments are determined using RL algorithms based on the current state (generated molecules) and available information on the reaction templates. Through iterative refining and optimizing of the process, our algorithm can generate candidates with highly rational structures and potential drug-like activity. In summary, our reference-guided, fragment-based RL forward synthesis algorithm builds upon previous research and incorporates reaction templates and fragment libraries to design and synthesize target molecules with rational structures and potential activity efficiently.  

Subsequently, the RL model was utilized to perform three rounds of molecular generation tasks based on compound  T0 and the TYR target, resulting in the generation of  ∼ 130 new molecules in each task. More importantly, this model offers predictions for various properties, including activity, ADMET characteristics, target interactions, synthesis routes, structural alerts, and overall and individual attribute distributions for each generated molecule. We finally selected 20 candidate com- pounds for actual synthesis by considering their structural similarity to compound  T0 , predicted activity parameters, drug- like properties, and feasibility of synthesis. Notably, some compounds appeared frequently across all three tasks, suggesting a higher probability for them to exhibit desirable activity as intended molecules.

Tyrosinase Inhibitory Activity.  The mushroom ( Agaricus bisporus ) tyrosinase is both low-cost and readily available, making it a highly stable and pure substrate for screening tyrosinase inhibitory activity. It has been acknowledged as an excellent alternative to human tyrosinase for  in vitro  screening. L -Dopa and  L -tyrosine serve as substrates for the diphenolase and monophenolase catalytic cycles, respectively. Kojic acid,  α - arbutin, and  β -arbutin were employed as positive controls. The enzyme inhibitory results are shown in  Table 1 . The IC 50  values of kojic acid,  α -arbutin, and  β -arbutin fall within the range reported in the literature. ,   Furthermore,  α -arbutin does not exhibit any inhibitory activity against diphenolase and shows weaker inhibitory activity against monophenolase when compared to  β -arbutin.  

The inhibitory activity of all compounds against mono- phenolase is superior to that against diphenolase. Similar to  α - arbutin, compound  T0  displays no inhibitory activity against diphenolase and exhibits weak inhibitory activity against monophenolase. Of the AI-generated molecules examined, 14 compounds demonstrated certain inhibitory activity. Among these, 11 compounds ( T1 − T4 ,  T10 − T12 ,  T14 − T16 , and T20 ) exhibited higher activity than  T0 , 3 compounds ( T17 − T19 ) showed similar activity with  T0 , and 8 compounds ( T1 − T4 ,  T10 − T12 , and  T16 ) also presented superior activity to  α - and  β -arbutin. Moreover, 7 compounds ( T1 − T4  and  T10 − T12 ) displayed certain inhibitory activity against both mono- phenolase and diphenolase. Notably, four of these compounds ( T1 − T4 ) exhibited inhibitory activity against monophenolase and diphenolase below 200 and 300  μ M, respectively. Molecular Docking and Further Binding Affinity- Verifying Structural Modifications.  To further explore and validate the interaction modes between compounds  T0  and  T3 with tyrosinase, we employed Discovery Studio software to perform CDOCKER docking of these compounds with the three-dimensional structure of tyrosinase (PDB ID: 2Y9X). The resulting docking interaction model, as depicted in  Figure 6 , represents the optimal binding configuration. It was observed that rings A and D of compound  T0  (CDOCKER score: − 12.7679) could not fully penetrate the active pocket of tyrosinase, thus failing to form interactions with the copper ions. Only the NH group on the amide portion formed a hydrogen bond with ASN260, while the hydrogen atom on the piperazine methylene moiety formed a carbon − hydrogen bond with ASN260. Moreover, ring D and its methyl group exhibited hydrophobic interactions with HIS244 and VAL248.  

However, linker C and ring D of compound  T3  (CDOCKER score:  − 72.0662) can well enter the active pocket of tyrosinase. The NH group on the amide also exhibits significant hydrogen bonding interactions with ASN260. Additionally, its hydroxyl groups on the D ring are capable of interacting with two copper ions and HIS259 and HIS263. Furthermore, ring D, the pyrimidine ring, and ring A of compound  T3  form hydrophobic interactions with surrounding amino acid residues such as ALA286, HIS263, VAL283, VAL248, SER282, PRO277, and PHE192. These insights offer an explanation for the comparatively potent enzyme inhibition activity observed with compound  T3 .

Metabolic Stability Study.  Because of the superior  in vitro tyrosinase inhibitory activity and  in vivo  anti pigmentation effect of compound  T3 , we further investigated its  in vitro  metabolic stability in human plasma ( Figure 8 A) and human liver microsomes ( Figure 8 B). The results showed that compound T3  exhibited good stability in human plasma and was resistant to metabolism. After incubation with plasma at 37  ° C for 120 min, the intact compound showed a retention rate of 94.58% and a half-life ( t 1/2 ) greater than 120 min, indicating that it can achieve a good blood concentration at the treatment site after administration and absorption. However, when it was incubated with human liver microsomes, the retention rate of the compound dropped to only 28.09% after 45 min, with a  t 1/2  of 24.6 min and an  Eh  value of 75.9%, indicating high clearance in liver microsomes. This suggests that the compound is mainly metabolized by the liver, and there is a reason to believe that it is not prone to accumulation and systemic toxicity in the body.

# EXPERIMENTAL METHODS  

Specific Algorithms for Molecular Generation.  The specific steps of the algorithm are as follows:  

•  Step 1 (reference molecule fragmentation): The BRICS algorithm is used to iteratively fragment the reference molecule based on single bonds. •  Step 2 (similar fragment retrieval): Compare the substructures obtained in the above step with the building blocks in the molecular building block library. Molecular Morgan fingerprints are extracted by using the function from RDKit. Dice Similarity is employed as the distance metric for comparing molecular similarities. Finally, fragments with a matching similarity score of 0.5 or higher are chosen as the fragment library for subsequent molecular generation. •  Step 3 (AI molecular generation): Use our predefined reaction template set and retrieved similar fragment set to perform AI molecular generation.  Figure 3  shows the structure of our RL model. The agent contains actor and critic modules. The  f 1 network in the actor module predicts the most probable reaction template,  T t , based on the input state,  R 1 t . Subsequently, the  f  2 network generates an action,  a t , based on the input state,  R 1 t , and template,  T t , which is the simulated embedding of another reactant. The critic network, referred to the  Q  network in the figure, evaluates the state,  s t  ( R 1 t ,  T t ), and the action,  a t , and calculates the expected value of the cumulative reward obtained by taking action  a t  in the current state. The critic network here actually simulates the process of learning the molecular docking algorithm (Vina) to evaluate molecules. In the Environment module, the molecular building block library is searched for the most similar fragment,  R 2 t , based on action  a  predicted by the actor. A synthesis operation based on the template  T  is carried out on  R 1 t  and  R 2 t  to obtain the reaction product. Then, molecular docking is performed on the reaction product, and the docking score is returned to the agent as a reward value. Simultaneously, the product is used as the input state,  R 1 t  , for the next step in the subsequent round of molecular growth. If the molecular weight of the final product molecule exceeds 500 or if the number of synthesis steps exceeds 3, the current synthesis process will be terminated. This method of generating molecules with a complete synthetic pathway enhances the practicality of the molecules produced. •  Step 4 (verification and filtering): Verification and filtration of the generated candidate molecules are conducted to ensure adherence to predefined physicochemical properties, including Lipinski’s Rule of Five and the limitation of the number of rings to less than or equal to five.  

•  Step 5 (evaluation and selection): The evaluation of candidate molecules that have successfully passed the verification and filtration process to select the most promising ones. This selection is based on various factors, including molecular docking results and key interactions.  

# The framework and training methods of the model are referred to ref 13 .  

For model parameters, the  f 1  and  f  2  networks both include three fully connected layers with 512 neurons in the hidden layers. BatchNorm and ReLU activation functions are applied to all hidden layers. The  Q network uses four fully connected layers with 512,256 neurons in the hidden layers. Mushroom Tyrosinase Inhibition Assay.  The tyrosinase inhibitory activity was determined by a modified method 50   using mushroom tyrosinase (EC 1.14.18.1) purchased from Sigma Chemical Co., Ltd. (T3824). Kojic acid,  α -arbutin, and  β -arbutin were used as positive controls. The stock solutions (100 mM) of compounds as well as positive controls were dissolved in DMSO and subsequently diluted with PBS (pH = 6.8) to obtain the desired concentrations (8 to 10 concentration gradients). Tyrosinase powder was dissolved in PBS to prepare 100 U/mL (for  L -dopa) and 300 U/mL (for  L -tyrosine) solutions. Also, the substrates ( L -dopa and  L -tyrosine) were prepared with PBS to obtain a 0.85 mM  L -dopa solution and 1 mM  L -tyrosine solution. One hundred sixty microliters of compound and 20  μ L of tyrosinase solution were added to a 96-well plate and incubated at 25  ° C for 10 min. Then, a 20  μ L substrate solution was added and incubated at 25  ° C for 20 min. Finally, the absorbance was measured at 475 nm immediately. PBS was used as the blank, and the mixture of tyrosinase and substrate was used as the control. Molecular Docking.  The X-ray crystal structure of the tyrosinase enzyme ( A. bisporus ) was first downloaded from the Protein Data Bank (PDB ID: 2Y9X). Subsequently, the crystal structure of TYR was prepared by adding hydrogen atoms and removing water molecules using the “Prepare Proteins” module in Discovery Studio 2020. Then, the active site of the prepared protein was defined and the original ligand tropolone (XYZ coordinates:  − 0.994776,  − 28.920136, − 43.937876; radius: 5.000000) was removed, keeping the receptor available for the upcoming molecular docking. The MDL SDfile format of compounds  T0 ,  T3 ,  T21 , and  T22  was prepared. The “Prepare Ligands” module was utilized to prepare the ligand compounds. High- precision docking of the compounds to the protein was conducted using the CDOCKER module within the docking module. Finally, the best scoring conformer of each docking result was analyzed by integrating the scoring results from the interaction energy, and the corresponding docking image was visualized. Additionally, the docking parameters were calibrated to replicate the crystal conformation of the ligand.

Metabolic Stability Study.  The metabolic stability study was performed in human plasma and human liver microsomes. For plasma stability assay, a prewarmed compound solution (1  μ L, 100  μ M) was mixed with prewarmed plasma (99  μ L) and timing started. At different time points (0, 5, 15, 30, 60, and 120 min), MeOH:MeCN (1:1) (400 μ L) containing an internal standard (IS) was added in the corresponding hole and the reaction stopped. Then, the plates were shaken for 5 min (600 rpm) and centrifuged for 20 min (6000 rpm). The mixture of supernatant (100  μ L) and ultrapure water (100  μ L) was further used for LC/MS analysis.  

For microsomal stability assay, a 1.5  μ M compound solution containing 0.75 mg/mL microsomes (30  μ L) was dispensed into the assay plates designated for different time points (0, 5, 15, 30, and 45 min) and the other plate was preincubated at 37  ° C for 5 min. At different time points, a NADPH solution (6 mM, 15  μ L) was added to start the reaction and timing. Subsequently, MeOH:MeCN (1:1) (150 μ L) containing IS was added to stop the reaction. Then, the plates were shaken for 10 min (600 rpm) and centrifuged for 15 min (4000 rpm). The mixture of supernatant (80  μ L) and ultrapure water (140  μ L) was further used for LC/MS analysis.
