# The Crystalline Behavior and Device Function of  Nonfullerene Acceptors in Organic Solar Cells  

Jinqiu Xu, Francis Lin, Lei Zhu, Ming Zhang, Tianyu Hao, Guanqing Zhou, Ke Gao,\*  Yecheng Zou, Gang Wei, Yuanping Yi, Alex K.-Y. Jen,\* Yongming Zhang,\* and Feng Liu\* The current research investigates the structure features and intermolecular  interactions of nonfullerene acceptors (NFAs) in single crystal and thin films, as  well as their solar cell applications. Guiding parameters and key intermolecular  forces that lead to 2D brickwork or 3D web packing are identified. The atomic  modification is shown as the key to induce hydrogen bonding or  π – π  stacking  column, which results in different crystalline packing. The molecular assembly  in thin film is initiated by hydrogen bonding, and completed by  π – π  stacking  reorganization. The packing energy is seen as a guiding parameter that dictates  the NFA crystalline morphology in blended thin films. The crystalline packing  motif is not directly related with device efficiency. However, the crystalline mor- phology is the key parameter to influence exciton/carrier dynamics and device  performance. A broader picture on the scaling behavior of organic semicon- ductor crystals ranging from oligoacenes to NFAs is established.  

# 1. Introduction  

Organic  semiconductors  are  widely  used  as  active  materials  in  opto- electronic devices.  The molecular  packing in thin film is regarded as one  of the key issues that influence device  performances, by which the carrier  transport is determined.  The single  crystal is taken as a perfect platform to  investigate the structure–property rela- tionship in organic field effect transistor  research.  In organic photovoltaic  (OPV),  phase-separated  morphology  is also coupled with the crystalline  behavior of molecules in blends, which  dictates the exciton splitting and carrier  transport.  As demonstrated in non- fullerene acceptor (NFA) solar cells, the molecular packing  dictates the crystalline morphology and phase-separated  nanostructure in bulk heterojunction thin films, which  strongly influences the photo-physics and solar cell device  performances.  

Molecules assemble into crystals via van der Walls forces,  and by adjusting the geometry and electrostatic interactions  to achieve tight packing.  Atom and group modifica- tion are commonly used to manipulate the intermolecular  interactions to fine-tune the crystalline structure.  For  NFA, the co-existence of the backbone and the side-chain  make crystalline packing complex, since the electrostatic  interaction and side-chain interaction need to be balanced,  and the geometry issue needs to be considered.  These  properties are of particular importance in organic solar cell,  which determine the NFA crystallization behavior as well  as its mixing with donor polymer.  One step further,  the carrier generation, transport, and recombination will  be affected.  In this work, we systematically investigate  the crystalline behavior of NFA materials. Classic ITIC and  its modified derivatives were chosen as a platform. Single  crystal structures with specific interactions were studied,  and fundamental mechanism of structure formation was  proposed. NFA crystallization in neat and blended thin  films was studied, and their crystal plane ordering prefer- ence was determined. The crystalline morphology and its  impact on device performances, including photo-physical  properties, energy losses, and power conversion efficiencies  were summarized. Finally, the organic semiconductor crys- talline scaling behavior from oligoacenes to NFAs has been  investigated.  

# 2. Results and Discussions  

# 2.1. NFA Molecular Structure and Crystalline Packing  

We collect ITIC,  TRID, SRID, IT4F,  TRID-4F, and SRID- 4F as a group to investigate the crystalline structure change  upon chemical modifications. The backbone of ITIC contains  one in d ace no dith ie no[3,2- b ]-thiophene (IDTT) center and two  2-(3-oxo-2,3-dihydroinden-1-ylidene)-malononitrile  (IC)  end  groups, which can form intramolecular push–pull structure.  TRID and SRID are selenium modified on IDTT at different  positions, and IT4F, TRID-4F, and SRID-4F are the fluorinated  derivatives (Figure S1, Supporting Information). Single crys- tals for these molecules are presented in  Figure   1 . We first  compared the structure differences to identify the conformers  (marked in different colors) and major packing modes. Four  interactions,  π – π  stacking, hydrogen bonding (C  H···N,  C  H···O, and S  F, shown in Figure S4 of the Supporting  Information), S– π  interaction between the two IDTT groups,  and side-chain interaction (the Van der Waals force between  alkyl side chains), are identified as the major packing forces,  and different combinations in molecules dictate the crystalline  structure. Three major packing modes, 2D brickwork, 3D web,  and crossover, are obtained. For ITIC, TRID, and SRID, twisted  2D brickwork structures are seen, which self-assemble via side- chain interaction and hydrogen bonding to form 3D crystal lat- tices. The twisting-angle is dictated by the packing energy of  hydrogen bonding, and their averaged packing energy of  π – π interactions are similar (Figure 1a–c). For IT4F, TRID-4F, and  SRID-4F, the fluorination leads to the enhanced packing energy  of  π – π  interactions, which breaks up the balance with hydrogen  bonding, and results in complicated crystalline structures. As  seen in IT4F, hydrogen bonding and  π – π  stacking induced  2D layers and 1D chains. They cross-overlaid via S– π  interac- tion to form 3D structure (Figure 1d). In TRID-4F and SRID- 4F, the  π – π  stacking is strong, which biases out the hydrogen  bonding interaction, and the Se at different position leads to  the change of  π – π  packing’s direction. For TRID-4F, the  π – π stacking extends to a column structure, which pins two sets  of grids to form a 3D web structure (Figure 1e). For SRID-4F,  linear  π – π  stacking is formed, which leads to 2D brickwork  packing and side-chain-mediated lamellae assembly (Figure 1f).  More detailed structure features can be found in Figures S2–S4  of Supporting Information. The balance between  π – π  stacking  and hydrogen bonding leads to different crystalline structures,  and the observations are extended to the Br and Cl atom-modi- fied ITIC derivatives (Figure S5, Supporting Information). The  ITIC-2Cl- β  and ITIC-2Cl- δ  molecules adopt a twisted 2D  brickwork layer with  π – π  stacking and hydrogen bonding, and  they further assemble in lamellar packing (Figure S8, Sup- porting Information). The ITIC-2Cl- γ  molecule turns to form  π – π  stacking column and a 3D web structure. And the 3D web  structure can also be observed in ITIC-2Br- γ  and a-ITIC- 2Cl  (Figure S9, Supporting Information). Thus, the position  of modification is critical in dictating the  π – π  stacking type and  direction to induce different crystalline structures.  

The hydrogen bonding interaction in NFA crystals is an  important feature that influences the crystalline packing struc- ture. In ITIC, no obvious hydrogen bonding is seen (Figure S4a,  Supporting Information). When Se atom is introduced into the  IDTT group or Cl atom modification on  β ,  δ  site of the IC group,  the electrostatic potential (ESP) value of H and N atoms in the  IC groups increases (Figures S10 and S12, Supporting Informa- tion), which results in stronger hydrogen bonding, and induces  the formation of 2D assembly. The hydrogen bonding is direc- tional, creating intersectional angles between conformers that  dictate the shape of the voids in-between lamellae layer. The 3D  web structure is formed via the  π – π  stacking columns. Thus, the  competition and balance of hydrogen bonding and  π – π  stacking  determine the crystal structure. In extreme cases, when the  π – π stacking and hydrogen bonding layers are not confined in one  plane, distorted assembly is obtained, as demonstrated by IT4F  (Figure 1d). The packing coefficient ( C K ), crystal density ( ρ ), and  packing energy density of crystal’s supercell ( ρ PE ) have been cal- culated to evaluate the crystal properties ( Table   1  and Table S1   of Supporting Information), the detail of calculation process  of  ρ PE  has been listed in Table S1 in the Supporting Informa- tion. It is noticed that the  C K ,  ρ , and  ρ PE  have no correlation  with crystal packing, since the complex molecule arrangement  leads to large unit cell volume variations ( Figure   2 a–c). For 2D  brickwork family (ITIC, TRID, SRID, SRID-4F, ITIC-2Cl- β , and  ITIC-2Cl- δ ), the SRID-4F molecules form a linear stacking, and  the other molecules pack similarly with a twisting angle. The  ρ PE  is quite small for SRID-4F ( − 0.144 kJ mol − 1  Å –3 , which sum- maries the packing energy from both backbone and side-chain.  The  ρ PE  for ITIC is  − 0.212 kJ mol − 1  Å –3 . It is thus considered the  side-chain in rhombus voids tends to exhibit a stronger contri- bution to condense the molecules. This speculation is supported  by the packing energy of side-chain on ITIC ( − 11.2  kJ mol − 1 and SRID-4F ( − 2.5 kJ mol − 1 , shown in Figure 1a,f). For the 3D  web family (TRID-4F, ITIC-2Cl- γ , ITIC-2Br- γ , and a-ITIC-2Cl),  a unique feature is that the packing energy of  π – π  stacking is  much higher comparing to that of 2D brickwork family. Since  π – π  stacking columns are formed in 3D web crystals, which  significantly enhances the packing energy. The 2D brickwork  and 3D web packing can thus be separated by the packing  energy of  π – π  stacking (Figure  2d). The packing angle in 2D  brickwork and the atom–atom interaction energy (hydrogen  bonding energy) relationship are summarized in Figure 2e. It  is seen that a larger packing energy leads to a smaller twisted  angle, and this interaction locks different lamellae assembly,  forming voids of different shape and size (Figure  1a–c and  Figure S8 of Supporting Information). Thus, even in the 2D  brickwork packing, there are interactions in-between the brick- work layers beyond side-chain interactions (except SRID-4F),  which make the assembly more robust. The relationship  between the packing angle and the packing energy of  π – π stacking in 3D web family is summarized in Figure  2f. The  twisted grids (TRID-4F) lead to a small packing energy. In other  cases, the  π – π  stacking energies are similar but in different  angles, which is due to atomic modification-induced geometry  change. The restrained electrostatic potential (RESP) atomic  charge has been calculated (Figures S14–S24, Supporting Infor- mation),  and the subgroup atomic charge influence on  molecular packing has been considered. The CN group (the  A part, Figure 2g) is negatively charged (Table S3, Supporting  Information), which interacts with peripheral atoms to form  hydrogen bonding, which is seen in the 2D brickwork family.

The current density–voltage ( J–V ) curves  and external quantum efficiency (EQE) spectra are presented  in  Figure   5 a,b, and the data are summarized in Table  1. Both Se and F atoms modification leads to a red-shift  in absorption and increases the  J sc  (Figure S31 of Supporting  Information). SRID blends exhibit a low FF, which leads to  a small EQE and PCE (9.19%). SRID-4F devices deliver the  largest  J sc  and the largest PCE  ≈ 13.68%.

# 2.4. General Scaling Behavior of Organic Semiconductor Crystals  

The current research reveals the crystalline structure of NFA  molecules, and a common ground comparison is made with  widely studied oligoacenes to establish the scaling behavior  of organic semiconductor crystals. We collect ten oligoacenes  and TIPS-pentacene derivatives with known crystalline struc- tures (Figure S40, Supporting Information), and compared  with NFAs to examine their crystalline packing properties.  Tetracene, pentacene, and hexacene adopt the herringbone  packing which induced by C–H– π  interaction, and these her- ringbone layers stack via molecular edge interaction to form  lamellar packing (Figure S41a–i of Supporting Information). For  Cl-modified tetracene, two molecules stack together through  π – π  interaction and form a unit, which then assembles to form  sandwich-herringbone packing (Figure S41j–l of Supporting  Information). Double Cl-modified tetracene or pentacene  molecule adopts a 1D slipped stacking that has a strong  π – π  inter- action and weak side interaction (Figure S41m–r of Supporting  Information).  In these molecules, the C–H– π  interaction  energy ranges from  − 15.3 to  − 59.4 kJ mol − 1 , and the  π – π  inter- action energy ranges from  − 54.8 to  − 83.6 kJ mol − 1 . And when  the  π – π  interaction exceeds the C–H– π  interaction, molecule  will adopt 1D slipped stacking (Figure S41 of Supporting Infor- mation). TIPS-pentacene that introduces the solubilizing TIPS  group excludes the C–H– π  interaction, and induces the 2D  brickwork  π – π  stacking. The TIPS group forms a separation  layer, similar to the side-chains in NFA. Different solubilizing  group has a profound influence on molecule packing as seen  from the difference of TIPS-pentacene and TES-pentacene  (Figure S42a–f of Supporting Information). Meanwhile, atom  modification of TIPS-pentacene (4Br-TIPS-pentacene) shifts  the  π – π  stacking and forms 1D slipped stacking (Figure S42j–l  of Supporting Information).  The situation becomes much  more complicated for NFA, in which the conjugated back- bone contains D/A segments that electronic structure changes  significantly. We compared the quadrupole moment, the crystal density,  the packing coefficient, and the packing energy density of  organic semiconductor super cells ( Figure   6  and Table S25 of  Supporting Information). For oligoacenes, the  C k ’s are around  72%, and the quadrupole moments have a small value. The  atomic modification significantly decreases the value of  ρ PE since the C–H– π  interaction energy density is much higher  than the  π – π  interaction energy density ( − 0.447  kJ mol − 1  Å –3 for pentacene, and  − 0.291  kJ mol − 1  Å –3  for 2Cl-pentacene,  Figure S41d,p of Supporting Information). In TIPS-pen- tacene derivatives, the  π – π  interaction dominates, and the  side-chain volume reduces  π – π  interaction density and  ρ PE ( − 0.158  kJ mol − 1  Å –3  for TIPS-pentacene). The  C k  decreases  since the side-chain packing is loose (63% for TIPS-pentacene,  smaller than 72% for pentacene). The quadrupole moments for  TIPS-pentacene derivatives are also small, and a rise is seen for  tetrabromo-TIPS-pentacene, indicating a large push–pull effect  leads to stronger electron static interactions. The NFA series  has a large quadrupole moment but with small  ρ PE  due to the  side-chain volume increase. The increased backbone length and  the balance between  π – π  interaction and hydrogen bonding is  key in tuning the packing angles, resulting in packing in 2D or  3D. The  C k ’s of NFAs are expected to be low ( ≈ 60%), which is  not correlated with packing type, since the side-chain packing is  not in crystalline order.  

# 3. Conclusion  

To conclude, we have systematically investigated the crystal- line packing of ITIC-based NFAs in single crystal, neat and  blended thin films. The packing of NFA molecules is critical  in influencing the exciton dissociation and charge transport,  which inevitably affects the performance of solar cell devices.  It is seen that two major types of packing, the 2D brickwork  and the 3D web structures exist in NFAs, which are determined  by the competition of  π – π  stacking and hydrogen bonding. The  3D web structure is a new packing motif for organic semicon- ductors, which is featured with the formation of the strong  π – π  stacking columns. The existence of strong intermolecular  interactions is more readily to drive the ordering of NFA mole- cules in blended thin films, leading to enhanced crystallinity.  The influence of crystalline phase in device affects mostly the  exciton diffusion and carrier transport, as seen from the strong  T diff  and performance correlation. It should be noted that the  crystalline structure could not be directly correlated with  device performances. In blended thin film, the crystal mor- phology, crystallinity, and packing motif need to be integrated  to construct the structure–performance relationship. And the  “molecular  structure–intermolecular  interaction–crystalline  structure–thin film morphology–performance correlation”  research streamline could afford key insights to support new  material design and device optimization.
