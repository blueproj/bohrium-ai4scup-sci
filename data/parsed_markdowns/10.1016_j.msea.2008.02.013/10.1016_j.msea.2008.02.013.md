Tensile properties of M951 alloy are shown to be signiﬁcantly dependent on temperatures in this study. Like in other superalloys, the yield and tensile strengths decreased slightly between room temperature and 600   ◦ C, then both strengths showed peak values at intermediate temperatures, and both decreased sharply above 850   ◦ C. The elongation ﬂuctuated with temperature with its minimum at 800   ◦ C. The TEM observations showed that at low temperature the dominant deformation mechanism was   ′   shearing by dislocation pairs and slip bands. At high temperature, the deformation was dominated by   ′   by-pass. At intermediate temperature it showed a transition from   ′   shearing to by-passing.

# 1. Introduction  

It is clearly understood that the high temperature behav- ior of most Ni-based superalloys are affected by the L1 2  type ordered   ′   phase  [1] . At temperature lower than 800   ◦ C, cuboidal  ′   precipitates almost maintain their size and shape during creep deformation because the diffusion rates of alloy elements are rela- tively low; while above 850   ◦ C, the   ′   precipitates become unstable and link together to form plates  [2] . The mechanical properties are inﬂuenced by the shape and size of   ′   precipitates  [3,4] .  

The alloy with high volume fraction of   ′   precipitates investi- gated in this work was newly developed by the Institute of Metal Research (IMR) and was named M951  [14,15] . It is a conventional cast Ni-based superalloy and is mainly used as gas turbine vane material. In this study, the tensile deformation behavior of M951 alloy has been systematically investigated at temperatures from room temperature to 1100   ◦ C. The deformation modes with dif- ferent temperature range were analyzed and determined by TEM observation.  

Very complicated yielding behavior is exhibited by nickel-based superalloys containing a high volume fraction of the   ′   phase [5,6] . For example, IN738LC alloy exhibits a ﬂat minimum of yield strength at 450   ◦ C and a maximum at 750   ◦ C due to characteristic deformation mechanisms  [7] , also this phenomenon was observed in the single crystal superalloy PWA1480  [8] . The anomalous increase of the ﬂow stress at high temperature may be attributed to a change in the mechanism, which involves cross slip of screw dislocations from (1 1 1) slip planes to the (0 1 0) planes in   ′   phase [9] . The driving force for the change of slip mode with increasing temperature is provided both by the anisotropy of the anti-phase boundary (APB) and by the resolved shear stress on the  { 1 0 0 } ⟨ 1 1 0 ⟩ system  [10] . # 2. Experimental procedure  

The nominal composition of M951 alloy used in this work is listed in  Table 1 . It is a   ′ -strengthened alloy containing about 58 vol.%   ′   phase in the as-cast condition, and Al is the main  ′   stabilizer in this alloy. The master alloy was melted in a VIM25F vacuum induction furnace, remelted in 10 kg ZG-0.01 vac- uum induction furnace, and then cast into test bars. The mold preheating temperature and pouring temperature were 900 and 1450   ◦ C, respectively. The specimens were directly aged at 1100   ◦ C for 4 h, and air cooled (AC) to the room temperature. Specimens for tensile test with a diameter of 5 mm and a gauge length of 25 mm were machined longitudinally from the heat-treated bars.  

The tensile tests were carried out at ten different temperatures from 20 to 1100   ◦ C using a AG-250KNE machine. The strain rate was maintained 3.3  ×  10 − 4   s − 1   up to yield and 1.7  ×  10 − 3   s − 1   after yield. Two specimens were tested at each temperature.

# 3. Results and discussion  

# 3.1. Microstructures  

TEM images of microstructures for as-cast and heat-treated M951 alloy are shown in  Fig. 1 . As can be seen the unimodal   ′ precipitates in the as-cast condition has cuboidal morphology with an average edge length of 0.649   m, and the total volume percent of which is about 58%. After the standard heat treatment (aged at 1100   ◦ C for 4 h followed by air cooling), the alloy consists of bimodal  ′   distribution with cuboids particles of 0.730   m average edge length and spherical particles of 60–70 nm average diameter. The cuboidal   ′   particles in the standard heat treatment are less reg- ular than that of as-cast, and the space between the cuboids are much larger. Since the solution temperature range of   ′   precipi- tates is 1153–1200   ◦ C, some of the   ′   precipitates may be dissolved at such a high aging temperature, and cause the precipitation of ﬁne spherical particles of   ′ . The total volume percent of   ′   precipitates is about 49.4%, less than that of the as-cast condition.  

# 3.2. Mechanical properties  

Tensile test results are illustrated in  Fig. 2 . The yield strength (YS) and ultimate tensile strength (UTS) show similar temperature dependence. As in other superalloys, the yield strength decreases slightly between room temperature and 600   ◦ C. It increases with temperature until it reaches a peak at 800   ◦ C. Beyond this point, the yield strength decreases sharply. The UTS-temperature curve has the same trend as that of YS-temperature curve. Similar strength

Similar to the temperature dependence of tensile strength, the elongation decreases slightly between room temperature and 600   ◦ C, and it reaches a small peak at 700   ◦ C. The minimum value of elongation occurs at 800   ◦ C corresponding to the peak stress, then it increases rapidly with temperature, the second unobvious ductility minimum exists at 1040   ◦ C. The decrease of elongation with temperature during the low temperature range (below 600   ◦ C) is different to that has been observed in other superalloys  [1,16,18] , where the elongation keeps constant or increasing with temperature. This could be attributed to the different deformation mechanisms.  

# 3.3. Deformation structures  

To describe the deformation structure of the fractured speci- mens, three temperature ranges are considered.  

# 3.3.1. Low temperatures (under 600   ◦ C)  

Fig. 3  shows the TEM micrograph of deformation structures at room temperature (20   ◦ C). High density tangling of dislocations is observed in the matrix channels where ﬁne   ′   precipitates are dis- tributed. There are also presence of dislocation pairs and residual loops within the   ′ , which indicates evidence of   ′   shearing  [19] . The tangling of dislocations in the matrix channels are related to the ﬁne spherical   ′   precipitates, which block the further move- ment of dislocations in to the large   ′   particles. Dislocations travel through the   ′   as closely spaced pairs in order to minimize the APB area created by the  a /2 ⟨ 1 1 0 ⟩ displacement of the superlattice  [20] . Both the tangling in the matrix channels and   ′   shearing lead to the relatively high strength at 20   ◦ C. It is seen that the dislocations are homogeneously distributed throughout the specimen as indicated by the uniform contrast in the micrograph, and this is in agreement with the relatively high elongation as shown in  Fig. 2 .  

Deformation at 600   ◦ C is different from that of room tempera- ture. The deformation structure is inhomogeneous, and it consists of intense single slip bands as shown in  Fig. 4 (a). The slip bands

# 3.3.2. Intermediate temperatures (600–850   ◦ C)  

With increasing temperature to 700   ◦ C, the number of slip bands increase, and multiple slip is activated on the octahedral  { 1 1 1 } ⟨ 1 1 0 ⟩ slip system, the intersecting slip bands are shown in  Fig. 5 . The alignment of strong residual contrasts parallel to the projec- tion of the slip direction is attributed to small loops and debris which cannot be resolved because of high density. This structure of slip bands was also observed in two single crystal superalloys  [21] . Dislocation substructure in the other regions is similar to that of 600   ◦ C. As a whole, the slight increase of tensile strength is caused by the similar dislocation substructures and the increase of ductility is attributed to the multiple slip.  

At peak temperature of ultimate tensile strength, i.e. 760   ◦ C, the dominant deformation mechanism is   ′   shearing by  a /2 ⟨ 1 1 0 ⟩ dis- locations within the large   ′   precipitates ( Fig. 6 (a)), which leads to the high strength. Also, intersecting slip bands is observed at this temperature, but their number is much less than that of 700   ◦ C. Comparing to low temperatures, the dislocations are mainly dis- tributed within the   ′ , and their distribution is more homogeneous. Another evidence of   ′   shearing is the array of dislocations, as show in  Fig. 6 (b). Actually the single dislocations in the   ′   are in pairs, they are constricted within the   ′   due to the high APB energy, while those of the same dislocations which emerge out of the   ′   precipitates are split into pairs due to the elastic repulsion  [19] .  

At 800   ◦ C the yield strength and elongation of the alloy reach maximum and minimum, respectively. The effect of mid range duc- tility minimum (MRDM) take place, and this effect is also observed at the same temperature in M963 alloy and it can be eliminated by heat treatment  [18] . The deformation structure is much more different than 760   ◦ C though the tensile strengths are very close. Fig. 7  shows the dislocation substructure of the fractured specimen deformed at 800   ◦ C. In  Fig. 7 (a) high density of dislocation tangles is observed at the interface of   ′ /   precipitates, however, they are not evenly distributed in the whole specimen. As can be seen, there are a few of dislocations cutting into the large   ′   precipitates. Some stacking faults fringes are also observed, as shown in  Fig. 7 (b), indi- cating that deformation begin to occur by slip of  a /3 ⟨ 1 1 2 ⟩ partials through the   ′ . Many researchers have investigated the formation of stacking faults  [7,19,22] , and their results showed that the stacking faults were created by dislocation of type  a /3 ⟨ 1 1 2 ⟩ shearing the   ′ phase. Like tangling structure in room temperature deformation, it appears that the dislocation tangles which impede the movement of dislocations and further elongation caused the high strength and low ductility. In addition, the shearing of   ′   by stacking faults also leads to the high tensile strength.

# 4. Conclusions  

The tension test was carried out in the range of 20–1100   ◦ C. The results show that tensile properties of M951 alloy are signif- icantly dependent on temperature. The yield and tensile strengths decrease slightly between room temperature and 600   ◦ C and grad- ually increase with peak values at intermediate temperatures, then both strengths decreased sharply with test temperature higher than 850   ◦ C. The elongation has its minimum at 800   ◦ C. The TEM analysis of deformation structure shows that the tensile behavior is related to the deformation mechanisms operating at different temperature regimes. At low temperatures the dominant deformation mech- anism is   ′   shearing by dislocation pairs and slip bands. At high temperatures, the deformation is dominated by   ′   by-pass includ- ing Orowan looping and climb-assisted by-passing. At intermediate temperatures there is a transition from   ′   shearing to by-passing.
