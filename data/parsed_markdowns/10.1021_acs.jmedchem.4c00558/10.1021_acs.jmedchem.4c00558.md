ABSTRACT:  Clostridioides difficile  ( C. difficile ) is one of the leading causes of healthcare- associated infections worldwide. The increasing incidence of strains resistant to currently available therapies highlights the need for alternative treatment options with a novel mode of action. Oxazolidinones that are connected to a quinolone moiety with a pyrrolidine linker, such as compound  1 , are reported to exhibit potent broadspectrum antibacterial activity. In an effort to optimize this class of compounds for the treatment of  C. difficile  infection (CDI), we have identified cadazolid ( 9 ), a first-in-class quinoxolidinone antibiotic, which is a potent inhibitor of  C. difficile  protein synthesis. In order to achieve narrow-spectrum coverage of clinically most relevant strains without affecting the gut microbiota, an emphasis was placed on abolishing activity against commensals of the intestinal microbiome while retaining good coverage of pathogenic  C. difficile , including hypervirulent and epidemic strains.

Clostridioides difficile , an anaerobic Gram-positive, spore- and toxin-producing pathogen, is the most common cause for nosocomial antibiotic-associated diarrhea and colitis in developed countries. − 4   According to a landmark report published by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC),  C. difficile  was categorized as an urgent threat to human health.   Furthermore, the emergence and rapid dissemination of hypervirulent strains of  C. difficile  (e.g., ribotype 027, 078, 244) lead to high burden in the U.S. with estimated cases of 476,000 and 462,000 in 2011 and 2017, respectively.   Approximately 30% of  C. difficile  infections (CDI) have a community onset,   and age, antibiotic use, and recent hospitalization were identified as independent risk factors for recurrence. , ,  

The emergence of antibiotic-resistant bacteria has been recognized as one of the biggest threats to global health today. ,   To overcome this challenge, efforts are needed to improve surveillance, stewardship, and innovative and more effective classes of antibiotics that evade contemporary resistance mechanisms are required to ensure the continued utility of antibiotics.   In addition to targeting novel enzymatic pathways not represented in clinically applied antibiotics, the combination of well-established pharmacophores, known to elicit a desired antimicrobial effect, into a single chemically and metabolically stable molecule represents an attractive alter- native to exploit multiple modes of action with new chemical matter. − 20

Fidaxomicin is now recom- mended as first-line treatment option for initial and recurrent CDI, and metronidazole is no longer recommended. , Unfortunately, however, significant disease relapse rates are observed with all approved antibiotics when hypervirulent strains are present. Interestingly, while keeping the pharmacophores in the heterodimeric compound constant, the nature of the linker was reported to have a significant impact on the drug properties, including potency on the target as well as antibacterial activity and spectrum.   Analogues tethered through a pyrrolidine linker, as in compound  1 , showed particularly good and well-balanced target potencies in protein synthesis and topoisomerase inhibition assays, irrespective of the stereochemistry at the pyrrolidine ring. Together, this translated into superior antibacterial activities against a broad range of pathogens, including difficult-to-treat Gram-negative bacteria, such as e.g.,  Escherichia coli . On the other hand, subtle changes made to the linker moiety in terms of the ring size and exit vector geometry led to compounds with reduced topoisomerase activity and an antibacterial spectrum largely limited to Gram-positive bacteria, thus suggesting protein synthesis as predominant mode of action, presumably via pronounced importance of the oxazolidinone scaffold.  

These findings encouraged us to further investigate this novel class of compounds as a possible treatment of CDI. Herein, we report the design, synthesis, structure − activity relationships, and biological evaluation of novel analogues starting from previously published lead compound  1 .   These efforts have led to the discovery of cadazolid ( 9 ), a first-in-class quinoxolidinone antibiotic designed for the treatment of CDI in humans, which had progressed into Phase 3 clinical trials. , − 38   A cryo-electron microscopy study dedicated to elucidating the mode of action revealed that  9  is inhibiting bacterial protein synthesis by interfering with the binding of tRNA to the A-site of the ribosomal peptidyl transferase center.   Therein, the oxazolidinone moiety of  9  was found to bind to the same pocket and in a similar orientation as reported for other oxazolidinone antibiotics. Although preclinical  in vitro  and  in vivo  antibacterial activities of  9 against  C. difficile  have been reported in detail,   in this work, particular emphasis is placed on drug profiling to ensure high local concentrations at the infection site in the gut while keeping systemic exposure minimal to avoid possible side effects.

# CHEMISTRY  

Conceptually, the scaffold of the heterodimeric quinoxolidi- none structure can be divided into three parts, the left-hand side (LHS) or quinolone part, the spacer moiety consisting of a cyclic or linear 4- to 7-atom linker, and the right-hand side (RHS) or the oxazolidinone part. Apart from the RHS acetamido headgroup, a variety of different headgroups were reported to yield oxazolidinone antibiotics with reasonable antibacterial activities, including e.g., five-membered hetero- cycles, ,   halogen atoms, and the hydroxy group.   Although efforts to substitute the acetamido headgroup with five- membered heterocycles as originally reported by Riedl 40   and Gravestock 41   resulted in analogues with markedly reduced activities (data not shown), the hydroxy group was found to be a reasonable replacement, giving rise to analogues  2  and  3  with a similar activity profile when compared to  1 . This finding helped us to facilitate the general synthetic access and to address possible cost of goods considerations associated with the anticipated high dose needed at an early stage. As a result, we started our investigations with hydroxymethyl analogue  3 displaying a quinolone scaffold (V = CH), which is more straightforward to synthesize than the naphthyridinone scaffold (V = N) of analogue  2  ( Figure 2 ). # RESULTS AND DISCUSSION  

In order to identify compounds with a product profile suitable for treatment of CDI, our primary focus was placed on potent antibacterial activity against strains of pathogenic  C. difficile . In addition to good activity against a wild-type (wt) strain ( C. difficile  ATCC 700057), particular emphasis was put on coverage of both LZD-resistant (LZD R ) and FQ-resistant (FQ R ) strains, including the hypervirulent NAP1/BI/027 strain ( C. difficile  NCTC 13366). Coverage of vancomycin- resistant enterococci (VRE) was also considered an important asset since increased intestinal colonization with VRE strains is frequently observed during oral therapy of CDI with vancomycin and metronidazole.   In addition, an enterococcal clinical isolate harboring the most prevalent oxazolidinone resistance mechanism, which at the same time is resistant to FQs, was included as well. Finally, a  Bacteroides  strain complemented the panel as a marker for effects on the cultivable Gram-negative anaerobic commensal gut microflora. The genus  Bacteroides  represents the most abundant cultivable group in the normal anaerobic gut microflora; 48   accordingly, Bacteroides theta iota omicron  was selected as it had been reported to be the most prevalent species.   The compounds were also evaluated in an  in vitro  transcription/translation (IVTT) assay using  C. difficile  extracts to measure their interaction with protein biosynthesis. As it turned out, none of the compounds were active in a  C. difficile  DNA gyrase assay at any measurable concentration (data not shown), indicating that the contribution of DNA replication to the antibacterial effect against  C. difficile  for this class of compounds can be neglected. Furthermore, in addition to a suitable antimicrobial profile, an ideal oral CDI drug is expected to achieve high local concentrations in the gut while exhibiting low systemic exposure to avoid possible side effects. When replacing the acetamido group in  1  with a hydroxy group, the antibacterial profile of the resulting analogue compound  2  remained unaffected. On the other hand, a loss in activity was observed when the 6-fluoro-1,8-naphthyridinone scaffold (V = N) was replaced with the more readily accessible quinolone scaffold (V = CH), such as that represented in CIP. In addition to a 2- to 3-fold lower target potency, the corresponding compound ( 3 ) did not cover strains resistant to LZD. The latter is a remarkable finding given the recently published mode of action studies, demonstrating that the fluoroquinolone moiety in  9  prevents the elongation of the polypeptide chain without interacting with the oxazolidinone binding pocket located in the peptidyl transferase center.  

Nevertheless, due to more readily accessible starting materials, we decided first to explore the impact of the spacer moiety based on tool compound  3 . To compensate for a possible loss in bioactivity, we envisaged to combine the identified spacer with different FQ-derived LHS scaffolds as well as known oxazolidinone RHS substituents at a later point. First, the 3-hydroxymethyl pyrrolidine linker found in compounds  1 − 3  was converted to the isomeric 4-hydrox- ypiperidine moiety (compound  4 ), which led to a slight decrease in remaining  C. difficile  activity and a complete loss of activity against the VRE strain. Elongation of the spacer moiety by inserting an additional methylene group to form compound 5  led to an improvement against the wt and hypervirulent strains. Interestingly, comparing  4  with  5 , the 4-fold decrease in MIC of  5  at constant activity in the IVTT assay may be explained by improved cellular uptake. On the other hand, adding yet another methylene group (compound  6 ) led to an improved profile, covering the double-resistant mutant  

(LZD /FQ ) as well as the VRE strain (MICs 2 g/mL). In a next step, we aimed at investigating the effect of a quaternary hydroxy group attached to the exit vector of the heterocyclic spacer. Thus, comparing the corresponding analogues  3  and  7 , the latter displayed a 3-fold better potency in the IVTT assay, resulting in a reasonable antibacterial spectrum covering all of the pathogens with the exception of the LZD- and FQ-resistant  E. faecium  clinical isolate. Subsequently, changes in the ring size were assessed as exemplified with a 3-hydroxy a ze ti dine ( 8 ) and 4-hydroxypiper- idine ( 9 ) spacer. Incorporating a four-membered ring led to a loss of activity, as seen for compound  8 . In significant contrast, however, expanding the ring size from a five-membered to a six-membered ring resulted in a considerable boost in activity. The corresponding compound  9  exhibited yet another 2-fold increase in target potency (IVTT IC 50 : 380 nM), which translated into an outstanding antibacterial profile charac- terized by MICs  ≤ 1  μ g/mL against all of the relevant pathogens. Very importantly, this was achieved while largely sparing the Gram-negative gut microbiota, suggested by the relatively poor activity against  Bacteroides  (MIC not decreasing below 16  μ g/mL). As it turned out,  9  was poorly soluble ( ≤ 2 μ g/mL), which would be in line with our objective of minimizing oral bioavailability to avoid systemic exposure. With this excellent result in hand, we further examined the impact of exit vector geometry on the piperidine ring. As exemplified with compounds  10 − 12 , a 3-substituted piperidine linker gave rise to more than 10-fold higher MICs and, hence to an antibacterial profile not suitable for CDI treatment. The same conclusion was drawn for acyclic spacers, as demon- strated with a 1,3-aminopropanol (compound  13 ) and a 1,2- aminoethanol linker (compound  14 ). Finally, replacing the ether linkage in  9  with an amino group (compound  15 ) resulted in an 8-fold lower potency against all strains tested, irrespective of their underlining resistance mechanism, with the exception of  Bacteroides  remaining unaffected.  

With the improved 4-hydroxy-hydroxy methyl piper i dine linker in hand, we next turned our interest to the importance of the quinolone LHS while keeping the rest of the molecule unchanged ( Table 2 ). In an effort to investigate the influence of the cyclopropyl group attached to the 1-position of the quinolone scaffold, compounds  16 − 18  were synthesized. As it turned out, replacing cyclopropyl with ethyl such as that found in nalidixic acid and oxolinic acid, both representatives of the first two decades of quinolone antibiotics discovery,   showed an attractive antibacterial profile similar to the one of  9 . However, MICs against resistant strains of  C. difficile  as well as E. faecium  were consistently higher by at least one dilution. Further degrading the cyclopropane ring to create either a methylated ( 17 ) or an unsubstituted analogue ( 18 ) resulted in a similar effect. Likewise, introducing an additional nitrogen atom in the bicyclic LHS core, thus forming a 1,8- naphthyridinone scaffold such as that found in two more recently marketed FQ antibiotics (gemifloxacin and zabo- floxacin), ,   only had a minimal impact on the antibacterial activity (compare  9  with  19 ) with the exception of a 4-fold more potent activity against anaerobic gut bacteria. Together with a trend toward increased aqueous solubility, the latter finding discouraged us from further following up on this scaffold. On the other hand, an 8-fold increase in MIC against key pathogens was observed in the case of  20  lacking a substituent on the additional heteroatom. Interestingly, compounds  17  and  20  exhibited the lowest IVTT values (<50 nM) of all of the compounds tested in this study, and it remains unclear at this stage why a 10-fold boost in target potency did not result in lower MICs. Subsequently, we further evaluated the contribution of the LHS by incrementally degrading the FQ scaffold. Manipulating the free carboxylate group by either masking it as a methyl ester ( 21 ) or by entirely removing it ( 22 ) gave rise to a significant loss in potency, presumably due to the combined effects of reduced target potency and a lack of cytoplasmic accumulation. On the other hand, analogue  23  lacking the fluorine atom in 6-position retained reasonable activities against the wt and hypervirulent strains of  C. difficile  but was 4- to 8-fold less potent against the rest of the pathogens in the panel. A marked decrease in antibacterial activity was observed when the FQ core was replaced by simpler scaffolds, such as representatives like the 2- naphthoic acid and quinoline analogues  24  and  25 , respectively. As could be expected, 2-naphthalene analogue 26  showed no antibacterial activity whatsoever.  

Finally, to explore the effect of the hydroxymethyl group on the oxazolidinone RHS pharmacophore, the corresponding enantiomer of  9  was synthesized and tested. As illustrated in Table 3 , inversion of the stereocenter in the 5-position of the oxazolidinone ring led to an inactive stereoisomer (compound 27 ), which is in agreement with literature findings for oxazolidinone antibiotics in general.   In contrast, replacing the hydroxymethyl warhead with an acetamido moiety common to LZD led to compound  28 , which is characterized by an increased solubility and an improved antibacterial spectrum, however, also affecting  Bacteroides  (MIC 4  μ g/ mL). − 56   Compound  28  was last studied in a Phase 2 exploratory open label study, but no development has been reported since 2022.

Taking into account that CDI and its effects on gut inflammation may have an impact on drug absorption, mice infected with pathogenic  C. difficile  were included in the study. As it turned out, only marginal concentrations of  9  reached the systemic circulation as reflected by very low AUC values not exceeding 147 ng · h/ mL and, likewise,  C max  levels not exceeding 12 ng/mL were observed. Finally, considering the hybrid nature of  9 , which is combining pharmacophores of antibiotic classes known for their oral bioavailability, we further wanted to examine compound stability under various conditions in order to exclude a breakdown into smaller fragments, which could either elicit toxic side effects by reaching the systemic circulation or could be disruptive to the gastrointestinal microbiota, both adversely affecting clinical outcome.  9  proved to be chemically stable under stress test conditions including heat and strong oxidizing agents across a broad pH range. To assess compound integrity in biological media, the metabolic profile of  9  in human preparations (liver microsomes and hepatocytes) was investigated. Apart from metabolites that can be ascribed to oxidation and conjugation (glucuronides and sulfates), no degradation or cleavage products were detected. However, even more significantly, no evidence for metabolism was found in human intestinal microsomes, indicating that depletion of gut flora by a released FQ fragment is very unlikely to occur.  

# CONCLUSIONS  

The discovery of novel antibiotics circumventing contempo- rary resistance mechanisms is of critical importance to improve currently available antibiotic treatment options for CDI. We have reported on our efforts toward a novel class of antibiotics tailored for CDI therapy, which has resulted in the discovery of cadazolid ( 9 ), a first-in-class quinoxolidinone antibiotic. Starting from an established broadspectrum heterodimeric tool compound ( 1 ) that is combining elements of fluoroquinolone and oxazolidinone antibiotics, we have identified the 4-hydroxy-hydroxy methyl piper i dine group as a privileged linker moiety, which in combination with the appropriate LHS and RHS pharmacophores led to potent and focused antibacterial activity against clinically relevant strains of  C. difficile  by targeting the bacterial protein synthesis machinery. Further characterization studies showed a favorable chemical stability profile of  9  in several relevant matrices as well as a remarkable inertia to form biologically meaningful metabolites or any kind of degradation products. Together with the fact that  9  is essentially insoluble in the gastro- intestinal tract, minimal drug absorption through the gut wall was anticipated and demonstrated experimentally. The latter is a major prerequisite for achieving high drug concentrations at the site of infection as well as for obviating potential systemic side effects. We could further demonstrate that a possible increase in bioavailability due to impaired gut wall as a consequence of CDI can be ruled out. Based on its overall beneficial profile, cadazolid ( 9 ) was eventually advanced into Phase 3 clinical trials.

In Vitro  Transcription/Translation (IVTT) Assay.  The cell-free protein synthesis reactions were carried out based on a procedure published for  E. coli 62   and adapted for  C. difficile  as described previously.   Briefly,  C. difficile  bacterial extracts were prepared according to a simplified  E. coli  procedure using a FastPrep-24 instrument from Lucerna Chem AG (Catalog No. 6004-500) and lysing matrix B (Lucerna Chem; Catalog No. 116911100) (diameter of silica spheres, <100  μ m).  

Test compounds diluted in 8% dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) were added to a reaction buffer containing bacterial extract, yielding either final concentrations of 40, 20, 10, 5, 1, 0.5, 0.1, and 0  μ M or ranges from 200 to 0.5  μ M and 20 to 0.05  μ M, depending on the compound. Samples were mixed and preincubated for 10 min at room temperature (RT) before plasmid pSP-Nluc (500 ng/ μ L), encoding the NanoLuc luciferase reporter, an ATP-independent enzyme that utilizes a novel furimazine substrate to produce high-intensity, glow- type luminescence was added.   After mixing, the plate was incubated at RT for 90 min. The luminescence was measured immediately after addition of luciferase substrate Nano-Glo (Promega; Catalog No. N1110) using a Tecan SpectralFluor Plus reader (GeniosPro) instrument (parameters: integration time of 1000 ms; normal intensity).  

Determination of the MIC.  The MICs of  C. difficile  isolates were determined using the Clinical and Laboratory Standards Institute (CLSI)-recommended reference agar dilution method for anaerobes as described previously.   For the inoculum, single bacterial colonies or cells collected from colonies were suspended in supplemented brucella broth and approximately 10 4 − 10 5   CFU were spotted. The plates were incubated in an anaerobic chamber for 48 h at 37  ° C (bioMe rieux GENbox anaer; Catalog No. 96124). The MIC was defined as the lowest concentration that completely inhibited visible growth compared to the drug-free control.  

Pharmacokinetic Studies.  Single-dose plasma p harm a co kinetics studies were performed in male Wistar rats and in the same strain of mice used previously for efficacy studies and with the same formulation.   All animal housing and experiments were performed in agreement with the Swiss Federal Ordinance for animal protection, the animal welfare guidelines from the Cantonal Veterinary Office Basellandschaft, and the Actelion Pharmaceuticals Ltd. internal animal welfare guidelines. Female C57BL/6 mice (Harlan Laboratories, An Venray, The Netherlands; Janvier Laboratories, Saint-Berthevin, France) at an age of 8 − 12 weeks were acclimatized for at least 7 days prior to experiments. Animals were housed in individually ventilated cage systems (SlimLine, Tecniplast, Buguggiate, Italy)  

using aspen wood as bedding, and given access to water and food ad libitum. The facility’s light was subjected to a 12 h day and night rhythm, and temperature and air humidity were kept constant to 23 ° C and  ≤ 60% relative humidity. For the study in infected mice,  C. difficile  VPI 10463 was used and the previously published protocol was followed.   Cadazolid was administered once p.o., starting 2 h post infection. Blood samples were collected at 0.5, 1, 2, 3, 6, and 24 h timepoints. A single mouse was used for each timepoint ( n  = 3 − 4), rats were sampled serially except for the portal vein and QWBA experiments, where destructive sampling was used. Plasma samples were analyzed using liquid chromatography coupled to tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) after protein precipitation. Pharmacoki- netic parameters were estimated with the Phoenix Winnonlin 6.3 software package (Certara) using non compartment al analysis.
