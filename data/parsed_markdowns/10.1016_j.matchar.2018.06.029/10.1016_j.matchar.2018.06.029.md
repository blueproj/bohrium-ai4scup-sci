# A B S T R A C T  

# A R T I C L E I N F O  

The uniaxial tensile experiments are performed on a nickel-based superalloy with di ﬀ erent initial micro- structures at intermediate temperatures (473 – 973) K and low strain rates (0.0001 – 0.001) s − 1 . E ﬀ ects of initial microstructures on plastic deformation mechanisms, serrated  ﬂ ow features, and fracture characteristics are analyzed. It is clearly demonstrated that the plastic deformation mechanism of serrated  ﬂ ow is irrelevant to the initial microstructures, and the dislocation across slip in the matrix is responsible for the serrated  ﬂ ow. However, the e ﬀ ects of initial microstructures on fracture mechanisms are obvious, i.e., the main fracture mechanism of the ST (solution treated) and HS (solution plus  γ ′ / γ ″  phases aging precipitation) superalloys are the nucleation and growth of micro-voids, which accelerates the break of carbides and interfacial failure of carbides/matrix. While for the SAT (solution plus  δ  phases aging precipitation) superalloy,  δ  phases and carbides play a signi ﬁ cant role in the nucleation of micro-voids. Furthermore, the increase of the deformation temperature reduces the elongation to fracture of the ST and HS superalloys. However, the better tensile ductility is obtained with raising the deformation temperatures for the SAT superalloy.

# 1. Introduction  

Generally, in nickel-based superalloys, the complex precipitates including a primary strengthening phase ( γ ″ , Ni 3 Nb), a secondary strengthening phase ( γ ′ , Ni 3 Al), an equilibrium phase ( δ , Ni 3 Nb) and a ﬁ ne carbide particle play important roles on mechanical properties [ 24 – 28 ]. Also, it is well known that the microstructures are greatly a ﬀ ected by the deformation processes for superalloys [ 29 ,  30 ]. In recent years, some scholars have investigated the in ﬂ uences of di ﬀ erent mi- crostructures on the serrated  ﬂ ow of some superalloys. For example, Cai et al. [ 31 ] found that  γ ′  precipitates can facilitate the dynamic strain aging e ﬀ ect in the tensile process of Ni-based superalloy. Yuan et al. [ 32 ] demonstrated that the mechanical properties and PLC e ﬀ ect are signi ﬁ cantly a ﬀ ected by ordered precipitates in Ni-Cr-Mo alloys with di ﬀ erent heat treatments. Sundararaman et al. [ 33 ] investigated that the e ﬀ ects of aging time on the serrated  ﬂ ow of IN718 alloy, and summarized the regime for the occurrence of serrations at elevated temperatures. Cui et al. [ 34 ] found that the mechanical properties and serrated  ﬂ ow features are greatly a ﬀ ected  γ ′  precipitates in a Ni-Co-Cr- base superalloy. Li et al. [ 35 ] studied the in ﬂ uences of annealing pro- cesses on PLC e ﬀ ect in a Ni-based superalloy, and revealed that the larger grain size easily induce the serrated  ﬂ ow. Also, Chandravathi

Although some researchers have discussed that the e ﬀ ects of mi- crostructures on serrated  ﬂ ow features and mechanical properties of some superalloys, few considerations are paid to the e ﬀ ects of initial microstructures, especially di ﬀ erent precipitates, on the plastic de- formation mechanisms, serrated  ﬂ ow features, and fracture character- istics at intermediate temperatures and low strain rates. Uniaxial tensile tests were conducted on a MTS-GWT2105 machine at (473 – 973) K and low strain rates (0.0001 – 0.001) s − 1 . The detailed uniaxial tensile experimental schedules are presented in  Fig. 2 . Prior to loading, all the deformed specimens with di ﬀ erent initial micro- structures were soaked at the tensile temperature for 10 min. During the tensile experiments, the  ﬂ uctuation of tensile temperature was con- trolled within 1 K, and the experimental data were automatically re- corded by the computer. To investigate the plastic deformation me- chanisms of the studied superalloy, the foils for TEM observations were cut from the deformed specimen perpendicular to the tensile axis. Subsequently, 50  μ m-thick foils were prepared by mechanical polishing and electro-polished with a solution consisting of 10% HClO 4  and 90% CH 3 CH 2 OH (vol%) at 248 K and the current of 50 – 60 mA. The TEM observations were performed on a Tecnai G2 F20 operating at 200 kV. Meanwhile, all the fracture morphologies were observed using scanning electron microscopy (SEM).  

# 3. Results and Analysis  

# 3.1. High-temperature Tensile Deformation Characteristics  

Fig. 3  presents the  ﬂ ow characteristics of the superalloy with dif- ferent initial microstructures at 0.0003 s − 1 . It is revealed that the su- peralloy with di ﬀ erent initial microstructures exhibits di ﬀ erent hot tensile deformation behaviors. Obviously, a sudden rise in the  ﬂ ow stress before yielding can be observed in the elastic deformation pro- cess, which accords with the Hooke's law. Then, due to the accumula- tion of dislocations, the dislocation multiple sliding appears [ 25 ]. Thus, the  ﬂ ow stresses approximately linearly increase to peak values, in- dicating the nearly linear hardening happens in the plastic deformation process. Finally, the  ﬂ ow stresses decrease until the  ﬁ nal rupture of specimens owing to the localized necking. Meanwhile, a large amount of serrations are found from the  ﬂ ow stress curves, which implies the occurrence of serrated  ﬂ ow in the superalloy during the hot tensile. Similar features were also found in other nickel-based superalloy [ 31 , 41 ]. Also, according to the morphological characteristics, magnitudes and periodicities of serrations, these serrations can be classi ﬁ ed into the following four types, i.e., type A serrations are commonly observed in the lower deformation temperature range, and its morphological characteristics are above the general  ﬂ ow curves. Meanwhile, the morphological features of type A + B / B serrations are above or below the general  ﬂ ow curves, which can be easily found at intermediate deformation temperatures. Furthermore, the serrations of type C usually occur at the higher deformation temperatures, and their char- acteristics are below the general true stress-strain curves. By comparing Fig. 3 a – c, it can be noticed that the regimes for serrations not only depend on the deformation conditions, but also relate to the initial microstructures. For example, when deformation conditions are 473 K and 0.0003 s − 1 , the serrated  ﬂ ow only occurs in the ST or SAT super- alloys. However, as the strain rate is 0.0003 s − 1 , the serrated  ﬂ ow disappears in the SAT superalloy once the deformation temperature exceeds 873 K. Besides, the ultimate strength decreases with increasing deformation temperature due to the thermal softening. This experi- mental result is in an agreement with other report on nickel-based su- peralloys [ 31 ]. Because of the occurrence of typical work hardening- dynamic recovery phenomenon [ 1 ,  27 ,  42 ] and the generation of strengthening phases in the matrix at low strain rates [ 27 ,  43 ,  44 ], the ﬂ ow stress curves at 973 K are di ﬀ erent from those at other tempera- tures.

3.2. In ﬂ uences of Initial Microstructures on Plastic Deformation Mechanism of Serrated Flow  

Generally, there are di ﬀ erent interpretations on the plastic de- formation mechanisms of serrated  ﬂ ow in nickel-based superalloys. Up to now, some researchers investigated the plastic deformation me- chanisms of serrated  ﬂ ow by TEM observations. For example, Yuan et al. [ 32 ] found the PLC e ﬀ ect in a Ni-Cr-Mo alloy containing ordered phases induced by the dislocation slip and twinning of matrix. Xu et al. [ 45 ] concluded that Suzuki segregation, which is enhanced by serious intersections of stacking fault e ﬀ ects, leads to the serrated  ﬂ ow in a Ni- Co-based superalloy. Han et al. [ 46 ] discovered that the Suzuki segre- gation near the stacking faults (SFs) is responsible for the serrated  ﬂ ow of MP159 alloy. Yuan et al. [ 47 ] investigated that the Co element ad- dition play signi ﬁ cant role on the deformation mechanism for PLC ef- fect in Ni-Co-base disc superalloys, and found that the increase of de- formation temperature transfers the deformation mode from the dislocations movement to SFs or deformation micro-twinning forma- tion. Cai et al. [ 48 ] demonstrated that the plastic deformation mode in a Ni-Co-based superalloy changes from dislocation motion from SF formation with the increased content of strengthening  γ ′  phases. Cui et al. [ 34 ] found that the dislocations shearing the secondary  γ ′  phases can result in the occurrence of PLC e ﬀ ect in a Ni-based superalloy. Moreover, Cai et al. [ 49 ] proposed that the serrated  ﬂ ow of austenite steel occurs through phase transformation-induced e ﬀ ect. In present work, in order to better investigate the plastic deformation mechanism of serrated  ﬂ ow in the studied superalloy, all TEM specimens were taken from the deformed superalloy with di ﬀ erent initial micro- structures. The selected strain rate is 0.0003 s − 1 , and the deformation temperatures are 573 K and 773 K.  

Fig. 4  shows the TEM micrographs of the researched superalloy with di ﬀ erent initial microstructures at di ﬀ erent deformation conditions. As presented in  Fig. 4 a – f, the superalloy with di ﬀ erent initial micro- structures exhibits evident dislocation density characteristics with raising the deformation temperature, i.e., the dislocation density of the superalloy deformed at 773 K is higher than that deformed at 573 K. Meanwhile, the dislocation pile-ups phenomenon easily occurs at the higher deformation temperature, as shown in  Fig. 4 f and g. In general, the segregation of Cr and Mo elements in the matrix result in a low stacking fault energy (SFE) for the investigated superalloy [ 50 ,  51 ], but the SFs only appear in the HS superalloy at 573 K and 0.0003 s − 1 ( Fig. 4 c). Moreover, the selected area electron di ﬀ raction (SAD) signals

Meanwhile, it is universally accepted that the serrated  ﬂ ow characteristics are induced by dynamic strain aging, which associates with dynamic interactions of solute atoms and mobile dislocations. This is in accordance with the plastic deformation model of serrated  ﬂ ow in the present study. As reported by Jiang et al. Besides, the increase of deformation temperature results in higher dislocation density in the matrix, which can easily lead to the occurrence of the pinning-unpinning e ﬀ ect and promote the transition phenomena of serrations types [ 32 ,  48 ]. This is because the increase of deformation temperature not only increases the solute atoms di ﬀ usion rate and the vacancy concentration, but also activates the number of dislocation slip bands, which can greatly intensity the interactions be- tween the solute atoms and mobile dislocations [ 53 ].

As presented in  Fig. 6 a, d and g, the fracture surfaces are indented, which implies that the plastic deformation has a predominant e ﬀ ect on the micro-damage evolution at 473 K – 973 K. For the ST superalloy ( Fig. 6 a – c), a large amount of micro-voids can be seen on the fracture surface, and lots of bulk car- bides (NbC) distribute around the micro-voids. It is well known that the bulk carbides are harder than the matrix. Also, the presence of carbides can hinder the mobile dislocations slip during high-temperature tensile deformation, which may lead to the stress concentration. Once the concentrated stress is larger than the fracture limit of carbide or the interfacial strength of matrix/carbide [ 42 , 54 ], micro-voids nucleate. In general, amounts of  γ ′  and  γ ″  phases can signi ﬁ cantly strengthen the matrix, which cause the higher fracture limit of the matrix than carbides. During the hot tensile deformation, these strengthening phases not only hinder the dislocation movement, but also lead to the appearance of dislocation pile-ups phenomenon. This brings out the formation of micro-voids around the broken carbides. From  Fig. 6 g – i, it is obviously observed that micro-voids nucleate and lots of long needle-shape  δ  phases distort on the fracture surfaces in the SAT superalloy. Usually, due to the precipitation of brittle and hard  δ phases in the matrix, the interfacial strength between  δ  phases and the matrix is greatly weakened, and the matrix is signi ﬁ cantly softened [ 40 ]. Meanwhile,  δ  phases and carbides play an important role in im- peding the dislocation movement during hot tensile deformation. Therefore, the concentrated stresses easily appear, and micro-voids nucleate at the sharp angles of  δ  phases or around carbides. The for- mation of micro-voids for this superalloy is a signi ﬁ cant process for the micro-void coalescence [ 51 ]. The micro-voids are extended along the tensile axis after its initiation. The cracks initiate due to the growth and coalescence of micro-voids. Besides, as shown in  Fig. 6 , it is observed that more micro-voids distribute in the vicinity of the fracture surface for the ST superalloy, which implies the ST superalloy can tolerate higher damage before fracture [ 55 ], compared to the HS and SAT su- peralloys.

Notably, due to the appearance of intermediate temperature embrittlement [ 8 ,  56 ], when the deformation temperature exceeds 873 K, the elongation to fracture drastically drops for the superalloy with di ﬀ erent initial microstructures. # 4. Conclusions  

The in ﬂ uences of initial microstructures on serrated  ﬂ ow features, hot tensile plastic deformation mechanisms, and fracture characteristics in a nickel-based superalloy are studied. Some main conclusions can be drawn as:  

(1) The superalloy with three di ﬀ erent initial microstructures exhibits the obvious serrated  ﬂ ow features. Meanwhile, the serration regime is related to the deformation conditions and initial microstructures.

 (2) The in ﬂ uences of initial microstructures on the plastic deformation mechanisms of serrated  ﬂ ow are slight, while the dislocation cross slip is responsible for the serrated  ﬂ ow.

 (3) The fracture mechanisms are sensitive to the initial microstructures. The main fracture mechanisms of the ST and HS superalloys are the nucleation and growth micro-voids, which are closely related to the break of carbides and the interfacial failure of carbide/matrix. While for the SAT superalloy,  δ  phases and carbides play a vital role in the nucleation of micro-voids.
