# Microstructure and grain growth direction of SRR99  single-crystal superalloy by selective laser melting  

Jingjing Yang  1 , Fangzhi Li  2 , Aiqiong Pan  3 , Huihui Yang  1 , Chunyang Zhao  1 , Wenpu Huang  1 ,  Zemin Wang  \*, Xiaoyan Zeng  1 , Xinliang Zhang  1 1  Wuhan National Laboratory for Optoelectronics, Huazhong University of Science and Technology,  Wuhan 430074, PR China  2  Wuhan Space Sanjiang LITRI Co.,Ltd  3 School of Mechanical Engineering, Zhengzhou University of Science and Technology, Zhengzhou,  Henan 450064, China  

Abstract:  Nickel-based Single Crystal (SX) superalloys have an excellent creep  ductility and thermal fatigue resistance at elevated temperatures. But SX structure is  difficult to be manufactured under the demand of increasing operating temperature and  complex shapes. In this work, the feasibility of manufacturing Nickel-based  SX-superalloys by selective laser melting (SLM) was investigated theoretically and  experimentally. Firstly, the solidification conditions including temperature field, thermal  gradient and solidification speed of the SLMed multi-track samples were calculated by  an established finite element model to judge the theoretical feasibility based on the  columnar to equiaxed transition (CET). Then, the single-track, triple-track and cuboid  samples on a single-crystal SRR99 substrate were deposited to judge the experimental  feasibility and explore their grain growth mechanism during SLM. The results showed  that CET can be avoided during the whole process of SLM. The unidirectional SRR99  SX structure with a height of 2 mm can be successfully obtained by SLM. On horizontal  section, the microstructures can be divided into [001] orientation zone, [010] orientation  zone and overlapping zone with a typical secondary dendrite structure. On vertical  section, the columnar dendritic structure along the dominant thermal gradient direction  is present. The primary dendrite spacing of the SLMed samples (\~1 ‒ 2  µ m) is more than  two orders of magnitude lower than that of the cast one (\~320  µ m). So, SLM technology  possesses the capacity to manufacture SX superalloy. When the deposited height  reaches 6 mm, the misorientation angle is about 25° and a crack initiates. Finally, the  grain growth mechanism of the SLMed SRR99 single-crystal superalloy was proposed  based on the local thermal gradient, the existing well-aligned crystallographic  orientation and the preferential grain growth direction.  

Keywords:  Nickel-based single crystal (SX) superalloy; SRR99; Selective laser melting;  Microstructure; Grain growth direction  

# 1.   Introduction  

Single Crystal (SX) superalloys, especially Nickel-based SX superalloys, have a  broad application in the manufacture of turbine blade  for their superior mechanical  properties at elevated temperatures in comparison with other alloy materials. The  absence of grain boundary  , elimination of the low-melting grain boundary  strengthening constituents and the alignment of the minimum Young’s modulus  direction along the turbine blade axis   lead to an excellent creep ductility   and  thermal fatigue resistance  .  

The directional solidification process is still the most popular method to obtain SX  turbine blades in the past three decades, where the solidification of the alloy is forced by  a directional thermal gradient and controlled by nucleation and growth processes .  However, under non-equilibrium solidification conditions, the sluggish diffusivity of the  refractory elements in the solid-state leads to significant segregation and the formation  of cored eutectic microstructures  . Besides, defects like subboundary structure and  misoriented grains may form for complex-shaped components to result in the  performance deterioration. Meanwhile, the indispensably subsequent finishing  processes may break the SX structure easily. Due to the further demand of increasing  operating temperature capability of gas turbine components, it is rather difficult to  manufacture SX structure because the alloys are more reactive with ceramic systems  and have smaller processing window for casting and heat treatment  .  

As is well known, an epitaxial growth of columnar dendrites is indispensable to  successfully produce SX components  , i.e. a columnar to equiaxed transition (CET)  has to be avoided and the epitaxy between substrate and deposit has to be ensured.  Metal additive manufacturing (MAM), mainly including electron beam melting (EBM),  laser cladding deposition (LCD), scanning laser epitaxy (SLE) and selective laser  melting (SLM), refers to layer-by-layer shaping and solidification of the metal powders  to a wide range of configurations, which is characterized as sharp thermal gradient and  rapid solidification speed  . When the selected feeding material is similar with  substrate, the epitaxial growth occurs during solidification process. Therefore, such  technology has been developed as a new and feasible method to obtain SX structure by  a careful selection of the processing parameters to avoid CET which is closely related to  thermal gradient (G) and solidification speed (V S ).  

Recently, EBM has been used to fabricate the CMSX-4 SX defect-free superalloy  samples . Also, LCD has been used to process SX Rene N4   and Rene N5   superalloys. The processing parameters  , inclination of coaxial nozzle  , substrate  orientation  , and molten pool geometry   can control the crystal orientation  of deposits during LCD. Recent studies of SLE process in depositing CMSX-4  ,  Rene N5  , Rene 142  , MAR-M247  , In100  and Rene 80   superalloys were  mostly focused on the epitaxy of substrate and deposits within a few layers and  scanning tracks through the control of processing parameters.  

SLM, another typical metal additive manufacturing technology based on powder  bed fusion, has a greater thermal gradient (>10 7  K/m)  and G n /V value, which makes  it more likely to avoid CET. Meanwhile, its lower laser power, higher scanning speed,  smaller laser beam diameter and lower heat input can also provide better manufacturing  precision and ability of fabricating complex structures. Therefore, SLM technology is  more promising to manufacture SX Nickel-based superalloy components. Recent  researches on the fabrication of nickel-based superalloy by SLM were mainly about its  microstructure and mechanical properties  . Only Chen et al.   tried to  manufacture the single crystal structure with maximum tolerance angles of less than 5°  in Inconel 718 superalloy by SLM. However, there is no investigations about  mechanism of grain growth direction on SX nickel-based superalloy.  

In this study, the solidification conditions including temperature field, thermal  gradient and solidification speed were firstly calculated by a finite element model to  judge the feasible of SLM to manufacture single-crystal superalloy in theory. Secondly,  the SLM experiments were conducted to deposit single- and triple-track and cuboid  SRR99 single-crystal samples under the various processing parameters. The  microstructures and grain growth directions of the samples were observed to explore the  grain growth mechanisms of the SLMed SRR99 superalloy. Finally, the unidirectional  crack-free SX structure with a height of 2 mm were successfully obtained by SLM.

# 4 Results and discussion  

4.1 CET of the SLMed samples  

One of the first considerations is the avoidance of equiaxed growth in order to  successfully manufacture SRR99 single-crystal superalloy by SLM. According to Hunt  et al.   and Gaumman et al.  , a full-columnar grain can be obtained when  G n / V s > K ,  where  n  and  K  are material dependent constants ( n =3.4 and  K =2.7×10 24  (K 3.4 /m 4.4  s)  ). The selected processing parameters of SLM must meet another necessary  condition of successful SX deposition that heat input is sufficient to re-melt the  substrate to ensure epitaxy between substrate and deposit. The black dotted line shows  the laser beam position in Fig. 2a, whereas the black isotherm shows the liquidus line of  SRR99 alloy in Fig. 2.

# 4.2 Deposition of single-track and triple-track samples  

In this part, the SLMed SRR99 superalloy samples were deposited to investigate  the experimental feasibility of manufacturing SX superalloy by SLM. Fig. 4 shows the  microstructures of single-track and triple-track molten pool samples under powder layer  thickness of 20  µ m. The grains at the border of the single-track molten pool grow  parallel to the horizontal direction and extend to the center of the pool, as shown in Fig.  

4a. While, the grains in the interior of the single-track molten pool grow parallel to the  building direction. The track-by-track deposition of metal powders yields overlap zone,  which experienced re-melting and re-solidification. The microstructure of overlap zone  is found to be quite similar with that of the edge of the single-track molten pool, as  shown in Fig. 4b. Furthermore, [001] grain orientation in the interior, and [010] or [0 1 0]  grain orientations at the border and overlap zone of molten pool were obtained.  According to the inverse pole figures of EBSD results in Fig. 4, no grain boundaries are  observed in the single-track and triple-track samples, implying that SLM technology  possesses the potential to manufacture the SRR99 single-crystal superalloy.

Fig. 5 shows the horizontal and vertical sections  of single-track wall under various powder layer thicknesses. Almost full-cellular grain is  observed under layer thickness of 20  µ m, whose average primary dendrite spacing  λ 1  is  less than 2  µ m (Fig. 5a and d). The secondary dendrite arm develops with the increase  of layer thickness from 20 to 40  µ m (Fig. 5a–c). The average primary dendrite spacing  λ 1  increases to 2\~3  µ m, and the prolonged secondary dendrite arms appear when the  applied layer thickness is 30  µ m. (Fig. 5b and e). The cellular dendrite with obvious  cross pattern structure is present with further increasing layer thickness to 40  µ m (Fig.  5c and f). On vertical section, the columnar dendritic structure along the dominant  thermal gradient direction ([001]) is observed in the samples under various layer  thicknesses. The actual layer thickness of the samples is approximately equal to the set  values during SLM based on the layer bands on vertical section (Fig. 5d–f).  

Fig. 6 shows the microstructures on horizontal section of triple-track wall structure  under hatch spacing of 110  µ m. The microstructure on horizontal section can be divided  into three regions: [001] orientation zone in center of track, [010] orientation zone on  the edge of track and overlapping zone (Fig. 6a). The overlapping zone consists of a  typical secondary dendrite structure with obvious cross pattern in Fig. 6b–e. As  compared with the center area, the power density of overlap zone is generally smaller  under the Gaussian distribution of laser energy, resulting in that a lower thermal  gradient and longer liquid residence time of overlap zone can promote the development  of secondary dendrite arms. The [010] grains originate from secondary dendrite arms of  overlapping zone and epitaxially grow toward [010] direction in Fig. 6c and d. Fig. 6f  shows the fine [001] orientation grains in the center part of the scanning tracks.  Similarly, no grain boundaries are observed in the single-track and triple-track wall  samples, implying that the single-crystal single-track and triple-track thin structure can  be manufactured by SLM.

4.4 Grain growth mechanisms of SRR99 superalloy during SLM  

4.4.1 Grain growth of SLMed single track  

![](images/dd5d907cef843101346b2ac2e98bfb4d91c1a140dcf587d3b956ffa35f84d32b.jpg)  
Fig. 11.  Schematic diagram of grain growth direction of single-track molten pool  

Generally, the preferential growth direction of material, crystallographic  orientation of substrate and thermal gradient direction of local solidification condition  determine the actual grain growth direction of the SLMed single-track molten pool. As  is well known, there are three preferential growth directions including [001], [010] and  [100] in face-centered cubic structured SRR99 superalloy. Fig. 11 presents the  schematic diagram of grain growth direction of a random position (P) in single-track  molten pool. As grains toward [100] direction can be barely seen in the microstructure  of the SLMed SRR99 sample, the thermal gradient along [100] direction was ignored to  simplify the computation in the subsequent analysis. So, the vector  G  at point P is  considered to be the resultant thermal gradient of vector G y  and vector G z .  

There are three kinds of grain growth modes (marked as A\~C in Fig. 11) in the  molten pool according to the variation of G y  and G z . As for point A where G z  plays a  dominant role in determining vector G (i.e., G z  >> G y ), the first mode is that the grain  growth direction is first influenced by the crystallographic orientation of the parent  grains of substrate/pre-layer and then keeps epitaxial growth along [001] direction under  the thermal gradient G. With increasing the distance from center of molten pool along  the Y-axis, for instance point B, G y  increases to a considerable order of magnitude  (>10 7  °C/m) to be comparable with G z  (i.e., G z   ≈  G y ), so the secondary dendrite arms  toward [010] direction coexist with [001] grains, which is the second mode. The  formation of [010] grains can be considered as an equivalent transformation of [001]  grains. As for Point C at the border of molten pool, G y  increases to be even higher than  G z  (i.e., G z  < G y ), thus the third mode is that dendrite trunks grow along the direction of 

 [010] rather than [001].  

4.4.2 Grain growth between successive layers and tracks  

Fig. 12 gives schematic diagram of grain growth direction between successive  tracks or layers. SLM deposition between successive tracks or layers produces the  overlap area. A larger hatch spacing means a lower overlap ratio between adjacent  tracks and more residual [010] grains (Fig. 12a). Similarly, a larger powder layer  thickness produces a smaller re-melting ratio between adjacent layers and more residual  [010] grains. Also, the primary dendrite spacing  λ 1  increases from bottom to top due to  an inverse correlation between primary dendrite spacing and thermal gradient. This is  why the value of  λ 1  increases when powder layer thickness increases from 20  µ m to 40  µ m in Fig. 5. The grains of (n+1) th  layer will be partially re-melted and re-grow affected  by the crystallographic direction of the already-deposited grain (such as n th  layer) and  local thermal gradient during SLM (Fig. 12b).

4.4.3 Effect of thermal gradient  

![](images/1ad1d586abef0daa11496949ef9d07f7af369897e768ca97223dcbe3ee867d82.jpg)  
Fig. 13.  The calculated thermal gradient toward X-axis (a), Y-axis (b), and Z-axis

Fig. 13 presents the calculated thermal gradient toward X-axis, Y-axis and Z-axis  in the single-track molten pool sample to discuss the local thermal gradient during SLM.  As compared with the thermal gradients toward Y and Z axis, the thermal gradient  toward X-axis is very low and can be neglected in the following analysis. Clearly, there  are five zones in the molten pool based on the relationship between thermal gradients  toward Y and Z axis, i.e. zone I to zone V are defined as the cases where G z >>G y ,  G z >G y , G z ≈ G y , G z < G y , and G z << G y , respectively. Moreover, the thermal gradient of a  random position depends on the residual thermal gradient of the pre-deposited tracks or  layers and re-input thermal gradient of the present track or layer deposition.  

The effect of thermal gradient and the existing crystallographic orientation on the  

grain growth direction is exhibited by schematic diagram in Fig. 14. Based on the  generally accepted theory for competitive grain growth, a grain having the fast growing  crystallographic [001] direction most closely aligned to the temperature gradient, will  overgrow all less favorably oriented grains. Therefore, as the already-deposited grains  are [001] orientation and the thermal gradient is the first type, i.e. G z  is much larger than  G y  (such as zone I in Fig. 13d), [001] crystallographic orientation is more aligned with  the temperature gradient and dominates during competitive grain growth. On the  contrary, when the third type of thermal gradient (G y  is much larger than G z , such as  zone V in Fig. 13d) was employed on the [001] orientation grains, the secondary  dendrites of [010] orientation overgrow to develop as the new primary dendrites from  [001] grain, resulting in the coexistence of [010] and [001] orientations. Moreover, as  the [001] orientation grains experience the second type of thermal gradient (G y  is  comparable with G z , such as zone II, III and IV in Fig. 13d), the actual growth direction  of the newly developed dendrite lies between the thermal gradient direction G and the  preferential crystallographic orientation, i.e. the [001] direction of the primary dendrite  trunk has a misorientation of  α  with respect to the building direction. Similarly, when  [010] orientation grains were imposed by these three types of thermal gradients  mentioned above, the coexistence of [010] and [001] orientations occurs in the first type  of thermal gradient, the [010] direction of the primary dendrites having a misorientation  of  β  forms in the second thermal gradient, and [010] orientations grains were produced  in the third type of thermal gradient (Fig. 14).

Therefore, with the increase of deposited height, the heat accumulation leads to a  reduction of G z , but G y  determined by the moving laser energy input is still constant at  the given processing parameters, resulting in that the misorientation angle of combined  G with the building direction increases. Based on the grain growth mechanism above  mentioned, the deviation of combined G from the building direction with the deposited  height also leads to the increase of misorientation angle of primary dendrites with the  building direction, as shown in Fig. 10. Consequently, grain growth of single-crystal  depends on the deviation of the primary dendrite of the existing well-aligned crystal to  the temperature gradient. The development of grain growth direction in SRR99  superalloy under the solidification conditions of SLM is a consequence of the local  thermal gradient, the existing well-aligned crystallographic orientation and the  preferential grain growth direction.  

# 5 Conclusions  

In the present work, the solidification conditions were calculated by an established  finite element model. The single- and triple-track and cuboid samples were deposited  under the various SLM processing parameters on a single-crystal substrate to investigate  the grain growth mechanism of the SLMed SRR99 superalloy. The major conclusions  are as follows:  

(1) Based on the calculated temperature field, thermal gradient and solidification  speed of the multi-track samples by an established finite element model, CET can be  avoided during the whole process of SLM based on the relationship between the ratio of   .!      at the boundary of the molten pool and threshold value (2.7×10 24  (K 3.4 /m 4.4  s)).  The theoretical feasibility of manufacturing SX superalloy by SLM was verified based  on the calculated solidification conditions.  

(2) The single-crystal single-track and triple-track SRR99 molten pool and thin  structure can be manufactured by SLM. On horizontal section, the microstructure can be  divided into three regions: [001] orientation zone in center of track, [010] orientation  zone on the edge of track and overlapping zone consisted of a typical secondary  dendrite structure. On vertical section, the columnar dendritic structure along the  dominant thermal gradient direction ([001]) is observed in the samples. The SLM  processing parameters can strongly affect the morphology and size of the dendrite  structure.  

(3) Under appropriate processing parameters, the unidirectional crack-free SX  SRR99 structure with a height of more than 2 mm can be successfully obtained by SLM,  whose primary dendrite spacing (\~1 ‒ 2  µ m) is more than two orders of magnitude lower  than that of the cast one (\~320  µ m). However, the misorientation angle of the [001]  orientation with the building direction becomes large with the increase of deposited  height. When the deposited height reaches 6 mm, the misorientation angle is about 25°  and a crack initiates.  

(4) The grain growth mechanism of the SLMed SRR99 single-crystal superalloy  was proposed based on the local thermal gradient, the existing well-aligned  crystallographic orientation and the preferential grain growth direction. The deviation of  combined G from the building direction is the main reason for the increase of  misorientation angle of primary dendrites with the building direction, as the deposited  height increases.
