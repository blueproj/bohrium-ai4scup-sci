ABSTRACT:  An efficient approach was developed for the synthesis of the well-known BlueCage by pre-bridging two 2,4,6-tris(4-pyridyl)-1,3,5- triazine (TPT) panels with one linker followed by cage formation in a much improved yield and shortened reaction time. Such a stepwise methodology was further applied to synthesize three new pyridinium organic cages,  C2 ,  C3 , and  C4 , where the low-symmetry cages  C3  and C4  with angled panels demonstrated better recognition properties toward 1,1 ′ -bi-2-naphthol (BINOL) than the high-symmetry analogue  C2 featuring parallel platforms.  

been extensively utilized in the realm of molecular receptors, mechanically interlocked molecules (MIMs), and functional materials. − 37   These applications take advantage of the rich redox and photochromic properties inherent to pyridinium units.   Our group has consistently concentrated on developing pyridinium-functionalized square-planar Pd(II) cation-bridged redox-active hosts. These hosts are tailored for applications such as selective desulfurization, the synthesis of coumarin derivatives, stimuli-responsive interconversion and transformation, biomimetic catalysis, etc. − 44   The ligands used in these studies feature two 2,4,6-tris(4-pyridyl)-1,3,5- triazine (TPT) panels, which are connected by either a single aryl linker to create single-armed bis-bidentate acyclic ligands or two aryl linkers to form bis-monodentate macrocyclic ligands, each introducing different conformational, steric, and electronic characteristics. Given the relatively high-yield formation of single-armed bis-bidentate acyclic ligands, we envision that methodically tethering the two TPT panels with three arms stepwisely could lead to the efficient synthesis of TPT-cored three-dimensional (3D) pyridinium organic cages, potentially even those with low symmetry.  

M olecular cages or containers featuring precisely defined cavities have garnered considerable interest for their extensive potential in applications such as molecular recognition, gas separation, mass transport, and enzymatic catalysis. − 6   The functionality of these cages is intrinsically linked to the attributes of their inner cavity, including its shape, size, and the functional groups. − 12   While pseudospherical cages with high symmetry are well-explored for their ability to encapsulate guests with spherical or similarly high-symmetry forms. − 15   The anisotropic and low-symmetrical nature of many biologically pertinent molecules and pharmaceuticals presents a significant challenge to traditional receptors with near-spherical cavities.   Consequently, there is a compelling need to develop molecular cages with reduced symmetry that are adept at recognizing and binding low-symmetry entities.  

Various synthetic approaches have been utilized to construct molecular cages with low symmetry, governed by either thermodynamic or kinetic control.   Dynamic strategies that permit error correction based on reversible coordinative and dynamic covalent bonds have proven to be effective in fabricating low-symmetry cages under thermodynamic control in high yields. − 22   However, creating purely organic cages with asymmetric cavities is notably challenging.   This process requires complex, multi-step irreversible organic transforma- tions and labor-intensive workup and purification steps, often resulting in low overall yields. Despite the arduous and time- intensive nature of this process, stepwise organic synthesis remains the predominant method for creating a variety of synthetic cage molecules. − 28   Therefore, it is essential for developing more efficient methods for synthesizing low- symmetry organic cages, considering the benefits of their robustness, porosity, solubility, and enzyme-like inner cavity. , ,  

In this work, we describe an efficient approach toward a series of TPT-cored 3D pyridinium organic cages in a stepwise fashion, wherein the well-known BlueCage was obtained with a largely improved yield of 57% in a much shortened reaction time of 1.5 days. Conditions for subsequent cage formation were screened, including the temperature, reaction time, template, and catalyst ( Table 1 ). The temperature was first examined, and a higher degree was found to be more efficient with a 66% yield at 115 ° C (entry 4) compared to a lower yield of 42% at 90  ° C for 12 h (entry 1). A prolonged reaction time did not contribute to an increased yield (entry 5). The addition of 20 mol % tetra but yl ammonium iodide (TBAI) as a catalyst was proven unhelpful (entry 6). It should be mentioned that, even in combination with 0.2 equiv of TBAI and 6 equiv of phenanthrene as a template, the yield was only slightly increased to 70% (entry 7). Nonetheless, entry 4 without a template and TBAI was selected as optimum reaction conditions. Such results encouraged us to explore the universality of our method. Correspondingly, a larger analogue of the BlueCage, cage  C2 , was prepared by simply replacing phenylene units with biphenylene linkers following our established procedure, with minor modification ( Figure S3  of the Supporting Information). In a similar way, low-symmetry cage  C3  was obtained by reacting the intermediate  M1 · 2BF 4  with a biphenylene linker ( Figure S11  of the Supporting Informa- tion). In addition, we were also able to fabricate much lower symmetry cage  C4  by bridging the two TPT panels with three different linkers, i.e., phenylene, biphenylene, and bipyridinyl, stepwisely according to our methodology ( Figure S19  of the Supporting Information). The different yields obtained for cages  C1 − C4  could be rationalized according to pre- organization of reactants and intermediates, where appropriate pre-organization of reactive sites on precursors contributes to a better yield.   For example, favorable pre-organization of the two TPT panels on intermediate  M1 · 2BF 4  led to high-yield formation of cage  C1 . Such a pre-organization effect could also explain improved efficiency for the synthesis of the BlueCage compared to the reported method, where the two pre- organized intramolecular TPT panels bridged by one phenyl- ene linker reacted more efficiently with 1,4-bis(bromomethyl)- benzene than the reaction between two intermolecular TPT units. Unlike cages  C1  and  C2 , both with a pair of parallel platforms, cages  C3  and  C4  possess two angled panels resulting from a shortened linker length. We anticipate distinct guest binding characteristics between the high and low symmetry cages as a consequence of their systematic variations in cavity shape and size. BINOL was chosen as the guest for host − guest studies, whose two non-coplanar naphthalene units create a scissors-like conformation that resembles the dihedral angle formed between the two TPT panels in cages  C3  and C4 . 1 H NMR spectra were recorded for a homogeneous solution of the cage host and 30 equiv of ( R )-BINOL guest in CD 3 CN to analyze the interactions between the hosts and guests. The resonances belonging to the pyridiniums (H a , H c , and H d ) in cage  C3  experienced an upfield shift, indicating encapsulation of the guest in the cavity through  π − π interactions ( Figure 3 ). Meanwhile, the resonances corre- sponding to H e  and H g  from the methylene bridge and adjacent phenylene unit underwent a downfield shift, implying a deshielding effect from the guest ( Figure 3 ). 1 H NMR titrations were performed to quantify the association constants between different cages and guests by successive addition of small aliquots of a concentrated acetonitrile solution of BINOL into the cage solutions. The binding stoichiometry was evaluated to be 1:1 for three inclusion complexes from ultraviolet − visible (UV − vis) Job plots ( Figures S42 ,  S49 , and  S55  of the Supporting Information), which was also evidenced by mass spectroscopy with 1:1 host − guest complexes detected ( Figure S39  and  S45 of the Supporting Information). The association constants ( K a ) were quantified as 48  ±  1.7 M − 1   for ( ± )-BINOL  ⊂ C2 · 6PF 6 , 171  ±  3.9 M − 1   for ( R )-BINOL  ⊂ C3 · 6PF 6 , and 302  ± 4.5 M − 1   for ( R )-BINOL  ⊂ C4 · 6PF 6 , respectively ( Figures S41 , S48 , and  S54  of the Supporting Information). It should be noted that the stock solution of cage  C4 · 6PF 6  was prepared in a mixed solvent of CD 3 CN/D 2 O (3:2, v/v) as a result of the solubility issue, which may be responsible for a slightly increased binding affinity toward ( R )-BINOL compared to cage  C3 · 6PF 6  in pure CD 3 CN, potentially as a result of the hydrophobic effect. Molecular modeling of host − guest complexes, performed using Materials Studio suite of programs, suggests that the cavity of the cage is sufficiently large to encapsulate the BINOL guest ( Figure S56  of the Supporting Information). The dihedral angle between the two naphthalenyl groups in BINOL  ⊂ C2  was found to be greater than those in BINOL  ⊂ C3  and BINOL  ⊂ C4  ( Figure S56  of the Supporting Information). This larger angle may weaken the π − π  interactions between BINOL and the TPT panels in cage C2 . Consequently, the shape complementarity observed with the low-symmetry cages  C3  and  C4 , which feature angled panels, and scissors-shaped BINOL could explain the superior recognition ability over the more symmetrical cage  C2 , which has three equally large windows. Notably, no en anti o selective recognition of ( S )/( R )-BINOL occurred with the low- symmetry cages  C3  and  C4  ( Figures S46 − S48  and  S52 − S54 of the Supporting Information), which aligns with expectations as a result of the non-chiral nature of cages  C3  and  C4 .  

In summary, we have developed an efficient synthetic protocol for the well-known BlueCage by pre-bridging two TPT panels with one linker followed by cage formation in a much improved yield and shortened reaction time from 6% in 13 days to 57% in 1.5 days. Such a stepwise methodology was further applied to synthesize three new pyridinium organic cages, including one regular triangular prism-shaped cage  C2 with three identical linkers and two low-symmetry cages  C3 and  C4  bridged by two and three distinct kinds of arms, respectively. The pivotal aspect of this method lies in initially tethering the two panels with a single linker, thereby providing a suitable pre-organization that eases the subsequent step of cage formation. Importantly, the structures of cages  C3  and  C4 were further illustrated with X-ray crystallography, featuring an irregular prism conformation. As a benefit from a reduced symmetric cavity, cages  C3  and  C4  exhibited enhanced recognition properties toward nonplanar guest BINOL compared to its symmetrical analogue  C2 . This research provides a general approach for creating low-symmetry pyridinium organic cages, promising broad utility in the field of supramolecular chemistry. Further work on the synthesis of chiral analogues is underway in our laboratory.
