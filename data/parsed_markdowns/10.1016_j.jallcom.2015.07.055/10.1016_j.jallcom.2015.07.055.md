The partitioning behaviors of alloying elements Cr, Co, Al, Ta, etc. on the  g  and  g 0   phases of high Cr- containing (\~9wt.%) nickel-base single crystal superalloys have been investigated with and without the addition of 3wt.% Ru. The  “ reverse partitioning effect ”  occurs, which means that the addition of Ru causes the partition of Cr and Co into  g 0   phase, while Al an Ta concentrate into  g  matrix. However, Ru promotes the precipitation of topologically close-packed (TCP) phases, since the diffusion coef ﬁ cient between the TCP phase and the matrix interface increases with the diffusion behaviors of refractory elements. For the coarsening and slightly rafting of  g 0   phases, the increase of elemental diffusion plays much more important role in its kinetic process than the decrease of lattice mis ﬁ t.

# 1. Introduction  

Throughout the development process of nickel-base superalloy, its chemical evolution possesses the following characteristics [1 e 4] : (i) The content of refractory elements including Re, W, Mo, Ta, etc. continuously increases from the  ﬁ rst to the third generation: 14.6wt.% / 16.4wt.% / 20.7wt.%. The creep capability of the third- generation alloys is much greater than earlier-generation alloys by adding \~3wt.% Re. (ii) As an important strengthening element, the mass fraction of Cr is ever decreasing. Local supersaturation caused by residual Cr and other refractory elements segregation in g  phase is relieved by the formation of topologically close-packed (TCP) phases  [5] . Cr itself is a TCP phase-forming element. (iii) From the fourth-generation superalloys, Ru is introduced because of its improvement in creep properties of nickel-base single crystal superalloys at higher temperatures by suppressing the precipita- tion of TCP phases.  

In this paper, great emphasis is placed on the in ﬂ uence of 3wt.% Ru on the microstructures in the high-Cr-containing (\~9wt.%) su- peralloys during extended isothermal exposure. Unlike previous reports, the addition of 3wt.% Ru causes the  “ reverse partitioning ” behavior of alloying elements Cr, Co, Al and Ta, but promotes the precipitation of TCP phases. This article attempts to analyze this gap

# 2. Experimental procedure  

The chosen samples used to reveal the function of 3wt.% Ru in high-Cr-containing (\~9wt.%) superalloys were named as 9Cr0Ru and 9Cr3Ru for clear comparison, whose experimental content of primary elements in weight percent is given in  Table 1 . These samples were aged at 1100    C for 1000 h. Its microstructure consists of  g  matrix and a homogeneous distribution of strengthening cubic g 0   phases, whose size is about 300 e 500 nm. The volume fraction of g 0   precipitates is about 60%.  

The thin foils for Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM) obser- vations were mechanically cut into 5 mm    5 mm in size and thinned to about 200  m m in thickness. And then they were pol- ished and chemically corroded using 40 g CuSO 4  and 60 ml sul- furic acid solution in 400 ml ethanol. The morphologies of  g / g 0 structure were examined using JEOL JSM 6500F SEM with BSD probe. The specimens for Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM) observations were mechanically punched into 3 mm in diameter and thinned to about 30  m m in thickness. And then they were further thinned by the twin jet polishing technique using 8% perchloric acid solution in ethanol at 30 mA and   25    C. The microstructures of  g / g 0   interfacial networks were examined using Tecnai 20 TEM instruments under an acceleration voltage of 200 kV and a point resolution of 0.19 nm.

# 3.3. TCP phase formation  

Fig. 4  shows the distribution of TCP phases in  g / g 0   structure, showing few, stubby clumps in non-Ru-containing samples and more, broadsword-like slices in Ru-containing samples, and revealing the promotion of Ru on TCP precipitation in the high Cr- bearing (\~9wt.%) superalloys.  Table 4  lists the EDS results of extracted TCP phases in the two investigated alloys. The elemental partitioning of the main constituents of the TCP phase, i.e., Cr, Co, W and Ta, is not greatly in ﬂ uenced by the addition of Ru. The tradi- tional view is that Ru can suppress the precipitation of TCP phases and improve creep properties of single-crystal superalloys  [6] . Based on above results, the addition of 3wt.% Ru causes the  “ reverse partitioning ”  behavior of alloying elements (Cr, Co, Al and Ta), but promotes the precipitation of TCP phases in high Cr-containing superalloys, which is con ﬂ ict with previous reports.  

In fact, the issue  “ Ru can cause  ‘ reverse partitioning ’  behavior of alloying elements and suppress the precipitation of TCP phases ”  is controversial. The number of TCP precipitations increases drasti- cally with the addition of 3wt.% Ru, though the  “ reverse parti- tioning ”  behavior of alloying elements Cr and Co occurs in this research. As a consequence, it is conditional to con ﬁ rm the sup- pression of TCP precipitations has directly correlation with the “ reverse partitioning ”  behavior of alloying elements. This discrep- ancy may result from the high-content Cr (\~9wt.%) in the experi- mental superalloys and the diffusion process caused by the  “ reverse partitioning ”  behavior of alloying elements.  

The compositions of TCP phases mainly maintain Cr, Co, Ta and W, which was investigated by the method of TEM-EDS. As one of the constituent elements of TCP phases, Cr may enlarge the su- persaturation of Cr, Co, etc. in  g  matrix and then promote the precipitation of TCP phases  [21] . In this study, though Cr and Co partition into  g 0   phase partly, the total amount of TCP phase- forming elements (Cr, Co, Ta and W) in  g  matrix shows little change from 40.4wt.% to 35.6wt.%. What's more, the addition of Ru increases the value of the diffusion coef ﬁ cient between TCP phase and the matrix interface (D interface ) in Equation  (3) . Therefore, the growth rate of TCP phases increases with the addition of 3wt.% Ru in the high-Cr-containing superalloys. The question in alloy designs is: (i) do not put as much Cr as possible into the alloys under structural stability premise, and (ii) do not put much Ru into high Cr-containing alloys or (iii) look for optimal Cr e Ru proportion.  

It should be pointed that the results in this research are not negative on previous reports in a absolutely term. Actually they are the bene ﬁ cial complements and further amendments to the func- tion of Ru in nickel-base single crystal superalloys, and provide theoretical guidance for alloy designs.  

# 4. Conclusions  

Using the superalloys with 3wt.% Ru addition to the base high- Cr-containing (\~9wt.%) nickel-base single crystal superalloys, the elemental partitioning, TCP phase formation and  g / g 0   coarsening have been researched with the help of EBSD and TEM. The following conclusions have been obtained.  

(1) TCP phase-forming elements Cr and Co have a tendency to segregate into  g 0   phase, while Al and Ta have a tendency to partition into  g  matrix, forming  “ reverse partitioning ” behavior with the addition of 3wt.% Ru. (2) With 3wt.% Ru added, the elemental diffusion process due to the movement of alloying elements accelerates kinetics of  g / g 0   coarsening, playing more crucial role than the reduction of the driving force caused by the decrease of lattice mis ﬁ t. (3) Ru can promote the precipitation of TCP phases in the high- Cr-containing (\~9wt.%) superalloy because the high-content Cr enlarge the supersaturation of TCP phase-forming ele- ments in  g  matrix and Ru accelerates the diffusion process of alloying elements.
