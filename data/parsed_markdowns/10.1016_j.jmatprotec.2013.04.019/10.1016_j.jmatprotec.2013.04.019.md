# 1. Introduction

D’Souza et al. (2008) described that solidiﬁcation tempera- ture range is normally utilized to evaluate the castability of precipitation-strengthened nickel-based superalloys. Hobbs et al. (2005) explained that solidiﬁcation temperature range is formed when the participating elements of liquid alloy tend to leave the ﬁrst solid (i.e. dendrite cores) and redistribute in the remained molten alloy. The broadening of the mushy zone temperature range is higher, when the segregation of the alloying elements is more severe. Heckl et al. (2010) clariﬁed that alloys having shorter solidiﬁcation temperature ranges exhibit less sensitivity to the hot tearing (i.e. better castability). Brewster et al. (2008) reported that to be able to design more-resistant superalloys to solidiﬁcation cracking, the solidiﬁcation temperature range has to decline. In the light of descriptions above, it is under- stood that the solidiﬁcation temperature range is an appropriate criterion for assessment of the castability which is directly a function of chemical composition, especially the segregating ele- ments having equilibrium coefﬁcient of redistribution ( k ) less than unity. Reed (2006) clariﬁed that the important segregating ele- ments such as titanium, aluminum and molybdenum are used in production of the precipitation-strengthened nickel-based super- alloys to form gamma prime (  ′ ) intermetallic particles. The other chief segregating elements, niobium and molybdenum are uti- lized to manufacture the precipitation-strengthened nickel-based superalloys containing gamma double prime (  ′′ ) intermetallic particles.  

The hot solidiﬁcation cracking (i.e. hot tearing) has been known as one of the most important challenges in case of designing and manufacturing of precipitation-strengthened nickel-based super- alloys. Zhang and Singer (2004) investigated the hot tearing of the polycrystalline castings produced by conventional methods in detail. Zhou and Volek (2007) reported the same problem for directionally-solidiﬁed nickel-based superalloys. Mokadem et al. (2007) presented their study on the hot cracking phenomenon in the single crystal castings. According to their ﬁndings, for nucleation and propagation of solidiﬁcation cracks in the nickel- based superalloys two major factors are required: mechanical factor which includes thermal stresses, phase transformation stresses and the die system effects. Metallurgical factor dependent to the stress-susceptible microstructural regions, which is dis- cussedbymanyresearchers(El-BagouryandNofal,2010).Inarecent study, Brundidge et al. (2012) focused on the solidiﬁca- tion metallurgical aspects of solidiﬁcation cracking. Franke et al. (2011) presented a numeric model on the dendritic structures in the nickel-based superalloys in order to assess the correlations between the microstructural features and the solidiﬁcation phe- nomenon. Some of other researchers ( Wei et al., 2010 ) published their results on the relation between the chemical composition and  

Dupont et al. (2009) demonstrated that Titanium, not only segregates to the ﬁnal liquid and increases the solidiﬁcation tem- perature range, but also facilitates the eutectic structure like  -Ni 3 Ti to form in the interdendritic regions. These results are also conﬁrmed by more recent research of Naffakh et al. (2010) . Based on the studies performed by Jiang et al. (2011) , Niobium and molyb- denum, similar to titanium, widen the mushy zone temperature range and produce structures prone to hot cracking. Wilson et al. (2008) showed that the terminal solidiﬁcation reactions; L →  - NbC and L →  -Laves, occurring in the interdendritic regions are very destructive to the castability of precipitation-strengthened nickel-based superalloys. Heretofore, Hobbs et al. (2005) and some others carried out researches in order to assess the effects of chem- ical composition and segregation phenomenon on the castability of precipitation-strengthened nickel-based superalloys. Neverthe- less, none of these studies attempt to discover the optimum chemical composition systematically, in which the best resistance to solidiﬁcation cracking (i.e. the best castability) of precipitation- strengthened nickel-based superalloys can be achieved. Recently, Naffakh Moosavy et al. (2012) conducted an algorithm to correlate the weldability to Ti/Al and Nb contents in the nickel-base super- alloys, in which, the boundary conditions adapted to be used for estimation of the mushy zone of ‘weld pool’. Also, the welding and microstructural parameters such as cooling rate ( ε ), gradient tem- perature ( G ), primary dendrite arm spacing (PDAS) and welding speed ( V ) were considered for solving the model. So, this model cannot be directly utilized for castability of superalloys, unless the boundary conditions are carefully deﬁned for the metal casting conditions. In addition, the work did not provide an experimental validation such as TG–DSC plots.  

Therefore, the current research tries to organize a new experimentally-veriﬁed model based on the Scheil mathemat- ical equation and microstructural characterizations to identify the solidiﬁcation paths, micro segregation in the interdendritic regions, solidiﬁcation temperature ranges, the formation of sec- ondary structures in the as-cast Ni-based superalloy. These results can lead to distinguish the best castable precipitation-strengthened superalloys.  

# 2. Experimental procedure  

Two types of the most versatile industrial precipitation- strengthened nickel-based superalloys having simple composition were selected as parent materials. The ﬁrst, Alloy 500 (i.e. Udimet 500) strengthened by  ′   intermetallic compound, and the second, Alloy 718 (i.e. Inconel 718) strengthened by  ′′   intermetallic com- pound. Based on these two superalloys, four new superalloys were designed. To reach to the required compositions, strips of pure tita- nium were used. Among the segregating elements, the Mo    content is constant in the composition of alloys, but, as the variant param- eter the amount of Ti and Al is decreasing from the Alloy 1 to Alloy 4. On the contrary, the amount of Nb is increasing. Chemical com- position of the parent and new-designed superalloys is mentioned in the Table 1 .  

The vacuum induction melting (VIM) furnace with the vacuum capacity of 10 − 4   bar, the frequency of 3.6 KHz and maximum power of 60 KW was employed for melting of the parent superalloys and adjusting the designed superalloy’s composition.  

Pure alumina crucible with 1000 l volume and steel die with dimensions of 150 mm × 100 mm × 50 mm were employed for pouring the resultant molten alloys. The temperature of 1450   ◦ C was selected for full melting of alloys, and the ingots were cooled at the ambient temperature. After the standard metallographic prepa- rations, the identiﬁcation of the microstructural evolutions was carried out by optical microscope equipped with image analyzer software. Further microstructural investigations were performed by scanning electron microscope (SEM) with accelerating volt- age of 30 KV equipped with energy dispersive X-ray spectrometer (EDS) for spot, line and map weight analysis. The TG–DSC anal- ysis were performed using the TG DTA/DSC apparatus equipped with related software for evaluating of the model results and the validity of solidiﬁcation temperature ranges obtained for the new-designed superalloys. The samples were put in to the 100  L alumina crucibles protected by argon shield. For each run the max- imum temperature of 1500   ◦ C and cooling rate of 20   ◦ C/min were applied. The results were presented as temperature ( ◦ C) versus electric potential (  V) plots.  

# 3. The analytical solidiﬁcation model  

# 3.1. The model description  

To present an appropriate perspective for the solidiﬁcation behavior and the alloying element segregation phenomenon in the designed superalloys the fundamental analytical concepts of solid- iﬁcation have been utilized. It is assumed that the molten alloys containing segregating elements with composition of C 0  have full and homogeneous mixing during entire time of solidiﬁcation pro- cess. In addition, this is assumed that the segregating elements have not interdiffusion inside the solid during the solidiﬁcation neither after the completion of the solidiﬁcation process. The solidiﬁcation follows the constitutional undercooling regime only. The governing conditions are schematically illustrated in the Fig. 1 .  

The model is developed on the basis of solidiﬁcation and segregation of the elements in to the liquid by utilizing ana- lytical mathematical equations that correlate the temperature, solid-liquid fraction, equilibrium coefﬁcient of distribution and composition of segregating elements in to the retained liquid of the melt. The model, for the ﬁrst time has been used by Dupont (1999) and Dupont et al. (1998) to evaluate the weldability of the austenitic stainless steels. Then, the model was adapted by Naffakh Moosavy et al. (2012) to be used for estimation of weldabil- ity in nickel-based superalloys. In the current research, the model has been improved to be utilized for the castability evaluation of precipitation-strengthened nickel-based alloy. By combining the mathematical equations with the microstructural characteristics, the solidiﬁcation temperature range of the melt and size of the mushy zone, in which the solidiﬁcation cracks propagate, can be calculated. The solidiﬁcation temperature range and mushy zone are valuable criteria to predict the susceptibility of castings to the hot cracking. Furthermore, prediction of the solidiﬁcation path and secondary structures formation in the ﬁnal solid are the other abil- ities of the model.

(2012) clariﬁed that Nb, Ti and Al display sever segregation in the dendritic boundary of Ni-base welds. They reported the k = 0.7 for their welds, while in the current work the k for Al is unity or more. This can be attributed to the non-equilibrium nature of weld solidiﬁcation.

This regulation is observed for all the segregation elements in the new-designed superalloys here. Moreover, the segregation phenomenon is the most for Alloy 2, which can cause to broaden the solidiﬁcation range and decrease the superalloys castability.

# 6. Conclusions  

•  An analytical solidiﬁcation model is developed for estimation of the castability behavior of Precipitation-Strengthened Nickel- based Superalloys.

 •  The model results have been presented as solidiﬁcation path curves, the segregation intensity in the interdendritic regions, solidiﬁcation temperature ranges and prediction of the secondary phases and structures formation.

 •  The calculated composition of segregating elements in the termi- nal liquid of solidiﬁcation displays intense segregation, especially for Ti and Nb in alloy 2 with segregated composition of 50 and 40 wt.%, respectively.

 •  The microstructural characterizations conﬁrm the formation of eutectic phases in all designed superalloys. These results are in agreement with model predictions.

 •  Model calculations demonstrate that the maximum and mini- mum solidiﬁcation temperature ranges are 211 and 71   ◦ C for the alloy 3 and alloy 2, respectively. These values exhibit an appro- priate conformity to the TG–DSC examinations.

 •  Based on the experimentally-approved model, superalloy with chemical composition of  -3.5%Mo,1.8%Al,4%Ti,2.9%Nb offered the least solidiﬁcation temperature range and the best castability behavior among the new-designed superalloys.
