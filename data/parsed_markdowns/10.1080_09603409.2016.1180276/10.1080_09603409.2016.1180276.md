# High temperature oxidation and γ ′  depletion  in the single-crystal superalloy PWA 1484  

Robert L. Amaro 1 , Stephen D. Antolovich \* , Richard W. Neu  and  Preet M. Singh 3  

Single-crystal Ni-base superalloys are a favoured material for blades in the hot section of aero turbines.  These materials exhibit excellent dimensional stability, deformation response and oxidation behaviour  at higher temperatures, relative to other metals. Due to the high service temperatures and fluctuating  loads experienced by turbine blades, a complete understanding of the material’s oxidation and  γ ′ depletion kinetics is sought to characterise this aspect of the material’s deformation response. This  work provides the results and subsequent analysis of stress-free  γ ′  depletion tests performed on the  single-crystal Ni-base superalloy PWA 1484. It was determined that the  γ ′  depletion kinetics of this  material followed a sub-parabolic rate law. Furthermore,  γ ′  depletion kinetics were dependent upon  surface roughness. Surface roughness can be an issue in practical application of oxidation principles  and data. A relationship is developed, based on the literature and current work, which relates the  stress-free  γ ′  depletion kinetics to exposure temperature, time and surface roughness.  

Keywords:   Oxidation kinetics,  γ ′  depletion, Surface roughness, PWA 1484  

# Introduction  

Ni-base superalloys consist primarily of a two phase micro- structure comprised of a γ matrix and γ ′  precipitates along  with secondary phases such as carbides. While the precip- itates have the Ni Al (L1 ) crystal structure, the matrix is  3 2 austenitic with a high Ni concentration and other elements  added for specific purposes (e.g. to confer oxidation resist- ance, to control the γ/γ ′  mismatch, to provide solid solution  strengthening, etc.). Given the strong affinity for Ni and Al to  form the precipitate, there is a minimal aluminium content in  γ matrix. When exposed to high temperatures the Al diffuses  from the γ ′  phase toward the material surface, forming an  oxide or oxides, according to the thermodynamic stability of  the oxides in question.  Once the aluminium diffuses from the  γ ′  precipitates, their stability is greatly reduced and a denuded  zone is formed which is effectively 100% γ matrix. As the  precipitates provide the primary strengthening mechanism  for the alloy at temperatures above \~373 K (100 °C),  the  γ ′  depleted zone resulting from high temperature exposure  creates a softer subsurface region which is then susceptible  to further damage at high temperatures.  

The majority of the superalloy oxidation studies to date  are predicated upon the findings of Giggens and Pettit,  who  examined the isothermal oxidation of nickel-chromium-  aluminium ternary alloys. This study focused on the three  key alloying elements that have the highest impact during  oxidation. In this work, the authors looked at 23 alloys  having differing weight fractions of elements, from Ni–5Cr  and Ni–3Al to Ni–30Cr–6Al. All of the alloys studied had an  initial transient oxidation period lasting up to an hour, during  which time the oxidation kinetics did not follow a simple rate  law. The transient period was characterised by rapid conver- sion of surface layers to oxides, where the morphology and  composition of the oxide layers changed with time. Once the  oxide stabilised, the alloys studied fell into one of the four  categories summarised in Table  1 .  

A study conducted on the isothermal oxidation behaviour  of directionally solidified superalloy CM247LC at 1373 K  (1100 °C) concluded that the material exhibited a long tran- sition period (20 h) during which time the oxide was charac- terised as Group I, followed by a transition to Group II.  This  study determined that the oxidation kinetics for this material  followed a parabolic rate law once the transition period ended.  Furthermore, the oxidation of this alloy produced a sizeable  γ ′ -depleted zone near the surface whenever an alumini- um-bearing oxide formed. The authors also examined the  cyclic oxidation resistance of the alloy. Not surprisingly the  2 3 authors determined that oxidation kinetics were dominated  by diffusion through the inner α-Al O  layer. 2 3  

In agreement with the preceding discussion, the complex- ity of oxidation in Ni-base superalloys was well illustrated  by the work of Andrieu  et al .,  where the effects of oxygen  partial pressure were examined in a study of fatigue in alloy  IN 718, a Ni–Fe-based alloy containing γ ′′  precipitates. They  demonstrated that NiO and FeO (spinels) would form ini- tially. These are fairly porous, friable oxides through which  oxygen can diffuse to the base metal enabling continued  growth. However, the partial pressure of oxygen is signifi- cantly reduced because of the tortuous path that oxygen must  follow. After some growth, the oxygen partial pressure was  insufficient for these oxides to be thermodynamic ally stable at  the interface, as can be understood by straightforward appeal  to a Richardson diagram for oxide formation.  However, at  the reduced pressure Cr O  is thermodynamic ally stable and  2 3 forms beneath the mixed oxide layer, thus passivating the  surface and reducing oxide-assisted crack growth. Further  illustrating the complexity of oxidation in this class of mate- rial, it was found that there was an incubation time for such  passivation to occur. This work clearly illustrates the interplay  between thermodynamic equilibrium and details of the kinetic  process during oxidation.  

Aluminium-rich oxides that form on Ni-base superalloys  create a subsurface region which is depleted of γ ′ . This occurs  by one of two processes: (a) either aluminium diffuses to the  surface of the alloy to create an Al-rich surface oxide, or (b)  oxygen diffuses into the alloy creating subsurface alumina  particles. Thermally induced γ ′ -depletion has been reported  for nearly every superalloy studied.  

Several researchers have debated whether to use oxida- tion or γ ′  depletion kinetics as the high temperature fatigue  degradation mechanism of importance in their life estimation  algorithms. Wright 14  concluded that oxidation kinetics were  more appropriate for crack initiation estimation ‘since ini- tiation is primarily a surface phenomenon’. This argument,  however, neglects the fact that the load carrying capabilities  of the subsurface material are diminished by γ ′  depletion,  and therefore could have a strong influence on crack initi- ation. Zhao 15 ,  and Dumoulin,  on the other hand, utilised  γ ′  depletion kinetics in the development of their material  deformation and life estimation schemes. Others have uti- lised both oxidation and γ ′  depletion kinetics.  The distinction  between the two damage mechanisms, however, may not be  necessary in practical situations. Additionally, the  kinetic relationship is modified to incorporate the effect of  surface roughness, the effects of which have been studied on  this alloy,  but not previously incorporated into an overarching  kinetics relationship.  

# Material  

Oxidation and γ ′  depletion studies were performed on the  second-generation single-crystal superalloy PWA 1484. The  material’s microstructure is comprised of small, coherent,  Ni (Al,X) L1  (FCC-like) precipitates (γ ′ ), embedded in a  3 2 nickel FCC matrix (γ) which is a substitutional solid solution  of Ni and other elements.  The nominal chemical composition  of PWA 1484 is shown in Table  2 .  

Standard heat treatment consists of a three-step process.  The first step is a 1588 K (1315 °C) solution heat treatment  for 4 h. Subsequent cooling results in the γ ′  precipitation into a  uniform array of fine cuboidal particles (side length <0.3 μm).  The second step is a 1353 K (1080 °C) heat treatment for four  hours which provides optimum γ ′  size distribution. Finally,  a 977 K (704 °C) heat treat at 24 h produces fine γ ′  particles  (side length <0.1 μm) between the larger particles, resulting  in enhanced ‘intermediate temperature’ yield strength 2 ,  The  heat-treated ready-for-service condition alloy has a primary γ ′ particle size between 0.25 and 0.35 μm, a γ ′  volume fraction  of approximately 63%, a density of 8.94 × 10 −3  kg/cm 3 , an  incipient melting temperature of 1611 K (1338 °C) and a γ ′ solvus temperature of 1572 K (1299 °C).  The representative  heat treated microstructure is shown in Fig.  1 .

# Discussion  

PWA 1484 oxidises in a manner similar to what Giggins and  Pettit 1  term a ‘Transition Alloy’. This oxidation behaviour is  characterised by several stable oxides competing concurrently  on the alloy surface accompanied by a subsurface γ ′  depleted  layer. The dominant oxide and relative amount of γ ′  deple- tion depend upon temperature and subsurface chemical com- position, among other things. The two most common oxide  morphologies found on PWA 1484 consists of either a friable  outer spinel (Ni(Cr,Al,Co) O ) with subsurface α-Al O  par- 2 4 2 3 ticles, or an outer oxide scale of (Ni,Co)Al O  and NiCr O 2 4 2 4 spinels with Al O , and an inner oxide of adherent α-Al O . 2 3 2 3  

Several studies have been conducted on both oxidation  and γ ′  depletion kinetics of superalloys. , , ,  The results  of these studies indicate that the diffusion coefficient,  D , and  the time exponent,  n , in Equation (1) are very similar for both  processes. This should therefore not be surprising, given that  a primary constituent of the oxides formed on these materials  is aluminium, and the primary source of aluminium is the γ ′ phase. Without γ ′  depletion, the oxides found on Ni-base super  alloys would not occur to the extent that they do. It is therefore  understood that the two processes are coupled in such a way  as to allow one to infer information about one from the other.  

The existence of competing oxides on a single surface  (Fig.  7 ) indicates that the oxides have similar kinetics d  and a  slight perturbation in the system may result in either a pro- tective or non-protective oxide. This situation sets up the  necessity for an ‘averaging’ of the effects from both oxide  types, if occurring concurrently, when determining diffusion  kinetics. Though the results of such a study will not provide  a full understanding of the activation energy for the growth of  Al O  on a particular alloy (as an example), it does however  2 3 provide sufficient insight into the kinetics of the  totality  of the  complex processes  actually occurring  and therefore provides  a useful engineering tool.  

A result of the less protective oxide which forms on PWA  1484 (Al O  and NiO-doped with spinel) is the existence  2 3 of aluminium platelets which form in the γ ′  depleted zone.  These platelets are the result of excess Al with respect to  that required for the oxides that are formed. The fact that the  platelets are composed of an Al-rich phase (largely Al) rather  than Al O , indicates that oxygen ingress into the base allow  2 3 is less likely in PWA 1484 than the superalloys which formed  subsurface Al O  platelets. 2 3

Thus, the activation energy determined in this study should be  thought of as effective activation energy for the γ ′  depletion  processes occurring between these temperatures. # Summary and conclusions  

The principal results of this study are:  

•   PWA 1484 oxidises as a transition alloy, characterised by  multiple competing oxides forming concurrently. Oxides  produced at1323  K (1050  °C) primarily consisted of  either Al O  and NiO or Al O  and NiO interspersed with  2 3 2 3 NiAl O  spinel particles. Exposure at 1223 K (950 °C)  2 4 resulted in two different oxides forming on PWA 1484.  The dendritic regions produced an oxide composed of  CoO and NiO, while the interdendritic region yielded NiO,  CoO with small amounts of Cr O  and Al O . The oxides  2 3 2 3 produced at 1123 K (850 °C) were composed of Cr O ,  2 3 CoO and NiO, with small amounts of Al O  and Ta O . 2 3 2 5 •   Oxidation kinetics may be inferred from γ ′  depletion  measurements due to the two processes being intrinsi- cally linked by Al diffusion. The result of γ ′  depletion  is a material lacking its primary strengthening phase. In  which case the principal hindrance to dislocation motion  would be the lattice, rather than the much harder γ ′  phase,  resulting in a far weaker and more ductile material. •   Characterisation of γ ′  depletion for PWA 1484 indicates  that the material follows a power law rate equation.  Whereas, depletion kinetics at 1123 K (850 °C) are very  nearly parabolic, the kinetics at higher temperatures are  sub-parabolic, indicating that a transition in oxide mor- phology and character occurs between 1123 and 1223 K  (850 and 950 °C). •   The effective activation energy was measured to be  162.9 kJ/mol, compared with oxygen diffusion in Ni  (309.2. kJ/mol 30 ) and Al in Ni-base alloys (230–256 kJ/ mol, depending upon Re content 31 ). •   Oxidation kinetics are highly surface roughness depend- ent. An increase in the arithmetic average surface rough- ness from  R  = 8 nm to  R  = 129 nm results in a twofold  a a increase in γ ′  depletion depth in 300 h.  

•   A relationship was proposed to predict γ ′  depletion  kinetics as a function of time and temperature (Equation  2). Furthermore, a separate relationship was proposed  which correlates the effects of surface roughness on γ ′ depletion (Equation 4).
