# ABSTRACT  

In this study, we experimentally measure the viscosity,  η , and ionic conductivity,  σ , of 1 mol kg − 1   LiPF 6  dissolved in a binary solvent of ethylene carbonate (EC) and propylene carbonate (PC) by varying the EC content from 0 to 60 vol%. Replacing EC with PC does not signi ﬁ cantly in ﬂ uence on the mechanism of  ﬂ ow and ionic conduction. The state of solvent solvating to Li +   is analyzed using Raman spectra and   13 C-NMR; the quantitative analysis suggests that the preference of EC in the Li +   solvation shell is almost similar to that of PC. The di ﬀ usion coe ﬃ cient,  D , of each species, Li + , PF 6 − , EC, and PC, is determined by pulse-gradient spin-echo NMR. In the electrolyte system dealt in this study, the molar conductivity is dominated by only the  D  of the charge carriers. The hydrodynamic Stokes radius of all constituent species is expressed in terms of  D  and  η  using the Stokes – Einstein relation. As for coordinated-Li + , a comparison between the Stokes radius and the van der Waals radius or the radius considering the free volume suggests that Li +   di ﬀ uses while dragging not only a coordination shell that can be spectroscopic ally detected but also an outer shell that binds loosely to Li + .

# 1. Introduction  

Non-aqueous electrolyte solutions, an essential component of the lithium ion batteries (LIBs), provide a medium for the migration of charge carriers between negative and positive electrodes. Transport phenomena in an electrolyte solution, which are one of the dominant factors determining the performance of the LIBs, are not only academically interesting but also industrially important. In typical LIBs, a binary solvent combines a high dielectric constant solvent with one of low viscosity. The former, for example, ethylene carbonate (EC) and propylene carbonate (PC) dissociate cations and anions, whereas the latter, for example, dimethyl carbonate (DMC) and diethyl carbonate (DEC), enhance ionic movement.   The role – of each solvent component has been intensively analyzed. For example, the self-di ﬀ usion coe ﬃ cients of the constituents were –   determined using pulse gradient spin-echo NMR 2 4 and the – solvation state of Li + , which is assumed to signi ﬁ cantly a ﬀ ect solid electrolyte interface formation, 11 has been intensively investigated using various spectroscopic techniques. 17 The knowledge and insights gained from these studies have still continued to support improvements of LIB ’ s performances. We have recognized the signi ﬁ cance of analytical studies on the electrolyte solutions and have also analyzed in detail and system- atically the transport phenomena in electrolyte solutions based on the binary solvents of EC and DMC.   Meanwhile, studies regarding lithium secondary batteries that use a lithium metal as a negative electrode tend to avoid the use of electrolyte solutions containing linear carbonates, in which dendritic lithium deposition which causes a short circuit, occurs with dissolution / precipitation cycles. 22   Furthermore, some candidates of positive electrode materials for next-generation lithium batteries developed in recent years exhibit poor cycle stability in electrolyte solutions containing linear carbonate. In these cases, an electrolyte system with a solvent composed of only cyclic carbonates is typically applied. ,   The electrolyte solutions, which comprise lithium salts and solvents with a high dielectric constant, have not received signi ﬁ cant attention as a practical solution previously; that is, not much basic information and insights have been accumulated on the electrolytes needed to design secondary batteries that take into account ion migration and interfacial behavior. Hence, a detailed analysis of the transport properties and solvation state of the abovementioned electrolytes should be conducted for the development of future lithium batteries.  

In the present study, we dealt 1 mol kg ¹ 1   LiPF 6  solution with binary solvent comprising EC and PC, in which the EC content ranged from 0 to 60  %  by volume. The density, viscosity, ionic conductivity, solvation or coordination states, and self-di ﬀ usion coe ﬃ cient were analyzed as a function of the solvent composition, from which we discuss the transport properties of the electrolyte solutions in terms of the coordination state, ionic dissociation, and static and hydrodynamic size of the constituent species.

# 2.2 Viscosity, ionic conductivity, and di ﬀ usion coe ﬃ cient  

The viscosity,  © , of the solvent and electrolyte solution and the speci ﬁ c conductivity,  · , of the electrolyte solution in the temperature range of 288 328 K were measured using a cone plate-type viscosity meter and an electrochemical impedance technique with four-electrode cell, respectively. Detailed measurement, calibration, and calculation methods can be reviewed in our previous paper. Two or three series of measurement and calculation processes were repeated for each solvent and solution and the mean was used as the ﬁ nal result.  

The self-di ﬀ usion coe ﬃ cient,  D , at  T  =  298 K for the solvent molecules, lithium ions, and anions was estimated using pulsed gradient spin-echo (PGSE) NMR. In the present study, a sym- metrical micro sample tube (Shigemi, BMS-005J) was adopted to improve the accuracy of the measurement results. Other measure- ment and analysis conditions were explained in our previous paper.   Figure S2 shows an example of the spin-echo intensity pro ﬁ le, in which good linearity proved a highly accurate measure- ment. The mean value of two measurements was adopted.  

# 2.3 Raman spectrum and   13 C-NMR  

A laser Raman microscope was used to investigate the mole fraction of EC solvating to Li +   in the solution with the EC solvent only. Detailed measurement methods are described in our previous paper.   Some samples with melting points higher than room temperature were measured in the liquid phase by maintaining an overcooled state. Two measurements were performed for each sample, from which the mean value of the peak area fraction was determined.  

13 C-NMR spectra related to the carbonyl group of EC in the solvent and the electrolyte solution were measured at  T  =  298 K using a 400 MHz spectrometer (JEOL, JMTC-400 / 54 / JJ) with a probe unit adjusted to the nuclide of   13 C (100 MHz) and a coaxial sample tube (Shigemi, SPT-404), where deuterated chloroform and the sample were sealed into an outer and an inner tube, respectively, to separate them from each other. All NMR spectra were referenced to tetra methyl si lane. Some samples were measured under over- cooled conditions in the same manner as Raman spectroscopy. The mean of the two measurement results was adopted.

# 4. Conclusions  

We experimentally measured a series of transport properties, i.e., the viscosity, ionic conductivity, and di ﬀ usion coe ﬃ cient, of EC + PC binary solvents with compositions of 0  ¯  x EC  ¯  0.663 and 1 mol kg ¹ 1   LiPF 6  solutions with the binary solvents. Further- more, we performed Raman spectroscopy and   13 C-NMR to estimate the quantity of species coordinated to Li +   in the solution. The experimental insights obtained in this study are summarized as follows.  

The average number of EC and PC solvating to Li +   was simply proportional to the solvent composition, that is, the preference of EC in the Li +   solvation shell was similar to that of PC. Replacing EC with PC did not a ﬀ ect the conduction and  ﬂ ow mechanisms; this suggests that EC and PC behaved similarly in the electrolyte with their binary solvent. The ratio of the hypothetical molar conductiv- ity, when the charge carriers were assumed to move independently, and the real one indicated that the apparent ionic dissociation degree was constant regardless of  x EC , which implies that EC and PC exhibited the same dielectric shielding e ﬀ ect despite their di ﬀ erent dielectric constants. Therefore, the molar conductivity of the solution with the EC + PC binary solvent was dominated only by the di ﬀ usion coe ﬃ cient of charge carriers and hence increased monotonically as  x EC  increased. A comparison of the hydrodynamic Stokes radius of Li +   to the static radii of coordinated-Li +   suggests that Li +   di ﬀ uses while dragging not only a coordination shell that is directly detected by spectroscopies but also an outer shell that binds loosely to Li + .  

The insights into the detailed transport properties obtained from the present study are presumably common among highly dissociative lithium salts, which provides some important perspectives regarding the appropriate electrolyte solution design when linear carbonates must be avoided because of their poor chemical or electrochemical compatibility with electrode materials in future lithium batteries.
