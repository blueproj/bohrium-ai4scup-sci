# Modeling of thermal deformation behavior near  γ ′  solvus in a Ni-based powder metallurgy superalloy  

⁎ Ming Zhang, Guoquan Liu, Hao Wang , Benfu Hu  

School of Materials Science and Engineering, University of Science and Technology Beijing, Beijing, China  

# A B S T R A C T  

# A R T I C L E I N F O  

We used two di ﬀ erent methods to model the thermal deformation behavior of a Ni-based powder metallurgy superalloy at large strains (> 0.6) near the  γ ′  solvus. We concluded that the Arti ﬁ cial Neural Network model, rather than the strain-compensated Arrhenius model, describes thermal deformation behavior accurately under large strains during hot extrusion. Arti ﬁ cial neural network, a data-driven machine learning approach, is more suitable for predicting unknown deformation behavior in extreme conditions by data learning based on a known experimental dataset.

# 1. Introduction  

Ni-based  γ ′  phase strengthened superalloys are widely used in manufacturing advanced gas turbine disks and other hot components due to their excellent high temperature performance  [1] . However, high alloying (mass fraction > 50%) in superalloys can decrease their plasticity, making it very di ﬃ cult to control the microstructure during hot working  [2,3] . Recent studies  [4,5]  show that when thermo- mechanical processing temperatures of hot isostatic pressed (HIPed) Ni- based superalloys are below the  γ ′  solvus, the microstructure changes gradually from the  γ ′  dispersion structure to the  γ  +  γ ′  duplex structure. The  ﬁ ne grain  γ  +  γ ′  duplex structure decreases  ﬂ ow stress and im- proves plasticity of the alloy  [6,7] .  

It is of great theoretical and practical signi ﬁ cance to study the thermal deformation behavior near the  γ ′  solvus, including constitutive relations, dynamic recrystallization kinetics, and microstructure evo- lution during thermal deformation. Thermo-mechanical simulation tests are usually used for this; however, for superalloys, it is di ﬃ cult to perform these simulations under large strains below the  γ ′  solvus. When deformation temperatures are below the  γ ′  solvus, there is some of  γ ′ phase (volume fraction 10 – 30%) in the microstructure, inhibiting dy- namic recrystallization  [8,9] . Even if true strain reaches 0.6, complete recrystallization is still not obtained, and  ﬂ ow curves do not reach steady state. When true strain is above 0.6, cylindrical compression samples may bulge or even crack  [10] . Therefore, it is impossible to obtain the  ﬂ ow curves from the beginning of deformation to steady- state (at the large strain level) with thermo-mechanical simulation tests.  

# 2. Material and methods  

The chemical composition in mass percent of the hot isostatically pressed FGH98 was: 12.7Cr - 20.4Co - 3.8W - 2.6Mo - 3.5Al - 3.7Ti - 0.9Nb - 2.4Ta - 0.05C - (bal.) Ni. The solvus of the alloy was 1160 °C.  

We carried out hot deformation tests under isothermal constant strain rate with a Gleeble-3500 thermo-mechanical simulator, and re- corded the true stress - true strain curves. The test temperatures were 1060, 1105, 1138, and 1165 °C, and the strain rates were 0.01, 0.1, 1, and 10 s − 1 . When the true strain reached 0.6, the load was removed and the samples were water-cooled to maintain the high temperature microstructure.

We constructed the model with the following steps  [13] . First, we used the true stress-true strain curves and derived the values of ln A ,  n ,  α and  Q  by multivariate linear  ﬁ tting when the strains are 0.05, 0.1, 0.15, … , 0.55, and 0.6, respectively. We established the ANN model with MATLAB 2014b  [12] . The structure of the ANN is a two-layer feed-forward network containing a hidden layer and an output layer. The network was trained by Bayesian Regularization based on the true stress-true strain curves. The number of hidden layer neurons was 10. A total of 960 data points were ran- domly divided into 3 groups: 70% for training, 15% for validation, and 15% for testing.  

We carried out the hot extrusion test on a 6000-ton hot extruder. The diameter of the HIPed billet was 250 mm, the height was 250 mm, and the thickness of the 316 stainless canning was 10 mm. The initial temperature of the billet was 1100 °C, the extrusion rate was 60 mm/s, the die angle was 40°, and the extrusion ratio was 4:1. We used DEFORM 11.0 software to simulate the hot extrusion process. The size of the billet and the process parameters were the same as those of the hot extrusion test.  

# 3. Results and discussion  

# 3.1. Prediction results of two models  

The constitutive relationships predicted by the two models are shown in  Fig. 1 .  

For the strain-compensated Arrhenius model, when the true strains were less than 0.6, the average absolute relative error ( AARE %) be- tween the predicted values and the experimental values was 6.14%, and the correlation coe ﬃ cient ( R ) is 0.97, indicating the model predicts accurately at small strains. When the true strains were greater than 0.6, the true stresses predicted by the model decreased gradually to 0 for all deformation conditions. This is not consistent with the actual behavior of the material. It is clear that the Arrhenius-type model is not suitable for describing thermal deformation behavior of superalloys at large strains. The reason possibly lies in the polynomial  ﬁ tting of the model parameters. Although polynomial  ﬁ t at small strain conditions is good, it is not suitable for predicting thermal deformation behavior at large strains without any data because the behavior of the thermal de- formation at small and large strains is di ﬀ erent.  

For the ANN model, when true strain was less than 0.6, the  AARE % between the predicted value and the experimental value was 0.52%;  R was 0.99. It is clear that prediction accuracy of ANN model is higher than the strain-compensated Arrhenius model at small strain condi- tions. Moreover, it is worth noting that the ANN model can predict steady state true stress-true strain curves at each deformation condition. The prediction also shows that the curves reach steady state easily as temperature increases or strain rate decreases in accordance with both theoretical analysis and actual behavior  [14] .  

3.2. Flow characteristics and microstructures evolution  

The ANN model was obtained through data training with data clo- sely related to the microstructure evolution. Therefore, the ANN model contained information related to microstructure evolution and could predict steady state. Taking the strain rate of 1 s − 1   as an example, we analyzed the close relationship between the curve characteristics and the microstructure evolution of the alloy during thermal deformation ( Fig. 2 ).  

We found that the  ﬂ ow stress curve is composed of three stages: stage I (work hardening stage), stage II (softening stage), and stage III (steady stage)  [14] . In the work hardening stage,  ﬂ ow stress rapidly increased to a peak value. The peak stress ( σ p ) and the critical stress ( σ c ) for substructure formation decrease with increasing deformation tem- perature. Increase in deformation temperature favors rearrangement or realignment of dislocations, leading to the formation of dislocation cells and subgrains. At higher temperatures, the substructure formation stage was indistinguishable from the dynamic recrystallization (DRX) initia- tion stage  [15] . Discontinuous dynamic recrystallization (DDRX), characterized by grain boundary bulging, is the dominant softening mechanism of as-HIPed superalloy under all studied hot deformation conditions  [16]  and is promoted by increasing deformation tempera- ture  [17,18] . The grain size of the HIPed FGH98 alloy was larger (about 30  μ m), and grain structures under various deformation conditions were re ﬁ ned because of DRX. The area fraction of recrystallized grains increases with increasing strain. Complete recrystallization throughout the entire microstructure can be obtained at the necessary strain level [19] . Meanwhile,  ﬂ ow curves can reach steady state  [20] . When the temperature was close to or higher than the  γ ′  solvus, such as 1138 and 1165 °C, true stress-true strain curves reached steady-state  ﬂ ow. The true stress at steady state remained constant with increasing true strain; this constant stress is called the steady-state stress ( σ s ), and the corre- sponding strain is called the steady-state strain. The microstructures at temperatures of 1138 and 1165 °C were completely recrystallized, and the curves at these temperatures reached steady state when true strain is only 0.4 – 0.5.  

The lower the deformation temperature, the more  γ ′  phase there is in the microstructure. Lower temperatures and a certain amount of  γ ′ will inhibit dynamic recrystallization  [9,10] . Therefore, when true strain reached 0.6, the microstructures are not fully recrystallized, and the curves did not reach a steady state. At the same temperature and strain rate, with an increase in true strain, the stress and grain size gradually decreased. Under the same strain and rate, the higher the temperature, the smaller the stress and the larger the grain size be- comes.  

In addition, when the temperatures are below the  γ ′  solvus, not only does dynamic recrystallization occur, resulting in  ﬁ ne grain size, but the microstructures change from  γ ′  dispersion to both  γ  and  γ ′  existing together as grains. This combined action makes the stress decrease greatly, and  ﬂ ow softening occurs  [5] . The thermal deformation be- havior of the alloy under this condition is complex, and its constitutive relation cannot be described by a simple model  [4] . The information of microstructure evolution is hidden in the curves, and the ANN model constructed by data training contained this relevant information, al- lowing it to describe and predict this special thermal deformation be- havior.  

# 3.3. Hot deformation behavior at large strains  

We analyzed the dynamic recrystallization kinetics of the alloy with the ANN model. For low stacking fault energy alloys, the dynamic re- crystallization fraction ( X ) could be derived from the true stress-true strain curves predicted by the ANN model and could be expressed as X  = ( σ p  − σ )/( σ p  − σ s )  [21] . At various temperatures, the relationships between steady grain sizes ( λ s ) and  σ s  can be expressed by ln σ s  = a  − n·ln λ s  [23] , where  a  and  n  are temperature dependent constants. Through the  σ s  predicted by the ANN model, we could cal- culate the steady-state grain size for each deformation condition. From the initial grain size, steady-state grain sizes, and dynamic re- crystallization kinetic curves, we drew the microstructure evolution curves as shown in  Fig. 3 c. # 3.4. Veri ﬁ cation of the ANN model  

With the ANN model, we can analyze the entire thermal deforma- tion behavior from the beginning of deformation to the steady state near the  γ ′  solvus. Using the predicted results of ANN model as input parameters, we carried out a  ﬁ nite element simulation of the real hot extrusion process to verify the reliability of the ANN model. We com- pared the simulation results with experimental observations.  

The simulation results showed that the diameter of the superalloy bar after extrusion was 117.8 mm the thickness of the 316 stainless steel canning was 5.5 mm, and the experimental results were 118 mm and 6 mm, respectively ( Fig. 4 a, b). It was accurate to simulate the shape change. The microstructure of the extrusion bar was a  ﬁ ne, uniformly recrystallized  γ  +  γ ′  microduplex structure ( Fig. 4 c). The grain size increased from 4.7 to 6.8, and then reduced to 4.1 from the core to the surface of the bar. Extrusion simulation results showed that the tem- perature distribution is not uniform. The strain of the core to the surface of the bar increases gradually, and the heat generated also increases. In addition, the surface of the bar transfers part of the heat to the mold (300 °C), so a maximum temperature occurs at a distance of 40 mm from the core. The strains of the core and surface of the bar were very large (> 1.5), so the microstructures had reached steady state and no longer varied with the strain. Also, the distribution of the strain rate was uniform (about 1.5 s − 1 ). Therefore, temperature distribution is the main factor determining the size distribution of grains; the simulation results for grain size distribution are shown in  Fig. 4 d. The simulation results are in good agreement with the experimental results. This in- dicates that the aforementioned analysis of thermal deformation be- havior under large strain is accurate, and the constructed ANN model can accurately predict the thermal deformation behavior under large strains without complete data.  

# 4. Conclusions  

We established the strain-compensated Arrhenius model and the ANN model to predict the thermal deformation behavior of a Ni-based powder metallurgy superalloy. The prominent  ﬁ ndings of the study are summarized below.  

(1) Although the prediction of the strain-compensated Arrhenius model is accurate when true strain is less than 0.6, the prediction results are not reliable when true stain is greater than 0.6, indicating that the polynomials in the model are not appropriate.

 (2) The ANN model constructed by data learning from the known ex- perimental dataset contains information about microstructure evolution and can be used to describe the complete thermal de- formation behavior for both low and high stain conditions.
