ABSTRACT:  Solubilities were measured for  ﬁ ve lithium salts (LiF, LiCl, LiBr, LiPF 6 , and LiTFSI) in 1-butyl-3-methyl i mid a zo liu m dicyanamide

 ([BMIM][N(CN) 2 ]) and in 1-butyl-3-methyl i mid a zo liu m tetra ﬂ uoroborate

 ([BMIM][BF 4 ]) from 298.15 to 343.15 K under atmospheric pressure. Measurements were made using inductively coupled plasma-optical emission spectroscopy (ICP-OES). Solubilities increase with rising temperature. The highest solubility is for LiPF 6 , followed by LiTFSI, LiBr, LiCl, and LiF. In both ionic liquids, LiF is essentially insoluble. A comparison of the solubilities of the same salts in di ﬀ erent [BMIM]-based ILs shows that the solubility order is [BMIM][OTf] < [BMIM][BF 4 ] < [BMIM][N(CN) 2 ]. The new data were correlated by the Margules equation where the standard state for the salt ’ s activity coe ﬃ cient is the hypothetical pure liquid salt. Estimates are given for total enthalpy, entropy, and Gibbs energy of dissolution.  

To increase the solubility database for lithium salts in ionic liquids, , − 26   we have measured the solubilities of  ﬁ ve lithium salts (LiF, LiCl, LiBr, LiPF 6 , and LiTFSI) in 1-butyl-3- methyl i mid a zo liu m dicyanamide ([BMIM][N(CN) 2 ]) and in 1-butyl-3-methyl i mid a zo liu m tetra ﬂ uoroborate ([BMIM]- [BF 4 ]) from 298.15 to 343.15 K using inductively coupled plasma-optical emission spectroscopy (ICP-OES).  

# 1. INTRODUCTION  

Lithium-ion batteries are e ﬃ cient energy-storage devices used, for example, in cell phones, laptop computers, and electric − 5   vehicles. However, organic solvent-based electrolytes pres- ently used in lithium-ion batteries carry some safety risks and reduce the operating temperature range due to solvent ﬂ ,   ammability. Ionic liquids (ILs) that consist of large organic cations and organic or inorganic anions are liquid salts at (or near) ambient temperature. Owing to their remarkable proper- ties (e.g., non ﬂ ammability, very low vapor pressure, chemical stability, and wide electrochemical window), ILs have received − 14 much attention as solvents in lithium-ion batteries.

# 4. CONCLUSIONS  

For assisting the design of lithium-ion batteries, solubilities were measured for LiF, LiCl, LiBr, LiTFSI, and LiPF 6  in [BMIM]- [N(CN) 2 ], and in [BMIM][BF 4 ] from 298.15 to 343.15 K. The solubility data were correlated by the Margules equation in which the standard state for the solute ’ s activity coe ﬃ cient is the pure lique ﬁ ed solute. For each salt, solubility increases with rising temperature, and decreases in the order: LiPF 6  > LiTFSI > LiBr > LiCl > LiF. LiF is essentially insoluble in both ILs. Solubility rises with decreasing salt melting temperature. Solutes containing weakly hydrated anions show lower solubilities. The solubility order is [BMIM][OTf] < [BMIM][BF 4 ] < [BMIM]- [N(CN) 2 ]. From experimental data and activity coe ﬃ cients at saturation, mixing properties were calculated. All enthalpies of dissolution are positive, indicating that the dissolution process is endothermic. The arithmetic average of attractive interactions for solute − solute interactions and those for solvent − solvent interactions exceed those for solute − solvent interactions.
