# Effects of alloying elements on dendritic segregation in iridium alloys

# Abstract  

The effects of alloying elements on dendritic segregation in Ir alloys were studied by Auger Electron Spectroscopy (AES). The addition of 10 at.% Nb induces signiﬁcant segregation of carbon and thorium to dendritic interfaces. The addition of 5 at.% Zr to the Ir alloy leads to the formation of an Ir 3 Zr intermetallic phase, which results in less dendritic segregation of carbon and thorium. This dendritic segregation appears to be linked to the severe cracking observed in the Ir–Nb alloy, but not in the Ir–Zr alloy, after casting and heat treatment. The mechanism for the dendritic segregation was discussed by consideration of the solidiﬁcation process and the change of carbon solubility in Ir by Zr addition.

# 1. Introduction  

Iridium has attractive properties for applications at temper- atures up to 2000   ◦ C. It has a high melting point (2443   ◦ C), good oxidation resistance and high strength at elevated tem- peratures, and has been used as components or thermal barrier coatings in spacecrafts  [1,2] . Though iridium has a face cen- tered cubic (FCC) structure, it usually fractures in a brittle manner at temperatures below 1000   ◦ C, showing a great ten- dency to cleave along grain boundaries  [3–5] . An Ir alloy that has been successfully used in space power applications is DOP- 26, which was developed by Liu et al. and has a nominal composition of Ir–0.3W–0.006Th–0.005Al (wt.%)  [5,6] . In this alloy, thorium serves two purposes  [5,7] : (1) it forms highly stable Ir 5 Th precipitates, which pin the grain boundaries and reﬁne the grain size, and (2) it segregates to the grain bound- aries where it enhances cohesion. Together, they contribute to improved ductility and a signiﬁcantly lower ductile–brittle tran- sition temperature (DBTT)  [5] . Besides Th, there are other elements, such as, Ce, Y and Lu  [8] , which strongly segre- gate to the grain boundaries of Ir. However, their effectiveness in improving grain-boundary cohesion is less than that of Th [9] . On the other hand, additions of some elements, such as Si and P, which also segregate to the grain boundaries of Ir, result in embrittlement and increase the tendency for intergranu- lar fracture  [10,11] . Thus, grain-boundary chemistry has a strong inﬂuence on the mechanical properties of Ir-based alloys. In as- cast alloys, dendritic segregation is another important factor that inﬂuences mechanical behavior and workability. However, such studies have not been carried out for Ir alloys.  

Using precipitation strengthened Ni-based superalloys as a guide, high-strength Ir-based alloys have recently been designed and investigated  [12,13] . It was found that Ir–Nb (or Ta) and Ir–Zr (or Hf) alloys show superior compression strengths up to 1200   ◦ C, and thus have a strong precipitation strengthening effect  [14] . The additions of Nb (Ta) and Zr (Hf) were aimed at introducing L1 2  intermetallic phases in the solid-solution irid- ium matrix. However, the effects of these alloying elements on the segregation of trace elements have not been studied. In this work, we modiﬁed the composition of DOP-26 iridium by adding Nb and Zr alloying elements. However, after casting and heattreatment,we found that severe cracking occurred in theN b- containing alloy while no cracking occurred in the Zr-containing alloy. Therefore, we decided to study the effect of these two alloying elements on dendritic segregation in as-cast Ir alloys. It was also reported that the strength of Ir-based binary alloys can be further improved by additions of B and C  [15] , so we added 0.3 at.% C in order to ﬁnd out how the segregation of C to dendrites is affected by the addition of Nb and Zr.  

# 2. Experimental  

Two Ir alloys, with nominal compositions of Ir–10Nb–0.5Zr–0.3W–0.3C– 0.006Th (Ir–Nb, at.%) and Ir–4.5Zr–0.3W–0.3C–0.006Th (Ir–Zr, at.%), were arc melted and drop cast into Cu molds measuring 6.4 mm  ×  12.7 mm  ×  25.4 mm. The ingots were then heat treated at 1500   ◦ C in vacuum for 1 h for homogenization. Auger specimens (approximately 10 mm  ×  2 mm  ×  1 mm) were cut from the ingots. Small notches were made perpendicular to the longitudinal dimension of each specimen to control the location of fracture. Two specimens of different compositions were loaded at a time into specimen grips such that they could be broken independently by bending in an ultra-high vacuum (UHV) Fracture and Introduction System (FAIS), which was attached via an all-metal gate valve to a Physical Electronics Model 590 Scanning Auger Microprobe (SAM). The standard operation procedures and parameters for Auger Electron Spectroscopy (AES) analyses were described in detail elsewhere  [16] . Both the secondary dendrites and transgranular cleavage areas were analyzed. Approximately 10 dendrites and transgranular cleavage areas were analyzed on each fractured sample. X-ray diffraction (XRD) analyses were conducted on Ir–Nb, Ir–Zr and DOP-26 alloys using a Norelco Powder Diffractometer, and the XRD patterns were processed with the commercial software package Materials Data Jade 5.

# 4. Discussion  

After solidiﬁcation, the Ir–Nb alloy exhibits greater dendritic segregation of the trace elements (thorium and carbon) than the Ir–Zr alloy. Possible reasons for this are discussed below.  

The solubility of Nb in Ir is 16 at.% at 2400   ◦ C, much higher than that of Zr in Ir, which is about 5 at.% at 2120   ◦ C  [18] . The calculated heat of formation of Ir 3 Zr (79 kJ/mol) is much higher than that of Ir 3 Nb (52 kJ/mol)  [19] . According to the Ir–Nb phase diagram  [18] , the addition of 10 at.% Nb to Ir forms a solid solution and does not necessarily lead to the formation of an intermetallic phase, while the addition of Zr leads to the formation of an intermetallic phase, consistent with our X-ray diffraction results shown in  Fig. 2 .  

The formation of an Ir–Nb solid solution may inﬂuence the solubility of trace elements in theIr matrix.WhentheIr–Nballoy solidiﬁes, a solid solution of Ir–Nb with a high concentration of Nb forms ﬁrst, leaving behind the liquid phase enriched in trace elements near the solidiﬁcation front, and leading to strong dendritic segregation. However, in the Ir–Zr alloy, the dendritic segregation behavior is almost the same as that in the DOP-26 alloy, since Zr itself has a low solubility in Ir and is, therefore, unlikely to have much effect on the solubility of trace elements.

This strong dendritic segregation, especially of C, appears to be associated with the cracking observed in the Ir–Nb alloy. As has been reported by Krajnikov et al.  [22] , in as-quenched Mo- based alloy, carbon atoms can accumulate at grain boundaries and form non-equilibrium segregation structures, resulting in the spontaneous nucleation of brittle microcracks. A similar mech- anism may explain the role of C in causing the thermal cracking observed here. However, additional work is needed to clarify the underlying physical mechanism of embrittlement. Interestingly, this type of cracking has not been reported in other work on Ir–Nb alloys even at higher Nb contents  [14] . Possible reasons for this difference include differences in composition, sample size and processing. The carbon content in our Ir–Nb alloy is higher than that in other work, our sample sizes are larger, and the drop-casting used in this work induces a much higher cool- ing rate than conventional casting. These factors induce a greater degree of carbon segregation and a higher level of thermal stress, which might explain why cracking was observed here but not in an earlier study.  

# 5. Conclusions  

Macroalloying additions of Nb and Zr affect the dendritic segregation of the trace elements C and Th in Ir-based alloys. Niobium has a strong effect on dendritic segregation probably because it has high solubility in Ir, allowing it to decrease the solubility of the trace elements and inducing them to segregate. Zirconium has less of an effect on dendritic segregation probably because of its low solubility in Ir which causes it to precipitate out as an Ir 3 Zr intermetallic phase and interfere less with the solubility of the trace elements in iridium. Dendritic segregation appears to be linked to the cracking observed in the Ir–Nb alloy, but not in the Ir–Zr alloy, during casting and subsequent heat treatment; additional experiments are needed to understand the detailed mechanism of this embrittlement.
