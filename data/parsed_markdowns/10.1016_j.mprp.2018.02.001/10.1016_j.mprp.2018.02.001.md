Over the course of this paper, a FGH100L nickel based alloy, which was prepared by spray forming, had been treated by hot isostatic pressing, followed by an optimized heat treatment. The microstructure evolution of gamma prime phase under different conditions was systematically analyzed. In conclusion, both the temperature and cooling rate during the heat treatment can affect gamma prime phase on its morphology, size and distribution. By varying solution treatment temperature, size variation of  c 0   phase and  ﬁ ne-grain microstructure was obtained at a relatively low solution treatment temperature. As for changing cooling rate, a doublet morphology of splitting  c 0   phase appeared at a relatively slow cooling rate under hot isostatic pressing progress. Based on microstructural character- ization of  c 0   phase, the reasonable heat treatment regime of FGH100L alloy was determined. The deformation recrystallization occurred when the as-deposited alloy was treated by hot isostatic pressing, which results in grain morphology changing from spherical or sphere-like to polygon. It can be obtained multi-sized  c 0   precipitates for the hot isostatic pressing alloy which treated by optimized heat treatment regime. The results indicate that the multi-sized  c 0   phase microstructure is bene ﬁ cial to obtain ideal comprehensive mechanical properties.  

# 1. Introduction  

Superalloy turbine disk is  one of the key components of aircraft engine. In terms of manufacturing disk superalloy, there are two main methods, which have been adapted around the world, including powder metallurgy (PM) technology and Cast & Wrought technology  [1,2] . PM superalloys have several defects such as the powder-particle boundaries (PPB), thermally intro- duced porosity (TIP) and inclusions due to their unique processing [3,4] . Therefore, the quality of raw materials and processing parameters must be strictly controlled. However, these will lead to a higher manufacturing cost and more complex fabricating pro- cess  [5 – 7] . So, it is important to reduce the manufacturing cost of turbine disks out of economic considerations and there is a trend of producing turbine disks with high properties and low cost at the same time  [2,8] .  

In recent years, a Cast & Wrought processing route for prepa- ration of disk superalloy with less than 45% volume fraction of gamma prime phase has been reported  [9,10] . The reported method could effectively save manufacturing cost better in con- trast to the traditional PM route. However, it has to be noted that an exceeding gamma prime phase volume fraction (>45%) could lead to dif ﬁ culties in forming, especially in forging of billets. Therefore, it is necessary to further optimize the low-cost manu- facturing route of disk superalloy.  

Spray forming (SF) is an advanced technology of rapid- solidi ﬁ cation, which stems from metal atomization and powder metallurgy  [11,13] . It possesses great potential for lowering the costs and shortening the manufacturing process for the superalloy based turbine disk-blanks, by the means of the Cast & Wrought and the PM route  [14] .  

On the other hand, disk superalloy is required to have high strength, low notch sensitivity, high plasticity, good low cycle fatigue property and high resistance to propagation of cracks. These properties are highly related to the microstructure of alloy, especially the microstructure of the ordered  c 0   phase  [15] . Fur- thermore, the structure features of  c 0   phase of spray-formed superalloy are determined by hot processes such as hot isostatic pressing (HIP) and heat treatment (HT)  [16] .  

As a new Ni-Cr-Co based alloy, which is speci ﬁ cally used in turbine disk manufacturing, FGH100L is one of the high alloy extent third generation PM superalloys and its  c 0   phase volume fraction reaches 55%. In the aim of developing a simple and low cost process to manufacture disk superalloy, spray forming was used to prepare the deposited preform of FGH100L alloy. Currently, the in ﬂ uences of hot processes on microstructure of spray-formed FGH100L alloy, especially on the microstructure of  c 0   phase, are still not clear. Therefore, this paper was focused on the investigation of effects on microstructure evolution of alloy made by varying hot process conditions. The aim of this paper is to offer theory foreshadowing for FGH100L superalloy manufacturing via spray forming.

# 3. Results and discussion  

# 3.1. Microstructure of the as-deposited preform  

3.1.1. In ﬂ uence of solution treatment on microstructure  

As mentioned above, the applied heat treatment was for 1 h for each sample (not treated by HIP) at 1110   C, 1130   C, 1150   C, 1170   C and 1200   C, respectively. The samples were subse- quently subjected to a fan cooling to maintain the microstruc- ture formed at high temperatures.  Fig. 2  indicates different microstructures formed at different solution temperatures.  

Several sizes of  c 0   precipitations were attributable to the com- bination of different solution temperatures and cooling meth- ods. When the solution temperature was relatively low such as 1110   C and 1130   C, secondary and tertiary  c 0   phases were observed as shown in  Fig. 2 b and d. The precipitations of sec- ondary and tertiary  c 0   phases provided necessary precondition for the following formation of  c 0   particles with different dimen- sions during aging treatment. The principle behind this phe- nomenon is that the solute atoms can not suf ﬁ ciently dissolve in matrix when the employed solution temperature was 1110  C or 1130   C. Thus the enrichment region of tiny solid solution atoms such as Al and Ti was still there in matrix, which results in c 0   phase precipitating during cooling step, while, there was little c 0   precipitation found on grain boundaries or intra crystalline matrix when the samples were applied solution treatment at 1150   C. In addition, using a higher solution temperature such as 1170   C and 1200   C, a single austenitic microstructure formed ( Fig. 2  g – j). The reason is that the solute atoms can fully diffuse and dissolve into matrix at high solution temperatures and any phase precipitations is restrained at high cooling rates. Therefore, a single austenitic microstructure was generated under this condition.

According to  Fig. 2 , it is obvious that the dissolution capabil- ity of  c 0   phase changes with the variation of solution tempera- ture. Below 1150   C (i.e. 1110   C or 1130   C), the primary  c 0 phase did not entirely dissolve in matrix but remained on grain boundaries ( Fig. 2 a, c). The pinning effect of the remaining pri- mary  c 0   precipitates can inhibit migration of grain boundaries and growth of grains, which is conducive to the formation of  ﬁ ne grains ( Fig. 3 a). When samples were treated at 1150   C, both the primary  c 0   precipitates within grains and the ones on the grain boundaries almost dissolved into matrix, thus the  c 0   solvus tem- perature ( c 0 s ) of alloy was proved to be about 1150   C. Above 1150   C (i.e.1170   C or 1200   C), austenitic microstructure was obtained. However, since both 1170   C and 1200   C are higher than  c 0 s , primary  c 0   can entirely dissolve, which means the pin- ning effect is eliminated in this case. Results from this super- solvus temperature treatment, grain coarsening occurred during solution treatment and coarse-grain structure emerged.  Fig. 3 shows grain structures generated under different solution tem- peratures. Compared to the grains obtained at 1110   C ( Fig. 3 a), coarse grains formed at 1170   C ( Fig. 3 b).  

On the basis of previous work  [17,18] , nickel based alloy has two typical features, known as  ﬁ ne-grain structure and multi- sized  c 0   phase, which results in high strength and good low cycle fatigue resistance. Therefore, in order to fabricate an overall per- formance of superalloy with high strength property, FGH100L should be applied a sub-solvus temperature heat treatment, which means the solution treatment temperature is lower than the  c 0   solvus temperature (1150   C).

The deposited billet was processed by HIP. The heat treatment regime of HIPed alloy is as follows: 1130   C/1 h, fan quenching +850   C/4 h, air cooling +770   C/8 h, air cooling.  

# 3.2.1. The evolution of grain structure  

The typical microstructures of alloy treated by HIP and HIP + HT are illustrated in  Fig. 6 . For comparison, the microstructure of the original spray deposited alloy is shown in  Fig. 6 , too.  

First of all, after HIP treatment, there was a signi ﬁ cant reduc- tion in the amount of micro porosity within deposited preform, which is illustrated by  Fig. 6 b. In other words, in the process of HIP, the density of deposited preform increased. During the spray forming process, different sizes of equiaxed grains were generated since the diameter of atomized droplets was not uni- form.  Fig. 6 a shows that the average size of small grains was in a range from 5  l m to 10  l m, while the average size of large grains was around 35  l m. Moreover,  Fig. 6 . also indicates that the main morphology of as-deposited grains was spherical or sphere-like. Before the alloy had been treated by HIP process, the metallurgi- cal combination among grains was not fully dense. By operating HIP on alloy, three-dimensional compressive stress can transfer equably within deposited preform alloy, thus the grains were rearranged, micro pores were eliminated and the densi ﬁ cation was improved ( Fig. 6 b).  

Secondly, recrystallization occurred during HIP process, which results in grain morphology changing from spherical or sphere-like to polygon. Meanwhile, different sizes of original- deposited grains suffered different contact stress during HIP pro- cess. Compared to large grains, small grains have small contact area but large contact stress  [21] . Therefore, different sizes of grains had different extent of deformation. Moreover, the larger the grain, the greater the deformation amount was required for deformation recrystallization at a same temperature. When the HIP process was operated at 1140   C, small grains reached the critical deformation  ﬁ rst and recrystallized since they had small contact area but large contact stress. While large grains had small degree deformation and only partial grains reached their critical deformation, thus incomplete recrystallization occurred in alloy with large grain structure. Furthermore, it is the deformation recrystallization that causes the grain morphology changing from spherical or sphere-like to polygon. As shown in  Fig. 6 b,

the homogeneous recrystallized grain structure was obtained by growth of completely recrystallized and merged small grains, which indicates that the HIP process homogenizes the size distri- bution of recrystallized grains. According to the grain size calcu- lation, the average grain size increases from 24.5  l m to 26.2  l m when the deposited preform is treated by HIP process. Because the total volume is constant, the growth of grains is at cost of the amount, which results in a decreasing of total grain boundary area. Thirdly, by employing heat treatment, grains slightly grew up and the spheroidization was improved as shown in  Fig. 6 c. 3.2.2. The microstructure features of  c 0   phase obtained under hot process

 3.2.2.1. The splitting behavior of c 0   particles under HIP process.  Fig. 7 shows the morphology development of  c 0   particles in the spray deposited preform. When the HIP process was employed at 1140   C under 140 MPa for 3 h, the cuboid  c 0   particles within preform dissolved in matrix but there were still some undissolved c 0   particles remaining on grain boundaries ( Fig. 7 a). During the furnace cooling stage of HIP treatment,  c 0   particles precipitated off. Since 25   C/min was a relatively slow cooling rate, alloy ele- ments had enough time to diffuse when the alloy was cooled down. The secondary  c 0   precipitates began to split under this condition and the splitting parts are highlighted with red circles in  Fig. 7 b and c. Because spherical tertiary  c 0   particles precipitated among the secondary  c 0   precipitates, the precipitation and growth of tertiary  c 0   particles result in the formation of irregular shaped split secondary  c 0   particles. As pointed by red arrows, there are spindal-shaped, bell-shaped (7b) and bow-shaped (7c) secondary particles.  

The previous research literature indicates that the elastic strain  ﬁ eld is greatest at the edge center of the {100} facet in cuboidal  c 0   particle  [22,23] . Nevertheless, the chemical driving force for the growth of cuboidal  c 0   particles is greatest along h 110 i  direction, which means the precipitation along  h 110 i direction is faster than other orientations. Results from this, the {100} crystal plan of cuboidal  c 0   particle turned to a surface with negative curvature radius and contribute a concave facet formed at the edge center of cuboid along  h 110 i  crystal direc- tion. There is an elastic stress concentration formed around the concave part, which results in the dissolution of  c 0   particles in this region being promoted. With the increasing of curvature radius, the concave part grows up, thus  c 0   particles continue to split. Doublet or octet (faces of eight cuboids are parallel to the 100 cubes) cuboids are split from an isolated  c 0   particle  [23] .  

Generally speaking, the growth of unstable  c 0   particles in nickel based superalloy has a splitting behavior when the alloy  

![](images/462fd7f06f625025204314f0c2a47d7fb318590d509563daf210f105d29b81f1.jpg)  
The SEM micrographs showing morphology of  c 0   particles after HIP treatment.  

is slowly cooled down. One cuboid particle will split into two or eight smaller  c 0   particles. The splitting from an isolated particle into two particles can be explained by the following two assump- tions, (1). The process of splitting to two particles lowers the energy and leads the particles to reach a stable state, the splitting process is completed. (2). This splitting is the intermediate step from the isolated particle to octets, doublet will split to octets when the transformation environment meets the requirements for further splitting  [24] .  

In combination with diffusion kinetic theory, the splitting of c 0   particles is explained by an interface diffusion controlling pro- cess. During the slow cooling stage of HIP process, there is substi- tutional atom diffusion of  c 0   formation elements such as Al and Ti. The diffusion speed is highly determined by the vacancy con- centration  [25] . Since the vacancy formation energy of liquid alloy is higher than solid alloy, the vacancy concentration of liq- uid alloy is higher than that of solid one. In the following solid- i ﬁ cation process, the quantity of vacancy decreases dramatically, which results from the fast diffusion of interfacial atoms. Under the experimental condition, the diffusion speed is much faster than grain growth speed. Therefore, the vacancies are eliminated during the normal solidi ﬁ cation process. However, the alloy melt undergoes rapid cooling and solidi ﬁ cation during atomized pro- cess of spray forming. When the alloy melt with highly concen- trated vacancy meets nitrogen, the process is similar to rapid quenching, in this case, the supersaturated vacancies cannot escape from melt. In other words, since the liquid – solid interface migration speed is faster than the escape speed of voids, lots of the vacancies were captured and the high concentration of vacancies was formed  [25] . Furthermore, compared to substitu- tional atoms of Al and Ti elements, interstitial atoms such as C and N diffuse fast during furnace cooling in HIP process and they are hardly affected by vacancy concentration. The cooling rate (25   C/min) is slow, substitutional atoms easily switch positions with vacancies, which prompts  c 0   interface to migrate and results in the splitting of  c 0   particles. Therefore, the diffusion and inter- face controlling are the mechanism of  c 0   particle splitting con- trolling in FGH100L alloy.  

In view of energy theory, the fundamental cause of  c 0   particle splitting is the competition among elastic strain energy caused by  c / c 0   lattice mismatch,  c / c 0   interface energy and elastic interac- tion energy of the  c 0   particles  [26 – 28] . The splitting of  c 0   particles occurs when they grow to reach the critical dimension during slow cooling in HIP process. The critical dimension was calcu- lated to be 525 nm by Image J software. At the end, an energy balance is reached by the reduction of elastic interaction energy and elastic strain energy offset to the increase in interface energy during splitting, which means a stable state is obtained  [27] . After the splitting of  c 0   particles, the system ’ s total energy was lowered. As shown in  Fig. 7 b and c, the morphology is a combi- nation of normal cuboid  c 0   particles and splitting  c 0   particles in different shapes.  

The preferential morphology of splitting  c 0   particles is affected by elastic strain energy, interface energy and elastic interaction energy. The optimized shape of splitting  c 0   particles is a stable state when the sum of the three is the lowest  [28] . According to this investigation, the majority shape of splitting  c 0   particles is doublet, the doublet did not further split into octets, indicating that the process of splitting doublet reduces the energy of the system to a steady state.  

Overall, the splitting behavior of  c 0   particles has in ﬂ uences on the morphology, quantity, distribution and dimension of  c 0 phase, which can further affect the precipitation of  c 0   phase dur- ing the following heat treatment.

c 0   particles continued to precipitate during further aging treat- ment.  Fig. 9  is an SEM micrograph that indicates the morphology of  c 0   phase after two-step aging. It can be seen that primary  c 0   par- ticles with dimension from 0.8  l m to 1.2  l m were obtained after heat treatment and they had chain-like distribution around grain boundaries ( Fig. 9 a). The precipitation of primary  c 0   particles on grain boundaries can be explained by the following mechanism. The precipitation of primary c 0   particles occurred in the cooling stage of solution treatment. A large amount of secondary  c 0   particles and dispersively dis- tributed tertiary  c 0   particles precipitated inside the grains. The particle dimension of secondary and tertiary  c 0   phases is 0.3  l m – 0.5  l m and 30 nm, respectively. As illustrated in  Fig. 9 b, the morphology of primary and secondary  c 0   particles is mainly cuboid, while, tertiary  c 0   particles are spherulites. Meanwhile, small amounts of triangular shaped primary and secondary  c 0 particles were also observed and they remained from the solution treatment. Furthermore, the white substance shown in  Fig. 9  was a small quantity of M 23 C 6  carbides that precipitated during aging process.  

The nucleation of secondary  c 0   particles occurred in the initial stage of cooling period during solution treatment and it contin- ued during aging treatment at 850   C. Since the applied aging temperature was high, the saturation degree of matrix was low. Under this condition, compared to the precipitation of tertiary c 0   phase, secondary precipitates were larger in size but smaller in number.  

Nevertheless, the nucleation of tertiary  c 0   particles occurred in the last stage of cooling period during solution treatment. When the alloy was aged at 775   C, a large quantity of tertiary  c 0   parti- cles precipitated and it results from the supersaturated solution underwent a temperature decreasing. In addition, spherical ter- tiary  c 0   and  c  matrix have coherency relationship, which can restrain the growth speed of  c 0   particles under the range of serv- ing temperature. Therefore, FGH100L has high structure stability under high temperature conditions.

The mechanical properties of alloy are highly related to  c 0 microstructure characteristics  [29] . The precipitation of primary c 0   phase formed in the process of heat treatment made up for the insuf ﬁ cient number of carbide such as M 23 C 6  on grain boundaries. The existence of both primary  c 0   phase and carbide formed an ideal strengthening microstructure together. More- over, as illustrated by  Fig. 9 a, the area with chain like distributed c 0   phase and no  c 0   phase zones had an interval distribution, which is bene ﬁ cial to improving the plasticity of the zones around grain boundaries. It also helps to relax the stress concen- tration induced by grain sliding [17] . Furthermore, this microstructure can contribute to preventing or delaying the extension of cracks, thus improve the mechanical properties of alloy. After the alloy had been operated by two-step aging treat- ment, there were dispersively distributed medium sized sec- ondary  c 0   particles and a large amount of spherical tertiary  c 0 particles formed within grains ( Fig. 9 b). The microstructure fea- tures mentioned above are bene ﬁ cial to the promotion of strength and plasticity, thus ensuring the alloy will have good comprehensive properties  [17,20] .  

# 4. Conclusion  

(1) According to the heat treatment investigation, the structure of alloy FGH100L is sensitive to the variation of solution temperature. When the solution temperature  

was above 1150   C, only a single austenite microstructure was observed, while, when the solution treatment was applied below 1150   C, the microstructure was a combina- tion of different sized c 0   particles and  ﬁ ne grains. The sol- vus temperature of alloy FGH100L is approximately 1150  C. Using  ‘ 1130   C/1 h, fan quenching +850   C/4 h, air cooling +770   C/8 h, air cooling ’  as the heat treatment sys- tem, a microstructure, which has a homogeneously dis- tributed c 0 phase with different sizes and various morphologies, was obtained.  

(2) During HIP at 1140   C, dynamic recrystallization occurred in spray deposited billet, grain morphology changed from nearly spherical shape to polygon. Meanwhile, equiaxed grains became more equally distributed. Further heat treat- ment didn ’ t contribute a lot to the change of grain size.  

(3) In the process of HIP ’ s slow-cool stage, there was a splitting of c 0   particles. Various shapes of c 0   particles appeared after c 0   phase splitting and there was a preferential formation of doublet c 0   phase.  

(4) After HIP + HT progress, there were three sizes of c 0   phase. Chain-like primary c 0   phase formed on grain boundaries, majority cubic secondary c 0   phase and spherical and dis- persed tertiary c 0   phase precipitated in grains. This forma- tion and distribution of c 0   phase contributes to the excellent mechanical properties of FGH100L.
