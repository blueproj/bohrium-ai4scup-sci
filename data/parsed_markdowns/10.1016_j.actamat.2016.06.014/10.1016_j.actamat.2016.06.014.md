The present study involves a detailed experimental investigation of the concurrent compositional clustering and long-range ordering tendencies in a Ni-33 at.%Cr alloy, carried out by coupling synchrotron-based X-ray diffraction (XRD), transmission electron microscopy (TEM), and atom probe tomography (APT). Synchrotron-based XRD results clearly exhibited progressively increasing lattice contraction in the matrix with increasing isothermal aging time, at 475    C, eventually leading to the development of long-range ordering (LRO) of the Pt 2 Mo-type. Detailed TEM and APT investigations revealed that this LRO in the matrix is manifested in the form of nanometer-scale ordered domains, and the spatial distribution, size, morphology and compositional evolution of these domains have been carefully investigated. APT results also revealed the early stages of compositional clustering prior to the onset of long-range ordering in this alloy and such compositional clustering can potentially be correlated to the lattice contraction and previously proposed short-range ordering tendencies.

# 1. Introduction  

Engineering alloys based on Ni-Cr-Fe have been utilized effec- tively throughout the entire modern history of materials develop- ment. While the Fe-rich alloys have been used to develop a variety of stainless steels, the Ni-rich alloys have been advanced to manufacture superalloys. Combinations of higher corrosion resis- tance, superior creep resistance, and better tensile properties at high temperature have made Ni-rich alloys most appropriate for industrial applications involving elevated temperatures.  

Ni-15Cr-8Fe (all in at.%) such as INCONEL 600 (or alloy 600) has found extensive use in both the chemical industry and in nuclear reactors, for example, control rod drive mechanism (CRDM) noz- zles, steam generator (SG) tubing, SG divider plates etc. that carry superheated steams in nuclear reactors  [1] . Alloy 690 (INCONEL 690) with the nominal Ni-29Cr-9Fe composition has been selected to replace alloy 600 due to its increased stress corrosion cracking resistance under the operating conditions common in light water  

The ordered Ni 2 Cr or  g 0   phase, which has a Pt 2 Mo-type crystal structure with Immm symmetry, has been reported to form in Ni-Cr alloys upon aging in the temperature range of 300 e 590    C for thousands of hours depending on the alloy composition  [2,4,7,8] . In a commercial Alloy 690, Delabrouille et al.  [9]  reported long- range ordering after aging for 70,000 h at 420    C. They reported dark- ﬁ eld TEM images that showed the formation of ordered Ni 2 Cr domains in the alloy after this very long term annealing treatment. While there are only a few reports of  g 0   precipitation in Alloy 690, the nucleation and growth of this phase has not been investigated in great detail. It is obvious that one of the major drawbacks is the time required for ordering to occur. The binary stoichiometric Ni- 33Cr alloy, on the other hand, has been reported to undergo an ordering reaction within the  ﬁ rst few hundred hours of aging in the temperature range indicated  [8,10] . It is also known that, in the case of off-stoichiometric Ni-Cr alloys, the ordering kinetics are sub- stantially slower than those of a stoichiometric alloy  [10,11] . The binary alloy with a ratio of two Ni atoms for one Cr atom, thus presents a more suitable option to study the early stages of this phase, and its subsequent evolution during aging.  

This study focuses on the ordering pathway involved in the development of LRO from a random solid solution in a Ni-33Cr alloy. Previous studies on ordering in Ni-Cr binary alloys report the formation of both short-range ordering (SRO) as well as LRO in these alloys  [6,8,10,12] . These studies focused on both Ni-25Cr (3Ni:1Cr) as well as Ni-33Cr (2Ni:1Cr) alloy compositions  [12] . The SRO in these alloys has been described based on a dispersed order model  [13]  where highly ordered precipitates, on the order of a few nanometers, are uniformly dispersed within a disordered matrix. The experimental evidence presented in support of this SRO model was largely indirect in nature, i.e., presence of diffuse scattering and streaking of fundamental re ﬂ ections in electron diffraction pat- terns, mottled contrast in bright- ﬁ eld TEM images, increases in resistivity and hardness, negative creep and lattice contraction. Marucco et al.  [13]  reported the formation of dispersed ordered zones, of the order of a few nanometers in dimension within a few hours of aging at temperatures below 500    C. Taylor et al.  [11] studied the formation of localized order in the Ni 3 Cr (or Ni-25Cr) alloy using resistivity and speci ﬁ c heat measurements during continuous slow heating and cooling experiments, and noted that LRO formation was sluggish in the same alloy  [5] . It has been pro- posed that the coarsening of dispersed SRO nuclei does not easily take place due to their coherency in the matrix  [5] . While these observations, which were captured by monitoring changes in cumulative physical properties such as resistivity, could be a result of the early stages of SRO in these alloys, there appears to be no de ﬁ nitive experimental evidence in support of the dispersed order model of SRO. In fact, Marucco  [10]  concluded that the TEM ob- servations of SRO in these alloys require very careful interpretation. The experimental evidence for LRO, on long-term annealing of the same alloys, is much more conclusive. For example, the previously reported results clearly show the presence of superlattice re- ﬂ ections (e.g. at 1/3 and 2/3{022} positions) that are attributable to LRO  [6,12] . Additionally, dark- ﬁ eld TEM images recorded using these superlattice re ﬂ ections also revealed the presence of coherent ordered domains  [10]  simultaneous with x-ray diffraction results that indicated substantial lattice contraction  [13] . Typically, LRO initiates after 100 to 1000 h annealing at \~500    C in a Ni-33Cr alloy and is accompanied by sharp superlattice re ﬂ ections in elec- tron diffraction patterns and clearly de ﬁ ned domains in corre- sponding dark  ﬁ eld images.  

Past studies on ordering in these Ni-Cr alloys have largely employed X-ray diffraction (XRD), resistivity measurements, dif- ferential scanning calorimetry (DSC) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and were focused primarily on the development of LRO  [14,15] . However, rather limited information is available regarding the early stages of ordering in Ni-Cr alloys, especially with regards to the proposed dispersed order model, as well as the pathway leading from the disordered fcc solid solution to the development of ordered domains. Furthermore, the size and composition evolution of LRO ordered domains has not been investigated in detail. In the present paper, a systematic investi- gation was conducted of the formation and evolution of ordered domains with aging in a Ni-33Cr binary alloy, speci ﬁ cally focusing on the following aspects:  

1. Synchrotron XRD experiments to identify the onset of long- range ordering (LRO) and evaluate the changes in lattice con- stant in the alloy with annealing. In addition, in-situ synchro- tron heating and cooling experiments were used to capture the order-disorder transition in this system.

 2. TEM to study the size, morphology and distribution of the LRO ordered domains after isothermal annealing.

 3. Site-speci ﬁ c sample preparation along speci ﬁ c crystallographic directions, for atom probe tomography (APT) investigations, to directly visualize the size and morphology of the nanometer- scale LRO domains within the matrix and to determine the compositions of the LRO domains.

 4. APT investigations of the early stages of compositional clus- tering prior to the onset of LRO.  

# 2. Experimental procedure  

The binary Ni-33Cr alloy used in this investigation, was pre- pared by the Korean Atomic Energy Research Institute (KAERI). A Ni-33Cr plate was made by vacuum induction melting followed by hot rolling at 1200    C, solution annealing at 1050    C for 1 h and water quenching (WQ) This solution-annealed alloy was subjected to 3 different aging treatments at 475    C (see  Table 1 ).

Samples for synchrotron studies were prepared for the four conditions given in Table 1 , with dimensions of 5 mm    5 mm    0.5 mm (thickness). The  ﬁ rst set of experiments was performed on the 11-IDC beam-line (111 keV,  l  ¼  0.11165  Å ) of the Advanced Photon Source (APS) at Argonne National laboratory (ANL). A CeO 2  standard (NIST diffraction intensity standard set: 674a) was used to calibrate the distance between sample and the 2D detector, the detector tilting angle, and position of the beam center. The scattered radiation was collected on a Perkin Elmer 2D detector system. The 2D diffraction data were integrated and con- verted to an intensity vs. two-theta (2 q ) plot using the program Fit- 2D  [16] . Lattice constant values were obtained from the intensity vs. 2 q  curves by Rietveld re ﬁ nement. The Reitveld analysis was done using GSAS software with exp-gui interface by peak  ﬁ tting with pure gamma-nickel fcc as the starting point. The  ﬁ rst 5 peaks i.e. fcc e  111, 200, 220, 311, 222 were used for the re ﬁ nement and deter- mination of the lattice parameters  [17] . Microhardness was per- formed using a Shimadzu Dynamic Ultra-micro Hardness Tester (ModelDUH-211) with a load of 1.96 N and a holding time of 10 s. TEM lift-out samples for the 90 h, 240 h, and 8000 h aged alloys were prepared using an FEI Nova 200 dual beam focused ion beam (FIB). TEM analysis was conducted using an FEI Tecnai F20 operated at 200 kV. Samples from the WQ, 90 h, 240 h and 8000 h aged alloys were also prepared for APT using the same FIB/SEM after identi- fying desired grain orientations using electron backscattered diffraction (EBSD) analysis. Standard lift-out techniques were used for APT sample preparation in the FIB before mounting the small sections of the samples on suitable holders for analysis. APT ex- periments were conducted on a CAMECA local electrode atom probe 3000X HR instrument. All experiments were performed in the temperature range of 20 K e 40 K with target evaporation of 0.5% and pulse fraction of 20% of a steady-state applied DC voltage. APT data reconstruction and analysis were carried out using CAMECA IVAS ®   3.6.8 software.

XRD results from the WQ, 90 h, 240 h, and 8000 h aged samples are shown in  Fig. 2  where the normalized intensity of all four samples have been plotted against 2 q . The WQ and 90 h aged alloys only show the fundamental fcc peaks and these have been marked in  Fig. 2 (a) and (b). However, in contrast, the longer-term aged samples show extra superlattice peaks in addition to the fundamental fcc peaks. These additional superlattice peaks can be attributed to the BCO structure and have been marked in  Fig. 2 (c) for the 240 h aged sample and in  Fig. 2  (d) for the 8000 h aged sample. These superlattice peaks with d-spacings of 3.76 Å, 3.24 Å, and 2.38 Å were indexed as the (020), (011), and (110) peaks respectively of the BCO structure, indicating Pt 2 Mo-type ordering in these two samples. Based on these superlattice peaks, the lattice parameters of this ordered BCO structure were calculated to be a  ¼  7.52 Å, b  ¼  2.50  Å  and c  ¼  3.55 Å, and these were consistent with the previously reported values by Chan et al.  [18]  from  ﬁ rst principle calculations.  

The experimentally measured lattice constant from the major fcc diffraction peaks in this case correspond to an average effect of the ordered domains and the surrounding disordered matrix. The average lattice constant in Ni-Cr alloys has been studied in great detail by Marucco et al. using XRD  [19]  who reported that it decreased due to the formation of ordered domains. In the present study, the matrix lattice constant for all the samples were accu- rately determined using Rietveld re ﬁ nement of the synchrotron XRD patterns shown in  Fig. 2  and are plotted against aging time in Fig. 3 . This plot reveals that the fcc lattice contracted signi ﬁ cantly during isothermal aging at 475    C. The largest lattice constant was observed in the case of the WQ alloy and it progressively decreased upon aging to 8000 h. The amount of lattice contraction was rela- tively low between the WQ and 90 h samples and was highest between the 90 h and 240 h aged samples. Therefore, the greatest lattice contraction can be attributed to the development of LRO in this alloy which occurs between the aging periods of 90 and 240 h, based on the appearance of distinct superlattice diffraction peaks in the XRD patterns ( Fig. 2 ). Referring to  Fig. 3 , it should be noted that there is a small degree of lattice contraction in the 90 h aged alloy as compared to the WQ alloy. This contraction indicates that there is some atomic rearrangement taking place in the random fcc solid solution at these early stages of annealing, that is not large enough to be comparable to the development of fully ordered domains. This early stage lattice contraction has often been attributed to short- range ordering (SRO) in the literature  [12] .  

To gain further insight into the order-disorder transformation, an in-situ heating experiment was conducted in the synchrotron. A sample from the 8000 h aged alloy, which clearly showed extra superlattice peaks corresponding to the Ni 2 Cr (or Pt 2 Mo type) ordering as shown in  Fig. 2 (d), was heated up to 800    C, held for 5 min and then cooled to ambient temperature. This experiment resulted in disordering of the ordered alloy and this disordered random solid solution is retained on cooling to ambient tempera- ture. The rate of heating and cooling was 0.5    C/s. X-ray data were collected during this whole experiment at 18 s or 9    C intervals. Lattice constants were determined from selected data points along the heating and cooling cycles, and these have been plotted against temperature in  Fig. 4 (a). At the early stages of the heating cycle, the lattice was expanding at a linear rate, due to coef ﬁ cient of thermal expansion. But above 620    C, the rate of expansion increased sub- stantially until nearly 750    C. The change in the slope or the change in the rate of expansion might be attributed to the order-disorder transition in this alloy. This is further evidenced by the fact that during the cooling cycle, the slope remained the same as the heating cycle and the re-ordering did not occur resulting in retention of the disordered solid solution at ambient temperature. XRD patterns, corresponding to three (3) regions, marked by circles are shown in  Fig. 4  (b), (c), and (d) and establish the fact that the initial alloy containing ordered domains completely disordered. A signi ﬁ cant difference in lattice parameter between the initial partially-ordered alloy and the subsequent disordered alloy is observed and clearly demonstrates that lattice contraction takes place due to ordering phenomena or conversely, disordering results in lattice expansion. The order-disorder transition temperature for this alloy is observed to be 620  ±  5    C by the synchrotron experi- ment which was previously estimated to be 590    C by DSC exper- iments  [8] .

The application of this technique of a priori identi ﬁ cation of the grain orientation using EBSD and site-speci ﬁ c APT sample prepa- ration is shown in  Fig. 8 . Greater details of this technique can be found in Ref.  [24] . As described in  Fig. 1 , the {100} of the ortho- rhombic structure of the Ni 2 Cr phase is parallel to the {110} axis of the fcc matrix. So a {110} oriented grain was chosen for the prep- aration of the lift-out specimen for APT analysis.  Fig. 8 (a) shows an SEM image during the major side milling of a {110} grain. After preparing the needle-shaped APT specimen from the {110} oriented grain from the 240 h annealed alloy, it was  ﬁ eld- evaporated under high vacuum. The pole  ﬁ gure of the {110} ori- ented grain is shown in  Fig. 9 (a), and the corresponding desorption map from the APT experiment is shown in  Fig. 9 (b). The desorption map is essentially a two-dimensional stacked view of the detector and only represents a limited region of surface of the needle. The map depends on the crystallographic direction during evaporation, and it is well known that poles, zone lines, etc. in ﬂ uence the map by introducing artifacts that appear as low-density hits or detection regions. Several poles could be observed in  Fig. 9 (b) that appear as darker and circular regions, and the strong pole near the center corresponds to the {110} fcc pole. The desorption map can also be regarded as a section of the pole  ﬁ gure shown in  Fig. 9 (a), but containing only a limited angular range from the center of the pole ﬁ gure. A 5 nm slice of the APT reconstruction is shown in  Fig. 9 (c) where the {110} fcc pole appears as a low-density region throughout the reconstruction volume. Manual rotation, tilting, and sampling sections of the reconstruction distinctly identi ﬁ ed atomic planes of the ordered cells. A magni ﬁ ed region embedding an ordered cell from the same reconstruction is shown in  Fig. 9 (d). They were distinctly different from the disordered fcc matrix because the disordered matrix will only reveal planes near the pole region whereas the ordered regions can maintain the planarity of atoms away from a pole. One possible reason is that the chemical bonding of atoms in the ordered regions is different from that of the disordered matrix, and thus the atoms  ﬁ eld evaporate at different rates and also appear differently in the APT reconstruction.  

It should be noted that the  ﬁ eld evaporation of atoms in APT is a sequential process and consequently when the evaporation axis is aligned near a crystallographic direction, the result is sequential evaporation of atomic planes. Since the resolution of these atomic planes is rather dif ﬁ cult to ascertain in the raw atomic view of the APT reconstruction, as shown in  Fig. 9 (d), the use of spatial distri- bution maps (SDM) is a useful tool to visualize these atomic planes [25] . An SDM is a 2D map that shows the distance of atoms relative to a central atom. Thus, the planarity of the atomic planes are much easier to visualize in the Ni-to-Ni SDM shown in  Fig. 9 (e), corre- sponding to the ordered region within the matrix where the Ni lattice planes appear as alternating yellow planes (many atoms present) with blue regions in between where few atoms are pre- sent. The interplanar spacing was measured to be 0.37 nm, and the angle between the fcc {110} pole and the planes of the domain is measured to be around 55  . Given the fact that the angles between fcc {110} family of planes is either 60    or 90  , it is highly probable that the ordered planes that are observed are the {200} planes of the Ni 2 Cr structure, lying parallel to a set of {110} fcc planes, whose plane normal is at an angle of 60    to the axis of the atom probe sample. Since the a set of {100} directions in the Ni 2 Cr structure consist of an alternating sequence of pure Ni and pure Cr planes, as shown in schematic in  Fig 9  (e) aligning this direction closer to the evaporation axis in atom probe can result in planar resolution. The bond energy of Cr-Cr bond is 152 kJ/mol and Ni-Ni is 204 kJ/mol [26] , therefore it is easier to evaporate Cr-Cr planes thus the merging of 3 planes (2 Ni-Ni and 1 Cr-Cr) in atom probe recon- struction is observed. The neighboring Ni-Ni planes are 0.125 nm apart which are possibly evaporating together. As the voltage ramps up to evaporate a Ni plane the adjacent Cr plane is evapo- rated preferentially along with this plane. As a result these 3 planes, which are 0.125 nm apart each, appear as a single plane in the reconstruction. As a consequence, the adjacent planes in the reconstruction appear to be separated by a spacing of 0.37 nm which is about half of the spacing between [100] planes as shown in the  ﬁ gure. It is worth mentioning here that the SDM of Cr ions did not reveal any clear signature of planarity possibly due to  ﬁ eld evaporation with the Ni ions. The presence of these distinct lattice planes permitted the identi ﬁ cation of LRO domains within the disordered matrix. Multiple such LRO domains were identi ﬁ ed based on the presence of lattice planes within the ordered regions in another APT reconstruction of the 240 h aged alloy, shown in Fig. 10 (a). The Ni-to-Ni SDM analysis was also applied to one of these domains ( Fig. 10 (b)). A normalized intensity plot along the white dashed line is shown in  Fig. 10 (c) to show the periodicity of the lattice planes.

This probably contributes to the lattice contraction as shown in  Fig. 3 . Bernard et al. # 3.5. Analysis of early stages of cluster formation using atom probe tomography  

In addition to the analysis of the well-developed LRO domains in the 240 and 8000 h sample, compositional clustering was investi- gated in the APT datasets for all the four samples, WQ, 90, 240, and 8000 h. The primary motivation for such an analysis was to determine any compositional changes within the disordered fcc matrix of the Ni-33Cr alloy, prior to the onset of LRO in this alloy. The data-mining technique of cluster  ﬁ nding was employed, the details of this technique can be found in the literature  [29] , and the step-by-step procedure is described in the IVAS ™ 3.6.8 manual. Cluster analysis, in essence, identi ﬁ es for a chosen atomic species, where the distance (d max ) between two atoms is smaller compared to the rest of the dataset. The values of d max  and N min  (minimum number of atoms in a cluster) were optimized to maximize the probability to detect any compositional clustering. With further data treatment the three-dimensional region with interatomic distances equal to or smaller than optimized valued of dmax and Nmin (that yielded the biggest count of non-random clusters) were extracted from the data set. # 3.6. Suggested ordering pathway in Ni-33Cr  

Since the average composition of the alloy is Ni-33 at.%Cr, starting from a disordered fcc solid solution, the simplest ordering pathway would be congruent ordering of the disordered solid so- lution to form the ordered Ni 2 Cr structure. However, the experi- mental results presented in this paper clearly indicate that the ordering pathway adopted by this alloy during isothermal anneal- ing at 475    C is not simple congruent ordering. Thus, coupling the experimental results presented in this paper; synchrotron x-ray diffraction results, TEM, and APT, an unexpected and novel ordering pathway can be proposed for the disorder-order transformation in the Ni-33Cr alloy. Based on both the synchrotron x-ray diffraction as well as the electron diffraction results, it can be concluded that Pt 2 Mo-type LRO in this alloy can be observed only after 240 h aging at 475    C while the WQ and 90 h samples do not exhibit any LRO. However, detailed analysis of the APT data revealed that, while the WQ sample exhibited a nominally random distribution of both Ni and Cr atoms, the 90 h aged sample clearly exhibited a tendency for clustering of Ni atoms and consequently the formation of discrete Ni-rich clusters within the fcc matrix of the Ni-33Cr alloy. These clusters have been reconstructed in  Fig. 15 (a), while the quantita- tive analysis of these Ni-rich clusters is shown in  Table 2 . While the average diameter of these clusters is approximately 1.1 nm and their average composition is Ni-15Cr (at.%) in case of the 90 h aged sample, the spread in their compositions is quite large, ranging from Ni-10Cr to Ni-25Cr. The presence of similar Ni-rich clusters was observed in APT investigations of Alloy 690 after isothermal annealing at temperatures less than 500    C, the results of which are yet to be published. Such a tendency for Ni-clustering has also been reported previously in Ni-33Cr and Ni-Cr-Mo alloys by Ref.  [30] , based on atom probe results. It is tempting to associate the for- mation of these Ni-rich clusters to the ubiquitous short-range ordering (SRO) claimed in these alloys at the early stages of isothermal annealing at temperatures less than 500    C. Since the existence of this SRO has largely been based upon its effect on lattice parameter, resistivity, and other physical properties, the exact structural and crystallographic details of the SRO structure are unknown. Furthermore, the diffraction data previously pre- sented in support of this SRO  [22,31]  clearly distinguishes this type   1 of SRO from the  〈 1 ½ 0 〉 or / 3 < 420 >  type ordering found in case of Ni-Mo  [31]  and Ni-Mo-Cr  [22]  alloys. Therefore, it can be conjec- tured that the formation of Ni-rich clusters at the early stages of isothermal annealing at 475    C could be related to the SRO that has been postulated to exist in these alloys.

# 4. Summary  

Ordering in the Ni-Cr-Fe alloys still remains a stimulating problem. Ni-33Cr alloy is not only an interesting alloy in terms of practical usage, but is a good candidate for studying the congruent ordering in this system due to its relatively faster ordering kinetics. Ordering was studied previously via the indirect measurement of change of relative resistivity and relating that to the lattice contraction in Ni-33Cr. Utilizing synchrotron, this report is the  ﬁ rst of its kind to directly measure the lattice parameters and experi- mentally show by an in-situ setup that lattice contraction occurs due to ordering. Also for the  ﬁ rst time, the ordered domains were identi ﬁ ed in atom probe tomography, the ordered lattice planes within these domains visualized, and their size scale and morphology were determined using data-mining algorithms. This study clearly shows experimental evidence of Ni clustering at the early stages of isothermal annealing at 475    C in a random Ni-33Cr solid solution, where the compositions of these Ni-rich clusters are of the type Ni X Cr (x  ¼  3 to 9). These Ni-rich clusters could be potentially associated with the previously claimed SRO in this system, which eventually transforms to LRO domains of the Ni 2 Cr type.
