# Optimum microstructure combination for maximizing tensile strength in a polycrystalline superalloy with a two-phase structure

# Abstract  

Diﬀerent microstructural features were obtained under various heat treatment conditions, which provided insight into the factors con- trolling the critical strength in a polycrystalline Ni–Co-based disk superalloy (TMW-4M3 alloy) with a two-phase structure. The con- tribution of each microstructural feature, namely, the grain size, annealing twin boundary and distribution of  c 0   precipitates, to the total strength was analyzed quantitatively by measuring the Vickers hardness over the nanometer to micron size range. Grain boundary strengthening decreased to nearly zero with increasing solution heat treatment temperature, while the secondary and tertiary  c 0   precip- itation hardening increased. Therefore, there is an optimum combination of microstructural features for achieving the highest tensile strength in such superalloys, the key factors being the temperature and time used for the solution heat treatment and the subsequent aging treatment. A method for determining the optimum factors for TMW-4M3 is proposed.

# 1. Introduction  

Polycrystalline Ni-based disk superalloys are widely used as turbine disk materials in advanced aircraft engines and land-based gas turbines because of their excellent ten- sile strength and creep properties, which depend mainly on their microstructure  [1–3] . Normally, Ni-based superalloys are composed of a dual-phase microstructure in which Ni 3 (Al,Ti) intermetallic precipitates, designated  c 0 , are embedded in a  c  matrix with a face-centered cubic (fcc) dis- ordered structure. Both phases can contain solution ele- ments, such as Mo, Co, Cr (mainly in  c ), Ta, Nb (mainly in  c 0 ) and W (in both phases)  [4] . Therefore, an Ni-based disk alloy with a  c / c 0   two-phase structure develops its strength mainly from solid solution hardening,  c 0   precipita- tion strengthening and grain boundary hardening. Solid solution hardening  [5,6]  and grain boundary hardening [7–9]  have both been well documented. However, the con- tribution of the  c 0   precipitation strengthening is not yet clearly understood, because it depends on the complex microstructure, which in turn is aﬀected by the heat treat- ment conditions during both solution heat treatment and the subsequent aging treatments.  

If the solution heat treatment temperature is below the  c 0 phase solvus (subsolvus), a ﬁne-grained microstructure can be obtained because the undissolved  c 0   precipitates (  1  l m), called primary  c 0 , pin the grain boundaries and restrict the grain growth  [10,11] . Supersolvus heat treatment dissolves the primary  c 0 , which produces a coarser-grained micro- structure with no pinning of the grain boundaries. In both cases, intragranular secondary and tertiary  c 0 , typically at precipitate diameters of approximately 100 and 30 nm and formed during cooling and the subsequent aging heat treat- ments, respectively, are present within the  c  grains. The volume fraction of the  c 0   precipitates and their size are crit- ical factors determining the alloy’s resistance to dislocation motion. Therefore, the strength of a turbine disk alloy has been found to depend critically on the heat treatment applied to it, which determines the grain size and the distri- bution of the  c 0   precipitates.  

There have been several attempts to model the contribu- tion of each feature of the microstructures to the total strength of a typical polycrystalline Ni-based superalloy [12–14] . However, most of these models have been con- structed using quantitative calculation with limited experi- mental results [12,13] or using alloys with simple microstructures  [14] . The contributions of the various microstructural features, such as the grain boundary and the distribution of  c 0   precipitates, to the total strength in Ni-based superalloys cannot be divided or separated clearly with these models, which makes the determination of the appropriate heat treatment steps required to achieve balanced properties based on the optimum combination of microstructural features both time consuming and costly. Therefore, new methods based on more complex micro- structures and detailed experimental results are necessary.  

An indentation test is one of the most common tech- niques used to extract the local material properties at the nanometer, submicron and micron scales  [15–18] . Recently we proposed a new method for measuring Vickers hardness values of disk superalloys on scales ranging from nanome- ters to microns  [18] . This technique makes it possible to quantitatively separate the contribution of each micro- structural feature to the tensile strength.  

In the current study, the contribution of each micro- structural feature to the total strength was analyzed quan- titatively in a relatively new advanced cast-and-wrought

 (C&W) Ni–Co-based disk superalloy (TMW-4M3 alloy)

 [3,10,11,18–21]  heat-treated at various sub- and supersol- vus solution temperatures. Based on the analysis, the opti- mum combination of microstructural features for achieving higher strength in TMW-4M3 is proposed.  

# 2. Experimental details  

# 2.1. Materials  

The material used in this study was a polycrystalline Ni–Co-based superalloy, TMW-4M3, produced by C&W processing at Mitsubishi Materials Corporation, Japan. The chemical composition of the alloy is listed in  Table 1 , together with the volume fraction of each component, calculated using Thermo-Calc. The material fabrication processes have been described in detail elsewhere [3,10,11,18–21] , so the processes for fabricating TMW- 4M3 are outlined only brieﬂy here. Ingots with a diameter ( / ) of 500 mm were prepared by triple melting. The ingots were forged at 1100   C into  / 200 mm billets. The billets were then isothermally forged at 1100   C into a  / 440 mm pancake-shaped disk. Then  / 10 mm    70 mm specimens were cut from the disk for hardness and tensile testing. For comparison, single-crystal bar specimens with the same chemical composition as TMW-4M3 were cast in a National Institute for Materials Science (NIMS) direc- tional solidiﬁcation furnace  [22]  (TMS-4M3). Specimens with a  h 001 i  orientation  6 3   from the longitudinal direc- tion were used for hardness and tensile testing. The samples were heat-treated at solution temperatures ranging from 1100 to 1180   C for 4 h and then air cooled (AC). Then, primary aging was carried out at 650   C/24 h/AC followed by secondary aging at 760   C/16 h/AC.  

# 2.2. Characterization of microstructural features for various heat treatment conditions  

The microstructures of the heat-treated alloy were char- acterized by electron backscatter diﬀraction (EBSD) in a ﬁeld emission scanning electron microscope and by trans- mission electron microscopy (TEM). EBSD was carried out in a JSM-7000F with a ﬁeld emission gun operating at 20 kV using the TSL OIM data collection program. TEM was performed using a Tecnai transmission electron microscope operating at 200 kV.  

The intercept length of the grain boundary  D GB , the intercept length of the grain boundary together with the twin boundary  D B , and the twin fraction were measured from the EBSD data using the TSL OIM analysis program. D GB  and  D B  should be the same as the grain boundary spacing and the boundary spacing, respectively. Further- more,  D GB  should also correspond to the grain size.  

The volume fraction  f vI  of the primary  c 0   precipitates was measured from the scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images using a point-counting method. From the TEM images, the average sizes  d II  and  d III  and the volume fractions  f vII  and  f vIII  of the secondary and tertiary  c 0   pre- cipitates were measured using NIS-Elements software (Nikon).

# 4.2. Optimum microstructure combination  

As mentioned above, both the converted and measured 0.2% ﬂow stresses reached peak values at the solution tem- perature at the boundary between regions I and II (1135   C). Thus, we can conclude that selecting 1135   C as the solution temperature for TMW-4M3 alloys could lead to the optimum combination of microstructures at which all strengthening factors reach their peak values at room temperature. We should also note the role of the pri- mary  c 0   precipitates. Many researchers have ignored the primary  c 0   precipitates because the contribution of the low  f vI  and their large particle size to the strength of disk superalloys is very small at room temperature. However, our ﬁndings clearly showed that the primary  c 0   evolution controlled the state of the other microstructures and thus their strengthening behaviors. An understanding of the solution and coarsening behaviors of the primary  c 0   during the solution heat treatment  [11]  may be a key to the optimi- zation of the microstructures and their various strengthen- ing behaviors in disk superalloys.  

# 4.3. High-temperature strength  

This new quantitative method can be extended to ana- lyze how these strengthening factors change with increasing test temperatures and to ultimately predict their behavior in real turbine disks at high temperatures.  Fig. 8  shows a schematic illustration of the temperature dependence of the multiple strengthening factors in TMW-4M3. The con- tributing factors active at room temperature may still be active, if slightly changed, up to about 725   C, because there is little decline in the experimental tensile strength from room temperature to about 725   C  [3,33] . For exam- ple,  D r GB  may decrease with increasing temperature because of the decrease in  k B   in the Hall–Petch relation y (see Eq.  (5)) [34] . Meanwhile,  D r c 0 II  may be almost con- stant or slightly increased, and  D r sol  +  D r c 0 III  might decrease because of a decrease in  D r sol  with increasing tem- perature. Finally,  r Ni  may decrease slightly from room temperature to about 725   C.

# 5. Conclusions  

In this paper, the microstructural features of a polycrys- talline C&W disk superalloy, TMW-4M3, were investi- gated after it was heat-treated under various conditions, and the contribution of each microstructural feature, namely, the grain size, annealing twin boundary and distri- bution of the  c 0   precipitates, to the total strength was ana- lyzed quantitatively. When the solution heat treatment temperature was increased, the grain boundary strengthen- ing decreased to almost zero, while the secondary and ter- tiary  c 0   precipitation hardening increased. The optimum combination of microstructural features for achieving the highest strength in such superalloys can be obtained under speciﬁc conditions (temperature and time) for the solution heat treatment and the subsequent aging treatment.
