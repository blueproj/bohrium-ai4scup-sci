# Microstructure and Properties Evolution of Co-Al-W-Ni-Cr Superalloys by Molybdenum and Niobium Substitutions for Tungsten  

HUADONG FU, YUHENG ZHANG, FEI XUE, YI ZHANG, HUAN XU,and JIANXIN XIE  

The microstructure and mechanical properties of Co-Al-W to Co-Al-Mo/Nb alloy systems have been investigated in an attempt to identify the inﬂuence of replacement for W in multicom- ponent Co-based alloys with 30 at. pct Ni and 12 at. pct Cr. Either Mo or Nb additions at the expense of W reduce alloy density to a similar extent. Increasing Mo substitution signiﬁcantly decreases the  c ¢  volume fraction and results in spherical  c ¢  morphology at 750   C, whereas Nb substitution shows opposite eﬀects on the  c ¢  volume fraction and morphology. Accordingly, the Mo and Nb additions raise and decrease the microhardness and high-temperature compression strength, respectively. The results can provide reference for the development of Co-based superalloys with low density and high strength.

# I. INTRODUCTION  

T HE  recent report of a  c ¢ -strengthened Co-Al-W- based alloy opened up a practical possibility for developing new Co-based superalloys, which have become an increasingly active research area over the 1 – 13 ]   past decade. After studying their potentials, it was found that the solvus temperature of  c ¢  precipitates can be increased up to 1200   C with high solidus tempera- 2 – 5 ]   [ 6 – 8 ]   ture, mechanical performance and oxidation [ 9 – 13 ]   properties as promising engineering materials can- didate for high-temperature applications. However, their high density and limited microstructural stability remain the biggest challenges for their further development.  

Special eﬀorts have been devoted to reducing or replacing the content of dense but essential  c ¢ -former element W. Some work studied the inﬂuence of alloying elements on the density and microstructural stability of 5 , – 20 ]   21 ]   Co-based superalloys. Neumeier  et al . and  

22 ]   Knop  et al . reported Co-based superalloys with better high-temperature strength than most of polycrystalline Ni-based superalloys, but the density is as high as 8.8 3 and 8.52 g/cm . As a result, variant alloy systems with low or zero W content have been investigated, develop- 23 , ]   25 , ] ing alloys based on Co-Al-Mo, Co-Ge-W, 27 ] 28 ] 29 ] Co-Ti-Cr, Co-Ti-V, Co-Nb-V, and so on. Accordingly, the density has been decreased from 9.5 3 to 8.5 g/cm , close to those of most commercial Ni-based and Co-based polycrystalline alloys. Among these FCC-L1 2 variants, Co-Al-Mo- and Co-N- [ 23 , , ]   b-V-based alloys are mostly investigated; how- ever, in some cases, undesired secondary phases were formed and harmful to the high-temperature mechanical performance. This was especially true when Cr was added to increase the oxidation resistance but further narrow the  c ¢  phase ﬁeld, even when high NI content was added to enlarge the  c - c ¢  two-phase ﬁeld and microstructural stability.  

In this study, the alloys with intermediate composi- tions between systems based on Co-Al-W, Co-Al-Mo and Co-Al-Nb have been studied with the presence of high Cr (12 at. pct) and Ni (30 at. pct) content. The density, phase transformation temperatures,  c - c ¢  two- phase microstructure, long-term microstructural stabil- ity and ﬂow stresses at intermediate temperature are examined to understand their change when the W content is replaced by an equal amount of Mo or Nb. This study will provide insights into the development of low-density, high-strength Co-based superalloys for intermediate temperature applications.  

# II. EXPERIMENTAL MATERIALS AND METHODS  

The nominal compositions of the experimental alloys are given in Table  I . Compared with the Co-9Al-5W-30- Ni-12Cr (at. pct) alloy, the compositional variants are made using respective Mo and Nb as equal replacements for W in a range of 1 to 4 at. pct. Hence, these alloys are referred to their relevant elemental content as 5W, 4W1Mo, 3W2Mo, 1W4Mo, 4W1Nb, 3W2Nb and 1W4Nb. Button ingots of 20 g were prepared by arc melting in an argon atmosphere and turned over eight times for remelting to improve the chemical homogene- ity. The actual compositions were measured by a JXA8230 electron probe microanalyzer (EPMA) with 20 kV accelerating voltage and 10 nA beam current after solution heat treatment at 1230   C for 24 hours, as listed in Table  I .  

The samples were encapsulated in quartz tubes, which were subsequently back-ﬁlled with pure Ar before solid solution and aging treatments. Similar to the work of 21 ]   Neumeier  et al ., the solid solution process was set to 1230   C/24 hours (air cooling), followed by a two-step heat treatment at 900   C/24 hours and 750   C/12 hours (air cooling), as a standard heat treatment. An addi- tional long-term heat treatment for 1000 hours at 750   C was used to evaluate the microstructure stability.  

The microstructure was observed by using a Nikon LV150 metallographic microscope after the polished sample had been etched by hydrochloric acid hydrogen peroxide reagent (proportion of erosion solution: 40 mL water + 20 mL hydrochloric acid + 10 mL hydrogen peroxide). For electron microscope observation, samples were electrolytic ally etched in an etchant consisting of 42 mL phosphoric acid, 34 mL sulfuric acid and 24 mL water. The  c / c ¢  microstructure was examined with a Zeiss Ultra 55 ﬁeld-emission scanning electron micro- scope (SEM) in secondary electron imaging mode. The INCA X-MAX 50 energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) equipped to this SEM was used to evaluate the phase compositions. The  c ¢  volume fraction was ana- lyzed by the standard point count method, and the size of  c ¢  precipitates (deﬁned as half of the average edge length of the cuboidal precipitates) was measured by the Image-Pro plus 6.0 software. For each alloy, at least ﬁve images with the same magniﬁcation were used to obtain suﬃcient statistics for the  c ¢  volume fraction and size measurement. The density was measured by Archi- medes’ principle. The DSC was used to obtain the  c ¢ solvus, solidus and liquidus temperatures on a discoid sample with a diameter of 2 mm and mass of about 50 mg. The measurements were from room temperature to 1600   C under Ar atmosphere with a heating rate of 10   C/minutes. The Vickers hardness of the alloys was tested by a HXD-1000TM Vickers hardness testing system, with a load of 300 g and dwell time of 15 seconds. Seven points were taken from diﬀerent grains of the polished sample to obtain an average hardness value. Compression tests were performed at 750   C at a strain rate of 10  4 /s on cylindrical com- pression samples with size of  F 4  9  5 mm.

# B.  Density and Phase Transformation Temperature  

Figure  2  shows the density of the experimental alloys. Replacing W with Mo and Nb signiﬁcantly reduced the 3   3 mass density from about 8.9 g/cm in 5W to 8.40 g/cm 3   in 1W4Mo and 8.29 g/cm in 1W4Nb. The substitution of 1 at. pct Mo for 1 at. pct W approximately lowers 3 the alloy density by around 0.11 g/cm , slightly less than 3 Nb by about 0.15 g/cm . The density of 1W4Mo and 1W4Nb are close to the recent reported low-density Co-based alloys Co-Al-Mo, Co-Ti-Cr, Co-Ti-V, 25 – 29 ]   C-Nb-V, etc . and commercial Co- and Ni-based alloys IN718, U720Li,  etc . for turbine disk application. The phase transformation temperature measured by DSC are included in Table  II , together with predicted values. Alloy 5W shows a  c ¢  solvus temperature of 973   C, which steadily drops to 949   C in 3W2Mo as W content reduces. Unlike Mo replacement, Nb additions continuously raises the  c ¢  solvus temperature from 996   C in 4W1Nb to 1035   C in 1W4Nb. Compared to the thermodynamic calculation, although the trend of decreasing  c ¢  solvus temperature by Mo replacement for W is in agreement with the prediction (Table  II ), the actual values are at least 20   C smaller. The measured increasing  c ¢  solvus temperature with increasing Nb is obviously inconsistence with the calculation, which exhibits an even lowering accuracy perdiction of the thermodynamic properties for the Nb-containing alloys than that of Mo. The signiﬁcant diﬀerence between prediction and experimental results in  c ¢  solvus temper- ature may be from the limited thermodynamic data input for Mo- and Nb-containing multicomponent Co-base alloys.

# C.  Cast Microstructure  

Figure  3  shows the as-cast microstructure of the experimental alloys, which exhibit the similar dendritic microstructure after solidiﬁcation. A small amount of eutectic structure is found in alloy 5W at interdendritic regions. The similar eutectic structure is detected in 4W1Mo but slightly decreases in volume fraction (Figure  3 (b)), and it disappears with higher levels of Mo replacement in 4W2Mo and 1W4Mo (Figures  3 (d) and (f)). In contrast, increasing Nb content promotes interdendritic eutectic structure with increasing volume fraction (Figures  3 (c), (e) and (g)). The composition of eutectic structure in these alloys is analyzed by SEM-EDS and displayed in Table  III . It implies that one phase of the eutectic structure in 5W (Figure  3 (a)) is enriched in Al, and most likely is  b -(Co,Ni)Al phase in c  +  b  +  l  eutectic. The eutectic structure in 4W1Mo exhibits the same chemical characteristics and is prob- ably the same eutectic. Diﬀerently, all the bright contrast eutectic phases in alloys 4W1Nb, 3W2Nb and 1W4Nb show enrichment of Nb and exhibits the same chemical features as that of Laves-(Co,Ni,Cr) 2 (Nb,W) 33 ]   phases in  c  + Laves eutectic. Due to the limited size of eutectic structure in experimental alloys, only the phase in 1W4Nb alloy is conﬁrmed of C15-Laves type in chemistry by point EDS analysis. The above results show that Mo addition to replace W may change the solidiﬁcation path by reducing the segregation of Al element during solidiﬁcation, while Nb replacement appears to change the solidiﬁcation behavior by pro- moting the formation of Laves phase.

However, replacing W with Nb leading to less spherical  c ¢  precipiates in 4W1Nb (Figure  4 ) and further cuboidal precipiates in 1W4Nb. After long-term aging at 750   C for 1000 hours, the  c ¢ precipitates coarsen to diﬀerent degrees in all experi- mental alloys, and the  c ¢  morphology become less spherical and more cuboidal in Mo- and Nb-containing alloys, respectively (Figure  4 ). The  c ¢  phase morphology in 5W and 4W1Mo changes from spherical to near cuboidal, and the  c ¢  morphology of 3W2Mo changed from spherical to near spherical. This shape change indicats that as the  c ¢  precipitates grow, the shape changes from a  c / c ¢  two-phase interface to a  c / c ¢ two-phase coherent strain energy control. With the increasing of Nb, the morphology of the  c ¢  precipitates changes from nearly cuboidal to cuboidal, most likely due to the relieved  c / c ¢  interfacial strain, which is consistent with the phenomenon observed in the alloys with the replacement of W by Nb after long-term aging. After aging for 1000 hours, as the  c ¢  volume fractions raise to diﬀerent extents to approach thermodynamic equilibrium, the tendency of increasing  c ¢  volume fraction with increasing Mo and Nb replacements seems unchanged. It is remarkable that after the heat treat- ment at 900   C for 24 hours and 750   C for 1000 hours, the  c ¢  phase volume fraction in alloys with the replace- ment of Mo for W increases more than twice as much as that in alloys with the replacement of Nb for W. The main reason is probably related to the diﬀusion rate of Mo, which is slower than that of Nb comparing the   34 ] diﬀusion rate of Mo and Nb in Co matrix at 750 C. Due to such slower diﬀusion rate, alloys with the Mo replacement for W are supposed to deviate from the equilibrium state more than alloys with Nb replacement after heat treatment at 900   C for 24 hours and 750   C for 12 hours. After long time aging like 1000 hours heat treatment, the system is approaching to the equilibrium state, and thus the volume fraction of the  c ¢  precipitates in alloys with the Mo replacement increases more than the alloys with the Nb replacement. The volume fraction in 5W is 48.5 pct, which continuously declines to 32.7 pct in 1W4Mo with increasing Mo content but raises to 74.4 pct in 1W4Nb containing higher content of Nb (Figure  5 ).

# E.  Mechanical Property  

Figure  6  shows the room-temperature Vickers hard- ness of the experimental alloys after solidiﬁcation and aging treatment. The hardness in as-cast condition is signiﬁcantly lower than that in both 12 and 1000 hours aging conditions, indicating that formation of  c - c two-phase mcrostructure apparently improves the resis- tance to deform. With the extension of aging time to 1000 hours at 750   C, the hardness of the experimental alloys with Mo replacement shows an increasing trend than 12-hour-aged alloys, most likely due to the slightly increasing  c ¢  volume fraction and cuboidal morphology, whereas negligible diﬀerence is found in Nb-containing alloys with the same increasing  c ¢  volume fraction. After the same aging time, Mo replacement reduces the hardness, while Nb replacement increases it. For exam- ple, after aging at 900   C/24 hours+750   C/12 hours, 1 at. pct Mo substitution of W reduces the hardness of the experimental alloys by  \~  15 HV, while 1 at. pct Nb substitution of W increases the hardness by  \~  13 HV.  

Figure  7  shows the 0.2 pct compression ﬂow stress of the experimental alloys at 750   C after the aging treatment of 900   C for 24 hours and 750   C for 12 hours. Compared to 5W, Mo substitution leads to slightly lower compression strength in 4W1Mo and 3W2Mo, in agreement with hardness properties. The ﬂow strength of 1W4Mo signiﬁcantly reduces to 256.8MPa, most likely due to the absence of  c ¢  precip- iates. Instead, Nb additions make a signiﬁcant rise of compression strength at 750   C, because of the 10 to 30 pct higher  c ¢  phase volume fraction than that in 5W alloy (Figure  5 ). It can be inferred from the morpho- logical evolution of  c ¢  in Figure  5  that 1 at. pct Nb substitution of 1 at. pct W leads to a signiﬁcant increase in the misﬁt of  c / c ¢  phase, and the increase in the lattice strain between  c / c ¢  phase caused by the signiﬁcantly increased misﬁt of  c / c ¢  phase may also contribute to the increase in strength.  

Compared to the conventional Ni-based superalloys, the high density of the novel Co-based superalloys may severely limits their applications in the aerospace indus- try. The high concentration of W in the Co-Al-W-based superalloys—typically between 3 and 10 at. pct (10 and 30 wt pct)—is the primary cause of their high mass [ 14 ]   density. Therefore, assessing the inﬂuence of reducing the amount of W on their microstructure stability and mechanical properties is reasonably necessary. In this experiment, the density of the alloys is signiﬁcantly reduced with the replacement of W by Mo and Nb, as shown in Figure  2 .  

Superalloys need to service at high temperatures for a long time. Therefore, one of the major concerns for the alloys design is to improve the microstructure stability of the alloys on the premise of acceptable mechanical properties. The mechanical properties of  c ¢ -strengthened alloy phase is closely related to the  c ¢  composition, morphology and size that aﬀect the stability of  c precipitates. In the present alloys, Replacing W with Mo gradually reduces the hardness and the compression yield strength, whereas the substituitons of Nb for W show the opposite eﬀects (Figures  6  and  7 ). This is   ¢ 19 ] because all W, Mo, Nb are  c -formers, and the ﬁrst principles calculation results show that in the  c ¢ -Co 3 (Al, W), Mo and Nb atoms tend to occupy the lattice site of 35 ]   W atoms, but Mo and Nb have diﬀerent formation ability of  c ¢  phase. It leads to the results that replacing W by Nb increase  c ¢  volume fraction, while replacing W by Mo decreases the  c ¢  volume fraction under the same heat treatment condition. Eventually the variations of the  c ¢  volume fraction for the alloys result in the opposite eﬀects on the mechanics performance.  

As for the microstructure stability, replacing W by Mo does not inhibit the formation of  l -(Co,Ni) 7 (Cr, Mo,W) 6  phase at grain boudnaries after aging at 750   C for 1000 hours, as shown in Figure  4 . The  l  phase is enriched in W and Mo, which will decrease the solid solution strengthening, and thus may reduce the creep and other mechanical properties of the alloys. However, given the small size and low volume fraction of  l  phase, its inﬂuence on the mechanical properties of experimen- tal alloys needs further study. Nevertheless, the results displayed in Figure  4  show that replacing W by 1 at. pct Nb can inhibit the formation of  l -(Co,Ni) 7 (Cr,Mo,W) 6 phase, but a higher Nb content leads to the formation of d -(Co,Ni) 3 Nb phase in 3W2Nb and 1W4Nb alloys. This happens because both Mo and W are  l -formers, and the total content ((Mo + W) at. pct = 5 pct) is not chan- ged by replacing W with Mo, so the similar forming ability of  l  phase is expected. While replacing W with Nb reduces the total content of  l -formers, which, hence, can inhibit the formation of  l  phase in 4W1Nb, 3W2Nb and 1W4Nb alloys. It is worth noting that the formation of  l  phase may be eﬀectively inhibited when (Mo + W) at. pct  <  5 pct as detected in the 4W1Nb. After aging at 750   C for 1000 hours, the detrimental phase ( l and  d ) volume fractions in the experimental alloys are not more than 1 pct, which means that the experimental alloy has good microstructure stability with lowered W

Whether in Co-based superalloys or Ni-based superalloys, researchers have been trying to develop the alloys with low  c / c ¢  two-phase misﬁt by adjusting alloy   [ 2 , ] ¢ compositions to suppress coarsening of the  c  phase in service environments. The shape of the  c ¢  phase in nickel based superalloys is closely related to the misﬁt ¢ [ 4 ] ¢ between the  c / c  two-phases : the  c  phase is formed as spheres at a 0 to 0.2 pct misﬁt, as cubes at about 0.5 to 1.0 pct and as plates above about 1.25 pct. For the precipitates coarsening at 750   C observed here, only the inﬁuence of interfacial energy and diﬀusing corﬃ- cient are discussed since the  c ¢  volume fraction is not constant but changes signiﬁcantly during aging. As is shown in Figure  4 , the spherical  c ¢  precipitates in the alloys containing Mo indicate that replacing W with Mo can signiﬁcantly reduce the  c / c ¢  two-phase misﬁt, while the cuboidal  c ¢  precipitates in alloys with Nb suggest Nb additions signiﬁcantly increase it. Furthermore, the coarsening of the  c ¢  phase was inhibited by the substi- tution of Mo for W, and is promoted by the substitution of Nb for W.  

To sum up, the eﬀects of replacing W with Mo and Nb on  c ¢ -strengthened Co-based superalloys are system- atically investigated in this study. The results show that they have diﬀerent eﬀects on the mechanical properties and microstructure stability of the alloys despite the similar inﬂuence on density reduction, as shown in Figure  8 . The substitution of Mo for W reduces the  c ¢ volume fraction, decreases the mechanical properties, suppresses the coarsening of the  c ¢  phase, and reduces the  c ¢ / c ¢  two-phases misﬁt. Replacing W with Nb increases the  c ¢  volume fraction, improves the mechan- ical properties, promotes the coarsening of  c ¢  phase, increases the  c / c ¢  two-phases misﬁt and inhibiting the formation of  l  phase. For the composition design of Co-based superalloys, it can be predicted that Mo and Nb may be added together to replace W elements to balance microstructure stability and mechanical properties.  

# V. CONCLUSION  

(1) Replacing W with Mo can reduce the segregation of Al during solidification and decrease the volume fraction of  c ¢  phase.
