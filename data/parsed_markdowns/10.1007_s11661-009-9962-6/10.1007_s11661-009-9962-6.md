# Improvement in Laser Weldability of INCONEL 738 Superalloy through Microstructural Modiﬁcation  

A.T. EGBEWANDE, H.R. ZHANG, R.K. SIDHU, and O.A. OJO  

Microstructural study of laser-beam-welded IN 738 superalloy was carefully performed to better understand the causes of heat-aﬀected zone (HAZ) cracking and to determine an improved approach of alleviating the weldability problem. The HAZ cracks in the alloy were intergranular liquation cracks that resulted from the liquation reaction of both secondary solidiﬁcation products (MC carbides and  c - c ¢  eutectic) and solid-state reaction products ( c ¢  particles) present in the preweld material. In contrast to the expectation based on Chadwick’s equation, a reduction of grain boundary liquid ﬁlm thickness did not produce a decrease in HAZ cracking owing to increased base alloy hardness that accompanied a preweld heat treatment designed to reduce the intergranular liquation. Moreover, a major factor limiting the eﬀectiveness of an existing preweld heat treatment with low base alloy hardness in reducing HAZ cracking was found to be the formation of intergranular M 5 B 3  boride particles during the heat treatment. These borides can widen the HAZ brittle temperature range (BTR) during weld cooling and increase the propensity for cracking. Based on the results, a new preweld heat treatment that induces a moderate hardness and precludes grain boundary boride formation was found and was shown to produce a signiﬁcant reduction in HAZ cracking in the welded alloy compared to the most eﬀective pre-existing preweld heat treatment.

# I. INTRODUCTION  

INCONEL\* 738, like many other nickel-base

superalloys, oﬀers a wide range of properties that allows its use under stringent service conditions, such as in land-based and aero gas turbine engines. Among its most notable properties are the following: excellent high-temperature strength, hot corrosion resistance, oxidation, and sulﬁdation resistance. The high-temper- ature strength, derived from the precipitation of the ordered L1 2  intermetallic Ni 3 (Al,Ti)  c ¢  phase within a  c matrix, allows it to be used at temperatures up to  980   C. Requirements for improved turbine eﬃciency in both aero-engines and power applications, which involves increasing the turbine inlet temperature, have necessitated that the alloy be used under increasingly more stringent stress and temperature conditions, result- ing in a more rapid degradation of components in  

1 ]   service. This necessitates that damaged components be either replaced or repaired. However, the cost of producing new components is exorbitant, and economic considerations usually favor the repair of damaged components. Also, in today’s highly competitive and proﬁt driven manufacturing industry, the ability of a company to supply its products at the lowest cost possible without compromising product quality and safety can be an important edge over competitors. Thus, whether for the purposes of fabrication of new compo- nents or repairs of service damaged ones, it is important that industries employ the most eﬃcient processes that reduce both the throughput price and production times. For these reasons, industries often employ some forms of welding during the fabrication or repairs of engineer- ing components.  

Laser welding is a relatively new fusion welding technique that oﬀers several advantages over conven- tional welding methods used in the joining of nickel-base superalloys. These advantages include rapid production through the use of higher welding speed, lower instal- lation and running costs, smaller ﬂoor space require- ment, ﬂexibility of use, and ability to weld complex structures by delivering lasers to remote parts with ﬁber optics and reduced heat input, which reduces the heat- 2 – 6 ] aﬀected zone (HAZ) size.  

However, the application of fusion welding processes to precipitation-hardened nickel-base superalloys con- taining high Al + Ti content such as IN 738 has been severely limited due to their high susceptibility to HAZ 7 – 9 ]   cracking during welding. The HAZ cracking in IN 738 has been shown to be of the liquation type, and its main cause—grain boundary liquation—has been attributed to the liquation of phases such as MC carbides,  c - c ¢  eutectic, and  c ¢  precipitates present in the 10 ]   preweld alloy. Previous studies have shown that carefully designed heat treatments can reduce the susceptibility of nickel-base superalloys to HAZ 11 – 14 ]   cracking. However, limited information was found in the literature on preweld heat treatment designed to reduce/eliminate HAZ cracking in welded IN 738. The most recent attempt at designing a heat treatment to reduce HAZ cracking of the alloy was done by Thakur 14 ]   et al. While this heat treatment, UMT ( i.e. , Univer- sity of Manitoba heat treatment), resulted in a reduction in the total HAZ crack length in IN 738 relative to the standard solution heat treatment (SHT), signiﬁcant HAZ cracking still occurs. The objectives of this research were to carry out a microstructural study of laser-beam-welded IN 738 to gain better understanding of the factors contributing to prohibitive HAZ cracking in the alloy and to use the acquired knowledge to determine a more suitable preweld heat treatment that will reduce or prevent HAZ cracking in the alloy. The results are presented and discussed in this article.

# C.  Reduced Grain Boundary Liquation and HAZ Cracking  

It is a widely held opinion that the main cause of HAZ cracking in nickel-based superalloys and many other 7 – 14 ]   alloys is liquation cracking. The amount of liquid formed on grain boundaries can be reasonably expected to inﬂuence the extent of HAZ cracking. Miller and [ 17 ]   Chadwick related the stress required to cause ﬁssion by overcoming surface tension at the solid-liquid inter- face on liquated grain boundaries ( c sl ) to the liquid ﬁlm thickness ( h ) by the equation  

The implication of this relationship is that an increase in grain boundary liquid ﬁlm thickness should reduce the stress required to separate an alloy at a solid-liquid interface. Accordingly, any factor that reduces the thickness of intergranular liquid ﬁlm could presumably reduce the susceptibility of the alloy to HAZ cracking. Reducing or preventing  c ¢  liquation can signiﬁcantly decrease grain boundary liquid ﬁlm thickness. Liquation of  c ¢  is caused by incomplete dissolution of  c ¢  particles before the  c - c ¢  eutectic reaction temperature is reached

18 ]   in the HAZ during the heating cycle of welding. A deviation from the equilibrium precipitate dissolution mechanism occurs during rapid heating such as that 19 ] experienced during welding. Soucail et al. have shown that the solvus temperature of  c ¢  particles may markedly depart from its equilibrium value during rapid heating, causing these precipitates to survive to signif- icantly higher temperatures than those predicted by the equilibrium phase diagram. Delay of complete solid- state dissolution of  c ¢  at their equilibrium solvus and persistence of  c ¢  particles to temperatures at which  c - c ¢ eutectic reaction can occur will increase both the possibility of liquation and amount of liquation that 19 ]   may occur. Soucail  et al. found that the departure from equilibrium condition is dependent on the initial size of the precipitate particles. The larger the particle size the higher the temperature at which complete  c ¢ dissolution occurs above the solvus temperature. For example, for secondary  c ¢  precipitates in the same alloy, they found that the  c ¢  solvus temperature was 1115   C at     –1   a heating rate of 300 Cs instead of 1090 C at equilibrium. A similar observation was made by one of the authors (OAO) during the study of simulated HAZ microstructure in IN 738 by a Gleeble thermome- 20 ]   [ 20 ]   chanical simulation system. It was observed that the smaller secondary  c ¢  particles dissolved completely at 1135   C, while the larger primary  c ¢  particles were unaﬀected at this temperature. The dissolution of primary  c ¢  particles was observed to start only at 1165   C and above. This indicates that reducing the size of  c ¢  particles can lower the temperature at which complete solid-state dissolution would occur and, thus, reduce their susceptibility to constitutional liquation. 21 ]   Aaron  et al. considered the inﬂuence of surface curvature on the second-phase precipitate dissolution rate and found that the presence of curvature tends to increase the speed of particle dissolution. Consequently, to reduce grain boundary liquation in IN 738, a viable approach might be to reduce the size of  c ¢  particles through a preweld heat treatment to obtain ﬁne spher- ical precipitates, which may yield a faster dissolution rate. Therefore, to investigate this possibility, cast IN 738 was subjected to a preweld heat treatment, SHT + 1200   C/2 h/AC (designated RHT or revised heat treat- ment), and welded along with specimens given the UMT and SHT heat treatments to compare their cracking susceptibility.  

The RHT heat treatment resulted in complete disso- lution of primary  c ¢  particles with subsequent formation of very ﬁne unimodal spherical secondary  c ¢  particles with diameters of about \~ 0.1  l m during cooling (Figure  7 ). The  c ¢  precipitates with an irregular shape measuring about 0.4  l m were also formed on grain boundaries in addition to pre-existing MC carbide particles. The  c - c ¢  eutectic was completely eliminated by the RHT heat treatment. The SEM examination of the welded alloy showed limited grain boundary liqua- tion in the RHT specimen (Figure  8 ). The most exten- sive grain boundary liquation occurred in the UMT specimen, followed by the SHT specimen, while the least grain boundary liquation occurred in the RHT speci- men. The larger size of  c ¢  particles in the UMT specimen compared to those in the SHT and RHT conditions could have contributed to the high extent of intergran- ular liquation in the UMT condition.

# D.  Formation of Intergranular Borides and HAZ Cracking  

The discussion in Section C indicates that low base metal hardness could mitigate HAZ cracking. However, a signiﬁcant amount of HAZ cracking still occurred in welded UMT specimens despite the associated low base alloy hardness in this heat-treatment condition. This suggests that the exclusive ability of the base alloy to accommodate welding stress (adequate ductility) may not be suﬃcient to eliminate HAZ cracking. Rather, in conjunction with this, the ability of the HAZ to recover useful ductility before high on-cooling tensile stress is developed might also be vital in reducing HAZ cracking, since liquated HAZ regions lose ductility during the 22 ]   heating cycle of the welding process. Generally, factors that enlarge the solidiﬁcation temperature range of grain boundary liquid also increase the temperature range within which the HAZ exhibits negligible ductility during weld cooling,  i.e. , brittle temperature range

22 ]   (BTR). These factors allow grain boundary liquid ﬁlm to survive to lower temperatures and delay ductility recovery, which can increase propensity to intergranular cracking. Prominent among these is the enrichment of intergranular liquid with melting point depressing elements such as boron.  

Careful analysis of HAZ intergranular constituents in welded UMT samples revealed the presence of Cr-rich boride particles among the resolidiﬁed grain boundary products (Figure  11 (a)). Boron is a known melting point depressing element that lowers the solidiﬁcation tem- 23 ]   perature in nickel-base superalloys. The Cr-rich particles that form on grain boundaries during aging heat treatments of IN 738, such as the UMT, have been generally reported to be M 23 C 6  particles. However, Ojo [ 18 ]   et al. suggested that these particles may not be exclusively M 23 C 6  particles. Hence, a careful micro- structural study of the intergranular particles in the UMT condition was performed to determine the pres- ence of any other second-phase particles besides the M 23 C 6  carbides. Although SEM-EDS analysis suggested

Therefore, the grain boundary particles were extracted on carbon extraction replica for detailed study by TEM techniques. Electron energy loss spectroscopy, which has a higher resolution and sensitivity for light elements such as boron, of particles suspended on the edge of the carbon substrate to eliminate interference from the carbon ﬁlm, showed signiﬁcant boron peak from some of the Cr-rich particles (Figure  12 ). To determine the crystal structure of these particles (Figure  13 (a)), TEM electron diﬀraction analysis was performed. Analysis of the selected area diﬀraction patterns (SADPs) obtained from diﬀerent zone axes (Figures  13 (b) through (d)) indicated that the Cr-B–rich particles are M 5 B 3  borides with a bct crystal structure and lattice parameters a  = 0.54 nm and  c  = 1.01 nm. This shows that the Cr-rich particles that form in this alloy in the UMT heat-treatment condition include some borides. Segre- gation of boron to grain boundaries has been shown to occur when nickel-base superalloys are held isother- 24 ] mally at high temperatures during heat treatment. Due to their small size, it will be energetically more favorable for boron atoms to segregate to loosely packed regions such as grain boundaries, as this could 24 , ] help to reduce the free energy of such interfaces.  

It has been shown that, rather than persisting on grain boundary in solid solution form, there is a strong tendency for boron to desegregate from grain bound- aries and become incorporated into second-phase par- 26 ]   ticles during isothermal holding. Hence, boron can preferentially form boride particles during heat treat- ments such as the UMT. During the heating cycle of welding, rapid decomposition of intergranular borides such as the M 5 B 3  in the UMT preweld condition can result in the release of boron atoms along grain boundary regions. Boron is a potent melting point depressing element in nickel alloys, and the enrichment of intergranular regions with it could not only reduce the temperature at which grain boundary liquation would start during heating, but could also signiﬁcantly reduce the terminal solidiﬁcation temperature during cooling. This could in eﬀect enlarge the BTR and delay ductility recovery of the HAZ, which may promote susceptibility to liquation cracking. This, we presume, is a likely factor that is reducing the eﬀectiveness of the UMT preweld heat treatment in reducing HAZ cracking in IN 738 despite the low hardness of the base alloy in this condition.  

Equilibrium segregation decreases with increasing 27 , ]   isothermal holding temperature.Hence, the extentof equilibrium boron segregation and boride formation on grain boundaries in IN 738 could reduce with increasing heat-treatment temperature. Consequently, by aging the alloy at temperatures higher than 1025   C, the amount of boron tied up in grain boundary boride particles may reduce, and the deleterious contribution of boron segregation to liquation cracking could also be minimized. Therefore, to improve the susceptibility of IN 738 to HAZ cracking beyond what the UMT heat treatment presently oﬀers, it is necessary to develop a heat treatment that prevents the formation of boride particles and produces as low a base alloy hardness as possible.

# V. SUMMARY AND CONCLUSIONS  

The microstructure of laser-beam-welded IN 738 was carefully studied to better understand the factors that contribute to weld HAZ cracking in the material in order to develop a more suitable preweld heat treatment to reduce the HAZ cracking. The results of the study provide the following information.  

1. A key metallurgical factor causing HAZ cracking in IN 738 is grain boundary liquation caused by constitutional liquation of  c ¢ , MC carbides, and melting of  c - c ¢  eutectic.

 2. In contrast to prediction based exclusively on Chad- wick’s equation, a preweld heat treatment (RHT) designed to reduce grain boundary liquation and HAZ cracking by reducing constitutional liquation of  c ¢  particles resulted in increased cracking com- pared to the standard SHT and UMT preweld heat treatment.

 3. The deviation from the prediction is related to the considerable increase in the base alloy hardness that accompanies the RHT. High base alloy hardness can reduce the ability of the material to relieve welding stresses that may cause signiﬁcant stress to accumulate on susceptible HAZ liquated grain boundaries resulting in microﬁssuring.

 4. A major factor limiting the eﬀectiveness of a previ- ously developed preweld heat treatment (UMT) in reducing HAZ cracking was found to be the formation of intergranular boride particles during the preweld heat treatment. Borides can liquate at low tempera- tures and also enlarge the BTR of the alloy during cooling, thereby aiding susceptibility to cracking.

 5. To avoid boride formation and to enhance HAZ ductility by reducing the BTR, a new preweld heat treatment (NUMT) was found, which was shown to reduce HAZ cracking in IN 738 superal- loy during laser beam welding, compared to the UMT heat treatment. Grain boundary borides were not observed in the NUMT material.
