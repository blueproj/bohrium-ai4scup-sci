Solid polyethylene oxides  \~ PEO !  are in effect ‘‘polymer liquids’’ due to their ﬂexibility and high solubility for alkaline salts. To clarify the role of PEO in electrolyte systems, electrolytes composed of members of the ‘‘glyme family’’  @ i.e., diglyme \~ DG ! , triglyme \~ TG ! , tetraglyme \~ TeG ! , pentaglyme \~ PG ! , and polyethylene glycol dimethyl ethers \~ PEGDM !#  doped with LiN(SO 2 CF 3 ) 2 were investigated. PEGDMs form a series of low molecular weight PEO-like homologues with molecular weights between 400 and 2500. Electrolytes of the glymes and PEGDMs were prepared for two salt concentrations  \~ ether oxygen: lithium; O:Li !  20:1 and 10:1. The ionic conductivities and the self-diffusion coefﬁcients of the solvent, anion and lithium ions in the electrolytes were measured using pulsed-ﬁeld gradient spin-echo  \~ PGSE !   1 H, 19 F, and   7 Li NMR, respectively. From the spin-lattice relaxation times ( T 1 ) it was found that the segmental motions of the CH 2 CH 2 O moiety and the lithium hopping motions are correlated and that the rate of the segmental motions decreases as the molecular size of the solvent increases. # I. INTRODUCTION  

Solid-state polymer electrolytes based on the polyethyl- eneoxide  \~ PEO !  doped with various salts have high ionic conductivity and consequently great potential for usage in batteries and capacitors.   To be suitable for practical usage these solid electrolytes must have high ionic conductivity at ambient temperature and consequently high solubility for al- kaline salts. A major stumbling block in the further develop- ment of these solid electrolytes is that the lithium ion con- duction mechanisms have not been fully clariﬁed. By using pulsed-ﬁeld gradient spin-echo \~PGSE!1H,19F, and7Linuclear magnetic resonance  \~ NMR !  diffusion measure- ments,   we have previously investigated the translational dynamics of the individual ions in PEO-based liquid- and cross-linked polymer-electrolytes with the precursors of ethyleneoxide-co-propyleneoxide  \~ EO-PO ! \~ Mw 8 000 !  and ethyleneoxide-co-2- \~ 2-methoxyethoxy ! ethyl glycidyl ether \~ EO-GE ! \~ Mw 10 000 !  doped with lithium salt. Since they have fairly high ionic conductivities  \~ about 10 2 4   S cm 2 1   at 333 K ! , the correlations between the ionic conductivity ( s N–E ) calculated by the Nernst–Einstein equation using the diffusion coefﬁcients ( D ) of the ions and that measured by the ac impedance method ( s ac ) were discussed. We proposed a model for the ionic conduction in the PEO-based polymer electrolytes in which the lithium ions are located near the polymer chains and rapidly hop ( ; 10 2 9   s) between the \~ faster moving !  polymer side chains. The anion, on the other hand, is more distant from the polymer chains and diffuses almost independently of them. Interestingly the observed  D of the anion varies with the measuring time  \~ i.e., non-Fickian behavior ! . At long times  \~ typically 0.07–0.2 s !  the  D apparent becomes constant and appears to be related to the observed ionic conductivity. Such a time scale corresponds to a root mean square displacement of the order of 10 2 6   m. This ‘‘anomalous’’ diffusion behavior and ionic conductivity of a particular PEO-based polymer electrolyte system is closely related to the structure and motion of CH 2 CH 2 O  \~ EO !  moi- ety.  

Due to the polydispersity of the PEO it is difﬁcult to formulate comprehensive models of such lithium salt-doped electrolytes. Further, the basic electrochemical theories are based on solution-state electrolytes. To obviate the problems with polydispersity we adopted liquid-state PEO homologues such as polyethylene glycol dimethyl ethers  \~ PEGDM !  with average molecular weights of 2500  \~ P25 ! , 1000  \~ P10 ! , and 400  \~ P4 !  and the so-called glymes with the structure of CH O(CH CH O) CH  , having  n 5 2  \~ diglyme, DG ! , 3 3 2 2 n 3 \~ triglyme, TG ! , 4  \~ tetraglyme, TeG ! , and 5  \~ pentaglyme, PG ! . Here  n  represents the number of EO units.  

The interactions of the alkaline and alkali earth ions with the CH 2 CH 2 O moiety have been widely studied. The static structure of liquid P \~ EO ! 7.5 LiTFSI @ LiTFSI 5 LiN(CF 3 SO 2 ) 2 #  has been studied by neutron diffraction and it was proposed that the lithium ions are on average bound to ﬁve oxygens belonging to the pairs of PEO coils. NMR and ionic conductivity studies of polyethylene glycol \~ Mw 400 !  doped with LiTFSI or LiCF 3 SO 3  have been re- ported and the relationship between ionic conductivity and diffusion was considered.   The  D ’s of the lithium, anion and solvent for the PEGDM  \~ Mw 400 !  complex with LiCF 3 SO 3 have been measured and found that the solvent diffuses the fastest.   On the other hand, in the PPO400-NH 4 CF 3 SO 3  sys- tem the solvent diffuses the slowest. In both systems, the differences between  s ac  and  s N–E  decreased with increasing salt concentration and decreasing temperature.  

The 1:1 complexes of a lithium ion with TeG, PG, and hexaglyme or low molecular weight glycols have been stud- ied using  ab initio  calculations and the coordination numbers of the lithium ion for the solvent oxygen atoms were pro- posed to range between 4 and 6.   Monte Carlo statistical mechanics simulation suggests that DME ( n 5 1) and DG form 2:1 complexes with a lithium where the oxygen coor- dination number is 6.   Sandwich complexes of the alkaline earth cations with TG in the gas phase have been observed by mass spectrometry.   The TG-LiCF 3 SO 3  system was stud- ied using  T 1  and diffusion measurements and it was found that both rotational and translational motions contribute to the  T 1  and that the solvent diffuses faster than the ions. We have also reported the solvation effect in DME, DG, and TG electrolytes together with other lithium-battery liquid electrolytes.  

In the present paper, the ionic conductivities, NMR  T 1 and the  D ’s  @ i.e., solvent ( 1 H), anion ( 19 F), and lithium ion ( 7 Li) #  were measured as functions of temperature for the fourteen electrolytes composed of one of the seven solvents @ DG  \~ 2 ! , TG  \~ 3 ! , TeG  \~ 4 ! , PG  \~ 5 ! , P4  \~ 10.6 ! , P10  \~ 22.9 ! , and P25  \~ 55.3 ! , where the number in braces represents the  n # doping with two different concentrations of LiTFSI and also the neat solvents. The NMR results for the DG electrolytes are not included since the data changed at the initial and ﬁnal stages of the thermal measurements. The relationships be- tween the correlation times of the CH 2 CH 2 O segmental and the lithium hopping motions, the individual diffusion coefﬁ- cients, ionic conductivity, ion dissociation, and the interac- tions between the solvent and the lithium ion are discussed in the terms of the dependence on the  n . Solvent aggregation effects in the electrolytes are also considered.

# B. DSC measurements  

DSC measurements were performed using a Seiko In- struments  \~ Tokyo !  DSC220C differential scanning calorim- eter. Each sample was cooled to 123 K and scanned at a rate of 10 K min 2 1   to 353 K. The measurement was then re- peated in the opposite direction. The glass transition tem- perature ( T g ) was determined from the midpoint of the heat capacity change during the heating process. The melting ( T m ) and the crystallization ( T c ) temperatures were deter- mined from the heat capacity change during the heating and the cooling processes, respectively, except for the few elec- trolytes where the crystallization occurred during the heating process.  

# C. Ionic conductivity measurements  

Ionic conductivity was measured using the ac impedance method on a Solartron Electrochemical Interface 1286 and Frequency Response Analyzer 1255. The electrolyte samples were poured into a glass cell with Pt electrodes. The imped- ance measurements were carried out from 0.1 Hz to 1 MHz in the temperature range 353–303 K starting from 353 K.  

# D. NMR measurements  

All NMR spectra were measured on a JEOL GSH-200 spectrometer with a 4.7 T wide bore magnet controlled by a TecMag Galaxy system. A JEOL current ampliﬁer was used to generate the gradient pulses. The 90° pulse widths for   1 H, 7 Li, and  19 F were 9.8, 6.0, and 9.5  m s, respectively. The  T 1 measurements were performed using the inversion recovery (180°- t -90°-Acq.) sequence.

# IV. DISCUSSION  

The declining ionic conductivity with increasing  n  can principally be interpreted by the concomitant increase in vis- cosity in the electrolytes. Speciﬁcally Arrhenius plots of the ionic conductivity change gradually from linear to convex \~ VTF-type !  plots with increasing  n . In contrast, Arrhenius plots of the NMR-derived ion diffusion coefﬁcients are al- most always linear. The  E ap  in Fig. 3 approach to an equi- librium value of about 5.5 kJ mol 2 1   as  n  becomes larger. Previously, the  E ap  value was estimated to be below 8 kJ mol 2 1   from the free volume model.  

On the basis of the conﬁguration entropy model it has been suggested that the segmental motions including crank- shaft motions signiﬁcantly contribute to the ionic conduction.   The present results agree with these predic- tions. When  n  is small, the solvent molecules can easily exchange in the coordinated sphere around the lithium ions due to the large free volume and hence such systems have low  T g  values  \~ e.g., 187 K for TG ! . On the other hand, as  n increases the free volume decreases and rearrangement of the polymer chains is hindered, which results in slower ion

# V. CONCLUSION  

The segmental motions of the CH 2 CH 2 O moiety in the glymes and the PEO-based polymers bring ﬂexibility and provide high solubility and transport of the doped ions. The correlation times of the segmental motions decrease from 1.5 10 2 11   to 1.6 10 2 10   s as the chain length increases 3 3 from neat TeG to the solid cross-linked polymers at 333 K. Even if the number of EO unit is only 3 as in the TG elec- trolytes, the lithium hopping motion is activated by the sol- vent segmental motion. The interactions between the solvent and lithium ions can be related to the solvation in the glyme electrolytes and the inclusion of the lithium ion in the chains in the PEGDM electrolytes where the lithium ion is more decoupled from the solvent and can transfer faster than the PEGDM’s. The increase of the apparent molecular weight of the solvent in the electrolytes can be interpreted as aggrega- tion induced by the interaction with the lithium salt in the glyme electrolytes and the lithium movement on the chain in the PEGDMs. The anions move independently from the sol- vent chains but are not completely independent in the glyme electrolytes, where ion pairing with the lithium ion occurs. In the PEGDM electrolytes, the anion motion is more indepen- dent of the chains but inﬂuenced by the solvent diffusion. The activation energies for the ionic conductivity, self- diffusion coefﬁcients, segmental and the hopping motions change smoothly as an exponential function of  n  from TG to P25. Since higher Mw linear PEGDMs are rigid and conse- quently reduced ionic conductivity in electrolytes, ﬂexible macromonomers and the corresponding cross-linked poly- mers based on polyether were designed. The activation ener- gies of these PEGDM variants smoothly extrapolate from the linear PEGDMs. Also the rates of the segmental motions, lithium hopping motions, and the self-diffusion coefﬁcients of all the components change continuously depending on the n  and can be extrapolated to those of the macromonomers and/or cross-linked polymers.
