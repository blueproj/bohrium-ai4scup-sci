# a b s t r a c t  

The in ﬂ uence of high thermal gradient processing on the creep and low cycle fatigue properties of the AM1 Ni-based single crystal superalloy has been studied. Isothermal creep (from 750  ° C up to 1200  ° C) and low cycle fatigue (750  ° C and 950  ° C) experiments were performed for AM1 alloy solidi ﬁ ed with a conventional radiation cooled (Bridgman) and higher thermal gradient liquid-metal cooled (LMC) casting process to produce coarse and  ﬁ ner-scaled dendritic structures, respectively. There was no signi ﬁ cant effect of the casting technique on creep properties, due to the very similar microstructures ( γ ′ -size and  γ - channel width) established after full heat treatment of both Bridgman and LMC samples. For low cycle fatigue properties, the bene ﬁ t of the higher gradient LMC process was dependent on the testing tem- perature. At 750  ° C, cracks primarily initiated at pores created by solidi ﬁ cation shrinkage in both Bridgman and LMC samples. Samples produced by the LMC technique demonstrated fatigue lives up to 4 times longer, compared to the Bridgman samples, due to re ﬁ ned porosity. At 950  ° C the low cycle fatigue properties of the LMC and conventionally solidi ﬁ ed material were not distinguishable due to a shift of crack initiation sites from internal pores to oxidized surface layers or near-surface pores. The bene ﬁ t of the LMC approach was, however, apparent in fatigue at 950  ° C when testing in a vacuum environment. Based on these results, a crack initiation model based on the local slip activity close to casting defect is proposed.

# 1. Introduction  

Ni-based single crystal superalloys are often used for blades and vanes in aeroengines and industrial gas turbines. They exhibit exceptional mechanical properties up to very high temperatures (of up to 1100  ° C)  [1 , 2] . Solidi ﬁ cation of larger or geometrically complex single crystal components for hot-section engine airfoils is increasingly challenged by low thermal gradients and/or com- plex thermal  ﬁ elds present during casting as well as by advanced alloys which contain high levels of refractory elements such as Re and W. In the conventional Bridgman process, the limiting heat transfer mechanism is typically radiation from the exterior mold surface  [3 , 4] , resulting in lower cooling rates and coarser dendritic structures as compared to other higher gradient casting processes [5] . Heat extraction can be improved by the use of a liquid metal coolant during solidi ﬁ cation. An early description of the liquid metal cooling (LMC) process was given by Tschinkel et al.  [6]  in the  

The exceptional creep properties of Ni-based superalloys result primarily from their unique microstructure  [1 , 2] , consisting of up to 70% of coherently precipitated  γ ′ -phase (L1 2 -structure) em- bedded in a face centered cubic (fcc) solid solution  γ -matrix. The creep properties of Ni-based superalloys have been studied for various temperatures and loading conditions  [17 – 21]  and the factors that strongly in ﬂ uence creep life include the  γ ′ -volume fraction, the initial  γ ′ -size and morphology – . Several stu- dies have shown that the LMC process can improve the creep behavior due to reduced eutectic content after heat treatment or a ﬁ ner carbide distribution in the microstructure compared to the standard Bridgman process  [13 , 28] .  

Low cycle fatigue (LCF) in nickel-based single crystals is strongly dependent on the defects contained in the material (e.g.  

pores, carbides, eutectics), which serve as crack initiators. Ad- ditionally, fatigue may be in ﬂ uenced by oxidation at higher tem- peratures  [29 – 32] . In a prior study, an improvement of the LCF properties at 538  ° C by the LMC technique compared to the Bridgman casting method was obtained for a second generation Ni-based single crystal superalloy  [14 , 33] , due to a reduced frac- tion and size of pores in the LMC processed alloy. However, there is still very limited information regarding the in ﬂ uence of LMC on intermediate and high temperature LCF properties of mono- crystalline superalloys, although the data is critical for applica- tions. The objective of this research is to study over a wider range of testing conditions the mechanisms by which creep and low cycle fatigue properties of AM1 superalloy processed by conven- tional Bridgman and LMC processing are in ﬂ uenced by high gra- dient solidi ﬁ cation and to propose a new LCF lifetime model sensitive to the crack initiation site size.

# 2.1. Material, casting and heat treatment  

The material used in this study is AM1, a  ﬁ rst generation Ni- based single crystal superalloy. The nominal composition in wt% is given in  Table 1 . Cylindrical single crystal bars with diameters of 9 mm and 16 mm and a height of 152 mm were solidi ﬁ ed by both a conventional Bridgman and a liquid-metal cooling (LMC) process (liquid Sn) in an ALD Technologies furnace at the University of California, Santa Barbara. The experimental setup of the furnace with the differences between the two casting methods is de- scribed in detail elsewhere  [34] . Bridgman and LMC castings were respectively withdrawn at a rate of 3.4 mm/min and 8.5 mm/min. These withdrawal rates were selected based on a ProCast TM   (a trademark of ESI Group, Inc.) model of the mold con ﬁ guration and the casting process, in order to achieve the  ﬁ nest microstructure possible for both casting techniques with no stray grains nucle- ating during the withdrawal. To produce nearly [001] oriented bars, starters were used to control initial grain nucleation and crystal selection. To ensure that the bars further used for me- chanical testing were single crystalline without defects after casting, they were subjected to a macro-etching in a solution made of 80 ml HCl 2 ml HNO 3 11 ml H 2 O 16 g FeCl 3 .

# 3.4. Low cycle fatigue at 950  ° C  

The results of the LCF tests at 950  ° C are presented in  Fig. 8 , where the applied alternating stress is plotted as a function of the number of cycles to failure for conventional Bridgman and LMC casting. The results are also compared to LCF tests in air done by Perruchaut  [43]  using conventional Bridgman single crystalline AM1 samples under the same conditions.  

Although the dimensions of the initiation sites are again smaller for the LMC casting process, at this higher temperature, the lifetime is independent of the casting technique. The fatigue lives measured here correlate well with data extracted from the literature  [43] . Tests performed under vacuum last, as expected for both casting techniques longer due to the prevention of oxidation of the samples. However, a difference in lifetime can be observed between LMC and Bridgman similar in magnitude to that observed at 750  ° C ( Fig. 6 ). At 950  ° C in the absence of oxidation, the LMC samples last 2 – 3 times longer compared to Bridgman samples. Cracks initiated for all samples from pores, which were connected or very close to the surface. According to these results, oxidation has a more signi ﬁ cant impact at 950  ° C than at 750  ° C and it is apparently the LCF life limiting factor in air at this temperature. Thus, the bene ﬁ ts provided by the LMC process in terms of pore size are not apparent when testing in the oxidizing environment without coatings. The in ﬂ uence of oxidation on one of these in- itiation sites is shown in  Fig. 9 , where a cross-section parallel to the stress axis at 950  ° C/300 MPa is shown. The oxides line the faces of the crack, extending up to the crack tip. Cracks are likely initiating from the largest pore connected to the surface and oxi- dation assists at crack propagation, especially in the tensile part of the cycle.

Unlike the creep properties, low cycle fatigue properties are process dependent at intermediate temperature (750  ° C) and un- der vacuum at high temperature (950  ° C). Considering the process dependence of the LCF life on the testing atmosphere, oxidation is a primary mechanism controlling high temperature (950  ° C) fati- gue properties in air. At 750  ° C in air and at 950  ° C under vacuum, the bene ﬁ t of the LMC technique on fatigue life can be up to 4-times due to the smaller casting pores in the microstructure [14] , which serve as crack initiation sites. At higher temperatures in air, crack initiation always occurs at pores connected to the sample surface. The crack propagation is also known to be affected by oxidation at 950  ° C in this alloy  [43] . The effect of oxidation in assisting crack propaga- tion clearly overcompensates the bene ﬁ cial effect of smaller pores in LMC samples, which can be seen comparing the difference at 950  ° C between LMC and Bridgman in air and vacuum. Hence, even if additional LCF tests under vacuum at this high temperature would be required, it can be strongly intuited that the oxidation at 950  ° C mainly affects the crack propagation stage, and, to a lower degree, crack initiation.

# 5. Conclusions  

Bridgman and LMC casting experiments were executed at withdrawal rates of 3.4 mm/min and 8.5 mm/min, which where modeled with ProCast TM   to achieve the  ﬁ nest microstructure possible for both casting techniques with no stray grains nucle- ating during the withdrawal. The dendritic microstructure (pri- mary and secondary dendrite arm spacing) was 40%  ﬁ ner, casting porosity was less and the pore size was smaller due to the faster casting technique. LMC solidi ﬁ ed specimens did not show any remaining eutectics after heat treatment, while the Bridgman samples still possessed a small amount of eutectics (0.2% in area fraction) in the heat treated microstructure. The  γ / γ ′ -micro- structure is very similar for both casting techniques.

For the low cycle fatigue conditions investigated, a substantial fraction of the fatigue life was consumed with fatigue crack in- itiation. A new fatigue indicator parameter (FIP) has been devel- oped in order to capture the dependence of the fatigue lifetime on the solidi ﬁ cation-induced porosity size. With this new parameter, it is possible to estimate the fatigue lifetime of a sample with a known largest defect site with an accuracy of factor 2. At 950  ° C in air, there is no improvement of the LCF life with the faster casting technique probably because of the pronounced oxidation of the specimens, which systematically leads to crack initiation at pores connected or close to the surface of the samples and not at the biggest pore contained in the volume.
