# Abstract  

Production of lithium from primary resources is lagging behind demand (12% versus 16% in  2016), cost of lithium is increasing (was increased between 40-60% in 2016), battery energy  density rapidly increasing versus declined cost, and estimated lithium ion battery (LIB)  markets size (\$77.42 billion by 2024) driven by projected demands for plugged in electric  vehicle (PEV) clearly justifies recycling. Wake of PEV technology and projected demand  raising several challenges, including, lithium demand/scarcity and futuristic technology to  recover lithium from all those LIB wastes. To address the circular economy, steady supply  chain security, self-reliance, environment safety, environment directive, energy security,  resources conservation, futuristic carbon footprint, WEEE directives and waste crime  recycling of LIB is at absolute essential. During last decade, LIB recycling research and  industrial recycling of LIB have attracted the interest of researcher, industrialist, and  environmentalist significantly. All those reported progress are with interest to the recovery of  valuable metals like Co, but rarely lithium recovery has been focused. Hence, this paper  address logical hypothesis and application of available technology in a fashion where lithium  recycling from LIB can be addressed.  

# 1.   Introduction and Background  

Worldwide lithium production was increased 12% whereas lithium consumption was 16%  in 2016.  In 2016 lithium carbonate price was increased more than three folds compare to  previous year only in China.  Worldwide lithium price was increased by 40-60% in 2016 .  The price instability mostly due to lithium battery supply in China tripled in 2015, as plugged  3 in electric vehicle (PEV) sales surged . Matt Bohlsen has estimated lithium carbonate or  lithium carbonate equivalent could reach 2430 kt for only PEV application and the same  could be 2830 kt for whole industry wide applications by 2025. The projected demand could  3 accounts for 86% of lithium share for lithium ion battery (LIB), from current 39% . The same  report also predicted, the estimated total lithium carbonate equivalent product could reach  3 1430 kt by 2025, which is a deficit by 1400 kt . MarketWatch, Inc. has estimated the LIB  markets projected to reach \$77.42 billion by 2024, which is account for 12.5% compound  4 annual growth rate (CAGR) from 2016 to 2024 . The LIB technology evolution favors of  battery energy density versus cost. US DOE, global EV outlook 2016 reported PEV battery  cost decreased from USD 1000/kWh to USD 268/kWh during 2008 to 2015, accounts a 73%  reduction in seven years. Major maker like Tesla and GM targeting to achieve USD 50/kWh  by 2022. The same report depicted the energy density of PEV batteries was at 60 Wh/L in  5 2008 and it was attained 295 Wh/L in 2015, improving by almost 400% . Favourable  evolution of battery energy density and cost, over the past decade creating an opportunity for  higher demands for LIBs. Considering the socio-politico-economic scenario, market  competition, supply deficit, increasing price, and higher energy density versus cost, lithium  supply security has become a topmost priority for LIB related technology companies in the  1 United States, Asia-pacific, and China . A  UNEP report on recycling rates of metals, a status  6 report has indicated that less than 1% of lithium being recycled . Although, researcher and  economist has divided opinion on economic need to recycle and critical supply chain stability  of lithium, but to address the circular economy, steady supply chain security, self-reliance,  environment safety, environment directive, energy security, resources conservation, futuristic  carbon footprint, WEEE directives and waste crime recycling of LIB is absolutely essential .  

Several authors have reviewed recycling of lithium ion battery (LIB), mainly focused on  Co recovery or associated divalent metal like Mn, Al, and Ni recovery  . Numerous  scholarly article for recycling of LIB has been published for recycling of lithium through a  various process like; hydrometallurgy, pyrometallurgy, bio-hydrometallurgy or hybrid  processes  . Different companies around the world commercially recycling the LIB by the  13 various processes has been discussed and reviewed in our earlier review . Classification of  13 LIB recycling processes has been being discussion elsewhere in our previous review . During  last decade, various researcher and industries around the world have achieved significant  progress from the Co recovery perspective. The advanced application and projected utilization  of LIB in the PEV sector definitely challenging the futuristic recycling technology of LIB and  resources. Among the all the scholarly publications, reviews and industrial processes,  invariably Co and other valuable metals being recovered or have attracted tremendous focus.  Lithium which is considered being the green energy metal rarely has an attention for recovery  from secondary resources like LIB. Lithium from LIBs is industrially recovered only by  Toxco Inc. (now retrieval technology Inc.) and BDC Inc. using cryo-milling followed by  13 LiOH recovery in their recycling plants in Canada and USA . Considering cost-effectiveness  of the process, technological challenge and lower level of lithium content, recovery of lithium  have received very marginal attention. Cost inefficient recovery process of lithium from LIB  is the vital contributing cause for poor attention. As lithium is very reactive in air or moisture  both pyro metallurgical process as well as cryo-processes are high energy to consume and  adverse the carbon footprint interest of recycling motto. Hence, cost-effective, lower energy  intense, the greener-recycling process needed to be developed to handle the challenges  associated with next-generation lithium demand and conserve the natural resources.  Hydrometallurgy can be the panacea for the cost-effective recovery for the forgotten lithium.  This perspective article discusses the integration of suitable technology for the cost-effective  recovery of lithium from wastewater of LIB recycling industry after the Co recovery, where  the LIB recycled through the hydro metallurgical process or a part of the process. Enrichment  of lithium from wastewater by reverse osmosis (RO) followed by lithium carbonate can be the  cost effective and feasible process. Finally, integration of proposed lithium recovery process  with our earlier LIB recycling process, which has been developed for Co recovery, has been  discussion  .  

# 2. Recovery of Lithium from LIB by Reverse Osmosis and Precipitation  

From above-reviewed processes, the hydro metallurgical process for recovery of LIB can  be simplified in Figure 1. From Figure 1, it can be clearly understood that lithium being  wasted in the waste stream and mainly Co is considered as a valuable metal in  hydro metallurgical LIB recycling. The wastewater disposed of after Co recovery in the  hydro metallurgical recycling process of LIB from here onward will be termed as LIB  recycling industry wastewater. This article discusses the enrichment of lithium from LIB  industry wastewater by RO followed by a sequential precipitation process for purification and  recovery of lithium carbonate. As explained above because of energy intensive process,  pyrometallurgy process has limitation for lithium recovery. Hence, lithium can efficiently and  cost-effectively recovered by hydrometallurgy process. Figure 1 shows in the industrial  hydrometallurgy process for recycling of LIB, the lithium being discarded to wastewater  stream. Since lithium is present in wastewater stream in the LIB recycling industry, in general,  it can be recovered mainly using following stages; (i) enrichment/concentrate (ii) purification  and (iii) recovery of lithium either as elemental lithium or as the salt of lithium. Our earlier  thorough investigation and review suggest that cost-effective recovery of lithium by liquid- liquid extraction is not feasible as most of the extractants like crown ether, crown ether  derivatives, β-diketones, and β-diketones are either very expensive or have very limited  29 selectivity over other monovalent cations . Usually, the lithium-bearing wastewater is not  free from other monovalent cations, hence, the liquid-liquid extraction of lithium using as  chelating extractants is not feasible from an industrial perspective, because of the high cost of  29 extractant and selectivity of lithium over other monovalent cataions . The inappropriateness  of liquid-liquid extraction of lithium by acidic extractants such as Cyanex 272, D2EHPA,  PC  29 88A, Ionquest 801 has been reported . Scope for liquid-liquid extraction of lithium by only  solvating extractants is very limited rather lithium extraction only possible when β-diketones  are used as an extractant for synergism. Apparently, the cost of extractant, selectivity of  extractant, and lower concentration of lithium in the waste stream adverse the interest for  liquid-liquid extraction process for recovery of lithium from the waste stream. Both the  fundamental and industrial recycling/recovery research for extraction of lithium by the liquid  membrane is not a well investigated and reported, which limits the liquid membrane process  application for lithium recovery.  

From a comprehensive investigation on lithium recovery from the open literature and  patents, feasibility for cost-effective recovery of lithium from wastewater of LIB recycling  industry by hydrometallurgy has been hypothesized. Considering, available information in the  literature, the lithium recovery from LIB recycling industry wastewater can be feasible  through a generalized process simplified in the flowsheet presented in Figure 2. Enrichment  of lithium bearing wastewater can be enriched cost effectively by RO or natural evaporation  similar to brine processing. Enrichment through heat-treated evaporation could be energy  intensive and expensive. In the tropical environment, enrichment of lithium-bearing  wastewater by natural evaporation can be an option. Lithium enrichment by RO process can  be an efficient cost-effective alternative to evaporation.  

Figure 3 explains conceptual diagram for the enrichment of salts and water purification  process by RO process. As shown in figure wastewater should be fed to RO membrane  module through pumping, the RO membrane concentrates the salts in the concentrate stream  (rejected stream) and purifies the water in the permeate stream. In contrary to water  purification process by the RO process, the rejected stream with a high concentration of salt  can be used for value recovery. Hence, for the recovery of lithium from the waste stream of  LIB process, the concentrate stream of RO process can enrich the lithium. From enriched  lithium (from concentrate stream) of RO process, lithium can be purified and recovered after  further processing. The RO process can address the lithium enrichment challenge from waste  stream cost-effectively. Separation of lithium in the brine by nanofiltration and RO process  30 31 has been reported in the literature . Some membrane can reject salts up to 100% . Better is  the rejection higher would be concentrated and should be an added advantage for concentrate  for salt/lithium recovery.  

Followed by enrichment of lithium, possibly the lithium can be either purified/recovered  by the following process, i.e., (i) liquid-liquid extraction, (ii) ion-exchange, and (iii)  13 adsorption. Detail of the methods has been reviewed by elsewhere . From literature  investigation, it can be concluded that for cost-effective recovery of lithium by liquid-liquid  extraction very limited. The limitations reflected in the literature. In fact, few possible  methods can be used for recovery of lithium by liquid-liquid extraction, i.e., LIX 51–TOPO,  LIX 54–TOPO, and LIX 54-Cyanex 923 as explained by Kinugita  et al.  and Pranolo  et al,  respectively .   Considering the residual amount of divalent metal present in the wastewater  may limit the liquid-liquid extraction process as the commercial extractant LIX 51, LIX 54,  TOPO, and Cyanex 923 has extracting ability of the divalent metal that may present in the  LIB recycling industry wastewater. The important drawback of ion exchange process would  be adsorption of organic matter as the lithium-bearing wastewater stream contain a small  residual amount of solvent, higher cost and operation complexity. Hence, precipitation of  lithium followed by RO enrichment can be cost effective and effective industrial process.

# 3.1.   Feasibility of Lithium Enrichment by Reverse Osmosis  

Precisely from a scientific perspective, ionic rejection behavior of membrane and from  economic recovery perspective cost of RO membrane can justify the feasibility. Typical ionic  rejection behavior is depicted in Table 1, data adapted from the handbook of industrial  34 membrane technology by M.C. porter . Salt rejection in RO process mainly driven by the  atomic/molecular size of impurities. Comparing the atomic size of lithium with the given  table reasonably lithium enrichment by RO though appropriate membrane selection can a  feasible option. The Table 1 clearly indicates that enrichment of lithium from wastewater of  LIB is quite feasible by RO process. Current membranes have accomplished rejection rates up  to 99.8 percent, means that 99.8% of the metallic content can be concentrated in the rejected  stream of LIB recycling industry wastewater  . Quist-Jensen et al. have reported more than  99.6% of lithium can be enriched/recovered by RO system from seawater where the lithium  38 content is only 0.17 mg/L . Numerous author has reported efficient removal of heavy metal  including Co, Ni, and Mn from wastewater by RO  . Above discussion altogether justifies  the technological feasibility for lithium recovery from wastewater of LIB recycling industry.

# 3.2.   Feasibility for Precipitation of Enriched Lithium  

Followed by enrichment residual divalent metals may interfere with lithium recovery and  purity during precipitation, hence sequential precipitation divalent metal followed by lithium  carbonate can the feasible option. To understand the precipitation tendency of possible metals  (Co, Ni, Mn, and Li) in LIB recycling industry wastewater, Pourbaix diagrams has been  ® constructed using HSC Chemistry  7.1, Outotec research software, Finland. Pourbaix  diagrams in Figure 4 represents the Eh-pH behavior of Co, Ni, Mn, and Li in the pH range of  O 1 to 14 for each element concentration of 1 molality at the 25  C for an atm pressure. The  Figure 4a-c shows the residual divalent battery metals in the waste stream can be precipitated  as hydroxide in the alkaline medium around 12. Figure 4d shows lithium should be  precipitated at very strong pH at ≥ 14. Pourbaix diagrams indicate that two different distinct  pH ranges for divalent metal precipitation and lithium precipitation preferably as hydroxide.  From Pourbaix diagrams it is clear that residual divalent metals can be precipitated out at  M(OH) x  around pH 12.00, leaving lithium in the wastewater. By further managing pH around  14.00 and adding saturated solution of sodium carbonate or ammonium carbonate, lithium can  be precipitated as lithium carbonate. Hence, sequential precipitation of residual divalent  metals followed by lithium precipitation it quite feasible without any interference. Table 2  depicts solubility of various lithium salt (Li 2 CO 3,  LiOH.H 2 O, LiCl, Li 2 SO 4 ) as a function of  + temperature and minimum Li  content required for precipitation of lithium as their respective  54 + salt . The minimum Li  content required for precipitation of lithium as salt were calculated  from solubility data of respective salts presented the Table 2. Possible precipitation of lithium  carbonate can be justified from the Table 2. The same table can answer why lithium carbonate  could be a cost-effective recovery process. Table 2 compares minimum concentration  required to precipitate out lithium as carbonate, hydroxide, chloride, and sulfate using  respective anions. From the table, it can be clearly observed that, to precipitate out lithium as  + O O carbonate minimum 1.45 g/L and 0.68 g/L of Li  are required at 25  C and 100  C,  respectively, which is the lowest concentration of all the lithium salts. Hence, lithium  recovery as lithium carbonate by precipitation can be relatively simple and cost effective  which add simplicity to the total recovery process.

# 4. Conclusions:  

From the hypothesis, theoretical analysis and literature it can reasonably be concluded that  cost-effective recovery of lithium by RO enrichment followed by sequential precipitation  from the wastewater of hydro metallurgical LIB recycling process is quite feasible.  Combination both cost-effective processes, i.e., RO and lithium carbonate precipitation for  recovery of lithium from LIB recycling industry wastewater can be an environment-friendly  novel process. The process can be integrated to existing process to recover lithium with  minimal facilitates. By integrating the proposed lithium recovery process with our earlier LIB  cathode material recycling process a total close loop hydro metallurgical process can be  developed.  The proposed process uses neither hazardous chemical, not expensive chemicals,  hence process can simultaneously address issues like the recycling of LIB with zero waste  disposal, urban mining, circular economy, and resources conservation.
