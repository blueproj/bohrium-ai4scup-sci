# ABSTRACT  

This paper reports the microstructural evolution and phase stability in a newly developed low- density Al 10 Nb 15 Ta 5 Ti 30 Zr 40  refractory high entropy alloy (RHEA) at different temperatures.  This alloy composition was adapted from the composition of the B2 phase in a two-phase  B2+BCC mixture at 1000°C in the refractory high entropy superalloy Al 0.25 NbTaTiZr. After  homogenizing at a high-temperature, followed by fast or slow cooling to room temperature, this  alloy exhibited a nano-scale mixture of co-continuous BCC and B2 phases, resembling a  spinodally decomposed microstructure with concurrent ordering. Interestingly, this novel nano- scale BCC+B2 microstructure exhibits excellent room temperature compressive yield strength  (\~1075MPa) and ductility (true strain at failure \~ 0.55). Annealing at 600°C and 750°C resulted  in the formation of additional ordered omega type AlZr 2  phase in this alloy. The experimentally  observed phase evolution is in fair agreement with CALPHAD predictions.  

Keywords:  Refractory alloy; high entropy alloy; microstructure; phase composition.  

# 1. INTRODUCTION  

Current Ni-base superalloys have a unique combination of properties at elevated temperatures.  However, their high density and low melting points (\~1300°C) limit their maximum operating  temperature. This demands for development of alloys with higher melting points in order to  increase the maximum operating temperatures. Alloys such as Co-Re or Co-Al-W [1], or two  phase FCC+L1 2  refractory superalloys [2, 3] based on platinum group metals (Pt, Rh, Ru or Ir)  have been explored in order to replace the conventional superalloys, however, their high density  and/or high cost usually limit their applications.  

High entropy alloys (HEAs), were originally proposed as a new paradigm in alloying, based on  the concept of using several alloying elements in near equi-atomic ratios to achieve single phase  solid solutions by exploiting the concept of decreasing Gibbs free energy by increasing the  entropy of mixing above \~1.5R, where R is the gas constant [4]. More recently it has been  established that HEAs often exhibit ordered intermetallic phases, due to the negative enthalpy of  mixing of certain constituent alloying elements [4-10]. Following this concept, HEAs based on  refractory elements, also referred to as refractory HEAs or RHEAs, are being developed for high  temperature applications, exploiting the largely unexplored phase space that exists in the central  regions of complex multi-component systems [11]. Many RHEAs, potentially with superior  elevated temperature mechanical properties as compared to Ni-base superalloys, have been  recently reported [7, 11-14]. Elements from group IV, group V and group VI, with minor  additions of Al, Si, Ni or Co have been used in developing these alloys. These RHEAs typically  have a body centered cubic (BCC) based crystal structure and may also contain secondary phases  such as B2, HCP or Laves [7, 11]. One of the more interesting types of RHEAs are those that  exhibit two coherent BCC and B2 phases, whose microstructure resembles that of Ni-base  superalloys [13-18]. However, unlike Ni-base superalloys, in these superalloy-like RHEAs, the  disordered BCC phase is usually present as nanometer-sized cuboidal precipitates dispersed  within a continuous ordered B2 matrix. While many of these two-phase B2 + BCC RHEAs  exhibit attractive elevated temperature strength and ductility, most of them are brittle or have  limited ductility at room temperature (RT). The poor RT ductility has often been attributed to the  continuous ordered B2 matrix. Detailed understanding of the thermodynamic and kinetic aspects  of phase transformations in these RHEAs is essential for identifying the alloy compositions with  required combinations of the mechanical properties in the temperature range of expected use, and  such studies are currently in progress. The ordered intermetallic B2 phase in these two-phase B2 

 + BCC RHEAs, typically contains four to five alloying elements in significant concentrations 

 [13, 15-18] leading to complex site occupancies on the two sub-lattices of the B2 structure [19].  Therefore, there is a need for investigating the structure and mechanical properties of such  compositionally complex B2 phases. Due to the massive increase in HEA research, several  multi-principal element intermetallics with a single-phase B2 crystal structure , such as  AlMo 0.5 NbTa 0.5 TiZr 0.5  [15], AlNbTa 0.5 TiZr 0.5  [15], AlCoFeNi [20] and AlCrVTi [21], have  recently been reported. The AlMo 0.5 NbTa 0.5 TiZr 0.5  and AlNbTa 0.5 TiZr 0.5  alloys have high RT  micro-hardness of 620 and 500 Hv, respectively, but suffer from limited ductility. The B2 crystal  structure is retained at 1000°C for both these alloys, and they exhibit good malleability and high  yield strength of 935 MPa and 535 MPa, respectively at this temperature [15]. Choudhary et al.  [24] showed that AlCoFeNi has a single phase B2 microstructure at T ≤ 1000°C and a high RT  hardness of 450 Hv at RT. AlCrTiV has RT hardness of 498 Hv and a single phase B2 structure  stable up to 723°C. Unfortunately, all these multi-component, single-phase B2 alloys have low  ductility at RT. A first principles study conducted by A. Pathak [22] on Ti-Al-X based B2  systems, reveals that addition of elements such as Mo, Nb, Ta, Zr, can reduce the directionality  of bonding and thus making the B2 phase more ductile, based on shear modulus to bulk modulus  ratios (G/B). Recently, Yang et. al. [23]  produced a  Al 0.25 Ti 0.25 CoFeNi HEA containing high  density of multi-component L1 2  precipitates in an FCC matrix, exhibiting an exceptional  combination of high tensile strength (yield strength of 1027 MPa, ultimate tensile strength of  1467 MPa) and large tensile ductility (\~50%). They also prepared a single-phase L1 2  alloy, based  on the composition of the L1 2  phase in their two-phase FCC + L1 2  mixture, and this L12 alloy  exhibited excellent compressive yield strength and ductility at RT. Such unique mechanical  behavior indicates the potential of enhanced ductility in multi-principal element intermetallic  phases.  

This paper is focused on the design, phase stability, and microstructural evolution in a newly  developed low-density RHEA. This new alloy is based on the composition of B2 phase in a two- phase BCC+B2 mixture stable at 1000°C, in a previously reported RHEA (Al 0.25 NbTaTiZr) [13,  15, 16].  The composition of B2 phase within this two-phase BCC+B2 microstructure after  annealing at 1000°C/24 hrs, was measured using atom probe tomography (APT) and was found  to be 10Al-15Nb-5Ta-30Ti-40Zr (at%) (Supplementary Figure S1). This composition was  melted separately and a new RHEA was processed. Equilibrating this new alloy at 1100°C for  24hrs followed by quenching, resulted in a microstructure consisting of nano-domains of BCC  and B2 phases in a co-continuous mixture. This refined two-phase microstructure resulted in an  excellent combination of room temperature compressive ductility and strength. Therefore, this  paper focuses on the phase stability and microstructural evolution in this newly developed 10Al- 15Nb-5Ta-30Ti-40Zr low density RHEA.  

# 2. MATERIALS AND METHODS  

The alloy was prepared by vacuum induction melting of high purity elements followed by  suction casting in rods of \~13 mm in diameter and 55 mm in length. The chemical composition  of the alloy measured using SEM/EDS and EPMA is given in Table 1. The microstructure and  properties of the alloy were studied in the as-cast (Cast) condition, annealed and slow cooled  (A+SC) condition and annealed and water quenched (A+WQ) condition. To produce A+SC  condition, cast samples were wrapped in a Ta foil and placed in a vacuum furnace. After the  vacuum of 2x10 -3  Pa was reached, the samples were heated to a temperature of 1100°C at the  heating rate of 50°C/min, held at the temperature for 24 hours and cooled at the cooling rate of  20°C/min or furnace cooling, whichever is slower. During the heat treatment, vacuum in the  furnace continuously increased and reached 1x10 -4  Pa by the end of the treatment. To produce  A+WQ condition, a cast rod was placed in a quartz tube, which was vacuumed, filled with high  purity argon and fire-sealed. The tube with the sample was then placed in a furnace preheated to  1100°C, held at the temperature for 24 h and then water quenched.  

X-ray diffraction patterns were taken from a flat, polished cross-sections of the annealed  specimens using a Bruker D2 Phaser diffractometer and Cu Ka radiation. The measurement error  in the lattice parameters reported in this manuscript using XRD is ±0.05 pm. Microstructure and  chemical composition were studied using scanning electron microscopy (SEM), electron probe  micro-analysis (EPMA), transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and atom probe tomography  (APT) methods. A FEI-TITAN G2 aberration-corrected (C s  correction for condenser lens) TEM  microscope (FEI, Hillsboro, Oregon) operating at 300 kV in the HAADF STEM mode, was used  for capturing the high-resolution images. Density of the alloy was measured using a helium  pycnometer and was determined to be 6.49±0.02 g/cm 3 . Differential thermal analysis (DTA) was  performed using SDT Q600 V8.3 Build 101, DSC-TGA standard module. A sample size of  233.535mg was placed in an alumina crucible and heated at a rate of 20°C/min in an inert  atmosphere (argon) from 25°C up to 1480°C.  

Atom probe tomography (CAMECA LEAP 5000XS) was used to determine the local element  composition of the alloy. The APT experiment was carried out at 40 K, using a voltage  evaporation mode at a rate of 0.5%. The pulsing voltage maintained to 20% of the steady state  voltage at a frequency of 200 kHz. Raw data from the atom probe experiments were analyzed  using integrated visualization and analysis software Cameca’s IVAS 3.6.8 (Cameca, Madison,  Wisconsin). A cluster analysis was used in APT to determine whether any compositional  partitioning has occurred in the alloy.  

The details of data mining technique of cluster finding can be found elsewhere [24], and the  IVAS TM  3.6.8 manual can be referred for a step by step procedure. Cluster analysis was  performed to identify regions where the distance between two atoms (d max ) is smaller compared  to other regions in the dataset, for chosen atomic species. By iteration, the values of d max  and  N min  (minimum number of atoms in a cluster) were optimized to maximize the detection of  compositional clustering. The regions with interatomic distances smaller than the optimized d max and N min  vales were subtracted from the total dataset and represented in a pictorial form in 3- dimension.  

Compression tests of rectangular samples with dimensions 4.6 mm x 4.6 mm x 7.4 mm were  conducted at room temperature using a constant ram speed of 0.0074 mm/s, which corresponded  to the initial strain rate of 0.001 s -1 . The true stress vs. true strain curves were recorded using  synchronized analogue signals from the load cell and digital image correlation (DIC) system,  respectively.  

The Vickers microhardness was measured using a diamond pyramid applied for 15 seconds  under the load of 500 g - 1000 g. The average value of 10 measurements for each tested  condition is reported.

# 3. RESULTS  

The cast alloy  has a typical dendritic structure (Figure 1a). The high-melting point elements Nb  and Ta are located preferably inside the dendrite arms (Figure 1b and Figure 1c), lower melting  point elements Al and Zr are concentrated inside inter-dendritic regions (Figure 1d and Figure  1e), and Ti is distributed almost evenly between dendritic and inter-dendritic regions. Due to the  elemental partitioning resulting in different average Z-values, the dendritic regions exhibit a  brighter contrast and interdendritic regions exhibit a darker contrast in the backscatter SEM  images. Using the whole gray color spectrum, the area fraction of the dendrites is estimated to be  68±7%. X-ray diffraction from the as-cast alloy reveals the presence of two BCC phases with  very similar lattice parameters:  a  = 340.2 pm for one (BCC1) phase and  a  = 341.9 pm for the  other (BCC2) phase (Figure 2a,b). Analysis of the area intensity of the adjacent peaks, like  shown in Figure 2b, allows estimation of the volume fraction of the BCC1 and BCC2 phases to  be 60% and 40%, respectively.  

After annealing at 1100°C for 24 hours and water quenching , the dendritic structure disappears  and the alloy has a coarse-grained structure with the average grain size of  >1 mm (Figure 3a).  Figure 3b shows a dark field TEM image taken from a (010) B2 superlattice reflection in the  [001] BCC zone axis, the selected area diffraction pattern (SADP) is shown in inset. The dark- field image shows a grainy contrast indicating the presence of high density of nanometer-sized  ordered B2 domains. Further careful analysis using high resolution HAADF-STEM in an  aberration-corrected TEM, revealed that both the ordered B2 and disordered BCC phases form a  coherent co-continuous mixture (at the nanometer scale) (Figure 4a). The Z-contrast in the  HAADF-STEM images indicates a two-dimensional sectioning through a complex co- continuous 3D network with interwoven BCC and B2 phases. This co-continuous microstructure  is possibly an indication of early stages of spinodal decomposition within a disordered BCC  phase, followed by B2 ordering within one of the phase separated regions. While most of the  darker regions appear to exhibit the ordered B2 structure, the overlap of nanometer scale BCC  and B2 regions due to the co-continuous domains of both phases, makes it difficult to discern  this ordering in all regions of the image. A higher magnification image of a small area from  Figure 4a is shown in Figure 4b, and the interface between BCC and B2 phases is marked by  blue lines. The corresponding intensity profile along one of the <011> directions (marked by  yellow arrow) across the BCC-B2 interface is shown in Figure 4c. Lower intensity of center  atoms in the B2 phase, compared to equal intensities in all atomic positions of BCC phase, is due  to the difference in site occupancy in the B2 lattice, caused by chemical ordering. The APT  reconstruction with Al ions, shown in Figure 5a, exhibits no easily observable partitioning  between the BCC and B2 phases. However, cluster analysis on a smaller cuboidal volume  (20x20x25 nm 3 ) highlighted by a rectangle in Figure 5a, reveals the presence of Al-Zr rich  clusters. A pictorial representation of the clusters is shown in Figure 5b, along with the  optimized parameters for cluster analysis in the table. The optimization procedure for choosing  these cluster-analysis parameters is described in the experimental section. Note that, since the  microstructure is co-continuous in nature, one cannot expect discrete clusters of either phase,  and, therefore, this analysis was performed just to confirm if any compositional partitioning has  occurred between the two phases. The average composition of the clusters is 15.2±3.3Al –  19.9±3.5Ti – 56.3±4.2Zr – 2.2±1.2Ta – 6.4±2.0Nb (at%). The remaining matrix in the volume  probed for cluster analysis is found to be richer in Nb, Ta and Ti. Again, note that these  compositions are not the accurate representations of the two phases at equilibrium, since at early  stages of decomposition, the interface between the decomposing phases is rather diffuse, and  therefore it is nearly impossible to define the extent of each phase.  

In addition, X-ray diffraction analysis of the quenched sample shows the presence of two BCC  phases with slightly different (by \~0.53%) lattice parameters (Figure 2c). One phase, BCC1, with  a  = 339.3 pm, has relatively sharp and more intense (by height) diffraction peaks than the other  phase, BCC2, with  a  = 341.1 pm, for which diffraction peaks are wide and shallow (Figure 2d).  From the relative ratios of the area intensities of adjacent diffraction peaks the volume fractions  of the BCC1 and BCC2 phases are estimated to be 33% and 67%, respectively, in the annealed  and water quenched sample. It can be seen that after homogenization at 1100°C, the lattice  parameter of both BCC phases slightly decreases and the volume fraction of the BCC2 phase  becomes larger than in the as-cast condition. These changes are likely due to more homogeneous  distribution of the alloying elements in the high-temperature BCC phase. The difference in the  lattice parameters is probably related to the combined effect of ordering and compositional  differences between the B2 and BCC phases.  

After annealing at 1100°C for 24 hours and slow cooling,  the microstructure of the alloy appears  similar to the water quenched condition. In particular, large single-phase grains, with no  evidence of second-phase precipitates, are visible in the SEM image (Figure 6a). HAADF STEM  (Figure 6b) and SADPs (Figure 6c-f) indicate the presence of a two-phase B2+BCC  microstructure. A dark field image taken from a B2 superlattice reflection is shown in Figure 6g.  While it is difficult to discern the exact morphology of the B2 and BCC phases based on this  dark-field image, it is possible that the phases may be co-continuous as observed in case of the  WQ sample. The fine streaks seen in between the primary reflections in the [101], [1   3] and  [102] BCC zone axes diffraction patterns can result from reciprocal lattice streaking (RLS)  arising due to shearing or shuffle-based instabilities in the BCC lattice [25, 26]. These inherent  instabilities, when they grow, can lead to different manifestations, such as discrete omega  precipitates, low-temperature HCP phase, or other phases. However, as such the RLS does not  correspond to well-developed precipitates, and consequently dark-field imaging from the streak  intersections do not reveal discrete precipitates. These streaks also do not correspond to any new  phases. X-ray diffraction pattern from the slow cooled sample reveals sharp intensity diffraction  peaks from a BCC phase with lattice parameter  a  = 331.7 pm and a B2 phase with lattice  parameter a = 340.2 pm (Figure 6h). Some minor diffraction peaks (not shown in figure)  observed may belong to a hexagonal omega-like phase with the lattice parameters  a = 470.1 pm  and  c  = 259.4 pm, which is in agreement with very faint superlattice spots at 1/3 (121) and 2/3  (121) type reflections associated with the omega-like phase (Figure 6e).  

The annealed at 1100°C and water quenched samples were additionally annealed at 600°C for  72 hours or at 750°C for 24 hours.  The microstructure of the 600°C-annealed sample is shown in  Figure 7a. Partial decomposition of the matrix phase in two phases occurred during annealing.  The new phases have distinct Z contrast (one phase is bright and another phase is dark, while the  matrix has gray color on the BSE images) and are precipitated along grain boundaries and inside  grains in the form of \~1  μ m diameter particles consisting of the two phases (Figure 7a). The  bright phase particles are always bordering with the dark phase, while some dark-phase particles  can be present separately from the bright phase (inset of Figure 7a). The volume fractions of the  bright and dark phases are estimated (using gray spectrum analysis) to be 20% and 17%,  respectively. The chemical composition of these phases was measured using SEM/EDS, and is  given in Table 2. It can be seen that, relative to the alloy composition, the bright phase is rich in  Nb, Ta and Ti, the dark phase is rich in Al and Zr, and the matrix phase is rich in Ti. Upon  annealing at 750 0 C, only ellipsoidal dark phase precipitates are visible at the grain boundaries  and also inside the matrix grains (Figure 7b). The volume fraction of these dark phase  precipitates was estimated to be \~2%. These particles are rich in Al and Zr and their  composition, along with the composition of the matrix phase, is given in Table 3. After annealing  at 750°C, both the dark phase and the matrix are richer in Al and Zr than after annealing at  600°C. Additionally, it should be noted that the white phase, visible after the 600°C annealing, is  not stable at 750°C.  

Figure 7c,d show x-ray diffraction patterns from the samples annealed at 600°C (c) and 750°C  (d). Diffraction peaks from five phases, BCC1 with the lattice parameter a = 339.4 pm, BCC/B2  with a = 341.8 pm, BCC3 with a= 331.3 pm, BCC4 with a= 337.7 pm and a hexagonal phase  with a = 486.0 pm and c = 589.9 pm are identified on the X-ray diffraction pattern from the  600°C annealed sample. Four distinct phases, BCC1 with a = 338.3 pm, BCC2 with a = 338.9  pm, Al 2 Zr 3  with a = 715.8 pm, c = 618.1 pm, and, AlZr 2  with a = 499.2 pm, c = 581.6 pm are  identified in the 750°C annealed sample. Based on the composition of dark ellipsoidal  precipitates from SEM/EDS (Table 3) for the 750°C annealed sample, one may conclude that  this phase is likely AlZr 2  (PDF 48-1378) phase observed in the XRD analysis. Accordingly, the  dark ellipsoid-shaped particles present on the BSE images of the 600°C annealed sample (Figure  7a) also represent the hexagonal, AlZr 2  phase with only slightly different lattice parameters than  that observed at 750°C. The BCC1 and BCC2 phases in the 600°C annealed sample have almost  the same lattice parameters as the respective BCC phases in the Cast and WQ conditions (Figure  2), as well as the matrix phase in the 750°C annealed sample. Therefore, they are likely represent  the spinodally decomposed matrix. The BCC3 phase has the smallest lattice parameter, which is  an indication of high enrichment in Nb and Ta; therefore, this phase is shown as bright particles  in Figure 7a. Finally, the BCC4 phase is likely an ordered B2 phase observed together with the  AlZr 2  phase inside the dark particles (see below).  

A detailed TEM examination of (A+WQ)-600 condition was carried out to specifically determine  the crystal structure, morphology, and spatial distribution of different phases. The results are  shown in Figure 8. Note that STEM image (Figure 8a), dominated by Z-contrast, and is  consistent with the BSE SEM image showing three different contrasts: grey matrix, dark and  bright precipitates. However, the SADPs (Figures 8b-g) from different regions indicate the  presence of at least four different phases in this microstructure. The grey matrix in the studied  area clearly exhibits a disordered BCC crystal structure (refer Figure 8b) (no superlattice  reflections are seen), which may indicate disordering of the initially (in the WQ condition)  ordered BCC2 phase. Similarly, the bright contrast particles in STEM images also exhibit  diffraction patterns from a disordered BCC structure, which supports our earlier suggestion that  this is the BCC3 phase. The SADPs from different dark contrast precipitates (in STEM image)  revealed that some of them are consistent with the B2 crystal structure, while other patterns can  be indexed as a hexagonal, ω-like AlZr 2  phase (Figure 8f,g). The SADP from one such dark  contrast region is shown in Figure 8e, where the superlattice reflections in the {001} positions of  the [1-10] ZA of the BCC structure conclusively show the B2 ordering. At the same time, the  SADP, shown in Figure 8f and collected from another dark region, displays a hexagonal  symmetry, which is unambiguously indexed as [11-20] zone axis of the hexagonal crystal. Note  the [11-20] zone SADP is acquired only from one ω precipitate and thus reflections form one  variant. The dark field image captured using (001) B2 superlattice reflections lights up some of  the dark-color phases (Figure 8h), whereas few dark precipitates were lighted up in the dark field  image acquired from only two variants (encircled with yellow color in Figure 8g) of the ordered  omega phase. Formation of ordered omega by the collapse of {111} planes of B2 has been  observed previously [27, 28], similar to the formation of disordered omega due to collapse of  {111} planes of a BCC crystal structure in Ti alloys [25, 26]. Due to the inherent chemical  ordering in the BCC lattice in a B2 structure, the (0001) planes of the hexagonal phase get  ordered to form an ordered omega phase. The ordered omega phase was confirmed to have a B8 2   structure (P6 3 /mmc). The orientation relationship of BCC/B2 and   /ordered    can be seen in  Figure 8g, and is as follows: (111) B2  || (0001)   and [1-10] B2  || [11-20]  , which produces four  rotational variants. Based on the SEM, TEM, and XRD analyses, it can be said that five different  phases are present in the 600°C annealed sample. These are disordered BCC1 and BCC2 with  only slightly different compositions representing partially spinodal decomposed matrix,  disordered BCC3 (rich in Nb and Ta), ordered BCC4 (rich in Al, Ti and Zr) and the hexagonal  AlZr 2  phase (rich in Al and Zr). The disordered BCC1 and BCC2 phases have the lattice  parameters similar to of the BCC1 and B2 phases in the WQ condition, which may indicate that  the order-disorder transformation (B2 to BCC2) occurs upon heating and annealing at 600°C. At  the same time, precipitation of the BCC3 phase, which is highly enriched in Nb and Ta, as well  as still limited diffusion of the alloying elements, result in the nearest regions to become highly  enriched in Al, Ti and Zr thus retaining B2 ordering. However, at this temperature the B2 phase  is likely metastable and it eventually transforms to the ordered hexagonal AlZr 2  phase.

Differential thermal analysis (DTA) was carried out on the SC condition for a temperature range  of 25°C - 1480°C, and the heating curve (rate of 20°C/min) is shown in Figure 9. One  exothermic reaction with a peak near 583°C, and one endothermic reaction with a peak near  752°C, can be clearly identified. The microhardness values of as-cast, WQ, SC and WQ followed by 600°C for 72 hrs and 750°C  for 24 hrs are: 329.4±5 HV, 328±6 HV, 327±5 HV, 337±9 HV and 329±4 HV respectively. The  hardness value of SC and WQ appear to be the same, likely because of no significant difference  in their microstructure. Moreover, the differences between the lattice parameters of both the  phases, in SC and WQ, are similar. The sample annealed at 600°C shows a slightly higher  (by\~10 Hv) hardness probably due to precipitation of additional AlZr 2  phase with a significant  fraction. Very small fraction (2%) of the AlZr 2  phase in case of samples annealed at 750°C  appeared to be the only difference as compared to the SC or WQ, and thus had a similar hardness  value. The measured yield stress at RT  for SC and WQ samples are 1098 MPa and 1049 MPa respectively. The alloy in both the  conditions exhibited good ductility with no evidence of cracking during the deformation.  

# 4. DISCUSSION  

In the as-cast condition, the alloy has noticeable elemental partitioning between the dendritic and  interdendritic regions, which is eventually due to different melting points of the alloying  elements and is typical of many refractory RCCAs [11]. Indeed, the dendritic regions, which  solidified first, are enriched in higher melting temperature Nb and Ta, while interdendritic  regions, which solidified last, are enriched in lower melting point Al and Zr. Only Ti is  distributed almost evenly in the entire volume, although its melting temperature is lower than  that of Zr. Accordingly, two BCC phases identified from the analysis of the respective X-ray  diffraction pattern are likely represent the Nb-Ta-rich and Al-Zr-rich regions. Comparable values  for the Nb-Ta rich dendrite area fraction (68±7%) estimated from BSE images and the volume  fraction of the BCC1 phase (\~60%) estimated from XRD seem to support the assumption that  BCC1 is Nb-Ta rich and BCC2 is Al-Zr rich. Very different atomic radii of Al and Zr cause  higher lattice distortions in the BCC2 phase than similar atomic radii of Nb and Ta in the BCC1  phase, which explains why the diffraction peaks from the BCC1 phase are narrower than from  the BCC2 phase (Figure 2b). The non-equilibrium solidification model by Scheil predicts micro- segregation of the alloying elements in accordance to the experimental data; viz. the solid phase  solidified at higher temperatures (closer to liquidus) is rich in Nb and Ta while that solidified at  lower temperatures (closer to solidus) is rich in Al and Zr (Figure 11a). However, the non- equilibrium solidification model predicts formation of only one BCC phase after solidification. It  is possible that the second BCC phase, presumably the one rich in Al and Zr, forms during  further cooling in the solid state due to the favorable composition in the interdendritic regions.  

Figure 11b shows the fraction of equilibrium phases at different temperatures in  Al 10 Nb 15 Ta 5 Ti 30 Zr 40  alloy calculated using the Pandat-2018 software and Pan_Nb2018a  database. Five equilibrium phases are predicted in the solid state. These are BCC1, BCC2 and  HCP solid solution phases and Al 2 Zr 3  and AlZr 2 -based compounds with ordered hexagonal  structures. BCC1 forms during solidification and is present as a single phase at temperatures  from 1052°C to 1598°C. The solvus temperatures for the BCC2, Al 2 Zr 3 , AlZr 2  and HCP phases  are 1052°C, 842°C, 791°C and 565°C, respectively. The Al 2 Zr 3  is stable only above 791°C and  at lower T it transforms to AlZr 2 .  

The phase prediction is in close agreement with the reported experimental data for the annealed  conditions. The thermodynamic prediction of a single BCC phase at 1100°C is consistent with  very large grains of the high-temperature, single-phase BCC structure observed in the WQ or SC  conditions (Figure 3a, Figure 6a). The observed co-continuous two-phase microstructure with  BCC and B2 phases is probably a result of decomposition during cooling, at lower temperatures.  Since the solvus of BCC2 is closer to the annealing temperature, it was likely to phase separate  into two phases (BCC1 and BCC2) during quenching or slow-cooling. The early stage of an  omega-like phase can be observed in the SADP from the SC condition, shown in Figure 6e, with  spots at the 1/3 and 2/3 of (112)BCC locations, and can be attributed to the stability of the  hexagonal phases (AlZr 2  and hcp) at lower temperatures as predicted by CALPHAD. This  comparative analysis of the observed phases in the WQ and SC conditions and the equilibrium  phases predicted by CALPHAD indicate that both WQ and SC conditions are non-equilibrium at  room temperature.  

According to the calculated equilibrium phase diagram, three phases, BCC1, BCC2 and AlZr 2   are present at 600°C with volume fractions of 65%, 16% and 19%, respectively. The CALPHAD  calculated compositions of these phases at 600°C are given in Table . According to these  calculations, the BCC1 phase is rich in Ti, BCC2 is rich in Nb and Ta, and AlZr 2  is rich in Al  and Zr (relative to the average alloy composition). These calculated results are in good  agreement with the respective measured data for the 600°C annealed sample (Table ). Comparing  the experimental and calculated data one can conclude that the calculated BCC1 phase represents  the matrix (i.e. the mixture of BCC1 and BCC2 phases identified by XRD), calculated BCC2  phase represents the bright particles (i.e. experimentally identified BCC3 phase) and the  calculated AlZr 2  phase represents dark particles (i.e. the experimentally identified mixture of B2  and AlZr 2  phases). The presence of additional phases during experimental observation may  indicate metastable condition or some lack in CALPHAD calculations. For example, CALPHAD  method does not have capability of prediction of multi-component ordered B2 phase, which is  likely a precursor for the AlZr 2  phase in the studied alloy.  

The CALPHAD calculations do not appear to predict the experimental observations at 750°C. In  particular three phases, BCC1, BCC2 and AlZr 2 , with volume fractions of \~80%,11% and 9%,  respectively, are predicted at 750°C (Figure 11b), although only the BCC matrix phase and an  ellipsoid-shaped AlZr 2  phase, with the volume fraction of \~2%, are identified on the SEM  images after annealing at 750°C for 24 hours (Figure 7b). XRD identified additional peaks with  weak intensities from Al 2 Zr 3  phase which could be a metastable phase at this temperature. The  composition of the three phases predicted by CALPHAD at 750°C are given in Table 5. These  results suggest that the solvus temperature for the BCC2 phase lies somewhere between 600°C  and 750°C and the solvus temperature for the AlZr 2  is only slightly higher than 750°C.  

The occurrence of an exothermic peak near 583°C (Figure 9) can be attributed to the  precipitation of AlZr 2 /B2 phases and the decomposition of the matrix BCC phase into BCC1  (rich in Ti) and BCC2 (rich in Nb and Ta) phases. This agrees with our experimental observation  in the sample annealed at 600°C for 72 hrs, and also with the stability of AlZr 2  phase predicted  by the CALPHAD calculations. Subsequently, the dissolution of all the secondary phases with  increasing temperature can be associated with the endothermic peak observed near 752°C. The  exothermic reaction at 790°C is likely the end of the endothermic reaction with a peak near  752°C. The reasons for the low temperature endothermic reaction at \~100°C and the change in  slope at \~288°C, are still unknown. Disordering of the nano-domains of B2 phase could possibly  be one reason for the endothermic peak at \~100°C. No melting was observed up to 1480°C,  rather, an exothermic reaction was seen at \~1400°C. The peak associated with this exothermic  reaction can be an indication of oxidation, however, there was no detectable mass gain/change in  the sample.

The formation of a co-continuous two-phase microstructure with BCC and B2 phases, upon  water quenching, is an indicative of an early stage of spinodal decomposition from a high  temperature single phase field. Small compositional fluctuations in the high temperature phase  and subsequent chemical ordering of one of the phases is likely to be the reason for this  microstructure. The absence of visible XRD B2 super- reflections, in as-cast and WQ samples, is  not surprising and could be due to weak ordering, as well as the presence of large grains, hence  inducing potential texture effects. Moreover, as shown recently in the Ta-Nb-Mo-Cr-Ti-Al  system [19], the absence of typical B2 XRD peaks might not reflect the absence of order.  

Following the degree of order of the B2 phase, i.e. elemental site occupancy, diffraction intensity  of specific B2 peaks could be weak and potentially lost in the XRD background noise, even if  order is present in the alloy. Since the B2 phase observed after annealing at 600°C/72 hrs is rich  in Al and Zr, it can be said that during annealing, this phase rejects Nb, Ta and Ti into the BCC  matrix. The simultaneous decomposition of the BCC matrix into BCC1 and BCC2 can be due to  the presence of strong miscibility gaps in the binary systems such as Ta-Zr or Nb-Zr.  Simultaneously, the reduction of free electron density of the B2 phase, due to increase in Al and  Zr elements, is expected to promote the formation of ordered omega phase [29]. Therefore, the  ordered omega phase fraction, relative to the B2 phase, is expected to increase with annealing  time until it reaches the equilibrium composition. The dark phase observed after annealing at  750°C is likely to have a higher fraction of the ordered omega-like phase since it is richer in Al  and Zr as compared to the dark contrast phase in the samples annealed at 600°C. The alloy in  both WQ and SC conditions exhibit ductile behavior and have almost identical yield strength  values. This is in agreement with the similar hardness values observed for these conditions.  Moreover, the microstructure in both the heat treatment conditions were similar, except signature  of omega phase in the SC condition which could be the reason for a \~50 MPa higher strength as  compared to the WQ sample.  

# 5. SUMMARY AND CONCLUSIONS  

A novel low-density RHEA has been developed with the composition Al 10 Nb 15 Ta 5 Ti 30 Zr 40 ,  exhibiting a unique nano-scale two-phase mixture of ordered B2 and disordered BCC. The nano- scale B2 + BCC mixture appears to be co-continuous in nature based on aberration-corrected  HAADF-STEM imaging at atomic resolution, and also exhibits far-from equilibrium phase  compositions as revealed from APT analysis. This indicates that the two-phase B2 + BCC  mixture is presumably a result of a concomitant or synergistic spinodal decomposition coupled  with chemical ordering occurring during the continuous cooling (water-quenching, WQ, or slow- cooling, SC, in the furnace) from high temperature. Additionally, preliminary compression  testing at room temperature revealed that this nano-scale B2 + BCC mixture exhibits a high yield  strength (\~1075 MPa) while maintaining very good compressive ductility (true strain at failure \~  0.55). Annealing at temperatures such as 600°C and 750°C results in the formation of Al x Zr y   type intermetallic compounds, consistent with the CALPHAD predictions. The 600°C annealed  sample exhibited precipitation of the hexagonal AlZr 2  phase with a B8 2  structure similar to an  ordered omega phase. The remaining matrix also decomposes into two BCC phases, one rich in  Nb and Ta, whereas other richer in Ti. Upon annealing at 750°C, the decomposition of matrix  was not observed likely due to absence of a miscibility gap at this higher temperature. The co- continuous microstructure of BCC + B2 phases in the WQ sample exhibited the lowest hardness.  Minor fractions of additional phases, such as omega phase in SC condition and AlZr 2  phase in  750°C annealed condition, had negligible effect on the hardness of the alloy. The 600°C  decomposed microstructure exhibited a higher hardness as compared to other conditions because  of the significant fraction and finer distribution of the BCC2 and AlZr 2  phases in the BCC1  matrix.
