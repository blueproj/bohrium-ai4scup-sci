# Significant decrease in interfacial energy of grain boundary through serrated grain boundary transition  

Hyun Uk Hong ab \* , Hi Won Jeong a , In Soo Kim a , Baig Gyu Choi a , a   a Young Soo Yoo and Chang Yong Jo  

a High Temperature Materials Research Group, Korea Institute of Materials Science, 797 b Changwondaero, Changwon, Gyeongnam 641-831, Republic of Korea;   Department of Metallurgy and Advanced Materials Engineering, Changwon National University, 20 Changwondaehak-ro, Uichang-gu, Changwon, Gyeongnam 641-773, Republic of Korea  

( Received 18 July 2011; final version received 6 March 2012 )  

The mechanism of serrated grain boundary formation and its effect on liquation behaviour have been studied in a wrought nickel-based superalloy – Alloy 263. It was newly discovered that grain boundaries are considerably serrated in the absence of     0 -phase or M 23 C 6  at the grain boundaries. An electron energy-loss spectroscopy study suggests that serration is triggered by the discontinuous segregation of C and Cr atoms at grain boundaries for the purpose of relieving the excessive elastic strain energy. The grain boundaries serrate to have specific segments approaching one {111} low-index plane at a boundary so that the interfacial free energy of the grain boundary can be decreased, which may be responsible for the driving force of the serration. The serrated grain boundaries effectively suppress grain coarsening and are highly resistant to liquation due to their lower wettability resulting from a lower interfacial energy of the grain boundary.  

Keywords:  Ni-based superalloys; grain boundary serration; grain boundary energy; grain boundary segregation; wetting  

# 1. Introduction  

It has been reported that serrated grain boundaries (GBs) can be formed in nickel- based superalloys produced by investment cast [1–3], powder processing [4–6] and forging [2,3,6–9] methods when controlled cooling treatments are employed. The GB serration in these alloys has been occasionally used to lower the rate of creep strain and crack propagation [5,6,8,9], since this serration is said to impede GB sliding or to retard intergranular cracking and propagation.  

There have been many attempts to identify the driving force behind GB serration   which can be frequently observed after     0 precipitation. It is known that the mechanism of the formation of serrated GBs in nickel-based superalloys is associated   with the heterogeneous nucleation of     0 at the GBs [3,5–8]. Most remarkably, Koul and Gessinger [3] reported that the model for GB serration was based on GB  

primary     0 movement causing displacement of the local GB segment. In line with this model, the strain energy difference between the matrix side and the boundary side of   the     0 particle–matrix interface provided a driving force for the movement of the   primary     0 particles in the direction of the boundary until this force was balanced by the line tension of the boundary. Additionally, Koul and Thamburaj [10] have   claimed that a     0 solvus temperature should be higher than the solvus temperature of   the major GB carbides (M 23 C 6  or M 6 C), and     0 particle size adjacent to GBs should exceed 0.8  m m for the development of serrations. Thus, earlier studies on the   serrations have been confined to alloys containing     0 volume fractions in excess of   15–20% since they believed     0 solvus temperature and size increases with increasing      0 volume fraction [3,10]. Little literature has been found related with the GB   serrations and their effects in wrought nickel-based superalloys containing     0 volume fraction below 15%.  

Meanwhile, Hong and Nam [11,12] reported that the formation of serrated GBs occurs spontaneously without the presence of M 23 C 6  carbides as a result of the total free energy minimization of a material in 316 stainless steel. They also stated that the improvement in creep-fatigue resistance by GB serration can be explained through the modification of carbide characteristics [13]. More recently, Hong et al. [14] have shown that serrated GBs can be formed through special heat treatment even in the wrought nickel-based superalloy, Alloy 263, which has only 10% volume fraction of     0 . They also found that creep resistance can be improved up to 40% by GB serration in this alloy.  

The present paper focuses on the mechanism of serrated GB formation and its effect on GB liquation in the simulated weld heat-affected zone (HAZ) of a wrought nickel-based superalloy, Alloy 263, which has been widely used in a variety of applications in power industries. The driving forces involved in the formation of serrated GBs will be dealt with via systematic observations. Previously, it has been reported that, in nickel-based superalloys, GB serrations are associated with the   precipitation of adjacent coarse     0 particles [3,5–8,10]. However, it is shown in the   present study that the GB serration in Alloy 263 occurs independent of     0 phase or M 23 C 6  at the GBs. Meanwhile, HAZ liquation cracking is the main weldability concern in the repair of superalloys. As HAZ liquation cracking is an intergranular phenomenon that occurs at elevated temperatures due to GB liquation and grain coarsening, the character of GBs in the HAZ of superalloys is of interest [15–17]. Another purpose of this study is to develop a better understanding of the role of serrated GBs in the grain coarsening and liquation in HAZ.  

# 2. Experimental  

The commercially available hot-rolled Alloy 263 with the chemical composition Ni- 20Co-20Cr-5.8Mo-2.1Ti-0.45Al-0.7Fe-0.06C (all in weight %) was used in this study. The as-received sample, which was solution-treated from the manufacturer, had a mean grain size of 65  m m with a small fraction of MC. Zhao et al. [18] confirmed, through both detail microstructural observations and thermodynamic   calculations, that Alloy 263 has a     0 solvus temperature (1173 K) lower than the   M 23 C 6  solvus (1192 K). In addition, the alloy has a 10%     0 volume fraction with an average precipitate size of   20 nm [14,18,19]. Hence, it appears that no serration is expected in Alloy 263 according to recent models [3,5–8,10] proposed by a series of phenom eno logical experiences in nickel-based superalloys.  

Test samples of about 7    10    10 mm in size were cut from a piece of the alloy. The samples were solution-annealed (1423 K/75 min) followed by water-quenching (WQ). The aging treatment (1073 K/8 h) was then conducted. This heat treatment, characterized by a two-step process, is conventionally used for the alloy. The special heat treatment was designed proprietarily by the present authors [14,20–22] to obtain serrated GBs with a proportion of over 80%. The samples were solution-annealed at 1423 K for 30 min, slow-cooled to 1073 K at a cooling rate of 10 K/min and subsequently aged for 8 h at the same temperature.  

The HAZ microstructures were simulated by a thermal cycle simulation system from Formastor FII (Fuji Electronic Co., Tokyo, Japan) equipped with a high frequency induction system of 400 kHz frequency/1.5 kW output. Cylindrical specimens of 3 mm in diameter and 10 mm in length were rapidly heated to 1173–1573 K at a rate of 111 K/s and then held for 1 s at each temperature followed by helium gas quenching.  

Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) was performed on a JEOL JSM-5800 microscope with a tungsten filament operating at 20 keV. Transmission electron microscope (TEM) characterization was performed on a field emission-type JEOL JEM-2100F operating at 200 keV. GB phase identification was established by analysis of the selected area diffraction pattern (SADP) and energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS). The electron energy-loss spectroscopy (EELS) signals were collected with 0.5 eV/channel dispersion using a JEOL JEM-2100F equipped with a post-column Gatan image filter system (Gatan Inc., Warrendale, UK). The EELS-based energy filtered TEM (EFTEM) technique facilitated the showing of carbon and chromium mapping along the GBs.  

The orientation relationships between GB carbides and two neighbouring grains were determined from Kikuchi or spot patterns. The serrated GB plane normal was obtained from the cross product of at least two beam directions which are parallel to the GB plane. In this situation with the narrowest boundary area, the GB could be seen as a line. This method has been exclusively used for other alloy systems [12,23–25].

It is clear from Figure 9 that the serrated GBs effectively suppress grain coarsening in HAZ.

# 4. Discussion  

# 4.1.  On the mechanism of GB serration  

After direct aging treatment coupled with a controlled cooling rate, GBs are   significantly serrated in the absence of     0 phase or M 23 C 6  in the wrought nickel-   based superalloy, Alloy 263, containing a     0 volume fraction below 10%. This is a significant new finding and differs from previous results [3,5–8,10] which reported   that GB serrations are associated with precipitation of adjacent coarse     0 particles. A modification in carbide characteristics occurs in the wake of GB serration. It is crucial to control the microstructure of these alloys as to whether the serrated GBs form before nucleation of the GB second phase particles or after (as occurs in other superalloys [1–10] or austenitic stainless steel [27]). Figures 2, 3 and 4 are evidence that GB serration occurs prior to the formation of M 23 C 6  or without an interaction   of     0 particles. This finding explains why GB serration involves modification of GB carbide characteristics in this alloy.  

The EELS study may infer that the discontinuous segregation of C and Cr atoms to GBs at high temperature is essentially related to GB serration. The discontinuous GB segregation of C and Cr atoms may lead to lattice distortional strain differences along the GB in the special heat-treated specimen, while continuous segregation may lead to a balanced strain in the conventional heat-treated specimen. The concen- tration of C and Cr atoms would be higher near the GB when they segregate to the GB, because the concentration gradient occurs across the GB. Hence, high lattice distortional strain energy near GBs will develop when a large number of C and Cr atoms are situated near the GB. McLean’s equation applies to the lattice distortion energy in the bulk. However, it can be applied to approximately ascertain the strain energy near GBs, because the periodic lattice is not disturbed near GBs (i.e. the elastic continuum is still maintained near GBs). Accordingly, the strain energies due to C and Cr segregation are estimated to be 47.9 and 1.2 kJ/mol, respectively, when it is assumed that a carbon atom is dissolved into the octahedral interstitial site and a chromium atom into the substitutional site of the nickel solvent. This strain energy will accumulate near the GB and exert a distortional force on the GB. When a distorted and undistorted lattice border is present near the GB due to discontinuous segregation, the distortional force will be exerted only on the GB local segment which is segregated. If the GB local segment subjected to a distortional force is placed in a pushed condition, then the other segment would be in a pulled condition to maintain the force in equilibrium. On the other hand, the local segments of the GB may be in a balanced condition if an even strain along the GB develops due to continuous segregation. Winning et al. [29] demonstrated that high angle GBs can be moved by an external shear stress which exerts a force on the dislocations in GBs retaining a strain field. The current understanding is that a stress due to an uneven distortional lattice strain may develop near GBs, and trigger the onset of GB serration. Furthermore, carbon segregation has a much more significant effect on the generation of lattice distortional strain than chromium segregation, because the strain energy due to carbon atoms is roughly 40 times higher than that generated by chromium atoms. This implies that no serration may be expected if the carbon content is extremely low. Thus, it is our experience to date that this alloy, containing a carbon content less than 0.03%, showed no serration. It is interesting that the carbon contribution to GB serration is also discussed in austenitic stainless steel [30]. Nothing of specific note was found in the initial flat GB region where C and Cr atoms appreciably segregated. More detailed observations are needed to find out the preferential site of the flat GB for the segregation in terms of the GB structure, such as a curved GB plane in 3-D or a high density of ledges.  

It is worth mentioning that the reduction in unbalanced strain energy along the GB was insufficient to provide a driving force for the formation of a serrated GB with respect to the increase in the GB surface. Crystallographic analysis indicated that the GB planes constituting the serrated GBs are much closer to the low-index plane compared to those constituting the flat GBs. In this investigation, it was found that, during serration, the GBs moved towards having specific segments approaching a one {111} low-index plane at a boundary. According to recent research [31,32], GB engineering via special thermo-mechanical processing results in an increase in the fraction of boundaries terminating on {111} planes. More recently, special GBs have been defined with a lower energy as those terminated by low-index boundary planes, such as {111}. Thus, it can be inferred that a serrated GB is more preferable than a flat GB from the viewpoint of interfacial free energy, which is responsible for the driving force in GB serration. The driving force for GB serration stems from a lowering of the interfacial free energy of the GB per unit area. The GB serration in the nickel-based superalloy, Alloy 263, is analogous in many ways to the faceting mechanism. It has been reported that GBs are frequently faceted in various materials to have lower energy interfaces, since certain GB inclinations are energetically preferred over others [33–43]. Cahn [34] has presented possible faceting phase transitions in which an initially flat GB (single phase) may transform to a faceted structure consisting of two or more facet types (phases) which coexist along their lines of intersection. The equilibrium crystal shapes determined by this transition have been calculated at various temperatures [35]. Most remarkably, Hsieh and Balluffi [40] and others [42,43] have directly observed a roughening/de-faceting phase transition in GBs, which was shown to be reversible. Of special interest in these studies is the result that, at high temperature, a flat GB quickly adopted a characteristic configuration at each temperature during cooling, and sharply faceted boundaries were recovered at room temperature without any interactions of GB second phase particles. Furthermore, Donald [41] demonstrated that the onset of faceting is often brought on by the addition of certain chemical elements. Therefore, owing to their similarities, our series of observations correlated the serration with the faceting mechanism. However, it should be noted that the alloy composition, the extent of strain differences along the GBs and time/temperature conditions allowing the GBs to adopt an equilibrated configuration control the occurrence of GB serration.  

The migration of GBs under the influence of preferential solute diffusion along it is known as diffusion-induced GB migration (DIGM). DIGM was first observed by Rhines and Montgomery in 1938 [44]. The mechanism of GB migration when solute atoms preferentially diffuse along a GB and the driving force for the reaction are not yet fully understood. Although still controversial, the coherency strain energy theory advocated by Hillert [45] and Yoon [46] is widely accepted, i.e. that the driving force stems from the elastic stress ahead of the moving boundary. A feature of DIGM is the fact that, in the volume of the material, there is a discontinuous change in composition, i.e. regions of the lattice swept by the migrating GBs show a change in composition. However, no compositional change was found across the serrated GBs in the present study. As shown in Figure 4, only segregation along GBs was detected, while the GBs during serration do not leave behind alloyed or dealloyed zones. Thus, it is difficult to consider DIGM as a mechanism for GB serration.  

# 4.2.  Influence of GB serration on the microstructural response in HAZ  

Observations of simulated HAZ microstructures in both unserrated and serrated specimens indicate that GB carbides dissolve at a  T p  higher than 1313 K, and grain coarsening begins to occur significantly at a  T p  higher than 1473 K. According to Bauer [47] and Gottstein et al. [48], high-angle boundaries migrate more easily than low-angle boundaries if the diffusion of atoms across a GB is considered to govern GB migration. Migration of low-angle boundaries is characterized by an activation energy nearly identical to that for volume diffusion, whereas random high-angle boundaries, characterized by a more ‘open’ structure, migrate under the control of direct atom transfer across a boundary. This can be extended to the correlation established in the present study between serrated GB and suppression of GB coarsening. The suppression of GB coarsening in the serrated specimen may correspond to a lower migration rate of serrated GBs due to the lower interfacial free energy.  

As regards the GB liquation necessary to cause HAZ microfissuring, GB liquid films form when the local alloy concentration significantly lowers the melting point at the boundaries. GBs may be infiltrated by a liquid layer considerably below the bulk melting temperature if wetting occurs. Alternately, the constitutional liquation of the precipitates on the GBs may lead to the formation of a liquid film. It was observed in this study that liquated regions on GBs show a similar composition to the matrix, except for a slight higher Cr content in both specimens. This observation suggests that the main cause of GB liquation in Alloy 263 is not associated with the constitutional liquation of M 23 C 6  precipitates. Depressant melting point elements, such as boron [15,16] or sulphur atoms [49,50], could be partly involved in the liquation as GB segregation. In addition, the smaller HAZ grain size in the serrated specimen indicates more triple junctions among the grains so that propagation resistance to intergranular liquation cracking increases due to higher crack arrestability [53,54]. Thus, the combined effects of lower GB liquation, smaller grain size and the GB configuration in the serrated specimen could contribute to the substantial resistance to HAZ microfissuring. From these results, it can be suggested that GB serration has significant potential in a damage-tolerant microstructures, leading to improvements in resistance to intergranular HAZ liquation cracking and high-temperature performance.  

# 5. Conclusions  

An investigation of GB serration, obtained by a direct-aging treatment combined with a controlled cooling rate, has been carried out in a wrought nickel-based   superalloy, Alloy 263, with a     0 volume fraction below 10%. The influence of serrated GBs on the microstructural response in simulated weld HAZ of this alloy has been studied. The following conclusions can be drawn from this work:  

(1) The GBs are considerably serrated in the absence of     0 phase or M 23 C 6  at the GBs. This is a new finding which differs from previous results reporting that the GB serrations are associated with the precipitation of adjacent coarse     0 particles.  

(2) The onset of GB serration may be triggered by the discontinuous segregation of C and Cr atoms at GBs for the purpose of relieving the excessive unbalanced elastic strain. Also, during GB local movement, the GBs are serrated to have specific segments approaching one {111} low-index plane at a boundary, so that the interfacial free energy of the GB is decreased.  

(3) The formation of serrated GBs with lower energy leads to a change in the carbide characteristics as well as the GB configuration: the carbide morphology changes from granular to planar and the carbide precipitation  

density decreases. Additionally, their coherency pattern to two neighbouring grains changes from consistent to zigzag. (4) The serrated GBs effectively suppress grain coarsening in HAZ and are highly resistant to liquation due to their lower wettability associated with lower interfacial energy compared with flat GBs.
