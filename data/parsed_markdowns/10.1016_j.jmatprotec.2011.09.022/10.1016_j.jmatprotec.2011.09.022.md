The    paper    presents the    results    of an investigation of surface    damage produced by whisker-reinforced ceramic cutting tools    in    the    ﬁnish turning of Inconel 718. The    effects    of    the    cutting parameters, of    the    tool wear and    of the    coolant condition on    damage to the    machined surface was investigated experimentally. The surface    generated by    machining was    examined by    use    of    both    a    scanning electron microscope (SEM) and a high resolution scanning electron microscope (HRSEM). This    showed the    regions of    the    surface that were damaged to    contain both    geometric defects and changes in    the    metallurgical structure of    the subsurface. The types and    the extent of the    surface damage were found to    be dependent upon the    cutting parameters, the size    of    the    wear land    and the    coolant conditions. The    extent    and    the    character of the coarse-scale and    the    ﬁne-scale damage were    analyzed on    72    specimens for which the cutting speeds, feed rates and    coolant conditions (wet    and    dry)    and the    degree of    tool    wear    differed. The    types of    damage that occurred in the    form    of    micro-cracking and    breakage, plastic    ﬂow,    cavities, side    ﬂow and built-up edges are discussed.

# 1. Introduction  

The surface characteristics of a part, along with its mechanical and physical properties, provide information essential for design engineering, since the surface properties of the part can have a strong effect on its performance when it is altered by a partic- ular machining process so that it differs from the bulk material ( Bailey et al., 1976 ). In aerospace and nuclear power plants, knowl- edge of the surface characteristics is particularly important for parts for which surface-sensitive properties have a strong impact upon the service life of the parts. One of the major characteristics of machined parts for aerospace applications is their service prop- erties, such as fatigue strength and creep ( Field, 1973 ). To ensure high-fatigue strength, the surface should ideally be damage-free after machining has been carried out. In the surface region, the damage caused by a machining operation can include geomet- ric damage, such as roughness and waviness, and metallurgical defects, such as cracks and phase changes ( Sadat and Reddy, 1992 ). Improvement in the surface integrity of the work material requires an adequate understanding of the mechanisms involved in damage to the surface during machining.

Generally speaking, surface damage results from the interaction of process energies, mechanical and thermo-chemical in partic- ular, with such material properties as tensile strength, ductility and hardness. The extent of the surface damage that occurs dur- ing machining and how it develops are important information, since components made of this material are often crucial parts in a product, such as an aerospace engine. An early study of surface damage during machining was conducted by Bailey et al. (1976) on a tube of AISI 4340 steel under orthogonal and dry cutting condi- tions. A wide variety of defects on the machined surface of it were observed by use of a scanning electron microscope (SEM), includ- ing chatter marks perpendicular to the direction of cutting speed, long straight grooves parallel to the direction of cutting speed, large cavities, workpiece debris, tool debris, plastic deformation, cracks and voids. In addition, the surface damage was found to decrease as the cutting speed and the tool wear increased. Later, in another study, by Bailey (1977) ,   of surface damage in the face turning of nickel maraging steel, the surface damage was classiﬁed into coarse-scale and ﬁne-scale surface damage. The coarse-scale damage, such as built-up edges, was suggested to be associated with the phenomenon of continuous chip formation and the nucle- ation of cracks in the vicinity of the tool nose region, whereas the ﬁne scale damage, such as cracks and cavities, was found to be associated with discontinuous chip formation and interaction between the tool and the workpiece. Cutting speed was reported to have noticeable effects on the surface damage. However, the fact that relatively low cutting speeds (0.3–80 m/min) were used in the study limits the effects of thermal factors which can play a major role in high-speed machining. Surface damage by cemented car- bide tools in machined Inconel 718 was studied by Sadat (1987) by use of natural and controlled contact lengths. Again, a low range of cutting speed, from 6 m/min to 60 m/min, was employed. Surface damage in the form of cavities, short and long grooves, microc- racks and fractured areas on the machined surface was observed. The authors suggested that the development of grooves, cracks and cavities could be attributed to the formation of micro-built-up edges as a result of ploughing action at the tool/workpiece inter- face. The surface quality was found to be improved by an increase in the cutting speed and by use a controlled ﬂank contact length of the tool, which led to a lowering of the cutting forces. It was also found that at low cutting speeds the effect of a lubricant on the surface damage was limited and that at higher cutting speeds it could be negligible. In a study conducted by Ezugwu and Tang (1995) , ceramic oxide was used as the cutting tool material in the machining of Inconel 718. A high cutting speed (152 m/min) was used much more in this study than in previous ones. Signiﬁcant sur- face deformation and considerable tearing on the machined surface was observed in the machining trials carried out. The cutting tem- perature induced during machining was believed to play a major role in the surface deformation and microstructure changes that occurred. The mechanisms involved in other types of surface dam- age, such as tearing and cracking, were not discussed. Axinte et al. (2006) investigated the existence of surface damages in turning of Ni-based alloys made by powder metallurgy method. In this study, the surface damages, such as cracking, laps and plucking are found to be related to material removal rates and tool wear. The discon- tinuous nature of material re-deposition was also observed on the path of the tool nose radius in the study after the increase of ﬂank wear of cutting tool around 0.12 mm. Pawade et al. (2007) ana- lyzed surface damage in Inconel 718 in the form of metal debris and smeared material particles on the machined surface produced by use of PCBN cutting tools. He suggested that the high cutting tem- peratures under dry cutting condition could result in adhesion of residual chip material or of tool particles to the surface. In a study by Zou et al. (2009) ,   the mechanism of surface cavities, plucking and cracking on the machined surface was analyzed on the basis of experimental results obtained in the turning of a nickel-base alloy. In that study, plastic deformation and carbide particles in the work material were believed to be responsible for the surface damage during machining. Also, surface cavities and plucking developed as a result of a high level of shear stress, this leading to carbide particles in the work material being removed from the machined surface. The literature that was reviewed suggested that investi- gation of the surface damage of Inconel 718 caused by machining has been mainly concerned with cemented carbide cutting tools used in a low speed range. In view of the poor thermal conductiv- ity of Inconel 718, use of more aggressive cutting conditions, such as higher cutting speeds and high feed rate, can result in higher temperatures and cutting forces in the cutting area. Accordingly, the surface damage to the machined surface brought about under such conditions may well differ both in its forms and in its ori- gins.

# 3.2. Overall observations concerning the machined surfaces  

Although a good surface ﬁnish in terms of surface roughness Ra (Ra < 1.6  m) was produced using the cutting conditions described, a close examination of the machined specimens under the scan- ning electron microscope (SEM) showed there to be surface damage that had several features allowing coarse-scale and ﬁne-scale dam- ages, as classiﬁed by Bailey (1977) , to be distinguished. Sometimes surface damage could also be induced by an unstable cutting pro- cess, such as when chatter occurs. In the case of brittle material or the material containing a brittle phase, such as carbide parti- cles, chatter can be an important factor in the occurrence of surface damages. In the tests carried out in this investigation, no chatter was observed in the process as well as on the machined surface. The coarse-scale damages included side ﬂow (SF), built-up edges (BUE), chip debris (CD), and grooves and ridges, whereas the ﬁne-scale damages included the micro-cracking and breakage of carbides (CR), surface tearing (ST), cavities formation (CV), plastic ﬂow and deformation of the grains. The type of built-up edges (BUE) discussed in this paper is surface integrity BUE, which is created at the interface between the tool and the workpiece but is deposited in the form of a lap on the surface, and which thus represents an addition to the surface. The features observed were found regardless of the cutting condi- tions or tool conditions involved. However, there was considerable variation in the intensity and the appearance of these features, the differences found being associated with the cutting speed, the feed rate and tool wear as reported by Sadat (1987) in the investiga- tion on machining of Inconel 718 using controlled contact length tools. A strong correlation between tool geometry and surface dam- age, such as side-ﬂow, was also reported by Kishway and Elbestawi (1999) . Chip formation can sometimes create surface damage in the case of continuous chips that entangle on the machined surface. In the present study, straight and continuous chips were formed dur- ing machining, but no chip entanglement was found. The machined specimens were examined by SEM over a wide range of magniﬁ- cations to determine in detail the variations in the structure of the surface produced by changes in the cutting speed, the feed rate, the tool wear land and the coolant conditions (wet or dry). Fig. 6 summarizes six types of surface damage found in the SEM exami- nation area of 15 mm × 10 mm for the 72 test specimens (3 types of feed × 4 speeds × 3 levels of tool wear × 2 coolant conditions = 72). The damage was classiﬁed as being of large, medium or small and individual in character. For CR and CV, the scale was set on the basis of the ratio of the number of cracked carbides to the number of observed carbides in the area in question. For SF, BUE TR and CD, the scale was set on the basis of the ratio of the damaged area to the total observed area. Since the area considered was selected randomly, the results shown in Fig. 6 reﬂected the surface quality in a certain sense statistically. In general, a higher degree of surface damage was observed on surfaces produced by a worn cutting tool than by a new tool, irrespective of the cutting conditions. A high level of cracking was found on the surfaces produced under conditions of WST and DST, as shown in Fig. 6 . On the one hand, the crack- ing of carbide particles was dominated by a mechanical process in which cracking of the carbide particles was induced, since they were unable to deform under the thrust force. On the other hand, the cracking of the carbides also depended on the temperature of the bulk material in the machining carried out. When the tempera- ture is sufﬁciently high, such as when a worn cutting tool is used, the carbide particles are pressed into the material rather than cracking.

This explains why cracking reaches a maximum in the case of WST and of DST. The effect of feed on the surface damage appears to be very slightly, especially when using semi-worn or worn tools. In the case of new tools, the effects of feed are still noticeable. The degree of coarse scale damage, such as side-ﬂow, built-up edge formation and chip debris, increased signiﬁcantly with an increase in tool wear. A higher degree of coarse-scale damage was also observed under dry-cut than under wet-cut conditions. The surface quality became worse when use of a worn cutting edge was combined with the use of dry-cut conditions in testing, due to the high cutting tem- perature and to the irregular cutting edge generated by tool wear, as shown in Fig. 13 a.  

Regarding ﬁne-scale damage, Fig. 6 shows the micro-cracks, tears and breakage of carbides that were produced in all the spec- imens. The degree of damage of these types is much higher than that for other types of damage within the range of test condition as a whole. A higher degree of carbide breakage was observed under wet-cut conditions when combined with lower cutting speeds (100 and200m/min)andlowerfeedrates(0.1and0.15mm/rev).Thedegree of cracking tended to be least when a worn cutting tool ( VB max  = 0.3 mm) was employed. This can be attributed to the tem- perature at the interface of the tool and the workpiece being highest under such conditions. The work material becomes highly plasti- cized then as a result of the high cutting temperature, which leads to the carbide particles sinking into the work material under the pressure of the normal cutting force. Fig. 7 shows typical ﬁne scale damages consisting of cracks, breakage of carbides and tears on the machined surface. Fig. 6 (a) shows a cavity produced by tearing of the surface by broken carbide particles. Sharman et al. (2001) suggested that the formation of a cavity is due to the removal of hard particles by the chips produced. In line with this, one observes when zooming in with a high level of magniﬁcation, as shown in Fig. 7 (b)–(d), the cracking of titanium carbide (TiC) surrounded by the breakage of niobium carbide (NbC). The micro-cavities are formed when primary carbides break off from the machined sur- face. The micro-cavities can vary widely in depth and in shape, as shown in Fig. 6 . A surface cavity can develop differently when the tools employed differ. In the study by Zou et al. (2009) involving the machining of a nickel-based alloy by a carbide tool, the surface cavity was found to be associated with the formation of surface plucking, which was not observed in the machining of Inconel 718 by use of a ceramic tool. In surface plucking, small particles of mate- rial, such as carbides, were plucked out by the cutting tool under the plastic deformation of the machined surface ( Axinte et al., 2006 ), when there was also adhesion between the tool and workpiece, leaving with clean cavity. The carbides can also be broken off. This is especially the case for the niobium carbides, which are more brittle. The broken particles tend to remain in the cavity ( Fig. 8 ).

# 3.3. Effects of the cutting parameters  

The effects of the cutting parameters, such as the cutting speed and the feed rate, on the degree of surface damage was particularly noticeable when a new cutting tool and wet-cut conditions were employed. The degree of built-up edge (BUE) formation and the production of chip debris (CD) tend to be higher at a low feed rate ( f = 0.1 mm/rev). Fig. 5 (b) is a typical micrographic photograph of a built-up edge when machining was carried out at a cutting speed of 200 m/min with use of a coolant. Under such conditions, the built- up edges appear in the area of interaction between the cutting-edge region and the freshly machined workpiece surface. The strong ten- dency for built-up edges to be formed under low feed conditions is due primarily to an increase in the plastic deformation area found at the interface of the tool and the workpiece. The built-up edges formed under low feed conditions are primarily from the rake face of the cutting tool, particularly in the region of the tool nose, which is employed in producing the surface. Use of low feed can increase the plowing area in the surface generation and the plowing effect can induce the formation of built-up-edge.  

When higher cutting speeds were employed ( v c  = 300 m/min), higher degree of plastic ﬂow and of material side-ﬂow were observed for all three feed rates, as shown in Fig. 9 (a) and (b), in which a new tool and wet-cut conditions were employed. The increase both in plastic ﬂow and in material side-ﬂow can be attributed to the higher cutting temperatures generated in the cut- ting area due to the higher cutting speed involved. At a high cutting temperature, the work material becomes more viscous. The plas- tic ﬂow that appears on the machined surface as shown in Fig. 9 (a) can be the result of the non-uniform material, such as an embedded primary carbide, beneath it. The change in intensity is less dramatic there due to changes in the cutting parameters when the state of tool wear and the coolant conditions changed, as is shown in Fig. 6 .

More than 30% of the machined surface was found to be involved in the side-ﬂow, built-up edge, and chip debris formation and the plastic ﬂow that occurred ( Fig. 13 (a)). The micro-cracking of carbides was found to be reduced in connection with occurrence of ﬁne-scale damages due to sinking of the carbides under the normal forces exerted on the highly viscous workpiece material at the surface.  

# 3.5. Effects of tool wear  

It was observed that, when machining with use of a semi-worn cutting tool ( VB max  = 0.2 mm), a feed level of 0.1 mm/rev, and a cut- ting speed of 200 m/min, the surface behaves like that of a ductile material, with the formation of grooves and ridges, despite the hardness of the material. Fig. 11 shows a surface produced by use of a semi-worn tool. Long, straight grooves parallel to the direc- tion of the work-tool motion are present. There are two factors that can be associated with the generation of grooves. The devel- opment of coarse-scale grooves is caused by broken particles of the carbide and by plastic deformation of the work material, as shown in Fig. 11 (a) and (b). When the carbides are broken in the shear plane by the cutting forces involved, part of the broken car- bides are removed in the form of chips, and other parts of them can be trapped under the cutting edge and be sheared off from the workpiece. Additional damage observed in the specimen in ques- tion was the presence of breakage of TiC, as shown in Fig. 11 (c) and (d). Backscatter images reveal the breakage of TiC here due to the cutting forces generated increasing as a result of tool wear ( Fig. 12 ). # 3.6. Micro-cracks and breakage  

Two of the more frequent ﬁne-scale surface damages encoun- tered in the turning of Inconel 718 are those of micro-cracks and of the breakage of primary carbides. Fig. 6 shows how cracks and the breakage of carbides can be readily observed in specimens of the machined surface. The primary carbides are complex TiC/NbC car- bides contained in the structure of Inconel 718. The development of cracks and of carbide breakages during the machining process is somewhat complicated. It can be induced by a thermal/chemical process, a thermal/mechanical process or a combination of the two processes ( Connolly et al., 2003 ). The results of an investigation by Zou et al. (2009) suggest that the carbide oxidation induced by high temperatures can cause cracks and breakage to be brought about by the cutting forces. At the temperatures involved, inten- sive oxidation is induced on the machined surface, even during intensive coolant ﬂow ( Sharman et al., 2001 ). Intensive oxidation of NbC at temperatures above 400   ◦ C has been reported by Ma    et al. (2009) . The fact that oxides formed during the machining pro- cess are not strong enough to sustain large strains generated on the tool/workpiece interface, in particular during machining, can lead to cracking and breakage of the carbides. Although a ther- mal/chemical process, such as oxidation, affects the cracking of carbides to some extent, the effect can be limited due to the very short period of time that chip generation takes up. Such mechani- cal effects as those of the cutting forces or thrust forces generated by the cutting process may well play a major role in the cracking of carbides. # 3.7. Subsurface damages  

Figs. 8 and 16 reveal typical subsurface damages caused by machining. The damages consist of deformation of the grain in the cutting speed direction ( Fig. 16 )    and of the cracking of car- bide particles ( Fig. 8 (a) and (b)). Damages of these two types can affect the mechanical properties of the workpiece. Cracking of the carbide particles was also found to occur beneath the machined surface, due to the carbide particles being unable to be deformed together with the bulk material. The deformation of the grain and of the grain boundaries can contribute to the combined effect of the thrust forces acting upon the workpiece and of the high cutting temperatures induced during cutting. Fig. 16 shows the subsurface deformation that can occur under wet-cut and dry-cut conditions, respectively. A marked elongation of the grain boundaries near the surface layer was found in the subsurface in dry-cut specimens. This suggests severe deformation to have occurred as a result of high cutting temperatures ( Fig. 16 (b)). Under wet-cut conditions, however, the cutting temperature was lower, and the plastic defor- mation observed in the subsurface layer was less ( Fig. 16 (a)). The depth of the major deformation zone was about the same under wet-cut as under dry-cut conditions. This may possibly explain the high thrust force found at the interface between the tool ﬂank and the machined surface, which appears to be due to the lower cutting temperature there because of the coolant.  

# 4. Conclusions  

In the investigation carried out, whisker-reinforced ceramic cut- ting tools were used in the ﬁnish machining of Inconel 718. The cutting tool material employed results in a much higher level of pro- duction efﬁciency and the possibility of using much higher cutting speeds than with use of a cemented carbide tool material. How- ever, achieving a high level of productivity and an improved surface quality in the ﬁnish machining of Inconel 718 remains a chal- lenging task. The experimental investigation undertaken aimed at analyzing, on the basis of surface morphology of machined part by SEM, the development of surface damage in the high-speed ﬁnish machining of Inconel 718 by use of a whisker reinforced ceramic cutting tool. The results allow the following conclusions to be drawn.  

A wide variety of surface damages were generated in the machining of Inconel 718 under the cutting conditions employed here, the cracking and breakage of niobium carbide (NbC) and of titanium carbides (TiC) being the most common type of damage that occurred under a wide variety of test conditions.  

The combination of new a cutting edge, low cutting speed and low feed rate results in a tendency towards the breakage of niobium carbide. At a low feed rate, the tendency for built-up edges to be formed is also higher than at a higher feed, due to an increase in the size of the plastic deformation area at the interface of the tool and the workpiece. The overall effect of the cutting parameters on the degree of surface damage is considerably less than the effect that the extent of tool wear and the coolant conditions have on it.  

The degree of tool wear and the coolant conditions have a major impact on the degree of coarse-scale surface damage. Surface dam- age in the form of side-ﬂow, and the formation of built-up edges and of chip debris is considerably greater under dry-cut conditions and when tool wear is greater. Cutting with the presence of a coolant generally results in a lesser degree of surface damage than under dry-cut conditions.  

The cracking and breakage of carbides in the ﬁnish machining of Inconel 718 can be induced by a combination of ther- mal/chemical and thermal/mechanical processes. The presence of a mechanical load or of a thrust force that act on the carbide particles plays a major role in the cracking or breakage of car- bides.  

Although surface damage of a wide variety of different types was generated in the ﬁnish machining of Inconel 718 when whisker- reinforced ceramic cutting tools were employed, a completed proﬁle of the surface quality produced can be drawn on the basis of various other factors as well, such as the geometric proﬁle and the degree of subsurface deformation and of residual stress. In prac- tice, whether a part produced under the cutting conditions that are present can be used or not can depend on other factors that may apply to the individual case. Nevertheless, the results here do provide general information concerning what can be achieved in terms of avoiding surface damage in the ﬁnish machining of Inconel 718 when whisker-reinforced ceramics are used as the cutting tool material. Such information is also important for efforts at determin- ing what follow-up operations can be best for achieving the level of surface quality that is targeted.
