The failure mechanism of a nickel aluminide coating cyclically oxidised for 1 h-cycles at 1100 °C and cooled by liquid water is described using a cyclic oxidation rig allowing very rapid cooling (15.9 s) between 1100 °C and ambient temperature. Repeated cycling  ﬁ rst provoked the development of cracks into the coating as well as martensitic transformations ( β /L1 0 ) within the  β -NiAl phase. The coating subsequently oxidised internally through the cracks during the oxidation stage. With increased exposure, these zones grew laterally, thus consum- ing the entire Al available in the intermetallic phase. A degradation mechanism based on crack formation and internal oxidation is proposed.

# 1. Introduction  

The effect of water vapour on the high temperature oxidation of alumina-forming materials has been a subject of increased interest over the last decade  [1] . The effect of water is an important parameter to enable better understanding of turbine part durability in aero-gas engines. Indeed, turbine components are regularly exposed to water during their lifecycle, due to both the surrounding environment (relative humidity, water droplets from clouds) and the regime of the combustion engine (take-off, cruise and/or landing), with contents varying between 10 and 15 vol.% H 2 O  [2] . The effect of water- containing environments on alumina-forming materials was  ﬁ rst studied by the addition of water vapour in  ﬂ owing air furnaces  [3 – 5] . It appeared that water vapour brought about spallation and cracking of the oxide scales especially under thermal cyclic conditions  [3] . The in ﬂ uence of water vapour was also shown to be dependent on the inter- facial metal/oxide toughness of the system studied and especially on the segregated sulphur content  [6] .  

In order to determine if liquid water can alter the integrity of oxide scales during service of the turbine, the combination of cyclic oxidation and water drop tests at ambient temperature was also performed at each cycle. Water drops were systematically added onto alumina- forming CeO 2  based coatings during the cold stage (T  b  80 °C) of cyclic oxidation  [13] . This aimed at simulating repeated condensation of water released by kerosene combustion or relative humidity of air and rain after engine operation. The results showed no spallation of the system to liquid water for the earliest stages of cyclic oxidation even though the mismatches of thermal expansion coef ﬁ cients between different layers (oxides, coatings, substrates) have been quoted to result in their spallation  [14] .  

At ambient temperature, the exposure of thick alumina scales to humid laboratory conditions (relative humidity) showed an enhanced effect of water on the onset of oxide spallation. This phenomenon, called “ Moisture Induced Delayed Spallation ”  [7]  provokes an early failure within a variety of different TBC systems, and is triggered by the addition of water drops  [8 – 10] . A delamination at the bond-coat/TGO  

When forced water quenching was introduced to high frequency cyclic oxidation testing of MCrAlY and TBC coatings to determine their resistance to thermal shock in the temperature range 900 – 1250 °C, an accelerated oxidation attack initiated by a crack growth was reported [15 – 18] . Compared to these works, the cooling rate is voluntarily decreased in the present study to reduce residual stresses in the oxide scales and to get closer to the real engine conditions. Thus, liquid water is mixed with compressed air and projected onto hot samples through an automatic thermal cyclic rig. This cooling mode, known as water (atomised) spray quenching  [19,20] , is used to carefully control the thermal treatment and the mechanical properties of forged metals. A stable vapour  ﬁ lm  ﬁ rst forms on the hot surface, followed by a liquid wetting of the surface when the specimen temperature becomes lower than the Leidenfrost temperature  [20] . This accelerated cyclic test is applied to an out-of-pack coating and compared to a conventional “ dry ”  laboratory air cooling. The failure mechanisms will be discussed in terms of microstructure evolution and oxidation mechanism.  

# 2. Materials and methods  

# 2.1. Materials of study  

A CM247 directionally solidi ﬁ ed (DS) Ni-based superalloy was employed in this study as a substrate material ( Table 1 ). Coupons of 20 mm of diameter and 2 mm thick were ground with SiC (180) paper, grit blasted with a 220 mesh Al 2 O 3 , then rinsed in deionised water and cleaned with ethanol under ultrasonic agitation before applying a coating. The aluminium diffusion coating ( β -NiAl, 70  μ m thick) was obtained at SR Technics Airfoil Services (Ireland) using an out-of pack aluminising process typically employed for protecting low pressure turbine components of an aeroengine. The process was carried out at 1080 °C for 6 h from a 70Cr – 30Al donor and NH 4 F activator. The ﬁ nal coating and composition (EPMA analysis) are shown in  Fig. 1 a and b, respectively. The additive layer is Al rich ( N 50 at.%) across the ﬁ rst 10  μ m and its content drops steadily towards the interdiffusion zone.  

# 2.2. Furnace settings  

The cyclic oxidation tests were performed at 1100 °C for 1 h-cycles with 5 min cooling (T  ≤ 40 °C) with an automatic water cooling system in a custom designed cyclic rig ( Fig. 2 a). The cooling chamber is delimited by a stainless steel cylinder, to avoid hot water projections and increased pressures due to the generation of steam. A tank is located underneath the cooling chamber and provides deionised liquid water (5.0  ≤ pH  ≤ 5.5) to cool down the samples. The samples are suspended in a FeCrAl (Kanthal) carousel using FeCrAl wires. The cooling rate was measured by a type K thermocouple close to the sample surface ( Fig. 2 b). The thermocouple indicates a rapid cooling (15.9 s) from 1100 °C to 100 °C, with a marked rate change at 250 °C. This tempera- ture corresponds to the Leidenfrost temperature. Above this tempera- ture threshold, a water vapour  ﬁ lm protects the hot sample surface from projected water. Beneath 250 °C, the vapour  ﬁ lm collapses and lets the surface in direct contact with the liquid water coolant  [20] Additional IR thermometer measurements were used to take into account the global sample surface. Three samples were employed and the average and standard deviation values were calculated and plotted as error bars. A sample was removed after 200 cycles and another one after 500 cycles for further analysis and observation.  

In dry air conditions, the same hot phase (1 h at 1100 °C) was chosen for comparative purposes. Then, cooling was carried out for 15 min. The temperature dropped from 1100 to 100 °C in 5 min. Then, the samples remained at 40 °C for 10 additional minutes.

# 2.3. Microstructural character is ation  

The microstructural character is ation of the coatings was performed by X-Ray Diffraction (Brüker D8 Advance,  λ Cu ), and Scanning Electron Microscopy coupled to Energy-Dispersive Spectrometry (SEM/EDS, FEI Quanta 200F/EDAX). Electron Backscatter Diffraction (EBSD) analyses were conducted on a Nordlys EBSD detector (with AZtec HKL/CHANNEL5 Software) from Oxford Instruments using a PHILIPS XL30 SFEG. Local surface analyses were carried out with non- destructive Raman spectrometry (Jobin Yvon Horibat LabRam HR, λ He – Ne ), as it allows to differentiate the degrees of hydration (i.e. oxides vs. hydroxides) as well as metastable non-metallic phases.  

# 3. Results and discussion  

# 3.1. Character is ation of the oxidised nickel aluminide

With conventional air cooling, the aluminide coating followed a sub-parabolic law during the  ﬁ rst 500 h of oxidation, suggesting the establishment of a protective alumina scale. In the case of water cooling, the net mass gain was similar to the reference air cooling during 80 cycles. The mass then increased rapidly until reaching 350 cycles before markedly decreasing till 500 cycles were completed. Then, a slow mass growing period was observed reaching a maximum weight gain at 820 cycles. The aluminide  ﬁ nally exhibited catastrophic spallation until the end of the test. Although the mass variations are much less marked, the overall behaviour of the coating in wet cooling conditions is reminiscent of a  “ breakaway oxidation ”  phenomenon, which usually occurs after long exposure time in this kind of systems, i.e. when the Al content of the alloy is insuf ﬁ cient to maintain a protec- tive oxide  [21,22] . Yet, such behaviour was not expected as early as for 80 cycles, since an aluminium-rich coating (NiAl 50 at.%) was used as a starting material (see  Fig. 1 a and b). The detrimental effect of water cooling was con ﬁ rmed when cycling raw CM247 bare alloy, since an important mass loss started from only 5 cycles and reached − 5 mg·cm − 2   after 100 cycles ( Fig. 3 b). In contrast, only a slight mass loss was observed in ambient air cycling conditions.  

The top-surface of the coating was characterised by SEM analysis whilst in the BSE mode after cyclic oxidation with water cooling ( Fig. 4 ). One can notice the appearance of a crack network inside the dark grey alumina matrix which seemed to develop probably at the grain boundaries from 200 cycles or earlier ( Fig. 4 a). In agreement with the results from Wang et al. on the forced water quenching of a MCrAlY coating  [15,16] , it is believed that these cracks developed at the surface where the thermal gradient between the hot coating and the cooling sprayed water is at its maximum. The cracks were surrounded by light grey oxide regions attributed to the formation of oxide scales containing nickel, aluminium and oxygen. After 500 ( Fig. 4 b) and 1000 cycles ( Fig. 4 c), the cracks propagated along the surface together with white oxide spots, distributed close to the crack regions after 500 cycles and to the rest of the surface after 1000 cycles, where the cracks were less apparent. These areas were characterised by blue spots that could be attributed to Co or Ni-rich spinels when looking macroscopically at the top of the samples. Moreover, some needles were visible on localised zones only after a long exposition time ( Fig. 4 b, c).

Additional analyses of the cross-sections of the cycled samples are provided in  Fig. 6  for both water and air cooling. After 200 cycles of cyclic oxidation with water cooling ( Fig. 6 a), the cracks have propagated from the surface through the interdiffusion zone of the  β -NiAl coating (50  μ m). These cracks appeared with a lateral periodicity of about 500  μ m. It is believed that the open porosity of the scale allows easy ox- ygen penetration and hence promotes the formation of thick oxides scales especially under the crack tip. The Al concentration at the crack tip is lower than at the top of the NiAl coating (the Al concentration is plotted in  Fig. 1 b). Therefore, a rapid  β -NiAl  → γ ′ -Ni 3 Al transformation occurs and results in the formation of non-protective internal oxides. A longer exposure (500 cycles) provoked the lateral growth of the inter- nal oxides through the IDZ, leading to a nodular morphology ( Fig. 6 b). The initial crack was located at the centre of the 100  μ m deep attacked area which was mainly composed of alumina and trapped bright refrac- tory elements. Only a small amount of  β -NiAl remained around the oxide area. After 1000 cycles of oxidation ( Fig. 6 c), the nodular internal oxidation zones coalesced and were connected by porous nickel-rich oxide phases. At this point, the Al concentration in the coating was too low (4.5 at.%) to form alumina exclusively. The reduced Al content also resulted in an enhanced  β -NiAl  → γ ′ -Ni 3 Al transformation in which re-dissolution of the TCP elements occurred  [24,25] . A large extent of porosities and cracks were visible in the nodule oxide area. The initial aluminide surface (top-left corner) revealed massive spall- ation of the oxide scales, in agreement with the severe mass loss from 820 to 1000 cycles ( Fig. 3 ). The same out-of-pack coating oxidised for 1000 cycles with an identical hot stage and an ambient air cooling showed a very different microstructure ( Fig. 6 d). The aluminide developed a 5 – 7  μ m thick external oxide scale made of alumina and embedded refractory precipitates. Limited internal oxidation developed within the intermetallic which contained remaining  β -NiAl in the additive zone.  

EDS elemental maps were performed on the nodular zone after 500 cycles to identify the oxide composition ( Fig. 7 ). Al and O mainly composed the internal oxidation zone except at the crack tip, where Al is depleted and replaced by Ni, Cr, Co and Ti. This con ﬁ rms the XRD and Raman analyses which showed the formation of alpha alumina, titanium oxide and nickel-based oxide spinels. An aluminium depleted zone surrounded the oxidised area that was balanced by enrichments of mostly Cr and Co from the superalloy substrate (10 at.%). As observed before, refractory elements of the interdiffusion zone were embedded in the nodules during the oxidation of the alloy. An EBSD analysis of the zone of interest allowed one to investigate the distribution of phases, where the red and blue colours correspond, respectively, to the presence and absence of a given phase ( Fig. 8 ). Both alpha alumina and chromia were revealed in the overall oxide area whereas mixed (Ni,Co)(Al,Cr) 2 O 4  spinel phases form along the crack tip.  

# 3.2. Proposed degradation mechanism  

A schematic of a degradation mechanism is proposed for the inter- pretation of the above data ( Fig. 9 ). As discussed below, cracking of the oxide and of the coating; followed by internal oxidation of the cracks appear as the major factors of such fast degradation with water cooling.  

# 3.2.1. Cracking  

The oxide scale started to wrinkle and cracked with the accumulated number of cycles. Then the cracks propagated along the coating normal to the oxide/coating interface due to the mechanical stresses provoked

Holmes and McClintock proposed that scalloping (wrinkling) of the oxide scale grown on a pack alumi- nized René N4 superalloy was associated with predominately compres- sive mechanical coating strains when thermally cycling in air. Then, their coating cracked due to peak tensile strains  [26] . Similar cracks were also observed for water quenched MCrAlYs  [15,16] . The non- uniform cooling from the surface to the bulk during the  “ quench ”  led to stress distribution into the coating. Moreover, important residual stresses could be induced by the CTE mismatches between the embed- ded oxides and the surrounding metallic phases ( Fig. 9 – 2). Swelling of the oxide nodules with Pilling and Bedworth ratios  N  1 within the metal- lic matrix is also known to provoke volume changes and increased strains, as do the transformations in the  β -NiAl phase. In particular, the  β -NiAl  → γ ′ -Ni 3 Al transformation was reported to create volume contractions and/or extension between 16 and 20%  [27] . In addition, martensitic transformations B2  → L1 0  within the  β -NiAl were clearly identi ﬁ ed by the observation of twinning planes in light microscopy ( Fig. 10 a) and by XRD ( Fig. 10 b). This aluminium sub stoic hio metric phase regularly appears during rapid cooling  [28]  and increases stresses by a volume variation of 0.2%  [29] . Cracking might also arise from the ductile to brittle transition (DBTT) of the  β -NiAl phase during the accel- erated water cooling. Indeed, the DBTT of  β -NiAl around 868 – 1060 °C [30] , could lead to the formation of cracks in this temperature range. These cracks then easily propagated through the low fracture toughness β -NiAl (5 MPa·m 1/2 ) and stopped once they encountered the more ductile  γ ′ -Ni 3 Al phase (20 MPa·m 1/2 )  [31] , which is strengthened by the refractory precipitates (TCPs) of the interdiffusion zone ( Fig. 9 – 3). Jackson et al. observed similar deep protrusions zones during the cyclic oxidation of a NiCoCrAlY coating at 1100 °C in dry air  [32] . These oxidation attacks were also periodically detected for a speci ﬁ c rough- ness and yttrium content. All these factors lead us to propose an overall thermal fatigue mechanism for the formation of such cracks as the ones quoted for CoNiCrAlY  [33]  and for pack aluminide coatings  [26] . Indeed, cracking initiated at the surface and propagated along the grain boundaries of the coating. Upon oxidation around the cracks, the  β  to y ′  transformation occurred as also observed here ( Fig. 9 – 3).  

# 3.2.2. Oxidation  

Once the cracks developed through the intermetallic phase, the hot stage at 1100 °C led to the rapid oxidation of the  “ fresh ”  metal crack. Oxides  ﬁ rst grew along the cracked area and progressively oxidised the metal underneath the crack tip, where the Al content appeared to be too low to form protective oxides. Therefore, the internal oxidation with nodular shape occurred ( Fig. 9 – 4). The acceleration of internal oxidation by cracking was also observed in Ni – Cr – Al alloys  [34,35] . In NiCoCrAIY coatings, Jackson et al. reported that the cracks were completely  ﬁ lled with oxide and therefore, no lateral growth or oxida- tion under the crack tip was observed  [32] . # 4. Conclusions  

A new kind of cyclic oxidation with water cooling was investigated in this study. The procedure consisted of spraying liquid water (water spray quenching) onto hot samples during the cooling stage of 1 h cyclic oxidation at 1100 °C. This method provided a rapid cooling between 1100 °C and ambient in 15.9 s. Above the Leidenfrost temperature ( ≈ 250 °C), a water vapour  ﬁ lm is expected to develop around the surface thus protecting the system against projected water. An out-of- pack aluminide coating was oxidised in the above conditions and compared to a conventional laboratory air cooling. The coated CM247 Ni-base superalloy exhibited some kind of breakaway oxidation with a catastrophic failure after 1000 cycles. Perpendicular stress-relieving cracks developed periodically at the surface and propagated until the interdiffusion zone of the coating/substrate interface. Internal oxidation ﬁ rst occurred under the crack tip before growing laterally during cycling. The oxide phases were composed of  α -Al 2 O 3  and embedded refractory elements of the IDZ. Cr 2 O 3  and (Ni,Co)(Al, Cr) 2 O 4  spinels formed once the Al reservoir of the interdiffusion zone was fully consumed, by Co and Cr migrations close to the oxide nodule zone.  

The origin of the failure was attributed to the high mechanical stress- es coming from the CTE mismatches and stress distributions induced by the water cooling, as well the DBTT and the phase transformations in the alloy including martensitic B2  → L1 0  transformation. Following cracking, oxidation proceeded fast and degraded the coating both around the crack and at the crack tip, where the Al content was too low to provide oxidation protection through alumina formation. It is believed that the projected water could penetrate in the cracks and further studies should be carried out to unveil whether some kind of electrochemical mechanism could foster the catastrophic degradation observed in the aluminide coating with water cooling.
