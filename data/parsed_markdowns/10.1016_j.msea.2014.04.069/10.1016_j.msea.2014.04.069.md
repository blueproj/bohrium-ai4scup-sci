Recently, a sixth generation Ni-base SC superalloy TMS-238 has been developed at National Institute for Materials Science (NIMS) for applications of gas turbine blades  [7] . The temperature cap- ability of TMS-238 to achieve 1000 h of creep life at 137 MPa is approximately 1120  1 C. Compared to MX-4/PWA 1497, which is a fourth generation SC superalloy, this new alloy has comparable creep resistance at 900  1 C and 1000  1 C, but much better creep properties at 800  1 C and 1100  1 C. For example, the creep rupture life for MX-4/PWA 1497 at 800  1 C/735 MPa is 22.2 h, whereas that is 1150.4 h for TMS-238. The creep strength of SC superalloys at high temperatures is very important in view of the high gas temperature. However, the creep behavior at relatively low tem- peratures (750 – 850  1 C) is also an essential concern because the rear part of turbine blades such as platform is subjected to high stress level and relatively low temperature.  

# 1. Introduction  

Ni-base single crystal (SC) superalloys are widely used in aircraft engines and advanced heavy industrial gas turbines. The typical microstructure of these superalloys consists of two phases, i.e.  γ  and  γ΄ , and the  γ  matrix is strengthened by  γ΄  precipitates. With increasing the inlet temperature of turbines, the Ni-base SC superalloys with a higher temperature capability are desired.  

Additions of refractory elements, especially Re and Ru, are well known to improve the creep resistance of Ni-base SC superalloys [1 – 4] . First generation SC superalloys contain no Re and Ru. Second and third generation superalloys contain about 3 wt% and 5 wt% Re, respectively. Fourth and  ﬁ fth generation SC superalloys have been developed by gradually increasing Ru content. Fourth generation Ni- base superalloys contain 2 – 3 wt% Ru, and the high temperature microstructure stability is improved due to Ru addition  [5] . Fourth generation superalloys have attained temperature capabilities 30  1 C higher on average than the previous generation superalloys in terms of high temperature creep strength. For  ﬁ fth generation superalloys, the content of Ru has increased to 5 – 6 wt%. The high temperature creep resistance of  ﬁ fth generation superalloys is better than that of fourth generation superalloys  [6] .  

The mechanism of creep deformation in  〈 001 〉 oriented SC superalloys is sensitive to temperature and applied stress  [8] . At high temperatures and low stresses, rafting process is a typical characteristic. At intermediate temperatures and stresses, disloca- tion activity is mainly restricted in the  γ  matrix. In the tempera- ture range of 750 – 850  1 C and high stresses,  γ΄  shearing and activation of  〈 112 〉 {111} slip are commonly observed during pri- mary creep, and the creep behavior is strongly dependent on the crystallography orientation  [9] . Especially,  a 〈 112 〉 dislocation rib- bon shearing both  γ  and  γ΄  was reported in CMSX-4 –  and DD6  [14] . In the present study, the different creep deformation in TMS-238 at 800  1 C and 735 MPa was observed, which is suggested to be related to the enhanced creep strength.

The Ni-base SC bars ( ∅ 10  130 mm) of MX-4/PWA 1497 (here-   after abbreviated as MX-4) and TMS-238 were cast by a directional solidi ﬁ cation furnace at NIMS. For both alloys, the crystallography misorientation determined by the Laue back-re ﬂ ection X-ray technique was within 6 1  of [001]. The specimens of diameter 4 mm and gauge length 22 mm were machined from the heat treated SC bars. Constant load tensile creep testing was performed in air at 800  1 C and 735 MPa and interrupted at a strain of 0.1% for each alloy.  

Before and after creep testing, the microstructures were exam- ined using transmission electron microscopy (TEM). TEM discs were cut perpendicular to stress axis. Then, the discs of MX-4 were manually ground to 100  μ m and perforated by a twin-jet electro- polisher at 20 V/20 mA and   20  1 C. The electrolyte consisted of 13% perchloric acid, 80% ethanol and 7% glycerol. The TEM foils of TMS-238 were prepared using Gatan's 656 Dimple Grinder and 691 Precision Ion Polishing System (PIPS) at 5 kV and  7 4 1 . A Tecnai 20 microscope was operated at 200 kV.

SFs shearing only the  γ  matrix remarkably decrease the creep rate in TMS-238. For SC superalloys, this is an unusual creep mechanism at 800  1 C/735 MPa. Similar deformation process was reported in Ru- containing SC superalloys with high Co and Re contents during high temperature creep at 950  1 C/290 MPa  [22] . A high density of SFs in the γ  matrix enhances the creep resistance. First, it is because of the interaction of SFs on different slip planes. In fcc structure materials, Shockley partial dislocations from different non-parallel slip planes may react to form a sessile stair rod dislocation at the lines of intersection, e.g.  a /6[121] þ a /6[211] - a /6[110]. The con ﬁ guration of two SFs associated with a stair rod dislocation is known as Lomer – Cottrell lock  [23] , which obstruct slip dislocations. Second, the inter- action between SFs and dislocations may also contribute to the enhanced creep strength. Recently, it is demonstrated that a high density of stacking faults as a new strategy enables an extraordinary combination of ultrahigh strength and good ductility in an hcp Mg alloy . Third, the Shockley partial dislocation is dif ﬁ cult to shear  γ΄ precipitates due to the creation of high energy complex SF, i.e. SF plus APB.  

# 3.5. Stacking fault energy of  γ  matrix  

The dislocation dissociation in the  γ  matrix is related to the  γ / γ΄  microstructure, applied stress, lattice mis ﬁ t stress and the SFE of γ  matrix. Basically, two alloys had similar  γ / γ΄  microstructure, such as precipitates size, volume fraction of precipitates and  γ channel width. The lattice mis ﬁ t (see  Table 1 ) in MX-4 and TMS- 238 was almost equal. It implies that the stress level in  γ  matrix is comparable. Consequently, it is believed that the  γ  matrix of TMS- 238 has lower stacking fault energy (SFE) than that of MX-4.  

Based on the chemical compositions (at%) of  γ  matrix predicted using the ADP at 800  1 C (see  Table 2 ), the difference of Gibbs energy was calculated using Thermal-Calc.  shows the results. For the  γ matrix of MX-4 and TMS-238, the energy barrier of phase transforma- tion from fcc to hcp is 0.21   10 4   J mol  1   and 0.17   10 4   J mol  1 , respectively. The former is 23.5% higher than the latter. Actually, SF in an fcc structure is a local lattice transformation from fcc to hcp. Thus, the lower energy barrier means the easier formation of SF. This accounts for lower SFE of TMS-238 with respect to MX-4.  

Alloying elements signi ﬁ cantly affect the SFE. Al is a main  γ΄ forming element, and the content of Al in the  γ  matrix in two alloys is same (see  Table 2 ). So the effect of Al on the SFE of  γ matrix is not the main cause of lower SFE in TMS-238. It means that Re and Ru are more effective elements than Co, Mo and Cr for decreasing the SFE of  γ matrix per unit content. From the viewpoint of crystal structure, Mo and Cr have stable bcc structure, and Co has stable fcc structure above approximately 450  1 C  [25] . In contrast, Re and Ru have stable hcp structure. The crystal structure is closely related to the phase transformation from fcc to hcp. Stable hcp structure favors the SF formation, therefore Re and Ru are thought to be mainly responsible for the lower SFE in TMS-238 though their content variation is smaller than Co and Cr. For accurate interpretation, the complicated interaction between alloying ele- ments should be considered.  

# 4. Conclusion  

The sixth generation Ni-base SC superalloy TMS-238 has much higher creep strength than MX-4 at 800  1 C and 735 MPa. The deformation microstructures in specimens with a creep strain of 0.1% were analyzed. For MX-4, the  a 〈 112 〉 dislocation ribbons sheared both the  γ  matrix and  γ΄  precipitates. The main deforma- tion characteristic in TMS-238 was the SFs formed in only the  γ matrix. These SFs did not shear the  γ΄  precipitates. A high density of SFs in the  γ  matrix effectively decreased the creep rate of TMS- 238. It is inferred that the SFE of  γ  matrix in TMS-238 is lower than that in MX-4. The calculation of difference of Gibbs energy indicates that higher Re and Ru additions are mainly responsible for the lower SFE in TMS-238.
