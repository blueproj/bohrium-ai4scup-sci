ABSTRACT:  Multitarget medications represent an appealing therapy against the disease with multifactorial abnormalities  cancer. Therefore, simultaneously targeting son of sevenless 1 (SOS1) and epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR), two aberrantly expressed proteins crucial for the oncogenesis and progression of prostate cancer, may achieve active antitumor effects. Here, we discovered dual SOS1/EGFR-targeting com- pounds via pharmacophore-based docking screening. The most prominent compound  SE-9  exhibited nanomolar inhibition activity against both SOS1 and EGFR and efficiently suppressed the phosphorylation of ERK and AKT in prostate cancer cells PC-3. Cellular assays also revealed that  SE-9  displayed strong

Overall,  SE-9  is a novel dual-targeting SOS1/EGFR inhibitor that represents a promising treatment strategy for prostate cancer.  

# INTRODUCTION  

Prostate cancer is the second most frequently diagnosed malignancy among males worldwide, resulting in over 350,000 deaths annually. ,   The majority of prostate cancer cases will inevitably escalate to a more invasive and metastatic stage after receiving the cornerstone treatment  androgen deprivation therapy. ,   Once this stage is reached, chemotherapy appears to prolong survival; however, ensuing drug resistance and toxicity limit its clinical benefits. ,   Until now, metastatic prostate cancer has remained incurable. , ,   Given this fact, the development of novel therapeutics against prostate cancer is urgently warranted.  

Epidermal growth factor (EGF) receptor (EGFR) is a transmembrane protein with tyrosine kinase activity. Activation of EGFR is driven by the binding of growth factors (such as EGF), which causes signals to be transferred through membranes, triggering further signaling pathways to stimulate cells to proliferate, migrate, survive, and differentiate. − 10 EGFR can also mediate cell cycle progression by propelling cells through the G1 phase. ,   It has been proven that EGFR is relevant to the pathogenesis and progression of malignancies, and its overexpression has been confirmed in prostate cancer cells and shown to be correlated with poor prognostic parameters of prostate cancer, i.e., high grade, advanced stage, and high risk. − 14   Therefore, EGFR is an attractive target for prostate cancer intervention. A number of small- molecule drugs targeting EGFR have been marketed, among which quinazoline-based structures, for example, gefitinib, erlotinib, and afatinib, are typical types.   However, they have been reported to have limited single-agent activity against androgen-refractory prostate cancer. ,  

Son of sevenless 1 (SOS1), which acts as a pivotal guanine exchange factor (GEF) capable of binding to KRAS and activating the transition from KRAS-GDP (inactive form) to KRAS-GTP (active form), is a central node of RAS-related signaling pathways. ,   SOS1 deletion would inactivate RAS protein and inhibit the phosphorylation of its downstream effectors, such as ERK and AKT, resulting in decreased survival, proliferation, and migration of tumor cells. , − 22  

Recently, the development of SOS1-targeting binders is considered a more prospective strategy for the suppression of abnormal RAS signaling as opposed to direct inhibition of the “undruggable” KRAS protein, due to a clear binding pocket located near the SOS1:KRAS interface at the SOS1 catalytic site. ,   In prostate cancer cells, SOS1 has been detected to be overexpressed and required for their proliferation.   More importantly, the pathologic signals of prostate tumorigenesis converge in the RAS signaling network, thus rendering the inhibition of SOS1, a crucial switch that triggers RAS-related signaling, an appealing therapeutic target for prostate cancer. − 28   Some SOS1 inhibitors have been disclosed. BAY-293 is a nanomolar SOS1 inhibitor that can effectively inhibit the proliferation of tumor cells without KRAS mutations while showing relatively weaker inhibition against mutant KRAS tumor cells.   BI-3406 sharing the same quinazoline scaffold as BAY-293 exhibited potent antiprolifer- ative effects on most wild-type and G12C-driven cancer cells, and its subsequent modification led to the identification of BI 1701963, which became the first SOS1 inhibitor to enter clinical trials. ,   However, two clinical trials of BI 1701963 (NCT04835714, NCT04627142) have been declared termi- nated because of unexpected toxicity findings.   So far, there are no available inhibitors targeting SOS1 on the market.  

Monotherapy with EGFR inhibitors or SOS1 inhibitors may achieve lower-than-expected effectiveness in complex diseases, particularly for advanced and resistant tumors, but can be made competent by using them as part of combination therapy. − 34   Some researchers have conducted studies of SOS1 inhibitors in combination with EGFR inhibitors, and the results showed synergistic activity against cancer, reduced toxicity of pan-KRAS inhibition on normal tissues, and even cytotoxicity on cancer cells resistant to EGFR inhibitors. , Nevertheless, these benefits are exposed to the risk of adverse drug − drug interactions, unpredictable p harm a co kinetics, and high-dose toxicity inherent in drug combination strategies. , Multitarget drugs, which target several molecules concurrently by one entity, represent a promising alternative for circum- venting aforesaid risks as well as increasing efficacy. ,   In view of this, it is extremely critical to design dual-targeting SOS1 and EGFR inhibitors, and blockage of both targets may engender better therapeutic outcomes in prostate cancer.  

Virtual screening is a time-efficient, cost-effective, and robust screening method for developing antitumor compounds. Since pharmacophore models are enriched with binding information on potential interactions between proteins and drug candidates, pharmacophore-based virtual screening methods are extensively used as reliable methods. , Among them, pharmacophore-based docking may obtain more optimized drug candidates at a faster speed.   By using this method, we have successfully discovered a series of dual- targeting antitumor agents, for instance, dual NRP1/KRAS G12D inhibitors and dual NRP1/MDM2 inhibitors. ,   Here, we report the discovery of small molecules that can simultaneously target SOS1 and EGFR using the pharmacophore-based docking protocol ( Scheme 1 ), and we performed molecular dynamics (MD) simulations and further bioassays.

Enzymatic Inhibition Assays and Structure − Activity Relationships (SARs).  All of the screened compounds (10 μ M) and positive inhibitors (AXE: SOS1 positive inhibitor; gefitinib: EGFR positive inhibitor) were subjected to inhibition assays to initially explore SARs and evaluate their dual inhibitory activity against SOS1 and EGFR. As shown in Table 1 , compared with other scaffolds, compounds containing a quinazoline scaffold ( SEs 5, 8 − 14 ) inhibited EGFR by over 80%, and almost only these compounds presented inhibition of SOS1. These results prove that the quinazoline core is essential for the simultaneous binding of compounds to SOS1 and EGFR. We first discussed the relationship between quinazo- line-based compounds and EGFR inhibitory activity. The structural differences of  SEs 5, 8 − 14  can be divided into three parts: substituents on the benzene, the C6/7-substituents of the quinazoline core, and the C − N linkage. The meta- substitution of benzene with fluorine and chlorine exhibited better EGFR inhibitory activities than ortho-/para-/non- substituents ( SEs 9, 11  >  SEs 5, 8, 10, 12 − 14 ), probably because of the enhanced hydrophobic interactions with the EGFR hydrophobic pocket as well as the optimal group position and size. Among the nonmetal-substituted com- pounds,  SE-8  had a slightly greater EGFR inhibitory rate than SE-5 , although they shared the same heterocycle substituent on benzyl. This may be correlated with their structural variations of substitution at the C6/7-quinazoline and C − N linkage. In general, compounds containing C6- and C7- substituents on the quinazoline scaffold had increased inhibitory activity against EGFR compared to those with or without only C6- or C7-substitutions ( SEs 8, 9, 10, 11  >  SEs 5, 12, 13, 14 ), and  SE-9  with tetrahydro-2H-pyran-4-yloxyl on C6-position and methoxyl on C7-position obtained the best EGFR inhibitory efficacy. As for C − N linkage, the rotatable C − N linkage may contribute to EGFR inhibitory activity ( SE- 9  > gefitinib); however, it may not be a major factor because SEs 13 and 14 , which also had a rotatable C − N linkage and a chiral methyl substitution while lacked a hydrophobic group substituting for benzene, was less active than gefitinib. Among SEs 5 and 8 − 14 ,  SE-9  exhibited the best EGFR inhibitory activity with an IC 50  value surpassing that of gefitinib ( Table S4 ).  

Next, the inhibitory effects of the substituents mentioned above on SOS1 were discussed. Although the rotatable C − N linkage and chiral methyl substitution may not play a major role in the enhancement of EGFR inhibitory activity, it is critical in achieving effective SOS1 inhibition.  SEs 9, 13, and 14  (all compounds had a rotatable C − N linkage and  (R) - methyl) possessed stronger SOS1 inhibitory activity than  SEs 8, 10, 11, and 12  (all compounds with a rotatable C − N linkage devoid of methyl substitution) and  SEs 1 − 7 (compounds containing only NH). The chiral methyl filled a small hydrophobic pocket of the SOS1 protein, thereby offering hydrophobic interactions. Similar to the achievement of high EGFR inhibitory activity, substitution at the C6- position of the quinazoline core favored the improvement of SOS1 inhibitory activity, whereas the absence of it decreased SOS1 inhibitory activity ( SE-14  had a SOS1 inhibitory rate <50%). Furthermore, among different C6 substituents, the heterocyclic substitution of  SE-9  showed better inhibitory activity against SOS1 as opposed to smaller substituents such as bromine ( SE-13 ), ethyl ( SE-14 ), and methoxy (AXE). The benzene substitution provided the shape complementary and hydrophobic interactions with the rear pocket of SOS1, and inhibition rates suggested that the simple benzene substitution of a fluorine atom (meta-position) in  SE-9  could potentially lead to stronger SOS1 inhibition.  SE-9  showed the best dual inhibitory activity against SOS1 and EGFR, with both inhibition rates exceeding 95%. AXE and gefitinib were inferior to  SE-9  in terms of their inhibition rates. In Vitro  Cell Growth Inhibition Activity of SE-9.  Cell viability experiments were performed to further probe the prospect of biological activity of  SE-9  in cells. Five human cancer cell lines were used to quantify IC 50  values for  SE-9 , and Table S6  shows the results.  SE-9  effectively inhibited cancer cell growth of DLD-1 (human colon cancer cells), K562 (human leukemic cells), HepG2 (human liver cancer cells), A549 (human lung cancer cells), and PC-3 (human prostate cancer cells) and obtained the lowest IC 50  against PC-3 (IC 50 = 0.45  ±  0.03  μ M), which was much lower than that against normal prostate cells RWPE-1 (IC 50  > 10  μ M). Also, we performed cell viability assays in four KRAS mutation-driven cancer cells, H358 (G12C), Calu-1 (G12C), A427 (G12D), and SK-LU-1 (G12D) ( Table S7 ). The results showed that SE- 9 exhibited better cytotoxicity to PC-3 cells (IC 50  = 0.45  ± 0.03  μ M) than these KRAS mutation-driven cancer cells (IC 50 values range from 0.55 to 0.81  μ M). Thus, we further tested the anti pro life rat ive ability of positive controls against PC-3. As shown in  Table S8 ,  SE-9  is the most active, which is about 21- fold more potent than BI-3406 (IC 50  = 9.64  ±  0.35  μ M), 19- fold more potent than gefitinib (IC 50  = 8.75  ±  0.26  μ M), and 3-fold more potent than the combination administration of BI- 3406 and gefitinib (IC 50  = 1.37  ±  0.09  μ M). The results showed that  SE-9  had synergistic anti pro life rat ive effects. Furthermore, to demonstrate whether the growth inhibition effect of  SE-9  was dependent on SOS1 and EGFR, nontargeting shRNA (shControl)-, SOS1-targeting shRNA (shSOS1)-, EGFR-targeting shRNA (shEGFR)-, and SOS1 and EGFR-targeting shRNA (shSOS1/EGFR)-transfected PC- 3 cells were constructed ( Table S9 ), and then, MTT assay was used to detect the cytotoxicity of  SE-9  to these cells. As shown in  Table S9 ,  SE-9  exhibited stronger cytotoxicity to shControl- PC-3 cells (IC 50  = 0.43  ±  0.04  μ M) than shSOS1-PC-3 (IC 50 = 3.92  ±  0.16  μ M), shEGFR-PC-3 (IC 50  = 5.48  ±  0.21  μ M), and shSOS1/EGFR-PC-3 (IC 50  > 10  μ M) cells, indicating that the growth inhibition activity of  SE-9  is dependent on SOS1 and EGFR.  

Binding Modes of Dual Inhibitor SE-9.  To gain structural insight into the binding models,  SE-9  was docked into the active sites of EGFR and SOS1. As can be seen in Figure 2 A,B,  SE-9  displayed favorable overlap with the original ligands. The benzene ring of  SE-9  interacted hydrophobically with residues Leu718, Ala743, Met766, and Leu792 of EGFR and residues Tyr884, Phe890, and Leu901 of SOS1. In the EGFR- SE-9  complex, a hydrophobic interaction was also formed by the quinazoline of  SE-9  with residue Leu844. In the SOS1- SE-9  complex, two  π − π  interactions were observed between the quinazoline of  SE-9  and residue His905. Notably, the formation of hydrogen bonds of  SE-9  with EGFR residue Met793 and SOS1 residue Asn879 strengthened the binding ability. Effects of SE-9 on the Progression of PC-3 Cells.  We further evaluated the effects of  SE-9  on the oncogenic traits of PC-3 cells. As shown in  Figure 4 A,B,  SE-9  induced the apoptosis of PC-3 cells in a dose-dependent manner, as evidenced by the increase in both early and late apoptosis. Furthermore, the effects of  SE-9  on the expression of cleaved PARP and caspase 3 (apoptosis executors) were determined in PC-3 cells. Consistently,  SE-9  treatment increased the expression of both executors in a dose-dependent manner in PC-3 cells ( Figure 4 C,D).  

Next, we measured the effect of SE-9 on the proliferative capacity of PC-3 cells. First, we evaluated the  SE-9  effect on the cell colony formation ability of PC-3 cells after being incubated with  SE-9  for 15 days. It was shown that  SE-9 indeed led to a dose-dependent reduction in the level of colony formation ( Figure 4 E,F). Then, 5-ethynyl-2 ′ -deoxyuridine (EdU) incorporation analysis was constructed to confirm the suppressive effects of  SE-9  on PC-3 cell proliferation. As Figure 4 G,H results indicated, the EdU-positive cell ratio was significantly decreased after the incubation of different concentrations of  SE-9  in PC-3 cells; specifically, a good inhibitory effect resulted from the lowest concentration (0.5 μ M). In consistent with the results on SOS1 and EGFR activity,  SE-9  held a stronger promoting effect on cell apoptosis and a suppressive effect on cell proliferation ability compared to individual SOS1 (AXE) or EGFR (Gefitinib) inhibitors, or a combination of both ( Figure S2 ).  

The action of  SE-9  on the cell cycle distribution of PC-3 cells was also evaluated. As shown in  Figure 5 A,B, cell distribution in the G1 phase was remarkably enhanced by  SE-9 treatment in a dose-dependent manner, while S and G2 phase distribution was reduced, indicating that  SE-9  could induce an arrest of the G1 phase of PC-3 cells. To further confirm this effect, the expression of cyclin D1 and cyclin-dependent kinase 4 (CDK4), both of which were the proteins activating specific CDKs essential for cell cycle progression, were measured in PC-3 cells through Western blot analysis.   It was shown that SE-9  treatment contributed to a concentration-dependent decrease of cyclin D1 and CDK4 expression ( Figure 5 C,D). Furthermore,  SE-9  exerted a stronger effect on cell cycle distribution compared to individual SOS1 (AXE) or EGFR (Gefitinib) inhibitors, or a combination of both ( Figure S3 ). Thus, our results demonstrate that  SE-9  can cause cell apoptosis and cell cycle arrest to exert potent anti pro life rat ive activity on PC-3 cells.

# CONCLUSIONS  

Prostate cancer requires novel therapies to address the reduced anticancer efficacy caused by resistance, metastasis, and toxicity. As a powerful alternative, multitarget drugs offer new insights into circumventing these issues. According to overexpressed proteins and signaling pathways relevant to the pathogenesis and progression of prostate cancer, the dual inhibition of SOS1 and EGFR may exert strong anticancer activity by intensively suppressing the transduction of their downstream signals critical to proliferation, apoptosis, cell cycle progression, and metastasis. We here successfully identified a potent compound  SE-9  through pharmacophore- based docking screening.  SE-9  was found to have great inhibitory activity against both SOS1 and EGFR as well as their downstream signaling effectors ERK and AKT in prostate cancer cells PC-3. Importantly,  SE-9  also exhibited excellent anti pro life rat ive potential, including induction of G1 cell cycle arrest and cell apoptosis as well as inhibition of angiogenesis and migration. Further  in vivo  results in PC-3 xenografts revealed that  SE-9  significantly inhibited tumor growth, and no apparent toxicity was observed. Compared to AXE,  SE-9 possessed better PK properties. In summary,  SE-9  can effectively target both SOS1 and EGFR and has excellent antitumor activity  in vitro  and  in vivo . These findings demonstrate that this dual SOS1/EGFR-targeting strategy could be taken into account for future medicine development in prostate cancer.

Synthetic Procedure for SE-9.  As shown in  Figure S5 , the commercial 4-chloro-7-meth oxy qui nazo lin-6-yl acetate  M1  (267 mg, 1 mmol) and (R)-1-(3-Fluorophenyl)ethanamine  M2  (418 mg, 3 mmol) were dissolved in isopropanol. The reaction mixture was stirred at 60  ° C for 8 h and then was concentrated in vacuo. The crude product was purified on a silica gel column to give  M3 , a yield of 33%. The intermediate  M3  (157 mg, 0.5 mmol), tetrahydro-2H- pyran-4-ol (61 mg, 0.6 mmol), and tri phenyl phosphine (183 mg, 0.6 mmol) were dissolved in dichloromethane. The reaction mixture was stirred at room temperature for 6 h and then concentrated in vacuo. The crude product was purified on a silica gel column to give  SE-9 , a yield of 21%.  

Virtual Screening.  SOS1 (PDB ID: 5OVE) and EGFR (PDB ID: 4WKQ) high-resolution crystal structures were available from the Protein Data Bank (PDB) ( http://www.rcsb.org ). The Molecular Operating Environment program (MOE, Chemical Computing Group Inc., Montreal, Quebec, Canada) was used to conduct a virtual screening operation, and the crystal structures were loaded into it and subjected to preprocessing for structure correction. Based on interactions crucial for binding, structure-based pharmacophore models of SOS1 and EGFR were developed using the pharmacophore query editor of MOE. SPECS, BIONET, and JUNling databases with a total of 278,000 compounds were screened by pharmacophore- based docking via the Dock program of MOE. The dG docking scoring program in MOE was performed to measure the binding free energy, and lower values indicate better binding affinity. To ensure the drug-like candidates would be obtained in advance, screened compounds needed to be filtered by SwissADME.   Also, the PAINS filter was implemented using PAINS-Remover.  

MD Simulations.  GROMACS was used to perform MD simulations under the AMBER99SB-ILDN force field. The initial binding models were obtained from the docking results. The complex system was solvated (extended simple point charges) in a cube (1.0 nm in diameter) and added counterions (sodium and chloride ions) to neutralize. Then, the steepest descent method was applied to minimize the system energy, followed by NVT (1 bar) and NPT (300 K) equilibrations (100 ps each). Eventually, 50 ns MD simulations were conducted and calculated RMSD, RMSF, and secondary- structure contents (Dictionary of Secondary Structures for Proteins, DSSP). Conformations were stored once every 100 ps.  

Cell Culture.  Cells were obtained from Pricell (Wuhan, China) and incubated in Roswell Park Memorial Institute (RPMI) 1640 medium (Sigma − Aldrich) with 1:100 penicillin/streptomycin and 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS, LONSERA, catalog no. S711 − 001S) at 37  ° C with 5% CO .

 (Cambridge, MA). The working buffer contained 10 mM HEPES

 (pH 7.4), 5 mM MgCl 2 , 150 mM NaCl, 1 mM DTT, 0.0025% Igepal, and 0.05% BSA, and the hit compounds were dissolved in it. The detailed procedure referred to the reported study.  

EGFR Kinase Assay.  EGFR and ATP were purchased from Carna and Promega, respectively. According to a previously reported method, ULight-labeled peptide substrates and appropriate Euro- pium-labeled antiphospho-antibodies were used in this study.   The compounds and kinases were added to assay plates and mixed by centrifugation. The enzymatic reaction began when the ATP-substrate mixture was added. The reaction was carried out at room temperature for 1 h before being halted by EDTA buffer. Finally, the solution used for detection was added, and the mixture was incubated for 1 h. The IC 50  values were calculated by GraphPad Prism 6 (GraphPad Software, Inc.).

MTT Assay.  Cells were harvested and seeded in a 96-well plate (5 ×  10 4   cells/well) for overnight culture. Then, they were treated with different concentrations of hit compounds and incubated at 37  ° C for 72 h. After incubation, the MTT solution (5 mg/mL) was added to each well and incubated at 37  ° C for about 4 h. The insoluble crystals were dissolved by dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO), and the absorbance value for each well was determined at 570 nm by using a microplate reader.

Fifteen days later, pure cold methanol was used to fix the growing colonies, which were subjected to the staining of 0.1% crystal violet for 30 min and observed and photographed under a microscope.

Vascular Inhibition Test.  HUVECs (8  ×  10 4   cells/well) were seeded into 96-well plates precoated with Matrigel in a medium containing different formulations (group settings: control group, 0.5, 2.5, and 12.5  μ M). After 8 h of incubation, the capillary-like tube formation was evaluated to quantify tube formation through the count of the number of formed tube-like structures. The tube number in the control group (untreated cells) was designated as 100%.  

Wound-Healing Assay.  HUVECs were seeded into 6-well plates. And when cells grow into complete confluence. A straight line was scraped on the cell monolayer by using a sterile 200  μ L tip to form a cell-free zone. The scraped cells were washed out, and cells were then incubated with various concentrations of the drug. Photos were taken under a microscope at 0 and 24 h after scratch.  

Transwell-Invasion Assay.  The concentration of HUVEC suspension (6  ×  10 4   cells/well) was adjusted and spread into the upper chamber of the Matrigel precoated transwell chamber, and different concentrations of the drug within 200  μ L of serum-free fresh medium were added. Fresh medium (800  μ L) with 20% FBS was added to the lower chamber, serving as an attractant. After 24 h of incubation, cells invading the lower side of the membranes were sequentially fixed and stained with 70% ethanol and 0.1% crystal violet for 30 min, respectively. Images were taken with an inverted microscope, and the OD values at 570 nm were detected on a microplate reader to quantify the invaded cell number.  

Antitumor Activity and Toxicity  In Vivo .  All of the experiments involving animals were approved by the Animal Experimentation Ethics Committee of China Pharmaceutical University (ethics approval number: 2022 − 04 − 011). Immunohis- tochemical stains against TUNEL, CD31, and Ki67 were performed using immuno his to chemistry kits according to the manufacturer’s instructions. In Vivo  Rat Pharmacokinetic Studies.  Compounds were administered (10 mg/kg) via p.o. Serial blood samples (0.25 mL) were collected at 0.25, 0.5, 1, 2, 4, 8, and 24 h after dosing and centrifuged to obtain the plasma fraction. In a 96-well plate, the plasma sample (50  μ L) was transferred, and the internal standard methanol/acetonitrile (1:1, v/v) solution (200  μ L) was added. After vortexing for 5 min, the mixture was centrifuged (12,000 rpm, 5 min) and the supernatant (60.0  μ L). The liquid chromatography and tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) system was used to study PKs.  

Statistical Analysis.  The entire statistical analysis was performed with Prism 8.0 (GraphPad Software, Inc.). For two-group comparisons, two-tailed unpaired  t -tests were performed; for multigroup comparisons, one-way ANOVA was performed.  P  values less than 0.05 were regarded as significant. All data were presented as mean  ±  standard deviation (SD).
