ABSTRACT:  The solubility of water in octan-1-ol was measured from 275.15 K to 369.15 K by coulometric Karl Fischer titration. The mass fraction solubility,  s c , at 298.15 K was found to be (48.91  ±  0.13) mg · g − 1 . Experiments indicated the existence of a pseudo equilibrium state when there was a signi ﬁ cant di ﬀ erence in the temperature of the liquid and the headspace gas. The density of water-saturated octanol (WSO) was measured from 288.15 K to 318.15 K. The density at 298.15 K was determined to be (831.7  ±  1.7) kg · m − 3   with a change in density of  − 0.6 kg · m − 3 · K − 1 .

While the NIST SRM has a certi ﬁ ed value of water, it cannot by de ﬁ nition be  “ saturated ”  since it is not provided as a biphasic material. # INTRODUCTION  

The water and octan-1-ol system is a well-studied mixture as it has many utilitarian purposes. Since water and octan-1-ol are immiscible and exist as a biphasic system, it often serves as a model for the interface between blood or cytoplasm and the cell membrane. The sheer number of papers on the partition coe ﬃ cient of drugs between water and octan-1-ol attests to − 3   this. However, as with many two-phase systems, there is a small but measurable amount of water that is soluble in the organic phase, and likewise there is some octan-1-ol in the aqueous phase.  

This work concentrated on determining the solubility of water in octan-1-ol as a function of temperature using the best available methods. The anomalous decrease in water solubility above 303 K reported by Margolis has been investigated, and an explanation as to the cause is proposed.  

The property of water being sparingly soluble in octan-1-ol has turned out to be extremely useful for Karl Fischer measurements. Speci ﬁ cally, water in octan-1-ol solutions are often used as calibration solutions for Karl Fischer titrations. These solutions are provided by many manufacturers in various concentrations, including the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) standard reference material (SRM) 2890, water-saturated 1-octanol (WSO).   The simplest calibration solution for Karl Fischer titrations would be a saturated system of water in octan-1-ol where there was both an aqueous and an organic phase to ensure saturation.  

# EXPERIMENTAL METHODS  

Certain commercial equipment, instruments, or materials are identi ﬁ ed in this paper in order to specify the experimental procedure adequately. Such identi ﬁ cation is not intended to imply recommendation or endorsement by the National Institute of Standards and Technology, nor is it intended to imply that the materials or equipment identi ﬁ ed are necessarily the best available for the purpose.  

Several earlier studies report values of the solubility of water in octan-1-ol. The reported value of water in a saturated solution of octan-1-ol has varied over the years as methods and techniques improved for the measurement of water have improved. However, the di ﬀ erences in the reported values are larger than would be desired. One of the earliest measurement of the solubility of water in octan-1-ol was done by Leo and Hansch, where the reported value of water saturation was reported as  “ 2.300 mol/L ” ; however, there was quite a bit of uncertainty in this value.   Although this value of WSO was used as a reference for other solubilities, there was little utility in this value since there was no information on the density of the solution. Using the density of WSO determined in this study, the value reported by Leo and Hansch is equivalent to 49.9 mg · g − 1 .   Further studies of WSO by Stephenson et al. give the temperature dependence of the solubility from 283 K to 363  

Anhydrous octan-1-ol was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (lot 09474MH) with a mass fraction purity of 0.990. Water was obtained from a deionizing system (conductivity  ≥ 18 M Ω ). The water content of the octan-1-ol was determined using a Metrohm 831 coulometric Karl Fischer system using a frit-less generator electrode with Hydranal Coulomat AG (Read al Hein, lot 9006A) as the working medium.  

The coulometric Karl Fischer system was calibrated by injecting subsaturated water in octan-1-ol standard solutions that were prepared in the laboratory a few days prior to calibration. The octan-1-ol solution masses were determined by di ﬀ erential weighings on an analytical balance with a sensitivity of 0.01 mg. The calibration standards were prepared gravi- metrically in oven-dried vials by the addition of deionized water to anhydrous octan-1-ol that had been placed over a 0.3 nm molecular sieve. The water content of the standard solutions ranged from approximately 20 mg · g − 1   to 47 mg · g − 1 . A vial of dry octan-1-ol was used as the blank. All liquid transfers were performed under a dry nitrogen blanket that had been thoroughly purged to minimize uptake of water from the atmosphere. A dry magnetic stir bar was placed into each vial; the vials were then crimp-sealed and placed on a magnetic stirrer. Contents of the vials were stirred for several days at room temperature to ensure complete dissolution of the water, particularly in the more highly concentrated solutions.  

Subsequent to the calibration of the Karl Fischer apparatus, the water content of WSO at various temperatures was measured. The primary batch of WSO was divided up into ﬁ ve di ﬀ erent aliquots that were initially placed in water baths to bring the samples to one of several temperatures (i.e., 277 K, 288 K, 298.15 K, etc.). An additional bottle of WSO was left out on the bench at room temperature to serve as a reference point for the measurements. The method of temperature control was later expanded to include an incubator and an oven, both utilizing a large aluminum heat sink to hold the samples at higher temperatures than a water bath. The samples were allowed to equilibrate with intermittent shaking or stirring to help hasten the equilibrium. This was done for a minimum of 72 h before sampling and injection into the Karl Fischer system. The bottles at subambient temperature were left to equilibrate for much longer periods of time. All containers of WSO contained both an aqueous and an organic phase to ensure that the octanol was indeed saturated, and the two-phase system was maintained at all temperatures.  

When the aliquots of the organic phase of WSO were sampled, care was taken to ensure that the organic phase was clear and that there were no water droplets in the organic phase, as con ﬁ rmed by visual inspection. After the initial measurements were taken at a given temperature, the bottles were agitated and allowed to stand for another 24 h to 48 h and then remeasured to con ﬁ rm that the sample was at equilibrium. For temperatures below 318 K, once su ﬃ cient replicates had been taken on a bottle at one temperature, the bottle would be transferred to a di ﬀ erent temperature bath, and a second bottle would replace the  ﬁ rst one at the  ﬁ rst temperature. This was to ensure repeatability at a given temperature and to allow equilibrium to be approached from more than one direction.  

Throughout the course of the experiment reference solutions of subsaturated water in octan-1-ol were measured to ensure a valid calibration. Temperature measurements below 333 K were made using a calibrated platinum resistance thermometer with a Tinsley Senator AC resistance bridge (SN 256694) to measure the thermometer resistance. The variation in the bath temperatures was less than 0.15 K. Above 333 K the temperature was measured with a calibrated mercury-in-glass thermometer, and the variation in temperature was on the order of  ±  1.0 K.  

Density measurements were made using an Anton-Paar 58 oscillating U-tube densimeter, which has a frequency display of six signi ﬁ cant  ﬁ gures. Calibration of the densimeter was performed by measuring the vibrational periods of two materials with easily calculable densities. The  ﬁ rst calibration point was air, where the density was determined based on the temperature, atmospheric pressure, and the relative humidity.  

The second reference point was degassed deionized water, with the density calculated from the temperature from McCutcheon et al.   Densities of the saturated solutions were measured from 288.15 K to 313.15 K, by sampling solutions that had come to equilibrium at the given temperatures.

An important point to note is that, during the course of the experiment, measurements on a nonthermally controlled aliquot of water and octan-1-ol were made periodically, with the room temperature measured between 294 K and 295 K. The moisture in octan-1-ol under these conditions was measured at (48.3  ±  0.3) mg · g − 1   as determined over several months. This value is consistently lower than the value determined at 295.15 K using a thermally controlled sample; this di ﬀ erence is statistically signi ﬁ cant at a 95 % con ﬁ dence level based on a  t -test of the two means. Although the exact cause of this di ﬀ erence is unknown, it is an important point of reference since samples of WSO are often used at ambient conditions and this data will re ﬂ ect many applications of the practical use of WSO.  

As mentioned previously, a second set of data was taken during this study that that has been classi ﬁ ed as a pseudosaturated state. The pseudosaturated data was collected by allowing the headspace of the sample bottles to be above the level of the temperature-controlled bath so that the headspace was closer to ambient temperature. This was done to attempt to replicate what may have been done in previous measure- ments of WSO.  

From Figure 1, it can be seen that the water saturation begins to decrease above 303 K for water and octan-1-ol in this pseudosaturated state. This trend follows the observations originally reported by Margolis and is compatible with the Margolis data at the higher temperatures.   The pseudosatura- tion levels were fairly repeatable, and the moisture values were generally consistent when the equilibrium point was approached from both higher and lower temperatures.

# CONCLUSION  

The mass fraction solubility of water in octan-1-ol is relatively insensitive to temperature changes below 298.15 K. Above 298.15 K the solubility increases much more rapidly with respect to temperature. However, at higher temperatures the system is susceptible to changes in the partial pressures of the component solvents, leading to larger uncertainties at higher temperatures. This e ﬀ ect is magni ﬁ ed when there is a large temperature di ﬀ erence between the headspace and the liquid portion, resulting in a state of pseudo equilibrium.
