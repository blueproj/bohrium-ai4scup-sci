# 1.   Introduction  

In the field of high-temperature aluminum alloys, much research has focused on  strengthening by coherent L1 2  Al 3 M nano precipitates, where M is a transition metal or a  lanthanoid (or Li) [1–5]. While Sc is the element that produces the highest strengthening  MANUSCRIPT increment on a per atom basis [2,4,6–9]; its current high cost is a strong impediment to the  commercial viability of Sc-containing aluminum alloys. Thus, much research has been performed  to partially or totally replace Sc with other elements that form L1 3 M)  nano precipitates   to reduce the alloy’s cost, while maintaining strength at ambient and elevated  temperatures and increasing aging- and creep-resistance, for example, from \~300 ºC  for alloys  with Al 3 Sc precipitates  to \~425 °C for alloys with Al 12,3,13–32].  

The authors have focused on optimizing the composition of Al-Zr-Sc-Er-Si alloys [24,28]  to minimize their Sc-content, while maintaining their ambient and elevated-temperature strengths  ACCEPTED [27,32]. The Sc concentration was strongly reduced, while Zr was concurrently increased; for  example, from an earlier Al-0.055Sc-0.005Er-0.02Zr-0.05Si composition [24] to the recent Al- 0.08Zr-0.014Sc-0.008Er-0.10Si composition [27,32]. Further adjusting the Sc, Zr, Er and Si  concentrations is not anticipated to cause a drastic increase in strength and maximum operating  temperatures. Adding other elements is therefore needed to further improve our Al-based alloys.  Attempts to increase the coarsening resistance of these and similar alloys  by adding slow  diffusers (Ti, Hf, V, Nb, or Ta) have been made, but to date the improvements have been modest  [16,17,30,31,33,34]. We thus decided to investigate a new approach to improve the high- temperature aging- and creep-resistances of Al-Zr-Sc-based alloys, whereby a second population  of temperature-resistant precipitates is created, which interacts synergistically with the L1 2  Al 3 (Sc,M) nano precipitates. Some prior examples include Al-Li-Sc alloys with Al 3 Sc and Al 3 Li  nano precipitates [35,36], and Al-Cu-Sc alloys with Al 3 Sc and  θ′ -Al 2 Cu precipitates [37], but the  second nano precipitates have to date been created at temperatures lower than the Al 3 (Sc,M)  nano precipitates, and dissolve or coarsen rapidly at or above 300  ºC. Similarly, a high- temperature coarsening resistant  α %)alloy was formed during high-temperature annealing at 540 °C, prior to low temperature  annealing at 200 °C to form  θ -Al 2 Cu and Q-Al  

In Zr-Sc-Er-Si-containing alloys, Zr, Sc and Er are the L1 MANUSCRIPT 3 M  nano precipitates, whereas Si is an inoculant, which helps to nucleate these L1 by forming Si-M-vacancy clusters acting as nuclei for growth of the (Al,Si) (Zr,Sc,Er)  nano precipitates [39]. The formation of these trimers also enhances the diffusivity of Sc, Er and  Zr, thus helping to reduce the processing time [24]. Coarsening, however, is a diffusion-limited  process and the enhanced diffusion by addition of Si also accelerates the coarsening of the L1 2   nano precipitates after peak-aging has been achieved [28]. Thus, ideally, Si should be present  ACCEPTED during the aging process through the peak-aging time, and then should be removed to prevent  rapid over-aging.  The motivation of the present study is to search for element(s) that scavenge  and remove Si from the matrix, albeit at a sufficiently slow rate that Si is still present during the  L1 2  nano precipitation stage.  To further increase an alloy’s strength, the added elements may also  induce solid-solution strengthening and/or precipitation strengthening via the Si-rich precipitates,  2  nano precipitates, and unreactive  with them.  Two candidate elements are manganese and molybdenum, used in parallel, as  described below.  

Manganese induces significant solid-solution strengthening of Al [40] and also  precipitates Al 6 Mn- and Al 12 Mn-phases [41,42], which are, however, unwanted due to their  brittleness. Although Mn diffuses faster than Zr in Al, it still diffuses relatively slowly, compared  to the much faster elements, Sc and Er [3]. To ensure solid-solution strengthening is maintained,  alloys containing Mn are thus usually restricted to low-temperature applications (250 °C  maximum). If Si and/or Fe are present in the alloy, Mn-Si-, Mn-Fe- or Mn-Fe-Si- bearing Al-rich  MANUSCRIPT phases form [42–44]. Here, following the literature, these phases are referred to as  α - Al(Mn,Fe)Si, but various compositions have been reported over the years, e.g. Al 2.5- 4 Si 2  [45–50]. Also, the Mn:Fe ratio in the alloy and the aging conditions affects composition and  crystal structure, e.g., changing from Im 3  to Pm these  α -phases during casting is undesirable because they deplete the matrix of Mn and thus  reduce the solid-solution strengthening effect. Large intermetallic phases that can form upon  casting are also unwanted because they embrittle the alloy and induce grain-refinement, which is  deleterious to the creep resistance. The formation of Mn-Si containing  would, however, fulfill our goal of removing the Si from the matrix.  

Several studies have concluded that Mn does not interact with Sc and/or Zr, if no other  ACCEPTED elements are present [42,51–54]. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and energy-dispersive  spectroscopy (EDS) have confirmed that Mn is not detected in the L1 2  Al 3 (Sc,Zr)  nano precipitates and Sc and Zr are not found in the Mn-bearing phases [52]. Jia et al. compared  the precipitation kinetics of two Al-Mn-Zr and Al-Mn-Zr-Si-Fe alloys, with the following  composition: Al-0.5Mn-0.05Zr(-0.15Si-0.10Fe) at.% [42]. The authors reported the formation of  6 Mn, Al 6 (Mn,Fe) and  α -Al(Mn,Fe)Si  precipitates in the alloy containing Si and Fe. Only a small number density of Al 6 Mn primary  precipitates was observed in the Si- and Fe- free alloy. The lack of Si prevented, however, an  enhancement of Zr’s diffusivity and thus inhibited the formation of a sufficiently high number  density of L1 2  Al 3 Zr precipitates upon aging [42]. Vlach et al. have investigated the mechanical  properties and phase transformations in Al-Sc-Zr [53] and Al-Mn-Sc-Zr [54] alloys upon aging.  A negligible amount of Si was present in the alloys (0.01 at.%). Molybdenum additions to Al have been recently studied by Farkoosh et al., who reported  the formation of  -Al(Fe,Mn,Mo)Si [55] nano precipitates upon addition  of Mo, with and without Mn, in Al-7Si-0.5Cu-0.3Mg (wt.%), a hypoeutectic Al-Si alloy. TEM observations revealed that the nano precipitates, with a  platelet shape and with broad lengths and thicknesses distributions (i.e. 70 to 600 nm long and 20  to 80 nm thick, depending of alloy composition and aging duration), are coherent with the matrix  when both Mn and Mo are added, due to the stabilization of their structure in a b.c.c. lattice  (Im 3 ), as opposed to a simple-cubic lattice (Pm 3 ) if the Mn:Fe ratio is high. As demonstrated in  Fig. 1  [3,57,58], Mn has an intermediate diffusivity in Al, slower than Sc but faster than Zr, whereas  Mo diffuses extremely slowly in Al, e.g., it is 200 times slower than Zr at 400 °C. The  α - Al(Mn,Mo)Si-phase could possibly form before the stable Al 3 Zr shell is fully formed and  encapsulate  the Al (Sc,Er) nuclei, which would compromise their thermal stability and  coarsening resistance. The purpose of this article is to test the hypothesis that Fe-free  nano precipitates can be precipitated subsequently to the L1 lean alloy (0.1 at.%). With that goal in mind, the precipitation behavior in an Al-Zr-Sc-Er-Si-Mn- Mo alloy was investigated, where the concentrations of Sc, Er, Zr and Si are held as close as  possible to the original Al-Zr-Sc-Er-Si alloy [27,32] for comparative purposes, and 0.08 at.% Mo  and 0.4 at.% Mn are added. The values are very close to the maximum solid-solubilities in the  Al-Mo and Al-Mn binary systems [41,55]. To determine if Fe is needed to form the Fe- containing  -Al(Fe,Mn,Mo)Si-phase, 0.1 at.% Fe was added to a second alloy.

# 5.   Conclusions  

The effects of micro-additions of 0.1 at.% Mo and 0.4 at.% Mn to a dilute Al-0.10Zr- 0.01Sc-0.01Er-0.10Si (at.%) base alloy (able to create L1 2  Al 3 (Zr,Sc,Er) nano precipitates upon
