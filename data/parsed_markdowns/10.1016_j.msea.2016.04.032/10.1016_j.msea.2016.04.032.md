# a b s t r a c t  

Ni-26W-6Cr based superalloy is considered a potential structure material for the molten salt reactors due to its high strength and good compatibility with the  ﬂ uoride salt. In the present work, the temperature dependence of the tensile behavior of the alloy was studied by tensile tests in the temperature range of 25 – 850  ° C. This alloy exhibited a good ductility at RT and 450  ° C, a ductility minimum from 650 to 750  ° C and an intermediate ductility at 850  ° C. TEM and EBSD characterization was performed on specimens tested at three typical temperature points (RT, 650  ° C and 850  ° C) to determine the deformation and fracture mechanisms accounting for the intermediate temperature embrittlement. At RT, the grain boundaries can accommodate enough dislocations to provide compatibility of the sliding between ad- jacent grains, then M 6 C carbides act as crack origins and cause the fracture. In case of 650  ° C, the grain boundaries cannot withstand the local stress even if only a small number of dislocation pile-ups exist. The premature cracks at grain boundaries impede the development of plastic deformation from single slips to multiple ones and cause the low ductility. If tested at 850  ° C, the fracture process is retarded by the dynamic recovery and local dynamic recrystallization at crack tips.

# 1. Introduction  

Molten salt reactor (MSR) is a class of nuclear reactors in which the primary nuclear reactor coolant, or even the fuel itself, is molten salt mixtures  [1 – 3] . Since the  ﬁ rst test reactor — Molten- Salt Reactor Experiment (MSRE) went critical in 1965 and was operated at about 650  ° C for  ﬁ ve years in Oak Ridge National La- boratory (ORNL)  [4] , many countries have made great efforts to develop new designs based MSR concept, including the recent Thorium Molten Salt Reactor (TMSR)  [5] , Fluoride Salt-Cooled High Temperature Reactors (FHRs)  [6]  and Molten Salt Fast Reactor (MSFR)  [7] . Due to the wide liquid temperature ranges of  ﬂ uoride salts from 450  ° C to 1400  ° C  [8 , 9] , the common interest in these designs is to raise the service temperature to reach salts ’  full po- tential and get a higher thermal ef ﬁ ciency. However, structural materials will be a bottleneck in these higher temperature designs [10] .  

In fact, some preliminary and signi ﬁ cant studies on Ni-26W- 6Cr ternary alloys or related alloys have been carried out for MSFR. The phase stability of Ni-W-Cr alloy and the transmutation cycle of W were evaluated based on theoretical calculations, and a good system stability and a small proportion of transmutation has been determined  [13] . It has been found that the remarkable short range order (SRO) occurred in Ni-W-Cr alloys, which plays a major role on the hardening mechanisms for room temperature (RT) and less accentuated role for high temperature (800  ° C)  [19] . The  

Hastelloy N is a Ni-Mo-Cr based superalloy developed in ORNL for MSRE, which possesses good chemical compatibility with the

The texture evolution and twin formation in Ni-26W-6Cr alloys were reported in other two studies  [21 , 22] . Our very recent work  [23]  in- vestigated the strengthening effect of W on the Ni-xW-6Cr alloys and the results showed that W can improve both the tensile strength and ductility of the alloys at RT due to the twin boundary density. However, the key advantage of this alloy, namely the high temperature mechanical properties, has rarely been con ﬁ rmed and reported in detail so far. Yield strength and tensile strength at temperature are used to set time independent primary stress limits for structural materials in various high temperature design codes. Therefore, it is essential to evaluate high-temperature ten- sile properties of the Ni-26W-6Cr alloy.  

In this work, tensile tests of one Ni-26W-6Cr based superalloy were carried out from RT to 850  ° C. Additionally, strengthening mechanism, dynamic recovery and dynamic recrystallization (DRX) process, and fracture feature are discussed in detail.  

# 2. Experimental procedure  

The chemical composition of Ni-26W-6Cr based superalloy used in present work is listed in  Table 1 . The cast ingot was pre- pared by vacuum induction melting (VIM) and then hot forged into 40 mm-in-diameter bar. The bar was solution-treated at 1200  ° C for two hours, followed by water quenching.  

The metallographic sample was mechanically polished and etched in aqua regia. The microstructures were characterized using a scanning electron microscope (SEM, Zeiss LEO 1530VP) equipped with an energy-dispersive spectrometer (EDS). Phase constituent in this alloy was detected by Bruker D8 advance X-ray with a Cu K α  radiation source ( λ 0.1504 nm).  

Tensile specimens with dimensions of 1 mm thickness, 3.5 mm gage width and 18 mm gauge length were machined along the length direction of the bar. The tensile tests were carried out using a Zwick Z100 mechanical testing machine at RT, 450  ° C, 550  ° C, 650  ° C, 750  ° C and 850  ° C with a constant strain rate of 0.1 mm/ min to rupture. The specimens were maintained in the furnace for 10 min at each temperature before tensile tests. A temperature ﬂ uctuation in the furnace did not exceed  7 2  ° C over the gauge length. At least three identical specimens were tested under each condition. One specimen was tested to a strain of 7% at RT with the same strain rate and then taken from the machine without rupture.  

After the tensile tests, the fractographs of specimens were analyzed by SEM. The local plastic strain distribution and re- crystallization in the severe deformation zone were studied by electron backscattered diffraction (EBSD), which was performed on the SEM equipped with an Oxford Instruments AZtec system. Specimens prepared for EBSD received a vibration-assisted  ﬁ nal polishing (VibroMet2) to improve the surface  ﬁ nish and to remove the damaged layer caused by the mechanical polishing.  

The foils for transmission electron microscopy (TEM, Tecnai G2 F20) observation were cut near the edge of the fracture (broken specimens) or at the middle of the gauge (the unbroken speci- men). After these foils were prepared by electrochemical polishing at 3  ° C in an 8% perchloric carbinol solution, deformation mi-   crostructures were investigated using a JEOL 2100 TEM operated at 200 kV.

# 3. Results  

# 3.1. Microstructures of Ni-26W-6Cr based superalloy  

Fig. 1  shows the XRD pattern of Ni-26W-6Cr based superalloy. The peaks of M 6 C carbides coexist with that of  γ  matrix but appear to be too weak due to their small quantity. The lattice parameter of  γ  matrix (0.358 nm) was obtained from the (111) diffraction peak.  

As shown in  Fig. 2 a, the microstructure of this alloy consists of equiaxed grains with a size of 30  m m and stringers of second-phase particles. The size of these particles varied from 0.5  m m to more than 3  m m. Further analyses by TEM prove that these second-phase particles are M 6 C type carbides, which is consistent with the XRD results ( Fig. 2 b). The enrichment of W, Mo and Si can be observed in M 6 C carbides from the linear SEM-EDS ( Fig. 2 c). The TEM-EDS point analyses give the composition of M 6 C carbides as shown in  Fig. 2 d. The main metallic atoms in M 6 C carbides are W, Ni, Mo and Cr. The Si atoms are deemed to occupy the metallic sites and promote the formation of M 6 C carbides in Hastelloy N alloy  [24] . Si atoms are likely to play a similar role in this alloy.  

# 3.2. Tensile properties  

The tensile properties of Ni-26W-6Cr based superalloy, tested from RT to 850  ° C, are summarized in  Fig. 3 . With the test temperature increasing, the yield strength (YS) decreases continuously. The ultimate tensile strength (UTS) drops at same speed as YS does from RT to 450  ° C, but very rapidly above 450  ° C. It causes a dramatic increase of yield ratio above 450  ° C, which reaches about 1 at 850  ° C. This alloy exhibits a good ductility at RT and 450  ° C and a ductility minimum as low as about 7% in the temperature range of 650 – 750  ° C. It is striking that the ductility returns to more than 30% at 850  ° C.  

The engineering stress-strain curves at different test temperatures are plotted in  Fig. 4 a. It can be seen that the  ﬂ ow stresses exhibit a strain hardening to rupture at all temperature points except 850  ° C at which the  ﬂ ow stress reaches the maximum near the yield point and then gradually decreases at high strain zone. The stress serrations occur in the whole plastic deformation stage in the specimens tested from 450  ° C to 850  ° C ( Fig. 4 b and c). However, it is absent in the ones tested at RT. The shape of serrations varied between saw tooth type ( Fig. 4 b) and sinu- soidal type ( Fig. 4 c), depending on whether they accompanied strain hardening sections of the curve or strain softening sections respectively. The stress drop, stress raise and period of the serrations or the oscillatory are shown in  Fig. 4 d. From 450 to 750  ° C, this alloy exhibited serrations upon reaching yield with a saw-tooth shape. The raise and drop of stress serrations were about from 9 MPa to 12 MPa. The period of these serrations was about from 0.03% to 0.05%. The relatively large stress raise indicate that serrations occur at the strain hardening stage. At 850  ° C, oscillations with a sinusoidal shape were accompanying softening sections of the curves with amplitudes between 2 MPa and 3 MPa in the strain range of 3 – 10%. The oscillatory behavior tends to dampen out at larger strains, as shown in  Fig. 4 c.

# 3.3. Fractographs  

Fig. 5  shows the fractographs for the specimens tested at different tempera- tures. It can be observed that a lot of deep and small dimples exist on the fracture of the specimen tested at RT ( Fig. 5 a). The presence of dimples and cracked M 6 C carbides indicates the occurrence of the transgranular ductile fracture in the spe- cimens tested at RT, which in accordance with the high ductility as shown in  Fig. 3 . As the test temperature increases to 550  ° C, not only these larger and shallower dimples and cracked M 6 C carbides, but also secondary cracks appear on the frac- ture surface ( Fig. 5 b). This result implies it is a mixed intergranular and transgra- nular fracture. At a higher test temperature of 650  ° C, the Ni-26W-6Cr based superalloy exhibits typical intergranular fracture surface, in which numerous sec- ondary cracks can be observed ( Fig. 5 c). When specimens were tested at 850  ° C, the outline of raised grains ( Fig. 5 d) is similar to that shown in  Fig. 5 c, indicating the intergranular fracture occurs in the alloy. In consideration of the low Cr content in this alloy, the layer covering on the facture surface is probably oxide layer, which masks a lot of details, such as secondary cracks.  

Fig. 6  gives the fractographs observed along the stress axis at different test temperatures. The smooth facture surface indicates the transgranular fracture at RT as shown in  Fig. 6 a, while the jagged facture surface implies the intergranular fracture above 650  ° C as shown in  Fig. 6 c and d. These results agree with the conclusions derived from  Fig. 5 . More details of the zone below facture surface are revealed in higher resolution. From  Fig. 6 a, the dense slip bands are observed to exist along different directions, which prove the activity of multi-slip. Meanwhile, many cracked or decohered M 6 C carbide particles can be found in the deformation zone. They are associated with the formations of dimples. As the test temperature increases to 550  ° C and 650  ° C, numerous intergranular cracks or wedge cracks are visible away from the fracture, which are predominantly normal to the applied stress and cover the entire grain width ( Fig. 6 b and c). A few of them extend along the grain boundaries as greater ones as shown in  Fig. 6 c. It is of note that more extended intergranular cracks form away from the fracture in the specimen tested at 850  ° C ( Fig. 6 d).  

# 4. Discussion  

The results that have been presented indicate that there is a complex relation between tensile behavior and the test tempera- ture in Ni-26W-6Cr based superalloy, which will be investigated by TEM and EBSD in this section. Three typical temperature points, namely RT, 650  ° C and 850  ° C, are picked out due to the signi ﬁ cant differences of ductility and fracture mode at the three points. As the number of dis- locations in a pile-up increases, the shear stress acting on the leading dislocation causes the activation of dislocation sources in neighboring grain. At the same time, a few multiple slips are present in another grain. During the tensile tests, the grain boundaries can accommodate enough geometrically necessary dislocations (GND) to provide compatibility of the sliding between adjacent grains. When the specimen was tested to rupture, mul- tiple slips have been activated and lead to a high-density dis- location distribution as shown in  Fig. 8 c. These dense slips leave obvious surface slip traces on the surface of samples as shown in Fig. 7 b. The intersection between dislocations on different slip system is responsible for the work hardening of this alloy. This alloy get its big RT ductility by slip mechanism. On the other hand, the fracture at RT is related to the primary M 6 C carbide particles. The strain incompatibility between the M 6 C carbide particles and the matrix leads to generation of GND in the matrix as shown in Fig. 7 b. The internal tensile stress due to these dislocation adds to that of the applied stress so that the total stress acting to cause particle fracture or decohesion from the matrix as can be seen in Fig. 6 a. With the applied stress increasing, voids around carbide particles nucleate and grow throughout the materials volume. Further void linkup by plastic  ﬂ ow in the neck volume causes the ﬁ nal fracture  [25] . The dimples on the fracture surface also prove the particles fracture, decohesion and void linkup.  

When test temperature increases to 650  ° C, this alloy exhibits a very low ductility (7%) which is related to the intergranular frac- ture. The dislocation con ﬁ guration of deformed zone is char- acterized by single slide on the slip system ( Fig. 7 d), which is si- milar to that in the specimen tested to the same strain (7%) at RT ( Fig. 7 a). In order to investigate the dislocation con ﬁ guration in a larger scale, we carried out EBSD measurements and got the mapping and values of kernel average misorientation (KAM) in the Ni-26W-6Cr based superalloys tested at RT, 650  ° C and 850  ° C are shown in  Fig. 8 . KAM is the average misorientation between every pixel and its surrounding pixels in the EBSD measurements, which can assess the local strain and re ﬂ ect the density of GND in crys- talline materials  [26 ,  27] . For the specimen tested at RT, the higher KAM can be observed to be at the grain boundaries and carbide- rich regions in the interior of the grains ( Fig. 8 a), indicating a higher strain concentration degree and a larger density of GND in these sites as shown in  Fig. 7 (b, c). In contrast, the uniform dis- tribution of KAM proves that there is no obvious strain and density concentration in the specimen tested at 650  ° C. This result agrees with the dislocation con ﬁ guration as shown in  Fig. 7 d, in which only a small number of dislocation pile-ups can be observed and are not enough to lead to a high KAM value at grain boundaries. It can be concluded that the grain boundaries are capable of ac- commodating enough GND at RT but cannot withstand the local stress even if only a small number of dislocation pile-ups exist at 650  ° C. Then, intergranular fracture initiated by triple junction cracks became the major fracture mechanism at 650  ° C. Grain boundary cracks result from the grain boundary cavities and the cavity formation depends on the locally applied stresses and grain boundary surface energy that relates to the coherence of the boundary  [26] . The cavity formation can be described by the cri- tical radius for stable cavity development, r c , where r c ¼ 2 γ s / s , and γ s  is the grain boundary surface energy and  s  is the local tensile stress normal to the grain boundary  [26] . At 650  ° C, the local stress due to the dislocation pile-ups and the applied stress cooperate to make cavities form and coalesce into cracks at grain boundaries. The deformation and fracture behaviors are responsible for the intermediate temperature embrittlement. At RT, the grain boundaries can accommodate enough GND to provide compat- ibility of the sliding between adjacent grains, then M 6 C carbides act as crack origins and cause the fracture. In case of tensile tests at 650  ° C, the locally applied stresses related to sliding is enough to lead to grain boundary cavitation. Failure  ﬁ nally occurs at wedge cracks located at grain boundary triple points. The premature cracks at grain boundaries impede the development of plastic deformation from single slips to multiple ones and cause a low ductility at 650  ° C.  

When Ni-26W-6Cr based alloy was tested at 850  ° C, its ducti- lity returns to more than 30% in spite of the intergranular fracture, and the strain softening indicates the dynamic recovery exists in the plastic deformation process ( Fig. 4 ). As shown in  Fig. 9 , it is clearly seen that dislocation-free cells, subdivided by dense dis- location walls (DDWs), are developed in grain interiors. It has been reported that W was effective in lowering stacking fault energy of nickel  [23 ,  25 ,  28] . For one alloy with low stacking fault energy, such as Ni-26W-6Cr based superalloy, the dynamic recovery can- not offset the dislocation multiplication, and then DRX occur. Sub- grain boundaries evolve from the DDWs migrate and absorb the dislocations in the deformed zone, then lead to the nucleation and growth of recrystallized grains. Oscillations ( Fig. 4 c) is just one obvious evidence that DRX occur in the specimen tested at 850  ° C. At a low strain rate, it takes longer time (and greater strain) to accumulate the critical dislocation density, which is needed to trigger DRX. The high-density structures develop synchronously in all grains and cause the stress maxima of the oscillations. While the stress minima corresponds to the occurrence of DRX. Strain hardening and DRX happen again and again and lead to the os- cillations in the stress-strain curve at 850  ° C ( Fig. 4 c).  

The recrystallized fraction maps directly indicate that local DRX has occurred ( Fig. 10 ). The internal average misorientation angle within the grain was measured by Aztec software. If the average angle in a grain exceeds the user-de ﬁ ned minimum angle to de ﬁ ne a subgrain,  θ c , the grain is classi ﬁ ed as being  “ deformed ” . Some grains consist of subgrains whose internal misorientation is under θ c  but the misorientation from subgrain to subgrain is above  θ c . In that case the grain is classed as  “ substructured ” . All the remaining grains are classi ﬁ ed as recrystallized. Then the deformed, sub- structured and recrystallized grains are assigned the color red, yellow and blue in the recrystallized fraction maps, respectively. In the specimen tested at 850  ° C, many recrystallized grains with a size less than 10  m m are distributed at grain boundaries, more typically, at the tips and edges of cracks and in the vicinity of carbides ( Fig. 10 a). The high magni ﬁ cation image of the cracks and adjacent recrystallized grains is given in  Fig. 10 b. As the de- formation continues, the locally applied stress related to sliding is enough to lead to grain boundary cavitation and then wedge cracks. As shown in  Fig. 8 a, the stress concentration at the tips and edges of cracks is much higher than that in other regions, and there is enough energy for dislocation motion and rearrangement to form cells or subgrains, which is a precursor of DRX. A similar phenomenon has been reported in Refs.  [29 – 31] . Then, the  ﬁ ne recrystallized grains dissipate the deformation energy and release the stress concentration at the crack tips and hander farther crack propagation. A similar effect was also reported in ZK60 magne- sium alloy, in which subsequent local DRX in the high strain rate rolling can inhibit competitive crack initiation  [29] .  

As shown in  Figs. 6 d,  8 a and  10 a, a lot of intergranular cracks or wedge cracks are visible away from the fracture in the specimen tested at 850  ° C. The development of these second cracks during the plastic deformation process is illustrated in  Fig. 11 . We ex- amined the second cracks in the deformed zones, and estimated that about half of the gauge width was covered by cracks. It is reasonable that the effective stressed area is also about half of the initial one. Before the fracture, the engineering stress is 175 MPa, and the stress on the effective stressed area can be estimated to be 350 MPa. Approximately, the stress on the effective stressed area during the whole plastic deformation was plotted as a red line ( Fig. 11 ). Due to the continuous formation of second cracks, the effective stressed area in the tensile specimen decreases and the stress concentration becomes more severe at grain boundaries (red line), which cannot be eliminated by the dynamic recovery and local DRX. At last, the specimens fracture at one primary crack, accompanied by numerous intergranular second cracks in the deformed zones.  

In contrast, there are fewer deformed and so-called re- crystallized grains in the specimen tested at 650  ° C ( Fig. 10 c). The fewer deformed grains result from the inadequate plastic de- formation at this temperature. Only single slips are activated be- fore the specimen rupture at a small strain of 7% ( Fig. 8 d). So it is reasonable that relatively fewer grains have an internal average misorientation angle above  θ c  and are classi ﬁ ed as being de- formed. On the other hand, it is very suspicious that the blue grains are real recrystallized ones when this alloy were tested at a temperature as low as 650  ° C ( Fig. 10 c). As another noteworthy phenomenon, the size of these blue grains is about 20  m m, which seems a little large for the local recrystallized ones. It can be de- duced that these blue grains are just these undeformed original ones other than recrystallized ones. The corresponding high magni ﬁ cation image of the cracks is shown in  Fig. 10 d. It can be observed that these is no relationship between the undeformed original grains with the cracks. Without the dynamic recovery and local DRX at crack tips as described at 850  ° C, the Ni-26W-6Cr alloy fracture in a rapid process at one primary crack ( Fig. 7 ). It can be concluded that the difference in tensile ductility at 650  ° C and 850  ° C results from the dynamic recovery and local DRX. For the former, the cracks develop rapidly at grain boundaries without any obstruction and cause a sudden failure at a low strain. In case of

# 5. Conclusion  

(1) The microstructure of Ni-26W-6Cr based superalloy consists of equiaxed grains and stringers of primary M 6 C carbides with the enrichment of W, Mo and Si.

 (2) If tested at RT, the grain boundaries can accommodate enough geometrically necessary dislocations to provide compatibility of the sliding between adjacent grains, then M 6 C carbides act as crack origins and cause the fracture.

 (3) In case of 650  ° C, the grain boundaries cannot withstand the local stress even if only a small number of dislocation pile-ups exist. The premature cracks at grain boundaries impede the development of plastic deformation from single slips to mul- tiple ones and cause a very low ductility (7%).

 (4) When tested at 850  ° C, the fracture process is retarded by the dynamic recovery and local DRX at crack tips. The ductility returns to more than 30%.
