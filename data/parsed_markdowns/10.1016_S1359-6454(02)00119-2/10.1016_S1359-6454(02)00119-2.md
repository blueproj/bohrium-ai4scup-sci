# Abstract  

The polycrystalline nickel-base superalloy (C263) is used for stationary components in aero-engines such as combus- tion chambers, casings, liners, exhaust ducting and bearing housings. It is a ﬁne-precipitate strengthened alloy at 800 ° C, with a precipitate solvus temperature of 925 ° C. Below the solvus, the precipitate coarsens at elevated temperature. A critical precipitate size exists below which particle cutting is the rate-controlling creep mechanism. Above the critical size, it is dislocation pinning and climb. Once the solvus temperature has been exceeded, however, the rate-controlling mechanism becomes pinning and climb within a dislocation network.  

This paper presents physically-based constitutive equations for creep deformation in C263. In the presence of the  g  precipitate, populations of climbing and gliding dislocations are assumed to exist in a similar way to that proposed by Dyson and Osgerby [3]. Climbing dislocations are assumed to be pinned at precipitates. The dependence of steady state glide dislocation ﬂux can then be obtained as a function of precipitate volume fraction. Above the  g   solvus, the pinning process is different and results from the establishment of a dislocation network, the size of which determines the pinning distance. The physical constants arising in the equations have been determined by the conventional minimis- ation of errors between experimental and calculated creep curves. They have also been determined quite independently using fundamental data and experiments. Remarkable agreement between the two sets of physical constants is achieved. The constitutive equations have been shown to capture the material’s creep deformation characteristics over a broad range of temperature, both below and above the  g   solvus. In addition, the effect of precipitate coarsening on creep rate is correctly captured, together with the effect of prior ageing on subsequent creep rate.  

The damage processes of cavitation and multiplication of mobile dislocation density have been coupled with the constitutive equations and used to predict failure in constant and variable stress creep.   2002 Acta Materialia Inc. Published by Elsevier Science Ltd. All rights reserved.

# 1. Introduction  

Aero-engine components operating under in-ser- vice conditions are often subjected to a range of complex cyclic mechanical and thermal loading, leading to combined creep and cyclic plasticity. The polycrystalline nickel-base superalloy (C263) is a commercial alloy used for stationary compo- nents in aero-engines such as combustion cham- bers, casings, liners, exhaust ducting and bearing housings. It is a ﬁne-precipitate strengthened alloy at 800 ° C, with a precipitate solvus temperature of 925 ° C [1]. Combustion chamber applications require the material to undergo temperature ﬂuc- tuations between 20 and 950 ° C, and the tempera- ture range is therefore such that the precipitate solvus can be exceeded during in-service oper- ation. Signiﬁcant microstructural change is there- fore likely to occur during ordinary operation, lead- ing to quite profound changes in the creep mechanisms in the material, controlling both defor- mation and component life. This paper is con- cerned with the establishment of a physically-based creep model which explicitly accounts for micro- structural change, and its inﬂuence on creep defor- mation and failure, in poly-crystalline nickel-base alloy C263 for temperatures both above and below the  g   solvus.  

# 2. Mechanistic aspects of creep in C263  

C263 is solid solution strengthened by chro- mium, cobalt and molybdenum. After standard heat treatment at 1150 ° C for 2 h, quenching in water and ageing at 800 ° C for 8 h followed by air coo- ling, the material has a mean grain size of 104  µ m, 9.5% volume fraction of Ni3(Ti,Al)  g   with an average precipitate size of 22 nm, many annealing twins and almost continuous precipitation of M 23 C 6 carbide at grain boundaries. The precipitation solvus temperature is approximately 925 ° C. Creep deformation becomes signiﬁcant once the tempera- ture has exceeded about 600 ° C. In standard creep tests at temperatures below the precipitation solvus, particle ( g  ) cutting, dislocation bowing and climbing have been observed, dependent on the size of the  g   precipitate [20–24]. During iso- thermal creep tests, the volume fraction of precipi- tate remains constant but the precipitate coarsens, that is, becomes more widely spaced, and increases in size. The rate of coarsening has been found to be independent of applied stress [19], and the kin- etics are discussed later. From TEM examination of crept specimens, there is evidence of particle cutting by matrix dislocations when the precipitate size is less than a critical particle size, and it is this that dominates in creep deformation. As the precipitate coarsens, eventually the critical particle size is achieved and exceeded, and dislocation (Orowan) bowing takes over and climb occurs as the rate-controlling deformation mechanism. Cavi- tation occurs at grain boundaries during creep in this material, but other microstructural ‘damage’ such as that due to precipitate coarsening and mul- tiplication of mobile dislocations are also observed. Below the  g  solvus temperature, the creep behav- iour is likely to be dominated by the presence of the  g  . Above the solvus, however, dislocation pin- ning must occur by other means. Both of these cases are addressed in this paper.

In the present paper, we aim to present a uniﬁed, physically-based model for creep in the nickel-base superalloy C263, over a temperature range that includes the  g   solvus. Many of the ideas reviewed above are employed here. Our approach is to argue that the creep rate is determined by average velo- city of glide dislocations, and that this, in turn, is determined by the nature of the obstacles that pin gliding dislocations, and the thermal activation events that are necessary to enable dislocation climb. We argue that the model presented must give constitutive equations similar to those of Dyson and McLean [5] for the case when the second phase precipitate is present, but that the fea- tures of Gibbs [10] model must be reproduced in the absence of a precipitate above the  g   solvus temperature.

# 3.3. Precipitate cutting  

Microstructural examination of crept C263 test specimens has indicated that a further rate-con- trolling process operates, namely, precipitate shear- ing, or cutting. # 4.2. Effect of prior ageing on creep  

In addition to the standard heat treatment speci- ﬁed above, a number of test samples were sub- jected to longer periods of heat treatment with the objective of generating different  g   precipitate sizes prior to subjecting the samples to creep testing. Test samples were aged for 8 h (as before), 30 h and 490 h, generating precipitate sizes, prior to creep testing, of 22, 45 and 85 nm, respectively. The samples were then creep tested at 800 ° C at 160 MPa, and the results are shown in Fig. 9. The corresponding predicted creep curves are shown in Fig. 9, which were obtained simply by specifying the appropriate initial precipitate size in the model equations. The predicted results for 8 and 30 h age- ing show that the creep curves diverge only after an elapsed creep time of about 0.7  ×  10 6 s. This is because before this time is achieved, the precipitate size is smaller than the critical value, and cutting is the rate-controlling mechanism in both cases. The 30 h ageing curve diverges at this time because the critical precipitate size has been achi- eved due to coarsening, and bowing has become rate-controlling. Because of this, precipitate coars- ening has a major effect on the creep strain rate.

In the case of the 490 h ageing, the initial precipi- tate size is already equal to the critical size (85 nm), so that dislocation bowing at precipitates is rate-controlling from the start of the test. The ste- ady-state strain rates are reasonably well predicted by the model, together with the effect of ageing on subsequent creep deformation.  

One further creep test was carried out in which the material had been aged for 146 h prior to creep testing, resulting in an initial precipitate size of 63 nm. The material was then subjected to creep at 800 ° C for a time of 115 h, at which the test was terminated, and the new precipitate size measured to be 73 nm. The creep curve is shown in Fig. 9. TEM was also carried out to identify whether precipitate shearing or dislocation bowing were operating. Examination revealed that bowing was occuring, but little, if any, cutting. It was con- cluded from this that the critical particle size, 2 r sc , that determines the change from cutting to bowing, lies between about 63 and 73 nm at 800 ° C. For this reason, the experimentally observed critical particle size was taken to be 70 nm in Section 4.1.

# 5. Conclusions  

A physically-based model has been presented for the creep of polycrystalline nickel-base superal- loy C263. The  g   solvus temperature of this material is about 925 ° C. Below this temperature,

the material undergoes creep at a rate which is determined by precipitate cutting, or by dislocation bowing at pinning occurring at either the  g   pre- cipitate particles, or within the dislocation network. Precipitate coarsening contributes signiﬁcantly to the creep rate if bowing at precipitate particles is rate-controlling. Particle coarsening has been quantiﬁed and embodied in the model. Above the γ   solvus, the separation of pinning distance is deﬁned by the dislocation network.  

The physical constants in the equations have been determined ﬁrstly by standard optimisation techniques which minimise the error between cal- culated and experimental macroscopic creep curves, and secondly, quite independently by use of fundamental measurements. Remarkable agree- ment is achieved. The model contains just two empirical constants to characterise the evolutions of mobile dislocation density and cavitation in this material. Their dependence on temperature has been determined.  

The model reproduces the creep characteristics of the material both below and above the  g   solvus temperature. In addition, the effect of ageing, lead- ing to precipitate growth prior to creep defor- mation, is reasonably well predicted. Creep tests with varying stress level are also modelled, and good comparisons with experiments are achieved.
