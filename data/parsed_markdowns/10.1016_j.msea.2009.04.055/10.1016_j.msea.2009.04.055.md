Mechanical behaviour of a nickel-based superalloy, RR1000, has been investigated at 650   ◦ C under cyclic and dwell loading conditions. The microstructural characteristics of the alloy have been studied using scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM), and the distribution patterns of the dislocations and slip planes have been compared between samples tested under fatigue and creep–fatigue loading conditions. Constitutive behaviour of the alloy was described by a uniﬁed constitutive model, where both cyclic plastic and viscoplastic strains were represented by one inelastic strain. The results show that the precipitation state is very stable at 650   ◦ C and only minor differences exist in the dislocation arrangements formed under pure fatigue and combined creep and fatigue conditions. Hence, a uniﬁed constitutive model seems to be justiﬁed in describing and predicting the constitutive behaviour in both cases.

Constitutive models have been developed to overcome the increasing complexities involved in the deformation processes of modern alloys, and to permit quantitative descriptions of material behaviour in terms of externally measured variables and inter- nal variables. Such an approach is useful in situations where microstructural characteristics are associated with the parame- ters in the constitutive formulation. For example, the directional dependence on the material resistance to dislocation ﬂow is often modelled through kinematic stress variables, which represent the net effect of spatially ﬂuctuating internal stress ﬁelds on the veloc- ity of dislocations necessary to maintain the current applied stress level. Kinematic stress arises from the unequal directional resis- tance to plastic ﬂow resulting from prior deformations, and it manifests itself when the applied stress is released and disloca- tions move from “soft” region to “hard” region  [16] . Since this stress is likely to have several sources, models have been developed to allow multiple additive back stress variables  [17] , where the ini- tial transient behaviour represents short-range fast kinetics while long-range behaviour describes slower kinetics at larger strains.  

Due to their superior mechanical properties at elevated tem- perature, nickel-based superalloys are favoured for applications in rotating discs in gas turbine engines. Modelling of fatigue  [1–3] , creep  [4,5]  and fatigue–creep interaction  [6–10]  of nickel alloys has been carried out using a variety of analytical and numeri- cal approaches, including so-called uniﬁed constitutive models. In these models, plastic and creep strains were represented by one inelastic strain to simulate the viscoplastic behaviour of materi- als. One of the most popular uniﬁed models, the uniﬁed Chaboche model, has been adopted by Tong and Vermeulen  [11]  successfully to simulate the cyclic behaviour with and without short strain-hold periods for a wrought nickel-based superalloy, Waspaloy. There are typically a large number of materials parameters associated with the model to be determined using the experimental data, hence the model behaviour critically depends on the experimental data from which the model parameters are calibrated, the qual- ity of the experimental data were shown  [12]  to be important. A dynamic recovery variable was found useful in the description of cyclic stress relaxation  [13,14] ; while plastic strain memory was also incorporated in the uniﬁed model  [15]  to improve the simula- tion of isotropic hardening. The understanding and control of grain growth is of particular technical signiﬁcance, as material properties are strongly depen- dent on the average grain size and grain size distribution. Usually, the term grain coarsening refers to migration of grain boundaries resulting in an increase in average grain size of a polycrystalline solid after annealing as a continuous process, as opposed to the discontinuous phenomenon of primary re crystal lisa tion  [18] . The phenomenon of grain growth is most signiﬁcant in powder metal- lurgy (PM) superalloys destined for high-temperature application. PM superalloys possess excellent tensile strength and toughness with good fatigue resistance because of their ﬁne grain size  [19] . A ﬁne grain size is normally advantageous for the fatigue behaviour at lower-to-intermediate temperatures. But at high temperatures, grain boundary sliding may gain signiﬁcance requiring a large grain size for sufﬁcient creep and high-temperature fatigue resistance [20] .  

Cyclic and creep deformation in precipitation hardened alloys depends largely on the size, volume and distribution of the precipitated particles in the matrix. In nickel-based superalloys, precipitates of the coherent   ′   (Ni 3 (Al,Ti)) phase act as effec- tive barriers to dislocation motion. This stable high temperature phase is primarily responsible for the static and dynamic strength at elevated temperatures in these alloys. For instance at room temperature, deformation in underaged hardened nickel-based superalloys occurs as a consequence of shearing of precipitates by dislocations. In the case of coarse incoherent   ′   particles (overaged condition), the dislocation bends between the particles leaving a dislocation ring around each particle (Orowan loop). At higher temperatures, there are a variety of interactions between disloca- tions and precipitates besides cutting and Orowan looping, such as climbing, cutting by pair-wise coupled dislocations and interfa- cial pinning  [21–23] . Cyclic plasticity and viscoplasticity of these alloys are direct consequences of the motion of dislocations and interactions between dislocations and precipitates.  

Earlier studies were mainly focused on the investigation of the creep behaviour of commercial Ni-based superalloys, including the effect of   ′   rafting  [24,25] . The   ′   phases exhibit pronounced changes in morphology, e.g., in the case of cast alloys rafting from the initial cubic morphology to lamellae takes place, perpendicu- lar or parallel to the applied tensile stress depending on the sign of the lattice misﬁt. It is also well known, that during creep at elevated temperature, a network of misﬁt dislocations form at the   /  ’ inter- face  [26] . Microstructure evaluation under fatigue and combined fatigue and creep deformation conditions has rarely been studied in this type of alloys.  

In this paper, strain-controlled experiments were carried out under cyclic and dwell loading conditions at a typical operational temperature of 650   ◦ C for turbine discs. The microstructural char- acteristics were studied using scanning and transmission electron microscopy for specimens tested under fatigue and fatigue–creep loading conditions. The material behaviour was described by a uni- ﬁed constitutive model, which was calibrated using the uniaxial strain-controlled experimental data.  

# 2. Materials and experimental methods  

Alloy RR1000 is a nickel-based superalloy produced via pow- der metallurgy route and was developed at Rolls-Royce plc for use in the hot section of the aero engine turbine disks. Cylindrical smooth specimens with a diameter of 6.9 mm and gauge length of 12.5 mm were used for the experiments. The tests were carried out on an Instron 8500 servo-hydraulic testing machine with computer- controlled loading spectra programmed as required. An electric resistance furnace was used with the temperature controlled at 650  ±  2   ◦ C. The strain was monitored continuously by means of an extensometer (12.5 + 2.5/ − 1.25 mm).  

The uniaxial strain-controlled cyclic tests were carried out at 650   ◦ C at three strain rates: 0.5% s − 1 , 0.05% s − 1 , 0.005% s − 1 . A strain ratio  R ε  =  − 1 and the strain ranges of 1.2%, 1.6% and 2% were used and the stress–strain hysteresis loops were obtained. Hold peri- ods of 10 s, 100 s and 300 s were introduced in selected cyclic tests to obtain dwell test data at 650   ◦ C. These tests are necessary in the evaluation of the model capacity in predicting creep–fatigue interaction of the material. Full material description, experimental procedures and results were given elsewhere  [14,15] .  

The grain size of the material in the as-received condition and after coarse-grain annealing was investigated by automated elec- tron backscatter diffraction with a Phillips XL30 LaB 6  scanning electron microscope (SEM). The data processing was executed by the software tool TSL OIM TM   (orientation imaging microscopy), which provides a complete description of the crystallographic ori- entations and the grain size distribution in the polycrystalline material. Microstructure stability of the material was studied by examining grain coarsening behaviour. Grain coarsening anneal- ing was performed in an electric furnace at a temperature ranging between 1050   ◦ C and 1250   ◦ C with annealing times from 2 h up to 150 h. Prior to measuring the grain size, the samples were mechani- cally ground and electrolytic ally polished. The polishing was carried out at 14   ◦ C with a voltage of 40 V; and an electrolyte consisting of 10 parts acetic acid (96%) and 1 part perchloric acid (60%) was used. The microstructure of the as-received and fatigued specimens was examined by transmission electron microscopy (TEM). The TEM studies were performed with a Hitachi H-8100 electron micro- scope at 200 kV operating voltage and a LaB 6  ﬁlament. The TEM samples were taken perpendicular to the stress axis. Specimens were prepared using standard preparation techniques including mechanical cutting, grinding, and polishing. Final electrolytical thinning of small discs with a diameter of 3 mm led to a thickness of a few hundred nanometers to obtain a transparency to the elec- tron beam. The electrolyte and the conditions for the electrolytical thinning were the same as described above for surface polishing.

The original uniﬁed Chaboche model as given in  Appendix A was employed to simulate pure fatigue deformation, and the simu- lated results using the optimised material parameters are compared with those obtained experimentally. # 6. Discussion  

The SEM studies show that alloy RR1000 exhibits excellent grain size stability up to 1100   ◦ C, which is well beyond the opera- tional temperature (up to 750   ◦ C) in the hot section of aero engine. Nickel-based superalloys are hardened by ordered   ′   precipitates and their strengthening mechanism largely depends on the size of the   ′   precipitates which are coherently dispersed in the   matrix. These   ′   precipitates act as obstacles to motion of dislo- cations and prevent inelastic deformation of the material. Essential deformation mechanisms of these materials at high temperatures include the shearing of   ′   by dislocations and the climb of dis- locations around   ′   [23,32–34] . During creep and creep–fatigue deformation, dislocation networks mainly form at the   /  ′   inter- face  [34–37]  and the dislocation spacing within these networks at the   /  ′   interface decreases with increasing creep strain  [36] It was suggested that the dislocation network affects the dislo- cation climb kinetics, hence resulting in an increase in creep rate [36] . Based on the TEM observation results on creep specimens of IN738LC, Li and Wahi  [37]  proposed that the local internal stress in the   ′   phase generated by   /  ′   interfacial dislocations is higher than the applied stress due to the shearing effects of the   ′   phase. This explains the continuous increase of the creep rate at high strains.  

The TEM observations of creep-fatigued specimen conﬁrmed the high-density dislocation networks in the    matrix and at the   /  ′ interface ( Fig. 5 ). Some slip bands were identiﬁed indicating the occurrence of fatigue damage. For pure fatigue situation, dislocation arrangements are mainly parallel slip bands, and there is a lack of high-density dislocation networks. In the fatigue case as well as

However, the model in this original form was insufﬁcient in the description of the material behaviour in creep–fatigue situation. This problem was overcome by introducing a dynamic recovery variable in the kinematic hard- ening formulation of the Chaboche model. As suggested in  [11] , a shift of the stress–strain hysteresis loop during the hold periods might be produced by the high density of dislocation networks formed at the   /  ′   interface under creep–fatigue, which cannot be accounted for by the original hardening rules. The introduction of a recovery term in the Chaboche kinematic hardening rules was successful to account for this dislocation network effect, leading to improved prediction of stress relaxation during the strain-hold periods. Under creep–fatigue conditions, experimental results of IN738LC at 950   ◦ C  [38]  showed that the average creep rate dur- ing the hold period exhibited an increase with the increase in the number of cycles. TEM observation for interrupted tests revealed a higher density of dislocations produced by glide processes both in   and   ′   phases at the end of compressive hold period, compared to that after the tensile hold period. The dislocations were homo- geneously distributed in the    matrix and at the   /  ′   interfaces after tensile hold period, and the directional coarsening of   ′   pre- cipitates was visible even after the ﬁrst hold period. The increased creep rate formed with the increase in the number of cycles was attributed to the dislocation substructure inherited from the pre- ceding compressive part of the cycle and directional coarsening of the   ′   precipitates. For the temperature of 650   ◦ C used in this study discernable changes in the dislocation networks cannot be detected.  

Admittedly, the current modelling approach is essentially phe- nomenological and has limitations in that a direct link between the microstructural features and model parameters is difﬁcult to estab- lish. To explicitly consider microstructure details, modelling tools such as discrete dislocation dynamics (DDD)  [35,36]  would be nec- essary to account for the heterogeneous dislocation arrangements observed in the TEM studies, although this is a direction to which our current endeavours are being made, this is beyond the scope of this paper.  

# 7. Conclusions  

Microstructural characteristics and constitutive behaviour of alloy RR1000 have been studied for fatigue and creep–fatigue loading conditions at 650   ◦ C. SEM studies of as-received mate- rial showed stable grain microstructure for annealing at up to 1100   ◦ C, above which grain coarsening was observed, with a sig- niﬁcant increase in grain size observed at 1250   ◦ C. TEM studies of samples tested under fatigue and creep–fatigue loading condi- tions showed similar distribution patterns of the dislocations and slip planes, although higher dislocation densities were found for creep–fatigue tested specimens. Dislocations are typically trapped in the    channels and there is evidence of shearing interaction between   ′   particles and dislocations. The morphology of the   ′ precipitation condition remains stable for both experimental con- ditions.  

Constitutive behaviour of the alloy was successfully described by a uniﬁed constitutive model. The original Chaboche model seems to be adequate to describe the simple cyclic deformation of the material using the optimised model parameters. For creep–fatigue condition, a modiﬁed Chaboche model was used to describe the constitutive behaviour, where a dynamic recovery variable was introduced in the kinematic hardening formulation of the orig- inal Chaboche model to account for the effects of dislocation networks formed due to creep deformation. The modifed model showed a much imporved prediciton of cyclic stress relaxation. The microstructural studies necessarily informed the modelling pro- cess, and the choice of the models and parameters seems to be justiﬁed.
