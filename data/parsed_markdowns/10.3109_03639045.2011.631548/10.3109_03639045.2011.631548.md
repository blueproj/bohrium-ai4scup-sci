# Self-micro emulsifying smaller molecular volume oil   (Capmul MCM) using non-ionic surfactants: a delivery   system for poorly water-soluble drug  

Mithun Mohanrao Bandivadekar 1 , Shyam Sundar Pancholi 2 , Ruchika Kaul-Ghanekar 3 , Amit  Choudhari 3 , and Soumya Koppikar 3  

1 Department of Pharmaceutics, AISSMS College of Pharmacy, Kennedy Road, Near RTO, Pune, Maharashtra, India,  2 Principal, Babaria Institute of Pharmacy, Vernama, Vadodara, Gujarat, and  3 Interactive Research School for Health  Affairs (IRSHA),Bharati Vidyapeeth University, Pune-Satara Road,Pune-411043, Maharashtra, India  

# Abstract  

The main purpose of this work is to formulate self-micro emulsifying drug delivery system (SMEDDS) using smaller  molecular oil with Atorvastatin calcium as a model drug. Solubility of the selected drug was accessed in oils and surfactants.  Percent transmittance (%T) test study was performed to identify the efficient self-micro emulsifying formulations. Those  formulations which showed higher value for %T were evaluated for droplet size, polydispersity index, ζ potential,  refractive index and cloud point measurement. Effect of drug loading on droplet size, increasing dilution in different  media, thermodynamic stability and  in vitro  dissolution was performed to observe the performance of the selected  formulation. Further cytotoxicity and permeation enhancement studies were carried out on Caco2 cell lines. Of all the oils  accessed for drug solubility, Capmul MCM showed higher solubility capacity for Atorvastatin calcium. Capmul MCM was  better microemulsified using combination of Tween 20 and Labrasol surfactant. Droplet size was as low as 86.93 nm with  polydispersity index and ζ potential at 0.195 ± 0.011 and −7.27 ± 3.11 mV respectively. The selected undiluted formulation  showed refractive index values ranging from 1.40 to 1.47 indicating the isotropicity of the formulation. The selected  formulation was robust to dilution in different media and thermodynamic ally stable. Dissolution profile was enhanced  for the selected drug as compared to marketed formulation with t85% and DE values at 10 min and 80.15 respectively.  Also cytotoxicity measurement showed minimum effect with good permeation enhancing capacity. Thus our study  demonstrates the use of smaller molecular oil (Capmul MCM) for developing self-micro emulsifying drug delivery system  for better  in vitro  and  in vivo  performance.  

Keywords:   %Transmittance study, droplet size, refractive index, dissolution efficiency, Caco2 cell lines  

# Introduction  

Self-micro emulsifying drug delivery system (SMEDDS)  has being used for lipophilic drugs which are associated  with poor water solubility and low bioavailability after  the oral delivery 1 . SMEDDS is isotropic mixtures of an oil,  surfactant, co-surfactant (or solubilizer) and drug. Such  a system form fine oil-in-water (o/w) microemulsions  under gentle agitation provided by digestive motility of  the stomach and intestine following dilution by aqueous  phase  in vivo . The spontaneous formation of an emul- sion in the GI tract advantageously presents the drug  in a dissolved form and the small droplet size provides  a large interfacial surface area for drug absorption .  

Most of the SMEDDS are formulated with the aid of co- surfactant; several marketed lipid-based products con- tain cosurfactant . The most popular excipients used to  formulate such a delivery system are triglycerides oils,  hydrophilic surfactants and one of the cosurfactants like  PEG 400, propylene glycol, ethanol and glycerol. Such  excipients are derived from vast resources either natural  or synthetically produce. Selecting right excipients from  such a vast pool is often an industrious job and also the  formulations with these selected excipients might show  ambiguity in performance ( in vitro  and  in vivo ).  

Oils have been the major part of the formulations and  play a vital role of maintaining the drug in solubilized form.  

A range of options are available for ranking from simple  fatty acids to more complex mixture of mono-di-tri-glycer- ides. Oils are further derived from semi-synthetic resources  most commonly prepared by chemically combining medi- um-chain saturated fatty acids or glycerides derived from  natural product plant oils and fully-synthetic monomeric  and polymeric liquid most of which are glycolic in nature  and relatively non-toxic. Recently smaller molecular vol- ume oils have been proven to have large capacity to solu- bilize poorly water-soluble drugs with added advantage of  easy of emulsification. They are defined as oils having fatty  acid chain length in the range of C8–C12. Lawrence et al  (1998 7 , 2000 8 ) has reported larger solubility of Testosterone  Propionate in smaller molecular volume oils, ethyl butyrate 

 (smaller molecular volume oil) as compared to soybean oil 

 (larger molecular volume oil) also ethyl butyrate was found  to be easier to solubilize in oil-water microemulsion. In the  present study small molecular volume oil was chosen as the  oil of interest for developing a SMEDDS for poorly water- soluble drug using pharmaceutical relevant surfactants.  The developed systems were characterized and further  accessed for cytotoxicity and permeation enhancement of  the drug across Caco 2 cell lines.

# Methods  

# Drug solubility determination  

The solubility of ATS in selected oils and surfactants was  determined. An excess amount of drug was added to 5 mL  of the selected oil and surfactants separately in 10 mL  capacity stopper vials and mixed using a vortex mixer.  The mixtures were then shaken for 48 h at 40 ± 0.5°C in an  orbital shaker and further kept for 24 h at room tempera- ture to reach equilibrium. The equilibrated samples were  centrifuged at 3000 rpm for 15 min. followed by filtration  through a 0.45- μ m membrane filter, the filtrates were  diluted with methanol and ATS solubility was subsequently  quantified by  High-performance liquid chromatography  ( HPLC). HPLC method used for the determination of ATS  was validated and consists Agilent Technologies 1120 LC  with a UV detector using C18 (4.6 × 250 mm) with 5 µm  particle size column. Acetonitrile and 0.05 % aqueous  acetic acid in the ratio of 65:35 (v/v) was used as mobile  phase at flow rate of 1 mL/min and run time was 10 min.  The detection was carried out at 248 nm. A 20 µl sampling  loop of Rheodyne injector was used to inject the samples.  The retention time was found to be 6.227 min. The calibra- tion curve was given by the equation: y = 979761x + 59093  with a correlation coefficient R 2  = 0.9964. Where y rep- resents area under the curve and x the concentration in  microgram per millilitre. The method was validated for  accuracy, precision, specificity and solution stability. All  measurements were done in triplicate.  

# Selection of oils, surfactants, for formulation study  

Oil:  Capmul MCM (CAP), Surfactant: Tween 20 (T20),  Tween 60 (T60), Tween 80 (T20) and Labrasol which  showed higher solubility capacity for the drug was  selected for further formulation study.  

# Preparation of self-micro emulsifying formulation with  selected oil  

A series of SMEDDS preconcentrate were prepared com- promising Capmul MCM (CAP) as an oil phase and using  combination of polyoxyethylene sorbitan fatty acid esters  surfactants (T20, T60, T80) with Labrasol. Non-ionic sur- factant combinations (Smix) were examined at various  weight ratios (Km) 1:1, 2:1, 3:1, and 4:1. The concentration  of surfactant (Smix) was varied as 10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 60,  70, and 80 (%w/w) of the total SMEDDS preconcentrate  (preconcentrate size being 1 g). Homogeneous mixtures  of surfactant (Smix) and oil at given weight ratio (Km)  were blended in a glass vial. The preconcentrate was  mixed on a cyclomixer (Remi, Mumbai) for 10 min. These  were then introduced into a 500 mL measuring cylinder  containing 100 mL of distil water, pre-equilibrated at  25°C. The measuring cylinder was then inverted (rocked)  once to provide minimum amount of shear for self- emulsification/dispersion. The resulting emulsions were  observed visually for the relative turbidity, appearance  and ease of emulsification. The emulsions were allowed  to stand for 30 min and their % Transmittance (%T) was  measured at 530 nm by UV–550 double beam spectro- photometer (JASCO, Japan) using double distilled water  as blank. Higher value of %T denotes formation of trans- parent system and thus helps in denoting the formed  microemulsion. So this technique was used instead of  pseudoternary phase diagram where visual observation  is made and thus are chances of human error. Those  SMEDDS preconcentrate dispersion showing higher %T  value were selected for further evaluation.

# Characterization and evaluation of the selected  formulations  

# Determination of droplet size and polydispersity index  

The globule size determination was performed using  photon correlation spectroscopy with in-built Zetasizer  (Model: Nano ZS, Malvern Instruments, Westborough,  MA, USA) at 633 nm. Helium–neon gas laser having an  intensity of 4 mW was the light source. The equipment  was programmed to provide 18 mm laser width. Aliquot  preconcentrate (0.5 mL) was diluted to 50 mL with distill  water; stir slowly to form a dispersion. Diluted samples  were directly placed into the module for measurements.  Measurement was made in triplicate.  

# ζ-potential determination  

SMEDDS (0.5 mL) was diluted to 50 mL withdistill  water in glass beaker with constant stirring.  ζ -potential  of the resulting microemulsion was determined using  the Zetasizer (Model: Nano ZS, Malvern Instruments,  Westborough, MA, USA). Electrophoretic mobility  ( μ m/s) was measured using small volume disposable  ζ  cell and converted to  ζ  potential by in-built software  using Helmholtz–Smoluchowski equation. All determi- nations were made in triplicate.  

# Refractive index determination  

The isotropicity of the SMEDDS preconcentrate (undi- luted) was determined by refractive index measurement.  Refractive index was measured by an Abbe refractometer  (Bausch and Lomb Optical Company, Rochester, NY).  

# Cloud point measurement  

Cloud point temperatures (Tc) were determined by visual  observation. 0.5 mL of preconcentrate was diluted to  50 mL with distil water in a glass beaker. The sample was  heated at the rate of 0.5°C / min. A close observation was  made at the appearance of the dispersion with increase  in temperature. The temperature at which the dispersion  became cloudy was taken as Tc. After the temperature  exceeds the cloud point, the sample was cooled below Tc,  and then it was heated again to check the reproducibility  of the measurements.  

# Effect of drug loading on droplet size  

Effect of drug loading on globule size of micro- emulsion was studied using optimized composition.  Formulations were prepared containing increasing  amount of ATS from 0 to 2.5 (%w/w) of the total formu- lation. The resultant SMEDDS preconcentrate, 0.5 mL,  was diluted to 100 mL with double distilled water and  the mean globule size of resulting microemulsion was  determined by photon correlation spectroscopy with  in-built Zetasizer (Model: Nano ZS, Malvern Instru­ ments, Westborough, MA, USA).

# SMEDDS cytotoxicity measurement  

Cell viability was determined by MTT assay. Human epi- thelial colorectal adenocarcinoma cell line (Caco-2) was  obtained from National Centre for Cell Science, Pune.  For evaluation of cytotoxicity, the cell line was grown in  Dulbecco’s modified eagle medium (DMEM) supple- mented with 2 mM L-glutamine, 100 Units/mL of peni- cillin/streptomycin (Gibco BRL, CA, USA) and 10% fetal  bovine serum (Moregate Biotech, Australia). The cells  were incubated in a humidified 5% CO  atmosphere at  2 37°C. Caco-2 cells were seeded at 1 × 10 5  cells/mL den- sity in 96-well plates (Axygen Scientific Inc, CA, USA).  SMEDDS formulations were prepared at 1:100 formula- tion-to-buffer ratio without the drug in Hanks Balance Salt  Solution (HBSS). After 24 h, cells were incubated with fresh  medium containing microemulsion in each well in tripli- cates. These plates were incubated for 24 h at 37°C in 5%  CO  incubator. The MTT solution (5 mg/mL) was added to  2  

each well, and the cells were incubated for another 4 h at  37°C in 5% CO  incubator. The intensity of colored forma- 2 zan derivative was determined by measuring absorbance  with the ELISA microplate reader (Biorad, Hercules, CA,  USA) at 570 nm. The mean absorbance value of three wells  was used for assessing the cell viability expressed as per- centage of control. Wells incubated only with HBSS were  used as a negative control, assumed to have 100% cell  viability, while 0.1% Sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) solu- tion was used as a positive control. # Preparation of self-micro emulsifying formulation with  selected oil  

Combinations of non-ionic surfactants were studied for  their ability to self-emulsify the selected oil. % T Versus  surfactant concentration were plotted to denote the  system which disperse into a microemulsion, indicated  by the higher value of %T. Higher value of %T denotes  that the system has small globule size and thus less scat- tering. When a combination of Labrasol as a primary  surfactant and polysorbate as secondary surfactant was  made as indicated in Figure 1 resulted into poor self- emulsification. As the fraction of Labrasol was increased  in the Km, the %T value approached almost zero, indi- cating the formation of crude milky emulsion (larger  globule size). Labrasol (as a primary surfactant) showed  poor emulsifying capacity to the selected oil (i.e. Capmul  MCM). Same trend of result were reported by Shah et al.  (1994 3 ). Emulsifying capacity was studied with combina- tion of Polysorbates as primary surfactant and Labrasol  as a secondary surfactant (Tween 20: Lab, Tween 60: Lab,  Tween 80: Lab). %T values for each of the combination  at various Km are presented in Figures 2–4. %T values  increased as the Km increased, indicating the effect of  polysorbates in formation of microemulsion. Enhanced  capacity was observed for Tween 20 as a primary sur- factant than the other polysorbates. Such a result can be  attributed to the chain length difference between the oil  and the primary surfactant. Tween 20, Tween 60, Tween  80 has the same ethoxy contained, and differs only in the  hydrophobic chain length and the level of saturation and  un-saturation (Tween 20; C12, Tween 60; C18, Tween  80; C18:1) while Capmul MCM is a mono- and di-glyc- erides of medium chain fatty acids (glycerin mono and  diester of Caprylic acid (C8), and of Capric acid (C10)).  It was observed that when a surfactant containing a  C12 hydrophobe is used, the smaller molecular volume  oils tend to be emulsified to the greatest extent than the  surfactant containing C18 hydrophobe. Since such oils  penetrate to a greater extend into the hydrocarbon chain  of the surfactant and itself act a co-surfactant further

# Characterization and evaluation of the selected  formulations  

The selected formulations (with and without drug) were  further evaluated for mean particle size,  ζ  potential,  polydispersity index, refractive index. Drug was loaded  at 10 mg per formulation, the result are indicated in the  Tables 4 and 5.  

# Mean particle size & polydispersity index  

Smallest particle size was observed for formulation con- taining Tween 20 as a primary surfactant, while largest  particle size was observed with Tween 80. This would be  attributed to the emulsifying property of the surfactant  with the selected oil. As indicated in the %T studies, com- bination Tween 20: Lab at a Km 4:1 exhibited excellent  emulsifying capacity for Capmul MCM as compared with  other surfactant combination. After drug loading there  was a significant increase in particle size. The particle size  observed for all the formulation was less than 250 nm.  Drug loading didn’t showed significant difference in the  polydispersity values.  

# ζ potential determination  

The  ζ  potential values were found to carry negative  charges due to the presence of free fatty acids. Significant  increase in the value of  ζ  potential was observed after  drug loading. Higher absolute values of  ζ  potential gen- erally, indicated an increase of electrostatic repulsive  forces between microemulsion droplets preventing the  coalescence droplets and increases in the stability.  

# Refractive index  

There was no significant difference in the refractive index  values of the formulations tested. The refractive index  values close to that of the water (1.333) prove the isotro- picity of the system.  

# Cloud point measurement  

The cloud point was found to be in the range of 65–70°C  for all the formulation. Incorporation of drug had very  little effect on the cloud point, as shown in Table 6. Such  a result can conclude that a stable microemulsion of ATS  can be formed at physiological temperature  in vivo .

# Transport of Atorvastatin SMEDDS formulation across Caco-2  cell monolayer  

Apparent permeability coefficient values were calcu- lated for each of the formulation and were compared  with control as shown in Table 11. It shows that the  permeability coefficient of the SMEDDS formulations  were higher than that of the control. In particular the  Papp value of ATS from M3 formulation was 3 times  higher than that of the control. Comparing the three  formulations the apparent permeability coefficient for  ATS was in the following order M3>M1>M2> control.  Such a result can be attributed to the type of SMEDDS  mixture used and its interaction with the membrane  components, drug transport system like P-glycoprotein  (P-gp) and metabolic enzyme like cytochrome P450.  It is reported that Atorvastatin is a substrate to P-gp 13 and extensively metabolized by the cytochrome P450 14 .  

Formulation M3 consist mixture of Capmul MCM,  Tween 80 and Labrasol, these excipients individually  affect one or the other component responsible for per- meation enhancement like Campul MCM has being  reported to enhance absorption due to their effect  on phospholipid bilayer 15 . Tween 80 is reported to  be a potent inhibitor of P-gp 16  and CYP 450 17  both of  which are responsible for efflux and metabolising of  Atorvastatin. While Labrasol increase the gap between  the tight junctions of the cells.  

# Conclusion  

Form the present study it can be concluded that Capmul  MCM is suitable for the development of SMEDDS formu- lations. But rather putting more emphasis on the ability  of the system to produce smaller globule size, increas- ing solubilization and dissolution of the drug, impor- tance should be given to the type of interaction does  the SMEDDS excipients do with the membrane com- ponents. Though in our study combination of Tween 20  and Labrasol proved to have greater capacity to emulsify  Capmul MCM, but failed to increase the permeability of  the drug. In contrast the formulation containing combi- nation Tween 80 and Labrasol which showed intermedi- ate capacity to increase the dissolution profile of the drug  showed enhance drug permeability. Further insight on  the mechanism of such complex mixture of SMEDDS to  enhance permeability and bioavailability are needed.
