# Abstract  

Ag nanopastes and Cu-Ag core-shell nanowire nanopastes were used for brazing  ACCEPTED Ni 200. The influences of the brazing temperature and brazing pressure on the  mechanical properties and microstructure of joints were investigated. The results  show that the shear strength of joint was significantly improved from 12 MPa to 46  MPa with increasing the brazing temperature from 300  o C to 850  o C and brazing  pressure from 7 MPa to 26 MPa. It is indicated that both a high brazing pressure  and brazing temperature are beneficial for the diffusions between adjacent  nanoparticles and nanowires, the seam and based materials and thereby improve  the shear bonding strength. Successful joints of 40.6 MPa bonding strength were  achieved with 700  C brazing temperature, which indicated that silver nanopaste  can be used for brazing Ni at a lower brazing temperature than that of commercial  BAg-8 brazing filler metals.  

Keywords :  Nanopaste; Low temperature brazing; Mechanical properties;  Microstructure  

# 1. Introduction  

Ni based superalloys, especially single crystal superalloys are extensively used  for manufacturing high temperature components of aero-engines, industrial gas  turbines and power generation turbines due to their superior performance, such as  MANUSCRIPT high tensile strength, specific electrical properties and good mechanical properties  at elevated temperatures [1-5]. Brazing is an effective joining method for joining of  Ni based superalloys with minimizing defects and dissimilar material joining with  distinguishable different melting points. Welding of Ni based superalloys is not  preferred since there re-crystallization of monocrystalline Ni based superalloys  leads to a remarkable decrease of strength and toughness [6]. Traditional brazing  alloys contain relatively large amounts of a melting point depressant (MPD), such  as phosphorus, silicon or zinc, to reduce the melting temperature of silver, copper  and nickel brazing filler metals. However, the application of these MPDs will lead  to formation of brittle hard phases (such as borides, silicides and carboborides) in  the joint and reduction of tensile, ductility, fatigue, and creep properties. As a  result, developing an innovative brazing with low melting temperature filler metals  without using a MPD has attracted extensive interest [7,8].  

Metallic nanomaterials have received much attention in recent years because of  ACCEPTED their unique properties [9], such as high diffusion coefficient and low melting  temperature, which differ significantly from those of bulk materials [10-13].  Metallic nanomaterials exhibit reduced sintering and melting temperatures with  decreasing particle sizes, which is known as the Gibbs-Thomson effect [14-15].  There are numerical successful studies to apply metallic nanoinks for printable  electronics and low temperature packaging [16-18]. The bonding strength around  10 MPa to 40 MPa bonding strength has achieved due to low temperature sintering  [19]. Although it is possible to apply metal nanoparticles for brazing at a low  brazing temperature there is limited research in this direction. Recently, Hausner et  al. used Ni nanoparticles to braze steel and stainless steel. Under a 20 MPa brazing  pressure, 60 MPa and 120 MPa shear bonding strengths had been achieved in steel  and stainless steel respectively when brazing at 850 °C in vacuum [20]. This  displays the significant potential of applying metallic nanopastes for brazing  materials to achieve a high bonding strength.  

In the present work, we developed three different kinds of nanopastes: Ag  nanowires (NWs), Ag nanoparticles (NPs) and Cu-Ag core-shell nanowires (NWs)  for brazing Ni 200 in both vacuum and air condition. The properties of joint with  different brazing temperatures and different brazing pressures were evaluated. The  tensile shear strength of 40.6 MPa was demonstrated with silver nanopaste brazing  at 700 °C. This may pave the way to develop innovative materials for brazing high  quality devices.

# 2.1 Fabrication of Ag NWs and Ag NPs  

All the chemicals and reagents used in experiment were of an analytical grade  and used in the as-received form without any purification. AgNO , NaCl,  ethylene glycol(EG), reagent alcohol and polyvinyl pyr ro li done (PVP) (average  molecular weight (M.W.)  ≈ Ag NWs synthesis) were purchased from Fisher Scientific International and  ACCEPTED Sigma-Aldrich Corporation. Cu-Ag core-shell NWs were synthesized based on a hydrothermal method [24- 26]. 30 mL CuNO (25 mM, DI water solution), 152 mg ascorbic acid and 0.38 g  PVP (M.W.  ≈  1,300,000) mixed together and stirring for 5 minutes until fully  dissolved. The mixture solution was then autoclaved in a stainless steel reactor  inside a 50 mL Teflon-lined container at 120 °C for 4 hours, and then naturally  cooled to room temperature. The reaction solution was centrifuged at 2000 rpm for  5 min. Then the supernatant was decanted and re-dispersed in 120 mL DI water.  Then the 2.5 mL AgNO 3  water solution which added 0.075 g PVP (MW  ≈   1,300,000) was poured slowly into the Cu NWs solution and stirring for 30  minutes. The Cu-Ag core-shell NWs paste was prepared by condensing reaction  solution with centrifuge at a speed of 3000 rpm for 5 min. The nominal Cu:Ag  atomic ratio is 4:1. The synthesized Ag nanopaste and Cu-Ag core-shell NWs were  characterized by scanning electron microscopy (SEM, Zeiss Auriga Crossbeam  FIB/SEM), X-ray diffraction (XRD) and ultraviolet-visible spectroscopy (UV-vis).  The differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) was conducted under nitrogen (50%)  and helium (50%) atmosphere with heating at a rate of 10 °C/min.

# 3.2 Effect of brazing temperatures on the joint strength and microstructure  

The shear strength of brazed samples with three kinds of pastes at different  ACCEPTED brazing temperatures is presented in Fig. 8(a) and (b). Fig. 8(a) shows the stress- strain curve of Ag NWs sample brazing with 700 °C. Shear strength of 23 MPa, the  yield strength of 15 MPa and the modulus of elasticity of 2.21 MPa can be deduced  from Fig. 8(a). To ensure the accuracy of the results, three specimens were brazed  with the same nanopaste and in the same brazing conditions. The error bar comes  from three trials. The significant improvement in shear strength of joint can be  observed with increasing brazing temperature (from 300 °C to 850 °C). The  highest shear strength increase was obtained using Ag NWs, providing the  improvement from 5.3 MPa to 45.89 MPa after brazed at 850 °C brazing  temperature. There are still lots of voids in the brazed joint and diffusion between  nanopaste and substrates is not enough when brazing temperature is low. So the  shear strength of brazed joint is low after brazing at 300 °C. For comparison, a  commercial filler alloy of BAg-8a (BAg 72 Cu 28 ) was also used for vacuum brazing  at different brazing temperatures, and this joint achieved shear strength of 95 MPa  compared to 40.6MPa achieved using Ag NPs paste in 700 °C. However, joint was  directly failed using BAg-8a with brazing temperature under 780 °C, the melting  temperature of BAg-8a. Therefore, low temperature brazing for the Nickel alloys  has been achieved using Ag nanopaste and Cu-Ag core-shell NWs compare to this  commercial filler alloy. Moreover, brazed joint with Ag nanopaste could work in  high temperature because the porous brazing can become further densifying at a  higher temperature. There are further sintering and neck growth of Ag  nanoparticles when operating at a high temperature condition [28].  

Fig. 9 shows SEM cross-sectional images of brazed joint using three kinds of  nanopastes at different brazing temperatures. There are no obvious microcracks  existing between brazing seam and substrate, which indicates a good surface  wettability and brazing quality of joint. At a brazing temperature of 300 °C, the  brazing seam exhibits a porous structure as shown in Fig. 9(a), (c), and (e),  especially for a Cu-Ag core-shell paste sample (Fig. 9(e)). It is indicated that no  sufficient brazing process occurs in the middle of the joint at a low sintering  temperature without an external applied force. The deleterious brazing mechanical  ACCEPTED properties can be attributed to the porous structure in brazed joints. However, at  700 °C a dense structure can be recognized in the inner brazing seam, in Fig.9(b),  (d), and (f). The metallurgical bond between the Ni substrate and the nanopaste  was found to cross the whole brazing interfaces. It is obvious that a high brazing  temperature is benefit for the diffusion between adjacent nanoparticles. Besides,  there is no oxide layer at the interface between the brazing seam and substrate due  to the vacuum brazing condition.  At 500 °C, significant densification of the  structure occurs with some porosity remaining especially in the Ag NP sample.  

DSC measurements provide some insight to the densification of the brazed joint  (Fig. 10). Both Ag NW and Ag NP (inlet) pastes have large endothermic peaks  around 100  o C to 200  o C caused by the vaporizing of surface absorption groups and  decomposition of the organic capping layers [10, 16]. Ag NP paste have  proportionately higher endothermic peaks than the Ag NW paste, suggesting that  less organic content is present in the Ag NW paste than the Ag NP paste. The  higher organic content hinders the sintering process of Ag NPs at lower  temperatures. This is possibly why the Ag NP joint retains a high porosity at  500 °C as shown in Fig. 9. For the Cu-Ag core-shell nanowires, the DSC profile is  almost entirely exothermic with a large exothermic peak that occurs at 457 °C. The  large exotherm correlates to significant densification of the Cu-Ag core-shell joint  (Fig. 9(h)). The exothermic peak is likely caused by either crystallization of the  brazing material or intermixing of Cu and Ag.  

To further study the interface characteristics of brazed joint, the brazed joint  using different nanopastes were investigated by EDS line scanning. Although there  is no obviously diffusion layer between the brazing seam and Ni substrate as  shown in Fig. 11(a) and (b), the EDS results show that significant diffusion  processes occur at both temperatures. According to the EDS analysis, Ni diffused  into the brazed joint with a much higher diffusion coefficient than that of Ag into  the substrate, which indicates the Kirkendall effect, i.e., a significantly higher  diffusion of Ni into Ag. The Ni plating layer was observed in Fig. 11(a), but  ACCEPTED disappeared after brazing, as shown in Fig. 9(b). The width of interfacial element  µ m as shown in Fig. 11(b).  However, the thickness of Nickel plating layer before brazing is around 15  micrometers. Therefore, it is the Nickel plating layer that mainly diffuses into the  brazing seam. Moreover, the content of Nickel in brazing seam at different depths  is stable, which indicated that Ni has diffused into the whole brazing seam during a  long time vacuum brazing. EDS results of brazed joint using a Cu-Ag core-shell  NWs paste are shown in Fig. 12(a) and (b). The content variation in the brazing  regime is due to the core-shell structure. It is evident that more Cu diffuses into the  brazing seam than Ag at a high brazing temperature. Moreover, the diffusion depth  of elements increases at a higher brazing temperature.  

Fig. 13 shows the optical image top fracture surface of joints brazing at  different temperatures. It can be seen that the crack in the brazed joint propagated  in the silver brazing seam when the brazing temperature is lower. However, the  failure position changes to the interface between silver layer and the Nickel  substrate when the brazing temperature is high. Fig. 14 displays a typical SEM  image of the top fracture surface of the brazed joints brazed at different brazing  MANUSCRIPT temperatures. It can be seen that the brazed joint formed with 300 °C still keep in a  porous structure, indicating interconnections between the adjacent nanoparticles  and nanowires arising from a sintering process at a low temperature. However, at a  high brazing temperature of 700 °C the nanomaterials behave like a bulk filler  metal and the obviously porous structure disappears which shown in Fig. 13(b) — (d). The microstructure features evidence that the higher brazing temperature  between the adjacent nanoparticles and nanowires is beneficial to the growth of  neck size during brazing process. A lot of dimples are found in the fracture  morphology of brazed joint, which result in high shear strength. There is difference  of fracture surface between brazed samples using Cu-Ag core-shell NWs and Ag  nanopaste. Even at 700 °C there is no obviously dimple structure on the surface of  fracture area. It is indicated that there is no sufficient diffusion for the core Cu in  paste during brazing process even in high temperature. The sintering feature is  consistent with the molecular dynamic simulation on the sintering of Cu-Ag core- shell nanoparticles [29]. However, for an in-depth understanding of the diffusion  mechanisms of the Cu-Ag core-shell NWs paste, further investigations are still  necessary.  

# 3.3 Effect of brazing pressure on the joint strength and microstructure  

The brazing processes at different brazing pressures from 7 MPa to 26 MPa at  350 °C were performed at an ambient atmosphere. Fig.15 illustrates the shear  strength of brazed joints using Ag NWs, Ag NPs and Cu-Ag core-shell NWs at  different brazing pressures. The high shear strength of brazed joint was 32.9 MPa  using Ag NWs at a brazing pressure of 26 MPa and 350 °C brazing temperature.  Compared to the pressureless brazing shown in Fig. 7(b), the current data indicated  that the brazing pressure exerts a significant influence on the mechanical properties  at the same brazing temperature. With the increase of brazing pressures from 7  MPa to 26 MPa, the shear strength of joint increases obviously. As studied in the  joining of copper with an Ag nanopaste and steel with Ni nanopaste, the pressure  also exhibits much significant influence on brazing behaviors than the brazing  temperature [30].  

The optical image top fracture surface of brazed joint at different brazing  MANUSCRIPT pressures is shown in Fig. 16. The results indicate that the failure occurs at the  interface between the silver layer and Nickel substrates in both low and high  brazing pressures, similar to vacuum brazing at 700  shear strength is a consequence of an increasing brazing activity caused by  increasing brazing pressures, which can be clearly seen in the SEM images of  fracture morphology of the brazed joints in Fig. 17. At a brazing pressure of 7 MPa,  the porous structure was seen on the fracture surface. For comparison, there are a  lot of dimples on the fracture surface of joint at 26 MPa. These dimples indicate  that the fracture was a toughness fracture [31], since the dimples are the typical  characteristic of a ductile fracture. Yan, et al had explained the effect of bonding  pressures by a simple sphere model [32]. The pressure between the two adjacent  nanoparticles in the sintered layer will increase with a high assistant pressure  ACCEPTED according to the Herring’s theorem [33]. The high brazing pressure will increase  the neck size of nanaoparticles during the brazing process and also will play a role  in increasing the density of brazed joint, it is thus reasonable to expect that joint  shear strength increases at a higher brazing pressure. The fracture appearances of  joint using Ag NWs exhibit the much finer and uniform structure than that in the  — (d). However, the particles structure  can be still identified in Fig. 17(f). Due to the melting and sintering temperatures  of Cu in Cu-Ag core-shell NWs are higher than Ag, the diffusion of Cu in the joint  is insufficient [29]. However, Cu-Ag core-shell NWs still provides an excellent  shear strength performance with a high brazing pressure. It is possible to further  improve the bonding strength by combination of a brazing pressure and a high  brazing temperature. The current microstructure characterization and element  distribution cross the interfaces display that the surface wetting is worth further  

investigated. To enhance the diffusion between brazing layer and the substrate is  critical for a strong bonding. On the other hand, the current low shear strengthen is  also limited by a low yielding strengthen of Ag and Ag-Cu brazing materials. A  nanobrazing material, such as Nickel or copper with a high yielding strengthen can  significantly contribute a high shear bonding strength [15].  

# 4. Conclusions  

The effect of brazing temperature and brazing pressure with different nanopastes  on the microstructures and shear strength of the Ni brazed joints were investigated.  The following conclusions can be derived from the experimental results:  

Ag nanopaste and Cu-Ag core-shell NWs offer a great potential for brazing Ni at  a lower brazing temperature. Although a high density structure of joint with high  shear strength can be achieved by increasing brazing temperature, brazing pressure  exerts a significant influence on the shear strength. Brazing pressure is benefit for  diffusion between adjacent nanoparticles and thereby enhances the brazing strength.  Element diffusion occurs during the brazing. Much more Ni can diffuse into joint  than Ag diffuses into the Ni substrate during brazing process. The current work  may stimulate further studies on innovative low temperature brazing with  nanopastes for materials joining for high temperature applications.

3.   High shear strength of joint is achieved at higher brazing temperature.
