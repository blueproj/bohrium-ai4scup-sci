# Microstructural Evolution and Mechanical Properties of a Ni-Based Superalloy, TMW-4  

C.Y. CUI, Y.F. GU, D.H. PING, and H. HARADA  

The microstructural evolution and mechanical properties were examined in a Ni-based super- alloy, TMW-4, with high Co and Ti contents at temperatures ranging from 700   C to 1200   C for various times up to 1000 hours. The results showed that solution treatments (temperatures: from 1100   C to 1200   C; time: 4 hours) essentially aﬀected the fraction and size of primary and secondary  c ¢  precipitations and grain size. No  g -Ni 3 Ti phase was observed in all solution heat- treatment samples. On the other hand, small amounts of  g  phase were observed in the samples after homogenization at 1220   C and aging at temperatures ranging from 1000   C to 1175   C. Based on the investigations, a time-temperature-transition (TTT) curve for the  g -phase for- mation was constructed. These microstructural eﬀects were successfully combined to obtain an increase of tensile properties in terms of both strength and ductility.

# I. INTRODUCTION  

R ECENTLY ,  TMW series alloys have been success- fully developed in our institute (NIMS, Tsukuba, Japan) 1 – 3 ] for turbine disk and compressor blade applications. The TMW alloys contain  c ¢ -forming elements: Al and Ti; matrix elements: Ni, Cr, Mo, Co, and W; and grain- boundary strengthening elements, such as B, C, and Zr. Typical TMW alloys contain  c ¢  volume fraction of about 45 pct to 50 pct, which is close to U720Li alloy (developed by Special Metals Corporation, Huntington, WV). In these TMW alloys, the Co content is higher than 20 wt pct and Ti content is higher than 5 wt pct, while normal commercial disk alloys only contain up to 3 ]   20-wt pct Co and 5-wt pct Ti. High amounts of Co are [ 4 ]   suggested to restrain the formation of the  g  phase and   ¢ 5 ] lower the  c  solvus temperature, which allows a large processing window and reduces the stress induced by cooling or quenching. Thus, TMW alloys have good workability, and they can be easily processed by casting 6 ]   and wrought routes. Titanium can substitute easily for Al in the  c ¢  phase for antiphase boundary strengthen-   7 ] ¢ ing. As a result, TMW alloys with  c  and  c  structure show improved phase stability and higher strength than 1 , ] the U720Li alloy.  

The properties requirements for disk alloys are very complex, and the optimal mechanical properties can be achieved by controlling the microstructures, which are usually determined by the alloy’s composition and heat- treatment condition. The chief microstructural param- eters in Ni-based disk alloys are the grain size, the fraction and distribution of c ¢ precipitation, and carbide/boride precipitation on the grain boundary. These parameters can be generally modiﬁed by subsol- vus or supersolvus treatments, cooling rate after high- 2 , ]   temperature treatments, and aging. Since the TMW alloys are newly developed, the role of heat-treatment condition, especially the subsolvus and supersolvus treatments, on microstructural evolution and tensile properties is still unknown.  

On the other hand, high Ti content and Ti/Al compositional ratio ( > 2 wt pct) in the TMW alloys promote the formation of the  g  phase (Ni 3 Ti) with a hexagonal close-packed structure (hcp) in preference to   ¢ 1 , ] the  c -Ni 3 Al. The existence of the  g  phase has a 10 ]   deleterious eﬀect on an alloy’s ductility. Generally, the formation of the  g  phase is strongly related to Ti 2 ]   content, Ti/Al ratio, and alloy composition. In a 4 ]   previous study, we investigated the eﬀects of solution heat-treatment conditions (temperatures: 1100   C, 1140   C, and 1180   C; time: 4 hours) on the evolution of the  g  phase in TMW alloys with various Ti contents and Ti/Al ratios. However, the heat-treatment temper- atures were very limited and also the time was too short to reach an equilibrium state; more work is needed to clarify the formation and evolution of the  g  phase.  

The purpose of the present article is to examine the eﬀects of solution treatments on grain growth, variation of primary and secondary  c ¢ , and mechanical properties in the newly developed TMW-4 alloy. Moreover, the formation and evolution of the  g  phase are discussed in terms of Co and Ti eﬀects and heat-treatment conditions.

# III. EXPERIMENTAL  

Table  I  lists the nominal chemical compositions of the TMW-4 alloy. A 50-kg ingot with 200-mm diameter and 250-mm height was cast by a vacuum induction melt and then hot-rolled at the Special Metals Corporation. The hot rolling was conducted smoothly at about 1120   C, and only light edge cracks were found at the sides, showing that the TMW-4 alloy has good workability. The thickness of hot-rolled plates with a rolling reduc- tion of 75 pct was about 12 mm.  

The rolled plates were solution heat treated at temperatures of 1100   C, 1120   C, 1130   C, 1140   C, 1150   C, 1160   C, 1180   C, and 1200   C, for 4 hours followed by air cooling (AC) and then aging at 650   C/ 24 h/AC + 760   C/16 h/AC to study the solution treat- ment eﬀects on microstructural evolution, such as grain size and primary and secondary  c ¢ . Some samples were also homogenized at 1220   C/1 h followed by water quenching and then aging at temperatures ranging from 700   C to 1200   C for various times up to 1000 hours to investigate the formation of the  g  phase. In order to know the total  c ¢  fraction in the near-equilibrium state, some samples were supersolvus heat treated at 1220   C for 1 hour and then cooled to 870   C or 760   C with a cooling rate of about 1   C/h. This slow cooling rate allowed all the  c ¢  to precipitate and grow at near- equilibrium state. The heat-treatment conditions and their purposes are summarized in Table  II . For the microstructural characterization, samples cut from the heat-treatment plates were polished and then etched in a solution of Kalling solution (100-mL HCl, 100-mL methanol, and 50-g CuCl 2 ). # B.  Phase Stability  

# 1.  The total  c ¢  contents  

Figure  7  shows the near-equilibrium microstructure and XRD of the TMW-4 heat treatment at 1220   C and then cooled to 870   C. As shown in Figure  7 (a), the samples mainly consisted of  c  and  c ¢  phases, the resulting  c ¢  precipitations were very large, and no  g phase was observed. The XRD results (Figure  7 (b)) also conﬁrmed that  c  and  c ¢  were the main phases. The total c ¢  contents measured by the point counting method were 41.9 pct at 760   C and 40.3 pct at 870   C, respectively.  

# 2.  The  g -phase formation  

Although the TMW alloy contained high Ti (6 wt pct) and Ti/Al ratio ( > 3 wt pct), no  g  phase was observed under solution heat-treatment conditions (temperatures: 1100   C to 1200   C, time: 4 hours). Probably, the heat- treatment time was too short to reach an equilibrium state. In order to get the equilibrium state, some samples were annealed at 1100   C for a long time (430 hours), and the microstructures are shown in Figure  8 . In addition to the large  c  and some undissolved  c ¢  phase, some plate-shaped features, hundreds of micrometer in length, were also observed, as shown in Figure  8 (a). At high magniﬁcation (Figure  8 (b)), two types of plate- shaped features, i.e., coarse and thin, were observed. Many undissolved  c ¢  precipitations existed not in the thin plate-shaped feature but in the coarse plate-shaped feature. The analysis of EDS results showed that the coarse plate-shaped feature had a similar composition to the  c  matrix; thus, the coarse plate-shaped feature was identiﬁed to be a twinning. In contrast, the Ti content in the thin plate-shaped feature was higher than that in the  c  matrix and  c ¢ . In order to identify the thin plate-shaped feature, TEM analysis was performed. Figures  8 (c) and (d) are bright-ﬁeld TEM images, showing the thin plate-shaped feature with correspond- ing selected-area electron diﬀraction (SAED) pattern. The analysis of the SAED pattern (Figure  8 (d)) conﬁrmed that this thin plate-shaped feature was the g  phase with a hcp structure. The volume fraction of the g  phase was roughly estimated to be less than 5 pct.  

Figure  9  shows a time-temperature-transformation (TTT) curve for the  g -phase formation in the TMW-4 alloy. It was based on results from samples that had gone through a 1220   C homogenized treatment fol- lowed by water quenching before aging at temperatures ranging from 700   C to 1200   C for 1 to 1000 hours. The existence of the  g  phase was checked by optical microscopy. The TTT curve exhibited a C shape and the nose of the C curve was about 1140   C. The temperature ranges of the  g -phase formation were conﬁrmed to be between 1000   C and 1175   C.

# V. DISCUSSION  

A.  Microstructure-Mechanical Properties Relationship  

The  c ¢  solvus temperature of the TMW-4 alloy was determined to be 1144   C, according to the DTA analysis (Figure  3 ) and microstructure observations (Figures  4  and  5 ). Thus, the selected solution tempera- tures can be divided into the subsolvus (below 1140   C) and supersolvus (above 1150   C) heat treatment.  

The increase of subsolvus heat-treatment temperature resulted in the following microstructural changes:

 (a) decrease of the fraction of primary  c ¢ , which indi- cated the increase of the secondary and tertiary  c ¢ fraction (Figures  4  and  5 );

 (b) slight increase of the average grain size due to the primary  c ¢  pinning eﬀect; and

 (c) increase of the secondary  c ¢  size.  

On the other hand, the increase of supersolvus solution temperature leads to the rapid increase of grain size due to no primary  c ¢  pinning. The samples heat treated at 1180   C and 1200   C had similar grain size, which can be ascribed to secondary recrystallization and twin formation in the 1200   C heat-treatment samples.  

The highest tensile properties of the TMW-4 were obtained with a microstructure of ﬁne grain size, ﬁne secondary  c ¢  size, and lower primary  c ¢  fraction. Thus, the highest yield strength was reached with larger grain

# B.  The  g -Phase Formation  

The  g  phase was observed not in the samples solution heat treated at temperatures ranging from 1100   C to 1200   C for 4 hours, but in the samples homogenized at 1220   C and aged at low temperatures. This means that the as-rolled TMW-4 alloy is in a nonequilibrium state and needs a long time ( > 4 hours) to reach an equilib- rium state, showing that the kinetics of  g -phase precipi- tation in the TMW-4 alloy are very sluggish. The sluggish precipitate kinetics is probably related to high Co content in the TMW-4 alloy. As it is well-known, the g  phase is an ordered geometrically close-packed (gcp) precipitation with a hcp structure. The gcp phases do not form easily in high Co-containing alloys because the chemical and crystallographic stability within Co-based alloys are aﬀected by a lattice mismatch that is rarely   15 ] ¢ less than 1 pct. In addition, the  c  in TMW-4 has a

The TTT curves for  g -phase formation within other Ni-based alloys containing low Ti (about 2 wt pct), such [ 11 ]   12 ]   as Nimonic 263 and Inconel 740, were compared with the TMW-4 alloy, as shown in Figure  9 . The TTT curves in the Ni-based alloy with low Ti content are quite diﬀerent from the present results. First, the g -phase formation temperature ranges are diﬀerent: the  g  phase forms between 1000   C and 1175   C in TMW-4, whereas the  g -phase formation temperature ranges are between 700   C and 1100   C in Nimonic 263 and Inconel 740. Second, the high Ti-containing TMW- 4 alloy has a nose temperature of about 1140   C, which is higher than the lower Ti-containing alloys Nimonic 263 (about 870   C) and Inconel 740 (about 900   C). Third, the kinetics of  g -phase precipitation is very fast in TMW-4 ( < 1 hour) when compared to the precipitation time at the nose temperatures in Nimonic 263 and Inconel 740 ( > 10 hours). This obtained TTT curve for the  g -phase formation is helpful for selecting suitable processing parameters. As mentioned in the experimen- tal part, the TMW-4 alloy was hot-rolled smoothly at 1120   C, and no  g  phase was observed in the as-rolled state. The facts indicate that, even if the processing temperature lies in the temperature range of the  g  phase, the  g  phase can be removed by selecting a suitable processing route.  

The formation of the  g  phase is related to (1) the Ti/Al ratio and (2) Co and Ti content. As for factor (1), it is well known that Ni and Al promote the c ¢ -Ni 3 Al formation. This  c ¢  phase has also liberally been described as Ni 3 (Al, Ti, Nb, Ta, Hf). But as the sum of (Ti + Nb + Ta + Hf) exceeds the Al, the formation 15 ]   of the  g  phase is favored. These elements, i.e., Ti, Nb, Ta, and Hf, are called  g -phase formation elements. In lower Ti-containing alloys, such as Nimonic 263 [ 11 ]  

 (wt pct: Ti 2.1, Al 0.4, Ti/Al = 5) and Inconel 740 12 ]

 (wt pct: Ti 1.8, Nb 2, Al 0.9, (Ti + Nb)/Al    4), the metastable  c ¢  can transform to the  g  phase during aging, as shown in Figure  9 . In higher Ti-containing alloys, such as U720Li (wt pct: Ti 5, Al 2.5, 9 ]   Ti/Al = 2), no  g  phase is observed. But the  g  phase can be formed in the TMW-4 with further increase in the Ti/Al ratio to about 3. Therefore, high Ti/Al ratio facilitates  g -phase formation in high Ti-containing alloys. As for factor (2), we compared the  g -phase 2 ] formation in TMW alloys with similar Ti/Al ratio. The  g  phase can form easily in TMW alloys with higher Ti content, suggesting that Ti content is also important for  g -phase formation. However, increased Co content can retard the  g -phase formation. For example, the  g  phase exists in TMW-3 with 23 wt pct Co and 5.4 wt pct Ti, but not in TMW-4 with higher 4 ]   Co (26.3 wt pct) and Ti (6 wt pct), when they were solution heat treated at temperature intervals of 1100   C to 1180   C.  

The Thermo-Calc software is successfully used to determine the phase fraction and possible equilibrium phase for Ni-based superalloys. For example, we calcu- lated the  g -phase formation in U720LI, Nimonic 263, and Inconel 740 using the NI-DATA database. The calculated results showed that (1) no  g  phase forms in U720Li; (2) the  g  phase forms in Nimonic 263, and the  g solvus temperature is 957   C; and (3) the  g  solvus temperature in Inconel 740 is about 1080   C. The calculated results are in good agreement with experi- 9 , , ]   mental observations, showing that the software can predict  g -phase formation in U720Li, Nimonic 263, and Inconel 740 alloys.  

On the other hand, for the present TMW-4 alloy with high Co and Ti content, the experimental results are diﬀerent from the predicted ones. First, the  c ¢  content and solvus temperature are underestimated. Second, the content and temperature range observed in the  g  phase is also diﬀerent from the predicted one. As shown in Figure  9 , the  g  phase exists only in the temperature interval of 1000   C to 1175   C. The maximum content of the  g  phase is estimated to be less than 5 pct, which is much lower than the predicted one (12.6 pct). Thus, the Thermo-Calc program cannot predict new Ni-Co alloys (TMW) accurately because the original database (NI-DATA) does not encompass TMW alloys with high contents of Co and Ti.  

# VI. CONCLUSIONS  

The microstructures and mechanical properties were examined in the TMW-4 alloy heat treated at temper- atures ranging from 700   C to 1220   C for 1 to 1000 hours and the following results were obtained.  

1. In the subsolvus heat-treatment samples, grain size, fraction of secondary and tertiary  c ¢ , and secondary c ¢  size increased with increasing solution tempera- ture. Grain growth was very sluggish due to pri- mary  c ¢  grain-boundary pinning.

 2. The supersolvus heat treatments resulted in a microstructure with all of the  c ¢  precipitated inside the  c  grains. In general, the supersolvus heat-treated samples had very large grain size.

 3. No  g  phase was observed in the solution heat- treatment samples at temperatures ranging from 1100   C to 1200   C, but the  g  phase was found in the samples homogenized at 1220   C and aged at temperatures ranging from 1000   C to 1175   C.  

4. The mechanical properties of the TMW-4 alloy were modiﬁed by solution heat treatment. The high- est yield strength and elongation at 750   C were reached with larger grain size (10.7  l m) and second- ary  c ¢  precipitate size (about 80 nm) in the subsol- vus heat-treatment samples.
