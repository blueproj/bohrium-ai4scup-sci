# A B S T R A C T  

A new set of AlFeCoNiC x  ( x  = 0, 0.02, 0.04, 0.08, 0.17) high-entropy alloys (HEAs) were designed and fabri- cated. The microstructure, phase evolution and mechanical behaviors of the alloys were systematically in- vestigated. Results show that the matrix alloy exhibits coarse columnar structure, while the C-doped alloys exhibit typical dendrite microstructure. The dendritic region (DR) is composed of single B2 phase, while the interdendritic region (ID) consists of FCC and E2 1  phases and presents an ultra ﬁ ne cellar structure caused by the coexisting of ordering and spinodal decomposition. Interestingly, both the strength and compressive strain greatly increased with the increase of carbon content. Particularly, the AlFeCoNiC 0.08  HEA possessed the most excellent mechanical properties, superior to many other HEAs with the yield strength, fracture strength, and fracture strain as high as 1115 MPa, 2517 MPa and 48.8%, respectively. The mechanical properties of the AlFeCoNiC 0.17  alloy were weakened by the presence of graphite. Further, it is found that there is close re- lationship between the fracture morphology and alloy properties. Overall, in this study the improved micro- structure and mechanical properties of the AlFeCoNi HEA have been accomplished by carbon doping, and our ﬁ ndings could shed light on a new alloy-design route to achieve bulk ultra ﬁ ne structure materials with desired properties via the direct solidi ﬁ cation.

# 1. Introduction  

Over the last decade, high-entropy alloys (HAE), as a new member in the materials family, has attracted intensive attentions due to its novel properties  [1 – 4] . Compared with traditional alloy design, HEAs contain multi-principal components in equal or near-equal atomic ratio. Therefore, the high con ﬁ guration entropy is usually regarded as the primary actor to promote the formation and stability of solid solutions rather than compound phases  [5] . At the earlystage of HEAs develop- ment, researchers mainly paid attention to obtain a single-phase mas- sive solid solution. As known, strength and ductility are always si- multaneously expected in the practical application of materials. However, previous single-phase HEAs studies have indicated that it is di ﬃ cult to simultaneously achieve high strength and good ductility, which will impede their applications as the structural materials. The HEAs with FCC crystal structures exhibited high ductility but the strength was not high enough  [6] , while the single-phase BCC struc- tured alloys showed high strength while sacri ﬁ cing of ductility  [7] . Given this, recently, much research e ﬀ ort has been spend on exploring  

of ductile through the  σ  and  μ  precipitated particles. Furthermore, as mentioned in previous literature  [13] , that the minor addition of Ti into the CoCrFeNi matrix could induce the formation of coherent nano- precipitates, which could strengthen the matrix alloy. Most recently, carbon, as an alloying element of HEAs, has attracted a large number of research interests due to its substantial e ﬀ ect on the microstructure and properties of HEAs  [14 – 18] . The carbon doped HEAs studies  [14 – 16] have shown that the integrated mechanical properties of HEAs can be optimized through alloying with the interstitial element carbon, fol- lowed by a set of mechanical working as well as annealing processing treatments.  

Inspired by these previous investigations, in the present study, the alloying addition of carbon was added into the AlFeCoNi matrix. Note that all the principle elements in this studied HEA are not the carbide forming elements except that Fe is the weak carbide-former. Unlike previous studies, in this study, the matrix alloy is the BCC structured HEA and no strong carbide forming elements in the studied alloys. What is more, carbon has larger negative mixing enthalpy with other prin- ciple elements in the alloy  [19]  and the atomic size of carbon element is much smaller than that of the other constituent elements  [20] , corre- sponding values are listed in  Table 1 . Subsequently, the in ﬂ uence of carbon on the microstructure, phase compositions and mechanical be- havior of this HEA were systematically investigated and ascertained. Therefore, it is necessary and interesting to get better understanding of the e ﬀ ect of added carbon on the microstructure and properties of the BCC-type HEA.

# 3. Results and discussion  

# 3.1. Crystal structure identi ﬁ cation  

The XRD patterns of the as-cast AlFeCoNiC x  alloys were shown in Fig. 1 . As shown in  Fig. 1 , the AlFeCoNi alloy (marked as C0) only exhibits the re ﬂ ection of ordered BCC (B2) crystal structure, which corresponded to the result reported previously by Zuo et al.  [21] . When the  x  value reaches 0.02 (marked as C0.5), a peak with very faint in- tensity around 49.06° occurs, indicating the existence of a small quantity of FCC phase. For the AlFeCoNiC 0.04  alloy (marked as C1.0), another two minor re ﬂ ection peaks corresponding to two FCC phase appear beside the (110) peak of the B2 phase. From  Fig. 1 b and c, one can obviously observe these new weak peaks of the FCC phase. Nevertheless, with increase of the carbon content, the intensity of the FCC di ﬀ raction peaks gradually increase, implying that the addition of carbon promotes the FCC phase formation. It is noticed that, with ad- dition of 2.0 at% C (i.e., alloy C2.0), a weak peak around 23.9° is found which corresponds to the (110) peak of the ordered FCC phase. In order to make it clearly, the di ﬀ raction patterns around the  2 θ  range of 18 – 42° are enlarged in  Fig. 1 b. Based on the XRD analysis, the ordered FCC and FCC phases appeared in the di ﬀ raction patterns can be iden- ti ﬁ ed as AlCo 3 C 0.5  ( κ -carbide) type and CFe 15.1  ( γ -austenite) type forms with the space group  Pm-3 m  and  Fm-3 m , respectively. It should be pointed out that, here, the ordered FCC phase (AlCo 3 C 0.5 ) is the E2 1 type  κ -carbide phase, and the E2 1  crystal structure is similar to the L1 2 (Ni 3 Al phase in Ni-based supperalloys) except that a carbon atom oc- cupying the octahedral interstice at the body center of the L1 2  [22] . Similar with C2.0 alloy, the C4.1 alloy contains B2 and E2 1  as well as FCC phases.

# 3.3. Mechanical properties  

In order to investigate the e ﬀ ect of C addition on the mechanical properties of the AlFeCoNiC x  alloys, room-temperature Vickers hard- ness and compression tests were employed.  Fig. 4  shows the room- temperature mechanical behaviors of the investigated AlFeCoNiC x HEAs. As shown in  Fig. 4 a, the true stress-strain curves varied with the C content, which indicated that the mechanical properties of the in- vestigated material system are signi ﬁ cantly in ﬂ uenced by the variation of the C content. The C0 alloy showed low yield strength of 810 MPa, and it exhibited very brittle so that it fractured with nearly no obvious plasticity. Interestingly, the comprehensive properties of the alloys were enhanced after C addition. By increasing the C content from 0 to 2.0, the yield strength and compressive strength increased from 810 MPa to 1115 MPa and 855 – 2517 MPa, respectively. It is worth noting that the corresponding fracture strain shows almost the same trend as strength, which di ﬀ er from many previous studies where the fracture strain decreases with increase of strength  [9,11,23,24] . When the C content reaches to 4.1, the strength and compressive strain of the C4.1 alloy decreased, this is attributed to the presence of graphite phase in the microstructure of this alloy as mentioned above in  Fig. 2 e. The fracture strength and strain to failure of the alloys are shown in  Fig. 4 b. In accordance with the observation of  Fig. 4 b, the C2.0 alloy shows an outstanding combination of compressive strength and strain, with the yield strength of 1115 MPa, fracture strength of 2517 MPa and fracture strain of 48.8%. The fracture strength and strain signi ﬁ cantly increased by 1.94 and 17.8 times compared with that of the C-free alloy, i.e., the based AlFeCoNi HEA. In addition, the detail mechanical properties as a function of the C content are summarized in  Table 3 .  

Fig. 5 a gave the variation of hardness and yield strength with C content in the AlFeCoNiC x  alloys. Apparently, both the hardness and yield strength of the as-cast alloys gradually increase with increasing C content from 422 HV and 810 MPa of the C0 alloy to 452 HV and 1115 MPa for the C2.0 alloy. However, with further increase of C content the yield strength of the C4.1 alloy decreases, on the contrary, the hardness of the C4.1 alloy increases. Such a di ﬀ erence can be at- tributed to the existence of the graphite phase in the C4.1 alloy. In addition, the variations of alloy hardness, DR hardness and ID hardness of the C0.5, C1.0 and C2.0 alloys are shown in  Fig. 5 b, from which one can clearly see that the alloy hardness and ID hardness increase with increasing C content, while the DR hardness was generally kept un- changed. Hence, it can be concluded that, the increase in the hardness of alloy was due to the increase in the hardness of the ID. It should be noted that, the hardness of the ID is larger than that of the DR, and the gap of hardness between them widens as the C content increases. Such di ﬀ erence should be related to the ultra ﬁ ne cellular structure of the ID region (see  Fig. 3 ).  

Representative fracture morphologies of the as-cast C0, C2.0, and C4.1 alloys after compression test are displayed in  Fig. 6 . The fracture surface of the C0 alloy, which fractured with lower strain, are composed of radial herringbone patterns, see  Fig. 6 a, indicating that the fracture mechanism of the C0 alloy is typical cleavage-fracture. For the C2.0 alloy ( Fig. 6 b), the fracture surface exhibited the deformed and sheared regions (corresponding with the ID and DR regions). The detailed fea- tures for the sheared and deformed regions of the C2.0 alloy were displayed in  Fig. 6 d and  Fig. 6 e, respectively. From  Fig. 6 b and d, a large amount of high-density short and cross-distributed tear edges could be obviously observed, and these tear edges were con ﬁ ned to the interior of the DR region grains. This is a typical quasi-cleavage fracture feature. Moreover, as shown in  Fig. 6 b and e, the deformed dimple-like structures were found, which were distributed in the alloy which would be bene ﬁ cial to the plasticity. It indicates that the ductile fracture of the ID region. Therefore, the fracture morphology of the C2.0 alloy re- vealed a combination of the typical quasi-cleavage fracture and ductile rupture microstructures. This is in good agreement with the mechanical properties of the C2.0 alloy ( Fig. 4 ). However, some secondary cracks were found to appear (as indicated by arrows in  Fig. 6 c) in the C4.1 alloy, which were the reason for the recession of both the fracture strength and strain of the alloy.

# 4. Discussion  

# 4.1. The e ﬀ ect of carbon addition on the microstructures of the as-cast HEAs  

The results (as shown in  Figs. 1, 2 and 3 ) demonstrate that the C addition could a ﬀ ect the microstructures of the AlFeCoNiC x  HEAs. The C-free AlFeCoNi alloy has the single B2 phase structure, while the C- doped alloys exhibited multi-phase and typical dendritic morphology. The moderate addition of C element (the  x  value  ≤ 0.08, i.e., the carbon content  ≤ 2.0 at%) to AlFeCoNi was found to have noticeable in ﬂ uence on the re ﬁ nement of grains and could promote the formation of the E2 1  and FCC phases. When the carbon contents was 4.1 at%, the graphite phase formed in the alloy, indicating that the carbon content exceeded the carbon solubility limit in the FCC matrix, and the  κ -car- bide was formed in the current HEAs. Similarly, carbon solubility was up to as much as 2.7 at% in the common austenitic TWIP steels  [29] , as well as 2.0 at% in the CoCrFeNiMn HEA  [16] . Unlike the results of previous researches about the C-doped HEAs  [14 – 16,23] , with in- creasing the carbon content, there is no M 3 C, M 7 C 3  or M 23 C 6  types carbides formed. # 5. Conclusions  

In this study, the microstructure and mechanical behaviors of the AlFeCoNiC x  ( x  = 0, 0.02, 0.04, 0.08, and 0.17) high-entropy alloys were investigated in the as-solidi ﬁ ed conditions. Alloying e ﬀ ect of the nonmetallic carbon element on the phase selection and properties, and the relationship between microstructure and properties of alloys were discussed. Following important conclusions can be drawn as follows:  

(1) The C-free AlFeCoNi alloy showed a typical columnar structure with single B2 phase, and the C-containing alloys exhibited typical dendritic morphology with single B2 phase in the DR and a mixture of the FCC and E2 1  phases in the ID. The volume fraction of the ID region increased with the increase of the carbon content. However, the graphite phase was formed in the ID when the  x  value reached to 0.17.  

(2) Interestingly and importantly, since carbon has large atomic size di ﬀ erence with other elements in current alloys, the C-doped alloys exhibited the coexisting of ordering and spinodal decomposition (COSD), and eventually gave rise to the formation of the ultra ﬁ ne cellular structure (ID region) consisting of the FCC and E2 1  phases. This type structure simultaneously posses high strength and good ductility and could makes tremendous contributions to improve the properties of the C-containing alloys.  

(3) The minor C addition signi ﬁ cantly enhanced the comprehensive performance of the AlFeCoNi HEA. The Vickers hardness increased from 422 HV to 469 HV with increasing the C content. The AlFeCoNiC 0.08  alloy (C2.0 alloy) exhibited a superior integrated property with the yield strength of 1115 MPa, fracture strength of 2517 MPa and fracture strain of 48.8%.  

(4) A complete brittle fracture in the AlFeCoNi alloy was found by fracture surface observation, and its fracture surface observation found a complete brittle fracture in the AlFeCoNi alloy, and its fracture mode is the typical cleavage-fracture with radial herring- bone patterns. For the C2.0 alloy, it revealed the quasi-cleavage fracture combined with ductile rupture, numerous short and cross- distributed tear edges were found in the DR region and deformed dimple-like structures were found in the ID region. However, some secondary cracks along with graphite appeared in the AlFeCoNiC 0.17  alloys (C4.1 alloy). These results were in good agreement with the results of mechanical property analysis.
