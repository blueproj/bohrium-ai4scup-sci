# Characterizing the Effects of a “Switchable Water” Additive on  the Aqueous Solubility of Small Molecules  

Jesse R. Vanderveen,  Sarika Burra,  Jialing Geng, Alexandre Goyon,  Alexander Jardine,  Hyejin  [a] E. Shin,  Tamer Andrea,  Paul J. Dyson,  and Philip G. Jessop\*  

Abstract:  “Switchable water” is an aqueous solution containing a  water-soluble amine additive that exhibits CO 2 -switchable properties,  such as large changes in ionic strength, by forming an ammonium  bicarbonate salt. Switchable water has been used to reversibly “salt- out” organic compounds from water. This study explores the salting  out of several compounds in switchable water when CO 2  is present  and also explores the solubility of small molecules in switchable  water, compared to pure water, when CO 2  is absent. The results  show that organic compounds are generally more soluble in  switchable water than pure water in the absence of CO 2 , but less  soluble in the presence of 1 atm CO 2 . Exceptions include carboxylic  acids and phenols which, presumably due to their acidity, are more  soluble in switchable water than in pure water, even when CO 2  is  applied. Kirkwood-Buff solvation theory was applied to gain insights  into the effects of the amine additive on the aqueous solubility of  caffeine. Furthermore, the switchable properties of the additives  allow for the preparation of switchable aqueous two-phase systems.  

# 1. Introduction  

Switchable water is an aqueous solution containing an  ionogen that can be reversibly switched between an ionic state  and an uncharged state.  The ionogens are water-miscible  organic bases such as amines or amidines. In aqueous solutions,  these bases can be protonated by the addition of CO 2 , creating  a bicarbonate salt. Removing CO 2  from solution reverses this  reaction and returns the solution to a state similar to that of the  initial solution.  

CO 2  is an ideal trigger for this type of acid-base switchable  system. In contrast to strong acids, the reaction of weak acids  such as CO 2  with amines is readily reversible and so requires  less energy input to reverse. As a gas, CO 2  can be more easily  removed from solution by evaporation, further decreasing the  required energy input. CO 2  is also inexpensive, readily available,  and generally considered safe to use. Other acids, such as  hydrochloric acid, can be used to protonate amine ionogens and  achieve similar salting-out effects as CO 2 .  However, for the  reasons described above, CO 2  is the preferred trigger for  switchable water systems.  

Protonating the switchable ionogen has multiple effects on  the properties of the solution. First, the ionogen is changed from  a neutral molecule to an ionic salt, causing the ionic strength of  the solution to increase. The ability to switch between solutions  with high ionic strength and low ionic strength has been explored  for applications such as separating water-miscible compounds  from  solution  (“salting  out”)   and  settling  clay  suspensions.  Second, the number of dissolved species  increases; a bicarbonate ion is formed for every protonated  basic site. A higher concentration of dissolved species increases  the osmotic pressure of the solution. This switchable property  has  been  used  in  forward  osmosis  water  purification  techniques.  

The ability of switchable water to salt-out organic  compounds has been attributed to the change in ionic strength  that can be achieved by adding CO 2  to switchable water. However, this salting-out ability is dependent on which ionogen  is used. In a study of diamine ionogens with varying alkyl chain  lengths between the two basic sites, it was found that ionogens  with long (≥7 carbon atoms) alkyl chain links were unsuccessful  in phase-separating tetrahydrofuran and water.  A molecular  dynamics computational investigation of such switchable  water/tetrahydrofuran mixtures also showed that diamine  ionogens with short alkyl chain links were superior to those with  long alkyl chain links for inducing phase separation.  The  molecular dynamics study also indicated that ionogens with long  alkyl chains are ineffective at inducing phase separation due to a  hydrophobic interaction between the central chain of the  ionogens and the tetrahydrofuran.  

Further insights into the interactions between the ionogens  and the solutes can be gained by the application of Kirkwood- Buff (KB) solvation theory. KB theory relates macroscopic  properties to molecular-level interactions using statistical  thermodynamics.  The inversion of KB theory reported by Ben- Naim allows for the derivation of molecular details from  macroscopic  measurements.   The  integrals  of  radial  distribution functions, called Kirkwood-Buff integrals (KBIs)  quantify the associations of the components of a mixture with  each other. KB theory has been applied to understanding the  ability of hydrotropes to increase the solubility of compounds in  solution.  Abbott  et   al.  have made online tools available to  facilitate the application of KB theory.  A similar application of  KB theory to the solubility of small molecules in switchable water  solutions could provide further insight into the effects of the  switchable ionogen on the solubility of organic compounds in  water as well as how the addition of CO 2  changes the behaviour  of the components of the solutions.  

Beyond changing the solubility of organic compounds,  switchable ionogens can be used in conjunction with other  additives to create aqueous two-phase systems (ATPSs). When  an aqueous solution containing sufficient concentrations of two  incompatible solutes is prepared, the mixture becomes an ATPS  and phase-separates into two immiscible aqueous phases, one  phase rich in one solute, and one phase rich in the other  solute.  Examples of solute combinations employed in  ATPSs are polymer-polymer, polymer-salt, and alcohol-salt  mixtures.  These systems are commonly used as non- denaturing separation media for proteins.  Recently, they  have also been applied to ionic liquids for extractions and  separations.  One particular example reported by Xiong  et al makes use of switchable water ionogens to create a switchable  ATPS with ionic liquids.  Adding or removing CO 2  from  switchable water solutions can therefore not only change the  solubility of a third component in solution, but also change the  compatibility of a third component with the switchable ionogen,  creating a switchable ATPS.  

In this study, the ability of switchable water solutions to  reversibly dissolve and precipitate organic compounds is  explored. The solubility of several compounds were measured in  both uncarbonated and carbonated switchable water solutions.  Reversing the changes in solubility of acetophenone, caused by  carbonation of the switchable water, was demonstrated over  three switching cycles. The effects of the switchable ionogen on  the solubility of caffeine was explored using KB solvation theory.  Finally, two reversible ATPSs were prepared using the  switchable ionogen and two different non-switchable additives.  

# 2. Results and Discussion  

# 2.1. Solubility of organic compounds in switchable water  

The solubility of 16 organic compounds in switchable water  were  measured  in  both  carbonated  and  uncarbonated  switchable (i.e. water containing a CO 2 -switchable amine  ionogen) water at room temperature (20 °C) and these solubility  were compared to those in pure water. The switchable ionogen  used in this study was  N,N,N’,N’ -tetra methyl butane-1,4-diamine  (TMBD). This amine was chosen because it was previously  shown to be a superior ionogen for separating tetrahydrofuran  from water.  The results are shown in Table 1, along with the  solubility of the various compounds in pure water at room  temperature. The switchable water solutions were prepared  using a 1:5 volume ratio of TMBD to water (\~0.9 M TMBD). In  general, compounds were more soluble in uncarbonated  switchable water than in pure water, demonstrating that TMBD  increases the solubility of these compounds in water. On the  other hand, compounds were generally less soluble in  carbonated switchable water than in pure water. These results  demonstrate that the TMBD bis-bicarbonate salt decreases the  solubility of these compounds in water. The decreased solubility  in carbonated switchable water is in agreement with previous  reports of tetrahydrofuran, acetonitrile, and  tert -butanol phase- separating from switchable water upon carbonation of their  mixtures.  Apart from a few exceptions, discussed below,  compounds tend to be more soluble in uncarbonated switchable  water than in pure water and less soluble in carbonated  switchable water than in pure water.  

In contrast, the solubility data collected for acidic organic  compounds (phenols and carboxylic acids) suggest that these  compounds interact differently with switchable ionogens than  non-acidic compounds do. Acidic solutes tend to have larger  relative  differences  in  solubility  between  uncarbonated  switchable water solutions and pure water than other  compounds. These increases in solubility are brought about by  an acid-base reaction of the solute with the basic switchable  ionogen to form a much more water-soluble salt. Thus, the effect  of TMBD on the solubility of acidic compounds is much greater  than it is for non-acidic compounds. Unlike the non-acidic  solutes studied, these acidic compounds are also more soluble  in carbonated switchable water than in pure water. In such  systems, the acidic solutes compete with CO 2  to protonate the  TMBD. The pH of a switchable water solution saturated with  benzoic acid (p K a  = 4.19)  was measured before and after  bubbling carbon dioxide through it. The pH of the solution was  not found to change after exposure to CO 2  (pH = 5.5 before, pH  = 5.5 after). Because the pH of the solution didn’t change and

Solutions prepared with different concentrations of TMBD  were expected to have different abilities to solubilize compounds.  The solubility of caffeine was measured in switchable water  solutions, both uncarbonated and carbonated, containing  different concentrations of TMBD (Figure 2). In the absence of  CO 2 , the solubility of caffeine increased in solutions with higher  -1 concentrations of TMBD up to a concentration of 2 M (\~0.3 g g ),  at which point the solubility began to decrease. At such large  concentrations of TMBD, the solvent may begin to take on the  properties of TMBD rather than those of water. The solubility of  caffeine in pure TMBD was found to be 2.2 ± 0.2 g/L, much  lower than its solubility in water. In the presence of CO 2 , the  solubility of caffeine in switchable water decreased as the  concentration of TMBD increased, even at TMBD concentrations  -1 greater than 2 M (\~0.3 g g ). These trends are in agreement  with the assertion that TMBD acts to increase solubility of  organic compounds in water, at least in concentrations up to 2 M  -1 (\~0.3 g g ), and that TMBD bis-bicarbonate acts to decrease the  solubility of organic compounds in water.  

# 2.2. Application of KB theory to the solubility of caffeine in  switchable water  

The KBIs for the water-TMBD-caffeine system were  determined and related to the solubilizing effect TMBD (or  TMBD bis-bicarbonate) has on caffeine in water. The changes in  the solubility of a solute (c u ) as a function of concentration of  additive (c 2 ) relate to the KBIs (G u2 , G u1 , G 22 , and G 21 ) through  Equation (1). In the water-TMBD-caffeine system, the subscripts  1, 2, and u denote water, TMBD (or TMBD bis-bicarbonate), and  caffeine, respectively. This equation demonstrates that changes  in solubility due to changes in additive concentration are caused  by one or both of two KBI comparisons. The first comparison,  G u2  – G u1 , describes the difference in association of the solute  with the additive and the association of the solute with water. If  the solute associates more with the additive, then increasing  concentrations of additive should increase the solubility of the  solute. The second comparison, G 22  – G 21 , describes the  difference in association of the additive with itself and the  association of the additive with water. If the difference is large  (meaning the additive preferentially associations with either itself  or with water), then the additive will not associate significantly  with the solute and will not affect its solubility substantially. Both  of these comparisons of KBIs relate to the solvating effect that  the additive has on the solute.

# 2.3.  Reversible  aqueous  biphasic  systems  containing  switchable water  

Mixtures of switchable water with common ATPS additives  were prepared to see whether these solutions would be  CO 2 -switchable ATPSs. In the absence of CO 2 , TMBD would act  as a low polarity solute that may form an ATPS with ionic  compounds. In the presence of CO 2 , the TMBD bis-bicarbonate  salt would act as an ionic solute and may form an ATPS with a  low polarity solute or a polymer. These mixtures should switch  between monophasic and biphasic depending on whether or not  CO 2  is present in solution as the switchable ionogen changes  between a low polarity state and an ionic state.  

A mixture containing water, TMBD, and an ionic compound 

 (K 3 PO 4 ) was prepared to observe the phase behaviour of the  mixture. The mixture formed an ATPS initially and the two  aqueous phases became miscible when CO 2  was passed  through the mixture (Figure 6a). Upon heating and passing  argon through it, the mixture separated back into two phases,  thereby demonstrating the formation of a CO 2 -switchable ATPS.  The presence of bicarbonate anion in the monophasic solution  13 was confirmed by  C NMR spectroscopy. It should be noted  that CO 2  causes the protonation of more than just TMBD in this  system; K 3 PO 4  is likely also being protonated. The p K aH   of  phosphate is 12.67,  making it more basic than TMBD (p K aH  =  10.3) and therefore more likely to be protonated by CO 2 . Part of  the switchable nature of this system might be ascribed to the  formation of K 2 HPO 4  and KHCO 3 . Protonation of K 2 HPO 4  is  possible, but only a small amount of KH 2 PO 4  is likely to be  present because the p K aH  of K 2 HPO 4  is 7.21,  which is lower  than the observed pH of the carbonated solution (approx. 8.0).  Regardless, all of these species are ionic compounds, so their  effects on the ATPS are expected to be similar. However, the  protonation of TMBD causes it to change from a low polarity  solute to a salt, which greatly changes its properties in solution  and likely plays a greater role in the switchable nature of this  ATPS than the protonation of K 3 PO 4 . Regardless of these  potential complexities, aqueous mixtures of K 3 PO 4  and TMBD  can form CO 2 -switchable ATPSs where the mixture is biphasic in  the absence of CO 2  and monophasic in the presence of CO 2 .  

Likewise, a mixture containing water, TMBD, and a  polymer (5000 MW polyethylene glycol mono-methyl ether,  mPEG-5000) was prepared to observe the phase behaviour of  the mixture. The mixture was monophasic in the absence of CO 2 and became biphasic when CO 2  was passed through the  mixture (Figure 6b). The presence of bicarbonate anion in both  13 phases of the ATPS were confirmed by  C NMR spectroscopy.  The two phases recombined when the mixture was heated and  flushed with argon to remove the CO 2 . By switching TMBD  between being a low polarity compound to a salt, a  CO 2 -switchable polymer-salt ATPS is formed. The behaviour of  this system is the opposite to that of the switchable ATPS  created using TMBD and K 3 PO 4 : monophasic in the absence of  CO 2  and biphasic in the presence of CO 2 . Thus, mixtures  containing TMBD and mPEG-5000 can be used as CO 2 - switchable ATPSs.  

# 3. Conclusions  

The ability of switchable ionogens to act as switchable  solubilizing/precipitating agents was explored. In the absence of  CO 2 ,  switchable  water  solutions  can  solubilize  organic  compounds to a greater extent than pure water. In the presence  of CO 2 , the opposite trend is observed: pure water solubilizes  organic compounds more than switchable water. In general,  acidic compounds such as benzoic acid and some phenols are  exceptions in that they are more soluble in switchable water,  even in the presence of CO 2 . The solubility of phenolic  compounds in switchable water still decreases when CO 2  is  added to the system, but the solubility of benzoic acid does not  change.  

The solubility of caffeine in switchable water solutions was  examined using Kirkwood-Buff theory. It was found that TMBD  associates strongly with caffeine at lower concentrations but  begins to associate more strongly with itself at high  concentrations. These effects cause the solubility of caffeine to  increase with respect to TMBD concentration in solutions with  low TMBD content, but decrease with respect to TMBD  concentration in solutions with high TMBD content. When CO 2  is  present, TMBD bis-bicarbonate associates poorly with caffeine  and has a negative effect on the solubility of caffeine.  

Mixing switchable water with water soluble salts or  polymers can result in the formation of switchable aqueous two- phase systems. When switchable water is mixed with K 3 PO 4 , an  ATPS is formed in the absence of CO 2 , but the ATPS can be  reversibly changed to a monophasic mixture by the addition of  CO 2  to solution. In contrast, a monophasic mixture is formed  when switchable water is mixed with mPEG-5000, but the  mixture can be reversibly converted to an ATPS by the addition  of CO 2 .  

This  switchable  solubilizing/precipitating  ability  switchable water can be useful for a variety of applications. One  possible application is in extracting and isolating organic  compounds using aqueous solvents, where the solubilizing  effects of the unprotonated switchable ionogen helps dissolve  the organic compound during the extraction step and the  precipitating effect of the protonated ionogen helps precipitate it  during the isolation step. Switchable water could also be useful  for creating switchable ATPSs for recovering polymers or ionic  liquids from aqueous solutions without creating aqueous phases  with permanently high salt concentrations.

 (GC) or high performance liquid chromatograph (HPLC). The GC used in  this study was equipped with a CP-volamine capillary column (Agilent)  and a flame ionization detector. The HPLC used in this study was  equipped with an Extend C18 column (Agilent) and an ultraviolet  absorbance detector.  

pH measurements were perfromed using an Orion 4 Star pH meter  and a Ag/AgCl electrode (Thermo Scientific). Conductivity measurements  were performed using a Jenway 470 conductivity meter.  

# Measuring the solubility of organic compounds in water and in  switchable water  

One calibration curve was created for each analyte in this study.  For each calibration curve, five standard solutions containing a suitable  range of analyte concentrations were prepared in methanol or acetonitrile.  2  The R value was greater than 0.99 for each calibration curve.  

To determine the solubility of a solute in water, an excess of solute  was added to 5 mL of water in a 22 mL vial. The vial was sealed, shaken  briefly, and then allowed to settle overnight at room temperature (20 °C).  The aqueous phase was sampled and the concentration of solute was  determined by GC or HPLC. This procedure was performed three times  and the average of the three measurements was taken to be the  solubility of the solute in water.  

To determine the solubility of a solute in uncarbonated switchable  water, an excess of solute was added to a switchable water solution  consisting of 0.6 mL TMBD and 3 mL water in a 22 mL vial. The vial was  sealed, shaken briefly, and then allowed to settle overnight. The aqueous  phase was then sampled and the concentration of solute was determined  by GC or HPLC. This procedure was performed three times and the  average of the three measurements was taken to be the solubility of the  solute in uncarbonated switchable water.  

To determine the solubility of solutes in carbonated switchable  water, one modification to the procedure for uncarbonated switchable  water was made. CO 2  was bubbled through the switchable water solution  -1 for \~30 min at a rate of 100 mL min  using a gas dispersion tube (10 mm  i.d., 25-50 µm porosity) before adding the solute. These conditions were  confirmed to be sufficient for fully carbonating switchable water solutions  by previous studies and by conductivity measurements of the solutions. No volume loss was observed as a result of the carbonation procedure.  

# Measuring the solubility of acetophenone in switchable  water over several cycles of uncarbonated and carbonated  states  

A 22 mL vial was charged with 12 mL water, 2.4 mL TMBD, and 

 2.5 mL acetophenone. The vial was sealed, shaken briefly, then allowed  to settle overnight. The aqueous phase was sampled, then CO 2  was  -1 bubbled through solution for \~30 min at a rate of 100 mL min  using a  gas dispersion tube (10 mm i.d., 25-50 µm porosity). The mixture was  sealed, shaken briefly, then allowed to settle overnight. The aqueous  phase was sampled, then heated to 80 °C for 1 h while argon was  -1  bubbled through solution at a rate of 100 mL min using the same gas  dispersion tube as the one used for CO 2 . The resulting mixture was  treated in a similar manner to that of the initial mixture and this procedure  was repeated until 6 samples of the aqueous phase were obtained. The  concentration of acetophenone in each sample was determined by HPLC.  

# Kirkwook-Buff analysis of the water-TMBD-caffeine system  

Solubility data for caffeine in switchable water with different  concentrations of TMBD were collected in the same way as described  above, but using different ratios of water and TMBD for each sample.  

Density and osmolality data were collected using the same  samples, prepared as follows. An approx. 60 mL solution of switchable  water was prepared with a pre-determined amount of water and TMBD  (e.g. 54 mL water, 6 mL TMBD) in a 100 mL two-neck round bottom flask.  The mass of each component was measured to confirm the composition  of the mixture. The mixture was stirred briefly (\~2 min.), and then a 25  mL portion of the mixture was used to fill a pre-weighed 25 mL volumetric  flask to volume. CO 2  was then bubbled through the remaining solution at  -1 a rate of 100 mL min  for 2 h using a gas dispersion tube (10 mm id, 25- 50 µm porosity). A condenser was placed in one of the necks to limit  evaporation during this process. After the solution was carbonated, a 25  mL portion of the mixture was used to fill a pre-weighed 25 mL volumetric  flask to volume. The density of the switchable water solutions was  determined from the mass of the solution in each 25 mL volumetric flask.  The density measurements were performed three times and the average  density was reported. The maximum standard deviation of these  -1 measurements was ±0.002 g mL . After density measurements were  made, the contents of the flasks were used to measure osmolality.  Osmolality measurements were performed using a custom-made  membrane osmometer following the description provided by Grattoni  et  al.  The osmometer was equipped with a Koch TFC-HR flat sheet  reverse osmosis membrane and a pressure transducer (0-100 bar,  Omega).  

The density of water/TMBD mixtures with high TMBD content were  measured as follows. A 10 mL volumetric flask was filled with TMBD and  the mass of the solution was measured. The TMBD was recovered,  placed in a 100 mL round bottom flask and sequentially diluted with  weighed portions of water. After each addition of water, the mixture was  stirred briefly (\~2 min.) and used to fill a 10 mL volumetric flask to volume.  After the mass of the 10 mL solution was determined, the solution was  returned to the round bottom flask and the next portion of water was  added. The volumetric flask was reused for every measurement and was  rinsed 3 times with the water/TMBD mixture before being filled to  minimize the loss of sample. For carbonated switchable water mixtures,  the same procedure was performed, but CO 2  was bubbled through the  solution for 2 h before obtaining the 10 mL portions. The standard  deviation of these measurements was assumed to be ±0.002 g mL based on the maximum error of the density measurements described in  the previous paragraph. Phase separation was observed in carbonated  mixtures with [TMBD] > \~3 M, so the density of these mixtures was not  recorded.  

The KBI values were calculated from these datasets using a  modified version of the web app developed by Abbott  et al .  The app  was modified to allow for analysis of dications with two counterions.

# ARTICLE  

CO 2 -switchable ionogens such  as N,N,N’,N’-tetra methyl butane- diamine (TMBD) can act as switchable  solubilizing/precipitating agents. When  CO 2  is absent, organic compounds  are more soluble in aqueous solutions  containing TMBD than in pure water.  When CO 2  is present, they are usually  less soluble than in pure water. The  same CO 2  switching method can be  used to create two different types of  switchable aqueous two-phase  systems.
