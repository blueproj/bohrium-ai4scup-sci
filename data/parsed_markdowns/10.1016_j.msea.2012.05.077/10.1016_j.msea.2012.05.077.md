The    mechanism of    primary MC    decomposition and    its    inﬂuence on    the rupture behaviors in    the    hot- corrosion resistant superalloy K444   are investigated by the   combination of scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and    transmission electron microscopy (TEM).    The results    reveal    that    the blocky primary MC mainly distributes at interdendritic regions and grain boundaries after the   standard heat treatment. During long- term thermal exposure, primary MC decomposes gradually, which can be summarized into three stages.            Firstly, the    primary MC    reacts    with    the   matrix and produces the   M 23 C 6  particles and     ′ ﬁlm,    which can be    described as    MC    +     → M 23 C 6  +     ′ . In    the    second stage, the    un-decomposed primary MC    arrests the Ni element and forms the M 6 C    particles and      phase, which can   be   expressed as   MC   +    → M 6 C +  .    In the third    stage, the    remaining primary MC    reacts with    the    diffused    Ni    element and    forms the        phase with M 6 C and    M 23 C 6  particles inside, which can    be    described as    MC    +     → M 6 C    +    M 23 C 6  +  .    The    reaction of the    second and third    stages should be ascribed to the    high (Ti    +    Nb    +    Hf)/Al    ratio and segregated W, Mo and    Cr element in the    decomposed region. In    addition, the platelet-like  phase is    found in    the    third stage. The primary MC decomposition has different effects    on    the    rupture behaviors between tensile and stress-rupture test.

# 1. Introduction  

Hot-corrosion resistant Ni-based superalloys have been widely used as blade materials of land-base gas turbine engines, which inevitably leads to various microstructural deterioration during the service for more than ten thousands of hours. Extensive stud- ies [1–5] have shown that prolonged thermal and stress exposure cause overaging of the microstructure, consisting of  ′ -phase coars- ening and coalescence, formation of continuous secondary M 23 C 6 carbide chains on the grain boundaries, primary MC    carbide decom- position and TCP phase formation, which is detrimental to the mechanical properties and, consequently, service life. Most of these features are reversible through appropriate rejuvenation heat- treatment, while primary MC carbide degeneration behavior is irreversible and might affect the aging kinetics of rejuvenated blades [3,6–8] .  

Generally, the evolution of carbides has obvious inﬂuence on the mechanical properties [12,13] . The effects of MC    decomposition on the mechanical properties have also been studied previously [14,15] . He et al. [14] reported the M 23 C 6  from MC    decomposition could effectively prevent grain boundary migration and strengthen grain boundary. However, Qin et al. [15] investigated the effect of MC decomposition on the high temperature rupture behavior, and concluded that the MC    decomposition was detrimental.  

Ni-based K444 superalloy is characterized by its excellent high temperature mechanical properties and hot-corrosion resistance capability due to the high content of Cr, Co, W and Mo    elements. However, the relative high level of W and Mo elements could inﬂu- ence the decomposition of primary MC    obviously. Moreover, the microstructure evolution during long-term thermal exposure is really very important, but till now no research has been done on primary MC    decomposition behavior in this alloy. Therefore, the investigation on primary MC    decomposition behavior in K444 alloy

# 2. Experimental procedure  

The chemical composition of the investigated alloy is given as follows (wt.%): 0.083 C, 15.56 Cr, 10.60 Co, 5.53 W, 1.98 Mo, 3.11 Al, 4.30 Ti, 0.21 Nb, 0.25 Hf, 0.053 B, 0.042 Zr and balance Ni. The alloy was melted in an industrial scale vacuum induction furnace and then cast into rods of 15 mm in diameter and 220 mm in length. As-cast rods used for long term thermal exposure were subjected to standard heat treatment: 1160   ◦ C/3 h/air cool + 1060   ◦ C/4 h/air cool + 850   ◦ C/16 h/air cool. After SHT, the rods were divided into three groups. The three groups were exposed at temperatures of 800   ◦ C, 850   ◦ C and 900   ◦ C for times of 1000, 3000, 5000 and up to 10,000 h, respectively.  

The rods subjected to exposure were machined into cylin- drical, threaded bars with gauge length of Ø5 mm × 25 mm and Ø6 mm × 30 mm for tensile and stress rupture test, respectively [16] .  

Metallographic samples were grounded, mechanically polished, and then etched by two different methods. Chemical etching method in a reagent containing 4 g CuSO 4 , 10 ml HCl and 20 ml H 2 O was used to reveal the  ′ , carbides, grain boundary structure and minor phases. Deep etching method in an electrolyte contain- ing 1 g tartaric acid, 10 ml HCl and 89 ml methanol, which stripped away  /  ′   matrix, was employed for three-dimension observation of various carbides. The etched samples were observed on a JEOL 6340 Field Emission Gun Scanning Electron Microscopy (FEGSEM) and Hitachi S-3400N scanning electron microscopy. Energy disper- sive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) analysis was performed to ascertain compositions of phases using an Oxford LINK ISIS system attached to the JEOL 6340 ﬁeld emission gun scanning electron microscopy.  

To prepare foils for transmission electron microscopy (TEM) observation, thin slices were sectioned and grounded. The foils were dimpled using gatan model 656 Dimple Grinder, and then ion milled to perforation using precision ion polishing system 691. TEM observation was performed on the JEOL 2000FX and TECNAI G2 F30 transmission electron microscopy. The photographs were recorded under bright-ﬁeld (BF), dark-ﬁeld (DF), and selected area electron diffraction (SAED). Z-contrast imaging was obtained in the Tecnai G2 F30 transmission electron microscopy equipped with a high-angle annular dark ﬁeld (HAADF) detector. The composi- tion analysis was also performed using an energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) attached to the TEM.

# 3.1. Primary MC    decomposition  

During long-term thermal exposure, the primary MC    decom- poses gradually, which depends on the aging temperature and time. Fig. 2 shows the initiation of the primary MC decomposition tak- ing place at 800   ◦ C for about 1000 h. It can be seen that amounts of ﬁne M 23 C 6  particles precipitate along the MC/matrix interface, as displayed in Fig. 2 (a). Further TEM observation indicates that a thin  ′ -M 23 C 6  ﬁlm forms a relative enclosing shell around the primary MC, as shown in Fig. 2 (b). And the SAED pattern in the inset reveals the M 23 C 6  has a perfect cube-on-cube [17] orientation relationship with the  ′   phase, which can be described as: (1 0 0) M 23 C 6 //(1 0 0)  ′   and [1 0 0] M 23 C 6 //[1 0 0]  ′ . According to the analysis above, the primary MC    decomposition in this stage can be expressed as: MC +  → M 23 C 6  +  ′ , which is consistent with previous reports [2]  

With the increasing of aging temperature and time, the sec- ond stage of primary MC    decomposition occurs. Fig. 3 shows the SEM observation on the decomposed MC after thermal exposure at 850   ◦ C for 3000 h. It can be seen that the  ′ -M 23 C 6  ﬁlm becomes thicker, and some brighter particles (Region 1) appear in the  ′ - M 23 C 6  ﬁlm, as shown in Fig. 3 (a). Moreover, a darker phase (Region 2) precipitates around the primary MC    in the deep etching speci- men, as shown in Fig. 3 (b). Fig. 3 (c) and (d) displays the EDS analysis of the brighter particles and the darker phase, respectively, which indicates that the brighter particle is rich in W, Mo and Cr elements while the darker phase is rich in Ni and Ti elements.  

TEM observation is performed to investigate the decomposed MC at the second stage, as shown in Fig. 4 . The HAADF micrograph clearly shows the morphology of the decomposed MC, which con- sists of M 6 C,  ,  ′ , M 23 C 6  and MC, as shown in Fig. 4 (a). The M 6 C and the  phases, as the decomposed phases of the primary MC decomposition in the second stage, form in the  ′ -M 23 C 6  ﬁlm. The SAED patterns conﬁrm the darker phase and the brighter parti- cle in Fig. 3 are  phase and M 6 C phase, respectively, as shown in Fig. 4 (b) and (c). The structure of  phase belongs to a space group P6 3 /mmc with the lattice parameters of a ∼ 5.07  A and c ∼ 8.22  A. While the structure of M 6 C phase belongs to a space group Fd3 m with the lattice parameter of a ∼ 11.16  A. In light of these ﬁndings, the MC decomposition reaction in this stage can be described as: MC +  M 6 C +  , which has not been reported previously.  

As thermal exposure proceeds, the third stage of the primary MC decomposition occurs. The SEM observation is carried out on the decomposed MC    after thermal exposure at 850   ◦ C for 5000 h. The most salient feature in this stage is the formation of mixed region around remaining MC, as shown in Fig. 5 (a). The mixed region, with the brighter M 6 C particles in the interior, exhibits mottled appear- ance, as seen in the inset. Through deep etching method, it can be seen that the mixed region around the remaining MC is composed of darker  phase, brighter M 6 C particles and some unknown grey particles (Region 3), as shown in Fig. 5 (b).  

Fig. 6 illustrates the TEM observation on the primary MC decom- position at the third stage. In the HAADF image, it can be seen that ﬁve different phases coexist around the remaining MC, as shown in Fig. 6 (a). The  ′ -M 23 C 6  ﬁlm is outside as a surrounding shell, while  phase with the M 6 C phase and M 23 C 6  particles in the interior is inside. The SAED pattern determines the unknown grey parti- cles as M 23 C 6  phase with the lattice parameter of a ∼ 10.53  A, as demonstrated in Fig. 6 (b). # 3.2. Cracks in the primary MC   decomposition region  

SEM observation on the longitudinal section of rupture speci- mens tested at RT, 900   ◦ C and 900   ◦ C/274 MPa is shown in Fig. 8 It can be seen that noticeable cracks, always penetrating the original MC    and the decomposed  phase, form in the primary MC decomposition region during tensile testing, as displayed in Fig. 8 (a) and (b). In addition, some cracks are observed at the grain boundaries and interfaces between various derivative phases from MC. In the stress rupture specimens, the cracks mainly form in the primary MC    decomposition zone, which is mostly located in the interdendritic regions, as shown in Fig. 8 (c) and (d). However, almost no cracks are observed in the grain boundaries even the primary MC    is present there. It seems that the primary MC    decomposition has different effects on the rup- ture behaviors of K444 alloys between tensile and stress rupture test.  

# 4. Discussion  

# 4.1. Mechanism of primary MC decomposition  

During long-term thermal exposure, the behavior of the primary MC    decomposition is a diffusion-controlled process [15] . Based on the analysis above, the primary MC    contains masses of C and Ti elements. Therefore, at the beginning of the primary MC decom- position, the C and Ti elements release from the primary MC and diffuse into the matrix, while the Ni, Al and Cr elements in the matrix diffuse towards the primary MC, so it leads to the forma- tion of an interdiffusion zone. The rapidly diffusing C atoms arrest the Cr atoms and form M 23 C 6  particles on the MC/  interface. The consuming of Cr atoms would increase the solubility of the  ′   form- ing elements in the  matrix [18] , which suppress the formation of  ′   phase. But the greater amounts of Ti atoms release from the pri- mary MC and diffuse into the interdiffusion zone, which promotes the formation of  ′   ﬁlm along the MC/  interface.  

With the increasing of thermal temperature and time, the  ′   ﬁlm becomes thicker, and the exchange of elements between primary MC and the  matrix becomes difﬁcult. The  ′   ﬁlm severely blocks the diffusion of Al through the  ′   ﬁlm from the matrix, while it also hinders the Ti diffusing into the matrix [19] , so it results in the high Ti/Al ratio on the MC/  ′   interface and promotes the nucleation of  phase. The diffusion of Ni element across the  ′   ﬁlm is compar- atively easier than that of the Al and Ti elements due to its larger diffusion coefﬁcient in the ordered  ′   phase [20,21] . Therefore, the (Ni, Ti)-rich   phase grows steadily after it nucleated.  

The extensive formation of   phase leads to the segregation of W, Mo and Cr elements due to their limited solubility in the  phase. The segregated W,    Mo    and Cr atoms combine with the C atoms

# 4.2. The formation of  phase around decomposed MC  

  phase, one of the most common TCP phases, is usually precip- itated during long-term thermal exposure in Ni-based superalloy [4,22] .    It has been reported that the formation of  phase is affected by various factors, such as chemical composition [23] , high-energy interfaces of second phases [24] . In this investigation,  phase, lying parallel to each other, forms around decomposed MC    after thermal exposure at 850   ◦ C for 5000 h, which is different from pre- vious investigation that  phase precipitates in dendrite core or interdendritic regions [4] . In order to investigate the mechanism of the precipitation of  phase in K444, the microstructure evolution is studied on another hot-corrosion resistant Ni-based superalloy K452 containing higher contents of Cr element ( Table 1 ), but no  phase is found in K452 during long-term thermal exposure [15] . So the difference on the concentration of metallic elements might not be the only reason for the formation of  phase around decomposed MC. Based on the examination above, carbon-depleted zone exists around the decomposed MC   in K444 while no carbon-depleted zone exists in K452. According to the previous research of B. Weiss et al. [25] , the high carbon content in solid solution can prevent or at least retard appreciably the nucleation of intermetallic phases. Therefore, it can be understood that the depletion of carbon around the decomposed MC    promotes the formation of   phase. Further analysis on the  phase around decomposed MC gives a hint that  phase might precipitate on the M 23 C 6  which acts as the preexisting interface reducing the barrier for the nucleation [24] . Furthermore, M 23 C 6  has a similar crystal structural relationship with  phase, and the decarburization could result in a topochemical transfor- mation of M 23 C 6  into  phase [26] . Combined the analysis above, it can be inferred that the carbon-depleted zone around decomposed MC leads to the formation of   phase on the M 23 C 6  particles. This is the reason that  phase only forms around decomposed MC.  

# 4.3. The formation of  phase from the decomposition of primary MC  

The formation of  phase from the decomposition of primary MC is also an interesting phenomenon in the present investigation. It precipitates after the formation of a thin  ′   ﬁlm, and expands inwards extensively after thermal exposure at 850   ◦ C for 5000 h. While in the previous research on K452 and GTD-111 alloys [10,15] ,  phase, as decomposed product of primary MC, has different for- mation features. In K452, only a little fraction of  phase forms after the formation of  ′ -M 23 C 6 -  ′   ﬁlm during thermal exposure at 850   ◦ C for 5000 h. However, in GTD-111,  phase forms at the initiation of primary MC    decomposition. Obviously,  phase forms at distinct aging stage in these three alloys, and this is attributed to the difference in chemical compositions of these three alloys.  

Based on the previous investigation [27,28] , the formation of  phase can be promoted by the high (Ti + Ta + Nb + Hf)/Al ratio. But the statistical data ( Table 1 ) reveal that the (Ti + Nb + Hf)/Al ratio is higher in K452 than that in K444. Therefore, it is concluded that the (Ti + Nb + Hf)/Al ratio is not the only factor related to the nucleation and growth of  phase. Further data analysis ( Table 1 ) indicates that (Ti + Nb + Hf) concentration is higher in K444 than that in K452, which might affect the formation of   phase. [11] has reported that the primary MC    decomposition is detrimental to the mechanical properties of K452 especially when the MC    is present in the grain boundaries. Choi et al. [30] has reported that the intergranular   phase transformed from pri- mary MC    reduces the room temperature ductility of GTD-111. But it still presents some interesting phenomenon about its effects on the rupture behaviors. In the tensile and stress rupture specimens, the primary MC    and its mainly derivative product,  phase, initiate cracks due to their little strain tolerance [31] . During the tensile test at room temper- ature and 900   ◦ C, the cracks mainly form in the grain boundaries other than the primary MC    decomposition region. While during the stress rupture test at 900   ◦ C/274 MPa, the cracks merely form in the primary MC decomposition region. This phenomenon is inter- esting and may be related to the load condition. During tensile test, the stress is strong, more than the yield stress, enough to initiate large populations of cracks in the grain boundaries. Therefore, the grain boundaries with large proportions are the weakest regions and lead to the ﬁnal intergranular rupture mode [32] . While in the stress rupture test, the stress is too low to initiate cracks even in the coarse continuous grain boundaries. The primary MC   decompo- sition regions become the weakest and produce masses of cracks from the brittle MC,  phase and the interfaces between various derivative phases from MC. Consequently, primary MC decom- position regions serve as the critical factor affecting the rupture mode of K444 alloy. However, it is difﬁcult to determine to what extent the primary MC    decomposition deteriorates the stress rup- ture properties because its effect cannot be distinguished from other microstructure degradation.  

# 5. Conclusions  

(1) In the standard heat treated K444 superalloy, the blocky pri- mary MC    mainly distributes at the interdendritic region and the grain boundaries.  

(2) During long-term thermal exposure, primary MC    decomposes gradually, which can be summarized into three stages. Firstly, the primary MC reacts with the  matrix and produces      the M 23 C 6  particles and  ′ ﬁlm, which can be described as MC +  → M 23 C 6  +  ′ . In the second stage, the un-decomposed primary MC    arrests the Ni element and forms the M 6 C particles and  phase, which can be expressed as MC +  → M 6 C +  . In the third stage, the remaining MC    reacts with the diffused Ni element and forms the  phase with M 6 C and M 23 C 6  particles inside, which can be described as MC   +   → M 6 C + M 23 C 6  +  . The reaction in the second and third stages should be ascribed to the high (Ti + Nb + Hf)/Al ratio and segregated W,    Mo and Cr element in the decomposed region.

 (3) The platelet-like  phase forms on the M 23 C 6  particles in the third stage, which can be attributed to the carbon depletion and the presence of M 23 C 6  around the decomposed MC.

 (4) Primary MC    decomposition has different effects on the rupture behaviors between tensile and stress rupture test.
