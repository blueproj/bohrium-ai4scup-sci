# a b s t r a c t  

Inconel X-750 is a Ni-based precipitation-hardened superalloy typically used in springs designed for high-temperature applications such as the hold-down springs in nuclear power plants.  c 0   is a major precipitate in X-750 alloys which affects the strength, creep resistance, and stress relaxation properties of the spring. In this study, a solution-treated X-750 wire coiled into a spring was used that was aged at various temperatures and submitted to stress relaxation tests with and without loading. Small angle neutron scattering was employed to quantify the size and volume fraction of  c 0   phase in the springs as a function of the aging temperature and the application of a load during stress relaxation. The volume fraction of  c 0   precipitates increased in the specimen aged at 732   C following stress relaxation at 500   C for 300 h. However, the mean size of the precipitates in the samples was not affected by stress relaxation. The specimen aged at the lower temperature (620   C) contained a smaller  c 0   volume fraction and gained a smaller fraction of  c 0   during stress relaxation compared with the sample aged at the higher temperature (732   C). The smaller increase in the  c 0   volume fraction for the sample aged at 620   C was associated with a larger increase in the M 23 C 6  secondary carbide content during relaxation. The Cr deple- tion zone around the secondary carbides raises the solubility of  c 0   thereby decreasing the volume fraction of  c 0   precipitates in Inconel X-750. In terms of stress relaxation, a larger increase in the  c 0   volume fraction was measured with loading rather than without. This is probably associated with the dislocation accumu- lation generated under loading that facilitate the nucleation and growth of heterogeneous  c 0   phase due to enhanced diffusion.

For X-750 alloys, the inﬂuence of work hardening and aging conditions have been studied on the mechanical properties such as tensile strength, yield strength, elongation, and hardness [5,6,9–11] . In particular, uniformly distributed  c 0   precipitates with an average size of 50 nm and an interparticle spacing of 50 nm have shown the optimum primary creep resistance and hardness [1] . Inconel X-750 is a Ni-based precipitation-hardened superalloy. It is commonly used in nuclear reactors either for original compo- nents or to repair hardware when high strength and corrosion resistance are needed  [1–4] . Speciﬁcally, X-750 wires are used in springs for high temperature operations, such as in exhaust gas  

over, coherent and homogeneously distributed  c 0   precipitates con- fer excellent high temperature tensile and fatigue properties  [1,4] .  

Stress relaxation characterizes the stress relieved under con- stant strain and is one of the most important properties of springs designed for high temperature applications. The stress relaxation has been shown to be delayed by  c 0   precipitates produced during aging, with additional  c 0   nucleating and coarsening during the stress relaxation test  [14,15] . However, very few studies have been conducted to quantify the size and volume fraction of  c 0   phase cre- ated during the stress relaxation of a spring with respect to the previous aging conditions, even though these are the main factors affecting the strength of the wire  [12–19] .  

This study focused on the effect of stress relaxation on the behavior of  c 0   in the absence of grain size variations. Stress relax- ation was tested at 500   C for 300 h to simulate the working con- ditions of the spring. The inﬂuence of stress relaxation with and without loading was quantitatively evaluated on the size and vol- ume fraction of  c 0   particles using small angle neutron scattering (SANS).  

# 2. Experimental methods  

The chemical composition of the Inconel X-750 superalloys used in the study is presented in  Table 1 . The material used was an annealed wire rod 5.5 mm in diameter. The ﬁnal wire was drawn to a diameter of 4 mm and solution-treated in a strand fur- nace at 1100   C for 130 s under a reduction atmosphere of hydro- gen and nitrogen. The wires were coiled into springs and underwent aging (hereinafter referred to AG samples) at 620   C, 680   C, and 732   C for 16 h. The aged springs were stress-relaxa- tion treated at 500   C for 300 h.  Fig. 1  shows the sample used for stress relaxation tests under an applied load. Hereinafter, this pro- cedure is termed AG-SR with loading. For the load-free tests, referred to here as AG-SR without loading, the spring was left unat- tached to the nut. The secondary carbide volume fraction and the  c 0 size and volume fraction were recorded as a function of the aging temperatures and of the stress relaxation conditions.  

The microstructure of the samples was investigated using opti- cal microscopy (OM) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM). Samples for OM and SEM analyses were etched with a mixture of oxalic acid (10 g) and diluted water (900 mL). The average grain size was measured following the American Society for Testing and Materials (ASTM) E112-10 intercept methods. The area frac- tion of the secondary carbides was analyzed with the LEICA LAS phase expert™software on a random subset of SEM images  [20] . The secondary carbide could be distinguished by color since car- bides are white while the matrix is dark gray. At least a dozen micrographs of SEM were taken at 5000   magniﬁcation to reduce local discrepancies.  

Small angle neutron scattering (SANS) experiments were per- formed on the 18 m instrument at the HANARO Center at the Korea Atomic Energy Research Institute. The wavelengths ( k ) used were 0.516 nm and 0.708 nm at 3 m and 9 m sample-to-detector dis- tances, respectively, and the wavelength spread  ð D k = k Þ  was about 12%. The intensity was obtained from  Q -values ranging 0.06– 1.1 nm  1 . Two-dimensional scattering patterns, collected using two-dimensional area detectors with an active area of 64.5    64.5 cm 2 , were averaged to produce a one-dimensional intensity proﬁle. # 3. Results and discussion  

# 3.1. Grain size and carbides formation as a function of aging temperature and stress relaxation conditions  

The effects of stress relaxation and aging temperature on the grain sizes are highlighted in  Fig. 2 . Grains having 27–29  l m in diameter were observed, with and without stress relaxation, for all aging temperatures in the range of 620–732   C.  

Fig. 3  shows the microstructures of the AG and AG-SR speci- mens, obtained by SEM, after aging at different temperatures. Energy dispersive spectroscopy results have previously revealed that the secondary carbides along the grain boundaries are M 23 C 6 enriched with Cr  [7] . Stress relaxation has no inﬂuence on precip- itation sites of the secondary carbides, i.e., along the grain bound- ary. However, stress relaxation increases the density of the secondary carbide precipitated for the aging temperatures 620   C and 732   C. Furthermore, raising the aging temperature from 620   C to 732   C leads to decreased carbide density along the grain boundary before stress relaxation ( Fig. 3 a and b) and after stress relaxation ( Fig. 3 c and d).

With additional stress relaxation (the AG- SR sample), the carbide volume fraction increases, by 0.45% follow- ing aging at 620   C, and by 0.1% after aging at 732   C. In order to clarify the relationship between Cr-rich carbides and  c 0   precipitation, quanti- tative analysis using a small angle neutron scattering (SANS) tech- nique was performed. # 4. Conclusion  

A quantitative assessment of the size and volume fraction of  c 0 phase was conducted on springs of Inconel X-750 alloy aged at var- ious temperatures and subsequently stress relaxed. Speciﬁcally, the effect of applying a load during stress relaxation was investi- gated on the  c 0   evolution using small angle neutron scattering (SANS). The results may be summarized as follows:  

(1) The size and volume fraction of the  c 0   precipitates increased with the aging temperature. Regardless of the stress relaxa- tion conditions, the mean diameter of the  c 0   particles was approximately 4 nm, 9 nm, and 17 nm, following aging at 620   C, 680   C, and 732   C, respectively. After stress relaxa- tion, the specimen aged at 732   C showed the most exten- sive  c 0   precipitation, with the nucleation of a large number of  c 0   particles around 10 nm in diameter. In contrast, the specimen aged at 620   C showed no increase in the  c 0   vol- ume fraction after stress relaxation.  

(2) For the specimen aged at 732   C, the large increase in the  c 0 volume fraction after stress relaxation was associated with a relatively small increase in the fractional volume of second- ary carbides, M 23 C 6 . A larger increase in the secondary car- bide content was observed in the samples aged at 620   C. This lead to reduced  c 0   precipitation due to the greater solu- bility of the  c 0   precipitates in the Cr depletion region around the secondary carbides.  

(3) The  c 0   size and volume fraction in the samples aged at 620   C did not vary following stress relaxation at 500   C both with and without loading. For the specimens aged at 732   C, how- ever, the increase in the  c 0   volume fraction was greater fol- lowing stress relaxation with loading rather than without,  

viz. 10% and 7.8%, respectively. During the stress relaxation, c 0   evolution is probably stimulated by dislocations (hence the favorable effect of loading) which act as nucleation sites and diffusion pathway for  c 0   formation. (4) The grain size in the samples varied little, either with increasing aging temperatures (from 620 to 732   C) or stress relaxation.
