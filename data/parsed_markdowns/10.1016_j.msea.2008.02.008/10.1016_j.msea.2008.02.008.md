# Abstract  

The present article examines microstructure stability and creep resistance of a 5th generation superalloy, which has Cr content at 4.6 wt%, 6.4 wt% Re and 5.0 wt% Ru, in comparison with that of a 4th generation superalloy (3.2 wt% Cr, 5.8 wt% Re and 3.6 wt% Ru). The aim is to elucidate the implication of increasing Cr, Re and Ru contents for future alloy developments. Experimental results have concluded that high Re + Ru content could promote formation of hexagonal    phase at 900   ◦ C; additional Cr and Re could enhance the precipitation of TCP phase at 1100   ◦ C. Although an increase in lattice misﬁt between    and   ′   in the 5th generation superalloy could strengthen the alloy against creep deformation under conditions at high temperatures ( ≥ 1000   ◦ C) and low stresses ( ≤ 245 MPa) whilst the microstructural stability remained, the tendency to raft should be avoided during creep at lower temperatures and higher stresses.

# 1. Introduction  

Nickel-base single crystal superalloys are state of the art turbine blade materials that consist of high volume fractions of cuboidal ordered L1 2   ′   precipitates in coherent with the disordered FCC    matrix  [1] . The turbine blades dictate performances of turbine section of the jet engine and have profound impact on the thermal efﬁciency  [2] . Hence, recent trends to further enhance the engine efﬁciency by increasing the turbine inlet temperatures have placed greater demand for new superalloys with improved elevated temperature properties, especially creep resistance  [2,3] . Current commercial Ni-base single crystal superalloys are heavily alloyed with solid solution strengthening elements, such as W, Mo and Re; the ability to incorporate further refractory content has been limited due to the precipitations of topologically close packed (TCP) phases during exposure at elevated temperatures  [4] . Formation of TCP phases can deplete strengthening elements such as W, Mo and Re from the   /  ′   matrix and is often associated with the initiation of creep damage  [1,5] . Ru addition in Ni-base superalloys can suppress the formation of TCP precipitates and has then become critical to the development of new generations of superalloy [6–8] . The enhanced microstructural stability due to Ru can accommodate additional refractory contents that not only further solid solution strengthen the alloy, but also increase the  /  ′   lattice misﬁt, which is one of the parameters for controlling the formation of   ′   raft and   /  ′   interfacial dislocation network [9] . Indeed, creep behaviour is strongly inﬂuenced by the microstructural evolutions  [10] . Under certain creep conditions, the onset of steady state creep can correspond to the completion of   ′   raft perpendicular to the stress axis and the formation of   /  ′   interfacial dislocation network  [11,12] . An alloy with larger negative lattice misﬁt can induce the formation of denser

  /  ′   interfacial dislocation network to conﬁne dislocations in

   phase during deformation  [11] . Although an increase in Ru and Re contents in superalloys can improve creep resistance, there are associated drawbacks in oxidation resistances  [13] . A previous work has shown that additional Cr content in a high Re + Ru bearing superalloy can improve its oxidation resistance [14] , however, the microstructural stability and creep behaviour of such an alloy have never been characterized. # 2. Experimental procedures  

Twoexperimentalsuperalloys,namelyTMS-138AandTMS- 196, have been examined in this study. Nominal compositions of these alloys are listed in  Table 1 ; chemical composition of TMS-196 is similar to that of TMS-138A with additional Cr, Re and Ru contents. The rationale behind the compositions was described in detail by a previous publication  [14] , comparing to TMS-138A, the reason for the increase in Ru and Re con- tents in TMS-196 was to provide further strength, whilst the moderate Cr content in TMS-196 was intended to improve the oxidation resistance.  Fig. 1  shows that TMS-196 outperformed TMS-138A against cyclic oxidation resistance at 1100   ◦ C. So, the following experiments were carried out to characterise the high temperature phase instability and creep for both alloys.  

Samples used for experiments were vacuum induction melted and fabricated inside the National Institute for Materials Science (NIMS) using standard directional solidiﬁcation casting proce- dures; materials were supplied as 10 mm diameter cylindrical bars with orientations within 10 ◦ of the [0 0 1] orientation. Sam- ples of TMS-138A and TMS-196 were subjected to a solution treatment at 1340   ◦ C for 10 h followed by a primary age of 4 h at 1100   ◦ C and a secondary age of 16 h at 870   ◦ C. Samples of TMS-138A, and TMS-196 were capsulated in silica glass tubes and then isothermally aged at 900 and 1100   ◦ C; microstruc- tural evolutions were carefully monitored at various exposure

Heat-treated bars of these two alloys were machined into standard speci- men geometry and creep tested along the [0 0 1] direction to tensile rupture under the following conditions that are relevant to the actual creep conditions experienced by turbine blades during service: (1) 900   ◦ C/392 MPa, (2) 1000   ◦ C/245 MPa, and (3) 1100   ◦ C/137 MPa. In addition, numbers of interrupted creep tests were performed for both alloys to monitor the microstructural changes during deformation in order to study the underlying mechanisms. Isothermally aged and crept specimens were observed under the JEOL-JSM6060 (SEM) and PHILIPS- XL30 (SEM-EDS) scanning electron microscopes to study the microstructures and compositions of constituent phases, e.g. TCP phases could be distinguished as the brighter phase with the backscattered image mode under SEM due to its higher refractory contents. SEM samples were prepared using standard metallographic techniques, with a ﬁnal polish of colloidal silica, then electrolytic ally etched by 5% HCl in ethanol at 10 V for 2 s to reveal the microstructures. Phase extraction by electrolytic method was performed by immersing specimens in an elec- trolyte composed of 10% HCl and 1% tartaric acid in methanol with a current density of 2 mA/mm 2 . Differential thermal anal- ysis (DTA) was conducted to study the   ′   solvus temperatures of both alloys.  

A JEOL 2000FX transmission electron microscope (TEM) was used to carry out dislocation analysis on creep deformed specimens. TEM samples were prepared from thin disks ( ∼ 0.4 mm in thickness) sectioned in parallel and perpendicular to the loading direction. The disks were mechanically ground to  ∼ 0.1 mm in thickness and electro chemically thinned using a twin-jet polisher with a chemical solution composed of 100 ml perchloric acid + 200 ml ethanol + 600 ml 2-butoxyethanol at around 273 K. Dislocation studies were based on a minimum of three TEM samples for every creep condition to ensure rep- resentative observations.

# 3.1. Phase instability  

After 600 h exposure at 1100   ◦ C, isolated batches of TCP precipitates could be detected in both alloys,  Fig. 3 , these TCP precipitates were shown to exhibit needle-like geometry along the (1 1 0) section and situated in the dendritic region outlined by the dotted lines. # 4. Discussions

Each of these factors for TMS-138A and TMS-196 has been examined and compared. The implications of these associ- ated differences between both alloys are discussed with respect to results presented.

Isothermal ageing of both alloys at 1100   ◦ C for 600 h has rendered the microstructures small degree of instability asso- ciated with precipitations of TCP phases,  Fig. 3 . TMS-138A exhibits greater microstructural stability than TMS-196 even though TMS-138A contains less amount of Ru. This microstruc- ture instability of TMS-196 can be attributed to higher Re and Cr contents, because both elements have electron vacancies in the d-shell orbitals that provide high potential to form strong TCP phase bonds between Mo and W  [17] ; Re tends to segre- gate heavily to the dendritic core, in which TCP precipitates can ﬁrst be observed. During isothermal ageing at 900   ◦ C, precipita- tions of    phase can be detected in TMS-196 whilst TMS-138A has remained stable for 1000 h,  Fig. 4 . Unlike TCP precipitates that are rich in Re and W, the    phase contains high levels of Ru and Re,  Table 2 ; this suggests that Ru can be a potent   phase forming element. Although there is no reported effect of   phase on mechanical properties in these advanced superalloys, the composition of    phase does indicate a degree of Re and Ru depletions from the matrix microstructure, hence the formation of    precipitate should be avoided. However, further reducing the residual Re segregation by further solution heat treatment should reduce the tendency for TCP and    phase formation in TMS- 196. These experimental results do suggest that high Ru content in superalloys may not necessary imply greater microstructural stability; the overall chemical composition needs to be balanced (e.g. Re and Cr contents) in order to prevent precipitations of both TCP and    phase. Since the orientation of TCP precipitates is along (1 1 1) of the matrix microstructure, the presence of TCP can disrupt the matrix microstructure, hence the presence of TCP phase can reduce its resistance against deformation, so a well-balanced composition with Ru addition to prevent TCP precipitation is important.  

The differences between creep behaviours of TMS-138A and TMS-196 demonstrate the important role of   /  ′   lattice misﬁt in inﬂuencing deformation mechanisms. The   /  ′   lat- tice misﬁt of TMS-196 compared to that of TMS-138A is larger toward negative due to higher amounts of Re and Ru that partition preferentially into the    phase and expand the lat- tice parameter of    [18,19] . Increasing the   /  ′   lattice misﬁt toward negative can not only enhance rafting kinetics, but also reﬁne the dislocation network at the   /  ′   interface during high temperature creep  [9] . Although the formation of raft is ben- eﬁcial against creep at high-temperature (1000   ◦ C)/low-stress (245 MPa) condition, the rafted microstructure can be detrimen- tal under intermediate-temperature (900   ◦ C)/intermediate-stress (392 MPa) creep condition. During the ﬁrst 200 h of creep under 900   ◦ C/392 MPa,  Fig. 5 (a), TMS-138A retained its cuboidal   /  ′ microstructure whilst that of TMS-196 had rafted to permit dis- locations cutting through the rafted   ′   under this stress condition, Fig. 6 (a) and (b), hence the primary creep strain of TMS-196 was signiﬁcantly larger than that of TMS-138A,  Fig. 5 (a). However, TMS-196 exhibited a slightly longer creep rupture life,  Fig. 5 (b); this could be attributed to higher contents of Ru and Re. Under 1000   ◦ C/245 MPa, both alloys rafted and pronounced the steady state creep,  Fig. 5 (c); TMS-196 was signiﬁcantly more resistant against creep.  Fig. 6 (c)–(f) compare both alloys in terms of the average   /  ′   interfacial dislocation network and the average raft spacings; TMS-196 had a denser interfacial dislocation network and ﬁner raft spacing. During creep deformation, TMS-196 was strengthened by these ﬁne interfacial dislocation network and raft spacing; the rafted microstructure of TMS-138A was cut by dislocations whilst that of TMS-196 could inhibit dislocation activity at the interface,  Fig. 6 (e) and (f). Under the creep condi- tion of 1100   ◦ C/137 MPa, TMS-196 under-performed against TMS-138A,  Fig. 5 (d). Both alloys had rafted to induce the steady state creep; however, the creep resistance of TMS-196 was degraded by the phase instability associated with the for- mation of TCP phases,  Fig. 7 (b). These TCP precipitates could deplete Re and W from the matrix and weaken the ability of the rafted structure to withstand load, hence the more stable alloy, i.e. TMS-138A, performed better than TMS-196, Figs.  5 (d) and 7 (a).  

Although the associated increase in   /  ′   lattice misﬁt due to higher Re, Ru and Cr for TMS-196 can enhance the rafting kinet- ics and reﬁne the   /  ′   interfacial dislocation network that can be beneﬁcial against creep at higher temperature (1000   ◦ C)/lower stress (245 MPa) whilst the microstructure remains stable, the tendency to raft should be avoided at lower temperature (e.g. 900   ◦ C)/higher stress (392 MPa) creep condition.  

# 5. Conclusions  

The phase stability, lattice misﬁt and microstructural evo- lutions are controlled by the compositions and are required to be balanced well in order to address deformation mechanisms across a wide temperature range. The amount of Cr, Re and Ru to be added in Ni-base single crystal superalloys should be adjusted in TMS-196 to avoid the formation of TCP and the precipitation of    phase. Finally, the increase in lattice misﬁt can enhance the raft formation and reﬁne the interfacial dislocation network for effective strengthening during high temperature creep, however, the rafting kinetics at lower temperatures should be avoided.
