# Intermetallic phases formed by ruthenium–nickel alloy interdiffusion  

B. Tryon \* , Q. Feng, T. Pollock  

Department of Materials Science and Engineering, 3062 H.H. Dow Building, 2300 Hayward Street, University of Michigan, Ann Arbor, MI 48109 USA  

Available online 15 April 2004  

# Abstract  

Diffusion couples were formed between elemental ruthenium and a low refractory-content nickel-base superalloy. The couples were heat treated at various temperatures to evaluate diffusion paths and phase equilibria between the constituent materials. The interfacial layers formed by interdiffusion have been evaluated by electron microprobe analysis and transmission electron microscopy. An intermetallic Heusler phase, based on Ru 2 AlTa, formed at the interface of the couples during heat treatment. Two buttons of similar composition to the interface layer were arc-melted and analyzed by X-ray diffraction and transmission electron microscopy. The results of these analyses and their implications for nickel-based systems and their coatings will be discussed.

# 1. Introduction  

Ruthenium ( d -Ru) is one of four elements that increase the liquidus temperature of nickel ( g -Ni) and is being investigated as an alloying addition to bulk Ni-base single crystal superalloys for improvement of high-temperature capabilities  [1–5] . While the diffusion of various alloying additions to Ni-base systems has recently been investigated [6,7] , there has been limited attention to diffusion of Ru and the resultant formation of Ru-rich intermetallic precipitates.  

In addition to improving the high-temperature properties of bulk Ni-base superalloys, RuAl-based B2 compounds are of interest as an interlayer component of superalloy thermal barrier coatings (TBCs). Due to the effectiveness of the B2 Ni(Pt)Al materials presently used as the bond coat (BC) layer for Ni-base superalloy TBCs, higher temperature RuAl-containing BC layers are being examined as a possible improvement in temperature capability and as a lower cost alternative to Pt.  

Pt-modiﬁed NiAl diffusion coatings are currently produced by depositing a thin ﬁlm of Pt on the superalloy surface and annealing at an elevated temperature  [8] . Aluminization of the coating produces the B2 Ni(Pt)Al bond coat structure. Determining a process path to establish a Ru-containing interlayer analogous to the Pt-modiﬁed aluminide system requires knowledge of interdiffusion and phase equilibrium characteristics with respect to the incorporation of Ru into Ni-base superalloys.  

In the present study, these issues were studied via fabrication of diffusion couples of nominally pure Ru and a low refractory-containing Ni-base superalloy.  

# 2. Experimental procedures  

The Ni-base superalloy, with composition listed in Table 1 , was cast into single crystals by the conventional Bridgman process and subsequently homogenized at 1300  8 C for 24 h. The superalloy used in this work contained relatively few alloying additions and was particularly lean in refractory additions, thus, it was denoted the ‘Lean’ alloy.  

The superalloy was sectioned into several blocks, and the diffusion couple surfaces and the opposing loading surfaces were ground ﬂat and parallel. An arc-melted button of nominally pure  d -Ru was fabricated from high purity stock of Ru (99.98%) and, from this, blocks were prepared similar to the superalloy specimens. The dimensions of each block were approximately 4 mm in length by 3 mm in width with a thickness of about 3 mm.  

Diffusion couples were fabricated by joining one  d -Ru block with one superalloy block under a constant load of 50 MPa in a vacuum furnace at an elevated temperature. # 3. Results  

Interdiffusion across the Ru-Lean alloy diffusion couple boundary during annealing produced a new phase at the interface of the couple, shown in the backscattered electron (BSE) images given as  Fig. 1 . It appears discontinuously at the boundary of the couple at 1100 and 1250  8 C. The composition of this phase was determined by EMPA and is given in  Table 3 . The interface phase primarily contained Ru, Al, and Ta with some smaller amounts of Ni and Cr. Any other elements observed in this phase were present only in trace amounts. EMPA line scans across this specimen suggest that the formation of this interface phase between the  d -Ru and the superalloy consumes Ta from the superalloy.

# 4. Discussion  

A B2 derivative Heusler phase intermetallic, Ru 2 AlTa, exists along the diffusion path between  d -Ru and the observed Lean Ni-base superalloy. This Heusler interface phase appears to consume Ta from the base alloy. This observation agrees with another recent assessment that suggests that Ru-rich layers inhibit the outward migration of elements from Ni-base superalloys  [12] .

It is interesting that this phase contains only very limited amounts of Ni, but substantial quantities of Ta. The limited solubility of Ni was observed in the interlayer as well as in the bulk arc-melted buttons. In the button, which did contain a Ni addition, the Ni was segregated to the interdendritic eutectic. In this same temperature range, the ternary Al–Ni–Ru system contains two distinct B2 phases: NiAl and RuAl. The RuAl has a substantial solubility for Ni, with at least 30% Ni found in solution, according to the current

Al–Ni–Ru phase diagrams  [13] . Further systematic alloy- ing studies would be required to determine the extent of solubility of Ta in RuAl and the point at which the B2 derivative Heusler phase, forms as a ternary phase.  

Site substitutions for Ta additions to RuAl have been modeled elsewhere with the Bozzolo–Ferrante–Smith (BFS) theoretical approach  [14] . This modeling predicts that in both Ru- and Al-rich RuAl materials that Ta prefers to reside on Ru sites  [15] . The results from EMPA, TEM, and the overall stoichiometry of the B2 phase observed in this study do not support this prediction, but instead suggest that Ta exists on the Al sublattice. This is similar to the site preference of Ta in NiAl  [16] .  

Characterization of the buttons which were fabricated with similar compositions to the interface phases observed in the diffusion couples conﬁrms the existence of the Heusler phase. Although the solidiﬁcation path for arc-melted materials does not typically promote uniform distribution of composition within a specimen, the uniform grain interiors in the buttons reveal that the increase in Ta concentration stabilizes the Heusler phase over the B2 phase. Very limited work on the Al–Ru–Ta ternary system has been performed [17]  and a phase diagram for this system has not been published to the authors’ knowledge; however, results from this work do indicate equilibrium phase ﬁelds between  d -Ru and the Ta-rich RuAl as well as between  d -Ru and Ru 2 AlTa.  

The presence of a B2-derivative Heusler phase inter- metallic forming at the interface of the diffusion couples is of interest to the area of coatings for Ni-base superalloys. Current diffusion and thermal barrier coating systems have an interlayer consisting of the B2 structure, which is Pt enriched NiAl  [18–20] . Since the Heusler phase identiﬁed here has limited solubility for Ni and consumes Ta from the base superalloy, it may serve as an effective barrier layer. This phenomenon is of interest because diffusion of elements such as Ta and W from the substrate into the ceramic layers of the TBC can degrade the YSZ coating  [18] .  

In the case of the thermal barrier system, the physical and mechanical requirements for this interlayer are complex. It must oxidize slowly, have compatible thermal expansion characteristics, and minimize diffusion of alloying elements from the base superalloy up to the oxide surface. NiAl provides environmental resistance for the substrate because of its ability to readily form an alumina scale  [19,20] . Advances in coating technology have shown that Pt-modiﬁed NiAl coatings outperform conventional NiAl coatings  [18] . It is thus interesting to consider improving the compatibility of this layer by substitution of other B2-type compounds with improved capabilities.  

Like the more conventional BC layer of B2 Ni(Pt)Al, RuAl is a B2-based system also stable over a range of composition, but with a melting point over 400  8 C higher than NiAl. RuAl has a lower coefﬁcient of thermal expansion than other common B2 aluminides  [21–23]  and exhibits characteristics of better intrinsic deformability than NiAl over a wide range of temperature  [24–26] . It also has a greater number of slip systems than NiAl and is reported to have a high toughness and compressional ductility  [27] . Alloying of RuAl may lead to further enhancement of its properties  [28,29] .  

Finally, formation of a Ta-containing RuAl or a Ru 2 AlTa Heusler phase is relevant to the processing of Ru-modiﬁed BCs. Both structures appear capable of acting as a diffusion barrier layer which may potentially prolong the life of TBC systemsbygetteringdetrimentalelementsfromthesuperalloy substrate. Determination a processing path capable of achieving a suitable diffusion barrier layer along an aluminization processing path between Ru and superalloys is of interest for analyzing the potential beneﬁts of a Ru- modiﬁed BC for TBCs.  

# 5. Conclusions  

A Ru 2 AlTa Heusler phase intermetallic exists along the diffusion path between  d -Ru and a Ni-base superalloy. The Heusler interface has a low solubility for Ni and consumes a signiﬁcant amount of Ta from the base material.
