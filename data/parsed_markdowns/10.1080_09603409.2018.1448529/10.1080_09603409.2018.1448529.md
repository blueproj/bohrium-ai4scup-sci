# ARTICLE HISTORY  

# ABSTRACT  

Effect of heat treatment on the recovery of microstructure and mechanical properties of a  service-exposed IN939 superalloy was studied. Four-stage heat treatment was performed on  the service-exposed alloy. The microstructures of the service-exposed and rejuvenated alloys  were examined by optical and scanning electron microscopes. The hardness, tension, and stress- rupture tests were carried out to characterise the mechanical properties. The results showed  that the heat treatment could rejuvenate the microstructure of the alloy that was deteriorated  during the service at high temperatures. Decomposed MC carbides were transformed to new  fine carbides, continuous M23C6 carbides were dissolved and new discontinuous carbides  along the grain boundaries were regenerated and, finally, the fine γ  particles were reformed. The  microstructural recovery resulted in an increase in the hardness and ultimate tensile strength of  the alloy. The results also showed that the creep behavior of the alloy at a testing temperature of  850°C could be improved by the heat treatment.

Nickel base superalloys used in gas turbine blades and  vanes experience high temperatures and stresses during  service. These service conditions could cause various  microstructural changes during the operation period  including coarsening and coalescence of the gamma  prime particles, the formation of continuous second- ary M23C6 precipitates on the grain boundaries, pri- mary MC degeneration and topologically close-packed  phase formation [ 1–14 ]. Degradation of the mechanical  properties such as hardness, tensile strength, and creep  resistance usually occurs because of the time-dependent  microstructural changes. The use of expensive materials  in gas turbine blades and vanes requires a repair process  providing the re-establishment of the initial properties  

A wide investigation was performed on the rejuvena- tion of nickel base superalloys including IN738 [ 15–17 ]  and GTD111 [ 18 ] using heat treatments and hot iso- static pressing (HIP) process. It was found that applying  a three-stage heat treatment of 1190°C/4 h + 1120°C/2  h + 845°C/24 h on a degraded IN738LC could produce  a microstructure analogous with that of the new alloy  [ 15 ]. In another research by Wangyao et al. [ 18 ], the  best heat treatment for reaching optimized microstruc- ture and high-temperature mechanical properties of a  service-exposed GTD-111 nickel base superalloy was  suggested to be 1140°C/2 h + 1055°C/1 h + 845°C/24 h.  The intrinsic loss of creep ductility induced by service  in an IN738 blade material was also fully recovered by  HIP rejuvenation [ 16 ].  

More recently, a high chromium alloy has been devel- oped namely IN939 with 22.4% chromium. This alloy  is more resistant to hot corrosion than IN738LC [ 19 ]  and has been used in industrial and marine gas turbines  [ 20 ]. Many studies were found on the heat treatment of  the cast alloy that resulted in developing of two, three  and four stage heat treatments [ 18 , 21 , 22 ]. The stand- ard four stage heat treatment consisted of the following  stages: (I) solution heat treatment at 1150°C for 4 hours  and air cooling to the room temperature, (II) first stage  aging at 1000 °C for 6 hours and air cooling to the room  temperature, (III) second stage of aging at 900 °C for 24  hours and air cooling to the room temperature, and (IV)  final stage of aging at 700 °C for 16 hours and air cool- ing to the room temperature. The microstructure of the  alloy after four stage heat treatment included primary  MC carbides, solid solution matrix (γ), γ ′  precipitates  within the grains and M23C6 carbides located at grain  boundaries [ 23 ]. This heat treatment gave an excellent  combination of room temperature yield strength and  ductility, together with good creep properties, and  the overall balance of properties was suitable for tur- bine vanes, fuel nozzles, casings, and some other high-  temperature applications [ 5 ].  

It was indicated that prolonged operation resulted in  some microstructural changes and mechanical weak- ening in the service-exposed IN939 alloy. The major  microstructural changes included the formation of  continuous grain boundary carbides, the degeneration  of MC carbides located at the grain boundaries, and to  some extent, the changes in the size and morphology of  γ ′  particles [ 5 ]. Ultimate tensile strength, elongation, and  reduction of the area of the service-exposed alloy were  also found to be very low at room temperature.  

The main aim of the present paper is to study the pos- sibility of rejuvenation of a service-exposed IN939 alloy  using heat treatments. To our knowledge, there was no  study on the effect of heat treatment on the microstruc- tural and mechanical recovery of the degraded IN939  alloy. Therefore, regarding the high cost of the turbine  vanes made of IN939 alloy, it is essential to study the pos- sibility of rejuvenation of microstructure and mechani- cal properties of the service-exposed IN939 alloy.  

# Experimental procedure  

Service-exposed 4th stage vanes of a 159 MW gas turbine  made from IN939 alloy after the prolonged operation  was used in the present study. The vanes experienced a  recommended service time of about 65,000 h at working  temperatures up to 800°C. The chemical composition  of the alloy was obtained using optical emission spec- troscopy (OES) analysis and is shown in Table  1 . The  chemical composition of the standard IN939 alloy is  also presented in the table. Chemical analysis in Table  1 revealed that the as-received vanes made of IN939 alloy.  

To investigate the microstructure of the alloy, the  samples were cut from the middle zone of the airfoil at  the leading edge of the vane. The samples were grounded  using silicon carbide papers, and then mechanically pol- ished using diamond paste. The samples were etched in  a solution contained 0.3 g MoO , 10 ml HNO , 10 ml  3 3 HCl, and 15 ml distilled water and their microstruc- ture were investigated by optical microscopy (OM). The  microstructure of some specimens was studied in a more  precise way using a scanning electron microscope (SEM)  equipped with an energy dispersive X-ray spectrome- ter (EDS) for chemical analysis. For this purpose, some  specimens were electro-etched in an oxalic acid solution  (10% in water) using a voltage of 3 V.  

In order to investigate the effect of heat treatment  process on the rejuvenation of the degraded alloy, dif- ferent solutionizing and aging treatments at various  temperatures and times were performed on the sam- ples. Comparing the mechanical properties and micro- structure evaluation of the various heat treatments, the  standard four-stage heat treatment, i.e. 4 h/1150°C +   6 h/1000°C + 24 h/900°C + 16 h/700°C was found to be  the most effective process. Therefore, only the properties  of the degraded alloy after the four-stage heat treatment  are presented in the present paper and compared to the  service-exposed alloy. The microstructure of the heat- treated alloy after various stages of heat treatment was  characterised using optical microscope and SEM.  

Vickers harnesses of the service-exposed and heat- treated samples were measured using an ESEWAY  hardness tester under a load of 30 kgf. Hardness was  measured at six different points on each sample and  the average values were reported as the hardness num- ber. Tensile tests were also performed as per ASTM: E8  specification at a temperature of 25°C. Standard round  tension samples, 50 mm in gage length and 12.5 mm  in diameter were prepared from the airfoil of the vane  by machining. The diameter of the smallest cross sec- tion of the fractured samples after testing was meas- ured and the reduction of area (RA) was calculated. In  order to characterise the creep behavior of the samples,  stress-rupture tests were carried out as per ASTM: E139  specification at a testing temperature of 850°C under a  stress of 350 MPa.

Figure  4 (d) shows a grain boundary of the rejuvenated  alloy at a higher magnification. This was attributed to the diffusion  of the precipitate forming elements from the matrix to  γ ′  particles. However, after the dissolution of γ ′  particles  during solutionizing, i.e. 4 h/1150°C in Figure  2 (d) the  new finer γ ′  particles were formed after four-stage heat  treatment in Figure  4 (d).

# Mechanical properties  

Figure  6  shows the hardness values of the as received and  rejuvenated IN939 alloys. The hardness of the alloy after  different stages of the heat treatment is also shown in the  figure. After the first stage of the heat treatment, i.e. 4 h  at a temperature of 1150°C, the hardness was decreased  from a value of about 404 Hv for the as-received alloy to  380 Hv. This could be attributed to the dissolving of γ ′   particles and M C  carbides during solution treatment  23 6 at a temperature of 1150°C as shown in Figures  2 (c)  and (d). Figure  6  shows that the first stage of aging treat- ment (i.e. 4 h/1150°C + 6 h/1000°C) resulted in a further  decrease in the hardness of the alloy. This could be prob- ably due to the coarsening of the primary fine γ ′ phases  during the aging treatment that was previously formed  during cooling from 1150°C [ 18 ].  

Next stages of the heat treatment resulted in an  increase in the hardness of the alloy as shown in Figure  6 .  The figure shows that a final hardness of about 420 Hv  was reached after the four-stage heat treatment. This  means an increase of about 15 Hv in the hardness of  the service-exposed alloy by applying the four-stage  heat treatment. Therefore, the standard four-stage heat  treatment could result in an increase of about 4% in the  hardness, due to the microstructure rejuvenation of the  deteriorated alloy. It seems that the size and the volume  fraction of γ ′  particles are the most important factors in  the hardness value of the nickel base superalloys [ 18 ].  Microstructural observations revealed that the size of γ ′   particles was decreased from values of 0·3–0·8 μm in the  service-exposed alloy (Figure  1 ) to values of 0·1–0·2 μm  in the heat-treated alloy (Figure  4 (d)). This is the main  reason for the increase in the hardness of the alloy after  four stage heat-treatment. The hardness of a heat-treated  as-cast IN939 superalloy was reported to be about 422  Hv [ 26 ] that was close to the hardness of the rejuvenated  alloy in the present study.  

Stress-strain curves of the service-exposed and heat- treated alloys at room temperature are shown in Figure  7 .  The tensile properties of the samples were obtained from  the curves as well as dimensional measurements and  the results are also presented in Figure  7 . The results  show that the heat treatment improved the mechani- cal properties of the alloy at a temperature of 25°C as  compared with the as-received sample. The ultimate  tensile strength (UTS) and elongation of the alloy were  increased by about 10 and 130%, respectively. This  resulted in a greater total area under the stress-strain  curve for the heat-treated alloy indicating its higher  toughness as compared with the service-exposed one  [ 27 ]. # Conclusions  

The possibility of recovery of microstructural and  mechanical properties of a service-exposed IN939 alloy  after exposing to temperatures up to 800°C for 65,000 h  was investigated. A four-stage heat treatment, i.e. 4 h/115 0°C + 6 h/1000°C + 24 h/900°C + 16 h/700°C was applied  on the alloy and the following results were obtained:  

•   Service-induced microstructural changes in the  vane consisted of MC decomposition, the forma- tion of continuous M C  carbides at the grain  23 6 boundaries and γ ′  phase coarsening. •   Four-stage heat treatment resulted in the rejuve- nation of the deteriorated microstructure of the  service-exposed IN939 superalloy. The micro- structural refurbishment included dissolution of  the decomposed MC carbides and formation of  some new fine MC carbides, dissolution of con- tinuous M C  carbides and formation of the new  23 6 discontinuous carbides along the grain boundaries  and formation of finer γ ′  particles in the matrix. •   The hardness of heat-treated alloy was only  increased by about 4% as compared to the ser- vice-exposed alloy.  

•   The stress-strain curves at a temperature of 25°C  indicated an increase of 9% in the ultimate ten- sile strength of the heat-treated alloy as com- pared with the service-exposed alloy. The room  temperature elongation of the heat-treated alloy  was more than two times of that service-exposed  alloy. •   Short term stress-rupture tests at a temperature of  850°C under a stress of 350 MPa revealed that the  heat treatment could improve the rupture life of  the alloy by more than four times at these testing  conditions.
