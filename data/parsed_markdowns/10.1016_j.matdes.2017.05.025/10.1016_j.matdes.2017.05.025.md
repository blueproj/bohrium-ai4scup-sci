# A B S T R A C T  

# A R T I C L E I N F O  

In powder metallurgy nickel base superalloy, the precipitation microstructure and mechanical properties are strongly dependent on the cooling rate. Thus, searching for the optimum cooling rate remains a critical research topic to maximize the mechanical performance. Previously, the  “ one sample, one experiment ”  approach is adopted for this optimization process, which is very costly and time-consuming. Here we demonstrate a high- throughput method to produce a gradient distribution of  γ ' precipitates within an individual specimen by introducing a wide range of cooling rates. The microscopic examinations show that the smallest average particle size of  γ ' phases corresponds to the fastest cooling rate, and vice versa. Unexpectedly, a two-stage distribution of Vickers hardness is observed, which is contradictive with the previous results and mechanistically explained by molecular dynamics (MD) simulation. Our results shed new insights on understanding the relationship between cooling rates and mechanical properties in polycrystalline nickel base superalloy.

# 1. Introduction  

Powder metallurgy nickel base superalloy is renowned for its superior capabilities with an excellent combination of high temperature strength, creep and fatigue resistance  [1 – 4] . These striking mechanical properties are inherently associated with the size distribution, mor- phology and volume fraction of  γ ' precipitates. Previous literatures have extensively documented that the solution cooling rate together with aging time act a critical role in tailoring these microscopic features of  γ ' precipitates  [5 – 10] . The nucleation, growth, coarsening, morphology, size distribution and volume fraction of  γ ' precipitates are strongly dependent on the solution cooling rate based on experiment results [5,7,11]  and phase  ﬁ eld calculation  [12] . Furthermore, the composi- tional variations of di ﬀ erent generation  γ '  [13]  and di ﬀ usion kinetics for di ﬀ erent alloy elements  [11]  can be signi ﬁ cantly in ﬂ uenced by the solution cooling rates. Both theoretic calculation  [14,15]  and experi- mental results  [16]  have shown that a smaller average particle size of  γ ' precipitates could increase yield strength comparing with the counter- parts with the same volume fraction, particularly emphasizing the role of nano-sized tertiary  γ '. The proportional relationship between cooling rate and Vickers hardness/yield strength is summarized in  Fig. 1  

In this research, a high-throughput methodology based on Jominy end quenching technique was designed to produce a gradient distribu- tion of  γ ' precipitates by means of introducing a wide range of cooling rates within an individual specimen. The microscopic examinations show that the size, morphology and volume fraction of  γ ' precipitates vary vastly along the specimen. The mechanical measurement indicates that the maximum Vickers hardness emerges in between the fastest and the slowest cooling rates, which contradicts with our previous wisdom. Moreover, six constant cooling rates ranging from 20 °C/min to 9000 °C/min, are employed to produce various distribution of  γ ' precipitates and the corresponding Vickers hardness results are in good agreement with those from the high-throughput tests. The MD simula- tion mechanistically unveils the roles of average particle size and inter- particle spacing of the tertiary  γ ' precipitates to tailor the mechanical strength. # 2. Materials and methods  

# 2.1. Materials  

The material in this research was a newly developed nickel base superalloy with a nominal composition shown in  Table 1 . The nickel base superalloy was prepared by vacuum induction melting technique and atomized by argon gas to obtain the nearly spherical powder. The powder with a size ranging from 50  μ m to 150  μ m was collected and transferred into a stainless steel container for hot isostatic pressing (HIP). The container was vacuum degassed at 400 °C for 24 h before sealing. Subsequently, HIP consolidation was performed at 1100 °C/ 140 MPa for 4 h. After HIP treatment, the stainless steel was removed and the superalloy billet was canned for hot extrusion (HEX). The billet was soaked under 1100 °C for 2 h and then extruded with an area reduction of 10:1.  

# 2.2. End quenching test and dilatometry test  

The Jominy end quenching sample was cut from the as-HEX billet and its sample size was 25 mm in diameter and 100 mm in length. The sample was coated by the ceramic  ﬁ ber wool and only the bottom side was exposed for subsequent water quenching. After that, it was super- solvus solution treated (the solvus temperature of this alloy is \~1154 °C based on the ThermoCalc calculation with TTNI8 database) at 1180 °C for 40 min and then immediately transferred for end quenching test. The cooling rate distribution throughout the specimen was simulated by specially developed code which utilized one-dimensional unsteady heat-conduction di ﬀ erential equation with the physical parameters based on laser  ﬂ ash apparatus (FLA, Netzsch LFA 457 MicroFlash, Germany) experiments.  

The dilatometry test was carried out on the DIL805A dilatometer (TA) at 1180 °C for 40 min and then cooled upon six di ﬀ erent cooling rates (20, 70, 120, 360, 6000 and 9000 °C/min). The specimens with a size of 4 mm in diameter and 10 mm in length were prepared for the relatively slow cooling rates (20, 70, 120 and 360 °C/min) and the hollow specimens with a thickness of 2 mm were prepared for 6000 and

# 2.3. Microstructures characterization and Vickers hardness test  

The microstructure observation was performed by  ﬁ eld emission scanning electron microscope (SEM, Quanta 650 FEG, FEI) at 20 kV using the secondary electron mode and transmission electron micro- scope (TEM, Titan G2 60 – 300, FEI) operating at 300 kV. Electron backscatter di ﬀ raction (EBSD) was used to measure the grain size using a step size of 0.5  μ m. After post-processing, the micrographics were used to measure volume fraction, average size and spacing of  γ ' precipitates through using ImageJ software  [23,24] . Vickers hardness test was carried out at the Buehler Micrometer 5100 hardness tester in accordance with ASTM standard E92 – 82, each value was obtained through averaging 10 measurements.  

# 2.4. MD simulation  

The simulations of an edge dislocation interacting with Ni 3 Al in Ni block were obtained by MD using LAMMPS. The embedded atom method potential of Ni 3 Al and Ni was used in MD simulations  [25] . According to the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution, the atom velocities in Ni-based alloys were set randomly. The temperature of the system was maintained at 10 K to suppress the atomic di ﬀ usion process on the motion of dislocation, and the time step was 1 fs. Prior to the shear loading, the energy minimization was carried out using the NVT ensemble for 100 ps at  ﬁ xed temperature for relaxing the system and alleviating the out-of-balance forces and net stresses. Here, the shear loading was applied by displacing atoms in the loading layer (see Fig. 4 a), and utilized for dislocation motion. At each step of strain deformation, the Ni-based alloys were relaxed to minimize the potential energy. We adopted the centrosymmetry parameter to analyze the local atomic structure, and the Ovito was adopted to visualize the evolution of the atomic structure  [26] .  

# 3. Results and discussion  

# 3.1. Cooling rate and Vickers hardness  

The temperature change along the end quenching bar during the cooling process from the soaking temperature are simulated using one- dimensional unsteady heat-conduction di ﬀ erential equation, wherein the thermal conductivity coe ﬃ cient, speci ﬁ c heat and the heat transfer coe ﬃ cient are determined by LFA technique. In this research, the cooling rate was de ﬁ ned as the average value as (1180 °C – 650 °C)/t (t is the time cooling from 1180 °C to 650 °C). The fastest and slowest average cooling rates are calculated to be 6346 °C/min and 35 °C/min, respectively ( Fig. 2 b). Vickers hardness measurements are applied to provide a rapid and e ﬀ ective way to assess the impact of the cooling rate on the mechanical strength  [27] .  Fig. 2 c shows that the Vickers hardness varies with the cooling rate. It can be found that the peak hardness value emerges at the point 3, corresponding to the position away from the bottom of around 24 mm. The thermal simulation results indicate that the average cooling rate of this point is 171 °C/min. It is very interesting to  ﬁ nd that the Vickers hardness decreases away from this position, which means that either increasing or decreasing cooling rate would result in a lower hardness value. To further verify this unexpected experimental  ﬁ ndings, a group of specimens with di ﬀ erent cooling rates (constant cooling rate) ranging from 20 °C/min to 9000 °C/min are employed to investigate the relationship between cooling rate and Vickers hardness. The hardness measurements ( Fig. 2 c) reveal that the fastest cooling rate of 9000 °C/min generates the minimum hardness with a value of 412, and the slowest cooling rate 20 °C/min corresponds to the value of 430. The highest hardness of 477 arises from the cooling rate of 360 °C/min. It can be observed that our results are slightly from previous results  [7,8,18] , which could be

The morphology of  γ ' precipitates is primarily controlled by the lattice mis ﬁ t between matrix and precipitate  [28] . In the case of high cooling rate, the di ﬀ usion of  γ ' forming elements are substantially suppressed, leading to a small lattice mis ﬁ t and consequently the nearly spheric  γ ' precipitates are observed. It is clear that, with the decreasing cooling rate ( Fig. 3 d, e and f), the secondary  γ ' precipitates tend to become cubical and butter ﬂ y-shaped owing to the high di ﬀ usivity of the  γ ' forming elements  [28] , corresponding to a lower lattice mis ﬁ t and an increased particle size. Meanwhile, the average particle size of tertiary  γ ' precipitates gradually increases but the morphology remains to be spherical. The manifestation of unimodal and bimodal  γ ' precipitates can be ascribed to the competition mechanism between undercooling and matrix supersaturation  [12,29,30] . In the case of bimodal PSD, the secondary  γ ' precipitates nucleate while the chemical concentration reaches the critical point. Owing to the relatively slow cooling rate, the accumulation speed of undercooling is slower and provides more time for the element di ﬀ usion. The tertiary  γ ' precipi- tates nucleate when the non-equilibrium condition is further reached as the temperature decreasing. In contrast, when the cooling rate is high enough, the undercooling available for precipitation nucleation invari- ably exists and the coarsening process is suppressed, leading to the formation of the unimodal PSD. For the precipitation strengthening, the lattice mis ﬁ t between precipitate and matrix generates a strain  ﬁ eld around precipitates which impedes dislocation movement  [31] . As mentioned above, nearly spheric precipitates have a lower lattice mis ﬁ t than cubic precipitates, corresponding to a lower strengthening e ﬀ ect of spheric precipitates. However, it should be noted that both elastic strain ﬁ eld and anti-phase boundary energy coordinately contribute to the strength of materials  [23] . Through careful measurements, the average particle size and inter-particle spacing of tertiary  γ ' precipitates ranging from point 1 to point 6 are shown in  Table 2 , where  d s  and  d t ,  L s  and  L t are diameter and inter-particle spacing for the secondary and tertiary  γ ', respectively. Herein, the EBSD measurement shows that the average grain size from point 1 to point 6 is approximately equivalent, and indicates that no substantial grain growth occurs during the solution cooling. A critical question is raised to be answered: what is the optimum particle size and spacing to maximize the mechanical strength?

# 4. Conclusion  

The classical Jominy end quenching method was utilized to produce a gradient distribution of  γ ' precipitates within an individual specimen via introducing a wide range of cooling rates in a polycrystalline nickel base superalloy. Through characterizing the processing-microstructure- property relationship, we can obtain:  

(1) Upon end quenching, the cooling rates range from 35 to 6346 °C/ min, and a two-stage distribution of hardness is found. This  ﬁ nding can be veri ﬁ ed by the constant cooling rates based on the dilatometry test.

 (2) Molecular simulation results reveal that the underlying physics of strength maxima is ascribed to the synergetic optimization of particle size and inter-particle spacing.
