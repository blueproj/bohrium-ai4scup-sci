# A R T I C L E I N F O  

A solid-solution strengthened Ni-based superalloy was welded with linear friction welding (LFW) under di ﬀ erent process parameters. Microstructural examination of the sound joints showed a re ﬁ ned microstructure in the weld zone (WZ) and the thermo-mechanically a ﬀ ected zone (TMAZ), with limited oxide presence at the edge of the weld for certain welding conditions. The  ﬂ ow line feature was present in the WZ and TMAZ due to carbide rearrangement. However, the mechanical properties of the joints, i.e. microhardness and tensile strength, were higher than those of the base metal. The weld microstructure underwent a discontinuous dynamic re- crystallization during LFW, which was accompanied by limited continuous dynamic recrystallization and static recrystallization, which lead to a remarkable increase in the fraction of low-angle grain boundaries (LAGBs) and a dramatic decrease of the  Σ 3 twin boundaries in WZ and TMAZ. In comparison to WZ, the TMAZ had a higher fraction of LAGBs because of the presence of stronger microtexture and less recrystallization.

# 1. Introduction  

Solid-state welding processes provide an excellent alternative to fusion welding as they do not su ﬀ er from defects related to melting and solidi ﬁ cation [ 1 , 2 ]. Linear friction welding (LFW) is a relatively new solid-state welding process aiming to extend the current applications for rotary friction welding to non-axisymmetric components such as blades and disk of aeroengines. LFW has been attracting increased at- tention due to its outstanding advantages of high reliability and re- peatability, low cost, and re ﬁ ned microstructure in the weld [ 3 ]. During LFW, a sound metallurgical joint is formed due to the frictional heat generated from the reciprocating movement of one component relative to another one under axial force. This process has four distinct phases, being the initial, transition, equilibrium, and forging phase [ 4 ]. LFW has been widely applied to join a range of materials, such as titanium alloys [ 5 , 6 ], superalloys [ 7 – 19 ], steels [ 20 , 21 ], aluminum alloys [ 22 – 24 ], and aluminum to copper [ 25 ] among others. The most suc- cessful application of LFW is welding of aeroengine blisks with titanium alloys [ 3 ]. In recent years, in order to manufacture high-temperature blisks for aeroengines, the LFW of Ni-based superalloys has been at- tracting increased attention [ 3 ].  

There have been a few studies on LFW of precipitation strengthened Ni-based superalloys. Chamanfar et al. [ 7 ] studied the LFW of Waspaloy  

possible occurrence of liquation during LFW of the newly developed AD730 TM   Ni-based superalloy. In addition, Ola et al. [ 15 ] studied the LFW of single crystal CMSX-486 superalloy where they reported that the compressive load during forging had induced rapid solidi ﬁ cation of the metastable liquid phase, which formed an Hf-based oxide phase along the weld. Wanjara et al. [ 16 ] found that with increasing forging pressure, the remaining recrystallized grains in the weld were mini- mized while the WZ and TMAZ had become smaller. Ma et al. [ 17 ] reported an increase in tensile strength and microhardness of a LFWed single crystal Ni-based superalloy (DD6) due to grain re ﬁ nement and the presence of  ﬁ ne  γ ' phase in the WZ. Karadge et al. [ 18 ] reported the important role of the orientation of the single crystal with respect to the normal pressure during LFW of a single crystal to polycrystalline Ni- based superalloy, as it is dependent on the orientation of the primary slip system in the fcc-based single crystal lattice. It can be concluded from the above that the strengthening phase can easily dissolve with high temperatures reached during LFW, which is associated with a re- duction in mechanical properties. This requires the improvement of the morphology and distribution of the strengthening phase with PWHT [ 19 ].  

In addition to the materials studied above, there is another type of superalloy used in aeroengine blisks, the solid-solution strengthened Ni- based superalloys, with excellent oxidation resistance, corrosion re- sistance and moderate heat resistance under 900 °C [ 26 ]. However, few reports have been on the LFW of solid-solution strengthened super- alloys. Therefore, the main objective of this work is to study the e ﬀ ect of welding parameters on microstructural evolution and mechanical properties of LFWed solid-solution strengthened superalloy joints.

# 3. Results and discussion  

# 3.1. Macroscopic view of joints  

Typical LFWed GH3044 joints for various welding parameters are shown in  Fig. 4 . It can be clearly seen that the joint  ﬂ ash is quite dif- ferent from that of titanium alloys [ 5 ], but similar to those of pre- cipitation strengthened Ni-based superalloy joints [ 10 – 12 ]. The  ﬂ ash does not join together as it  ﬂ ows out of the interface and there are not ridges on the  ﬂ ash surface. According to Chamanfar et al. [ 19 , 29 ], the interface material has a lower viscoplasticity and retains its strength at high temperatures, with the superalloy containing many alloy elements which can oxidize readily at high temperatures. As a consequence, the ﬂ ash from both parts cannot join easily as they are expose to the at- mosphere when they move out of the interface. Unlike welding titanium alloys [ 3 – 6 ], forming and extruding yielded material from the weld interface of GH3044 joints shows no periodic behavior, and no ridges form on the  ﬂ ash surface.  

It can be seen from  Table 4  that axial shortening increases with the amplitude of oscillation, and higher friction pressure also increases axial shortening. This is because of two reasons: the  ﬁ rst is that welding frictional heat input increases with friction pressure, amplitude, and frequency of oscillation [ 30 ], producing more plastic metal on the weld interface. And second, is that the higher friction pressure and amplitude of oscillation extrude yielded material into the  ﬂ ash. The bigger ex- truded interfacial material increases axial shortening of the joint.  

Figs. 5 a – d shows the cross-sectional microstructure of the joints with various welding parameters. All weld interfaces contain black lines (as marked by the black arrows) to a di ﬀ erent extent, also observed in LFWed IN718 joints by Mary and Jahazi [ 9 ]. A limited number of black lines can be observed at the edge of WZ of joint No. 2. The EDS analysis of these identi ﬁ es a high content of oxygen, as oxides were not extruded as shown in  Fig. 5 e. As it can be seen in  Fig. 5 f, the joint is divided into three zones: WZ, TMAZ, and BM. The WZ has clear  ﬂ ow lines parallel to the direction of oscillation. However, in the TMAZ these  ﬂ ow lines change from a vertical to a parallel direction compared to the direction of oscillation. The width of WZ and TMAZ is about 0.5 mm and 2 mm, respectively.

This phenomenon is quite di ﬀ erent from that occurring in the TMAZ of LFWed titanium alloy joints, in which there is limited or no recrystallization at all [ 34 , 35 ]. As GH3044 has a low stacking fault energy (SFE), dynamic recrystallization (DRX) during plastic deformation occurs readily [ 36 ]. So, a large number of  ﬁ ne grains form not only in the WZ but also in the TMAZ. The degree of grain re ﬁ nement is di ﬀ erent for each of the four welding parameters, which a ﬀ ect grain size of the WZ in the same manner.  

From  Fig. 6 , it can be seen that the carbide particles in the BM disappear and the white phase precipitates at grain boundaries in the WZ. The white phase is identi ﬁ ed as carbides by EDS ( Fig. 6 e). It has been reported that the M 23 C 6  carbides will dissolve fully at tempera- tures over 1010 °C to 1040 °C [ 29 ]. Unlike the massive  ﬂ ash of titanium alloy joints which removes a large amount of heat from the weld in- terface during LFW [ 3 – 6 ], the heat generated during LFW of superalloys accumulates in the vicinity of the weld interface due to the relatively small amount of  ﬂ ash. In addition, the welding parameters used in this study produce more heat input than those commonly used for the precipitation strengthened Ni-based superalloy IN718 [ 10 ], leading to a higher temperature at the interface. As a result, the carbide particles initially present in the WZ have adequate time to dissolve fully under high temperature and they then reprecipitate from the matrix during the cooling stage which follows. As cooling is so quick compared to the solution heat treatment or aging process for wrought superalloys, there is not enough time for carbides to grow and remain closely packed at grain boundaries. However, the volume fractions of carbides pre- cipitated in the WZ of joints No. 3, No. 1, No. 2 and No. 4 are about 3.1%, 5.3%, 7.9%, and 7.7%, respectively. This is because the heat inputs to the joints vary with welding parameters, leading to di ﬀ erent cooling conditions. In addition, it can also be seen in  Fig. 6 d that the black massive TiN remains in the WZ una ﬀ ected, because the melting point of TiN is far higher than the temperature reached at the WZ.  

The carbides in the TMAZ as observed in  Fig. 7 , dissolve to a limited extent and then precipitate at grain boundaries while most of them retain their morphology as white particulate phase in the BM, due to the existing temperature gradient from WZ to BM.  

Although the grain size in the WZ is uniform and equiaxed, the macrostructure of WZ does show clear  ﬂ ow lines. It can be assumed that carbides are not distributed evenly in the whole WZ, which is a ﬀ ected by the large deformation of the thermoplastic metal in the WZ during LFW, but they are governed by their original position in the BM. Similarly to WZ, a large number of  ﬂ ow lines form in the TMAZ.

Compared to the BM, the proportions of LAGBs in WZ and TMAZ increase, and the fraction of LAGBs in the TMAZ is higher than that of in WZ. This is because of the di ﬀ erence in deformation, strain rate and temperature between the WZ to BM during LFW. The temperature and strain rate that the material undergoes in the WZ are much higher than those of the TMAZ, which produces su ﬃ cient recrystallization in the WZ. As a result, the fraction of LAGBs in the WZ tends to decrease. In addition, it can be seen from  Fig. 8 b that there exist a large number of large sized green grains with an < 110 > orientation, inside of which many LAGBs can be found. This suggests that this microtexture forms during deformation in the TMAZ. According to Mironov et al. [ 37 ], if the texture is su ﬃ ciently strong, the orientation of some neighboring grains may converge and decrease the misorientation across the boundaries separating these grains into the LAGBs, which can cause a decrease in HAGBs.  

In  Fig. 8 b and c it can be seen there are wavy grain boundaries in the TMAZ and WZ (marked by white arrows). This is a typical feature of local grain boundary migration, which gives rise to bulges along the original grain boundaries (marked by white circles), which then transform into  ﬁ ne equiaxed grains. These  ﬁ ne grains are typically surrounded by HAGBs, and contain no subgrains at most times. All these phenomena indicate that discontinuous dynamic recrystallization (DDRX) occurs during LFW of GH3044, which is consistent with the DDRX during deformation of a low SFE metal [ 38 ]. In addition, in Fig. 8 b and c there is a small number of HAGB segments (marked by black arrows) inside individual grains. This is due to the misorienta- tions which increase when subgrains absorb dislocations by rotation, whereas eventually these subgrains turn into HAGBs. This phenomenon indicates that continuous dynamic recrystallization (CDRX) also occurs during LFW of GH3044.  

Compared to BM, the fraction of  Σ 3 twin boundaries reduces dra- matically in the WZ and the TMAZ. This is because twin-grains lose their ideal orientation relationship relative to the matrix when they rotate because of deformation during LFW, which transforms bound- aries into ordinary HAGBs. In  Fig. 8 e and f, however, there are twin boundaries inside some small grains (marked by black arrows) while no LAGBs can be found in these grains. The absence of LAGBs in these grains indicates that the material has been statically recrystallized

# 3.4. Mechanical properties of joints  

# 3.4.1. Microhardness  

Fig. 9  presents the microhardness distribution of the four joints with di ﬀ erent welding parameters. Their distributions are quite similar: as the distance from the weld interface increases, hardness decreases. The main di ﬀ erence between them is that hardness at the same point in the joint increases from joint No. 1 to No. 4.  

As a solid-solution strengthened Ni-based superalloy, GH3044 ’ s hardness depends on  ﬁ ne grain strengthening and second phase particle strengthening [ 40 , 41 ]. As the dissolved carbides in the joints re- precipitate, their e ﬀ ect to hardness can be ignored. The grain sizes of WZ and TMAZ are both re ﬁ ned, with those of the WZ being the smal- lest, making the hardness of the WZ the highest. As mentioned in Section  3.2 , the grain size of joints decreases with amplitude of oscil- lation and friction pressure, leading to successive increases in micro- hardness in the joints No. 1, No. 3, No. 2 and No. 4.  

# 3.4.2. Tensile properties test  

From  Fig. 10 , it can be seen that two tensile test specimens (1 – 1 and 1 – 2) of joint No. 1 and all tensile test specimens of joints No. 2, No. 3 and No. 4 fail at the BM, far away from the WZ, which indicates that the middle part of a joint is sound. However, two tensile test specimens (1 – 3 and 1 – 4) of joint No. 1 which were cut from the edge of the weld, fail at the WZ, as they contain many oxide inclusions, forming a weak bond. The tensile test results are shown in  Table 5 . It can be seen that the values of ulitmate tensile strength (UTS), average yield strength (YS) and average elongation of the joints (except specimens 1 – 3 and 1 – 4) are comparable to those of BM.  

The mechanical properties of LFWed GH3044 joints are di ﬀ erent from those of Waspaloy or IN718, whose mechanical properties are lower than their BMs [ 7 , 9 ]. For the precipitation strengthened Ni-based superalloys, the main strengthening phase  γ ′  in the WZ will dissolve during LFW, while it will not reprecipitate during the subsequent rapid cooling, which is found in LFWed Waspaloy or IN718. Although re ﬁ ned grains a ﬀ ect the strength of the joints, their positive e ﬀ ect is not suf- ﬁ cient to compensate for the loss of strengthening e ﬀ ect of  γ ′ . In ad- dition, liquation possibly occurs during LFW of precipitation strength- ened Ni-based superalloys, which is a potential problem to joints [ 12 – 14 ].  

The question remains whether  γ ′  will reprecipitate or not during cooling which depends on its volume fraction. For superalloys with low volume fraction of  γ ′ , such as Waspaloy and IN718, although the whole of the  γ ′  dissolves, the low levels of Al and Ti cannot provide a su ﬃ cient driving force for  γ ′  reprecipitation, developing a  γ ′ -free region in the joint, and therefore a suitable PWHT is required to recover the  γ ′ . In superalloys with high volume fraction of  γ ′ , such as Astroloy, Udimet TM 720LI, and RR1000, although  γ ′  dissolves completely during friction welding, very  ﬁ ne  γ ′  particles tend to reprecipitate due to the high le- vels of Al and Ti in these alloys which drive the reprecipitation of  γ ′ , and therefore produce no  γ ′ -free region in the welded joint. However, the size, morphology, and composition of reprecipitated  γ ′  are di ﬀ erent from those of the BM. Hence it still remains necessary to conduct a proper PWHT to generate the desired  γ ′  particles in the joint [ 29 ].  

In the case of LFW of solid-solution strengthened Ni-based super- alloy, with the e ﬀ ect of  ﬁ ne grain strengthening, the WZ and TMAZ see a remarkable increase in tensile strength. Although the distribution of carbides in the WZ and TMAZ does change, the dissolved carbides in the joints reprecipitate, reducing the impact on the tensile strength of the joints. However, it should be noted that PWHT is still necessary to modify the morphology of M 23 C 6  carbides from continuous  ﬁ lms to discrete particles, thereby, improving the fatigue and creep properties of the joints [ 29 ]. # 4. Conclusions  

Based on the characterization of the microstructure and mechanical properties of LFWed GH3044 joints under di ﬀ erent process parameters, the following conclusions can be drawn:  

(1) For a solid-solution strengthened Ni-based superalloy, as GH3044, sound joints can be obtained with LFW, i.e. of comparable me- chanical properties to the BM. The black lines appearing at the joint edge contain oxides which have not been extruded, and the extent of oxide inclusion depends on the selection of welding parameters. In addition, the  ﬂ ow line feature in both the WZ and TMAZ is due to the rearrangement of carbides.

 (2) The weld undergoes mainly DDRX during LFW, accompanied by limited CDRX and SRX. Compared to the BM, the fractions of LAGBs in the WZ and TMAZ increase remarkably while those of  Σ 3 twin boundaries decrease drastically. In comparison to the WZ, the TMAZ has a higher fraction of LAGBs because of the presence of stronger microtexture and less recrystallization.

 (3) The grain sizes of the welds are a ﬀ ected markedly by welding parameters. The sizes of re ﬁ ned grains are smaller under a larger friction pressure. Grain sizes in the welds decrease with increasing amplitude of oscillation. The increase of friction pressure and am- plitude of oscillation increases heat input and strain rate, leading to further grain re ﬁ nement.

 (4) With the e ﬀ ects of  ﬁ ne grain strengthening and second phase par- ticle strengthening, there is a gradual decrease in microhardness from the WZ to the BM, and the average tensile strength of the joints is comparable to that of the BM. Although the distributions of carbides in the WZ and TMAZ change, the dissolved carbides in the joints reprecipitate, and therefore do not a ﬀ ectthe tensile strength of the joints.
