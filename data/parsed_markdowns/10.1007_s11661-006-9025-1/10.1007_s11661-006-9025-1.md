# Liquation Microﬁssuring in the Weld Heat-Affected Zone of an Overaged Precipitation-Hardened Nickel-Base Superalloy  

O.A. OJO and M.C. CHATURVEDI  

The eﬀect of preweld overaging heat treatment on the microstructural response in the heat- aﬀected zone (HAZ) of a precipitation-hardened nickel-base superalloy INCONEL 738LC subjected to the welding thermal cycle ( i.e. , rapid) was investigated. The overaging heat treat- ment resulted in the formation of an interfacial micro constituent containing M 23 X 6  particles and coarsening of primary and secondary  c ¢  precipitates. The HAZ microstructures around welds in the overaged alloy were simulated using the Gleeble thermo mechanical simulation system. Microstructural examination of simulated HAZs and those present in tungsten inert gas (TIG) welded specimens showed the occurrence of extensive grain boundary liquation involving liquation reaction of the interfacial micro constituents containing M 23 X 6  particles and MC-type carbides. In addition, the coarsened  c ¢  precipitate particles present in the overaged alloy per- sisted well above their solvus temperature to temperatures where they constitutionally liquated and contributed to considerable liquation of grain boundaries, during continuous rapid heating. Intergranular HAZ microﬁssuring, with resolidiﬁed product formed mostly on one side of the microﬁssures, was observed in welded specimens. This suggested that the HAZ microﬁssuring generally occurred by decohesion across one of the solid-liquid interfaces during the grain boundary liquation stage of the weld thermal cycle. Correlation of simulated HAZ micro- structures with hot ductility properties of the alloy revealed that the temperature at which the alloy exhibited zero ductility during heating was within the temperature range at which grain boundary liquation was observed. The on-cooling ductility of the alloy was signiﬁcantly dam- aged by the on-heating liquation reaction, as reﬂected by the considerably low ductility recovery temperature (DRT). Important characteristics of the intergranular liquid that could inﬂuence HAZ microﬁssuring of the alloy in overaged condition are also discussed.

# I. INTRODUCTION  

G AS  turbines are generally designed within the limits of current technology for the best possible eﬃciencies and output, and as such, complex shaped parts made of nickel-base superalloys are often required to withstand the stringent demands encountered in aero engines and power generation turbines. INCONEL\* 738 superalloy  

\*INCONEL is a trademark of INCO Alloys International, Huntington WV.  

is one of the most widely used hot-corrosion-resistant superalloys in both land-based and aero gas turbine engines. In addition to its remarkable hot corrosion/ oxidation resistance, this superalloy also exhibits excellent high-temperature strength primarily due to precipitation hardening by the ordered intermetallic Ni 3 (Al,Ti)- c ¢  phase. The weldability of heat-resistant nickel-base superalloys has gained importance because of the wide use of welding to fabricate and repair service-damaged hot section components of gas tur- bines. Unfortunately, application of fusion welding to fabrication and repair of precipitation-hardened nickel- base superalloys such as IN 738 has been severely restricted. This is because these alloys, especially those containing a substantial amount of Al and Ti (> 6 wt pct), are highly susceptible to microﬁssuring predomi- nantly in the heat-aﬀected zone (HAZ) during welding   and postweld heat treatment (PWHT). The HAZ microﬁssuring susceptibility is known to depend upon the composition and microstructure of a material, both of which have already been optimized for acceptable mechanical properties. Similar to many other precipita- tion-hardened nickel-base superalloys, cracking during welding of the IN 738 alloy has been attributed mostly to large shrinkage stress occurring as a result of rapid precipitation of the main strengthening phase of the alloy,  c ¢  precipitate particles, during cooling   from welding temperatures. It has been suggested that in order to minimize HAZ cracking in this class of superalloys, these materials should be welded in   overaged condition. Even though this preweld heat  

treatment condition has resulted in a considerable reduction in HAZ cracking in some superalloy weld-   ments including IN 738, it has not been able to eliminate its occurrence. The suggestion of overaging heat treatment was based primarily on the premise of minimizing the eﬀects of rapid  c ¢  precipitation reaction on cracking. However, very limited information is available about other important eﬀects of overaging heat treatment on grain boundary liquation, which is known to inﬂuence HAZ microﬁssuring by signiﬁcantly reducing hot ductility during welding. Also, Duval   et al. observed that ductility, which is required for suﬃcient welding stress relaxation, is the most inﬂuen- tial HAZ property governing the cracking tendencies of superalloy weldments during PWHT. They indicated that cracking occurred due to the exhaustion of the available ductility, which is maintained throughout the PWHT aging at a moderately low level by a combina- tion of microstructural interactions produced during both welding and the subsequent heat treatment.  

In the present work, microstructural examination of tungsten inert gas (TIG) weld HAZs, and of those simulated by the Gleeble thermo mechanical simulation system in IN 738 superalloy, was performed and correlated with its hot ductility properties. The aim of the study was to understand the eﬀect(s) of preweld overaging heat treatment on metallurgical reactions that contribute to HAZ grain boundary liquation and on susceptibility concomitant loss of hot ductility and thus cracking susceptibility during welding of IN 738LC superalloy. The results of this investigation are pre- sented and discussed in relation to HAZ microﬁssuring in this article.

# B. Microstructure of Welded Specimens  

Microcracks were observed in HAZs around welds made after the overaging heat treatment, an example of which is shown in Figure 8. The microstructural exam- ination of HAZs showed that microcracks originated mainly in the HAZ region slightly removed from the fusion boundary, and in some cases, they extended as much as 300  l m into the base material and occasionally partly into the fusion zone. The cracks were intergran- ular and displayed a relatively irregular and zigzag morphology that is typical of liquation cracks. A closer and careful examination of cracked boundaries at higher magniﬁcations by SEM revealed the presence of resolidiﬁcation constituents along the cracks (Figure 9). The resolidiﬁcation constituents, which formed mostly on one side of the microﬁssures, suggest the origin of microﬁssuring in the HAZ of this alloy to be liquation cracking. This suggests that in the liquid ﬁlm stage, while the liquated phase is still liquid, the HAZ microﬁssuring generally occurs by decohesion across one of the solid-liquid interfaces when on- cooling welding tensile stresses exceed the local strength at such an interface. Nevertheless, a number of highly liquated but uncracked grain boundaries were also observed in the HAZs (Figure 10). Intergranular liquid ﬁlm, from which complex eutectic-like constituent formed along such liquated grain boundaries, appeared to have been of considerable thickness as the eutectic width was found to be over 2.5  l m. Evidence of extensive grain boundary penetration by liquid ﬁlm, even down to the lower subsolidus temperature HAZ regions, where primary  c ¢  particles had essentially undergone little solid-state dissolution, was observed in the form of resolidiﬁed intergranular eutectic con- stituents (Figure 10).

# IV. DISCUSSION  

A.  Microstructural Response of Overaged Alloy to Rapid Weld Thermal Cycle  

1.  Liquation of micro constituent containing M 23 X 6 particles  

As mentioned earlier, intergranular liquation in Gleeble-simulated specimens was observed ﬁrst at 1150  C in a continuous fashion at the interface of  c - c ¢  eutectic and along the adjoining grain boundaries. The grain boundary liquation at this temperature could be related to the eﬀect of interfacial segregation of boron during the presimulation aging heat treatment. M 23 C 6  carbides are known to form in nickel-base superalloys during the aging heat treatment (750   C to 1055   C) that normally follows   the solution heat treatment. The true nature of the Cr-rich particles, however, requires some discussion. It has been proposed that the addition of boron to nickel- base superalloys can inﬂuence the structure and chemistry   of grain boundary precipitates. The solid solubility of boron in austenitic  c  alloys is very low, and Goldsch-   midt reports, for example, a maximum solubility of 97 ppm of B at 1125   C in 18 pct Cr-15 pct Ni stainless steel. The solubility decreases rapidly with decreasing temperature, becoming less than 30 ppm at 900   C. Furthermore, the size of the boron atom is larger than the common interstitial elements in nickel ( e.g. , C) and smaller than the substitutional elements (Co and Cr). This size misﬁt of boron atoms for both the substitutional and interstitial sites of the austenitic lattice implies that it would be energetically favorable for boron atoms to segregate to loosely packed regions such as interfaces. Considering the low solubility of boron in nickel, partic- ularly at temperatures at which the aging treatment of nickel-base superalloys is commonly performed (750   C to 1050   C) and the strong tendency of boron to segre- gate, it can be expected that boron aﬀects the interfacial precipitation behavior. The segregation and precipitation of boron containing particles during heat treatments have been studied by several investigators in nickel- and iron-   base austenitic alloys. It was found that segregation- induced precipitation of boron containing particles occurred during isothermal holding at intermediate temperatures (750   C to 1050   C), subsequent to anneal- ing at a higher solution treatment temperature. Chen  et   al. observed pronounced nonequilibrium segregation of boron to grain boundaries during air cooling from a pre homogenization heat treatment temperature at 1200   C for 2 hours in IN 718 superalloy containing 43 ppm of B. A subsequent holding of 5 to 30 minutes at 1050   C resulted in a precipitation process along inter- granular regions. In their analysis, secondary ion mass spectroscopy (SIMS) revealed a signiﬁcant association of boron atoms with the particles precipitated at the grain   boundaries. Kruger  et al. observed, by electron energy loss spectroscopy analysis, that boron-rich M 23 X 6  pre- cipitated in Ni-16Cr-9Fe-0.03C containing 50 ppm of boron during heat treatment at 1100   C for 20 minutes. Hu  et al. [23]  used neutron-activated micro radio graph y to study boron distribution in a cast nickel-base superalloy. They reported that segregation of boron to grain bound- aries occurred during an aging heat treatment at 760   C for 16 hours followed by air cooling. High concentration of boron was detected in association with M 23 C 6 -type carbides that precipitated during the aging heat treat- ment. By performing microchemical and X-ray diﬀrac- tion analysis on electrolytically extracted carbide residue, Hu  et al.  showed that the secondary carbide particles that precipitated along the grain boundaries were (CrCoMo) 23 (CB) 6 , containing 0.0011 to 0.0013 wt pct of boron, with a lattice parameter of 10.7 A ˚ . The increase in lattice parameter compared to pure M 23 C 6  was suggested to be due to more carbon atoms being replaced by larger boron atoms in the M 23 (CB) 6  particles.  

It is known that boron can substitute for carbon in     M 23 C 6  crystal lattice. Delargy  et al. reported using ﬁeld ion microscopy analysis; a signiﬁcant concentration of boron was detected in the M 23 C 6  that precipitated during aging heat treatment of cast IN 939, which is similar in composition to IN 738 alloy. Therefore, based on the aforementioned and considering the relatively high boron concentration of 120 ppm in IN 738 superalloy used in the present study, it is not unreason- able to expect interfacial boron segregation to cause the surrounding of the M 23 X 6  type particle, which is often referred to as M 23 C 6  in IN 738, to be enriched with B atoms or the particle itself to contain a signiﬁcant amount of boron. The small size of these particles and the inherent limitations of conventional electron optical techniques made it diﬃcult to carry out a satisfactory quantitative SEM/EDS compositional analysis on the particle observed in the present work. However, the boron peak in some of these particles was detected, as shown in Figure 16. Furthermore, an indirect indication of the presence of B in Cr-Mo rich particles is also provided by the observation of ﬁne  c ¢  particles in the region surrounding some of the network containing Cr- Mo rich particles (Figure 7(b)), as it has been suggested that boron stabilizes ﬁne  c ¢  precipitates in nickel-base superalloy.

can be reversible, suggesting that pure M 23 C 6  may dissolve by solid-state reaction upon heating to a suﬃciently high temperature. Similarly, Owczarski   et al. in their study of weldability of nickel-base superalloys indicated that M 23 C 6  particles would under- go solid-state reaction well below the solidus tempera- ture and would not liquate during welding. In the present work, after rapidly heating a specimen to 1150   C and holding there for 0.5 seconds followed by water quench- ing, the grain boundary network of M 23 X 6 , which was observed in overaged specimens and in specimens simulated at 1135   C for 0.5 seconds, was no longer observed. Instead, resolidiﬁed product was observed along the grain boundaries and boron-rich particles were detected as a part of the resolidiﬁed constituent. Apart from being a melting point depressant in nickel, boron is known to enhance liquid wetting of the grain boundary by lowering the solid-liquid interfacial energy. In this context, the extensive interfacial liquation in specimens simulated at 1150   C appears to be related to boron segregation to interfaces in this alloy during the overaging heat treatment. Interface segregation of boron during the heat treatment of IN 718 has been reported to produce low-temperature grain boundary liquation in the HAZs during welding.  

# ¢   2.  Constitutional liquation of  c particles, MC carbides, and M SC sulfocarbide  

The main mechanism through which  c ¢  precipitates contribute to poor weldability of precipitation-hardened nickel-base superalloys has generally been reported to be related to their rapid reprecipitation and aging kinetics during postweld cooling and PWHT. This was the basis for the suggestion that an overaging preweld heat treatment would minimize the weld HAZ cracking. It has been also suggested that such a heat treatment could lower the susceptibility to liquation due to a possible   homogenization eﬀect. However, these suggestions do not seem to consider the possibility that overaging the  c ¢ precipitates could promote their tendency to constitu- tionally liquate in these alloys. Constitutional liquation of second-phase particles during rapid heating of two- phase alloys was ﬁrst proposed by Pepe and Savage and has been subsequently observed by other investiga- tors in various alloy systems involving carbide, boride,   and sulﬁde particles. Constitutional liquation of the main strengthening phase of precipitation-hardened nickel-base superalloys,  c ¢  precipitates, however, was only reported recently for the ﬁrst time by the present   authors. The liquation phenomenon is known to occur generally by a eutectic-type liquation reaction between a second-phase particle and the matrix produc- ing a nonequilibrium solute-rich ﬁlm at such a particle/ matrix interface. The basic requirement for the occur- rence of constitutional liquation of a second-phase intermetallic particle in an alloy is the existence of such a particle at a temperature equal to or above the eutectic reaction temperature during heating. Therefore, the susceptibility of such an intermetallic particle to consti- tutional liquation is dependent on its solid-state disso- lution behavior during rapid heating.

Therefore, an increase in  c ¢  precipitate size due to preweld overaging would promote persistence of these particles to temperatures well above their equilibrium solvus temperature to temperatures at which they could constitutionally liquate by a eutectic-type reaction with the surrounding  c  matrix.  

In the present alloy,  c - c ¢ eutectic reaction has been reported to occur over a range of temperatures, which could be below 1180   C. Rapid heating at a rate of 111   C/s by the Gleeble simulation system showed that the coarsened  c ¢  particles in the overaged IN 738 alloy persisted well above their solvus temperature of 1160   C to over 1245   C, which resulted in their constitutional liquation, as observed in specimens simulated above 1165   C. Careful microstructural examinations of welded specimens likewise showed not only liquation of intergranular  c ¢  particles but also that of the intragranular  c ¢  precipitates. The extent of  c ¢  liquation increased with decreasing distance from the fusion line, that is, with increasing peak temperatures (Figure 8). Besides  c ¢  particles, MC carbides and M 2 SC, which are secondary solidiﬁcation reaction products in the alloy, were also observed to have liquated above the simula- tion temperature of 1225   C and contributed to inter- granular liquation.  

# B.  Effect of Liquation of Phases on Grain Boundary Embrittlement  

The metallurgical basis for HAZ microﬁssuring in fully austenitic iron and nickel-base alloys has been recognized to involve the presence and persistence of low melting liquid phase on HAZ grain boundaries during the welding cycle. Inability of the intergranular liquid to accommodate thermal and shrinkage stresses and mechanical constraint during cooling often led to microﬁssuring. It has been recognized in many studies on liquation cracking that the mere occurrence of liquation is not suﬃcient to produce a crack susceptible microstructure. The emphasis has been that it is necessary that the liquid species wet, inﬁltrate, and spread along grain boundary regions in a continuous/ semicontinuous fashion reducing the solid-solid energy along the grain boundary. If wetting is ineﬀective, the liquid will exist in isolated pockets allowing substantial intergranular solid-solid contact.

Absorption of segregated B atoms or those released by the decomposition of M 23 (CB) 6  and M 3 B 2 particles by the intergranular liquid could substantially aid the wetting behavior of the liquid through the inﬂuence of the surface-active boron atoms in lowering the solid-liquid interface energy along the grain bound- ary. Furthermore, the metastable liquid produced by the constitutional liquation of intergranular second-phase particles always reacts with the adjacent solid grain through back diﬀusion of solute atoms across the solid- liquid interface. Therefore, in these situations, the nonequilibrium solid-liquid interfacial energy is extre-   mely low, which would make the liquid eﬀectively wet the grain boundary and exhibit extensive penetration. A   recent model developed for describing the penetration of the liquid phase along a grain boundary has also shown that the grain boundary penetration requires an undersaturated solid. This is essentially the situation prevalent in the subsolidus HAZ regions during non- equilibrium rapid heating and cooling of the welding operation.  

An alternate or complementary case, which is appar- ent from Eq. [5], is that for a given solid-liquid interfacial energy, the higher the grain boundary energy, the higher is the tendency for it to be wetted and penetrated by the liquid phase. It has been found that grain boundaries in the cast IN 738 alloy are more of a ‘‘random’’ nature with a higher order of ‘‘ S ‘‘ values than   special boundaries. Random high-angle grain bound- aries are inherently of higher energy than the special boundaries, including twin boundaries, and as such the character of a grain boundary could be a factor in aiding extensive grain boundary wetting and penetration by liquid ﬁlm in IN 738, as observed in this work. Guo     et al. and Kokawa  et al. have conﬁrmed that liquid penetration at the grain boundary was greatest at high- angle boundaries and was relatively insigniﬁcant at low- angle boundaries. Moreover, because of the classical   study of Bailey and Watkins, it has been recognized that a relationship exists between mutual solubility or the formation of intermetallic compounds and solid- liquid interfacial energy and, thus, with wetting prop- erties. The greater the solubility of metals in the solid state, the lower will be the interfacial energy when these metals are brought into contact with one another in liquid the state. Hence, it is probable that a solid metal will be wetted by a liquid metal if the two are at least fairly soluble in each other. In the IN 738 alloy, it is known that  c ¢  has a high solid solubility in  c  while MC carbide also exhibits some solubility in the  c  matrix. Therefore, in IN 738, the interface energy between  c grains and the liquid phase produced by constitutional liquation of  c ¢  and MC carbide can be expected to be relatively low, thereby exhibiting good wetting behavior and penetration of the grain boundary regions. This was observed in the microstructures of weld HAZs and in Gleeble simulated samples, as shown earlier. Intergran- ular liquid ﬁlms generally serve to embrittle HAZ grain boundaries and the presence of mechanical and/or thermally induced restraint imposed during weld cooling could lead to HAZ cracking.  

# 4.  Hot ductility properties  

As stated earlier, Gleeble hot ductility tests were conducted to access the eﬀect of grain boundary liquation on resistance of the alloy to HAZ cracking. It is interesting to note that from the result of the tests, the temperatures at which the alloy exhibited zero ductility during hot ductility tests fall within the temperature range within which an appreciable amount of grain boundary liquation was observed in the Gleeble-simulated specimens. It has been reported by   other investigators that the occurrence of grain boundary liquation resulted in austenitic alloys exhib- iting practically zero ductility during hot ductility tests. The ZDTR, within which the material exhibits practi- cally zero ductility during heating, has been found to correlate with the width of HAZ regions in which grain boundary liquation may occur (crack susceptible region)   during the welding cycle. The ZDTR is a material- speciﬁc constant and often allows the HAZ regions that are susceptible to liquation cracking to be quantiﬁed, thus facilitating a comparison of cracking susceptibility of diﬀerent alloy materials. The ZDTR of the present alloy was estimated to be around 172   C ( T L  – NDT).  

In addition, it has been found that when the peak temperature exceeds the NDT, the values of on-cooling ductility are less than those of the on-heating values at the same temperature. This is due to the grain boundary liquation, which tends to delay the ductility recovery to temperatures much lower than NDT. The larger this BTR, the higher is the propensity to HAZ cracking. It is generally accepted that HAZ intergranular microﬁssur- ing occurs within this discrete temperature envelope during cooling. Therefore, any factor that causes grain boundary liquation at a temperature much lower than an alloy’s equilibrium solidus temperature during heat- ing is not only potentially detrimental in terms of increasing the HAZ crack susceptible region of the alloy ( i.e. , ZDTR) but would also lead to increased BTR during cooling. The subsolidus grain boundary liquation involving liquation of intergranular micro constituent containing M 23 X 6  particles, constitutional liquation  c ¢ particles, and MC carbides in the present alloy resulted in a considerably wide BTR of 245   C for a peak temperature of 1245   C. Hence, the relatively wide ZDTR of the present alloy (172   C) compared to other austenitic alloy systems known to be resistant to HAZ cracking such as type 310 stainless steel (61   C ) and solid-solution-strengthened nickel-based superalloy IN 625 (102   C ), coupled with a considerable loss of on- cooling ductility, constitute an important factor that could impair the resistance of the alloy to HAZ cracking. Nevertheless, it should be noted that the occurrence of extensive and continuous grain boundary liquation, with the attendant wide BTR, does not necessarily imply that a corresponding HAZ cracking would occur. The thickness/amount of the intergranular liquid is another important factor that controls hot cracking in welds and could limit the extent of HAZ cracking, as discussed in Section C.  

# C.  Inﬂuence of Intergranular Liquid Film Thickness on the Occurrence of HAZ Microﬁssuring  

The amount/thickness of intergranular liquid is another important factor that controls the occurrence of hot cracking during welding. The thickness of grain boundary liquid ﬁlm can aﬀect an alloy’s resistance to liquation cracking through its inﬂuence on (1) the resolidiﬁcation temperature range, (2) the level of stress required to cause microﬁssuring at a given temperature during weld cooling, and (3) stress relaxation by grain boundary liquid penetration and liquid healing of incipient cracks. In a relatively large volume of inter- granular liquid, solute segregation may occur during resolidiﬁcation, which usually tends to lower the termi- nal resolidiﬁcation temperature with an attendant buildup of higher welding stresses across the grain boundaries during cooling. Even in situations in which rapid resolidiﬁcation  via  liquid ﬁlm migration (LFM) is enhanced by substantial local grain boundary curvature or diﬀusional coherency strain energy, thick intergran- ular liquid could still result in an enlarged resolidiﬁca- tion temperature range. This could result from a reduction in the driving force for LFM or be due to an appreciable increase in the time required to achieve complete resolidiﬁcation exclusively  via  LFM.

However, this does not necessarily mean that the actual occurrence of liquation cracking will mono- tonically increase with an increase in liquid ﬁlm thick- ness in a given alloy system. For a given alloy, hot cracking susceptibility will most likely increase with an increase in intergranular liquid thickness up to an amount at which the phenomenon of liquid healing begins with the attendant decrease in the extent of cracking. Liquid healing occurs when a large enough amount of intergranular liquid is present to eﬀectively backﬁll and heal incipient cracks as they form. Many braze and solder alloys implicitly use the phenomenon of liquid healing and thus are virtually immune from the problem of hot cracking during solidiﬁcation. The amount of liquid necessary to fully aﬀect liquid healing   can be alloy dependent. Clyne and Davies suggest that at least 10 vol pct is required, which is supported by   the work of Arata and co-workers. Likewise, Cross [53]   et al. in their study of hot cracking in aluminum alloys reported an increase in total crack length with an increase in the amount of terminal eutectic liquid up to a peak value beyond which a further increase in the amount of the liquid resulted in a decrease in the observed total crack length.  

The cooling of intergranular liquid in the HAZ is similar to the ﬁnal stages of solidiﬁcation of castings and welds. Hence, to a ﬁrst approximation, the criteria that govern weld solidiﬁcation cracking can be adopted to explain liquation cracking in the HAZ of weldments. In   an alternate or complementary treatment, Borland, in his work on fundamentals of solidiﬁcation cracking in welds, recognized that while continuous interfacial liquid phase provides an easy extension path for an existing crack, the presence of liquid does not necessarily account for an easy formation of the initial crack. He considered the energies required for formation of a crack by decohesion/separation of solid-liquid compo- nents and that required to cause liquid penetration of a grain boundary under the inﬂuence of stress. He showed that for systems exhibiting very low solid-liquid inter- facial energy,  c SL , liquid penetration is more likely to occur than liquation cracking. This suggests that in a good wetting system, stresses can be relaxed by liquid penetration along the boundary regions. This is in agree- ment with the ‘‘Strain theory’’ of hot cracking developed   by Pellini  et al. and the ‘‘generalized theory’’ of hot cracking in welds and castings developed by Bor-   land. According to these theories, cracking occurs during the thin ﬁlm stage of solidiﬁcation, which allows high stresses to build up locally across the adjoining grains. It is considered that thick intergranular liquid would resist cracking through better accommodation of welding or casting strains (stresses). Therefore, whether a crack will open depends on the magnitude of welding stresses/strains, the amount of intergranular liquid, and the ease of ﬂow of liquid, as controlled by the wetting property. If a large amount of liquid is present and has good wettability, welding stresses could be appreciably relaxed by the penetration of intergranular liquid and, at the same time, heal any incipient ﬁssure. Owczarski   et al. in their detailed study of HAZ cracking in nickel-base superalloys noted that cracking occurred predominantly in regions containing a small amount of liquid compared to the more extensively liquated HAZ grain boundary regions ( i.e. , regions closer to the fusion zone boundary). This extensively melted region, how- ever, has been recognized to experience the most damage to ductility among the HAZ crack susceptible regions, as   reﬂected by Gleeble hot ductility BTR tests. This again shows that in spite of a wide BTR, formation of thick intergranular liquid could limit HAZ cracking. A similar observation has also been reported in IN 718 superalloy where highly liquated HAZ grain boundaries appeared uncracked after welding while cracking was predominant in HAZ regions that exhibited a limited     amount of liquation. It has been observed that a greater extent of grain boundary liquation along with more uncracked liquated grain boundaries with resolid- iﬁed product on them occurred in specimens welded in the overaged condition used in the present work as compared to the solution-heat-treated condition, despite the occurrence of reduced cracking in the latter.  

The overaging heat treatment used in the present work produced a higher volume fraction of liquating  c ¢ particles (30 pct) compared to the standard solution heat treatment (20 pct) with possible grain boundary segregation of boron during holding at the aging temperature. These two factors can account for the considerably thicker HAZ intergranular liquid exhibit- ing a good wetting property that was observed in the overaged specimens. It is normally assumed that pre- weld overaging heat treatment reduces HAZ cracking in precipitation-hardened nickel-base superalloys by increasing the ductility of the base alloy, which subse- quently improves its capability of relieving welding stresses. Nevertheless, in the preceding context, it is also possible that the thicker intergranular liquid with good wetting property produced in the HAZs of overaged IN 738 superalloy contributes to its reduced cracking through accommodation of welding strains by grain boundary liquid penetration and healing of incipient cracks.  

It should, however, be mentioned that even though a reduction in microﬁssuring is produced by preweld overaging heat treatment, the HAZ regions in welded specimens containing highly liquated but uncracked grain boundaries with resolidiﬁed micro constituents could exhibit nonuniform deformation during stress relaxation of PWHT. This might result in crack initia- tion or propagation along such grain boundaries due to exhausting local strain concentration. It has also been found that such liquated grain boundary regions of welded Ni-base superalloy components are most susceptible to PWHT cracking and also to rapid formation of deleterious sigma phase during service.  

# V. SUMMARY AND CONCLUSIONS  

1. The preweld overaging heat treatment used in the present work resulted in formation of an interfacial micro constituent containing M 23 X 6  particles and coarsening of  c ¢  precipitates.  

2. Extensive grain boundary liquation was observed in Gleeble-simulated HAZs and in those around welds in the overaged IN 738 superalloy. Some of the li- quated grain boundaries exhibited liquation crack- ing with resolidiﬁed products formed along the cracked paths.

 3. The preweld overaging heat treatment promoted HAZ grain boundary liquation by the liquation of interfacial micro constituent containing M 23 X 6  parti- cles and constitutional liquation of coarsened  c ¢  pre- cipitate particles.

 4. Correlation of simulated HAZ microstructures with the hot ductility property of the alloy showed that the temperatures at which the alloy exhibited on- heating zero ductility fall within the temperature range at which grain boundary liquation was ob- served. On-cooling ductility was signiﬁcantly dam- aged by the liquation reaction during heating, as reﬂected by a wide BTR of 275   C for a peak tem- perature of 1245   C.

 5. Nevertheless, the thick/large amount of intergranu- lar liquid with a good wetting property in HAZs of the overaged alloy could contribute to a reduction in HAZ cracking through stress relaxation by grain boundary liquid penetration and incipient crack healing phenomenon.
