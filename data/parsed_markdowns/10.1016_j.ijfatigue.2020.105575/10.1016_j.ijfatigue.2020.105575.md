# A B S T R A C T  

# A R T I C L E I N F O  

Inherent defects induced in casting process seriously a ﬀ ect mechanical performance and fatigue properties of the material. Micro-structural investigations reveal that the grain size and dendrite microstructure are sensitive to casting processes and change materials property. The fatigue performance of the material decreases with por- osity and is characterized by distributed voids on grain boundaries. Fatigue cracks nucleated from casting defects dominate the whole fatigue failure process. A developed fatigue micro-crack model based on Paris ’  law can be used to calibrate low cycle fatigue property of the casting material with di ﬀ erent microstructures and agrees with experimental results well.

# 1. Introduction  

Nickel-based superalloys are widely used in aircraft engines because of their high temperature fatigue strength. Components, such as turbine blades, guide vanes etc., experiencing high temperature during service operations are often made of nickel-based superalloys  [1 – 3] . These components are commonly complex in shapes due to aerodynamic performance, air cooling and so on, so that they cannot be machined and have to be casted in production. The casting alloy plays an im- portant role in turbine engines.  

The casting alloys are porous and contain di ﬀ erent defects, such as voids, shrinkage porosities etc. Gas porosity are usually produced by air bubbles contained in the liquid during casting, which form round voids in the material. Shrinkage porosity like interdendritic defects are formed during solidi ﬁ cation process, these defects are detrimental to casting components su ﬀ ering fatigue loading, the fatigue life of casting parts are highly correlated by the size and location of the defects [3,9 – 13] . Although additional processes, such as hot isostatic pressing (HIP), are used to reduce shrinkages and cavities in casting compo- nents, defects cannot completely be removed. The presence of defects diminishes material performance and causes large scatters in fatigue life data  [7,14] . Evaluation of the casting defects in high performance structures is of importance for engineering design.  

′ Microstructures such as  γ /  grains and carbides are common micro- ′ structural components in casting nickel-based superalloy. The γ  phase and MC carbides are the most important strengthening mechanisms in casting polycrystalline nickel-based superalloy  [4] . The carbides par- tially exist in a special script form, that is, both carbides and dendritic metallic phases co-exist in a thin layered structure building scripts. The carbide scripts between interdendritic phases act as fatigue crack site and provide the path of crack propagation in both rupture and low cycle fatigue (LCF) tests  [4 – 6] . But under high cycle fatigue (HCF) loading conditions no carbide cracking or debonding with matrix were found in fracture surface  [7] . So the content of MC carbides in interdendritic area a ﬀ ects fatigue life of casting nickel-based superalloy. But how to quantify the e ﬀ ect of the complex microstructure is still not clari ﬁ ed.  

To evaluate the fatigue life of casting components, the defect size and distribution were studied  [15 – 18] . It is shown that the defect size satis ﬁ es the logarithmic-normal distribution, that is, the number of defects decreases with defect size  [17,18] . Prediction methods, such as the largest extreme value distribution (LEVD) introduced by Murakami [19] , were used to describe the largest defect area in specimen and achieved good results  [14,20 – 22] . In particular, Kunz et al.  [14]  ﬁ gured out that cracks may generate from defects cluster other than single defect in cases by comparing the predicted single defect and defect

Di ﬀ erent models were proposed to predict fatigue life based on the single-crack and multi-crack models. Couper et al.  [23]  applied a single- crack model to analyze crack growth and fatigue life of a casting alu- minum alloy and obtained a good agreement with experiments. It was concluded that the fatigue process can be prolonged by reducing the defect size to a certain extent. But for multi-crack propagation, the prediction from the single-crack model may lead to signi ﬁ cant devia- tions. In analyzing fatigue of laser cladding, welding and pre-corroded specimens, the multi-crack model was used in many published works. Walde et al.  [24]  treated defects observed under scanning electron microscope (SEM) as initial cracks in pre-corroded 2024-T3 aluminum specimens. The results showed that the number of cracks exposed in the fracture surface have a positive correlation with stress amplitude. Both single- and multi-crack models were used in the work, the defects were assumed to be semi-elliptic. The multi-crack model provided a higher accuracy. Chew et al.  [25]  proposed a multi-crack model to predict the fatigue life of laser clade AISI 4340 specimens. It was assumed that the stress intensity factor can be calculated without considering interac- tions of two cracks before crack coalescence  [25] . A method so called no interaction and immediate transition (NIIT) simpli ﬁ ed method was often used to deal with the combination of multiple surface cracks [24 – 28] . Lin et al.  [26]  calculated the fatigue crack growth process of single surface crack and multiple surface cracks by  ﬁ nite element method (FEM), the result showed that cracks almost grew in- dependently and kept the shape unchanged before coalescence. Pang et al.  [28]  proposed a multi-crack life prediction method combined with fatigue crack closure e ﬀ ect and generated good results by using the threshold stress intensity factor approach in predicting the fatigue life of four-point bending fatigue tests. The fatigue life prediction based on methods mentioned above takes only edge cracks into account. More general crack merging from multiple surface cracks as well as internal defects were not considered.  

Besides the size of defects, the location of defects also has a great in ﬂ uence on fatigue life. Serrano-Munoz et al.  [9]  studied the fatigue crack growth process in casting alloy AlSi7Cu3Mg by monitoring fa- tigue tests synchronously and found that surface defect is more harmful to the fatigue performance than internal defects. The stress intensity factors of both internal and surface defects can be estimated by the LEVD method  [9,29,30] . However, recent publications considered only single defect e ﬀ ects to fatigue performance of dense materials. There was no results on fatigue in porous casting alloys available.  

In the present paper di ﬀ erent fatigue crack models are discussed based on experimental results of porous casting nickel-based super- alloys. The material property depends on both microstructure and de- fect distribution. The mechanisms of fatigue crack formation and coa- lescence in the present porous superalloys seem more complicated than those discussed in the literature. A new fatigue crack growth model considering internal and surface defects and defect coalescence is pro- posed to predict the fatigue life of casting alloy specimens.

# 2.2. Distributions of micro voids  

Defects in the casting alloy can be divided into two categories: Gas pores and shrinkage voids. Gas pores are generated by air bubbles trapped and formed round cavities in the material. Shrinkage voids are produced during solidi ﬁ cation, since the liquid metal cannot  ﬁ ll inter- dendritic voids from thermal contraction. It is important to note that the microporosity does not locate in the centre of a mechanical part due to the random distribution of void nucleations  [33] .  

The size and distribution of inevitable casting defects strongly de- pend on the material composition and casting conditions  [20] , so the casting and heat treatment parameters led to visible di ﬀ erent micro- structure in the materials. In the process of solidi ﬁ cation, di ﬀ erent dendrite structures formed and grew gradually, the residual liquid in the interdendritic area solidi ﬁ ed at last  [34] . The interdendritic por- osities formed when there was not enough liquid in the vicinity area. Because of randomness and nonuniform distribution of casting defects, it is not accurate to express the overall porosity of the specimen through the defect content of a certain micro area. So density measurement test were conducted on these two batches of specimens. Macro-scale density measurements con ﬁ rmed 8.235 g/cm 3   and 8.243 g/cm 3   for the CG and FG specimens, respectively. The relative di ﬀ erence is only 0.097%, which is under the measure error. Therefore, the macroscopic porosity of the two batches are the same.  

As shown in  Fig. 1 (c)(d), dendrite structures and inter-dendritic shrinkage defects can be found in the cross section surface. The defects appear both within the grain and on the grain boundary in CG specimen ( Fig. 1 (c)). As the grain size of the FG specimen is small, almost all defects are on the grain boundary ( Fig. 1 (d)). It can be seen from Fig. 1 (c)(d) that the defect content of the CG specimen is signi ﬁ cantly higher than that of the FG specimen, but the area ratio of defects in these micro regions are inappropriate to represent the overall defects content situation. In literatures  [17,18] , nondestructive method like X- ray micro-tomography technique is used to construct the defect dis- tribution of casting materials of aluminium alloy. However, due to the poor penetrability of nickel base superalloy, this method is di ﬃ cult to obtain high precision in the test of large diameter specimen.  

# 2.3. Distributions of MC carbides  

The  ′  phase and MC carbides are the most important strengthening phase in the K465 alloy  [4,31,35] . The carbide particles precipitated on grain boundaries and interdendritic micro-structures strengthen the material at elevated temperature. However, due to its brittleness, the script-like carbides may act as the crack initiation site and provide the crack propagation path  [36] . The volume fraction and the size of the carbide scripts are determined by solidi ﬁ cation conditions according to analysis in  [37] . Detailed observations  [38]  showed that the carbide scripts formed in the quenching process after the  γ  matrix crystal- lization, the cooling rate of the residual liquid is characteristic for the ﬁ nal size and geometry of carbides. The di ﬀ erent grain sizes of the CG and FG specimens imply that the actual solidi ﬁ cation process is dif- ferent, which a ﬀ ects the carbide distribution. As described in the pre- vious section, the content of carbides a ﬀ ects the mechanical perfor- mance and fatigue life of the nickel-based superalloy  [4 – 7] . So it is necessary to study the distributions of carbides in both batches of specimens.

# 4.1.1. Single crack model  

Since the fatigue crack process in K465 is characterized by the complex microstructure as well as casting defects. The macroscopic material property is even sensitive to the casting process. Experimental determination of fracture property of the base material become a dif- ﬁ cult task. To study fatigue crack propagation, the present work took the model of Murakami  [29]  as the  ﬁ rst approximation and established a single crack computational model as follows ( Fig. 13 ):  

•   The fracture surface in a thin-walled tube can be modeled by a planar crack. In calculation of the stress intensity factor the in ﬂ u- ence of the crack surface curvature is neglected.

 •   Initial cracks are assumed to circular or and semi-circular, as shown in  Fig. 13 (a). A growing crack keeps the assumed shape and char- acterized by the radius.

 •   If an internal crack touches the specimen surface, the crack is con- verted into a semi-circular surface crack. The crack area remains unchanged.

 •   The crack is converted into a through-wall crack, as soon as the radius of a semi-circular surface crack or the diameter of an internal circular crack exceeds the wall thickness. The crack area remains unchanged.

 •   The crack centroid is the circular crack center. If the crack becomes semi-circular, the center is determined by the circle.

# 4.1.2. Multi-crack model  

Interactions of cracks to the overall property of the material are of important in many cases, especially for fatigue crack propagation with high loading amplitudes. In modeling with several initial cracks, the crack coalescence has to be considered properly. For simplicity, crack propagation process is assumed to take place in the same plane, the interactions of defects in calculation of  K -factor is ignored. The coa- lescence of two cracks occurs as soon as they are in contact as used in literatures  [24 – 28] . The crack coalescence is modeled as follows:  

•   The resultant crack remains circular or semi-circular. The area of the resultant crack is equal to the sum of two original crack areas.

 •   If both cracks are internal, the center of the resultant crack is located in the area centroid of both cracks.

 •   If one of two cracks is a surface crack or both are surface cracks, the resultant crack is a surface crack, with the center on the surface. The circumferential location is determined from the area centroid.

 •   If two surface cracks are from di ﬀ erent sides of the tube wall, the resultant crack becomes to a through-wall crack. The length of the crack is determined from the area equivalence.

 •   If one of the cracks is through-wall crack, the resultant crack re- mains through-wall crack. The circumferential location of the re- sultant crack is calculated from the area centroid of the two crack.

The circular and semi-circular hypothesis preserves two important parameters: The crack size and the distance to the specimen free surface. The shape of the circular or semi- circular crack is simple and is convenient in calculation. It is especially advantageous in the coalescence calculation of multiple cracks with both surface cracks and internal cracks.

# 5. Conclusions  

In the present work a casting nickel-based superalloy K465 with di ﬀ erent microstructures are investigated experimentally and compu- tationally. Based on experimental observations fatigue damage process in the materials are investigated. A fatigue crack propagation model is developed to explore fatigue damage mechanisms and fatigue life pre- diction. From the experimental and computational results the following conclusions can be drawn:  

•   The casting materials contain large amount of defects, which play an important role in fatigue process. SEM analysis displays signi ﬁ cant di ﬀ erent voids and defects in specimens, which lead to signi ﬁ cantly deviations in macroscopic fatigue behavior. Since the casting defect distributions are inhomogeneous, the fatigue life curves are a ﬀ ected by the casting process and may include large scattering.

 •   E ﬀ ects of grain size to fatigue performance of the casting material are not signi ﬁ cant. In large grains the numerous dendrite micro- structure characterizes material property. Since the secondary den- drites in di ﬀ erently sized grains are in a similar size and regularly distributed, the macroscopic behavior is hardly a ﬀ ected by the grain distribution.

 •   In the fatigue process the casting defects on grain boundaries − characterize the fatigue failure. A modi ﬁ cation of S N  diagram based on the e ﬀ ective specimen area signi ﬁ cantly reduces scatter band of the fatigue life data and provides a meaningful explanation for the fatigue mechanism. Micro-structural investigations con ﬁ rm that the fatigue failure is dominated by fatigue crack propagation from casting defects on grain boundaries.

 •   A fatigue model based on the fracture mechanics concept is developed and able to predict the fatigue damage in the casting alloy properly. From the fatigue crack growth process, one can ﬁ gure out that the large cracks dominate material damage process. The predicted fatigue life agrees with experimental results.  

•   E ﬀ ects of the material porosity in the casting alloy can be integrated into the model parameters. The present single crack model without considering multiple crack interactions provides meaningful pre- dictions for both di ﬀ erent micro-structural materials, which pro- vides a simple way to predict fatigue property of porous materials.

 •   Defects were converted into circular or semicircular cracks in the present work, which introduce errors into life time estimate. The elliptical cracks should re ﬂ ect defects more reasonably, but the al- gorithm is more complex. In the model no fatigue crack initiation was considered, i.e. defects are directly treated as growing cracks, and the model errors were smeared into the Paris ’  law. By com- bining with detailed  ﬁ nite element computations and computed tomography, the present crack model can be further improved to approach more realistic failure mechanisms.
