# Nanoscale lamellar γ/γ ׳  structure and preferred distribution of oxide  particles in a new ODS superalloy  

S. M. Seyyed Aghamiri , HR. Shahverdi , S. Ukai 2 , N. Oono 2 , M. Nili Ahmadabadi 3 , T.  Okuda  

1  Department of Materials Engineering, Tarbiat Modares University, Tehran 14115-143, Iran  2  Material Science and Engineering, Faculty of Engineering, Hokkaido University, Sapporo 060-8626, Japan  3 School of Metallurgy and Materials Engineering, University of Tehran, Tehran 14395-731, Iran  4   Kobelco Research Institute Ltd., Kobe 651-2271, Japan  

# Abstract  

Two microstructural phenomena have been studied in a newly designed Ni-based ODS superalloy with a  high solvus temperature. For the first time, a nanoscale lamellar γ/γ׳ structure was obtained along the  elastically soft [100] direction by a eutectoid-like reaction from a severely deformed matrix. In addition, the  preferred distribution of semi-coherent Y 2 Hf 2 O 7  oxide particles was observed only in the γ phase due to the  prior partitioning of Y and O to this phase. The microstructure development during the processing route is  discussed based on the solvus temperature of this new ODS superalloy in comparison to commercial ODS  superalloys.  

Keywords:  Metals and alloys; Ni-based ODS superalloy; Microstructure; lamellar γ/γ´ structure;  Y 2 Hf 2 O 7  oxide particles  

# 1.   Introduction  

Nickel-based oxide dispersion strengthened (ODS) superalloys produced by mechanical alloying are  promising materials as the next generation of superalloys for modern gas turbines in advanced aerospace  and power plant applications [1-4]. For this purpose, the microstructure development for promoting high- temperature strength is of significant importance. Precipitation hardening is the main strengthening  mechanism of superalloys, as achieved by the L1 2  ordered γ׳ precipitates embedded coherently in the fcc γ  matrix. The morphology and distribution of the γ′ phase inside the γ matrix strongly affects high- temperature properties [5,6]. As reported in the literature [1,2,7], up to now, the “spherical” or “cuboidal”  shapes are the usual morphologies of γ׳, which have been characterized in single crystal superalloys  (CMSX10, etc.) or commercial ODS superalloys (MA6000 and MA760, etc.). In addition, compared to  non-ODS superalloys, oxide dispersion strengthening using the uniform distribution of fine oxide particles  into the matrix allows commercial ODS superalloys to be used at higher temperature services [8-10]. The  degree of strengthening is highly dependent on the size of particles, interparticle spacing, and the  oxide/matrix interface structure [11, 12]. Generally, it has been proven that oxide particles in the  commercial Ni-based ODS superalloys consist of uniformly dispersed incoherent Y-Al oxide phases  including YAM (Al 2 Y 4 O 9 ), YAG (Al 5 Y 3 O 12 ), YAP, and YAH (AlYO 3 ) [13-16]. However, our earlier  studies have demonstrated that the addition of Hf is effective in oxide particle refinement via the formation  of new Y 2 Hf 2 O 7  oxide particles in Ni-based ODS superalloys [17]. The distribution of Y 2 Hf 2 O 7  oxide  particles is crucial to pin the motion of dislocations in the γ matrix rather than γ′, because dislocations in γ  cannot enter the γ′ phase without the formation of anti-phase boundaries [2]. In this work, the γ′  morphology and the distribution of oxide particles were studied in a new ODS superalloy with a high  solvus temperature, and the new findings were discussed in comparison to commercial ODS superalloys.  

# 2.   Materials and methods  

A new Ni-based ODS superalloy, designated ODS-CMSX10, was developed with a composition based  on the CMSX-10 single crystal superalloy. The composition of the ODS-CMSX10 superalloy is: Ni:  balance, Cr: 2.7, Co: 2.7, Al: 5.3, Ta: 7.2, W: 4.4, Re: 5.2, Fe: 0.3, Mo: 0.4, Hf: 0.86, Y 2 O 3 : 0.64 in mass %.  An atomized superalloy powder with a composition similar to CMSX-10 was mixed with the Hf and Y 2 O 3   powders to process this ODS superalloy. The powder mixture was mechanically alloyed (MA) in an Ar gas  atmosphere using an attrition-type ball mill for 48 h. The MA powder was extruded and then forged at  \~1150°C. The specimens from the extruded bar and the MA powder were given a subsequent three-step  aging treatment: 1150°C/4 h/AC-870°C/24 h/AC-760°C/30 h/AC (AC: Air cooling), which is the standard  aging procedure for the CMSX-10 single-crystal [18].  

XRD diffraction was conducted by means of Cu–Kα radiation with a Philips X´Pert PRO to characterize  the phases. JEOL JXA-8530F field emission scanning electron microscopy equipped with a field emission  electron probe micro analyzer (FE-EPMA) specified the distribution of elements in the two-phase γ/γ′  microstructure. Thin foils were prepared by means of a JEOL JIB-4600F focused ion beam (FIB) to analyze  the microstructures. For the observation of oxide particles, a final thinning of the foils was also performed  using electro polishing at 20V in a solution of 80%C 2 H 5 OH+20%H 2 SO 4  at about 0°C after FIB preparation.  The microstructure was characterized by JEOL JEM-2010 transmission electron microscopy (TEM) with  an acceleration voltage of 200 kV.  

# 3.   Results  

Figure 1(a) shows the relatively broad peaks of the γ matrix in the XRD analysis of the MA-ODS- CMSX10 superalloy, indicating high strain energy and defects have been induced in the ultrafine-grained  matrix by severe plastic deformation during ball milling. However, the XRD of the three-step aged ODS- CMSX10 superalloy includes the sharp double-peaks of γ and γ׳, which are very close to each other,  suggesting the relaxation of strain energy. The microstructure of the ODS-CMSX10 superalloy exhibits a  combination of the different morphologies of γ׳, as illustrated in Fig. 1(b): the upper part shows the new  nanoscale lamellar structure and the lower part presents the typical dispersion of fine spherical γ׳ with a  diameter of 50–80 nm precipitated in the γ matrix, as shown by the arrows. The diffraction pattern (DP) in  Fig. 1(b) shows that fully coherent γ׳ phase exists inside the γ phase. This microstructure caused a low  lattice misfit of 0.16% between γ and γ׳ (a γ  = 0.36216 nm, a γ′  = 0.36274 nm), as determined at a high  temperature (about 1,000°C) using XRD measurements. The fine γ׳ lamellae with a thickness of about 10  nm distributed in the γ matrix can be observed in a dark field TEM image (Fig. 1 (c)). The change in the  elastic strain energy value of the lamellar structure is minimized via the alignment of fine γ׳ lamellae along  the elastically soft [100] direction with the lowest Young’s modulus, as shown in diffraction pattern of Fig.  1(c). Therefore, severe strains during mechanical alloying modified the γ׳ morphology into the fine lamellar  structure with respect to the elastic strains associated with the lattice misfit.  

TEM observations of the distribution of oxide particles in both the γ and γ׳ phases in the ODS-CMSX10  superalloy have been shown in Fig. 2. In the γ phase (Fig. 2 (a)), many fine oxide particles with semi- coherent interfaces are revealed by the Moiré fringes, according to Ashby-brown contrast [19,20]. The  composition of the majority of oxide particles was modified in Y 2 Hf 2 O 7  by adding Hf, which was  confirmed by XRD analysis in Fig. 1(a). The oxide particles were refined to the average size of 8 nm with  interparticle spacing of 76 nm, according to calculations in Ref. [21]. However, in the γ׳ phase, interestingly,  no or few oxide particles could be found, as seen in Fig. 2(b). Figure 3 shows the backscatter SEM image of  the ODS-CMSX10 superalloy and the related elemental maps of Hf, Y, and O. It clearly demonstrates the  partitioning of Y and O to the bright γ phase, while Hf has a homogenous distribution in both the γ and γ׳  phases. Thus, the Y 2 Hf 2 O 7  oxide particles precipitated preferentially due to the higher concentrations of Y  and O in the γ phase.  

# 4.   Discussion  

Compared to the commercial ODS superalloys, the different γ׳ morphologies and distribution of oxide  particles observed in the ODS-CMSX10 are discussed according to the schematic illustration in Fig. 4. As a  general route for the processing of commercial ODS superalloys, such as MA6000 and MA760, after  mechanical alloying of the powder mixture, thermo mechanical treatment is performed at a temperature  (\~1,150°C) above the solvus temperature [1,22,23]. Therefore, the microstructure contains the γ single- phase, and the precipitation of Y-Al oxide particles happens in the γ phase homogeneously. Then,  appropriate aging treatments are applied below the solvus temperature to precipitate cuboidal γ´ particles  inside the γ phase, as shown in the left side of Fig. 4. In contrast, the solvus temperature of ODS-CMSX10  is relatively high at about 1,300°C, according to calculations made by the thermodynamic analysis code.  The thermo mechanical treatment temperature is adequately below the solvus temperature and therefore,  both the γ and γ´ phases are stable in this condition. According to Fig. 1 (c), it is supposed that the high- energy matrix transforms to a nanoscale lamellar γ/γ׳ structure by a eutectoid-like reaction, as follows:   High-energy severely deformed matrix → γ + γ׳  

Then, due to the partitioning of Y and O to the γ phase during the above transformation, the preferred  distribution of Y 2 Hf 2 O 7  oxide particles happens in the γ phase. In addition, the spherical γ׳ particles are  precipitated in the γ matrix by the following aging treatment, as shown in the right side of Fig. 4. Thus,  severe plastic deformation induced by ball milling brings about the formation of a nanoscale lamellar γ/γ׳  structure and consequently the preferred distribution of oxide particles.  

# 5.   Summary  

In summary, a new oxide dispersion strengthened superalloy (ODS-CMSX10) with a high solvus  temperature was developed. Severe plastic deformation led to the nanoscale lamellar γ/γ´ structure aligned  along the elastically soft [100] direction by a eutectoid-like reaction. In addition, the preferred distribution  of fine semi-coherent Y 2 Hf 2 O 7  oxide particles happened only in the γ phase due to the prior partitioning of  Y and O to the γ phase.
