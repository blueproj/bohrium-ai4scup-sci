A physics-driven and microstructure-based model has been formulated for monotonic stress – strain behavior and applied to a polycrystalline nickel based superalloy, René 104. While the yield strength of this alloy is strongly dependent on size and distribution of the ordered  γ ' precipitate, the post-yield behavior is found to be fairly insensitive to the same. Additionally, post-yield hardening and softening rates are found to vary quite signi ﬁ cantly within the narrow range of test temperatures examined. For a model to be able to consistently explain all these observations (a) it must explicitly account for microstructural effects and temperature dependence, and (b) it must be aware of the speci ﬁ c strengthening mechanisms unique to nickel-based superalloys. Accordingly, the present effort starts with Estrin's dislocation based framework (Estrin (1996)  [4] ), and then extends the same to incorporate post yield strengthening/softening mechanisms. This paper presents details of this mechanism guided semi-empirical monotonic stress – strain model and its ability to describe  ﬂ ow behavior for different microstructures over a range of temperatures.

# 1. Introduction  

René 104 is a polycrystalline nickel based superalloy used for turbine disc applications in aero-engines. Development of a model for ﬂ ow behavior of this material is essential for predicting location speci ﬁ c properties in turbine disc components, and for development of a damage tolerant model.  

For the last 45 years, there have been considerable efforts towards constitutive modeling of stress – strain response of metal- lic materials  [1 – 6] . An excellent review article on constitutive theories can be found in  [7] . Chaboche's own constitutive theories [8]  are based on yield surface considerations. These involved choice of the viscosity function following von Mises elastic domain equation and choice of isotropic and kinematic hardening equa- tions. These models are phenom eno logical in nature and are not motivated by microstructure considerations. As a result, these are not capable of predicting material response beyond the range of experimental conditions used in the estimation of parameters. Roters et al.  [9]  have given a more recent overview of available constitutive laws. They have clearly differentiated between phe- nomenological models, where the material state is only described  

internal variables representing the micromechanical state of the material were related to the dislocation density, Estrin included microstructure data through equations describing the evolution of dislocation density  [4,35,36] . However, these microstructure dri- ven models are developed for materials which do not show various unique behaviors as shown by polycrystalline  γ ' strength- ened nickel based superalloys. These unique behaviors include retention of or even improvement in strength with temperature, higher initial hardening rates at higher temperatures etc. A more recent work by Huang et al.  [37]  describes discrete dislocation dynamics modeling of nickel based single crystal superalloy, where precipitate size and shape effects have been accounted for, however, temperature variation effect and grain size effect (as it is developed for single crystal) have not been explicitly con- sidered. Using similar dislocation dynamics approach, Zhang et al. [25]  have developed constitutive model for fcc metals, however this model is applicable only for pure FCC metals. The work by Shenoy et al. and Pryzybyla et al.  [38,39]  introduced microstruc- tural features as inputs in modeling  ﬂ ow behavior of a nickel based superalloy, IN 100 ™ . However, this model is applicable only at a speci ﬁ c temperature. A more generic model would incorporate various deformation mechanisms operative over a range of temperatures.  

The goal of our present work is to introduce both microstruc- ture and temperature as input variables to the model, so that the model can be applied at different temperatures for various micro- structural conditions. In the present work, Estrin's modeling framework  [36]  has been adapted and applied to the polycrystal- line nickel based superalloy René 104. While the general frame- work has been maintained, a few additional strengthening mechanism terms, speci ﬁ c to this alloy group have been added to capture the uniqueness shown by the  ﬂ ow behavior of this type of alloys. Plausible deformation mechanisms operating at different temperature regimes have been  ﬁ rst identi ﬁ ed and the corre- sponding terms have been incorporated in the constitutive model for post yield stress – strain behavior of René 104 under monotonic uniaxial loading. The model has been made modular in nature, meaning that the yield strength and post yield strengthening contributions have been made additive. Hence, even though the temperature dependence of yield strength (YS) has been modeled empirically at this stage making the present model semi-empirical, further reduction in empiricism is possible over the present state of the model by incorporating physics of temperature dependency of the yield strength. The model is able to capture all the experimental data on the yield and post yield constitutive response at different tempera- tures well within the range of scatter in experimental data.  Fig. 6 shows the prediction of stress as a function of temperature for different levels of plastic strain. The model is able to display the contributions of different deformation mechanisms operating in different temperature regimes. As mentioned in the model description, the following temperature dependent deformation mechanisms are captured by the model: (1) monotonic decrease in shear modulus with temperature, (2) recovery through dislocation cross-slip at low temperature range, (3) creep based recovery at high temperature regime and (4) anomalous strengthening with temperature due to cross-slip followed by pinning of dislocations (formation of Kear – Wilsdorf (KW) lock) operative at high tem- perature regime.  

It should be clari ﬁ ed here that the present model is semi- empirical in nature. While effects of temperature on post yield hardening and softening has been taken into account through mechanistic approach, effects of temperature on YS and shear modulus have been incorporated empirically. However, as the present model is modular in nature, where YS and post yield strengthening can be dealt with separately, more physics can be incorporated for temperature dependencies of YS and shear modulus at any time without affecting the post yield hardening. Capturing of temperature dependent mechanisms operative for the post yield part is independent of YS prediction.

# 6. Conclusion  

The following conclusions can be drawn from the above analysis:  

  A physics driven constitutive model has been developed to describe the deformation behavior of René-104 under mono- tonic loading over a range of temperature. Mechanistic approach has been taken for modeling the effects of microstructure and temperature speci ﬁ cally on post yield  ﬂ ow behavior. For room temperature YS prediction, mechanistic approach has been taken, however the effect of temperature on YS has been taken empirically in the present model, which in future should be made mechanism based.

   This has been formulated using internal state variables having separate contributions due to yield strength and post yield strengthening. A modular approach has been adopted to describe yield and post yield hardening as a function of microstructure and temperature. This modular approach would help in further decreasing the empiricism through incorpora- tion of effects of temperature on YS and shear modulus.

   The model has provision to select appropriate deformation mechanisms operative at different temperatures. It also allows selection of appropriate microstructure based parameters like grain size,  γ ' size, and volume fraction for different populations of precipitates (e.g., primary, secondary, tertiary precipitates etc.).

   The anomalous strengthening with temperature in the  ﬂ ow behavior has been attributed to cross slip followed by disloca- tion pinning (formation of KW locks).

   Stress – strain plots of René-104 having two different  γ ' size distributions have different yield strength, but identical post yield strengthening behavior. This has been attributed to the fact that precipitate size affects the yield strength only; whereas its effect on post yield deformation behavior is insigni ﬁ cant signifying that grain size plays a dominant role in determining post yield deformation behavior.

   The model based predictions has been found to be fairly close to experimental stress – strain plots of René-104 having two different initial microstructures. This justi ﬁ es the inclusion of the above micro-mechanisms in developing the predictive model describing the deformation behavior of this nickel based superalloy.
