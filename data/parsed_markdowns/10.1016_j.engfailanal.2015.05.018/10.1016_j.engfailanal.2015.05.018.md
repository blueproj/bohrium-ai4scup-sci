# a b s t r a c t  

A combustor liner from an aircraft engine, which is made of a wrought Ni-based superalloy GH3039, was investigated after service exposure of 1600 engine operating hours. A series of experiments, including macroscopic examination and microscopic examination, fractog- raphy observation and thermal exposure experiments, were conducted to investigate the formation mechanism of hole-edge cracks. The results show that microstructural degrada- tion and thermal fatigue should be responsible for the formation of hole-edge cracks in complementary combustion zone (CCZ) of the combustor liner. CCZ is the highest temper- ature area instead of primary combustion zone (PCZ) during service, which results in sig- niﬁcant microstructural degradation, e.g., the stripping of coating and intergranular oxidation, in the hole-edge in CCZ. The cracks initiate at the hole-edge due to coating microstructure degradations and intergranular Al 2 O 3 -type oxidation under the coating, and then propagate under the action of thermal fatigue, which results in ﬁnal failure of the combustor liner.

# 1. Introduction  

As the place where fuel atomizing, air–fuel mixing and fuel burning take place, the combustor liner is one of the most important components in aero-engine combustion chamber. During service time, the combustor liner is exposed to very complicated and severe conditions, including high temperature, gas corrosion and vibration, readily leading to crack forma- tion, local fracture, even malfunction and failure  [1,2] . Accordingly, research on the microstructure degradation and damage behavior of the combustor liner under these conditions is an important issue.  

Previous reports have shown that the combustor liner expands and shrinks rapidly, namely thermal fatigue, is one of the main reasons for failures  [2,3] . Most of these studies on the thermal fatigue were based on the fracture analysis, and the working temperature distribution of the combustor liner was usually determined by using a numerical modeling method [2] . However, the experimental data coming from the practice service is still in shortage, leading to an imperfectly under- standing of the mechanism on initial cracks formation and propagation in the combustor liner.  

In this work, a failure combustor liner due to hole-edge cracks is investigated through a failure analysis method combined with microstructure degradation imitation by thermal exposure. The thermal exposure experiments on the original material are conducted to examine the thermal effects on microstructure and evaluate the service temperature of the combustor liner. The reasons for hole-edge crack formation will be discussed.  

# 2. Experimental procedure  

The investigated combustor liner was taken from a serviced aircraft engine, which had been in service for 1600 engine operating hours. The combustor liner is made of a wrought Ni-base superalloy GH3039, with an enamel coating on its inside and outside surfaces. Chemical compositions of the combustor liner are presented in  Table 1 .  

The outside surface proﬁle of the investigated combustor liner is shown in  Fig. 1 . Corresponding to the combustion char- acteristics and the size of the dilution hole, the combustor liner can be orderly divided into three zones from the head to the tail, which are primary combustion zone (PCZ), complementary combustion zone (CCZ) and cooling zone (CZ). There are totally 10 rows of dilution holes in the combustor liner. Generally, Rows 1-3 are included in PCZ, while Rows 4-6 and Rows 7-10 are included in CCZ and CZ, respectively.  

The samples in transverse section were cut from neighborhood of the holes in PCZ, CCZ and CZ, respectively, as shown in Fig. 1 . Two positions in the samples were observed to investigate the matrix microstructure and coating microstructure, as shown in  Fig. 2 . Location A was used for matrix microstructure examination while location B was used for coating microstructure observation. The samples with hole-edge crack propagation and tip were sectioned and observed. The crack fracture surface was also investigated after the sample was intentionally ruptured along propagation direction.  

The thermal exposure experiments were conducted in order to examine the thermal effect on microstructural degrada- tion and speculate service temperature of the combustor liner. Since the head of the combustor liner was far away from the combustion gas and the service temperature here was much lower than that in CCZ, the microstructure in the head was almost unchanged during service and considered as an unserviced one. Therefore, the original samples for the thermal expo- sure experiment were sectioned from the head of the combustor liner. The samples were exposed in an air-circulated furnace for a period of 100 h at 600, 650, 700, 750, 800 and 850   C or for periods of 500, 1000, 1500 and 2000 h at 750   C, respectively.  

The metallographic specimens were characterized after mechanically polished and etched in a solution of 30 ml HCl, 10 ml HNO 3  and 10 ml C 3 H 8 O 3 . The microstructural examinations were performed using a ZEISS SUPRA 55 ﬁeld-emission scanning electron microscope (FE-SEM). The compositions were analyzed by the energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) detector on FE-SEM. Thicknesses of coating were measured from about ﬁve areas and obtained by calculating the mean value of the measurements on each coating microstructure sample. The fracture surface of hole-edge crack was treated by immersing in a hot solution of 10 g KMnO 4 , 20 g NaOH and 100 ml H 2 O to remove the oxide scale.  

# 3. Experimental results  

# 3.1. Macroscopic inspection and fracture surface observation on hole-edge cracks  

Macroscopic inspection was conducted to investigate surface crack and macro damage of the combustor liner. No obvious geometric deformation or severe corrosion attack was observed on the outside surface. Different colors along axial direction were observed on the inside surface, as shown in  Fig. 3 a. The inside surface of CZ and PCZ exhibits light green, which is the color of enamel coating, but that of CCZ exhibits dark grey. Additionally, totally 21 hole-edge cracks were observed in CCZ while no cracks were observed in PCZ and CZ. Typical hole-edge cracks morphology is shown in  Fig. 3 b. Some of the cracks connect two holes. The cracks growth directions are almost parallel to the axial direction of the combustor liner and the length of the cracks is about 5–10 mm.  

Fig. 4  shows the SEM fractographs of typical hole-edge fracture surface. The fracture surface proﬁle consists of three dis- tinguished regions, which are marked I, II and III in  Fig. 4 a. In Region I, the fracture surface is relatively rough and mainly characterized by intergranular fracture, as shown in  Fig. 4 b. The grain size of fracture surface is about 40–80  l m. In Region II, however, the fracture surface has a smooth appearance. Typical fatigue striations in different directions were observed at higher magniﬁcations, as shown in  Fig. 4 c. Region III is intentionally broken but not the region of original hole-edge crack propagation.

Typical SEM images and EDS mapping results on the coating microstructures at the hole-edge in original sample and in CCZ are presented in  Fig. 6 a and b, respectively. As shown in  Fig. 6 a, there exists a compacted coating, which mainly consists of two granular phases in original sample. EDS results indicate that the white contrast phase, marked as 1, is enriched in Cr and O while the dark contrast phase, marked as 2, is enriched in Si and O. These phases should be undissolved particles of Cr 2 O 3  and SiO 2  produced by coating processing. All of the undissolved phases and coating composition are consistent with the reported chemical constitution of the enamel coating  [4,5] .  

In CCZ, however, two oxide layers instead of the enamel coating were observed, as shown in  Fig. 6 b. EDS results show that the loose outer layer, marked as 3, primarily contains Ni and O elements and the continuous inner layer, marked as 4, pri- marily contains Cr and O elements. It can be speculated that the outer layer consists of NiO and NiCr 2 O 4  and the inner layer consists of Cr 2 O 3 , which are also correspondent with the chemical constitution of the oxide layers for this type material  [6– 8] . In addition, intergranular Al 2 O 3  phases in black contrast were observed beneath the oxidation layer.  

Fig. 7  shows the typical SEM image and EDS mapping results on the microstructure beneath the oxide layers in CCZ. Deep cracks extended to matrix and ﬁlled with oxidation products are observed beneath the oxide layers. Some black contrast phases, marked as 5, are continuously formed in intergranular regions neighboring the cracks. EDS results show high con- centration of Al and O is aggregated in the phases. Some small hole-edge cracks close to the oxide layers are also observed at this location, as shown in  Fig. 7 . It is suggested that the CCZ suffered from intergranular oxidation and the oxidation pro- duct should be Al 2 O 3 . A slightly Cr-depleted region is observed neighboring the cracks, as shown in EDS mapping results.

# 3.3. The matrix microstructure  

Fig. 8  shows the typical microstructures in PCZ, CCZ, CZ and the original microstructure in the combustor liner. Equiaxial grains with uneven grain sizes, annealing twins and bloky MC-type carbides were observed in all the samples. EDS results indicate that the MC carbides in bright contrast are enriched in Nb while that in black contrast are enriched in Ti, thus they should be NbC and TiC, respectively  [9] . The microstructures in PCZ and CZ are similar to the original microstructure. However, besides the MC carbides, the abnormal cellular and granular carbides are observed along the grain boundaries and twin boundaries in CCZ, as shown in  Fig. 8 b, which suggests that the microstructures in CCZ were degenerated obviously. EDS results indicate that the cellular and granular carbides are enriched in Cr and C.

# 4. Discussion  

The combustor liner is subjected to a complex combination of high temperature, oxidation and corrosive condition. Different zones of the combustor liner play different roles during service. PCZ is the main place for fuel combustion, while CCZ is the place for residual fuel combustion and CZ is used for fuel gas cooling, so the combustor liner is served in a complex of non-uniform temperature ﬁeld. Generally, PCZ is exposed to the highest temperature during service. As shown in  Fig. 3 ,

Thermal fatigue, caused by internal stresses generated by cyclic temperature gradients, is an important life-limiting factor for many operating parts, especially for those serving in fast heating and quickly cooling conditions such as vanes of turbines and combustor liner  [10,11] . However, there are very few research results focusing on the microstructure correlation and crack formation related to the original materials, which results in an imperfectly understanding of the mechanism on initial cracks formation and propagation.  

A large number of fatigue striations on the fracture surface indicates that the combustor liner in CCZ undergo a thermal fatigue process  [12] , as shown in  Fig. 4 . The fatigue striations were only observed in crack propagation region (Region II) but not in crack initiation region (Region I). As shown in  Fig. 7 , intergranular Al 2 O 3  is formed neighboring the hole-edge cracks tip and the cracks initiate and propagate along the oxidated grain boundary, which results in the intergranular cracking mor- phology at Region I, as shown in  Fig. 4 . It is suggested that the coating microstructure degradation and intergranular oxida- tion should be responsible for the hole-edge cracks initiation.  

When the combustor liner starts or stops working, the temperature variation and gradient should be very large in CCZ. Meanwhile, the thermal stress will produce and easily concentrate at hole-edge in CCZ. Since the service temperature in CCZ is the highest in different zones, the unexpected high temperature in CCZ can lead to more serious brush of burning gas and larger thermal stress, then the enamel coating in CCZ is inclined to strip off, especially at the surface of hole-edge in CCZ, as presented in  Figs. 5 and 6 . Once the coating is destroyed, the unprotected alloy will exposed to a high-heat and oxygen envi- ronment, leading to surface oxidation and intergranular Al 2 O 3 -type oxidation, as shown in  Figs. 6 b and  7 . The intergranular oxidation takes place under the coating, which reduces the grain boundary strength of the matrix and leads to the initiation of thermal fatigue crack at this oxidized grain boundary  [13,14] .  

As shown in  Figs. 6 b and  7 , the coating microstructures in CCZ indicate that there is no abnormal Cr-rich carbides beneath the oxide layer at hole-edge. The main reason is probably that the practical service temperature of coating microstructure is higher than that of matrix microstructure. The coating has direct contact with the burning gas during service, thus most of Cr element near the hole-edge has participated in oxidation instead of precipitation of carbides. The Cr-depleted regions in grain boundaries can be produced by intergranular oxidation neighboring the cracks under the coating ( Fig. 7 ), which pos- sibly decreases the oxidation resistance in these regions, eventually accelerates the intergranular oxidation. It is probably the reason why the crack initiated at the hole-edge then propagated mainly along the grain boundary. Accordingly, protecting the matrix from oxidation and enhancing the binding force between the matrix and coating may be efﬁcient ways to decrease crack formation and propagation tendency of high temperature components.  

# 5. Conclusions  

1. In the experimental combustor liner, the CCZ is the highest temperature area instead of PCZ during service. Based on the thermal exposure experiments, the highest service temperature is assessed to be less than 600   C in both PCZ and CZ but close to 750   C in CCZ.

 2. After service, the abnormal cellular or granular carbides enriched in Cr and the intergranular Al 2 O 3 -type oxidation is pro- duced in matrix and under the coating in CCZ, respectively. Coating microstructure degradations and intergranular oxi- dation are responsible for initiation of the thermal fatigue cracks at the hole-edge of the combustor liner.

 3. The hole-edge cracks propagated under the action of thermal fatigue, which results in ﬁnal failure of the combustor liner.
