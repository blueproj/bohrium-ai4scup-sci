# Precipitation of nanosized DO 22  superlattice with high thermal stability in an Ni–Cr–W superalloy  

Yang Chen, Rui Hu, ⇑ Hongchao Kou, Tiebang Zhang and Jinshan Li  

State Key Laboratory of Solidiﬁcation Processing, Northwestern Polytechnical University, Xi’an 710072, People’s Republic of China  

Received 23 October 2013; revised 21 December 2013; accepted 22 December 2013 Available online 29 December 2013  

It is demonstrated for the ﬁrst time that a nanoscale superlattice with a DO 22  structure can precipitate in an Ni–Cr–W alloy by means of a simple ageing treatment at 700   C. The existence of tungsten in the superalloy stabilizes the DO 22  superlattices, which are transient metastable phases in Ni–Cr–Mo alloy. Because of the ﬁne, spherical and dispersed precipitates in the Ni–Cr–W matrix, the microhardness of the alloy is improved remarkably.

Keywords:  DO 22  superlattice; Ni–Cr–W superalloy; Ageing; High thermal stability  

Ni–Cr–W-based superalloys with excellent mechanical strength and long-term creep rupture strength have been developed recently  [1] . They have great potential in the ﬁeld of nuclear industry  [2] . It has been reported  [3]  that Haynes 230, with a nominal composition of Ni–22Cr–14W (in wt.%), is a very prom- ising candidate as the heat exchanger in the fourth- generation supercritical water-cooled reactor system (GEN IV SCWR). In order to meet the demands of the GEN IV SCWR program, particular attention has been focused on the lasting creep performance of the Haynes 230 alloy  [4,5] . Kakehi  [6]  has reported that secondary precipitates have a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the creep strength of Ni-based superalloys, which suggests that creep resistance can be improved by superlattice precipitation.  

It has been proved that the superlattices precipitate through order–disorder transformation in some Ni–Cr- based and Ni–Mo-based superalloys, and these ordered particles play a dominate role in improving the mechan- ical properties of the Ni-based superalloys  [7–13] . It is known that the closely related Pt 2 Mo-type, DO 22 and D1 a  superlattices can precipitate after a series of long-range ordering reactions in Ni–Mo alloy and/or Ni–Cr–Mo alloy  [14,15] .  

However, the precipitation of superlattices in the ter- nary system Ni–Cr–W superalloy during prolonged exposure to proper temperature has not yet been spe- cially studied. The understanding of the microstructural changes occurring in this superalloy during long periods of ageing may help in the prediction of its application life. Rtishchev et al.  [16]  found that the orthorhombic Ni 2 (Cr,W,Mo) superlattice precipitates in the matrix during long-term ageing in some Ni–Cr–W–Mo superal- loys. Nevertheless, the superlattice in the Ni-based alloy with high tungsten content has rarely been reported on. Chatterjee and Roy  [17]  seemed to have found that the Ni 2 (Cr,W) superlattice exerts a positive inﬂuence on the creep performance of Haynes 230 alloy. However, the precipitates which they considered to be Pt 2 Mo-type superlattice were probably carbide, judging from the size, morphology and electron diﬀraction patterns of the phase.  

In this letter, the crystallographic changes occurring during long-term exposure to an appropriate tempera- ture in the Ni–Cr–W-based superalloy were studied with three primary objectives: ﬁrst, to determine whether superlattices will precipitate in the Ni-based alloy, which is a solid-solution-strengthened austenitic superalloy with a high tungsten content; secondly, to identify which type of superlattice is thermodynamic ally stable in the Ni–Cr–W alloy; and thirdly, to determine whether the mechanical properties of the Ni–Cr–W alloy will be im- proved by the precipitation of superlattices.  

The experimental alloy in this work had the composi- tion 59.2Ni–20.8Cr–18.8W–1.2Mo (in wt.%). The cast material was prepared by vacuum arc melting. The ingot was then homogenized at 1200   C for 24 h in an argon environment and furnace cooled to room temperature. The specimens were cut from the homogenized ingot and annealed at 1160   C for 0.5 h, followed by water quenching. Subsequently, the specimens were aged at 700   C for 5–40 h then air cooled.  

All samples underwent microhardness testing at room temperature to estimate the eﬀects of the precipi- tates on the hardness. The load for the Vickers microh- ardness test was set to 100 gf and the test time was 15 s. The median values of hardness were obtained after 10 test repetitions.  

The specimens were prepared by standard metallo- graphic techniques to analyze their microstructures. The microstructures were characterized by transmission electron microscopy (TEM) with energy-dispersive spec- trometry (EDS) analysis. An automatic twin-jet elec- tropolisher, with a polishing solution of 8% perchloric acid carbinol, was used to make thin foils, which were examined in a Tecnai F30 G2 transmission electron microscope operated at 300 kV. The acquisition time for recording diﬀraction patterns was 0.5 s. Since the parent face-centered cubic (fcc) structure and the super- lattice structure are closely related, all diﬀraction pat- terns were indexed in terms of the fcc structure.

In the as-quenched condition, diﬀuse intensity max- ima caused by short-range order (SRO) can be seen at {11/20} positions in  Figure 2 (a), which is similar to the case of Ni–Mo–Cr alloy  [15,18] . However, not all se- lected-area electron diﬀraction patterns from the matrix regions show extra reﬂections or intensity maxima that can be attributed to the sizes of SRO domains that are below the electron diﬀraction limit. No dark-ﬁeld (DF) images from these intensity maxima at {11/20} could be taken owing to their extremely weak nature. During the early stages of thermal ageing (5 h at 700   C), nano- sized precipitates with an average dimension of about 4 nm were observed, as shown in the example of Figure 2 (b). The DF TEM image shows the precipitate assuming the morphology of small sphere. The charac- teristic reﬂections at the {100}, {110} and {11/20} positions observed in  Figure 2 c and d suggest that the high-density nanoscale precipitates show the DO 22 structure.  Figure 3  shows TEM images of the ageing- treated (40 h at 700   C) specimen and the EDS spectra of the precipitates. It can be seen that the sizes and mor- phology of the particles do not change with ageing time. It has been reported that the mechanism of SRO–long-range order (LRO) transformation at lower temperature ( 6  700   C) is continuous ordering, which changes to a nucleation-and-growth process at higher temperature (750   C)  [18] . When the transformation oc- curs with by continuous ordering, the {11/20} diﬀuse intensity maxima representing motifs of SRO structures (coherent  “ 420 ” -ordered structures) and the diﬀuse intensity linkage between the SRO and LRO positions are observed in reciprocal space. In the Ni–Cr–W alloy, the mode of the SRO–LRO transformation is continuous ordering due to the presence of the {11/20} intensity maxima in Figure 2 (a). However, because the characteristic diﬀrac- tion spots of SRO are also available in the reciprocal space of the DO 22  superlattice, the diﬀuse intensity link- age between SRO and LRO cannot be observed, so the mechanism cannot be identiﬁed with certainty. Kumar and Vasudevan  [18]  have pointed out that the changes in microhardness as a function of ageing time could indirectly reﬂect the transformation mode. To identify the mechanism, it is necessary to analyze the trend of microhardness variation (discussed later in this letter).  

It should be noted that the electron diﬀraction pat- terns of only DO 22  superlattice are observed during the initial stages of thermal ageing in the Ni–Cr–W alloy. Whereas the DO 22 , D1 a  and Pt 2 Mo-type superlat- tices coexist during the early stages of ageing in the Ni–Mo–Cr alloy, the DO 22  superlattice is a transient metastable phase that decomposes with continued espo- sure  [15] . The eﬀect of the ternary alloying element on the relative stabilities of the three competing LRO struc- tures in the binary alloy has been reported  [20–22] . The experimental alloy in this work can be considered as an Ni–Cr-based alloy according to its contents (66.21 Ni–26.26Cr–6.71W–0.82Mo in at.%), and tungsten as a ternary element plays an important role in the SRO–LRO transformation. Although tungsten and molyb- denum are both in the VI B group of the periodic table of elements, they exert opposite eﬀects on the superlattices. The gradual substitution of Cr by Mo can have a positive inﬂuence on the stability of the Pt 2 Mo-type superlattice when compared to W, which causes a destabilization of the ordered phase with this type of structure. Regarding the eﬀects of Mo and W on the stability of the Pt 2 Mo-type superlattice, 2 wt.% Mo has a similar eﬀect to 1 wt.% W  [23] . As a rule of thumb, Ni 2 Cr with a Pt 2 Mo-type structure is a stable and equilibrium phase in the Ni–Cr system, and it decomposes at about 600   C. Pai et al.  [20]  found that the solvus temperature of the Ni 2 (Cr,Mo) phase in Ni 0.67 Cr 0.33– x Mo x  alloys varies from 627   C (for x  = 0) to 773   C (for x  = 0.166). Nevertheless, the decomposition tempera- ture of Ni 2 (Cr,W,Mo) superlattice determined by Rtishchev et al. [16] in Ni–18.5Cr–5.8 W–4.5Mo (in wt.%) superalloy is 565   C, which is much lower than that of Ni 2 (Cr,Mo). The results of contrast analysis indi- cate that tungsten can reduce the solvus temperature of Pt 2 Mo-type superlattice despite the presence of molyb- denum. Due to the high content of tungsten in the Ni–Cr–W alloy, this ordered phase cannot precipitate at 700   C. On the other hand, a change in the overall free electron to atom ratio of the alloy resulting from tungsten addition appears to have a positive inﬂuence on the stability of the DO 22  superlattice with respect to the Pt 2 Mo-type superlattice. Although the atomic ratio of Ni/M (M = Cr,W,Mo) is almost 2/1 in the Ni–Cr–W alloy, it is DO 22  superlattice, rather than the expected Pt 2 Mo-type superlattice, that is observed dur- ing the early stages of thermal ageing. This phenomenon indicates that the precipitation condition of DO 22  super- lattice is not rigorous and that the composition range of DO 22  superlattice is relatively wide.  

As can be seen in  Figure 3 (a), the DO 22  superlattice assumes the morphology of nanosized sphere particles with an average size of 4–6 nm, which is slightly larger than in  Figure 2 (b). Also, it can be observed that, as the ageing time is extended from 5 h (Fig. 2(c) and (d)) to 40 h (Fig. 3(c) and (d)), the diﬀraction intensity of the DO 22  superlattice becomes stronger. The changes in particle size and diﬀraction intensity illustrate that the SRO–LRO transformation occurs throughout the whole ageing process and that the precipitates grow ex- tremely slowly. The diﬀusion kinetics in the temperature range where the formation of the DO 22  superlattice takes place is slow due to the high content tungsten (large atomic radius and heavy atomic weight) in the Ni–Cr–W alloy. The change in superlattice morphology is insigniﬁcant, demonstrating its relatively high thermal stability.  Figure 3 (b) presents the corresponding EDS spectra, which conﬁrm the composition of the DO 22 superlattice. Because the EDS signal must integrate the volume fraction of the matrix around the ﬁne particles, it is diﬃcult to determine the exact composition of the DO 22  superlattice. In order to minimize any inaccuracy, the EDS analysis should be conducted several times to compare the variations in composition in diﬀerent re- gions, as marked in  Figure 3 (a). Although the volume fraction of the superlattice in each area is diﬀerent, these EDS results are similar indicating that the composition of the DO 22  superlattice may be similar to that of the matrix. In a future publication we will present a detailed analysis on the chemical composition of the precipitates using such analytical methods as high-angle annular dark ﬁeld scanning transmission electron microscopy.  

The transformation from SRO to LRO is also reﬂected in the changes in mechanical properties of the alloy (e.g. microhardness) as a function of ageing time. Figure 4  shows the variation in Vickers microhardness of the Ni–Cr–W alloy as the ageing time is extended from 0 h (as-quenched condition) to 40 h. A great increase in microhardness is observed over the value for the as-quenched condition and a continual upward trend is noticed on ageing for about 40 h followed by a levelling oﬀ, presumably as the ordering reaction approaches completion. In contrast, because the kinetics of precipitate growth is fast when the mechanism is nucleation and growth, the hardness rises quite rapidly and then drops as the precipitates quickly overage  [18] . The two completely diﬀerent rusults prove indirectly that the DO 22  superlattice precipitates in the Ni–Cr–W alloy through continuous ordering.  

In conclusion, it is demonstrated that a nanosized DO 22  superlattice can precipitate in the Ni–Cr–W alloy by solid solution and ageing treatment at 700   C. The presence of tungsten in the Ni–Cr–W alloy stabilizes the DO 22  superlattices, which are transient metastable phases in Ni–Cr–Mo alloy. In the case of the Ni–Cr–W alloy, the increase in microhardness is signiﬁcant because of the ﬁne, spherical and dispersed precipitates with a DO 22  structure.
