Many organic carboxylic acids used as nonsteroidal anti- inflammatory drugs have relatively low intrinsic solubilities in water. While their solubility may not be sufficiently low to affect the bio availability of the tablet dosage forms,solubility maybe a limiting factor in the development of alternate dosage forms or delivery systems.A solution formulation for intravenous administration,for example,may require a highly water-soluble form of the drug.A suspension for oral delivery may require a relatively insoluble, tasteless form of the drug.A sustained-release preparation may require a derivative in which the physicochemical properties have been even more carefully designed. A simple, almost trivial, means ofendowing a parent drug having ionizable functional groups with unique properties in order to overcome some undesirable feature of the parent drug is through salt forma- tion. In this sense, salts can be viewed as prodrugs of the parent compound.1  

In this report, the solubility behavior of the nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory agent 2-(2-fluoro-4-biphenylyl)propionic acid(flurbiprofen)(1)hasbeenexamined.Severalmetaland organicaminesaltsofflurbiprofenweresynthesized,and their solubility behavior was also investigated.Predictive relationships which would enable the pharmaceutical chem- ist to estimate the aqueous solubility of salts from physico chemical properties of thecounterions are virtually none x is- tent.Since such relationships would have obvious utility,an attemptwasmadeinthisstudytoidentifysomeofthe parameters which are important in determining relative salt solubilities.

Infuence of Amine Structure on' Ksp—-The solubility or Kspof a salt should,it would seem,depend on the crystalline forces present in the crystal lattice and on the affinityof water for the dissolving species. Therefore, an attempt was made to rationalize the observed Kspvalues in terms of parameters reflecting these forces. The standard free energy of a solution of a 1:1 salt is related to the Ksp by AGsol 1二 RT·ln Ksp, where AGsol is also related to the sum of the energies for two processes:(a)the removal of ions from the crystallattice and (b) transfer of the ions to water.Unfortu- nately,measuring these two steps independently would re- quire a heroic effort.  

For nonelectrolytes,△Ggois frequently expressed as a sum of terms representing(a)the free energy of solution in an ideal solvent (the supercooled melt of the solute)and (b) transfer of the solute from an ideal solvent to water.This approach,as applied by Yalkowsky et al., with two additional and somewhat tenuous assumptions(i.e.,thatoctanolis equivalent to the supercooled melt of any solute and that entropiesoffusionareconstantwithinarelatedseriesof solutes),leads to a predicted linear relationship between the log of the water solubility and the melting point and log octanol-water partition coefficient of the solute.14,15  

Because data were available for such a correlation,a multiple linear regression analysis was carried out to evalu- ate the influence of melting point and cation hydrophilicity on the Ks. values of the amine salts. Interpretation of the relationships observed in salt so lub i- litiesis complicated by thelackof a suitable reference state for separately measuring the energies ofremoval ofionsfrom the crystal lattice and the energies of solvation by water.For nonelectrolytes,inert hydrocarbon has been recommended as such a reference state since differences in so lub ili ties of structurally similar solutes in a hydrocarbon solvent may be largely attributable to differences in crystalline energies.17,18 Thesupercooledmeltofasaltisnotanappropriatereference for comparing the solubilities of salts in which the counterion is varied, since ion solvation in the supercooled melt may depend on the hydrophilic nature of the counterion. There- fore, the strong relationship seen between melting point and log Ksp and the apparent lack of a correlation between log K and counterion hydrophilicity should not be taken as evi- dence that the crystalline interactions are the sole determi- nant of relative salt so lub ili ties.  

The lack of a strong correlation between Ksp and counter- ion hydrophilicity conflicts with the intuitively attractive notion that higher salt solubilities can be achieved by select- ing more hydrophilic counterions (as suggested in several papers in the literaturei9). As demonstrated in a previous study of prodrugs of prostaglandins,2 suchargumentsne- glect the likelihood that interactions in the crystal become stronger as the soluteor salt-forming species are made increasinglypolar.
