The objective of this study was to examine the stress relaxation response and corresponding changes in microstructure of single crystal superalloys. The effects of temperature and time on stress relaxation response were examined for single crystal superalloys CMSX-4 and EPM-102. Stress – strain rate data from relaxation tests was in good agreement with constant load tests; it was further re ﬁ ned by testing samples from the same casting, and by eliminating the effects of primary creep in the stress relaxation test. From these tests, it was determined that EPM-102 had higher resistance to stress relaxation than CMSX-4 at and above 982  ° C. Additionally, time-dependent strain recovery ( “ viscoelasticity ” ) was observed after unloading during these tests. The single crystal alloys displayed directional coarsening after stress re- laxation testing at high temperatures.

# 1. Introduction  

High temperature creep testing and stress relaxation testing are closely related experimental approaches that measure an alloy's time dependent deformation behavior. The stress – strain – time path is different for stress relaxation tests, in which a strain rather than creep stress is applied and then held constant. Stress re- laxation tests have been explored as an ef ﬁ cient means for gath- ering rate data over several decades in strain rate  [1 – 3] . It has been observed in these studies that strain rate-stress data from stress relaxation tests can be compared to that from creep tests. Lack of exact agreement has been attributed to at least two factors. First, the secondary creep rate in a creep test is typically measured after several tens to thousands of hours duration, (depending on the applied stress), whereas the decades of strain rate data from a stress relaxation test are typically obtained in 24 h or less. This difference implies that the early portions (at least) of the stress relaxation test include effects of primary creep, whereas the creep test is measuring a secondary creep rate. Second, the different test durations allow for different microstructural evolution, which can also affect the measured strain rates.  

Compared to creep test data, stress relaxation data for super- alloys is sparse  [1 , 2 , 8 – 10]  and especially rare for single crystal alloys  [2 , 9 , 10] . Test durations at constant strain beyond 24 h are essential for a higher  ﬁ delity design of the high temperature seals, but such data are absent. Observations of microstructural changes during stress relaxation testing are similarly uncommon. Micro- structural evolution is especially important for these high tem- perature applications, where  γ ʹ  rafting is well characterized in creep testing  [11 – 15]  above the  ﬁ nal aging temperature of these alloys, but has yet to be reported in any stress relaxation testing at these temperatures. Thus, the objectives of this study were to explore the stress relaxation responses and corresponding changes in microstructure of single crystal superalloys tested at high temperatures. The effects of temperature and time on stress re- laxation were examined for two advanced alloys. Several genera- tions of single crystal superalloys have been developed over the years, having increase levels of Re and corresponding creep re- sistance  [16] . The 2nd generation alloy CMSX-4 and the 4th gen- eration alloy EPM-102 were chosen because of the interest in determining the temperature capability of high temperature seals based on these superalloy springs. A few additional tests were performed on a  ﬁ rst generation alloy NASAIR 100, to generate an exact comparison of the two test types (creep and relaxation) from specimens taken from a single casting.  

Stress relaxation testing is also considered an essential cali- bration test for advanced deformation models that couple creep and plasticity  [4 , 5] . However, the prime motivation for the tests in the current study was the need to increase the temperature

# 2. Materials and test procedures  

The compositions in weight percent of the two tested single crystal blade superalloys are listed in  Table 1 . CMSX-4 is a 2nd generation single crystal superalloy  [17]  which has often been used for many turbine airfoil applications. It has a modest Re content of 3.0% and displays good creep resistance at a relatively low measured density of 8.69 g/cm 3 . EPM-102 is a 4th generation superalloy  [16]  having a higher Re content of 6% and higher creep resistance, albeit at a higher density of 9.16 g/cm 3 .  

These superalloys were produced as rectangular slabs using standard single crystal casting practices. The slabs each had a nominal width of 5 cm and length of 15 cm, and thicknesses of 0.6 – 1.3 cm. CMSX-4 slabs were homogenized and solution heat treated to about 1300  ° C, then gas fan quenched. EPM-102 slabs were homogenized and solution heat treated to 1305  ° C, then gas fan quenched. Both materials were then given a subsequent heat treatment of 1141  ° C for 6 h plus 871  ° C for 20 h. Slabs were then macro-etched and X-rayed, to insure they contained no high angle grain boundaries. Specimens were machined so as to be oriented within 10 °  of the [001] crystallographic direction.  

Additionally, two con ﬁ rmation tests were performed on com- panion specimens of a  ﬁ rst generation single crystal superalloy NASAIR 100, of the same material composition and casting mold which had been extensively characterized for creep response and associated directional coarsening in references  [12 , 18] .  

Tensile stress relaxation tests were performed at temperatures of 870 – 1093  ° C on specimens having nominal gage diameters of 3.2 mm, with a gage length of 21 mm. They were performed in an electro-mechanical universal testing machine (Instru-Met Corp.), using a clamshell resistance heating furnace (Applied Testing Systems) and a contacting axial extensometer (MTS Systems Model 632.53E-14) measuring strain over a gage length of 12.7 mm. These tests were performed in general accordance with elevated temperature tensile testing speci ﬁ cation ASTM E21-09. However, tests initiated with strain ramped at the speci ﬁ ed strain rate of 8.3    10  5   1/s were interrupted at a strain corresponding to 0.85\* yield strength at 870  ° C, 983  ° C, and 1093  ° C for each alloy, and held there with strain held constant for 100 h to measure relaxation of stress as a function of time. Supplementary tests of EPM-102 were also performed at lower percentages of yield strength and for different test times. A few tests were also per- formed to examine a lesser known behavior  [4 , 5]  of time-depen- dent strain recovery at nominally zero stress. In these tests, the specimen was given a standard stress relaxation period of 100 h, and then unloaded to a very small load suf ﬁ cient to maintain a tight load train and the strain recovery was monitored while held

# 3. Results and discussion  

# 3.1. Mechanical Response  

Results of initial screening tests comparing stress relaxation at 0.85 yield strength are shown as stress versus time in  Fig. 2 . The alloys had similar responses at all three temperatures, displaying rapid initial relaxation of stress within the  ﬁ rst hour as shown in Fig. 2 a, followed by more gradual, slower relaxation. This initial rapid relaxation could be examined more closely as a function of log(time),  Fig. 2 b. The initial stress attained by EPM-102 was higher than that for CMSX-4 at each temperature, due to its higher yield strength at each temperature. This improvement in stress capability for EPM-102 was eliminated with increasing time at constant strain for 870  ° C, but remained at 100 h for 982  ° C and 1093  ° C.  

As the specimen was held at constant applied strain, inelastic strain accumulated that was proportional to relaxation in stress and inversely proportional to the elastic modulus. The rate of stress relaxation can then be used to determine an inelastic strain rate, by dividing by the elastic modulus. This allowed for a com- parison of this strain rate as a function of applied stress for all these tests, as shown in  Fig. 3 . Such plots have sometimes been used to estimate steady state or minimum creep rates in creep tests at constant applied stress  [1 , 2 , 3] . Power law and power law breakdown equations were used to model this response, and gave satisfactory regression results as indicated by the coef ﬁ cient of determination ( R 2 ). EPM-102 displayed more curvature than CMSX-4 in  Fig. 3  at 982  ° C and 1093  ° C. This was due to slower initial stress relaxation at high stresses for EPM-102 than for CMSX-4, evident in  Fig. 2 . Using either equation, the stress ex- ponents for CMSX-4 and EPM-102 were comparable at 871  ° C and 982  ° C. However, at 1093  ° C the exponents of EPM-102 were about 50% lower for than those for CMSX-4, re ﬂ ecting signi ﬁ cantly lower rates of stress relaxation at high applied stresses. This con ﬁ rmed that the improved creep resistance of EPM-102 did translate to improved resistance to stress relaxation at high temperatures over 2nd generation single crystal superalloys such as CMSX-4.  

Supplementary tests were performed on EPM-102 to examine the effects of lower applied strains and longer times on stress relaxation response at each of the test temperatures. Stress is compared versus linear and log (time) in  Fig. 4 , while strain rate versus stress is compared in  Fig. 5 . The tests at lower applied strains did not attain the initial high stresses of those at high strains, as all stress – strain curves remained linear with a slope equal to the modulus. Yet, strain rates were comparable at equivalent stresses. Tests at 982  ° C continued beyond 100 h up to 300 h exhibited further relaxation of stress, indicating a true sa- turation stress had not yet been attained. A comparison of the  ﬁ nal relaxation stresses attained for EPM-102 at varied times and ap- plied strains is shown in  Fig. 6 . While initial stress did signi ﬁ cantly increase with increasing applied strain, stress after relaxation times of 10 h or more did not signi ﬁ cantly vary with applied strain. Duplicated strain, stress, and time points attained in multiple tests were comparable, indicating good reproducibility.  

At the conclusion of some tests, stress was reduced to a con- stant value of 3 MPa, and strain was then monitored for 24 h. This is illustrated for a typical stress – strain path shown in  Fig. 1 . Strain recovery is shown as a function of time for all these tests in  Fig. 7 . Signi ﬁ cant strain was recovered during this period, for all three test temperatures. The limited tests suggested this varied with the prior strain maintained during stress relaxation, with higher maintained strain giving more subsequent strain recovery at 3 MPa. This represents viscoelastic response analogous to that observed for polycrystalline titanium alloys and powder me- tallurgy disk superalloys  [4 , 5] , and single crystal superalloy CMSX- 10  [19] . Many additional, carefully formulated tests described in these references would be necessary to properly calibrate a model which can fully describe such behavior. However, these results do indicate that some deformation generated during stress relaxation can be recovered in single crystal superalloys. This indicates that the mechanism for viscoelasticity here is dislocation motion,

Substantial constant strain, stress relaxation and constant load, creep test data exists for CMSX-4, thus allowing comparison to the current stress relaxation results, as shown in  Fig. 8 . Published data was generated at test temperatures often different from those used here, but still allowed comparison of trends. The present data agree well with stress relaxation test results previously reported for CMSX-4 at 900  ° C  [2] , with a quite comparable stress exponent and similar creep rates to that observed here at 871  ° C. The data generated from the present stress relaxation tests agreed sur- prisingly well with stress – strain rate creep test results reported for CMSX-4 by Reed at 850  ° C to 1100  ° C  [15] , Schneider at 950  ° C  [20] , Peng at 980  ° C  [21] , and Svodoba at 1000  ° C ref.  [22] . However, the data at 950  ° C published by MacLachlan did not agree as well, having strain rates several orders of magnitude higher  [23] . Power law constants and stress exponents for each data set were calcu- lated by linear regression and are compared in  Tables 2  and  3 . Overall, the general agreement of power law stress exponents between short duration stress relaxation and longer duration creep tests suggests the short duration stress relaxation tests can be useful for screening single crystal superalloy materials for high temperature seal applications. These power law exponents are shown versus temperature in  Fig. 9 .  

Since CMSX-4 data were collected using different batches of material reported to be subjected to the same heat treatment temperatures and times as for the material tested here, a cer- tain scatter in the data is to be expected and, for some of the creep experiments, data was sparse for some test tempera- tures. Despite this, there is considerable agreement between these separate data sets. The exponents in stress relaxation and creep tests were signi ﬁ cantly lower at 950 – 1093  ° C in com- parison to those at 871 – 900  ° C. This reduction will be shown to also be correlated in stress relaxation tests with directional coarsening of the  γ ʹ  precipitates.  

In order to eliminate the effects of batch-to-batch variations, stress relaxation testing and creep testing of superalloys were compared using samples from a single casting of NASAIR 100. This ﬁ rst-generation superalloy was chosen because a large constant- load data set already exists  [12] , and a few stress relaxation tests were performed from some test bars that remained from the older work. In contrast to the other superalloys tested here, NASAIR 100 exhibits an unusually high primary creep response, which is at- tributed to the very rapid rafting in this alloy. Thus two different loading histories were adopted to examine this effect on stress relaxation. As shown in  Fig. 10 , one stress relaxation test was performed in the conventional mode at a constant strain of 0.27%. In a second test, a constant stress of 207 MPa was  ﬁ rst applied for 20 h, which included the faster primary creep-time response and induced rafting  [18] . This  “ pre-rafted ”  specimen was then sub- jected to stress relaxation at a strain of 0.27%. The improvement in stress relaxation resistance observed after pre-rafting was re- markable: the pre-rafted sample was able to maintain a stress

The strain rate versus stress relaxation responses of these two tests are compared in  Fig. 11 . The non-linear response observed in the standard stress relaxation test was eliminated with prior pri- mary creep. Strain rates for a given stress were reduced by one to two orders of magnitude after the prior creep, giving greatly im- proved stress relaxation capability. Furthermore, the agreement between the constant load and stress relaxation data was ex- cellent, but only in the case with prior primary creep exposure. This shows that primary creep mechanisms must also be ac- counted for in the analysis of stress relaxation data. Another aspect of this experiment is that prior creep (i.e., pre-rafting) may be a promising path to improve stress relaxation resistance for en- gineering applications such as the high temperature seals.  

# 4. Microstructural evolution during stress relaxation  

In order to understand the microstructural changes that result in the previously described mechanical behavior, the morphology of  γ ' precipitates was also explored in this study. While it is re- cognized that dislocation structures would also be evolving during these tests, evaluations of this evolution were reserved for future work. Typical microstructures of the fully heat treated alloys be- fore testing are shown in  Fig. 12 . These images were analyzed by image analysis software (SigmaScan Pro5), which indicated that each superalloy had about 58 area % of cuboidal  γ ʹ . Their mean edge width was 0.434  μ m with a 95% con ﬁ dence interval of 7 0.038  μ  μ m  7 0.023  μ m for EPM-102.  

Images of typical  γ ʹ  precipitates after stress relaxation at 871  ° C are compared in  Fig. 13 . The cuboidal  γ ʹ  precipitate morphology is still prevalent in both alloys. However, after stress relaxation at 982  ° C directional coarsening oriented normal to the stress axis is apparent, as shown in  Fig. 14 . So as in creep tests, directional coarsening was encouraged in stress relaxation tests at tempera- tures above the  ﬁ nal aging heat treatment temperature. The  γ ʹ precipitate raft thickness and  γ  channels are both smaller for EPM- 102 than for CMSX-4, which re ﬂ ect the smaller initial cuboid size in EPM-102. The extent of rafting at 982  ° C was also slightly more rapid for CMSX-4, which is presumably the result of its lower Re content compared to EPM-102. It is interesting to note that in EPM-102, increasing the stress relaxation exposure from 100 h up to 370 h did not appreciably increase the extent of rafting,  Fig. 14 c. This was also the case for lower applied strains,  Fig. 14 d. Unlike creep tests, in stress relaxation the stress driving force for direc- tional coarsening and inelastic strain accumulation decreases with time. Perhaps the stress had relaxed to such a low level that both the rafting and creep deformation rates had become exceedingly slow.

(3) All three superalloys displayed directional coarsening during stress relaxation at test temperatures signi ﬁ cantly higher than the  ﬁ nal aging heat treatment temperature, as also observed in the literature for creep tests.  

(4) Additional experiments showed that viscoelastic response was also inherent in these single crystals. Similar observations on polycrystalline superalloys and Ti alloys show that this is a general behavior and lends credence to deformation models that incorporate this effect. This recovery of deformation generated during stress relaxation is also a potentially useful response for high temperature spring and seal applications.

 (5) The potential to improve stress relaxation resistance by prior creep exposure was demonstrated in NASAIR 100.  

(6) This study points to avenues for continued research in the future. In particular it would be valuable to evaluate a wider range of alloys and crystal orientations to more completely compare stress relaxation to creep testing. The effects of multi- axial loading on stress relaxation resistance also would need to evaluated, to support design for applications having other than uniaxial loading. The bene ﬁ cial effects of pre-rafting on stress relaxation should also be examined for other alloys and loading conditions. Finally, a more thorough examination of coupled creep-viscoelasticity models appears to be warranted.

 (7) Single crystal superalloys show promise for applications as high temperature seals. However, as for polycrystalline su- peralloys, at high applied strains the stress continues to relax even after 100 h. Therefore, applications could require allow- ance for a continuous stress – time relationship rather than a saturation stress, which could take advantage of the creep- viscoelastic responses observed here. Microstructural coarsening appears to stabilize for the present test conditions, but this will also need further consideration concerning effects of coarsening on strength and other properties relevant to speci ﬁ c applications.
