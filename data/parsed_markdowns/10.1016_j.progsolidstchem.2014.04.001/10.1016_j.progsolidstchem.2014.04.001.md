This review covers various aspects of electrolyte investigations. The  ﬁ rst section reports on synthesis and characterization of lithium salts and ionic liquids, including some unpublished recent work. The next part is devoted to transference number measurements of lithium ions. It contains recently published work and new results on this rarely investigated but important topic. Studies of anodic aluminum dissolution with our novel fast impedance scanning electrochemical quartz microbalance (FIS-EQCM) follow next. After a short introduction to the method, some recently published results are reviewed along with some yet unpublished material. We have also shown that the solubility of solids and gases in liquids can be measured with this equipment, including the solubility of lithium salts in ionic liquids. First re- sults of FIS-EQCM studies show that electroplating and corrosion of lithium and subsequent dissolution of the SEI can be studied as well. The last parts of this manuscript are dedicated to the investigation of miscellaneous topics that are of interest for studies of electrolytes for LIBs.

# 1. Introduction  

Better batteries with reduced cost and increased performance and lifetime are urgently needed for electric vehicles and stationary en- ergy storage systems. Lithium ion batteries (LIBs) for automotive and stationary application are the frontrunner for both applications regarding performance and commercial success; however, im- provements are still necessary  [1 e 6] . Numerous possible improve- ments have been proposed  [7 e 11] . Currently, even several types of batteries are considered for grid applications including sodium e sulfur batteries, redox- ﬂ ow batteries, and lithium ion batteries  [12] .  

The electrolyte is the key for the performance of a lithium ion cell  [13] . Current LIB electrolytes contain solvents, typically a blend of organic carbonates, and at least one lithium salt, typically LiPF 6 , and several additives  [14 e 17] . Ionic liquids (ILs) or blends of ILs and solvents might be used as well  [18] .  

In the electrolyte, charge transport takes place by ions via migration and diffusion. The conductivity of the electrolyte de- termines the cell resistance and hence maximum power capability and generation of Joule ’ s heat. To understand the charge transport in electrolytes, the charge fraction transported by cations and an- ions should be known. A measure for this ratio is the transference number. A high lithium ion transference number signi ﬁ cantly re- duces the detrimental effects of concentration polarization in LIBs.  

Electrolytes are decomposed at the electrodes to form passiv- ating lithium ion conducting  ﬁ lms, e.g. the well-known but still poorly understood solid electrolyte interphase (SEI)  [19,20]  on graphite and lithium anodes or the decomposition products on high voltage cathodes. Aluminum current collectors are protected from anodic dissolution at high potentials by electrolyte decom- position products as well. To sum up, the electrolyte plays a crucial role for the performance of a LIB cell.  

# 2. Synthesis of novel lithium borates and ionic liquids  

The commonly used lithium salt for lithium ion cells, LiPF 6 , offers well-balanced properties in terms of ionic mobility, dissociation, and conductivity  [13,21] . However this salt has also several drawbacks, e.g.low thermal stability and high sensitivity towards hydrolysis even with traces of moisture, forming HF  [13] . Fluoride forms highly insoluble lithium  ﬂ uoride (LiF) and HF prevents the use of cheap and environmentally benign cathode materials. LiBF 4  surpasses LiPF 6  in terms of thermal and hydrolytic stability and its BF    anion shows the highest ion mobility in various non-aqueous systems among com- mon anions of useful lithium salts. However, this salt suffers from poorion dissociation and resulting low ionic conductivity  [13,21] . The two anions PF    and BF    are both based on the Lewis acids PF 5  and BF 3 which may also initiate polymerization of solvents at elevated tem- peratures. Therefore, the search for new thermally more stable salts which shouldn ’ t produce HF has been initiated and many new che- latoborates and some chela to phosphates have been synthesized and characterized at the University of Regensburg in the past  [22 e 31] .

We investigated various effects including increasing  ﬂ uorination of the anion, the stability and yield of salts with 5- to 7-membered rings attached to boron, and various acids as starting materials for bis-chelatoborates  [13] .  

Numerous papers  [32 e 38]  related to the synthesis and behavior of lithium chelatoborates have been published since our  ﬁ rst paper in 1995. The most important development was the synthesis of lithium bis(oxalato)borate (LiBOB)  [39 e 41] , a salt that has been patented by Chemetall, Frankfurt. Due to the fact that no electrolyte salt performs as well-balanced as LiPF 6  does up to now, some modi ﬁ cations of the salts ’  anions were done as well in order to improve the salts ’  properties in particular  [42 e 51] .  

We and others  [13]  also tried to synthesize and investigate borate anions based on monodentate ligands instead of bidentate ligand despite their lower thermal stability  [22] . During our research we synthesized and investigated many novel lithium salts [13]  based on weakly coordinating anions (WCAs)  [39,52,53] , some are shown in  Table 1 . Due to their weakly coordinating anions, the lithium salts ’  solubility in aprotic dipolar solvents was increased up to more than 1 mol kg  1   solvent. This is a consequence of the large anionic radius and the charge delocalization over the whole anion [13] . Despite of their large radii, WCAs lead to increased conduc- tivities due to reduced ion pair formation. Furthermore, the charge delocalization has a favorable effect on electrochemical stability, leading to higher oxidation potentials  [13,24] . This follows e.g. from semi-empirical quantum-mechanical calculations which showed that the highest occupied molecule orbital (HOMO) energies and the oxidation potentials are correlated linearly  [13,54,55] . However, with successive  ﬂ uorination of the anion ’ s ligand, increasing hy- drolysis rate constants are observed, indicating an increasing ability of the more electron-withdrawing  ﬂ uorinated ligands to leave boron  [56] .  

Further electrolyte performance improvements were achieved by reducing the symmetry of the anion. Salts based on polyatomic anions of reduced symmetry are known to show higher solubility in many solvents than salts based on symmetrical ions  [57] . The most prominent example is the di ﬂ uoro(oxalato)borate (DFOB) anion. Various syntheses procedures for DFOB salts succeeded and are reported in literature  [41,58 e 61] . In one of the early reports, a successful DFOB preparation was achieved in a two-step process [60] . A correlation between low melting point and low symmetry is often observed for ionic liquids (ILs). # 4. Transference number measurements  

Generally the characterization of a new electrolyte comprises the determination of bulk properties such as viscosity, conductivity and mobility of electrolyte solutions, thermal and electrochemical stability and sometimes investigation of intrinsic processes such as ion e ion and ion e solvent interactions  [13,90 e 93] .  

A full characterization of a battery electrolyte including modeling of cell properties  [13,94]  requires the knowledge of the transference numbers of ions, as well. Concentration gradients of the salt built up during current  ﬂ ow can result in precipitation of the dissolved salt at the anode and depletion at the cathode at high (discharge) currents. This may cause serious cell failure. A high lithium ion transference number signi ﬁ cantly reduce the effects of concentration polarization  [95] . The importance of lithium ion transference numbers for lithium ion battery electrolytes resulted in efforts to develop rapid and reliable measurement methods  [96 e 99] .  

Transference numbers for various electrolytes have been measured at various very pure electrolytes based on LiPF 6 , LiBF 4 , and LiDFOB dissolved in organic carbonates  [100,101] . Values and ﬁ gures for LiBF 4  in propylene carbonate (PC) are published with this paper, see  Table 5 . Various methods were used to compare their advantages and disadvantages and to gain a deeper insight in the concentration dependence of transference numbers. We used: the potentiostatic polarization method  [102] , the galvanostatic polari- zation method  [103] , measurements of electromotive force  [104] and measurement of diffusion coef ﬁ cients via pfg-NMR  [105,106] . Cell types and measurement techniques are described in detail in Refs.  [62,100,101] .

# 5. FIS-EQCM-measurements  

# 5.1. Introduction  

The current collectors aluminum (cathode) and copper (anode) are important parts of LIBs. If an electrolyte is corrosive for a current collector, it cannot be used in LIBs. A novel type of an electro- chemical quartz crystal microbalance was used to get information on corrosion of aluminum in various typical LIB-electrolytes.  

The fast impedance-scanning electrochemical quartz crystal microbalance (FIS-EQCM) is an advanced derivative of the well- known hybrid of standard QCM and electrochemical instrumenta- tion. The functional principle of the QCM is assumed to be known to the reader [116,117].  

Standard principles such as the Sauerbrey equation  [118] , equation  (19) , allows conclusions to be made about changes of layer thicknesses and deposited or withdrawn mass  e  assuming rigidly deposited layers. Change of resonance frequency  D f  correlates directly with the change of mass  D m .  S f  is a constant re ﬂ ecting the mass sensitivity.

More advanced models such as developed by Kanazawa et al. [119]  allow further interpretations of visco-dynamic parameters and more. Standard QCMs usually employ some kind of electronic oscillator circuits  [120]  which use a quartz crystal as frequency determining element. A frequency measuring device  [121]  will determine the frequency of the oscillation. These electronic oscil- lator circuits depend mostly on the electronic phase shift behavior of the quartz crystal.  Fig. 6  shows that loading a quartz crystal with some water greatly reduces the dynamics of the phase shift behavior of the quartz crystal by comparing against an air-loaded behavior. Depending on the circuit used, the phase margin can become so small that the electronic oscillator will cease to work. Manual compensation techniques  [122]  can be found in numerous commercial oscillator-based QCM circuits. A variable capacitor in- troduces extra phase shift. The user must set this capacitor manually before starting a measurement. If the loading of a quartz crystal changes during an electrochemical deposition process there is a good possibility that a conventional oscillator circuit will stop to work during an ongoing experiment.

Another approach to determine loading parameters of a quartz crystal is to use an electric impedance scanner or a network analyzer  [123]  such as the Solartron 1260. As no active oscillator circuits are involved, this passive method will always deliver a result. However, commercially available impedance scanners are very slow. The Solartron 1260 takes ten to twenty seconds  [124]  to take an entire and coarse spectrum. This is too slow for a lot of electrochemical applications.  

Our approach  [124 e 126]  was to use a fast impedance-scanning technique to overcome speed limitations of general impedance scanning and reliability issues of most oscillator circuits.  

Compared to general impedance scanning, the electrical equivalent circuit diagram of a quartz crystal  [127]  is well known and understood. The parameters change but not their amount or composition. Therefore, one can simplify the detection equipment quite a bit allowing for much higher speeds  [124] . The major dif- ference is not to measure voltage and current signal as function of time in order to calculate the complex impedance value. Instead, we built a capacitive voltage divider as shown in  Fig. 7  and measured the frequency-dependent amplitude response across the voltage divider. As the equivalent circuits of the instrument and the crystal are well known, the amplitude information can be derived mathematically to calculate indirectly the impedance as well as parallel and series resonance frequencies  [124] .

# 5.3. Solubility measurements with the FIS-QCM  

The solubility of any compound in another compound is a parameter which is of great interest  [151]  regarding chemical processes like synthesis and puri ﬁ cation processes  [59] , in biology [152,153] , or in medicine  [154] . These values can be achieved and modeled from many theories, e.g. from theoretical chemistry [155,156] , calculations, or classical thermodynamics  [157] . Never- theless, most of the experimental approaches to get valid solubility data make use of complex, fragile experimental procedures, which are sensitive to disturbances and errors  [158] . In this section we review two recently developed methods based on the FIS-QCM. Solubility data of solids in liquids as well as gas solubility data in hyper-branched polymers  [124]  or adsorbents boiling at high temperatures can be obtained. Results from measurements of gas solubility data with a FIS-QCM are presented here for the  ﬁ rst time.

# 6.2. SEI investigations in LiBOB and LiBF 4  based electrolyte blends  

To gain a better understanding of the characteristics of the su- perior SEI which is built in LiDFOB-based electrolytes, we per- formed a comparative study of LiBOB and LiBF 4  electrolyte blends and the single salt electrolytes LiBOB, LiBF 4 , and LiDFOB in EC:DEC (3:7, by wt.)  [191] . All of the tested blend electrolytes showed better cycling behavior than the pure LiBOB and LiDFOB electrolytes in the dissolution rate (D-rate) test (see  Fig. 15 ).  

Furthermore, cycling performance of the blend electrolytes was improved with increasing LiBF 4  content. It was found through AC impedance spectroscopy  [191]  and X-ray photoelectron spectros- copy (XPS)  [191]  that with increasing LiBF 4  content the character- istics and the composition of the SEI of the blend electrolytes became more and more similar with the SEI of the LiDFOB-based electrolyte. Therefore, LiBOB/LiBF 4  blend electrolytes could be also a promising liquid electrolyte system for secondary lithium metal based electrolytes as they show similar characteristics to the LiDFOB-based electrolyte.  

# 6.3. Corrosion studies of electroplated lithium metal by the electrochemical FIS-EQCM  

The corrosion behavior of deposited lithium on a metal sub- strate showed a completely different corrosion mechanism for the LiDFOB-based electrolyte (see  Fig. 16 ) than for the LiPF 6 -based electrolyte (see  Fig. 17 ).  

Furthermore, the potential of the bare copper foil was  ﬁ rst reached after 86 h with the LiPF 6 -based electrolyte and only after 272 h with the LiDFOB-based electrolyte. The results of the novel corrosion experiment with the electrochemical fast impedance scanning quartz crystal microbalance (FIS-EQCM) are able to con ﬁ rm that the LiDFOB-based electrolyte not only creates a thinner SEI than the LiPF 6 -based electrolyte. It can be also shown that the SEI growth during the corrosion process of deposited lithium is not as fast as with the LiPF 6 -based electrolyte  [192] . The observed mass decrease of the quartz sensor of the LiDFOB-based electrolyte is not smooth but contains a series of abrupt mass ﬂ uctuations in contrast to that of the LiPF 6 -based electrolyte (see Figs. 16 and 17 ). After each slow decrease of mass, a rather fast increase of mass is observed several times (see  Fig. 16 ). The slow mass decrease can be attributed to a consolidation process of the SEI or to the partial dissolution of the SEI leaving  ﬁ nally lithium metal unprotected. Hence a fast  ﬁ lm formation sets in which leads to the observed fast mass increases.  

# 7. Miscellaneous  

In this section, some further related topics are brie ﬂ y mentioned which have been addressed over the last years but which are a bit out of the main focus for this manuscript. During our studies we also discussed reference electrodes suitable for electrolytes for lithium ion cells. Generally, for such studies lithium pseudo- reference electrodes are used. However, a better and real refer- ence electrode of second kind should have a constant lithium ion concentration in the direct vicinity of the electrode and a potential most preferably within the electrochemical stability region of the electrolyte to reduce corrosion effects. Finally, our choice was a Li 2 Sn 5 /Sn/lithium salt solution that is known to have the highest electrode potential at the lowest lithium content. The appertaining reversible redox-reaction at constant potential is:

It was prepared by plating lithium onto tin metal at 680 mV vs. Li/Li þ   during 20 h. The process was studied chrono am pe rome tri- cally. A constant potential of about 742 mV was observed in 1 M LiPF 6  in PC which increased to about 745 mV over 8 days. In LiBOB solutions with the same solvent, the potential reached a maximum value of 742.5 e 743.0 mV vs. Li/Li þ   and maintained a constant value of 741.0 e 741.5 mV for 14 days. This value can be considered as the potential of this reference electrode. It is in accordance with a value reported by Winter et al.  [193] .  

Another brie ﬂ y addressed topic is related to semi-empirical quantum-mechanical calculations which can be useful to esti- mate the electrochemical stability of possible new lithium salts. Here, we extended these calculations that were already started in the 1990s. For a short summary see Ref.  [13] . Estimations of ion e ion interactions and ion solvent interactions are running.  

Further, we also worked on oxidation protection of copper foils used as current collectors for the LIB anodes. These foils are pro- tected from oxidation by adsorbed agents. The FIS-EQCM can be used to study thermodynamics and kinetics of adsorption. For studies on a protecting monomer (benzotriazole) and a polymer (poly(vinylimidazole)), see Refs.  [194,195] .  

Finally, it is well known that the liquid range of electrolytes, especially towards the low temperature limit, determines the us- ability of LIBs under such conditions. Therefore, we measured phase diagrams of different electrolytes to determine their solidi- ﬁ cations temperatures and melting points. For the determination of phase diagrams, a multichannel conductivity meter combined with a multichannel thermometer was developed  [196 e 199] . Using a small and stable thermistor with very high accuracy in the sub-mK range, small uncertainties were obtained over a large temperature range  [199] . This makes the instrument also useful for determina- tion of other physicochemical properties such as heat capacities [125] . For studies of battery related materials including solutions and ionic liquids, see Refs.  [197,200] .
