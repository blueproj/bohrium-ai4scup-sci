# Transport of Glyburide by Placental ABC Transporters: Implications in Fetal Drug Exposure

# Abstract  

Much evidence has demonstrated that a number of ATP-binding cassette (ABC) efﬂux transporters including P-glycoprotein (PGP), the mul- tidrug resistance-associated proteins (MRPs) and the breast cancer resistance protein (BCRP) are highly expressed in placental tissues and are believed to profoundly limit the passage of therapeutic or toxic xenobiotics to the fetus. Recent studies indicate that the oral hypoglycemic gly- buride does not cross the human placenta to an appreciable extent. Our objective was to identify placental transporters potentially involved in 3 limiting the transplacental transfer of glyburide to the fetus. Thus, [ H]-glyburide transport was examined in BCRP, PGP, MRP1, MRP2 and MRP3 over-expressing cell lines in the presence or absence of speciﬁc inhibitors. Our results demonstrated signiﬁcant increases in the intracel- 3 lular accumulation of [ H]-glyburide in BCRP and MRP3 over-expressing cells in the presence of the inhibitors novobiocin and indomethacin, 3 respectively. PGP inhibition with verapamil or MRP inhibition with indomethacin did not affect [ H]-glyburide accumulation in the PGP or MRP2 over-expressing cell lines and only limited changes were seen in the MRP1 over-expressing cell line. On the other hand, glyburide was found to signiﬁcantly inhibit MRP1-, MRP2- and MRP3-mediated efﬂux of 5-carbo xy u ores ce in diacetate and PGP-mediated transport of rhodamine 123. Our evidence is the ﬁrst to clearly indicate that glyburide is preferentially transported by BCRP and MRP3.

# 1. Introduction  

The placenta plays an important role as a site for nutrient and waste exchange between the mother and the fetus and may serve as a protective barrier for the fetus against maternal blood-borne toxins. Much evidence has demonstrated that the placenta expresses a number of transporters, some of which function in the removal of endogenous compounds and xeno- biotics. Speciﬁcally, several of the ATP-binding cassette (ABC) transporters, P-glycoprotein (PGP), the multidrug resistance-associated proteins 1 e 3 (MRP1, MRP2, MRP3)  

and the breast cancer resistance protein (BCRP) are highly ex- pressed in placental tissues  [20] . It is believed that these trans- porters may profoundly limit the passage of therapeutic or toxic xenobiotics into the fetus. Fundamental studies in PGP and BCRP deﬁcient mice, demonstrating increased fetal drug accumulation and increased teratogenicity, have clearly established the importance of placental transporters in fetal drug exposure  [9,10] . However, to date the impact of trans- porters on fetal exposure has been studied for only a few xenobiotics.  

Gestational diabetes affects up to 20% of pregnant women. Untreated, it is associated with serious increases in perinatal risks  [1] . The mainstay of management is diet and insulin in- jections. However, many women do not comply with insulin injections and the cost of insulin is prohibitive in many coun- tries. Due to fears of neonatal hypoglycemia, there has been only limited use of oral hypoglycemics in pregnancy. Interest- ingly a drug used to treat gestational diabetes, glyburide (also known as glibenclamide), does not appear to demonstrate sig- niﬁcant maternal to fetal transfer. In vitro studies by Elliot et al.  [28]  demonstrated negligible levels of glyburide in the fetal compartment even when maternal concentrations were eight times greater than therapeutic concentrations. Moreover, offspring of mothers treated with glyburide reportedly do not exhibit hypoglycemia or other adverse events  [1] . Glyburide’s poor maternal to fetal transfer may be due to its high protein binding (99.8%) but it may also be due to interaction of gly- buride with ABC efﬂux transporters expressed within the pla- centa  [11,23] . Recently, we have demonstrated in placental perfusion studies that glyburide is efﬂuxed against its concen- tration gradient from the fetal to the maternal circulation, and that inhibition of PGP activity with verapamil does not impact its transport across the placenta  [23] .  

Presently, there is little knowledge on the mechanisms by which glyburide is transported across epithelial cell mem- branes and there is a lack of clinically-relevant studies regard- ing its transport. Although it has been recently shown by Payen et al.  [18]  that glyburide inhibits MRP1 and MRP2 ac- tivity in human cancer cells, the transport of glyburide itself has not been characterized. Hence, the objective of the present study was to identify placental transporters which are involved in the transport of glyburide in the context of understanding its efﬂux from the fetus to the mother.  

# 2. Methods  

# 2.1. Materials

# 2.2. Tissue culture  

PGP over-expressing cells A2780/Adr (ECACC, UK) and BCRP over-ex- pressing MCF7-MX cells (kindly provided by Dr. E. Schneider, Wadsworth Center Albany, NY) were cultured in RPMI 1640 medium supplemented with 10% heat inactivated fetal calf serum and 50  m g/ml penicillin and strep- tomycin. Transfected MRP1 HeLa cells (kindly provided by Dr. S. Cole, Queen’s University, Kingston, ON) and MRP2- and MRP3-transfected MDCK cells (kindly provided by Dr. P. Borst, The Netherlands Cancer Insti- tute, Amsterdam, The Netherlands) were maintained in Dulbecco’s medium eagle medium (GLUTAMAX) containing 5.55 mM  D -glucose, supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum and 50  m g/ml penicillin and streptomycin. All cells were maintained at 37    C in a humidiﬁed atmosphere containing 5% CO 2 . Over-expression of each transporter was conﬁrmed by RT-PCR  [26]  us- ing previously described methods (data not shown). Over-expression of BCRP and lack of detectable expression of PGP were also conﬁrmed in MCF7-MX cells by surface protein analysis using antibodies speciﬁc for PGP and BCRP (17F9 and BXR) with ﬂow cytometry detection (data not shown).  

# 3 2.3. [ H]-Glyburide transport studies  

Glyburide uptake studies were performed on conﬂuent monolayers of PGP, BCRP, MRP1, MRP2 and MRP3 over-expressing cell lines on 24-well poly- styrene plates. Brieﬂy, cells were washed and pre-incubated for 30 min in Earle’s balanced saline solution (EBSS) containing 1.8 mM CaCl 2 , 5.4 mM KCl, 0.8 mM MgSO , 138 mM NaCl, 1.0 mM Na PO , 5.5 mM  D -glucose 4 2 4 and 20 mM HEPES. Cells were then incubated for various times (5, 10, 20, 30 or 60 min) at 25    C with [ 3 H]-glyburide (0.2  m M, 50  m Ci) in the presence and absence of standard inhibitors of PGP (verapamil, 200  m M), BCRP (novo- biocin, 200  m M), and the MRP transporters (indomethacin, 200  m M). At each time point, the incubation medium was aspirated and the reaction was termi- nated with 600  m l of ice-cold 0.16 M NaCl solution. The cells were then sol- ubilized with 200  m l of 1.0 N NaOH for 30 min and transferred to 7 ml scintillation vials containing 175  m l 2.0 N HCl to neutralize the NaOH. Intra- 3 cellular concentrations of [ H]-glyburide were measured by a Beckman LS500TD (Beckman, Coulter, Fullerton, CA) liquid scintillation counter with automated quench correction. All samples were corrected for ‘‘zero- 3 time’’ and background radioactivity. Intracellular accumulation of [ H]-gly- buride was standardized to protein concentration (picomoles per mg of protein) as determined by the Bradford colorimetric method (Sigma e Aldrich, Canada) with bovine serum albumin (Sigma e Aldrich, Canada) as standard. # 2.4. BCRP transport assay  

The potential interaction of glyburide with BCRP was examined in BCRP over-expressing MCF7-MX cells using a mitoxantrone efﬂux assay previously 6   described by Shiozawa et al.  [25] . Brieﬂy, 10 cells were pre-incubated in me- dia containing 3  m M mitoxantrone for 30 min at 37    C. Cells were washed, ali- quots collected and the medium was replaced with mitoxantrone-free medium in the presence of various concentrations of glyburide (1 e 1000  m M). Novobi- ocin was used as a positive control. Efﬂux was terminated at 90 min, cells were washed and re-dissolved in ice-cold PBS. The amount of intracellular ac- cumulated mitoxantrone at time 0 and time 90 min was measured on a Becton Dickinson FACSCalibur (BD Biosciences, San Jose, CA) using an excitation wavelength of 488 nm (argon laser) with detection in the 633 nm range. The amount of efﬂuxed mitoxantrone after 90 min was calculated as the difference between intracellular accumulation of mitoxantrone at time 0 and time 90 min. IC 50  values were obtained from GraphPad Prism 3.0 Software (San Diego, CA) by nonlinear regression analysis with a sigmoidal dose response equation. The analysis was repeated twice to conﬁrm reproducibility of values.  

# 2.5. MRP transport assay  

The potential interaction of glyburide with MRP was examined in MRP1-, MRP2- and MRP3-transfected cells using a previously described 5-CFDA/5- carbo xy u ores ce in (5-CF) efﬂux assay  [2,26] . In this assay, the non-ﬂuores- cent 5-CFDA passively and rapidly diffuses into cells where it is converted to the ﬂuorescent anion 5-CF by intracellular esterases. 5-CF is efﬂuxed from cells by the MRP family of transporters and is not a substrate of PGP or human organic anion transporter  [2] . 5-CFDA was dissolved in ethanol and diluted in Ringer buffer to a ﬁnal concentration of 50  m M and the cells were allowed to incubate for 5 min. After 5 min, the buffer was replaced with fresh solution of glyburide (200  m M) or indomethacin (10  m M, 200  m M) in Ringer’s buffer or Ringer buffer alone (controls). Efﬂux was al- lowed to occur for 30 min, at which time cells were solubilized in 1% trypsin and transferred to a 96-well plate for measurement. Fluorescence of 5-CF was measured using a SpectraMAX Gemini XS plate reader (Molecular Devices) with excitation and emission wavelengths of 490 and 520 nm, respectively.  

# 2.6. PGP transport assay  

The potential interaction of glyburide with PGP was examined in the PGP over-expressing A2780/Adr cells using a previously described rhodamine 123 efﬂux assay  [26] . Brieﬂy, A2780/Adr cells were grown to conﬂuency in 6-well plates, cells were washed twice with PBS buffer and then incubated for 15 min in 2 ml of a 1  m g/ml solution of rhodamine 123. The rhodamine 123 solution was then aspirated and replaced with fresh PBS buffer containing inhibitor (verapamil, 200  m M; glyburide, 200  m M). Cells were allowed to incubate for 30 min. Cells were then washed with ice-cold phosphate buffered saline and then solubilized in 1 M NaOH and neutralized with 1 M HCl. Fluorescence in- tensity of rhodamine 123 was measured against a calibration curve of pure rhodamine 123 using a SpectraMAX Gemini XS plate reader (Molecular De- vices) at excitation wavelength of 518 nm and emission wavelength of 535 nm.  

# 2.7. Data analysis  

Results are reported as a mean    SE from three separate experiments per- formed in monolayer cells. For each individual experiment, each data point represents triplicate trials. Uptake data were analyzed by two way analysis of variance followed by Tukey’s post hoc comparisons (Sigma Stat, 2.03). In- hibition data were analyzed by one way analysis of variance followed by either Dunn’s or Newman Keuls Test as appropriate (Sigma Stat, 2.03).

# 4. Discussion  

Glyburide is a second generation sulfonylurea that has been shown to effectively treat gestational diabetes while exhibiting marginal placental transfer  [1] . Indeed, in a recent placental perfusion study we have documented that glyburide is trans- ferred from the fetal to the maternal circulation against its con- centration gradient  [23] . To elucidate mechanisms involved in this phenomenon, we examined the potential involvement of several highly expressed placental ABC transporters in glybur- ide efﬂux. Overall, the results from these studies demonstrated that glyburide is effectively transported by BCRP and MRP3 and that these transporters may play a role in its distribution. Our results also indicate that glyburide is an inhibitor of sev- eral members of the ABC transporters including BCRP, PGP and the MRPs, however, inhibition occurs at concentrations which are higher than those normally achieved in patients.  

Transport studies demonstrated a BCRP-mediated transport of glyburide. Treatment of the BCRP over-expressing MCF7- MX cells with the BCRP inhibitor novobiocin, resulted in 3 signiﬁcant increases in the intracellular accumulation of [ H]- glyburide and an overall 3-fold increase in the total intracellular levels (AUC 0 e 60 ). Generally, BCRP differs from other ABC transporters in that it is only composed of one trans-membrane region and one ATP binding domain, while other transporters are composed of two trans-membrane regions and two ATP binding domains  [3,24] . BCRP is largely located in apical mem- branes of epithelia, present in tissues such as liver, intestine, colon and pancreas  [5] . BCRP is also highly expressed in human placenta. Potentially, the presence of BCRP in the placental syn- cy tio troph oblast suggests that it may have a protective function for the fetus such as prevention of hypoglycemia by glyburide [6,7] . BCRP has a wide substrate speciﬁcity including clinically important drugs such as mitoxantrone, antracyclines, daunoru- bicin, doxorubicin, camptothecins, topotecan and SN-28  [4,6] . Results from the mitoxantrone efﬂux studies also demonstrated that glyburide inhibits BCRP activity with low potency (IC 50  ¼  150  m M). While previous studies have reported an inter- action of glyburide with other ABC transporters, this is the ﬁrst study to demonstrate that glyburide is both a substrate and inhibitor of BCRP.  

As PGP is highly expressed on the apical (maternal) facing trophoblast of human placenta, it has been suggested to play an important role in protecting the fetus from potentially toxic xenobiotics which may occur due to unintentional exposure or therapeutic use in mothers  [8,9,20] . For example, Smit  [10] showed a 16-fold increase of digoxin accumulation in PGP de- ﬁcient fetuses as compared to wild-type fetus. Furthermore, Lankas et al.  [9]  also demonstrated an enhanced susceptibility to chemically induced birth defects (such as cleft palate) in PGP deﬁcient mice. However, our studies failed to

Results from our studies also indicate that the MRP3 trans- porter may play a role in the transplacental transfer of glybur- ide. Treatment of the MRP3 over-expressing MDCK cells with the MRP inhibitor resulted in a pronounced increase in the in- tracellular accumulation of glyburide. Furthermore, treatment of the MRP3 over-expressing MDCK cells with glyburide re- sulted in a 4-fold decrease in the cellular efﬂux of the 5-CF. MRP transporters are involved in the transport of many clini- cally important anionic drugs as well as their glucoronide and gluthathione metabolites  [12,13] . As glyburide is an anionic drug, it is not surprising that it would be a substrate of this family of transporters. MRP3 is highly expressed in fetal blood vessels and in sync y tio troph oblasts of the chorionic villi, there- by protecting the fetus from noxious substances and allowing the active secretion of endogenous, anionic compounds in the fetal e maternal direction  [14,15] . Thus, it is highly plausible that MRP3 is involved in the active secretion of organic anion drugs such as glyburide from the fetal compartment into the maternal circulation.  

The MRP1 transporter is also expressed on the basolateral surface (fetal) of the placenta  [16,17] . However, glyburide transport in MRP1-transfected cells was dissimilar to that ob- served in the MRP3 cell line, in that inhibition of MRP activ- ity resulted in a decreased rather than increased intracellular 3 accumulation of [ H]-glyburide. Therefore it is possible that MRP1 may be involved in the cellular uptake of glyburide. Alternatively, it is highly plausible that indomethacin may in- hibit other organic anion uptake transporters expressed in HeLa cells. Indeed, it has recently been reported that the or- ganic anion transporter, OATP-B, mediates uptake of glybur- ide in stably transfected HEK293 cells  [27] . On the other hand, glyburide was found to inhibit MRP1-mediated efﬂux of 5-CF. Likewise, Payen et al.  [18]  reported glyburide-medi- ated inhibition in the MRP1 over-expressing GLC4/Sb30 lung cancer cells. As other sulfonylureas did not inhibit MRP1, it was suggested that structural features unique to glyburide may be required to interact with MRP1. Similar to our ﬁnd- ings, inhibition was found to occur at concentrations greater than therapeutic levels. The MRP2 transporter is also thought to serve to protect the fetus from toxins  [20,21] . However, our results failed to demonstrate MRP2-mediated transport of gly- buride transport. On the other hand, glyburide was found to inhibit MRP2-mediated efﬂux of 5-CF suggesting that glybur- ide is an inhibitor but not a substrate of MRP2. While the po- tential for involvement of other placental transporters such as MRP5  [22]  in glyburide’s transport exists, the functional and physiological roles of these transporters have yet to be elucidated.  

In summary, data from our study clearly indicated that gly- buride is preferentially transported by BCRP and MRP3. Fur- thermore, glyburide was found to be an inhibitor of BCRP, PGP, MRP1, MRP2 and MRP3. With regards to the human placenta, it has clearly been shown not to metabolize glyburide [29] . However, it should be noted that the transport of its high potency metabolites (3- cis  and 4- trans  hydroxyglyburide)  [19] may be mediated by alternative mechanisms and transporters throughout the rest of the body. As the role of these transport- ers in pregnancy is revealed, factors such as inter-individual variability and placental expression should be considered when measuring the placental transfer of compounds. Another important clinical implication includes the potential for drug e drug interactions which could contribute to drug induced fetal toxicity  [11,19,21] . The inability to prevent drug transfer across the placenta has become an accepted norm in treating pregnant patients. These transporters can possibly serve as prospects in protecting the fetus by preventing exposure to noxious substances. However, they also allow for the opportu- nity to enhance exposure of the fetus to certain xenobiotics needed for the treatment or prevention of certain fetal conditions.
