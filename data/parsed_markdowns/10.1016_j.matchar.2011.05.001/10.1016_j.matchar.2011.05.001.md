# A R T I C L E D A T A  

# A B S T R A C T  

High-Temperature Confocal Laser Scanning Microscopy (HT-CLSM) was used to observe the initiation of incipient melting of primary  γ ′  in a Ni-base superalloy, and to quantitatively determine the liquation temperature. Specimens were cycled using different heating rates (5 and 50 °C/s) to peak temperatures of 1200 and 1300 °C to observe the influence of rapid heating on the development in the  γ ′ -morphology. It was found that rapid heating prevented the dissolution of primary  γ ′ , which melts leading to constitutional liquation and the formation of dendritic  γ ′  at the grain boundaries upon cooling. These observations are discussed in terms of the microstructural development of Nickel-based superalloys during welding, and their potential influence on the properties.

In high-temperature materials (e.g. steel, titanium, and Ni- based superalloys), PTs normally occur at temperatures above \~700 °C for solid – solid PTs, up to 1300 – 1600 °C for solid – liquid PTs. These temperatures pose major concerns when using simple  in-situ  observation setups (e.g. light optical microscopy using a hot stage) due to the possibility of oxidation, as well as thermal emission either from the heating element or the specimen, which ultimately lead to a poor image quality  [3] . Over the past decade, high-temperature confocal laser scan- ning microscopy (HT-CLSM) has been used in several studies investigating both the solid – solid PTs  [4,5]  and solid – liquid PTs [6,7] . HT-CLSM utilises the advantages of laser (high coherence and monochromatic it y), confocal optics, halogen lamp heat- ing, and infrared image furnace (IIF) to produce focussed images showing the PT sequence.  

There has been a recent progress in the development and utilisation of  in-situ  techniques to study the microstructural phase transformations (PTs) in inorganic materials. Synchrotron X-ray diffraction (XRD) has been successfully used in several studies to  in-situ  investigate the phase evolution in reciprocal space during melting and solidification (e.g.  [1] ), despite the technical difficulties associated with heating during  in-situ diffraction. Nonetheless,  in-situ  XRD does not enable the direct observation of the microstructural mechanisms during melting and solidification (e.g. grain boundary or second-phase particle melting), especially when kinetic phenomena (rapid heating cycles) are being observed. Similarly, variations in the enthalpy or electrical resistivity can be used to study the PTs using dif- ferential scanning calorimetry (DSC) or electrical conductivity measurements, respectively. However, they are generally limited to heating and cooling rates close to equilibrium conditions (up to \~1 °C/s), although new advanced DSC furnaces have been  

In this investigation, HT-CLSM was used to observe the local melting of primary  γ ′  in a Ni-based superalloy during rapid heating to above the solvus temperatures (T Solvus ). Rapid heating is normally encountered during welding, which is known to result in constitutional liquation in some alloys  [8] , where certain phases melt at their specific chemical compo- sition and melting temperatures. Under equilibrium heating conditions, secondary phase particles like primary  γ ′  have sufficient time to dissolve into the matrix by solid-state diffusion, resulting in a single phase of homogeneous composition. However, during rapid heating, time is insuffi- cient for homogeneous dissolution, which means that some particles remain in the matrix above T Solvus . Upon reaching a certain temperature above T Solvus , depending on the heating rate, the secondary phase particles start to react with the matrix, prior to forming a liquid film below the equilibrium solidus temperature of the alloy (T Solidus ). Although the mechanism and microstructural morphology of constitutional liquation is well-established in the field of welding metallurgy since it was discovered by Pepe and Savage  [9] , the present study aims at describing its occurrence using real-time microscopic observation, and also assessing the influence of the heating rate and peak temperature on the microstructural morphology.  

# 2. Experimental  

# 2.1. Material  

Metallurgical specimens were extracted from a forged disk of RR1000 provided by Rolls-Royce plc. RR1000 is a  γ ′  strength- ened Ni-base superalloy produced by powder metallurgy, followed by forging, sub-solvus solution treatment, and aging. The aim of this treatment is to create a tri-modal γ ′ -(Ni,Co) 3 (Al,Ti) distribution within the  γ  matrix. The chem- ical composition of RR1000 is given in  Table 1  with other alloys for comparison. The composition shows that RR1000 (similar to 720Li and Astroloy) has a much larger wt.% of  γ ′ -formers (Al and Ti) than the other wrought superalloys (e.g. Waspaloy) and consequently a significantly higher volume fraction of  γ ′ (\~45 – 50%) compared to Waspaloy (\~25%)  [10,11] .  

# 2.2. High-Temperature Confocal Laser Scanning Microscopy (HT-CLSM)  

A Lasertec TM   1LM21 confocal laser scanning microscopy system (CLSM) was used to  in-situ  investigate the primary  γ ′ - development, in combination with a heating stage mounted at the focal point of an ellipsoidal gold-plated infrared image furnace (IIF), powered by a 1.5 kW halogen lamp,  Fig. 1 -a. The CLSM uses a 1.5 mW He – Ne laser ( λ =632.8 nm), with a 0.5  μ m diameter laser beam. The laser beam is reflected by the specimen and scanned at a rate of 15.7 kHz by an acoustic optic deflector (AOD) in the horizontal direction, and by a galvano-mirror in the vertical direction at 60 Hz. The obtained signal represents a quasi-topographical representation of the specimen surface through recording several captures at different depths, making it possible to distinguish the surface relief caused by any phase transformations  [12] . The combined in-focus images are then recorded on a PC at a rate of 30 frames/s.  

Small specimens ( ϕ  5 mm, 1 mm height) were sectioned, ground and mechanically polished to 0.04  μ m oxide polishing suspension finish. Each specimen was placed in an alumina crucible placed on the Pt stage in the HT-CLSM enclosure

 ( Fig. 1 -a), below a quartz viewing-window, and in a high purity

 (99.9994%) Ar atmosphere. The specimens were cycled through three different programmes ( Fig. 1 -b). Due to the nature of the temperature control in the HT-CLSM, it was not always possible to exactly achieve the exact target tempera- ture during rapid heating or force a certain cooling schedule. Consequently, overshoots of the target temperature or non- linear cooling trends were observed during each experiment. At the low heating rates (5 °C/s), the amount of overshoot was quite limited (\~7 °C), whereas at the higher heating rate (50 °C/s) the overshoot was \~95 °C. In spite of these deviations, the thermal cycles (with the overshoot) were generally repeatable for the same  “ target ”  temperature and heating rate (as in the 5 °C/s cycles in  Fig. 1 -b). The overshoot in the 50 °C/s run, though significant, was actually desired since it resembled the typical thermal cycle in welding processes (e.g.  [13,14] , whereby an initial rapid heating rate is experi- enced, and then slowed down as the peak temperature is approached. It is important to mention that thermal imaging calibration measurements showed that the temperature difference between the specimen surface and the recorded (actual) thermocouple temperature does not exceed ±20 °C.

# 2.4. Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)  

Following thermal cycling, the HT-CLSM cycled specimens were prepared for microstructural character is ation, by grind- ing and polishing the specimens to expose the bulk micro- structure (\~500  μ m below the observed surface). Electrolytic etching was then performed in a 10% H 3 PO 4  aqueous solution for 5 s at 5 V until the specimen surface changes to a faint blue – brown colour. This etching method attacks the  γ -matrix grains, leaving the  γ ′ -particles intact. Specimens were exam- ined in FEI-Sirion field emission gun scanning electron microscope (FEG-SEM), equipped with a backscattered elec- trons (BSe) detector, and operated at 5 kV.  

# 3. Results  

3.1. RR1000 Microstructure and Equilibrium Phase Transformations  

As shown in  Fig. 2 -a, the microstructure of RR1000 shows three size ranges of  γ ′ : the incoherent primary  γ ′  (1 – 2  μ m), second- ary  γ ′  (150 – 300 nm), and tertiary  γ ′  (<100 nm). The coarse primary  γ ′  (\~1 – 3  μ m) usually remains undissolved along the γ -grain boundaries during the sub-solvus heat treatment, and thus helps in grain size control  [16] . However, other  ‘ low solvus ’  γ ′  particles are dissolved, and eventually re-precipitate upon cooling from the sub-solvus solution treatment, forming the remaining two size ranges (secondary and tertiary  γ ′ ). Both secondary and tertiary  γ ′  nucleate intragranularly during cooling from the sub-solvus treatment at different tempera- tures (higher temperature for secondary  γ ′ ), but they do not grow rapidly during cooling due to the slow diffusion rates [16,17] . Upon further aging, they grow into their final sizes. Due to the pinning influence of the primary  γ ′ , the grain size is quite fine, with an equivalent circle diameter (D Eq ) within the range \~5 – 10  μ m,  Fig. 2 -b.  

By performing DSC scans for the as-received alloy, it was found that the alloy shows three small endothermic peaks between 960 °C and 1160 °C, followed by a large endothermic peak between 1245 °C and 1350 °C,  Fig. 3 . It is believed that the small peaks correspond to the  γ ′ -dissolution, since the end temperature (1160 °C) is equivalent to the  γ ′ -solvus tempera- ture reported for RR1000  [18] , and also close to the values reported in other  γ ′ -containing alloys (e.g. T Solvus =1153 °C in U720Li  [19]  and 1140 °C in Astroloy  [11] ), which are the closest alloys to RR1000 in composition. # 3.2. In-Situ Observation of  γ ′ -Incipient Melting  

In addition to the snapshots that were taken at different temperatures in the attempted thermal cycles, the full sequence of the entire process can also be viewed in the attached video files. Upon performing rapid heating at 50 °C/s, the specimen surface was found to retain a featureless surface until 1270 °C, indicating the lack of any surface relief. Following that, thermal etching started to reveal the  γ -grain boundaries and a spotty distribution of particles that bulged out of the surface. These particles were identified as the primary- γ ′  particles (due to their morphology, location on the γ -boundaries, and distribution) ( Fig. 4 -a), prior to bursting, melting ( Fig. 4 -b), and eventually creating a liquid film on the specimen surface ( Fig. 4 -c). As the temperature started to decrease, the liquid film started to form a network of equiaxed cells, with relatively thick boundaries ( Fig. 4 -d). After perform- ing the thermal cycle, the specimen was ground and observed under the FEG-SEM to study the influence of the thermal cycle on the development of the final structure in the bulk. It was found that thermal cycling led to a noticeable grain growth, with the  γ -grain size growing to a range between D Eq \~20 – 40  μ m ( Fig. 4 -e), compared to 5 – 10  μ m in the starting condition. It is obvious that the final grain size matched the size of the cells that formed during cooling ( Fig. 4 -d). Furthermore, it was found that the thermal cycle resulted in a complete annihi- lation of the original tri-modal microstructure, leading to the formation of a dendritic  γ ′ -structure across most of the  γ -grain boundaries ( Fig. 4 -f), yet with a uniform distribution of fine round  γ ′  (50 – 100 nm) within the grains and mostly straight  γ - boundaries.  

Upon decreasing the heating rate to 5 °C/s, the first changes in the surface morphology appeared at 1188 °C, where the grain boundaries started to become thermally etched, after which the primary- γ ′  particles started to burst out of the  γ - boundaries ( Fig. 5 -a). By 1250 °C, the particles started to spread across the surface ( Fig. 5 -b), creating the liquid film that became quite homogeneously spread on the surface by 1300 °C ( Fig. 5 -c). Similarly, upon cooling, a cellular network started to form at \~1290 °C (20 – 40  μ m in size,  Fig. 5 -d). This network later developed to the final grain structure ( Fig. 5 -e), which was considerably coarser and non-uniform size distri- bution compared to the parent structure and even the previous heating rate, with a grain size range of D Eq \~20 – 60  μ m. With respect to the  γ ′ -structure, dendritic  γ ′ -structures were also observed along the grain boundaries ( Fig. 5 -f), but they were more elongated and feathery compared to the relatively round dendritic entities that formed in the high heating-rate specimen. Previous work on RR1000 by Mitchell  et al.  [17]  observed similar structures and referred to them as “ fan-type structure ” , which were found to form due to slow cooling from above the  γ ′ -solvus temperature at cooling rates between 0.68 and 3.53 °C/s. The presence of these structures was also associated with serrated grain boundaries, similar to that shown in  Fig. 5 -f.

# 4. Discussion  

Although the topic of liquation in Ni-superalloys has been repeatedly studied, previous work has mainly focused on the liquation of the other low melting point phases (e.g. carbides, Laves phase, sulphides, etc. … )  [8] , with very limited studies investigating the microstructural development due to  γ ′ - liquation  [20] . In this investigation, HT-CLSM was used to observe the occurrence of constitutional liquation in  γ ′ - strengthened Ni-based superalloys. Although the mechanism itself is very familiar since its description by Pepe and Savage [9] , the present investigation of the primary  γ ′ -liquation showed additional features to this, especially in relation to the development of the grain boundary morphology. In the classical mechanism ( Fig. 7 ), the base metal microstructure (d 0 ) experiences grain growth (d 1 ) above the effective grain growth temperature, which combines with the initiation of localised melting, leading to the intersection of the moving grain boundaries with the solute-rich liquid films (d 2 ), and ultimately more grain boundary wetting. During cooling, the solidifying films pin the grain boundaries, but a ghost solute- rich grain boundary network may form if cooling is performed rapidly (d 3 )  [8] .  

In the present investigation, liquation occurred through primary  γ ′ , which controls the grain size by Zener pinning  [16] , and is characterised by a high equilibrium solvus temperature and has a relatively considerable volume fraction in this alloy (\~0.25). This differs from the other reported mechanisms in Ni-based superalloys, where liquation occurs in low volume fraction phases (e.g. MC, M 6 C, or Ni 2 Nb Laves phase  [8,21] ). Due to the pinning-ability of primary  γ ′ , both the grain size and morphology were affected following liquation, whereby grain growth was experienced in both the high and low heating rates. In addition, it seemed that slow heating rate (5 °C/s) resulted in the formation of curved serrated grain boundaries, which was correlated to the influence of the cooling rate rather than the heating rate in a previous study on RR1000  [17] . The grain boundary morphology (curved versus straight) is actually an important characteristic that influences the mechanical properties of Ni-superalloys, especially the inter- granular crack growth and creep, since wavy or serrated boundaries create a mechanical locking effect  [22] . However, as rapid heating led to the formation of straighter boundaries, and both thermal cycles had the same cooling rate, it is possible to argue that the grain boundary curvature is caused by the un-restricted grain boundary motion, in the absence of the primary- γ ′  pinning effect. In fact, the influence of the similar rapid cooling rate can be visualised in the similarity of

Primary  γ ′ -liquation phenomenon has several sides that need to be further investigated and are beyond the scope of this study, especially its influence on the mechanical proper- ties, in addition to thermodynamic modelling of liquation, which can be used to predict the influence of non-equilibrium heating on the microstructure. Although some other studies performed thermodynamic modelling for rapid heating in Astroloy  [11]  and IN738  [21,23] , the present  in-situ  approach provides a simpler approach to visualise and quantify the departure from equilibrium due to rapid heating. For instance, the findings of the present investigation agreed with the modelling-based findings of Soucail and Bienvenu  [11]  who predicted that the primary  γ ′  solvus shifts from 1140 °C at equilibrium heating to 1265 °C at a heating rate of 300 °C/s. Some studies investigated the consequences of  γ ′ -liquation on the weldability of certain Ni-superalloys, especially on the occurrence of hot cracking, where it was reported that the formation of the  γ – γ ′  eutectic and its associated hot cracking can be reduced by increasing the carbon content in the alloys to consume the liquid to form the carbides (L ←→ γ +MC)  [20] .  

Other studies used numerical models to simulate the influence of rapid heating on the development of the  γ ′ - precipitates  [21,23] . Although this approach remains contro- versial, it was suggested that a critical heating rate existed below which the  γ ′ -particles do no experience liquation  [21] . This heating rate was suggested to be inversely proportional to the square of the particle size (i.e. the coarser the particle is the lower the critical heating rate). However, due to the complex- ity of the  γ ′ -constitutional liquation phenomenon, the pre- diction of the critical heating rate becomes more difficult since the liquation phenomenon is believed to form through a  γ – γ ′ reaction involving diffusion (a multi-component reaction), rather than the exclusive melting of the  γ ′ -phase  [24] . This is a limitation in HT-CLSM where the resolution is insufficient to observe the constitutional liquation at the  γ – γ ′  interface.  

It is important to mention that the HT-CLSM simulations should always be combined with other  in-situ  techniques (e.g. DSC) and post-cycling or  ex-situ  microstructural investi- gations for the bulk since the HT-CLSM microstructural observations are performed on a free surface. However, it has been shown previously that the HT-CLSM surface observations are generally consistent with the bulk by sectioning the thermally cycled specimen  [25] , which was also performed in the present study. Additional unpublished studies by the authors on other alloys showed that the free surface effect appears negligible in the diffusional trans- formations with solute partitioning, but becomes more obvious in solute invariant diffusional transformations (e.g. re crystal lisa tion and grain growth).  

# 5. Conclusions  

In this investigation, the HT-CLSM was used to visually investigate the influence of rapid heating on the initiation of constitutional liquation of primary  γ ′  in a Ni-based superalloy.  

The differences between this mechanism and other classical liquation mechanisms were highlighted, especially the influ- ence of the heating rate on the grain size, grain boundary morphology, and the distribution of the  γ ′ .
