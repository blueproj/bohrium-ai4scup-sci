# A R T I C L E D A T A  

# A B S T R A C T  

The hot deformation behavior of a new Ni – Cr – Co based P/M superalloy was studied in the temperature range of 950 – 1150 °C and strain rate range of 0.0003 – 1 s − 1   using hot compression tests. It was characterized by true stress – true strain curves, constitutive equation, strain rate sensitivity  m  contour maps, power dissipation  η  maps and hot processing maps. The microstructural validation of processing maps was also done. The results show that the flow stress decreases with increasing temperature and decreasing strain rate. The hot deformation apparent activation energy of the Ni – Cr – Co based P/M superalloy at peak stress is 805 kJ/mol. The  m  and  η  contour maps are similar, and the values of  m  and  η  in the peak zones increase with increasing strain. When the strain is 0.5, a domain with its peak  η  of 40% and peak  m  of 25% occurs at 1050 °C and 0.0003 s − 1 , which corresponds to dynamic recrystallization and can be as an optimum condition for good workability.

In 1980s, Prasad et al. established a processing map based on the dynamic material model (DMM), which was successfully applied to titanium, zirconium, aluminum, magnesium, copper, superalloy, stainless steel and other materials  [9 – 16] . However, many experts and scholars had different views on the theory of DMM. In 1996, Montheillet et al.  [17]  questioned with the dissipator  G , the dissipator content  J  and the efficiency of power dissipation  η . They considered that  G  and  J  of visco-plastic materials did not have the same physical interpretations as the strain energy density  U  and the complementary energy density  V  of elastic materials. They suggested the usage of rate sensitivity  m  for the prediction of flow localization. In 1996 and 2008, Omar et al.  [18,19]  predicted the optimum processing parameters of medium carbon microalloyed steel by  m  and  η contour maps respectively. They found that the predicted results were similar and could be verified by the actual production. In 2000 and 2002, Ghosh  [20,21]  hypothesized that DMM was lacking theoretical basisand rigor when itwas applied for the analysis of microstructural evolution and flow instability during hot forming. He pointed out that an error in predicting the instability zone for materials occurred when they used the instability criterion based on DMM, while the well established instability criteria given by Semiatin and Jonas  [22]  were conceptually sound and had good predictive power. However, in 2008, Bakkali El Hssani et al.  [19]  used the phenom eno logical and continuum criteria to characterize the instability and the flow localization in medium carbon microalloyed steel. They found the continuum instability criterion predicted microstruc- tural changes more accurately, while a value for the phenom- enological criterion varied between materials and needed to be confirmed by intensive microstructural examinations. At pres- ent, there is little work on the comparison of the rate sensitivity contour maps and the power dissipation contour maps for superalloys. Meanwhile the universal instability criterion has not existed until now. The aim of the present investigation is to study the hot deformation behavior of the new Ni – Cr – Co based superalloy under different deformation conditions, with a view to understand the constitutive relationship of the material. The m  and  η  contour maps are established and compared. The microstructural validation for the processing maps is also done. All of these provide a theoretical reference for the actual hot working process.  

# 2. Experimental Procedure  

The hot isostatic pressed (HIPed) Ni – Cr – Co based P/M superalloy was selected as the experimental material. The details of the preparation procedures were as followed: the vacuum induction melting (VIM) and electroslag remelting (ESR) methods were used for alloy smelting; the plasma rotating electrode process (PREP) was used for powder preparation, and the vibration screening and electrostatic separation methods were combined to remove inclusions and produce powder with particle sizes of 50 µm – 150 µm. The powder particles were encapsulated in AISI 304 stainless steel capsules and sealed after vacuum degassing. The capsules were then HIPed in a single stage at 1180 °C for 4 h at a pressure of 120 MPa. The HIPed rods were then skinned to remove the stainless steel encapsulation. The chemical com- position of the Ni – Cr – Co based superalloy used in this study is given in  Table 1 . Hot compression tests were conducted using a Gleeble-1500 simulator at the temperatures of 950 °C, 1000 °C,

1050 °C, 1100 °C and 1150 °C and in the strain rate range of 0.0003 – 1 s − 1   with 8 mm diameter, 15 mm high cylindrical specimens. All specimens were heated to deformation tem- peratures at a heating rate of 10 °C/s and held for 5 min in order to gain the uniform microstructure before the hot compression test. Then they were deformed to a true strain of 0.7 and water cooled to freeze the microstructure. The load-stroke data obtained in compression were processed to obtain true stress – true strain curves using standard equations. The deformed specimens were sectioned parallel to the compression axis and the cut surfaces were prepared for microstructure observation. The microstructure of deformed samples was observed using an Olymas optical microscope and with the chemical etchant of CuCl 2  (10 g)+HCl (50 mL)+H 2 O (50 mL) and etching time of 30 – 60 s. Grain sizes were determined according to ASTM E112 linear intercept procedures using circular grid overlays. Besides, the microstructure of HIPed samples was observed using a XL30TMP scanning electron microscope (SEM), and the chemical composition analysis of carbides was performed using a Phoenix energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS). For SEM and EDS studies, the preparation method of samples started with electro polishing with 20% sulfuric-methanol and followed by electrolytic etching in a CrO 3 – H 3 PO 4 – sulfuric acid solution.

# 3.2. True Stress – true Strain Curves  

The true stress – true strain curves of the Ni – Cr – Co based P/M superalloy recorded at various temperatures from 950 to 1150 °C under strain rates from 0.0003 to 1 s − 1   are shown in Fig. 4 . These stress – strain curves exhibited a flow softening feature, which was more significant at lower temperatures and higher strain rates. The initial rapid rise in stress is associated with an increase in dislocation density and the formation of poorly developed subgrain boundaries, as a result of work hardening and dynamic recovery. As the Ni – Cr – Co based P/M superalloy has low stacking fault energy, the dynamic recovery proceeds slowly. The high dislocation density stimulates the occurrence of dynamic recrystalliza- tion once a critical strain (usually less than 0.1) is exceeded, then the flow stress decreases.

The power dissipation maps are continuum maps and are interpreted in terms of the microstructural processes. These maps reveal the limiting temperature and strain rate condi- tions where fracture and instabilities occur during the deformation of materials. For domain I, the sample at 1050 °C and 0.1 s − 1   shows obvious dynamic recrystallization characteristics ( Fig. 10 a), with the grain size of ASTM 8.5 – 9.0. For domain II, according to the atomistic processing map of Raj  [26] , the observed domain may correspond either to superplasticity or DRX. The efficiency of power dissipation for superplasticity is generally very high (>60%). This is not observed in the map of  Fig. 9 (a) since the peak efficiency is not higher than 40%. In view of this observation, the domain observed does not represent superplastic deformation. Hence, the domain in the processing map can be interpreted in terms of DRX. On the basis of the hot deformation microstructural observations ( Fig. 10 (b)), the recrystallization region can be identified in the processing map at a strain of 0.5 ( Fig. 9 ). The average grain size of the sample ( Fig. 10 (b)) is ASTM 10 – 11. For domain III, as shown in  Figs. 8 and 9 , there are low values of  m and  η  under different strains. Further, they decrease with increasing strain. This is because a significant grain growth appears at high temperature and low strain rate condition. The grain size is ASTM 6.0 – 6.5 ( Fig. 10 (c)). The plasticity deteriorates and the hot workability becomes worse. Meanwhile, wedge cracking occurs at grain bound- ary triple junctions, as shown in positions C and D of  Fig. 10 (d). It is very similar with the result of P/M IN-100  [27] .

# 4. Conclusions  

Hot compression tests of a new Ni – Cr – Co based P/M superalloy has been studied in the temperature range of 950 – 1150 °C and the strain rate range of 0.0003 – 1 s − 1 . The hot deformation behavior is characterized by true stress – true strain curves, constitutive equation, strain rate sensitivity contour maps, power dissipation maps and hot processing maps. The following conclusions have been drawn from the results of this investigation.  

1. The true stress – true strain flow curves of the Ni – Cr – Co based P/M superalloy show the typical characteristics of dynamic recrystallization and dynamic recovery. A hot deformation equation is given to characterize the depen- dence of peak stress on the temperature and strain rate. The hot deformation apparent activation energy of the Ni – Cr – Co based superalloy is 805 kJ/mol.  

2. The  m  and  η  contour maps of the Ni – Cr – Co based P/M superalloy under different strains are similar. The values of m  and  η  both increase with increasing deformation temper- ature and decreasing strain rate. Within the deformation conditions of this study, it is also found that the peak zones in the upper right corner of  m  and  η  contour maps change from a semi-circle at the strain of 0.1 into a closed circleat the strain of 0.7. The peak zones move to the region of lower temperatures and higher strain rates. The values of  m  and  η also gradually increase with increasing strain.
