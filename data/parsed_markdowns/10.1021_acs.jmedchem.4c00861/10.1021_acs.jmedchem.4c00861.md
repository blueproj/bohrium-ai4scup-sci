# INTRODUCTION  

Epilepsy is a common chronic neurological condition, affecting more than 50 million individuals worldwide and ranking as the second most common neurological disorder.   It is typically characterized by unprovoked recurrent seizures that encom- pass both generalized and partial seizures, affecting a variety of mental and physical functions. Current treatment strategies primarily depend on anti-seizure medications (ASMs), which typically exert their effects through multiple mechanisms, including modulation of various cellular targets such as voltage- gated ion channels (sodium, calcium, and potassium channels), ligand-gated ion channels (ionotropic  γ -aminobutyric acid type A and glutamate receptors), neurotransmitter transporters, and associated metabolizing enzymes. ,   However,  ∼ 30% of patients continue to experience seizures, and many suffer side effects from existing drugs that cannot be ignored. Consequently, there is an urgent need for novel and more effective ASMs that target specific pathogenic mechanisms.  

Voltage-gated sodium (Nav) channels play a key role in the generation and propagation of action potentials (APs) and represent an important target for the development of novel antiepileptic drugs.   Among the nine Nav channels (Nav1.1 − Nav1.9), Nav1.2 is strongly expressed in the adult mammalian central nervous system, where the corresponding encoding genes together account for >95% of brain sodium channel transcripts. Nav1.2 channels are primarily expressed in excitatory neurons. Notably, Nav1.2 is widely expressed in the proximal axon initial segment (AIS) that promotes back- propagation to the soma and dendrites, and the distal AIS responsible for the generation of the APs. ,   Gain-of-function (GoF) mutations in Nav1.2-encoding gene SCN2A have been identified in patients with developmental and epileptic encephalopathy (DEE) and benign familial neonatal-infantile seizures (BFNIS). − 11   Given that these GoF mutants increase neuronal excitability, the inhibition of Nav1.2 channels would be beneficial for alleviating seizures. ,   Many currently available ASMs, including lamotrigine, phenytoin, carbamaze- pine, and oxcarbazepine, are Nav channel antagonists. − 18  

While these conventional sodium channel blockers have shown efficacy in patients with GoF variants, most individuals still experience some seizures and undesirable side effects. Thus, inhibitors targeting the Nav1.2 channel with improved potency and selectivity are anticipated to have fewer side effects and are being pursued by many pharmaceutical groups for the treatment of epilepsy.

# RESULTS AND DISCUSSION  

Rational Design of Novel Nav1.2 Inhibitors.  Through a high-throughput screening system, compound  7  was obtained and exhibited potent  in vitro  inhibitory activity against Nav1.2 currents ( I Drug / I Control  = 0.24  ±  0.05, and Nav1.2 inhibition ratio = 76% at 10  μ M). The intriguing scaffold of compound  7 has inspired our interest in performing further structural modifications, due to its good druglike property for CNS applications (cLogP = 3.74, and TPSA = 90.46 Å 2 ). To further improve the inhibitory activity of compound  7  against Nav1.2 currents and its physicochemical property, we applied a dominant conformational restriction strategy to design compound  8  based on the intriguing scaffold of compound  7 ( Figure 2 ). Notably, compound  8  demonstrated strong inhibition of Nav1.2 currents, with  I Drug / I Control  values of 0.01 ±  0.01 (Nav1.2 inhibition ratio = 99% at 10  μ M), surpassing that of compound  7 . Meanwhile, compound  8  exhibited improved physicochemical properties, as indicated by more favorable cLogP and TPSA values, making it a promising candidate for CNS applications. ,   Next, we conducted a comprehensive structure − activity relationship (SAR) study based on compound  8  to design compounds  9 − 48 .  

Structural analysis revealed that compound  7  possesses the potential to be effectively tethered to the pore domain (PD) of Nav1.2, as depicted in  Figure 3 A. In this configuration, the amino moiety could establish hydrogen bonds with Ala1419, while the aliphatic chains occupied a P-loop, as illustrated in Figure 3 B. Further exploration indicated that compound  8 maintained a similar binding pattern closer to the P-loop, as shown in  Figure 3 C. Notably, in  Figure 3 D, it was evident that the amino moiety of compound  8  formed a more stable hydrogen bond with Ala1419, while the cyclization moiety effectively filled the P-loop pocket and fixed the molecule in place. Additionally, in the 3-methoxyphenyl region of compound  8 , there are surrounding amino acids, such as Ser1713 and Thr1711, that provide additional space for optimization and modification. Furthermore, the cyclization moiety can undergo further modification and optimization to enhance binding affinity. On the basis of the molecular docking analysis presented above, a total of 42 new derivatives were further designed and synthesized to explore their detailed SAR using a structure-based drug design strategy.

Antiepileptic Activity of the Compounds in the 6 Hz (32 mA) Model.  Diverging from the MES model, representative of generalized tonic-clonic seizures, the 6 Hz test commonly serves as a model for treatment-resistant seizures. To investigate the broad-spectrum antiepileptic potential of compounds  14 ,  15 , and  35 , particularly concerning treatment-resistant seizures, their antiepileptic activities were then evaluated in the 6 Hz (32 mA) seizure model. ,   During the test, the vehicle group experienced seizures, while mice given 200 mg/kg VPA remained free of seizures ( Table 9 ). Unexpectedly, compound  14 , the most potent one in the MES test, failed to confer antiepileptic activity in the 6 Hz test at a dose of 50 mg/kg ( Table 9 ). When the dose was increased to 100 mg/kg, the protection ratio reached 25% ( Table S4 ). Notably, we observed a significant reduction in seizure duration after the administration of compound  14  at the larger dose (vehicle, 22.7  ±  1.6 s; compound  14 , 12.9  ±  3.4 s; \* p  < 0.05) ( Table S4 ). However, the accompanying neuro- toxicity hindered further dose escalation. In addition, it is indeed noteworthy that many antiepileptic drugs, including rufinamide, safinamide, and vinpocetine, exhibit robust antiepileptic effects in the MES model but demonstrate limited or partial effectiveness in the 6 Hz model, even at large dosages.   This pattern could be partly attributed to the distinct mechanisms underlying the seizures in these models. Specifically, the MES model primarily simulates generalized tonic-clonic seizures, whereas the 6 Hz model represents a psychomotor seizure model of partial epilepsy, exhibiting a markedly different pattern of seizure-induced neuronal activation compared to that of the MES models, as evidenced by the differing levels of c-Fos staining.   Further compre- hensive studies focusing on the pathological roles of Nav1.2 in epilepsy might provide valuable insights. The protective effects of compounds  15  and  35  in the 6 Hz model were comparable to those observed in the MES model, with compound  35 displaying the most effective antiepileptic activity ( Table 9 ). The estimated ED 50  values for compounds  15  and  35  in the 6 Hz test were 38.0 (17.4 − 83.0) mg/kg and 18.5 (9.1 − 37.6) mg/kg, respectively ( Figure 6 B). Our findings underscore the efficacy of compounds  15  and  35 , which not only dose- dependently alleviated generalized tonic-clonic seizures in the MES model but also mitigated treatment-resistant seizures in the 6 Hz model. These results suggested that the developed compounds, particularly compound  35 , targeting Nav1.2 channels stood as promising and effective antiepileptic candidates.

# CONCLUSIONS  

Dysfunction of voltage-gated sodium channel Nav1.2 is closely linked to epileptic seizures, and Nav1.2 has been identified as a crucial drug target associated with antiepileptic activity. In our study, a novel series of pyrimidine-based derivatives acting as inhibitors of the Nav1.2 channel were identified, and these compounds showed promising inhibitory activity against Nav1.2. Detailed SAR investigations highlighted compounds 14 ,  15 , and  35  as being notably potent against Nav1.2, with IC 50  values of 120  ±  20, 635  ±  57, and 65  ±  6 nM, respectively, with compound  35  being one of the strongest inhibitors. Compound  14  exhibited favorable pharmacoki- netics ( F  = 43%) following intraperitoneal injection and excellent brain penetration potency (B/P = 3.6).  In vivo experiments utilizing the MES assay revealed that compounds 14 ,  15 , and  35  displayed robust potency, with predicted ED 50 values of 3.2 (1.8 − 5.6) mg/kg, 15.8 (8.2 − 30.6) mg/kg, and 11.1 (5.8 − 21.2) mg/kg, respectively, after intraperitoneal administration in mice, indicating significant antiepileptic activity. Furthermore, compounds  15  and  35  demonstrated protective effects against the treatment-resistant seizures, evaluated using the 6 Hz (32 mA) model of drug-resistant epilepsy, with ED 50  values of 38.0 (17.4 − 83.0) mg/kg and 18.5 (9.1 − 37.6) mg/kg, respectively. Additionally, the antiepileptic activity of compound  15  was well-tolerated as it did not induce significant motor impairment at the effective doses in the rotarod test. Combined with the antiepileptic potency observed in the MES test, therapeutic indices for compounds 14 ,  15 , and  35  were calculated, yielding values of 8.2, 14.7, and 2.9, respectively. In summary, our study identified multiple novel and potent Nav1.2 inhibitors and provided valuable leads with therapeutic potential in epilepsy for further structural optimization.

Molecular Docking Study.  The crystal structures of Nav1.2 (PDB entry 6J8E) were obtained from the Protein Data Bank. Before the docking process, the protein structure was treated, including adding hydrogen atoms, deleting water molecules, assigning AMBER7 FF99 charges, and performing a 100-step minimization process using Sybyl X_2.1. The molecular structure was generated with the Sybyl/ Sketch module, optimized using Powell’s method with the Tripos force field with convergence criterion set at 0.005 kcal Å − 1   mol − 1 , and assigned charges with the Gasteiger − Hu ckel method. Other docking parameters were kept to the default values. Molecular docking was carried out via the Sybyl/Surflex-Dock module. All docking studies were performed using Maestro of Schro dinger Suites, and the obtained poses were analyzed with PyMOL.  

Cell Culture.  Human embryonic kidney 293 (HEK 293) cells stably expressing human Nav1.1, Nav1.2 were grown in high-glucose DMEM (Gibco) supplemented with 10% tetracycline-free fetal bovine serum (Gibco) and selected with 50  μ g/mL hygromycin B (Invitrogen). Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells stably expressing human Nav1.6 were grown in F-12 (Gibco) medium containing 10% fetal bovine serum and selected with 500  μ g/mL hygromycin B. Chinese hamster lung (CHL) cells stably expressing human Nav1.5 were grown in high-glucose DMEM supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum and were selected with 500  μ g/mL Geneticin (Invitrogen). The cells were cultured in a humidified incubator at 37  ° C and 5% CO 2 . Before electrophysiology recording, the cells were plated onto poly- L -lysine-coated glass coverslips (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO) and used within 24 h.  

Patch Clamp Recordings.  Whole-cell voltage clamp and current clamp recordings were performed at room temperature using an Axopatch 700B patch clamp amplifier (Molecular Devices). Pipettes were pulled from borosilicate glass capillaries (World Precision Instruments) with an electrode resistance typically ranging from 1.5 to 4 M Ω . To test the inhibitory activities of the compounds on hNav1.2 channels, the recording pipet solution contained 140 mM CsF, 10 mM NaCl, 10 mM HEPES, 1.1 mM EGTA, and 20 mM glucose (pH 7.3 adjusted by CsOH); the bath solution contained 140 mM NaCl, 3 mM KCl, 1 mM MgCl 2 , 1 mM CaCl 2 , 10 mM HEPES, and 20 mM glucose (pH 7.3 adjusted by NaOH). During the recordings, the bath solution was continuously perfused by using a BPS perfusion system (ALA Scientific Instruments). Recordings were conducted after a 5 min equilibration period at  − 80 mV after breaking into the whole-cell configurations. To record sodium currents, the cells were clamped at a holding potential of  − 40 mV at which >90% sodium channels are inactivated, and then, a 20 ms prepulse to  − 150 mV was applied to partially recover channels from inactivation, followed by a 10 ms pulse to 0 mV to activate the channel at a rate of 1 Hz. Currents were acquired at a sampling frequency of 50 kHz and filtered at 2 kHz. Series resistance compensation was used and set to 80%. P/N subtraction was never applied throughout the experiment.  

Use of Animals.  All animal procedures were performed in accordance with the National Institutes of Health Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals, under protocols approved by and strictly following the guidelines of the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committees (IACUC). The IACUC checked all protocols and approved this study. Male ICR (Institute of Cancer Research) or C57BL mice were obtained from Shanghai SLAC Laboratory Animal Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). Mice were housed and maintained at a controlled temperature (22  ±  2  ° C) under a 12 h light/dark cycle with free access to food and water. For the behavior tests, the compounds were dissolved in a 5% DMSO/5% Tween 80/90% saline mixture and intra peritoneal ly administered 30 min prior to the tests.  

Pharmacokinetic Profile Studies.  Male ICR mice (19 − 24 g) were randomly assigned to the animal room under controlled conditions (temperature of 18 − 29  ° C and relative humidity of 30 − 70%). The temperature and relative humidity were monitored daily. An electronic time-controlled lighting system maintained a 12 h light/ dark cycle. Animals were orally treated with compound  14  [po, 20 mg/kg, 5:95 (v/v) DMSO/0.5% HPMC], and other treatment groups were injected [ip. 10 mg/kg, 5:5:40:50 (v/v/v/v) DMSO/EtOH/ PEG300/0.9% NaCl; iv, 1 mg/kg, 5:5:40:50 (v/v/v/v) DMSO/ EtOH/PEG300/NaCl]. Subsequently, blood samples were collected at 0.25, 0.5, 1, 2, 4, 8, and 24 h for po-treated groups and 0.05, 0.25, 0.75, 2, 4, 8, and 24 h for ip- and iv-treated groups. All samples were centrifuged at 12 000 rpm for 3 min, and the plasma was harvested. Plasma samples were stored at  − 80  ° C until they were analyzed. The serum concentrations of the selected compounds were determined by liquid chromatography/tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS).  

Brain Penetration Study in ICR Mice.  Male ICR mice (21 − 29 g) were randomly assigned to the animal room under controlled conditions (temperature of 18 − 29  ° C and relative humidity of 30 − 70%). The temperature and relative humidity were monitored daily. An electronic time-controlled lighting system maintained a 12 h light/ dark cycle. Mice were fasted for 12 h before administration and remained fasting for 2 h. Animals were orally treated with compound 14  [po, 20 mg/kg, 5:95 (v/v) DMSO/0.5% HPMC], and other treatment groups were injected [ip, 10 mg/kg, 5:5:40:50 (v/v/v/v) DMSO/EtOH/PEG300/0.9% NaCl; iv, 1 mg/kg, 5:5:40:50 (v/v/v/ v) DMSO/EtOH/PEG300/NaCl]. Blood samples were collected at 0.25 and 0.5 h for groups treated with compound  14 . The blood samples were centrifuged to obtain the plasma fraction, and compound concentrations in the supernatant were measured by LC-MS/MS.  

Maximal Electroshock-Induced Mouse Seizure Assay. Electro-convulsions were generated by an alternating current (stimulus duration of 5.4 s, fixed current intensity of 4 mA, and maximum stimulation voltage of 160 V) delivered via ear-clip electrodes through a physiological and pharmacological electronic stimulator (Ji-nan, China) in male ICR mice (18 − 22 g). The criterion for the onset of seizures was the presence of tonic-clonic generalized seizures that result in tonic hind limb extension. The day before the experiment, mice who had experienced generalized tonic-clonic seizures induced by MES were selected to test the anticonvulsant effects of the compounds. Animals were thought to be protected from MES-induced seizures in the absence of the hind limb tonic extension component of the seizure.  

6 Hz (32 mA) Epilepsy Test.  Corneal stimulation (0.2 ms rectangular pulses at 6 Hz for 3 s) was delivered by a constant-current device (CCU1) connected to a model S48 square pulse stimulator (Grass Technologies, West Warwick, RI). A fixed current intensity of 32 mA was used. Before stimulation, a drop of saline was applied to each eye of male C57BL mice (20 − 25 g) to ensure optimal current conductivity. Seizures were characterized by a stunned or fixed posture, often accompanied by rearing, forelimb clonus, and twitching. The duration of the seizure activity was measured as the evaluation criterion for the protective effect of the compound. The animal was considered to be protected if it resumed its normal exploratory behavior within 7 s of the stimulation. In contrast, the compound had no protective effect if the typical characteristic behaviors described above occurred in the animal after drug administration or if the duration of the seizure was >7 s.  

Neurotoxicity in Mice.  The standard rotarod test was performed with male ICR mice (18 − 22 g). The mice were placed on a rotarod appliance (YLS-4C, Biowill, Shanghai, China) with a rod with a diameter of 3 cm rotating at a constant speed of 6 rpm. The day before the compound test, all of the mice were pretrained and only the animals able to remain on the rod for at least 1 min every time in three consecutive trials (3 min) were retained. On the test day, the mice were measured in the rotarod test 30 min after intraperitoneal administration. The motor coordination of an animal unable to remain on the rod for three consecutive periods was considered impaired.  

Data Analysis.  Patch clamp data were processed using Clampfit

 10.3 (Molecular Device) and analyzed with GraphPad Prism 8.0

 (GraphPad Software). To examine the inhibitory activities of the compounds, the peak current magnitudes of the corresponding channels after ( I Drug ) and before ( I Control ) application of the compounds were measured. The dose − response curves were fitted with a four-parameter Hill equation:  Y  = bottom + (top  − bottom)/ [1 + 10 (log IC 50 − X ) K ], where bottom and top are the minimum and maximum inhibition, respectively,  X  is the log of concentration,  Y  is the inhibition efficacy ( I Drug / I Control ), IC 50  is the drug concentration producing a half-maximal response, and  K  is the Hill slope. The peak currents of the steady-state activation curves were transformed into conductance values by utilizing the equation  G  =  I /( V  − E X ). Then, the activation curves were fitted by the Boltzmann equation  G  =  G min + ( G max  − G min )/[1 + exp( V  − V 1/2 / k )], and the inactivation curves were fitted by the equation  I  =  I min  + 1/[1 + exp( V  − V 1/2 / k )]. In these equations,  E X  is the inversion potential of Nav1.2 currents,  G max represents the maximum conductance,  G min  represents the minimum conductance,  V  represents the test potential,  V 1/2  represents the midpoint voltage of kinetics, and  k  represents the slope factor. The results are presented as means  ±  SEM. The ED 50  (median effective dose) and TD 50  (median toxic dose) values were estimated by computer-assisted log-probit analysis, according to Litchfield and Wilcoxon. The therapeutic index was the ratio of TD 50  to ED 50 . The significance was determined using the unpaired two-tailed  t  test and one-way analysis of variance. A  p  value of <0.05 was considered statistically significant. Detailed information is provided in the legends.
