Consecutive layer of a cobalt alloy, stelliteX-40, were deposited onto a GH4133 superalloy plate. The microstructure and mechanical properties of laser cladding were examined. An inappropriate combination of laser power, scanning velocity or pre-placed powder height can result in the formation of incomplete fusion interface between the cladding layer and substrate, or reheat cracking along the coarse grain boundary in the heat-affected zone (HAZ) after ageing treatment. The SEM and energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) show the absence of precipitate particles within the reheating crack with similar average compo- sitions for the crack and the substrate. The plastic deformation due to residual thermal stress induced by thermal cycle of laser cladding process is concentrated on the coarse grain boundaries within the HAZ as grains are strengthened by precipitating particles dur- ing the aging process. This concentrated plastic deformation is mainly responsible for reheat cracking. The laser cladding layer with integrated melt interface and without reheat crack- ing can be obtained by optimizing laser power, scanning velocity and powder thickness. The experimental results of microhardness show the homogenous property distribution within the material after the ageing treatment.  

Keywords: Laser cladding GH4133 alloy Interfacial quality Single-track clad Reheat cracking

The technique of laser cladding is a ﬂexible process that has been successfully applied in deposition of coating of a wide variety of materials, such as Co-based hardfac- ing alloys ( Vilar, 1999; Lin and Chen, 2006; Zieli ´ nski et al., 2005; Sun et al., 2006 ), Ni alloys ( Huang et al., 2003 ), com- posites ( Mei et al., 2005 ). Repair of deteriorated industrial components or machine parts is another important appli- cation of cladding ( Capello et al., 2005; Kathuriau, 2000 ). More recently, another increasingly important application of laser cladding is the production of metallic objects from  

Several authors introduced the use of laser cladding tech- niques for turbine blade repair ( Sun et al., 2005; Ojo et al., 2004; Shepeleva et al., 2000; Sexton et al., 2002 ). However, the effects of the process parameters on the interfacial properties of the laser cladding layers and the substrate have not been thor- oughly investigated. The tensile residual thermal stress and the thermal strain are due both to the differences between the thermal expansion coefﬁcients of interfacial materials during the heating/cooling cycles of the laser cladding process.  

The purpose of this paper was to repair a worn part of aeroengine blade. According to the literature ( Lin and Chen, 2006; Zieli ´ nski et al., 2005; Sun et al., 2006; Kathuriau, 2000; Shepeleva et al., 2000; Sexton et al., 2002 ), Co-based stellite powder and laser–clad were chosen, the laser cladding pro- cess, performed as single track in one layer, was done in the way that the powder was pre-placed ﬁrst on the substrate and then melted with the laser beam. We had performed a series of laser cladding experiments with stelliteX-40 powder and GH4133 superalloy to obtain the optimum process parameters and a full fusion between the clad layer and the substrate. In addition, the effects of processing parameters, residual stress and aging treatment on the interfacial quality and the crack formation were analyzed in detail.  

# 2. Experimental procedures  

# 2.1. The materials  

The alloy materials used in this experiment and the chemical compositions are shown in  Table 1 . The powder of stelliteX-40 used as the cladding material, has a particle size in the range of 44–110   m. The substrate material is GH4133 superalloy, and is given a standard heat treatment at 1080   ◦ C for 8 h followed by air cooling and subsequent ageing at 750   ◦ C for 16 h followed by air cooling. The specimens are 20 mm  ×  20 mm  ×  3 mm in size.  

# 2.2. Experimental methods  

In order to prevent defect caused by moisture in the pow- der and cleaning sample, the specimens were cleaned in acetone solution prior to the laser cladding, and dried by keeping at 100   ◦ C in an oven furnace. The laser used was a ROFIN TR050 5 kW CO 2  laser. A CNC work table was used to achieve the desired range of scanning speeds. Reﬂective telescopes with water-cooled mirrors made of pure copper consisted of a ﬂat reﬂection mirror and a parabolic mirror with the focal length of 286 mm were used to focus the laser beam. Argon was applied as a shielding gas at the ﬂow rate of 2.5 L min − 1 . The interaction zone between the laser beam, pre-placed powder and substrate is shown in  Fig. 1 . The laser beam was out of positive focus at the substrate, and the laser cladding was performed as single track in one layer. Laser

# 3.2. Quality analysis of interface between clad layer and substrate  

The effects of pre-placed powder thickness, scanning velocity and laser power on fusion zone are shown in  Figs. 5 and 6 . Fig. 5 (a) and (c) show that the interface between the cladding layer and the substrate deteriorates by increasing the thick- ness of pre-placed powder. When the powder thickness is equal to 1.2 mm, there are almost two thirds of the interfaces not to be fused. With the increase of the scanning velocity, the heat input of laser cladding decreases, the duration of the laser beam interaction on the powder and substrate is relatively shorter, and therefore energy available is insufﬁ- cient to fuse powder to the substrate, and the incomplete fusion has formed as shown in  Fig. 5 (b).  Fig. 5(a,d) and 6(a) show the effect of laser power on the interface, it can be seen that the incomplete fusion forms when the laser power is 1000 W and the crack forms when laser power is 3000 W.

The addition of a clad layer results in the generation of residual stresses in the weld region, high-thermal gradi- ents involved in the laser cladding are responsible for the generation of residual stresses, often harmful tensile nature

According to the the- ory of elasticity, the stress near the melt tip increases with decreasing the curvature radius at the melt tip. The curvature radius at the melt tip depends on the depth-to-width ratio of melt region in the substrate, and decreases with increasing the depth-to-width ratio. Therefore, the depth-to-width ratio of melt region may indirectly reﬂect a change of the stress near the melt tip: the larger the depth-to-width ratio is, the larger the residual stress will be.  

Fig. 8  shows that the depth-to-width ratio of the melt region in the substrate increases with increasing laser power, so the residual stress induced by thermal cycle of laser cladding pro- cess increases with increasing laser power, particularly the zone near the melt tip. The precipitates in the heat-affected zone are dissolved as a consequence of laser heat, and will not precipitate fully again during cooling. # 3.3. Microhardness and chemical composition analysis of layer cladding under the optimum process parameters  

The geometric parameters of cladding layer in  Figs. 2–4  and the interfacial properties of the interface between cladding layer and substrate in  Figs. 5 and 6  show the interdependence of laser power, scanning speed and thickness of pre-placed powder. Based on the criterion for which the cladding layer has no incomplete fusion and reheat cracking, the required clad dimensions of (1.8–2.0 mm) ( W )  ×  (0.8–1.0 mm) ( H ) and the depth of fusion less than 0.2 mm, the optimum process param- eters for a single-track clad in one layer on GH4133 superalloy obtained by the experimental results given above is as follows: the pre-placed powder thickness of stelliteX-40 is 0.6 mm, the laser power is 1500 W and the scanning velocity is equal to 380 mm min − 1 .  

It should be pointed out that GH4133 superalloy gain their strength essentially by precipitation of   ′ -phase which has the average formula (Ni 0.94 Cr 0.06 ) 3 (Al 0.42 Ti 0.47 Nb 0.11 ), and the volume fraction of   ′ -phase determines essentially the microhardness ( Editing Board of China Aeronautical Materials Handbook, 2002 ). # 4. Conclusions  

Based on the results presented above, the following conclu- sions can be drawn:  

(1) The clad height, clad width and depth of fusion increase with the increase of laser power. The clad width and depth of fusion reduce with increasing the scanning velocity, the clad height ﬂuctuates narrowly. The clad height and width increases and the depth of fusion decreases with increas- ing the pre-placed powder height.

 (2) The interface between the laser cladding layer of stelliteX- 40 and substrate of GH4133 high-temperature wrought alloy may appear incomplete fusion and reheat cracking with the change of scanning velocity, laser power and pre- placed powder thickness.

 (3) The formation of reheat cracking is related to both the residual tensile stress relief and a precipitation in the heat-affected zone of GH4133 superalloy during ageing treatment.  

(4) The laser–clad layer with required sizes, metallurgical bonding and defect-free can be obtained by optimizing laser power, scanning velocity and pre-placed powder thickness.
