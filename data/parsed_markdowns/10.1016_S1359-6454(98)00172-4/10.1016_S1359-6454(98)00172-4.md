# MEASUREMENT AND ANALYSIS OF BUCKLING AND BUCKLE PROPAGATION IN COMPRESSED OXIDE LAYERS ON SUPERALLOY SUBSTRATES  

J.-S. WANG {  and A. G. EVANS Division of Engineering and Applied Science, Harvard University, Cambridge, MA 02138, U.S.A. (Received 13 March 1998; accepted 30 April 1998)  

Abstract ÐThe mechanisms whereby thin thermally grown aluminum oxide layers detach from superalloy/ bond coat substrates have been investigated. Experimental studies have been based on the use of applied compressions that superpose on the residual compression and activate the failure mechanisms. These stu- dies have revealed a sequence comprising buckling, buckle propagation and spalling. The observations have been analyzed using the established decohesion mechanics of compressed ®lms. Consistency between the measurements and the mechanics has been established, enabling the interface adhesion parameters to be ascertained. The results suggest a relatively weak interface, with adhesions typical of those found for metal/oxide interfaces subject to weakening by segregation.  #  1998 Acta Metallurgica Inc. Published by Elsevier Science ltd. All rights reserved.  

# 1. INTRODUCTION  

Superalloy components are protected against oxi- dation by using overlay (bond) coatings comprising Ni or Pt alloys having a composition that enables Al 2 O 3  formation upon oxidation. The ecacy of the approach is dominated by the resistance to spal- ling of this thermally grown oxide (TGO). This re- sistance is enhanced by incorporating into the coating rare earth elements [especially Y (e.g. Ref. [1])]. While there is an extensive literature in this area, mechanism-based models of the spalling phenomenon are sparse and incomplete [2±7]. With the premise that such models are important for coating design and life prediction, this paper describes a methodology for identifying mechanisms and analyzing them in technologically relevant ma- terials.  

The elements of a mechanistic interpretation are as follows: (i) The TGO is subject to a large re- sidual compression at ambient temperature (of the order of 3±5 GPa) caused primarily by the thermal expansion mismatch between the Al 2 O 3  and the superalloy. This stress activates the spalling mech- anism upon cooling. Some stress may also be pre- sent during oxide growth [8±10]. (ii) The TGO thickens upon high temperature exposure and thereby increases the energy density available for spalling [11, 12]. (iii) It is widely thought that impu- rities in the coating (particularly S) segregate to the interface with the TGO, while at elevated tempera- ture, and that this reduces the interface adhesion [13±15]. This assertion is consistent with the established role of segregation in embrittling metal/oxide interfaces [16, 17]. (iv) The large energy density in the TGO, combined with the embrittled interface, instigates spalling by one of two mechan- isms: buckling [2±5] or wedging [6, 7]. The former involves the sequence depicted in Fig. 1. It is domi- nated by events occurring at the metal/oxide inter- face, elaborated below. It is analogous to mechanisms that operate in compressed thin ®lms made by chemical vapor deposition [such as SiC and diamond (e.g. Refs [2, 5, 18±20])]. The latter requires that the TGO be susceptible to inelastic shear deformation occurring by \`\`shear-crack'' mechanisms [6]. These deformations induce tensile stress concentrations that motivate interface crack- ing. The phenomenon is analogous to compressive fracture mechanisms found in brittle solids, such as rocks and concrete. Distinguishing among these two mechanisms is important because they have dier- ent implications for the material properties that control spalling.  

The principal objective of this article is to describe an experimental approach that facilitates mechanism identi®cation and which, together with the related mechanics, allows key interfacial and TGO properties to be determined. Accordingly, it becomes a quantitative benchmark for developing a life prediction scheme.

The composition of the oxide layer was analyzed using a Camaca MBX electron microprobe equipped with a Tracor Northern TN-5502 Energy Dispersive Spectrometer (EDS) and a TN-1310 Wavelength Dispersive Spectrometer (WDS). The beam voltage was 15 kV and the beam current 25 nA. The compositions after 10 and 100 h ex- posures are summarized in Table 2. X-ray diraction measurements performed on the TGO indicate that the oxide is predomi- nantly  a -Al 2 O 3 .  

Upon spalling of the TGO (by means of the mechanical measurements described below), the morphologies of the oxide and the underlying bond coat were characterized by scanning electron mi- croscopy (SEM). The attached TGO exhibited two domains. One comprised faceted, equiaxed poly- crystals  0 1/2  m m in diameter [Fig. 4(a)]. The other included acicular grains ( 0 5   1/2  m m) above the equiaxed layer [Fig. 4(b)]. The latter features are in- dicative of vapor phase growth and imply the pre- sence of Al and/or Al suboxide in the annealing atmosphere. Similar features have been found by others [21, 22]. The consequences are elaborated later.  

In regions containing spalled TGO, the underside of the TGO and the exposed bond coat exhibit a morphology widely reported in the literature (e.g. Refs [21, 22]) [Figs 5(a) and (b)]. Namely, the TGO has an equiaxed appearance with a grain size in the range 0.5±1  m m. A corresponding grain imprint is apparent in the exposed bond coat. There is no evi- dence that plastic deformation occurred in the bond coat, contrasting with the appreciable slip observed during the fracture of most diusion bonded metal/ oxide interfaces [23, 24].

This background information may be coalesced into a buckling mechanism map with three domains (Fig. 11). Construction of the map requires three non-dimensional indices. The indices are as follows:  

(i) A buckling index based on equations (3a)± (b):  

Buckling occurs when  P  exceeds a critical value, P c =1.22.  

(ii) An adhesion index, based on equations (4), (5) and (7):  

Buckles will not propagate unless  S  exceeds a critical value,  S 0 , of the order of 2±3.  

(iii) A mode mixity index,  l , which depends on the interface roughness. In principle,  l  can be determined by measuring  G i ( c ). In practice, it must be inferred by ®tting the buckling measure- ments to buckling map trajectories.  

The resultant domains of behavior are characterized by three mechanism transitions (Fig. 11):  

(I) When  P  <  P c , buckling is prohibited for all  S . In this range, the ®lm is stably adherent, despite the presence of interface separations.  

(II) When  P > P c , but  S E S 0 , buckling occurs, but the buckle will not propagate. This is also a stable situation.  

(III) When  P > P c  and  S e S 0 , the buckle both forms and propagates. In most cases, it also arrests [5, 20]. This happens because of the posi- tive slope of the boundary between buckling and buckle propagation (Fig. 11), caused by the eect of mode mixity on the interface toughness, es- pecially at small  l . This is also the basis for the stable growth found experimentally (Fig. 10).  

Accordingly, the domains encompassed by  P < P 0 and  S < S 0  are \`\`spall- safe''. Conversely, when P > P c  and  S > S 0 , there is a high potential for spalling. Whether a spall actually forms depends on the tensile stresses that develop in the TGO as a consequence of bending deformations [11].

# 7. CONCLUSION  

Direct observations of the buckling of thin ther- mally grown alumina layers formed on a commer- cial bond coat have allowed comparison with expectations from mechanics models and have pro- vided measurements of the interface adhesion. The sequence of buckling and buckle propagation is consistent with the expectation of a mechanics model based on a rough interface [2, 11, 33]. The major features comprise: (i) initial buckling above interface separations at a critical stress, (ii) abrupt buckle expansion along the interface at a second critical stress, and (iii) stable buckle growth along the interface upon further stress elevation.  

Comparison of the measurements with the mech- anics has provided several explicit implications.
