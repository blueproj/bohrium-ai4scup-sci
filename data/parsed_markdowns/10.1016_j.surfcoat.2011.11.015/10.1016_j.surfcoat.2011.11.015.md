Dense and well-adherent glass-alumina-NiCrAlY tri-composite coatings were prepared on K38G superalloy substrates at 950 °C. Their thermal shock resistance at 1000 °C was investigated, and compared with two glass-alumina composite coatings. Results indicated that the tri-composite coatings exhibited the best resis- tance to thermal shock and provided enough protective effect to the substrates from high temperature oxi- dation. The inclusion of alumina particles and NiCrAlY alloy platelets increased the indentation toughness and thermal expansion coef ﬁ cient, accounting for the enhancement of thermal shock resistance of the tri- composite coatings. Oxidation of the superalloy substrates, combined with their interaction with glassy coat- ings, was also discussed.

# 1. Introduction  

Traditional protective coatings for Ni-based superalloy gas turbine components are divided into two types: aluminide by aluminizing or overlay NiCrAlY coatings by physical vapor deposition or thermal spray- ing  [1,2] . In general, these coatings  ﬁ nally fail due to Al depletion by oxide formation on their surface and interdiffusion between the coating and substrate during service. As an alternative inert oxide coating, with- out this interdiffusion issue, might spall off by thermal shock because of the mismatch of CTEs (Coef ﬁ cients of Thermal Expansion) between coatings and substrates  [3 – 5] .  

In the traditional  ﬁ elds of structural materials, metallic phases or tetragonal zirconia reinforced glass matrix composites have been stud- ied for several years, where a pronounced improvement in toughness could be observed  [22 – 27] . NiCrAlY alloy is well-known for its oxida- tion resistance and mechanical stability at high temperatures  [28 – 30] . It possessed a high melting point and formed a dense protective oxide scale, such as Al 2 O 3  and Cr 2 O 3 , on the surface during service. In the pre- sent study, NiCrAlY alloy platelets were chosen as the toughening phase of the glass coating. The glass-alumina-NiCrAlY tri-composite coatings were prepared on K38G superalloy substrates. Their thermal shock behavior, depending on the preparation process of the coatings and the thermo physical properties of the materials, was investigated.  

It has long been recognized that many glasses could, under suitable conditions, bond well to a number of metals and alloys. The strong, well-adherent bond has led to the extensive use of glasses, e.g. vitre- ous or porcelain enamel, as the coating for metals and alloys  [6 – 9] More recently, it was demonstrated that glass-ceramics, prepared by controlling the crystallization of the parent glasses, could combine the generally superior properties of crystallite ceramics with the ease of processing of glasses. Glass-ceramics could offer the advantages of higher mechanical strength and more refractory behavior than its glassy counterpart, coupled with the ability to tailor the CTE to the range from zero to 100 (×10 -6 /°C) in order to match the substrates [10 – 15] .

Prior to applying the glass-alumina-NiCrAlY composite coatings, the substrates were oxidized at 850 °C for 5 min for the purpose of surface cleaning and to form a thin oxide  ﬁ lm. Thereafter, the specimens were sprayed at room temperature with the glass-alumina-NiCrAlY tri-composite suspensions, dried at 250 °C for 15 min and  ﬁ nally heat-treated at 950 °C for 10 min to form the composite coatings.  

Thermal shock cycling tests were conducted in a muf ﬂ e furnace where the temperature had been settled to 1000 °C. Each cycle con- tains inserting samples into the furnace, followed by 10 min holding them in the furnace and then plunging them into water at room temperature (about 25 °C). There were two parallel specimens for each group. After each  ﬁ ve cycles, the weight change of the speci- mens was recorded using an electronic balance (0.01 mg precision, Sartorius BP211D, Germany). Characterization of the coatings was carried out using scanning electron microscopy (SEM, Inspect F 50, FEI Co., Hillsboro, Oregon) coupled with an energy dispersive spec- trometer (EDS, X-Max, Oxford instruments Co., Oxford, UK). Phases were identi ﬁ ed by X-ray diffraction (XRD, X ’  Pert PRO, PANalytical Co., Almelo, Holland, Cu Ka radiation at 40 KV).  

To measure the thermo physical properties of the composite materials, the blended powders were compressed in fused quartz dies, pre-dried at 250 °C for 2 h and then sintered to form bulk samples with adapted dimensions. A non-destructive resonance frequency technique was adopted to measure the Young's modulus of the composites with the nominal dimensions of 4.7 mm in diameter and 80 mm in length. Three-point bending tests were conducted to measure the fracture strength. At least  ﬁ ve bars with the nominal dimensions of 2×4×36 mm 3   were tested per group. The CTE of the composites (nominally 0.8×0.8×8 mm 3 ) was measured using a di- latometer designed on the theory of optical lever at a heating rate of 10 °C/min. The indentation tests were carried out on the polished top face by using Buehler Micromet 5114 (Mitutoyo Co., Kanagawa, Japan) with a standard Vickers diamond tip. # 3. Results  

# 3.1. Microstructures of the glass-alumina-NiCrAlY tri-composite coatings  

The surface and cross-section morphologies of the as-manufactured glass-alumina-NiCrAlY tri-composite coatings are shown in  Fig. 1 . Alu- mina particles and NiCrAlY platelets tended to randomly disperse around the larger glass particles, as shown in  Fig. 1 A and B. One can noticed that alumina particles and small glass particles formed ag- gregating zones with diameters of about 10  μ m, which presented island-like morphologies on the surface of the composite coatings, possibly because of the lowed- ﬂ uidity at high temperature. Accord- ing to the cross-section image of the as-manufactured specimen ( Fig. 1 B and C), the composite coating, whose thickness was about 15  μ m, was very dense and adhered well to the superalloy substrate. The inclusion of NiCrAlY alloy platelets and alumina particles were observed also bond well to the glass matrix. Thin interfacial layers formed at the glass-alumina, glass-NiCrAlY and coating-substrate in- terfaces. Particulate crystallites can be seen precipitated from the glass matrix, which was tetragonal zirconia from our previous re- ports  [20] .  

# 3.2. Thermal shock behaviors of the tri-composite coatings heat-treated at 950 °C  

The glass-alumina-NiCrAlY tri-composite coatings prepared at 950 °C exhibited excellent thermal shock resistance on the superalloy substrates. After  ﬁ ve cycles of thermal shock, the coating surface be- came coarser ( Fig. 2 A), and a few cracks, parallel to the surface of the specimens were observed in the outer part of the coating ( Fig. 2 B). The cracks might initiate at the glass matrix, propagated in the coatings, and were de ﬂ ected when they encountered the embedded NiCrAlY alloy platelets and alumina particles, as shown in  Fig. 2 B and C (indicat- ed by arrows). Therefore, formation of though-thickness cracks, which always result in coating spallation, was prevented. One might notice that discontinuous white particles formed within the coating and near the coating/substrate interface. EDS analysis on the interfacial particle showed that they were metallic phase, and rich in Ni, Al and Si. Chromi- um, one of the main alloy components of the substrates, however, could hardly be detected in the interfacial particle.  

Fig. 3  shows the microstructures of the tri-composite coatings after thermal shock tests at 1000 °C for 50 cycles. The coating surface became coarser further ( Fig. 3 A), and the amount of the metallic in- terface particle increased ( Fig. 3 B). But crack was observed no longer. A continuous bright layer, as indicated by letter  “ a ”  in  Fig. 2 B, formed at the coating surface. EDS results showed that it was oxide rich in Zn, Cr and Ni. Cr and Ni must come from the substrate since the as- manufactured coating did not contain Ni and Cr. At a higher magni ﬁ - cation as shown in  Fig. 3 C, one can  ﬁ nd that many small precipitates appeared in the coatings and a continuous dark layer at the coating/ substrate interface. In our previous study  [20,33] , we have identi ﬁ ed that the glass used in this study is prone to precipitating ZrO 2  at 1000 °C, and reacting with the alumina particles to form ZnAl 2 O 4 . The continuous dark layer at the coating/substrate interface is possi- bly alumina, according to EDS analysis. Therefore, large amounts of metallic phases appeared in the coating might be due to complex phenomena, such as the formation of interface alumina layer, diffu- sion of Ni and Cr from substrate to the coating surface, crystallization of the glass, reaction between alumina particles and glass. # 3.4. Thermo physical properties of the tri-composite materials  

Table 3  shows the measured values of Young's modulus (E), frac- ture strength in bending ( σ ), Vickers hardness (HV 0.5), indentation toughness (K IC ) and CTE of the matrix glass and E20A10M tri- composite materials. From a general point of view, the inclusion of alumina particles and NiCrAlY alloy platelets enhanced the values of the thermo physical properties, except for the fracture strength which was almost same with that of the glass matrix. Higher tough- ness always means better resistance to crack initiation and propaga- tion. The glass-alumina-NiCrAlY tri-composite materials exhibited nearly 50% increase in indentation toughness than their parent glass. Additionally, there was a slight increase in CTE after the inclu- sion of alumina and NiCrAlY. It is believed that the higher the CTE value of the coating materials, the lower are the internal stresses formed due to the CTE mismatch between the substrate and coatings (the CTE value of the metal substrate is always higher than that of the ceramic coatings).

# 4. Discussion  

The ease of processing the glassy coatings results from the viscous ﬂ uidity of the glass at high temperature, which ensured that the coatings covering on and adhering well to the metallic substrates. However, there were many other factors in processing which affect- ed the performances of the glass coatings. In the dense E20A10M tri-composite coatings heat-treated at 950 °C, only few cracks formed and were readily stopped by the inclusions (as shown in  Fig. 2 B) before they further propagate. These preformed cracks were then healed during the following holding process at high temperature (1000 °C) by the  ﬂ ow of the matrix glass. There have been a large number of studies that have reported crack healing in glasses  [34,35] .  

Donald  et al.  [6,8]  had discussed in detail the interfacial reactions and bonding theories between the glassy coatings and Ni-based al- loys. The following reaction was suggested:  

Chromium from the alloy substrate reacted with ZnO in the glass to form a CrO layer at the interface and the reduced metallic zinc gas (the boiling point of zinc is about 907 °C, lower than the  ﬁ ring tem- perature of E20A10M composite coatings). After that, the CrO layer is dissolved rapidly into the glass and diffused toward the outer sur- face of coatings to form Cr 2 O 3 . While the volatile zinc gas arrived at the surface of the coatings, it might again form ZnO by reacting with oxygen in air or form ZnCr 2 O 4  by reacting with the surface Cr 2 O 3  and oxygen. This is demonstrated by the EDS ( Fig. 3 ) and XRD ( Fig. 4 ) analyses which demonstrated the existence of spinel ZnCr 2 O 4 . There are still two additional points to be noticed. Firstly,

It engenders very likely a competition with reaction (2). Secondly, the heat-treatment temperature of 950 °C was a little higher than the boiling point of zinc and the softening point of tri-composite coating materials. The sintering of coatings was then conducted in a lower speed so that the slowly formed volatile zinc gas had enough time to escape from the coatings. These two points combined avoided the formation of globular voids by the entrapment of zinc vapors.  

Due to the big mismatches in CTE and Young's modulus between the glass matrix and inclusions (especially the NiCrAlY alloy platelets that possessed average values of 13.5\*10 -6 /°C and 200 GPa for CTE and Young's modulus, respectively), large internal stresses are pro- duced, which could steer the propagating cracks around the inclu- sions  [28] . As shown in  Fig. 2 B, crack de ﬂ ection was detected in the vicinity of NiCrAlY alloy platelets. This process greatly dissipated the energy for crack propagation and avoided the formation of through- thickness cracks after thermal shock tests, and is very likely the main contribution to the increase in indentation toughness of the E20A10M composite materials. Though only the mechanism of crack de ﬂ ection was detected in the tri-composite coatings after thermal shock tests, there were still another two possible toughening mecha- nisms, i.e. crack bridging and interfacial debonding, which accounted for the toughening of glass by inclusion of NiCrAlY alloy platelets  [28] . Additionally, the inclusion of alumina and NiCrAlY enhances the CTE of the composite slightly, thus decreasing the mismatch to the super- alloy substrates and the associated thermal stress. This, to some ex- tent, contributes to the improved resistance to thermal shock of the tri-composite coatings. The increase in the softening point of the tri- composites as shown in  Fig. 7  is attributed to the increase in viscosity of the glassy matrix composite, which arose from Al 3+   dissolution from the alumina inclusions to the glass matrix and a pinning effect of the alumina particles  [36,37] .  

Because of the higher diffusion rate in the glassy coatings of Cr than that of Ni and Al from the alloy substrates, Cr was detected in the form of ZnCr 2 O 4  at the surface (Layer  “ a ”  as depicted in  Fig. 3 B) of the composite coatings, while Ni and Al were still remained mainly in the interfacial zone. This dissolved metallic phase, similar to the included NiCrAlY alloy platelets, could also modify the properties of glassy coatings at the bottom layer, thus matching the alloy sub- strates and glassy coatings better. In such a case, the thermal stress originated from CTE and Young's modulus mismatches between the alloy substrates and glassy coatings was decreased, so the resistance to thermal shock was increased. In order to get a better insight into this mechanism, further investigations of the interaction between the K38G alloy substrates and glassy coatings would be conducted.  

# 5. Conclusions  

From the above study, the following conclusions can be drawn.  

(1). Glass-alumina-NiCrAlY tri-composite coatings were success- fully prepared on K38G superalloy. They exhibited a dense structure and, apparently, good adhesion to the substrate.

 (2). The glass-alumina (20 wt%)-NiCrAlY (10 wt%) tri-composite coatings showed a better resistance to thermal shock than that of the glass-alumina (20 wt% or 30 wt%) bi-composite coatings.

 (3). The inclusion of alumina particles and NiCrAlY alloy platelets enhanced the thermo physical properties of the glass matrix, and hence contributed to the excellent performances of the glass-alumina (20 wt%)-NiCrAlY (10 wt%) tri-composite coat- ings in the thermal shock tests.
