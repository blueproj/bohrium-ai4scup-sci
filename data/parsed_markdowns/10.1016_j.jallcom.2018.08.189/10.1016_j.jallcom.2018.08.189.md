Abstract  

Long-term operation of Cr 3 C 2 oxidative and corrosive environments, leads to significant changes in the coating composition and  microstructure. A critical, but often overlooked, aspect of high temperature service conditions is interdiffusion  between the coating and substrate. This leads to changes in the coating/substrate compositions and  microstructures with significant consequences in terms of the functionality of the coating. The aim of this work  was to characterise the magnitude and mechanism of interdiffusion between a Cr based Alloy 625 substrate at 500–900°C for up to 30 days. Interdiffusion was not observed at 500°C, but  occurred to a limited extent at 700°C, and very intensively at 900°C. The precipitation of M 23 6 6 C grains  within the alloy grain boundaries was proposed to occur due to carbon diffusion from the coating into the  3 C 2  grains in the coating due to formation of  (Cr,Ni) 7 C 3 . Chromium diffusion from the substrate into the coating resulted in the formation of a continuous  oxide layer at the coating–substrate interface and the formation of a continuous (Cr,Ni) 7 C 3  layer in the coating  adjacent to the interfacial oxide. # 1. Introduction

This is based on the high corrosion and oxidation resistance, but also on the coefficient of thermal expansion of  Cr 3 C 2 -NiCr coatings, which is comparable to many steel or other alloy substrates [1-3]. Investigations  characterising the effect of thermal post-treatment and/or high-temperature service conditions on the  microstructures of these coatings started in the 1970’s for air-plasma sprayed (APS) and Detonation gun (DGS)  sprayed coatings from blends of coarse Cr 3 C 2  (or Cr 23 C 6 ) and NiCr powders [4-6]. Following on from this,  newly developed Cr 3 C 2 -NiCr  composite  feedstock powders were used with different high velocity oxygen-fuel  (HVOF) and high velocity air-fuel (HVAF) spray processes [1, 2, 7-21]. Extensive studies were undertaken to  discover the compositional and microstructural developments of these coatings depending on temperature and  time of the heat treatment. This included the goal to describe their long-term behaviour under high-temperature  service conditions [1-3, 17-27], as well as their coating oxidation characteristics [7, 9, 28-31]. The change in  coating hardness in dependence on the annealing time was established, and linked with the microstructural  changes of the coatings [16, 23]. Over the years, the influence of thermally induced changes on the room and  high temperature erosion [5, 22, 25, 32], abrasion [18, 19] and sliding [7-9, 11, 17, 21] wear resistance of this  hardmetal composition have been described for coatings obtained from very different combinations of spray  process and feedstock powder.

Long duration, high-temperature interdiffusion studies between thermal spray coatings and substrates have  previously focused on NiCr coatings for steam oxidation at 600-700°C [40-42] and MCrAlY (M=Co, Ni or  CoNi) coatings at 850–1000°C [43-45]. Sundararajan et al. observed Fe diffusion from 9Cr-1Mo steel substrates  into NiCr coatings, and counter-current Ni diffusion into the substrate, during trials at temperatures up to 750°C,  for periods up to 1000-3000 hours [40-42, 46]. For 80Ni-20Cr coatings, interdiffusion increased the coating  adhesion, with Ni interdiffusion leading to the formation of austenite and martensite layers in the steel substrate  near the interface [41].  When the coating Cr content increased to 50 wt%, Cr-rich M 23 C 6  formation occurred at  the interface by reaction of Cr with carbon from the steel substrate. Up to 700°C, the carbide formed as  disbursed, interfacial precipitates, but developed into a continuous interfacial layer at 800°C [40]. This carbide  layer blocked Ni diffusion to the substrate. However, the loss of carbon from the steel lead to the formation of a  band of soft ferrite adjacent to the interface, while also reducing the coating adhesion strength [40, 42, 46]. In  MCrAlY coatings, interdiffusion with the substrate has led to the formation of complex multilayered interface  microstructures [43, 44]. Changes in the elemental composition have led to the formation of brittle phases linked  to interfacial cracking [43], a reduction in the oxidation performance through the formation of a less protective  oxides [43] and the formation of Kirkendall voids within the substrate [44].  

In addition to the diffusion of large, metallic alloying elements discussed above, the high carbon content of  Cr 3 C 2 -NiCr coatings means that carbon has the potential to play a key role in diffusion based phase  transformations. # 3. Results  

3.1 Changes in the coatings due to heat treatments  

The as-sprayed coating as well as the compositional and microstructural developments  within  the bulk of the  coating as a function of temperature have been presented previously [3]. They are briefly summarised here to  provide a basis of comparison for the coating microstructure developments near the interface due to  interdiffusion with the substrate.  

During spraying the Ni binder melts, leading to partial dissolution of the carbide grains. Rapid solidification  MANUSCRIPT upon impact generates a complex phase composition ranging from amorphous and metastable phases in the  metallic binder through to retained crystalline phases originating from the starting powder. Figure 3 presents  cross-sectional BSE images of the bulk coating and the coating/substrate interface. The darkest contrast features  are regions of porosity and oxide, the brightest contrast regions are the Ni binder, the dark grey blocky features  are the Cr 3 C 2  grains, while the light grey zones are regions where carbide grains dissolved into the Ni binder.  The coating was dense, with limited evidence of the splat boundary structure. The coating was well bonded to  the grit blasted profile of the substrate, with minimal interface contamination from retained alumina blasting grit  and little interface cracking. These features were the same for the mild steel and Alloy 625 substrates. Rietveld  analysis of the coating XRD pattern identified the phases in Table 2 [3].  

As a result of the heat treatment, the individual splats existing in the as-sprayed coating underwent a  densification by sintering. At the same time, the binder composition underwent a rapid transition into crystalline  phases of equilibrium composition [3, 10, 22-24, 35, 62] and the coating started to interact with the substrate by  interdiffusion.  

At 500 °C the zones of carbide dissolution rapidly transformed via carbide precipitation into the equilibrium  phases Cr 3  and a Ni alloy, to reach a steady state composition after five days, Table 2. The precipitates rapidly  grew in size over the first five days, beyond which only subtle changes in carbide grain size and morphology  occurred. The steady state coating microstructure consisted of large carbide grains retained from the starting  powder, surrounded by a continuous Ni binder within which high concentrations of individual, sub-micron sized  precipitates had formed, Figure 4a. At 700 °C and 900 °C the steady state composition formed within one day of  treatment, Table 2. At 700 °C the carbide precipitates grew by Ostwald ripening at a faster rate than at 500ºC  and began to form agglomerates within the Ni binder regions. After 30 days the coating microstructure consisted  of large carbide grains surrounded by a continuous Ni binder throughout which irregularly shaped carbide  agglomerates and interconnected networks of carbide precipitates had formed, Figure 4b. After one day at 900  °C the degree of carbide precipitation and agglomeration exceeded that seen at 700°C after 30 days. With  continued treatment the carbide grains became increasingly interconnected into a continuous network, while the  Ni binder became disbursed in more isolated pockets, Figure 4c.  

3.2 Heat treatment at 500°C  

After one day at 500°C, the coating microstructure near the interface underwent the same changes as in the bulk  of the coating, Figure 5a. The coating–substrate interface remained a distinct step change in microstructure with  no indication of interdiffusion (Note: the subtle brightness in the substrate contrast near the interface was due to  charging of the alumina grit particles and not a change in composition, as confirmed by EDS analysis). The  Alloy 625 microstructure was homogeneous from the interface down into the bulk of the substrate. Dark  MANUSCRIPT contrast, angular, cuboid particles, postulated to be a Ti-rich cubic MC ((Nb,Ti)(C,N)) [63, 64] based on EDS  analysis, were intermittently disbursed throughout the alloy, Figure 5b. Bright contrast, globular grains were  homogeneously distributed at the parent alloy grain boundaries, as highlighted by the black arrows in Figure 5c.  EDS analysis indicated that these were Mo-rich M 6 phase developments within the alloy, and not as a result of interdiffusion with the coating. With continued heat  treatment out to 30 days, the coating microstructure at the interface reflected the same microstructural  developments as seen in the bulk coating, while the substrate microstructure remained homogeneous from the  interface to the bulk alloy, Figure 6a-c. While this temperature was high enough to enable precipitation of  carbide grains in the former carbide dissolution zones of the coating, there were no evident microstructural  developments to indicate interdiffusion with the substrate.  

EDS "spot" analyses showed that, after one day at 500°C, the bulk coating binder composition was dominated  by Ni and Cr, with the concentration of Mo and Nb both below 1 wt%. After one day and 30 days, the coating  binder composition near the interface showed no further change from the initial bulk coating binder  composition, implying no significant interdiffusion occurred. Analysis of variations in the binder Cr content  were complicated by the inclusion of small carbide precipitates within the EDS analysis volume, leading to  elevated Cr concentrations and preventing any definitive conclusion on Cr interdiffusion.  

The concentration profile ratios for Cr, Mo and Nb relative to Ni, obtained from the EDS line scans showed no  changes after 30 days at 500°C, confirming that no detectable interdiffusion occurred, Figure 7a.  

The Ni, Cr, Mo and Nb elemental profiles from the averaged area EDS analysis showed no changes out to 30  days, Figure 7b, further confirming the results of the line scan analysis. The significant variations in the Ni and  Cr profiles within the coating indicated that the area of analysis was not large enough relative to the  microstructural features to measure the average coating elemental composition. Future works adopting this  approach are recommended to follow the procedure in [35], using a wide rectangle of relatively thin height to  give a large area of analysis but retain a sensitivity as a function of depth into the coating.

4.4 Mechanism of microstructural development - Region four: Substrate precipitate zones  

The formation of a carbide-rich precipitate zone beneath a Cr-poor precipitate-free zone due to the consumption  of Cr by external Cr  formation has been previously reported [54, 55, 80, 81]. In those works it was  postulated that the reduction in Cr generated a carbon activity gradient that resulted in back–diffusion of carbon  from the interface into the bulk substrate. The increase in carbon within the alloy generated the observed carbide  formation i.e. carbide formation occurred due to a redistribution of the carbon within the alloy itself. To assess if  this mechanism occurred in this work, the volumetric concentrations of the M 23 C 6  (assumed to be Cr 23 C 6 ) and  M 6 C (assumed to be Mo C) precipitates were quantified by image analysis. Calculations indicated that the mass   precipitates alone was more than six times the mass of carbon within this  volume of the alloy (at an alloy concentration of 0.03 wt% C, Table 1).  

Oxidation of Alloy 625 at 900°C for up to 1000 hours generated a precipitate-free zone at the interface, below  which M 6 C carbides were formed [54, 55]. Notably, no M 23 C 6  carbides were observed to form. Given the back– diffusion mechanism of carbon from the interface to the bulk driven by the activity gradient, it is unlikely that  the additional carbon required to form the M 23 C 6  precipitates observed in this work would have diffused towards  the interface from within the bulk of the substrate. This is especially so given the uniform precipitation of M 6 C  throughout the bulk alloy. These observations, therefore, imply that carbon must have diffused from the coating  into the substrate. This conclusion is supported by carburisation trials on Ni-Cr-Mo alloys which observed the  formation of M 23 C 6  precipitates in the carbon–rich zone near the surface, below which M 6 C precipitates formed  at the lower carbide concentrations [52, 53]. The morphology of the precipitates and preferential grain boundary  formation of the M 6 C carbides at the furthest depths of the precipitate zone observed in this work, match the  model presented for carbon diffusion between a carbon–rich AISI 4130 steel and Inconel® 625 [49, 51]. In  particular, this highlights the preferential diffusion of carbon into the alloy along the alloy grain boundaries. # 5. Conclusions  

This work presents the first systematic study of the compositional and structural changes that result  -NiCr HVOF-sprayed coating and a Ni-based Alloy 625  substrate over the temperature range 500–900°C for periods up to 30 days. Important to mention that in this  layered structure is that both the substrate and the coating contain Ni and Cr as the main metallic constituents.  Kirkendall voids were not observed in the microscopic studies in this work. The key conclusions from this work  were:  

At 900°C, carbon diffused from the coating into the substrate leading to the precipitation of M 23 C 6  and  M 6 C carbides within the alloy grain boundaries. As source of this carbon it is proposed, that this carbon was  liberated from the Cr 7 C 3 . Within the substrate, Cr diffused  from the alloy to the coating–substrate interface to form a continuous oxide layer. It is postulated that Cr also  diffused through the oxide into the coating. The elevated Cr concentration within the coating enabled the  formation of a continuous band of (Cr,Ni) 7 C 3  along interface. The reduction in Cr within the alloy substrate led  to the formation of a barren, precipitate free zone adjacent to the interface.  

At 700°C, carbon also diffused into the substrate. This led to precipitation of M 23 C 6  within the  substrate and the precipitation of Cr 7 C 3  within the coating. However, the depth of precipitation was significantly  smaller than that seen at 900°C.  

At 500°C, significant microstructural development occurred within the coating and substrate, but there  was no microstructural or compositional evidence of interdiffusion between them.  

No interfacial oxide formed on the substrate at 500°C and 700°C, indicating that the coating was an  effective barrier to oxidation of the substrate.  However, significant oxide formation occurred within one day at  900°C and continued out to 30 days.  

For the very complex processes of diffusion and interaction between a Cr 3 C 2 -NiCr coating and a  substrate for high-temperature application many open questions remain and require more detailed experiments.

# Highlights  

•   First systematic study of interdiffusion between a Cr 3 C 2 -25NiCr coating and Alloy 625 at 500°C,  700°C and 900°C in air for up to 30 days 

 •   Precipitation of M 23 C 6 /M 6 C grains in alloy grain boundaries due to C diffusion from the coating  

 •   C was liberated from the Cr 3 C 2  grains in the coating due to formation of (Cr,Ni) 7 C 3  

 •   Cr diffusion from the substrate resulted in a continuous (Cr,Ni) 7 C 3  layer in the coating  

 •   Loss of Cr from the substrate led to the formation of a precipitate free zone at the interface
