The hot deformation behavior of the Waspaloy was investigated by means of hot compression tests, metallographic observations and processing map at temperature between 950  1 C and 1150  1 C, strain rate between 0.01 s  1   and 10 s  1   under strain of 0.8. The processing map was developed on the basis of experimental data, showing variations of the efﬁciency of power dissipation related to temperature and strain rate at constant strain. The processing map shows one stable domain, in which the dynamic recrystallization is the dominating microstructural phenomenon, and one instable domain. The results of interpretation of ﬂow stress curves and processing map were veriﬁed by microstructural observa- tions. The efﬁciency peak of the processing map is 0.37 at the temperature of 1150  1 C and the strain rate of 10 s  1 .The stable domain is within the temperature range between 1050  1 C and 1150  1 C and strain rate between 0.01 s  1 and 10 s  1 , and leads to recrystallized and equiaxed microstructure. The grain size in this domain increases at increasing temperature or decreasing strain rate. The instability domain is within the temperature range between 950  1 C and 1000  1 C and strain rate between 0.01 s  1   and 10 s  1   (the temperature of 1000  1 C and strain rate of 0.01 s  1   is excluded). In this domain, the instability can be observed in terms of fracture, shear bands, ﬂow localization, intergranular and triple junction cracking.

# 1. Introduction  

Waspaloy is a widely used wrought nickel-based superalloy for gas turbine discs, rings, and blades  [1] , which exhibits sufﬁciently high temperature creep, fatigue, and oxidation resis- tance properties for operating at temperatures up to around 700  1 C  [2] .  

The alloy high-temperature strength is derived mainly from precipitates of the Ni 3 (Al,Ti) phase ( g 0 , ordered L1 2  structure), which are embedded within the disordered face centered cubic matrix ( g  phase)  [3] . Furthermore, solid solution strengthening elements, e.g. molybdenum, cobalt and chromium, have a minor high-temperature strength effect. The addition of carbon to the alloy composition promotes the formation of carbides in the form of MC and M 23 C 6  along grain boundaries, which enhance the creep resistance of the alloy through grain boundary pinning  [4] .  

To ensure the required product properties for each speciﬁc application, the Waspaloy is usually forged and heat-treated to achieve a proper microstructure; in particular, two types of micro- structures are of interest: (1) a microstructure with a grain size  

Shen et al.  [5]  developed a model to predict the evolution of the Waspaloy microstructure during thermo-mechanical processing in terms of dynamic recrystallization, metadynamic recrystallization, and grain growth phenomena. They found similarities between the hot-working response of Waspaloy and that of C–Mn steels. These include the Avrami dependence of the recrystallization kinetics on strain for dynamic recrystallization or time for metadynamic recrys- tallization; moreover, the effect of as-preheated grain size, imposed reduction, and temperature-compensated strain rate ( Z ) on dyna- mically and metadynamically recrystallized grain size was analyzed.  

Chang and Liu  [7]  researched on the effects of the alloy chemistry, cooling rate and solution temperature on the mechan- ical behavior of the Waspaloy alloy system. They reported that the tensile strength increases with increasing Al þ Ti content in the alloys. They found that the solution temperature and cooling rate had almost no effect on the Waspaloy tensile properties; conversely, the increase in precipitation content improved the stress rupture life of the alloys tremendously.  

Semiatin et al.  [8]  established the slip systems that operate during hot working of Waspaloy by hot-compression testing, and conducted slip-trace analysis on Waspaloy single crystals. By using slip-trace analysis and ﬂow-curve measurements, they concluded that deformation occurred via a classical octahedral 111 / 110 S slip system.  

All of these researches are related either to ﬂow behavior or microstructure evolution, while an intensive investigation that can describe the ﬂow behavior with regard to microstructural phenomena still lacks. Therefore, the objective of the paper is to study the hot deformation behavior of Waspaloy and its correla- tion with its microstructural evolution. In particular, two different microstructural phenomena will be investigated, namely dynamic restoration phenomena (dynamic recovery and/or dynamic recrystallization, which is desirable), and ﬂow instability (ﬂow localization, shear bands, intergranular cracks, triple junction cracks, which have to be avoided).  

The ﬂow behavior was investigated by carrying out hot compres- sion tests on cylindrical samples of Waspaloy under a wide and applicable range of temperature and strain rate. The obtained stress–strain curves were studied through conventional hot working ﬂow models, and then the processing map was developed and interpreted to evaluate stable and instable domains for deformation. Microstructural observations were performed on the deformed samples for the results validation.

# 3. Flow behavior  

The shape of the ﬂow stress curves obtained under hot deformation behavior depends on microstructural phenomena arising during deformation at different temperatures and strain rates. The ﬂow curves have generally three stages: (I) work hardening till a peak of stress is reached, (II) softening and continuous decrease in the ﬂow stress, (III) steady state ﬂow stress  [9] . The work hardening in the ﬁrst stage is associated with the increase of dislocation density  [10] , which can be seen under all the investigated deformation conditions. In the second stage, subgrains are formed and dynamic recovery takes place, therefore ﬂow stress will remain steady. Although in low staking fault alloy (e.g. nickel alloys) the development of subgrains is difﬁcult, the density of dislocations increases to a critical amount after that recrystallization occurs. Recrystallization develops new grains, making the elimination of dislocations faster than their creation, so the ﬂow stress starts decreasing. In the third stage, the rate of work hardening due to the dislocation density increase and the rate of softening due to the dislocation elimination, reach a balance, therefore allowing a steady state ﬂow stress.

# 5. Microstructural analysis  

Figs. 4 and 5  show the microstructures and deformed samples, respectively, at strain of 0.8 and different deformation conditions.  

In  Fig. 4 a the microstructure of the sample deformed at temperature of 950  1 C and strain rate equal to 0.01 s  1   shows ﬂow localization in the middle region. Longitudinal cracks appear on the sample ( Fig. 5 a) and propagate in the entire sample height. These cracks can be a result of the tensile stress in the hoop direction. Because of hoop stress and dislocation tangles at the grain boundaries, the stress increases locally to such an extent that cracks at grain boundaries open. The microstructure of this sample, near its surface, shows intergranular and also triple junction boundaries cracks ( Fig. 6 ). The ﬂow instability in terms of shear bands is observed clearly in the microstructures of samples deformed at temperature of 950  1 C and strain rates of 0.1 s  1   and 1 s  1   ( Fig. 4 b and c). The images of the deformed samples ( Fig. 5 b and c) show cracks, which are concentrated on an imaginary plane inclined at an angle of 45 1  with regard to the compression axis. The dominating stress state in these conditions is the shear stress that is maximum at 45 1 .These ﬂow instabilities are the reason of the continuing decrease in the ﬂow stress curves observed in  Fig. 2 (a).  

Fig. 5 (d) shows the sample compressed at 950  1 C and 10 s  1 : it does not almost experience any plastic deformation and breaks in a brittle way at an angle of 45 1 . The high strain rate rapidly increases the dislocation density. Due to dislocation tangling, most of the slipping systems remain inactive; at 950  1 C, the activation energy is not enough to initiate dynamic restoration, therefore the sample breaks in a brittle way.  

At 1000  1 C and strain rate of 0.01 s  1   and 0.1 s  1   ( Fig. 4 e and f) shear bands are not evident, although some elongated grains are present. Very ﬁne grains can be seen at the boundaries of the elongated grains, which is an indication of dynamic recrystalliza- tion onset through the nucleation of new grains. Although ﬂow instability is not observed in their microstructures, the samples shown in  Fig. 5 e and f show non uniform metal ﬂow, with very ﬁne cracks on their surface. # 6. Conclusions  

The hot deformation of Waspaloy was studied in the tempera- ture range between 950  1 C and 1150  1 C, at strain rate between 0.01 s  1   and 10 s  1   and at the strain of 0.8 by means of hot compression tests. The ﬂow stress curves and the resulting proces- sing map were interpreted, and then veriﬁed by microstructural observations. The obtained results can be summarized as follows:  

1) There is a stable region (domain 2) on the processing map in the temperature range between 1050  1 C and 1150  1 C and in the strain rate range between 0.01 s  1 and 10 s  1 . The efﬁ- ciency of power dissipation in this domain is more than 0.3, indicating that dynamic recrystallization has occurred. The ﬂow stress curves corresponding to domain 2 ﬁrst reach a peak, then the ﬂow stress decreases before attaining a steady state. These kinds of ﬂow curves are typically observed when dynamic recrystallization occurs. Microstructural observations reveal that the initial grains are replaced by ﬁner and equiaxed grains with wavy or serrated grain boundaries, indicating that dynamic recrystallization has occurred.  

2) There is an instability region (domain 1) on the processing map in the temperature range between 950  1 C and 1000  1 C and in the strain rate range between 0.01 s  1   and 10 s  1   (the temperature of 1000  1 C and strain rate of 0.01 s  1   is excluded). The ﬂow stress curves show a continuous decrease after reaching a peak. The samples microstructure observations clearly indicate the occurrence of ﬂow instability in form of fracture, shear band, ﬂow localization, intergranular and triple junction cracking.  

3) There are two optimum conditions for the hot working of Waspaloy at the strain of 0.8: (1) the optimum condition to obtain a ﬁne grained microstructure is at temperature of 1050  1 C and strain rate of 10 s  1 ; (2) the optimum condition to produce a coarse grained microstructure is at temperature of 1150  1 C and strain rate of 0.01 s  1 .
