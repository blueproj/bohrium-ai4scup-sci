# Scaling up of High-Pressure Sliding (HPS) for Grain Reﬁnement and Superplasticity  

YOICHI TAKIZAWA, TAKAHIRO MASUDA, KAZUSHIGE FUJIMITSU, TAKAHIRO KAJITA, KYOHEI WATANABE, MANABU YUMOTO, YOSHIHARU OTAGIRI, and ZENJI HORITA  

The process of high-pressure sliding (HPS) is a method of severe plastic deformation developed recently for grain reﬁnement of metallic materials under high pressure. The sample for HPS is used with a form of sheet or rod. In this study, an HPS facility with capacities of 500 tonnes for vertical pressing and of 500 and 300 tonnes for horizontal forward and backward pressings, respectively, was newly built and applied for grain reﬁnement of a Mg alloy as AZ61, Al alloys such as Al-Mg-Sc, A2024 and A7075 alloys, a Ti alloy as ASTM-F1295, and a Ni-based superalloy as Inconel 718. Sheet samples with dimensions of 10 to 30 mm width, 100 mm length, and 1 mm thickness were processed at room temperature and ultraﬁne grains with sizes of  \~ 200 to 300 nm were successfully produced in the alloys. Tensile testing at elevated temperatures conﬁrmed the advent of superplasticity with total elongations of more than 400 pct in all the alloys. It is demonstrated that the HPS can make all the alloys superplastic through processing at room temperature with a form of rectangular sheets.

# I. INTRODUCTION  

G RAIN  reﬁnement is an important requirement for high strength and/or enhanced ductility in metallic materials. For the former, the strength increases with a decrease in the grain size through the Hall–Petch 1 , ]   relation. Extra strengthening may be expected if the grain size is reduced to the submicrometer or to the nanometer levels and the ﬁne-grained structure is free of 3 ]   dislocations. For the latter, the ductility is well enhanced as an advent of superplasticity, although the deformation should be carried out at elevated temper- 4 ]   ature. According to the constitutive equation for the deformation, the superplasticity may appear at higher strain rates when the grain size is reduced to the 4 – 10 ] submicrometer ranges.  

It is well known that the grain reﬁnement to the submicrometer and/or nanometer range is achieved using the process of severe plastic deformation 11 ]   (SPD). Although several processes are available for 12 , ]   the SPD, the process under high pressure, known 14 – 16 ]   as high-pressure torsion (HPT), is eﬀective for the grain reﬁnement in hard and less ductile materials even 17 – 22 ]   23 – 26 ]   such as intermetallics, semiconductors, and 27 , ]   ceramics. The sample for the HPT processing is [ 14 ]   [ 29 – 31 ]   used in a form of disk or ring and it may be extended to ribbons or wires if they are processed in a 32 , ] continuous way using continuous HPT (CHPT). However, the direct application to sheet or rod samples should be feasible when sliding under high pressure, called high-pressure sliding (HPS), is used as schemat- 34 ]   ically illustrated in Figure  1 . This process was devel- [ 34 ]   [ 35 ]   oped such that two samples of sheets or rods were placed between the anvils and plunger. The plunger was then pushed for shearing while applying a load on the samples between the anvils.  

In this study, the HPS facility is scaled up so that it is possible to increase the sample size and apply to high-strength materials such as a Ni-based superalloy and a Ti-based alloy. It is then demonstrated that the scaled-up HPS facility can produce superplasticity with the total elongation of  > 400 pct not only in conven- tional high-strength aluminum alloys such as A2024 and A7075 but also in high-strength alloys as Inconel 718 known as a typical heat resistance alloy and an ASTM F1295 Ti alloy used for a good biocompatible alloy. It is also shown that the scaled-up facility can also produce superplasticity in a common magnesium alloy as AZ61 which is rather less ductile in deformation at room temperature.

# IV. DISCUSSION  

# A.  Scaling up and Reciprocal Sliding  

It has now been demonstrated that the HPS process can be scaled up to 500 tonnes so that it is successful to 3   extend the sample size to 30  9  100  9  1 mm for the alloys with relatively lower strengths as the Mg alloy and the AlMgSc alloy. The present study has also demonstrated that it is feasible to process the higher strength alloys such as the Ti-based and Ni-based alloys. Although the HPT process allows unlimited straining by continuous rotation of the upper anvil with respect to the lower anvil, it should be possible to introduce unlimited strain into the sample by reciprocating the plunger in the forward and backward directions. # B.  Microstructural Homogeneity  

Microstructural homogeneity may be evaluated from hardness measurements. Figure  14  shows the hardness variations on the longitudinal planes as well as on the cross-sectional planes from the A2024 samples after HPS processing for the sliding distances of (a) x  = 10 mm under 2 GPa, (b) x  = 20 mm under 2 GPa, and (c)  x  = 20 mm under 3 GPa. Here, the hardness is plotted by normalizing by the value at the hardness saturation which is 260 Hv as reported earlier 39 ]   on the same alloy. The mean hardness value becomes higher with the increase in the sliding distance and with the increase in the applied pressure. Despite such diﬀerences in the hardness, the local hardness variation on each of the planes is minor as within the range of \~ 10 Hv. Figure  15  shows the hardness variation and the total elongation as a function of the distance from the rear edge in the F1295 alloy. While there is little diﬀerence in the hardness along the longitudinal axis, the total elongation is low at the rear edge. The reason for this lower elongation is now in investigation. It is concluded that the microstructural homogeneity exists throughout the cross section and along the longitudinal planes except the rear edge part.  

# C.  Dynamic Recrystallization and/or Grain Growth  

TEM observation has shown that the microstructures have not reached the state consisting of grains with high-angle misorientations but subgrains are mostly developed for the Mg alloy and partially for the Ti alloy (F1295) and Ni alloy (Inconel 718). It is considered that dynamic recrystallization and/or grain growth occurred during the tensile testing in these alloys because super- plastic deformation requires ﬁne-grained structures with high angles of misorientations which facilitate grain boundary sliding. In order to check the occurrence of dynamic recrystallization and/or grain growth, TEM observation was conducted after tensile testing on the HPS-processed F1295 and Inconel 718 alloys. # V. SUMMARY AND CONCLUSIONS  

1. Scaling up of the HPS developed as a process of SPD under high pressure was achieved and the process was successfully applied to various alloys including a Mg alloy (AZ61), three Al alloys (Al-3 pctMg-0.2 pctSc, A2024, and A7075), a Ti alloy (ASTM-F1295), and a Ni-based superalloy (Inconel 718).

 2. Using this scaled-up HPS process, grain refinement to the sizes of 200 to 300 nm was feasible in all the alloys with sheet samples having dimensions of 10 to 30 mm width, 100 mm length, and 1 mm thickness.

 3. All samples processed by the HPS at room temper- ature gave rise to superplastic elongation of more than 400 pct together with significant reductions of the maximum flow stress. In particular, the Inconel 718 exhibited superplastic elongations more than 460 pct with an initial strain rate of 2  9  10  2   s  1   at a testing temperature of 1073 K (800   C).

 4. In the AZ61 alloy, Ti alloy, and Inconel 718, dynamic recrystallization during tensile deforma- tion occurs and this might have promoted grain boundary sliding for the superplasticity.

 5. It is demonstrated that the HPS process is effective to create superplastic property in sheet forms of com- mercially available Mg, Al, Ti, and Ni-based alloys.
