# Influence of thermo mechanical processing on grain boundary character  

# distribution of Hastelloy X alloy  

Xiaoyan Wang 1 , Arnaud Dallemagne 2 , Yaqing Hou 1 , Sen Yang  

1  School of Materials Science and Engineering, Nangjing University of Science and  Technology, No. 200 Xiaolingwei, Nanjing 210094, China  2 Ecole Nationale Supérieure des Mines d'Alès, No.6 avenue de Clavières, Alès, 30319,  France  

# Abstract  

The present work investigates the effect of thermo mechanical processing on grain  boundary character distribution (GBCD) in Hastelloy X alloy. The deformation ratio, and  annealing condition were considered to obtain optimum process. The experimental results  showed that low pre-strain provided appropriate driving force for CSL boundary  regenaration through static recovery process. The short time annealing treatment at high  temperature was performed to obtain the optimized GBCD, as well as control grain growth.  The fraction of special boundaries reached 72% and the random boundary network was  dispersive to a great level.

# 1.   Introduction  

Hastelloy X alloy, also known as HX alloy, is a solid solution strengthened nickel based  superalloy and possesses a good combination of excellent oxidation resistance, formability  and high-temperature strength [1-4]. It has been extensively used in gas turbine engines for  combustion zone components and also recommended as a potential candidate material for  high-temperature gas cooled reactor (HTGR) [5, 6]. For many of plant components of  HTGR, materials selection is strongly influenced by high temperature and the highest  safety and most reliable operation are required. Therefore, development of materials with  high performance under extreme environment becomes an meaningful issue [7, 8]. It has  been proved that HX alloy suffered from intergranular attack and internal oxidation in high  temperature gas mixtures [9]. A few research works on HX alloy have been conducted to  achieve better environmental resistance and more excellent mechanical properties at  elevated temperature. Some protective coating processes have been conducted to improve  the high temperature properties of HX alloy, but thermal stress induced cracks and the  spalling of the protective layer remain unsolved [4, 10]. Regarding the high temperature  scaling on HX alloy, a modified version, Hastelloy XR, has been developed by Japan  Atomic Energy Research Institute [9]. However, the failure related to intergranular attack at  high temperature is still inevitable [11].  

A large number of experimental results have shown that intergranular failure presents  strongly selective associated with grain boundary energy [12-14]. In the last three decades,  grain boundary engineering (GBE), which was first proposed by Tadao Watanabe, as “an  approach to grain boundary design and control for strong and ductile polycrystals” in 1984  [15], has been extensively applied to improve bulk properties in polycrystal materials [16],  such as stress corrosion resistance, high-temperature creep, and fatigue crack propagation  [17-19]. Property improvements associated with GBE have been attributed to the increase  in the proportion of low Σ (1≤Σ≤29) coincidence site lattice (CSL) boundaries and the  redistribution of grain boundary network topology. In fact, many researchers have shown  that, the connectivity of the boundaries is more susceptible to damage than the character of  individual boundaries [20, 21]. In other words, the boundary network structure has stronger  effect on materials properties, especially on arresting the extension of intergranular failure.  In recent years, the discussions about the grain boundary network evolution mainly focus  on the regenerated Σ3 boundaries of low or medium stacking-fault energy (SFE) materials,  however, the boundary evolution mechanism remains open [22-24].  

Thermo mechanical processing (TMP) is mainly utilized to create conditions of grain  boundary migration and achieve optimization of GBCD. In recent decades, some  investigations have been carried out to study the effect of processing routes on GBCD [21,  25, 26]. M. Michiuchi et al. [27] obtained a rather high fraction of CSL boundaries (86%)  in type 316 austenitic stainless steel by one-step process. However, there is no agreement  reached on the optimal TMP route until now when considering the material characteristics.  HX alloy has been reported to be prone to intergranular failure in the simulated high- temperature HTGR helium environment[11]. However, the grain boundary optimization in  HX alloy has not been considered until now. Therefore, the aim of present work is to study  the feasibility of GBE to HX alloy and systematically investigate the influence of TMP on  the boundary network evolution.  

# 2.   Experimental  

The chemical composition of HX alloy used in this study is listed in Table 1. The as- received materials were cut into 10 mm ×10 mm ×100 mm for subsequent process. In order  to avoid grain boundary pinning by carbides while grain boundary migrates, HX alloy was  solution annealed at 1175 °C for 15minutes followed by water quenching in advance,  termed as the based materials (BM). And then the BM was cold rolled and annealed to  modify the GBCD. The effect of reduction ratio, annealing temperature and annealing time  on the grain boundary microstructure were explored, which are respectively designated as  types A, B and C. Due to the great influence of solute elements on boundary migration,  annealing temperature for HX alloy was chosen from 1140 °C. The processing schedules  and the corresponding names are listed in Table 2.  

Following thermo mechanical treatment, the specimens were cut perpendicularly to the  rolling direction. The samples were prepared with a general metallographic procedure to a  mirror-like surface followed by electro-etched in a solution containing 10% oxalic acid in  deionized water at a voltage of 20V for 8s. The microstructure observations were conducted  by Carl Zeiss AxioVision microscope. Hitachi SU1510 Tungsten Filament Gun Scanning  Electron Microscope (SEM) equipped with an Oxford Instruments Nordlys EBSD system  was employed to quantify the average intragranular misorientations and determine the  character of grain boundaries. Grain boundaries were classified on the basis of the CSL  mode as special boundary and random boundary. Brandon’s criterion was adopted for the  critical deviation in the determination of the Σ value. The fraction of grain boundaries was  obtained from the average result of three different regions.  

3. Results  

# 3.1 Grain boundary microstructure of BM  

Fig.1 shows the microstructure characteristics and GBCD of the BM specimen, where the  red and black lines represent Σ3 boundary and random boundary in Fig. 1(b), respectively.  Under the solid solution condition, equiaxed austenite grains were observed in Fig. 1. The  grain size presented a bimodal distribution that the diameters of the bigger grains were  among 100-200μm and that of the smaller grains were among 20-70μm. Some strings  consisting of the primary M 6 C carbide particles along the longitudinal direction were  observed, as shown in Fig. 1(a). As can be seen, there were almost no other CSL  boundaries presented except the numerous long and parallel-sided Σ3 boundaries in Fig.  1(b). Statistics analysis of grain boundary length revealed that the fraction of low Σ CSLs  was 58.76%, including 55.80% Σ3 boundaries. Most of twins were present in the large  grains in the form of coherent Σ3, which indicated that twins were mainly generated during  grain growth stage. In addition, Σ9 and Σ27 boundaries are regarded as the ramification of  incoherent Σ3 regeneration and critical segments in the development of grain boundary  networks, which were rarely seen on BM. As a consequence, the random boundaries  presented a connective network covering the whole EBSD map. Therefore, to improve the  intergranular resistance at elevated temperature, GBCD of HX alloy was optimized further.  

# 3.2 Effect of pre-strain on grain boundary microstructure  

Fig.2 shows the microstructure characteristics evolution undergoing type A process. The  low-strain process (A-1) mainly caused grain boundary migration and slight grain growth,  and larger reductions induced recrystallized grains and new annealed twins. Some  inhomogeneous grains could be observed, especially in large strain samples. They were  caused by cold rolling process and the carbides strings. During cold rolling process, the  grains that had special orientations with reduction direction might suffer a larger strain than  other grains, and the inhomogeneous plastic flow had a greater impact on the  microstructure on large reduction samples. On the other hand, it could be noticed that  carbides strings were apt to block boundary migration and many new grains nucleated  around the carbides on strain-recrystallization process. Fig. 3 presents the grain boundary  distribution of samples subjected to type A process. It can be observed that, CSL boundary  type and distribution varied from irregular and dispersive to ordered and paralleled with the  reduction ratio increased. It indicated that random boundary network was disrupted under  lower pre-strain, especially 5% reduction in thickness. Correspondingly, Fig.4 reveals the  fractions of CSL boundaries with different reduction ratios. What should be noted is that,  the contribution of Σ1 was not included in the fraction of low Σ CSLs due to the high mis- indexing ratio to Σ1 boundaries. Here, only the fraction of Σ9 was used to predict GBE  level because the fraction of Σ27 was much less than that of Σ9. With increase of the  reduction ratio, the fraction of low ΣCSL boundaries decreased firstly, and then increased.  The maximum CSL fraction showed at 5% reduction and the minimum one at 20%. It can  be found that there was a turning point of CSL distribution at 20% reduction from  regenerated incoherent twins to coherent twins. On the other hand, with increasing  reduction ratio, the fraction of Σ9 boundaries decreased, which meant the lower pre-strain  was benefit for optimizing GBCD.  

# 3.3 Effect of annealing treatment on GBCD  

Annealing treatment was conducted to supply thermal activation energy for grain  boundary migration during GBE treatment. Fig. 5 shows the microstructure characteristics  of the CR 5% samples undergoing annealing for 8 minutes at a series of temperature. Grain  growth rate presented a great dependence on temperature ， which kept slow and uniform at  1140°C and was accelerated at or upon 1160°C. As can be seen in B-1 sample, boundaries  very slightly migrated at 1140°C which was primarily attributed to the dragging of solute  atoms. Localised recrystallization and boundary enrichment were also observed along  carbides string, and this was indicative of stress concentration on carbides and the role of  migrating obstacle. However, the microstructures after annealing at or above 1160°C  presented apparent grain growth and curving boundary network. The carbides dissolved  with increasing temperature, which was also one reason of active boundaries migration. It  could be concluded that 1160°C was the critical temperature of boundary migration for HX  alloy. Fig.6 shows the GBCD of the pre-strained specimens annealed at various  temperatures, and Fig.7 shows the relationship between the fraction of low ΣCSL GBs and  annealing temperature. The results further verified that a lower temperature (1140°C) was  not sufficient to supply mechanical activation energy to overcome the diffusion energy  barrier, and the twins were distributed mainly in the internal grains and the network of  random boundaries remained high connectivity. Annealing at medium temperature induced  formation of more CSL GBs and caused interruption to a greater extent. However, the  grains grew rapidly as annealing temperature was in excess of 1180°C, reaching the biggest  grain size of 500μm at 1220°C. Though the fraction of CSL and Σ9 boundaries showed a  steady increasing trend with the rise in temperature, the grains at 1200°C and 1220°C were  too large to get accurate test result. In conclusion, 1160°C was considered as the optimal  annealing temperature to balance the GBCD and grain size.  

Fig.8 shows the microstructure evolution of CR5% sample annealing at 1180°C. It  presented a typical process of boundary migration and grain growth under different stages.  The effects of annealing time on GBCD and the fraction of low ΣCSL GBs were observed  in Fig.9 and Fig.10. The grain boundary migration and reaction mostly happened at the first  five minutes of annealing, and then grain growth was dominated as the annealing time  increased. It also can be known that the fractions of CSL and Σ9 boundaries increased  rapidly at the first five minutes and then rarely changed with time increasing, especially for  Σ9 boundaries. The CSL fraction of C-2 sample reached 72% and the random boundary  network was dispersive to a great level. Therefore, 5 minutes annealing could achieve grain  boundary interactions, as well as avoid grain growth. Hence, the GBCD of HX alloy could  be effectively optimized through a 5% reduction followed by annealing treatment at  1160°C for 5 minutes.  

# 4. Discussion  

The dislocation evolution in cubic metal during cold rolling has been well studied [28-30].  The main features of the microstructures are summarized into four stages as a function of  strain. Dislocations exist as planar arrays or tangles at low strains, and then with further  deformation, the forms of dislocations present cells and subgrains. Due to either  inhomogeneous stress transmitted by neighboring grains or the intrinsic instability of the  grain during plastic deformation, microbands appear and divide a grain into regions with  different orientation. When the strain is up to 60%, shear bands inclining to the rolling  plane occur through several grains or even all the specimen. During annealing treatment,  strain-recovery and strain-recrystallization occurred respectively at low strain and high  strain samples. Previous researches [31-33] mentioned that the incorporation processes of  extrinsic dislocations in singular, vicinal grain boundaries required the interaction with the  intrinsic network and the decomposition of dislocation into discrete observable products.  For A-1 sample, homogeneous planar linear dislocations after 5% strain were aroused and  migrated at high temperature. Based on Nazarov model [33], the dislocations decomposed  into glissile and sessile components at coherent Σ3 boundary. The glissile dislocation  moved away and accumulated at triple junctions as shown in the dashed circles in Fig. 8;  the sessile one reorganize within the grain boundary and the twins kept the original  microstructure and type, as showed in the schematic illustration of Fig.11. The intensive  dislocations at triple junctions provided a driving force to boundary reaction. Therefore, a  plenty of coherent Σ3 boundaries were retained and at the same time the connective random  boundary network was interrupted through boundary reaction. Subjected to 10% strain,  dislocation tangles might occur in A-2 sample. Meanwhile, the dislocations rearrangement  and counteraction decreased the stored energy for grain boundary migration. Consequently,  the microstructure in A-2 sample did not develop as expected. For A-3 sample, medium  pre-strain led to boundary redistribution through partial recrystallization, which induced  some CSL boundary disappearing. Recrystallization happened from locally to  homogeneously with strain increasing. Correspondingly, annealing twins within grains  appeared again which makes the problem go back to the BM point.  

Controlling the mobility and migration of boundaries played a critical role on annealing  treatment. High temperature was required to overcome the dragging impact of solute atoms  and precipitations, and on the other hand, the diffusion coefficient of grain boundaries  rapidly increased with the increasing temperature. From type B samples, boundary  migration was activated at 1160 °C which resulted in CSL boundary clusters formation.  However, at higher temperature, smaller grains were annihilated and some instable CSL  boundaries were swept out with the boundaries migrating. The study on annealing time was  much more apparent. When boundary reaction caused by lattice distortion finished, grain  growth was dominated to decrease boundary energy.  

# Conclusions  

GBE was employed to HX alloy by means of TMP in this study. The effects of pre-strain  and annealing treatment on grain boundary evolution were investigated. Low pre-strain was  beneficial to CSL boundary regeneration through recovery process. Annealing treatment at  high temperature for short time could not only obtain the optimized GBCD, but also  effectively control grain size. The optimized GBCD was obtained through a 5% pre-strain  followed by annealing at 1160°C for 5 minutes, where the fraction of special boundaries  could reach 72%. The optimized  boundary microstructure retained plenty of coherent twins  of BM during strain-recovery and obtained dispersive random boundary network caused by  boundary migration and reaction.
