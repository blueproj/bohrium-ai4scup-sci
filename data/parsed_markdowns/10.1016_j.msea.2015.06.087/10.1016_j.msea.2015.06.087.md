# Abstract  

Electron beam welding (EBW) of shrouds in Inconel-713LC low pressure gas turbine blades was  associated with cracking in fusion zone (FZ) and heat affected zone (HAZ) leading to a high  scrap rate in manufacturing of gas turbine blades. In this study, in order to develop a detailed  map of cracks and understand the root cause of cracking, a comprehensive microstructural and  numerical analysis was performed. The elemental mapping in scanning electron microscope  (SEM)-energy dispersive spectral analysis revealed segregation of alloying elements in the  cracked area of FZ and HAZ. In other words, one of the cracking mechanisms in FZ and HAZ  was found to be segregation induced liquation and subsequent cracking due to thermal and  mechanical tensile stresses generated during EBW. Cracking in FZ also occurred because of low  strength of the solidifying weld metal as well as solidification contraction. As well,  γ’  dissolution  and reprecipitation in HAZ leading to decreased ductility and generation of contraction stresses  was another mechanism for cracking in HAZ. The numerical model was capable to predict the  cracking location as well as cracking orientation with respect to the weld line.  

Key Words : Electron beam welding; Inconel-713LC; cracking; segregation induced liquation;  contraction; numerical modeling  

# 1. Introduction  

Inconel-713LC is an investment cast low carbon (LC) Ni-based superalloy which is  widely used as stage 3 blades in hot sections of power generation and aircraft gas turbines with a  service temperature in the range of 500 to 915   C [1, 2]. This application needs a high strength  and good oxidation/corrosion resistance at elevated temperatures. The nominal chemical  composition of this alloy by weight percent is: 12.8 Cr, 4.15 Mo, 0.41 Co, 1.73 Nb, 6.2 Al, 1.04  Ti, 0.02 Si, 0.19 Fe, 0.06 Ta, 0.05 Zr, 0.12 C, 0.017 B, 0.007 P, 0.007 S, and balance Ni [3, 4].  The room and high temperature strength of the alloy is mainly provided by the    particles, an  intermetallic Ni 3 (Al, Ti) compound, coherently precipitated in the    matrix. Also, solid solution  elements,  e.g.  Fe, Cr,  Nb, and Mo supply additional strength for the alloy [5]. A cast coarse   grain size also contributes to the high temperature creep resistance [1]. Presence of MC and  M 23 C 6  carbides at grain boundaries is essential as well to have the desired strength and ductility  through inhibiting or retarding grain boundary sliding [1, 6]. Furthermore, the oxidation and  corrosion resistance of the alloy is provided by Al and Cr through formation of protective Al 2 O 3   and Cr 2 O 3  oxide films [6].  

Advanced fusion welding techniques such as EBW and laser welding have been widely  used to assemble or repair various metallic components including cast Ni-based superalloys in  gas turbines [7, 8]. For instance, EBW has been used to join together the shrouds of Inconel- 713LC aircraft low pressure turbine blades. In EBW process, electrons are emitted from a  filament and they are directed toward and concentrated on the work piece by electromagnetic  coils. The kinetic energy of electrons is transformed into thermal energy at the work piece  surface which leads to melting of the metal and production of a weld.  

Compared to other fusion welding techniques, the sizes of FZ and HAZ in electron beam  welded (EBWed) component is minimum due to lower heat input. Also, lower levels of  distortion and residual stresses are generated. Moreover, the high vacuum avoids contamination  of the molten metal and generates deep penetration [9]. However, fusion welding of cast Ni- based superalloys has been a challenging issue over the years because of cracking in FZ and  HAZ [10-13]. Although the FZ and HAZ cracking mechanisms in several cast Ni-based  superalloys such as Inconel-738LC [8, 14] and René 80 [15] have been well investigated and  reported, in open literature little data are available on the observation of cracking in FZ and HAZ  of EBWed Inconel-713LC and even less on the cracking mechanisms. In other words, in open  literature the factors contributing to the extensive cracking in FZ and HAZ of EBWed Inconel- 713LC have been neither related to the pre-weld microstructure nor the EBW process  parameters.  

Accordingly, this study has been undertaken to develop a detailed map of cracks and  investigate the contribution of the microstructural features and EBW process variables to the  cracking. Specifically, microstructural analysis included compositional study to map macro and  micro segregations of alloying elements in the microstructure using SEM combined with energy  dispersive spectroscopy (EDS). The obtained microstructural results will be used as a basis for  optimizing the pre-weld microstructure for reducing or suppressing cracking.  

Also, a numerical model for the EBW process was developed and validated based on the  different similar works such as Ferro  et al.  model on EBW of Inconel-706 [9] to recognize the  correlation between the EBW process parameters and generated thermal/mechanical stresses in  FZ and HAZ. Understanding of this relationship is useful in optimization of the process  parameters to minimize the generated thermal/mechanical tensile stresses and thereby, reduce the  chance of cracking. By this means, the scrap rate of expensive components and thus, the  manufacturing cost can be reduced considerably.  

# 2. Experimental Material and Procedures  

Five scrap pieces of Inconel-713LC stage 3 cast gas turbine blades EBWed in the shrouds  (Fig. 1) were received from Siemens Canada Limited, the manufacturer of gas turbines. The  dimensions of the shrouds are provided in Fig. 1. EBW of the shrouds consisted of a main  welding pass and a cosmetic pass while during EBW no longitudinal, transverse or vertical  constraints were applied. Initially the shrouds were sectioned from the blades using an abrasive  alumina oxide cut-off wheel. Since some of the cracks were visible by eye, the whole FZ and  HAZ of EBWed shrouds were initially examined under a DSX500 Olympus optical microscope  to determine initiation site, geometrical location, orientation with respect to the weld line,  morphology, and length of cracks.  

Then, transverse sections to the weld line were prepared using the same cutting wheel.  Automated grinding and polishing procedures were subsequently used to prepare the samples for  microstructural examination. The microstructure of FZ, HAZ, and parent material (PM) were  evaluated by an optical DSX500 Olympus microscope, Hitachi 3600 N SEM and a Hitachi  TM3030 SEM. From the optical microscopy investigations, the cracking features were  determined. Some of the optical microscopy investigations were performed in the as-polished  condition and some of them in the etched condition. To etch the samples for optical microscopy  examinations, the polished surface was electroetched in a solution of 10 g oxalic acid dissolved  in 100 mL deionized water [16] with voltage of 4.5 to 6 V for 4 to 10 s using a stainless steel  cathode.  

To characterize the  γ’  precipitates and other second phase particles from the weld line to  the PM, Hitachi 3600 N and TM3030 SEMs operating at an accelerating voltage of 15 kV in the  secondary electron mode were used. Some of the SEM investigations were also performed in the  as-polished condition and some of them in the etched condition. To etch the samples for SEM  studies, etching procedure was the same as the one used for optical microscopy examinations.  The electroetch solution dissolved the  γ  matrix from the polished surface and rendered the  γ’   precipitates and other second phase particles visible.  

The Hitachi TM3030 SEM was equipped with an EDS system which allowed identifying  the main elements in the second phases. In particular, the EDS system was useful in revealing the  macro and micro segregation tendencies of alloying element in the cracked area. Also, by SEM  studies the characteristics of very small cracks were determined.  

The volume fraction of the  γ’  and MC carbides was determined based on the point  counting method of the ASTM E 562-02 standard. Since  γ’  particles exhibited mostly a cuboidal  morphology, cube edge measured using a Clemex ®  image analysis software was considered as  the representative particle size. The average size of 100  γ’  particles from at least 6 micrographs is  reported for each location of interest across the weld.  

Finally, a three-dimensional numerical model using finite element method (FEM)  representing the entire EBW process for shrouds was developed using ABAQUS software. The  model was initially validated by comparing its predictions with similar works, such as those  reported by Ferro  et al.  [9] on EBW modeling of Inconel-706. One of the main challenges in this  novel numerical model was the development of an accurate heat source model which would  realistically represent the shape of the weld zone.  

Most of the model’s material data such as density, melting point, thermal conductivity,  specific heat, thermal expansion, elastic modulus and their variation with temperature were  provided by the manufacturer (Siemens Canada Limited). Also, stress-strain data in the 25 to  800   C temperature range were available in the manufacturer’s database. However, the stress- strain data in the 900 to 1200   C temperature range were generated using hot compression  testing. To this end,   compression samples with 6 mm diameter and 9 mm height wire electro- discharged machined from root of blades were heated to the deformation temperature at a rate of  20 °C/min. After soaking at the deformation temperature for 5 min, the sample was deformed to  a strain of 0.6 at a strain rate of 0.001 s -1  and then water quenched. As an example, the stress- strain curve for Inconel-713LC at the deformation temperature of 1200   C and strain rate of  0.001 s -1  is presented in Fig. 2.  

To attain mesh convergence several approaches were considered. Initially, low order  tetrahedral (DC3D4 and C3D4) was used for the preliminary assessment. Since low order  elements are not generally recommended for nonlinear structural analysis, a hybrid mesh  consisting low order hexahedral (DC3D8 and C3D8) and high order tetrahedral (DC3D10 and  C3D10) were used and contact elements where created to connect the two types of elements.  However, the use of contact equations increased significantly the analysis run time. Therefore,  high order tetrahedral was selected for both thermal and structural analysis with sufficient  refinement in the FZ and HAZ regions. It was concluded that at least 8 nodes are needed through  the thickness to have accurate results. Total number of elements was 192 741 with 240 927  nodes connecting them. The model could predict the developed tensile stresses during EBW  which was useful in identification of the areas that are more prone to crack initiation. The details  of this model will be published in another contribution.  

# 3. Results and Discussion  

# 3.1. Parent Material Microstructure  

Figure 3 shows the low magnification optical image at the 32.8 mm from the bottom edge  of shrouds revealing the grain structure of the cast PM. The dendrite of  γ  grains with a 190  µm   linear intercept length can be observed. SEM studies (Fig. 4) on the cast microstructure of PM  Inconel- 713LC at the same location revealed uniform and coherent precipitation of coarse  γ'   particles in the  γ  dendritic matrix with a high volume fraction (52 %). The  γ’  precipitates  exhibited a cuboidal morphology with edge length from 0.5  µm  to 1  µm . In Inconel-713LC,  γ'  is  formulated as Ni 2.95 Cr 0.95 Al 0.78  Ti 0.06 Nb 0.06 Mo 0.06  [17]. The  γ'  solvus in Inconel-713LC is reported  to be 1205 °C which is comparable to the solidus (1260 °C) of the alloy [4].  

Dendritic growth and nonequilibrium solidification lead to intensive micro segregation of  alloying elements at interdendritic regions. Specifically, the partition coefficient ( k ) is defined as  the ratio of the dendrite core composition relative to the nominal composition [11].  Alloying  elements with  k  values significantly smaller than 1,  i.e.  Ti (0.55), Mo (0.82) [18], and C tend  strongly to depart the dendrite cores and segregate into the interdendritic regions. By this means,  MC-carbides with semi-continuous morphology are formed at the interdendritic regions (Fig. 4).  The volume fraction of MC carbides was determined to be about 1 %. The chemical composition  of MC carbides in Inconel-713LC is formulated as (Nb 0.4 Mo 0.25 Ti 0.18 Cr 0.16 )C [18] indicating that  Nb is a strong carbide former in this alloy. Similar to other cast Ni-based superalloys, alloying  elements with  k  values smaller than 1 have important impacts on the solidification and  weldability of Inconel-713LC as will be discussed in Section 3.3 and 3.4.  

Consistent with [18],  γ - γ’  eutectic was not observed in Inconel-713LC. However, as  shown in the inset of Fig. 4, isolated MC- γ  eutectic was occasionally observed in the  microstructure of the PM. In this eutectic constituent, the large MC carbide is intersected by thin  lamellae of  γ.  This low-melting point eutectic is formed in the interdendritic regions at the  terminal stages of the solidification process.  

# 3.2. Cracking Observations  

In Fig. 5 the presence of 11 transverse cracks along the weld line can be observed. These  cracks were located in the area between the two fins (Fig. 5e). The distance of these cracks from  the bottom edge of the EBWed shrouds along with the dimensions of the shrouds are provided in  Fig. 5e. The specifications of the cracks (length, morphology, orientation with respect to the  weld line, geometrical location, and initiation sites) are summarized in Table 1. Since these  cracks adversely affect the service capability of the EBWed Inconel-713LC shrouds, crack size is  not allowed to exceed a critical size. The longest crack with a length of 574.7 µm was  significantly smaller than the limit set by the manufacturer,  i.e.  2 mm for the transverse cracks  [19]. However, crack growth can take place during service and leads to surpassing the limits.  

Also, these cracks had straight or jagged/zigzag morphologies. The cracks with jagged  morphology are displaying a typical character of liquation cracks [20]. Additionally, cracking  has occurred mostly transverse to the weld line. Moreover, most of these cracks are initiated and  extended into the FZ,  i.e.  both ends of the observed cracks are located in FZ. In other words,  most of the cracks occurred exclusively in the FZ without any apparent relationship with the  HAZ. However, it is probable that cracks number 1, 7, 8, and 11 have initiated from FZ/HAZ  border. Also, it is possible that these cracks (number 1, 7, 8, and 11) have initiated from HAZ  and propagated toward FZ or vice versa. To support this interpretation the results of SEM  studies, attained by grinding the weld metal flat and going through the thickness for the 31 mm  and 37 mm locations from the bottom edge are presented in Fig. 6.  

Based on the results of the numerical model illustrated in Fig. 7, the maximum amount of  tensile stresses during welding has been observed at the FZ/HAZ border, where is more prone to  cracking as evidenced by cracks number 1, 7, 8, and 11 (Figs. 5 and 6). Furthermore, as  predicted by the numerical model, amount of the generated stresses parallel with the weld line  (longitudinal stresses) are larger than the transverse stresses (Fig. 7b and c). This justifies  transverse cracking in EBWed Inconel-713LC blades. Moreover, consistent with the model,  cracking was observed mostly in the area between two fins where the model predicted the  highest level of thermal and mechanical tensile stresses. It is noteworthy that in this study  shrouds were not clamped during EBW and free displacement or expansion were possible in all  directions. Therefore, clamping was not a source of stress generation during EBW and generated  stresses during EBW (Fig. 7) were mostly due to the thermo mechanical cycle of the EBW  process. In the following sections more results of optical and electron microscopy studies are  presented with the objective to explain microstructural features contributing to cracking in FZ  and HAZ.  

As shown in Fig. 8 for the 32.8 mm location from the bottom edge, area between two  fins, microstructure investigation revealed the presence of cracks and other defect (Fig. 8a). A  higher magnification SEM image (Fig. 8b) showed the presence of several cracks with almost  straight morphology in the FZ. In other words, these cracks were initiated and propagated in FZ.  The other defects which are lack of filling are generated during main pass (not the cosmetic pass)  as they are located in the bottom portion of the FZ. They are probably produced due to a high  welding speed and lack of proper filling. Mounting material inside these defects initiated from  grinding and polishing during sample preparation are visible.  

# 3.3. Mechanisms of Cracking in Fusion Zone  

Because of a wide solidification temperature range, Ni-based superalloys especially those  with a high  γ’  volume fraction such as Inconel-713LC are susceptible to solidification cracking  during fusion welding. Based on the microstructural analysis and numerical modeling results, in  EBWed Inconel-713LC solidification cracking or cracking in FZ occurs by three mechanisms:   (i) Segregation induced grain boundary liquation:  As shown in Fig. 9 for other areas between  two fins (37 mm location from the bottom edge), microstructure investigations indicated the  existence of several cracks in FZ. These cracks with straight and zigzag morphologies are  initiated and propagated in FZ. For cracking root cause analysis specially correlation between  cracking and presence of second phases, EDS for various alloying elements present in Inconel- 713LC and potential impurities were performed. Specially, it is probable that cracking has  initiated from the area selected for EDS (Fig. 9c) as evidenced by a wider opening of the cracks  in this region. According to the EDS results presented in Fig. 9d to i, local segregation of Nb and  Si, and uniform distribution of other alloying element,  e.g.  Cr and Mn, in the cracked area of FZ  was observed. Apart from these elements, presence of B and C can be observed in the cracked  area. Due to absorption of x-rays generated by light elements such as C and B through bulk of  the material as well as the detector window, the EDS system has limitation for quantitative  analysis of the light elements. On the other hand, since in this study the surface of the samples  prepared by standard metallographic methods was examined, at least the EDS detector can detect  the x-rays of the light elements coming from the surface. Moreover, in this study a  qualitative   assessment of light elements was carried out. All of these factors contribute to decrease errors in  a qualitative assessment of light elements in the cracked area. Accordingly, this EDS analysis  qualitatively suggests segregation of alloying element such as Nb, Si, B, and C in the cracked  area. Also, EDS has been previously used [14, 21, 22] for qualitative elemental analysis  including light element analysis (B and C) for cracking/microstructure investigations in  fusion/friction welded nickel-based superalloys.  

Therefore, segregation induced liquation is the cracking mechanism in this case. In other  words, alloying elements segregate in particular at grain boundaries and form low melting point  liquid films along solidified grain boundaries. Presence of an intergranular liquid film during  EBW or cooling cycle of EBW results in microcracking since the liquid film cannot withstand  the thermal and mechanical  tensile  stresses (Fig. 7) induced during EBW or cooling cycle of  EBW. Consequently, microcracking occurs by the solid–liquid interface separation. In particular  presence of B in the cracked area significantly contributes to microcracking. Boron is one of the  most important elements in lowering the local melting point and thus, increasing the  solidification temperature range. Hence, the presence of B in the liquated grain boundaries leads  to survival of the liquid film to lower temperatures during weld cooling where sufficient  accumulation of tensile stresses occurs. Thereby, the local strength is exceeded and cracking  occurs.  

Figure 10 presents another area between fins with extensive cracking in FZ (Fig. 10b).  The closed area selected for EDS mapping is presented in Fig. 10c. Segregation of Mo, Si, Ca,  Fe, S, and C in the cracked area (Fig. 10d to i) and uniform distribution of other alloying  element,  e.g.  Ti Fig. 10j, can be observed. Therefore, cracking has taken place due to liquation of  Mo-rich sulphocarbides, M 2 SC. It is probable that alloying elements such as Si, Ca, and Fe  segregated into the sulphocarbides. It is noteworthy that Ca is a beneficial minor element added  during vacuum induction melting of nickel-based superalloys in the form of lime, Ni-Ca alloy  addition or a combination of both to desulfurize and deoxidize the molten metal [1, 23].  Although Ca is used as a refining aid element it is rarely listed in the chemical composition of  the alloy.  

(ii)   Cracking due to low strength of solidifying weld metal:  as demonstrated previously  (Fig. 7) during cooling cycle of the EBW significant tensile stresses (700-850 MPa) are  generated in the FZ. On the other hand, based on the hot ductility tensile test data, the nil  ductility temperature (NDT) for Inconel-713LC is in the 1310 to 1220   C range [24]. Therefore,  the mechanical resistance of the alloy in the 1310 to 1220   C range, a temperature range that  somehow represents the temperature of the solidifying weld metal, is not significant.  Accordingly, the presence of large thermal and mechanical (solidification contraction) stresses  induced during welding and/or cooling combined with the low strength of the solidifying weld  metal can result in cracking. Therefore, it is most likely that cracks with a straight morphology  are generated by this mechanism.  

Also, based on the numerical modeling results (Fig. 11), generation of tensile longitudinal  stresses behind the heat source where the volume fraction of liquid is still considerable (yellow  and light red regions in Fig. 11) can cause cracking. As well, the high amounts of alloying  additions in Inconel-713LC cause a wide solidification temperature range. Accordingly, in the  terminal stage of solidification where temperature is slightly above the solidus, dendrites are  separated by liquid films. Once the stress is applied on the solidifying weld metal, high level  strains develop in the interdendritic regions. Therefore, the local rupture strain is exceeded which  results in cracking [25].  

# (iii) Cracking due to contraction:  

An overall macrograph of the FZ at the location of 15.5 mm from the bottom edge in  EBWed Inconel-713LC is presented in Fig. 12. Indeed, during EBW a partially ionized plasma is  generated around the focal point of the electron beam which creates a wider region in the upper  part of the FZ [9]. Therefore, a FZ with characteristic wine glass shape is formed (Fig. 12). An  SEM micrograph of the FZ cap taken at \~ 1 mm away in the right side direction of the crack  number 8 in Fig. 6b is shown in Fig. 13. Dendrites of the  γ  are visible while some of the  dendrites are inclined in the opposite directions. From Fig. 13, it can be observed that cohesion at  the dendrite boundaries is missing. Also, solidification shrinkage is noticeable as arrowed in Fig.  13. In fact, on cooling from welding temperatures, a mushy zone (solid + liquid) exists. On the  other hand, during solidification contraction occurs as evidenced by arrowed regions in Fig. 13.  Once the volume of solidified weld metal is above 90 %, solid phase limits the flow of the  molten metal to backfill the interdendritic regions. Therefore, cracking can occur in the  interdendritic regions due to contraction [13, 26].  

# 3.4. Mechanisms of Cracking in HAZ  

Cracking in HAZ   was the second type of cracking observed in the EBWed Inconel- 713LC which occurred by two mechanisms:  

(i) Segregation induced grain boundary liquation or incipient melting:  As shown in Fig. 14 for  the 32.8 mm location from the bottom edge, area between two fins, further microstructure  investigation indicated the existence of cracks in HAZ (Fig. 14b) independent of cracking in FZ.   The crack with an almost straight morphology was observed to have initiated and propagated  along liquated grain boundaries in the HAZ.   It can be observed that the crack extends along the  boundaries of the precipitate. For cracking root cause analysis in HAZ also EDS mapping for  various alloying elements present in Inconel-713LC and potential impurities were performed  (Fig 14c to h). According to the results presented in Fig. 14c to h, segregation of alloying  elements such as Mo, Zr, Nb, S, C, and B in the cracked area of HAZ was observed. The low  solubility of B and Zr in  γ  and  γ’  leads to the preferential segregation of these elements to the  grain boundaries and formation of low melting point compounds. Zr generates sulfides or  sulfocarbides, Zr 4 C 2 S 2 , and excessive amounts of B reacts with alloying elements such as Mo  and forms complex borides, M 3 B 2  [5, 15, 27, 28]. Formation of low melting point Mo-rich  sulphocarbides and Nb carboborides, Nb (C, B) are other possibilities. Sulfocarbides and M 3 B 2  borides are problematic during fusion welding because of their low melting point, respectively   1245   C and 1140   C [15, 28-30]. Specially, they form liquid films at grain boundary in HAZ  during EBW due to thermal cycle of the welding process and lead to intergranular microcracking  since the liquid film cannot withstand the thermal and mechanical  tensile  stresses induced during  cooling. Therefore, similar to cracking in FZ, formation of Mo-rich sulphocarbides, Zr  sulphocarbides, and Nb carboborides or in general segregation induced liquation is one of the  cracking mechanisms in HAZ.  

The coarse grain size of the as-cast PM (190  μm ) leads to a smaller grain boundary area.  Therefore, formation of coarse detrimental second phases with semi-continuous morphology  along grain boundary and high volume is promoted (Fig. 4). By this means, once the as-cat  microstructure becomes the HAZ of EBW, the thickness of the intergranular liquid film would  increase which results in higher susceptibility to grain boundary liquation induced cracking.  

These results are consistent with the occurrence of segregation induced liquation  microcracking in HAZ of different fusion welded Ni-based superalloys such as Inconel-738 [8],  Allvac ®  718 Plus [31], Inconel-718 [32, 33], and Waspaloy [10, 34, 35]. Intergranular  segregation induced liquation of second phases such as MC-type carbides [10, 36], M 5 B 3  borides  [15], carboborides [10, 34], Mo-rich carbides/borides [37], and Ti-carbonitrides [35] are reported  in HAZ during the fusion welding of these alloys. Cracking normally occurs on locally liquated  grain boundaries with resolidified products on the cracks [31, 38].  

# 3.4.1. Confirmation of Segregation Induced Liquation  

In Fig. 15, cracking in FZ and HAZ of the 37 mm location from the bottom edge of the  shrouds is shown. A magnified micrograph of the area within the circle in Fig. 15b is presented  in Fig. 15c. Similarly, higher magnification images of the area within the circle in Fig. 15c are  shown in Fig. 15d and e. Cracking is associated with liquated and resolidified products as can be  seen from Fig. 15e. The liquated and resolidified components were identified to be Mo-rich  sulphocarbides, Zr-sulphocarbides, and Nb carboborides. Therefore, considering results  presented in Figs. 9, 10, 14, and 15 it can be concluded that one of the principal cracking  mechanisms in FZ and HAZ is liquation of second phases such as Mo-sulphocarbides, Zr- sulphocarbides, and Nb carboborides.  

# (ii)     Dissolution and Reprecipitation in HAZ:  

As shown in Fig. 16 for the 37 mm location from the bottom edge, the  γ’  particles in the  parent material (Fig. 16b) are coarser than the HAZ (Fig 16c). This indicates  γ’  particles are  either dissolved and reprecipitated with a smaller size due to high cooling rate of the EBW  process, or they are partially dissolved. The former can results in microcracking:  γ’  dissolution in  HAZ during welding due to thermal cycle of welding and rapid  γ’  reprecipitation upon cooling  from welding temperatures has a two-fold effect: (a) it strengthens the alloy substantially,  leading to a drop in ductility and, consequently, cracking along the grain boundaries if the  weldment is subjected to strains higher than the available intergranular ductility [26, 39]; and (b)  because of rapid  γ’  reprecipitation in HAZ,  γ’  forming elements leave the crystal lattice of the  γ   matrix leading to lattice contraction. As a result, high contraction stresses develop at grain  boundaries [26].   These contraction stresses alone or along with cooling stresses generated during  weld solidification can lead to cracking [26, 40].  

Ni-based superalloys with a high  γ’  volume fraction (>40 %) such as Inconel-713LC are  more susceptible to HAZ cracking by this mechanism since there is high driving force for  γ’   reprecipitation during cooling cycle of welding in HAZ due to high percentage of   γ’  forming  elements,  i.e.  having combined Al and Ti more than 6 wt.%.  

# 5. Conclusions  

From the microstructural analysis and numerical modeling of electron beam welded (EBWed)  shrouds of Inconel-713LC cast blades following conclusions can be drawn:  

1- The microstructure of the parent material (PM) cast Inconel-713LC consisted of dendrite of  γ ,  uniformly precipitated  γ’  particles in the  γ  matrix, and primary and eutectic MC carbides.  

2- Extensive cracking was observed independently in the fusion zone (FZ) and the heat affected  zone (HAZ) mostly in the area between two fins. Moreover, some of the cracks were also  initiated from HAZ and propagated toward FZ and vice versa. Several cracks were initiated from  the FZ/HAZ border as well.  

3- The numerical model predicted the maximum level of tensile stresses during EBW at the  FZ/HAZ border where significant cracking occurred. Also, the model predicted that longitudinal  stresses generated during EBW in FZ are greater than the transverse ones which justifies  transverse cracking in FZ.  

4- Due to alloying elements segregation, Mo-sulphocarbides, Zr-sulphocarbides, and Nb  carboborides form in FZ and HAZ. These low melting point phases remain liquated over a wide  temperature range along grain boundaries during EBW thermal cycle where significant amount  of thermal and mechanical  tensile  stresses can accumulate and lead to cracking.  

5- A considerable volume of potential liquating phases such sulphocarbides, borides, and  carbides with semi-continuous morphology along the grain boundaries coupled with a coarse cast  grain size (190  μm ) as well as a high level of alloying elements segregation in the cast Inconel- 713LC play role in grain boundary liquation and consequently extensive cracking in FZ and  HAZ.  

6- Cracking due to low strength of solidifying weld metal as well as cracking as a result of  contraction were other cracking mechanisms in FZ. Cracking in HAZ also occurred as a result of  γ’  dissolution during EBW and  γ’  reprecipitation on cooling due to reduced ductility and  generation of substantial contraction stresses.
