# Microstructure Evolution during Supersolvus Heat Treatment of a Powder Metallurgy Nickel-Base Superalloy  

S.L. SEMIATIN, K.E. MCCLARY, A.D. ROLLETT, C.G. ROBERTS, E.J. PAYTON, F. ZHANG, and T.P. GABB  

Microstructure evolution during the supersolvus heat treatment of a powder-metallurgy, low- solvus, high-refractory (LSHR) superalloy was established. For this purpose, three lots of LSHR with varying initial carbon/boron composition and thermo mechanical history were subjected to a series of short-time (induction) and long-time (furnace) heat treatments followed by scanning electron microscopy/electron backscatter diﬀraction and quantitative metallography. The size of the (pinned) gamma grains exhibited a limited dependence on heating rate and soak time at peak temperature, and it was generally smaller than the predictions based on the classic Smith-Zener model. The diﬀerences were rationalized in terms of stereological and pinning-particle location eﬀects. Observations of limited coarsening of the carbide/boride pinning particles were inter- preted in the context of prior experimental observations and a modiﬁed Lifshitz-Slyosov-Wagner model applied previously for the coarsening of compound phases in steels.

# I. INTRODUCTION  

N ICKEL - BASE  superalloys are important materials for high-temperature applications such as those in the aerospace and power-generation industries. Typically, they are manufactured by one of two routes involving initial alloy synthesis via an ingot metallurgy (IM) or a 1 , ] powder metallurgy (PM) technique. The ﬁrst approach comprises the melting and solidiﬁcation of ingots, homogenization to reduce micro segregation, and a number of thermo mechanical processing steps to break down the coarse cast microstructure to obtain a uniform, recrystallized structure of gamma grains. The grain size of fully wrought IM superalloys is typically of the order of 50  l m. More highly alloyed materials are prone to macro segregation and hence are fabricated by PM methods. Usually, powders are consolidated by hot isostatic pressing (HIP) or blind-die compaction fol- lowed by hot extrusion. PM billets so produced have a much ﬁner grain size ( \~ 5  l m) and a uniform distribution of second-phase precipitates such as gamma prime. At elevated processing temperatures, this microstructure yields low ﬂow stress and high rate sensitivity typical of superplastic ﬂow and, therefore, is useful in part fabrication via closed-die, isothermal forging.  

The ﬁnal heat treatment of isothermally forged PM superalloy components consists of heat treatment either below or above the solution temperature (solvus) for the strengthening precipitates. Subsolvus heat treatment results in the retention of a ﬁne gamma grain size giving optimal strength and resistance to fatigue-crack initia- tion. Solution treatment above the solvus gives rise to a coarser gamma grain size ( \~ 30 to 50  l m) which is attractive with respect to creep and low-cycle fatigue (dwell crack-growth) properties. Unfortunately, PM nickel-base superalloys can be prone to the growth of abnormally large grains (several hundred microns in size or greater) when subjected to supersolvus heat treat- ment. For this reason, extensive experimentation has been conducted to establish processing routes and composition modiﬁcations that may limit the suscepti- bility to such defects. For example, the eﬃcacy of processing routes that impose limits on the strain rate during isothermal forging (to maintain superplastic conditions) and the control of carbon level (to ensure a minimum amount of various carbide phases that are stable above the solvus and can pin the gamma grain 3 – 6 ] boundaries) has been demonstrated.  

Despite the development of an extensive phenomeno- logical knowledge base describing the eﬀect of process- ing parameters/alloy composition on supersolvus grain- growth behavior of PM superalloys ( e.g. , Reference  3 ), little work has been done to understand the underlying physical mechanisms. One key area for which funda- mental knowledge would be useful concerns the pinning of gamma grains above the precipitate-solvus temperature by inert particles. An examination of an extensive collection of experimental 9 ]   observations by Manohar  et al. suggests that m is indeed unity but that K may be as low as 0.34 instead of the theoretical value of 1.33. With regard to PM [ 10 ]   superalloys, the research of Song and Aindow and [ 11 ]   Payton revealed that carbides, borides, and oxides are eﬀective in pinning the gamma grain boundaries. Two factors complicate the comparison of observed and predicted pinned gamma grain sizes developed during the supersolvus heat treatment of superalloys. One is the fact that the pinning particles may not be distributed randomly within the matrix and along the 13 ]   grain boundaries. Second, the pinning particles may not be inert, but rather they undergo static coarsening. The coarsening of compound phases such as carbides has been investigated extensively from both experimen- 14 – 23 ]   tal and modeling standpoints for steels, but rela- tively little work has been performed for PM superalloys. As the particles coarsen, the gamma grain size in superalloys would be expected to increase. The 10 ]   results of Song and Aindow, however, did not reveal the linear increase in grain size with average particle radius suggested by the Smith-Zener expression (Eq.  1 ).  

The objective of the current research was to establish the applicability of the Smith-Zener relation to predict the pinned grain size developed during supersolvus heat treatment of a prototypical PM nickel-base superalloy. For this purpose, three lots of the superalloy LSHR with diﬀerent carbon/boron contents and thermo mechanical histories were subjected to short and long heat treat- ments followed by microstructure determination via scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and quantitative metallography.  

# II. MATERIALS AND PROCEDURES  

# A.  Materials  

The program materials comprised three lots of the advanced gamma-gamma prime PM superalloy LSHR (denoting ‘‘low-solvus, high refractory’’), which was developed by NASA for jet-engine disks. LSHR pro- vides an attractive balance of properties at the bore and rim of disks subjected to a graded-microstructure heat treatment in which only the component rim is exposed above the solvus temperature to promote local growth [ 24 , ] of the gamma grains ; the exposure time may vary 26 – 30 ]   from minutes to several hours. The gamma-prime solvus  T c ’  of this alloy is 1430 K (1157   C).  

The materials were received as (1) 170-mm diameter, 40-mm thick, isothermally forged pancake; (2) 230-mm diameter extruded billet; and (3) 75-mm diameter billet that had been fabricated by further extrusion of the 230-mm diameter billet. The materials were produced by standard techniques starting with gas-atomized powder with a mesh size less than 325 (for the forged pancake) or 400 (for the extrusions) (Figure  1 ); the average powder-particle sizes were approximately 32 and 27  l m, respectively. After atomization, the powder for the pancake was subjected to a series of subsolvus operations comprising HIP consolidation, canned hot extrusion to a 5:1 reduction, and isothermal forging of a cylindrical preform to a 5:1 reduction. The powder for the billet products was also subsolvus hot isostatic pressed followed by a single 6:1 reduction extrusion (230-mm billet) or a three-stage extrusion that included additional 2:1 and 4.5:1 reductions (75-mm billet). The compositions of the pancake and extrusions were similar except for the levels of carbon and boron (Table  I ).  

The microstructure of the as-received materials com- prised ﬁne equiaxed grains whose average size was 2 to 3  l m in each material (Figure  2 ).

# B.  Procedures  

A series of cylindrical bars, each measuring 10-mm diameter x 90-mm length, was electric-discharge ma- chined from the as-received isothermally forged LSHR pancake and the two LSHR billets. The bars were subjected to short-time induction heat treatment or long-time furnace heat treatment (Table  II ) to establish the eﬀect of thermo mechanical processing history and composition on the supersolvus gamma grain size. The supersolvus furnace heat treatment was similar to that used in industrial practice.  

The induction heat treatments used an 80- to 200-kHz, 7.5-kW power supply (manufactured by Ameritherm, Inc., Scottsville,NY)andhelicalcoil.Prior to induction heating, a type-K thermocouple was attached to each sample at the midlength, mid-diameter position to monitor and control the temperature. The principal processing variables were the peak temperature  T p , the heating rate to  T p , and the soak time at  T p . Speciﬁcally, the processing sequence consisted of heating to 1310 K (1037   C) at a rate of \~ 85 K/min ( \~ 85   C/min), soaking for 5 minutes to equil- ibrate the temperature, heating to a supersolvus temper- ature of  T c ’  + 25 K (1455 K [1182   C]) or  T c ’  + 80 K (1510 K [1237   C]) at a rate of 5 K/min (5   C/min) or 85 K/min (85   C/min), soaking for 1 or 10 min, and then cooling at a rate of 85 K/min (85   C/min) (Table  II ). The moderate cooling rate was selected to avoid cracking associated with water quenching.  

For the long-time heat treatments, a number of 15-mm long slugs were cut from the 10-mm diameter bars. Each slug was encapsulated in a quartz tube backﬁlled with argon. The experiments used two furnaces, one at 1313 K (1040   C) and a second at one of the two supersolvus temperatures used in the induction trials,  i.e. ,  T c ’  + 25 K (1455 K [1182   C]) or  T c ’  + 80 K (1510 K [1237   C]).  

Heat treatment consisted of inserting a sample into the 1310 K (1037   C) furnace, soaking for 5 minutes or 1 hour, rapid transfer to the supersolvus furnace, soaking at temperature for 2 hours, air cooling (Table  II ).  

After heat treatment, the induction samples were sec- tioned at the precise location of the control thermocouple; the furnace samples were sectioned at the midlength. The sections were prepared using standard metallographic techniques with ﬁnishing by polishing with 0.05- l m colloi- dal silica. The grain structures were photographed using backscattered electron (BSE) imaging in a SEM equipped with a ﬁeld-emission gun (XL-30 or Quanta 600F, both manufactured by FEI,Hillsboro, OR). The grain structures in a number of samples were also determined via electron- backscatter diﬀraction (EBSD) in the XL-30 using EDAX/ TSL OIM software (EDAX Corp., Mahwah, NJ). For this purpose, the scans were performed over an area measuring approximately 200  9  200  l m using a step size of 1  l m. Carbide and boride particles were BSE imaged using an FEI Sirion SEM in the ultrahigh-resolution mode; alter- nating the ﬁnal surface preparation with silica and then alumina followed by BSE imaging of identical areas was used to conﬁrm that the carbide/boride observations were not contaminated by polishing compound.  

The average grain size and grain-size distribution for each material and set of heat-treatment conditions were determined via a semiautomated image analysis using at least ﬁve BSE micrographs with FoveaPro (Reindeer Graphics, Asheville, NC)/Adobe PhotoShop software (Adobe Systems, Inc., San Jose, CA) as well as the grain- delineation software in the EDAX/TSL OIM system. The grain boundaries determined by EBSD were deﬁned using a 15 deg criterion. During such evaluations, the twins were removed manually (FoveaPro approach) or automatically by applying the twin-removal ( R 3,  R 9 boundary) capability in the OIM software. The grain size was taken as the diameter ( D ) of a circle with area equivalent to that observed in section and was thus an underestimate of the true (three-dimensional) diameter. Because of the moderate cooling rate from the supersol- vus temperature at the end of each heat treatment, the gamma-prime precipitates were very ﬁne. Their eﬀect on grain-size analysis was eliminated manually via hand painting (FoveaPro approach) or automatically because of their coherence with the matrix (EBSD).  

The volume fraction ( f ) and average radius ( r p ) of carbide/boride particles were determined by threshold- ing and analyzing BSE images with the aid of MATLAB (MathWorks, Natick, MA). For this purpose, between 10 and 20 micrographs at a magniﬁcation of 2000 times, containing a total of approximately 200 to 1200 parti- cles, were analyzed for each heat-treatment condition. Particle radii ( r p ) were calculated using the equivalent- circle method with no stereological correction. The approximate scatter in the measurements of  f  and  r p  was approximately  ±  10 pct of the reported values.  

# III. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION  

The primary results from this research consisted of (1) the gamma grain size measurements, (2) measurement and interpretation of the volume fraction and average size of the pinning particles, and (3) interpretation of the pinned grain size in the context of the Smith-Zener model.

For  T p  =  T c ’  + 25 K (1455 K [1182   C]) (Figure  3 , Table  III ), the isothermally forged pancake developed a grain size that was slightly smaller for the short-time (induction) heat treatments (15 to 18  l m) compared with that for the long-time (furnace) heat treatments (20 to 22  l m). These ratios of the ALA-to-average grain size exceed those postulated in some models as a precursor to abnormal grain growth [ 31 ] ( e.g. , the value of 2 in the Hillert model ). In contrast, several numerical simulations and other experimental observations of normal grain growth (in the absence or presence of pinning particles) do indeed reveal such 32 , ]   ratios. The measured values of the ALA-to-average grain size ratio were also mirrored in the standard deviations of the grain-size distributions, which were 8 to 10  l m. Thus, there was considerable overlap in the distributions for the various heat treatments.  

For  T p  =  T c ’  + 80 K (1510 K [1237   C]) (Figure  4 , Table  III ), the eﬀect of the heat-treatment method on the gamma grain size was similar but smaller than that at the lower supersolvus temperature. The ratios of the ALA to the average grain size ( \~ 2.5) were similar also. The major diﬀerence was an increase in the grain sizes by  \~ 50 pct relative to those at the lower temperature. The larger grain sizes ( \~ 30 to 36  l m) were comparable with the size of the starting powder ( \~ 32  l m) and can be rationalized at least partially by coarsening of the pinning particles. In addition, the microstructures developed during exposure at the higher peak temper- ature showed evidence of thermally induced porosity (TIP), particularly at grain boundaries and triple junc- tions, irrespective of the heat-treatment method (Figure  4 ). The occurrence of TIP at elevated supersol- vus temperatures has been ascribed to the melting of a 11 , , ]   34 , ] boride phase. According to Huron  et al., the liquid weakens the grain boundaries and residual inert gas from the atomization process coalesces, there- by providing the pressure needed to nucleate and grow cavities.

The average volume fraction ( f ) and radius ( r p ) of the particles observed after the supersolvus heat treatment of the isothermally forged pancake and 230-mm diam- eter billet (Tables  III  and  IV , respectively) revealed some unavoidable scatter because of the considerable diﬃculty in imaging/thresholding such ﬁne microstruc- tural features as noted in Section  II–B . Despite the approximate nature of the data, however, the eﬀect of alloy composition on particle volume fraction and radius were ascertained. Attention was focused solely on the results for  T p  =  T c ’  + 25 K (1455 K [1182   C]) to avoid the complicating factor of boride melting and TIP observed at the higher supersolvus temperature.  

On average, the pancake forging had  \~ 0.13 vol pct particles for the various heat treatments for which T p  = 1455 K (1182   C) (Table  III ). By contrast, the 230-mm diameter billet had  f  \~ 0.33 vol pct (Table  IV ). The measured diﬀerence in volume fraction can be rationalized partially on the basis of the diﬀerent carbon (and boron) levels in the forging and billet products. Because of the scarcity of borides, attention focused primarily on the MC carbides. Assuming that each carbide particle contained a single metallic element M, the maximum volume fraction of particles was estimated assuming no solubility of C in the matrix. The calcula- tion was based on the atomic number of M and C and the density of the carbide compound. For M = Mo, Nb, Ta, W, or Ti, the eﬀects of diﬀerences in the atomic number of M and the density of the compound on the predicted volume fraction are largely compensatory. Speciﬁcally, for a carbon content of 0.030 wt pct (as in the pancake forging, Table  I ), the hypothetical carbide volume fraction was calculated as 0.255  ±  0.01 pct. The corresponding value for a carbon content of 0.045 wt pct (as in the billet products, Table  I ) was 0.380  ±  0.015. A comparison of the maximum volume percent of carbide to the observations suggests that a non-negligible amount of carbon was in solution in both product forms. In particular, the carbon solubility was estimated to be approximately 0.015 (= 0.03  9  0.125/ 0.255) or 0.006 (= 0.045  9  0.050/0.380) from the data for the pancake forging and billet products, respectively. Such estimates are somewhat conservative inasmuch as some of the particles were indeed borides, not carbides.  

Despite the scatter in the data, the values of the particle radii ( r p ) for the pancake forging and 230-mm diameter billet did exhibit a weak trend (Tables  III  and IV ). For both lots of material, the smallest particle sizes were found in the induction-heat-treated samples that were heated rapidly to the peak temperature. Samples that were heated slowly (5 K/min) from 1310 K (1037   C) to  T p  = 1455 K (1182   C) via induction exhibited coarser particle sizes because of the greater time for coarsening. Similarly, samples that were fur- nace heat treated for 2 hours showed mildly greater degrees of coarsening for both the pancake forging and the 230-mm diameter billet.  

# C.  Pinned Grain Size  

Because the grain sizes developed during both short- and long-time heat treatment for each peak temperature ( T p ) and lot of LSHR were similar, it was assumed that a stable (pinned) size had been reached in each exper- iment. # IV. SUMMARY AND CONCLUSIONS  

Isothermally forged pancake and extruded billet of the PM superalloy LSHR were subjected to various short- and long-time heat treatments. After heat treat- ment, the gamma grain size and pinning-particle volume fraction and size were determined. The following con- clusions were drawn from this work:  

1. For the processing parameters typically used for fabrication of billet and ﬁnished components, ﬁnal supersolvus heat treatment of LSHR gives rise to normal grain growth. For the range of heat-treat- ment parameters investigated, the ﬁnal average grain size is relatively insensitive to the heating rate and soak time, but it depends on the peak tempera- ture. High supersolvus temperatures, however, are inappropriate for processing because of the occur- rence of TIP.

 2. The pinned grain sizes in LSHR are smaller than those predicted on the basis of the Smith-Zener model but are comparable with measurements for a variety of other materials as well as the theoretical models developed by Rios. The divergence from the Smith-Zener model may be the result of factors such as the nonrandom location of the pinning par- ticles and stereological considerations.

 3. Conventional hot extrusion (to diﬀerent reductions) and isothermal forging (under typical superplastic conditions) give rise to similar grain sizes during subsequent supersolvus heat treatment.

 4. The pinning particles in LSHR seem to undergo static coarsening during heat treatment. However, the LSW model overpredicts the observed coarsen- ing rate.
