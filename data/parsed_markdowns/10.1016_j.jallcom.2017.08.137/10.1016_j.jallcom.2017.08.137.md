A new Ni-based superalloy with high-Cr content was designed for industrial gas turbines application. The microstructural homogenization and high-temperature cyclic oxidation behavior was investigated sys- tematically. The results showed that the optimum heat treatment condition was 1563 K (1290    C)/ 4 h  þ  1573 K (1300    C)/6 h, A.C. (air cooling)  þ  1393 K (1120    C)/2 h, A.C.  þ  1143 K (870    C)/24 h, A.C., which can completely dissolve the  g / g 0   eutectic and the Ni 3 (TiTa) phase in the interdendritic region. The cyclic oxidation results showed that a multilayer of continuous and dense oxide-rich regions formed and the kinetic curves followed a parabolic law. The mass gain at 1323 K (1050    C) and 1343 K (1070    C) was 2   2 4.02 mg/cm and 4.23 mg/cm , respectively. No severe spallation of the oxide-rich region was observed. The outer layer of the oxide-rich region mainly consisted of Cr 2 O 3 , TiO 2 , and (Ni, Co)Cr 2 O 4  spinels, while the layer at the substrate-oxide interface was found to be Al 2 O 3 . The results show that this new su- peralloy has a better cyclic oxidation resistance at 1343 K (1070    C) than the commonly used IN738 and GTD-111 superalloys.

# 1. Introduction  

Industrial gas turbines (IGTs) are widely used in power plants for electricity generation due to the high thermal ef ﬁ ciency and convenient operability of the combined cycle system  [1] . In the past few decades, these superalloys used in aero-engine blades was directly chosen as IGTs blade material, especially those systems possessing superior oxidation and hot-corrosion resistance, and microstructural stability during long-term performance  [2,3] . This is because the service duration of IGTs is much longer than that of aero-engines, combined with the possibility of corrosion by sulfur from the fuel or inlet air  [4 e 7] . Recently, the turbine inlet gas temperatures of the advanced H/J-class gas turbine such as M701J [8,9] , SGT6-8000H [10] , and 9HA [11] has reached up to 1773 e 1873 K (1500 e 1600    C). In order to develop the next grade of gas turbines (i.e., the 1973 K (1700    C)-class), the high-temperature performance of the materials must be improved. At present, the second-generation single-crystal superalloys used in aero-engines  

An excellent hot-corrosion resistance is usually guaranteed by a high concentration of Cr and a high Ti/Al ratio (   1)  [5] , because the Cr/Ti-sul ﬁ de is the only solid-type corrosion product in the per- formance temperature range  [4,16] . However, with the high Cr addition, the introduction of a large content of high melting point solid solution strengthening elements, including W, Mo, and Re, seems infeasible due to the reason that these elements (W, Mo and Re) tend to precipitate as the topologically close-packed phases (TCP) with Cr  [17,18] . Furthermore, the excessive addition of Ti may cause the formation of the  g / g 0   eutectic phase and  h  (Ni 3 Ti) phase during solidi ﬁ cation, which lead to a drop of the incipient melting point  [19] .  

In order to develop a new superalloy with superior hot- corrosion resistance and lower density, this paper designed a new system with a higher Cr addition based on the previous analyses and thermodynamic calculations (by JmatPro). The content of Cr was set at 14 wt.%, and the Ti/Al ratio was decreased to lower than 1. The content of Al was intended to guarantee the formation of continuous alumina layer according to the oxidation map of the ternary NiCrAl alloys obtained in air at 1273 e 1373 K (1000 e 1100    C)  [20] . The addition of 6 wt.% Ta was to increase the g 0   solvus temperature and volume fraction, and to maintain the strength at elevated temperatures by precipitation strengthening

 [21] . Furthermore, Ta can also improve the corrosion resistance

 [22] . In addition, the total content of the solution strengthening elements was about 7 wt.%, which reaches the up limit of the common hot-corrosion resistance superalloy composition  [23] . Through analyzing the elements distribution in the dendrites and the microstructural evolution during the heat treatment, this paper systematically studied the homogenization of the segre- gated elements. In addition, the cyclic oxidation behavior of this cast superalloy, as well as the oxidation mechanism was another topic.  

# 2. Experimental procedures  

The nominal chemical composition of the designed superalloy was Ni\~3.5Al\~3Mo\~14Cr\~10Co\~3Ti\~4W\~6Ta (wt.%). The master ingot was produced by induction levitation melting in a high- purity argon atmosphere. The density of this alloy, measured by 3 the Archimedes method, was about 8.12 g/cm . The heat treat- ments were conducted in a GSL1800X tube furnace. Cylindrical specimens with  f 15    10 mm were machined for incipient melting tests. Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) was carried out with a METTLER TOLEDO for heating and cooling tests at a rate of  ± 10    C/min. The incipient melting specimens were heated at different temperatures between 1533 K and 1573 K (1260 and 1300    C) for 1 h, and then water quenched to maintain the microstructure. The heat-treated samples were machined to 3    8    10 mm, and then ground to a P2000 surface  ﬁ nish for the oxidation tests. Cyclic oxidation kinetic tests were performed at 1323 K (1050    C) and 1343 K (1070    C) for 100 h in air, and the weight changes were measured by an electronic balance (Sarto- rius CPA225D, Germany) with a precision of 10  5   g. The oxidized specimens were air-quenched and weighed at 1 h intervals during the  ﬁ rst 10 h and then every 10 h. The microstructures and chemical compositions of the cross-sections were analyzed by optical microscopy (OM) and JEOL JSM-7001F scanning electron microscopy (SEM), and with a JEOL JXA-8230 electron probe microanalyzer (EPMA). The phases of the oxides were identi ﬁ ed by a Rigaku SmartLab X-ray diffractometer (XRD) with Cu K a  ra- diation generated at 40 kV and 200 mA. The scanning rate was 4  / min over a 2 q  range of 20  e 90  .

The failure of the superalloy in certain circumstances is known to be caused by oxidation  [29] . Therefore, the investiga- tion of the oxidation behavior at high temperatures seems extremely necessary. After the standard heat treatment, cyclic oxidation tests were conducted at both 1323 K (1050    C) and 1343 K (1070    C) in air for 100 h. The kinetic curves of the bare alloy during cyclic oxidation are presented in  Fig. 6 . All mass-gain curves follow a parabolic law, and no substantial differences of the oxide products were observed according to the XRD diffrac- tion peaks. For the lower and higher oxidation temperatures, the 2   2 mass gains are 4.02 mg/cm and 4.23 mg/cm , respectively. The oxidation constant  K p  was determined based on the Wagner equation  [30] . The calculated values of  K p  for the different oxidation temperatures are about 4.50    10  5   mg 2 /cm 4   s and 4.97    10  5   mg 2 /cm 4   s over 0 h e 100 h. This superalloy shows a better cyclic oxidation resistance than the commonly used IN738 and GTD-111 superalloys at 1343 K (1070    C), these two super- alloys were shown to exhibit severe oxide-rich region spallation during cyclic oxidation at 1339 K (1066    C)  [31] . Furthermore, the ﬁ rst-generation single-crystal superalloy PWA1480 began to exhibit weight loss during cyclic oxidation at 1323 K (1050    C) after 120 cycles (80 h)  [32] . In this work, the polycrystalline superalloy did not exhibit weight loss at either oxidation tem- perature according to the mass-gain curves. No severe spallation of the oxide-rich region was observed.

Furthermore, peaks corresponding to NiO were not observed in the XRD patterns.

To further investigate the oxide-rich region, the morphology of cross-sections, as shown in  Fig. 9 , was analyzed by SEM, EDS, and by XRD. The SEM micrographs in  Fig. 9  (a) revealed that a thick and continuous oxide-rich region was formed on the surface of the alloy, and this oxide-rich region was divided into several layers in comparison with the microstructure after standard heat treatment (see  Fig. 9  (d)). The overall oxide layer includes two regions, as indicated by the dashed line in  Fig. 9  (a). The continuous and compact outer layer mainly consisted of Cr 2 O 3 , a small amount of TiO 2 , and (Ni, Co)Cr 2 O 4  spinels, as shown in  Fig. 9  (c), while the layer at the substrate-oxide interface was found to be Al 2 O 3 . Moreover, the enrichment of Ti and Ta appears as a TiTaO 4  phase that existed in the Cr 2 O 3  layer. The average depth of each layer measured from outside was about 7.5  m m, 2.4  m m and 2.8  m m. Beneath the alumina layers, a third region was observed as shown in  Fig. 9  (b), which was oxide-free and  g 0   phase-depleted in com- bination with the presence of Ti-nitride precipitates. The morphology of the Ti-nitrides precipitates, as well as the selected element distribution is shown in  Fig.10 . Furthermore, no W and Mo oxides were detected.  

The kinetic curves of this experimental alloy during cyclic oxidation followed a parabolic law, which is usually used to describe the growth of NiO and Al 2 O 3  [33] . However, in this study, the existence of NiO would be negligible. Therefore, the formation of Cr 2 O 3 , TiO 2 , and the (Ni, Co)Cr 2 O 4  spinels was responsible for the oxidation behavior. In the initial 20 h of oxidation, the mass of the samples increased signi ﬁ cantly due to the growth of the oxide-rich region, and the solid-state reactions leading to the (Ni, Co)Cr 2 O 4  spinels correspond to the high oxidation rate. With the increase in oxidation time, the kinetics decreased as a result of the longer diffusion pathways caused by the formation of the dense Cr 2 O 3  oxidation layer. Additionally, the oxygen activity at the matrix/oxide interface would decrease because of the hindrance of the dense oxide-rich region  [29] . The selective growth of Al 2 O 3 below the outer oxide-rich region was promoted due to the reduced oxygen partial pressure at the boundary between the outer and inner layers once the Cr 2 O 3  ﬁ lm was compactly formed, which resulted in an internal Al 2 O 3  phase growing along the interface. Although Ti bene ﬁ ts corrosion resistance, the negative effect of Ti was reported by many researchers  [33,34] . The disso- lution of Ti into Cr 2 O 3  and Al 2 O 3  causes the increased vacancy density that leads to the increased oxidation rate. However, the formation of the Ti/Ta-enriched TiTaO 4  could effectively prevent Ti incorporation into the outer Cr 2 O 3  layer and prohibit further in- ternal oxidation of Al. Moreover, this TiTaO 4  layer can prohibit the diffusion of cations that form the outer oxides and anions that sulfurize the alloy, as reported in Han's work  [22] . The formation of the Ti-nitrides could be related to the diffusion of nitrogen through the broken external layer during cooling, as well as the defective internal precipitation zone. Similar behavior was re- ported in other studies  [35 e 37] .  

Based on the above results and discussion, the oxidation behavior of this superalloy can be summarized as follows. The initial high mobility of Ti led to the fast formation of TiO 2 . Then, a continuous and dense Cr 2 O 3  layer was formed on the substrate, and further oxidation caused the formation of the spinels. Due to

# 4. Conclusion  

This paper studied the microstructure evolution of a new designed high-Cr content Ni-base superalloy (Ni\~3.5Al\~3- Mo\~14Cr\~10Co\~3Ti\~4W\~6Ta) during heat treatment, as well as its cyclic oxidation behavior at 1323 K (1050    C) and 1343 K (1070    C). The conclusions are as follows:  

1. The as-cast microstructure of this alloy was a typical dendrite structure. In the interdendritic region, the  g / g 0   eutectic micro- structure was daisy-like interspersed with Ni 3 (TiTa) phase.

 2. A two-step solution treatment can fully dissolve both the  g / g 0 eutectic and Ni 3 (TiTa) phase in the interdendritic region. The optimum heat treatment condition was 1563 K (1290  C)/ 4 h  þ  1573 K (1300    C)/6 h, A.C.  þ  1393 K (1120    C)/2 h, A.C.  þ  1143 K (870    C)/24 h, A.C.
