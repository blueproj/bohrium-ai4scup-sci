Article history: Received 4 November 2016 Received in revised form 21 March 2017 Accepted 22 March 2017 Available online 23 March 2017  

The microstructure and mechanical behaviour of a novel magnesium (Mg) doped Co-9Al-4.5W-4.5Mo- 2Ta-0.02B alloy (doped 0.1, 0.3, 0.7 and 1.0 at.% Mg, referred to as 0.1Mg, 0.3Mg, 0.7Mg and 1Mg al- loys hereafter, respectively) at room- and high- temperatures have been investigated. All alloys aged at 800    C for 50 h consist of a Co solid-solution matrix ( g -Co SS ) and nano-scale cuboidal  g 0 -Co 3 (Al, W) precipitates. Mg is observed to segregate primarily at the grain boundaries and the  g 0 -Co 3 (Al, W) phase. In the 0.7Mg and 1Mg alloys more Mg segregation at the grain boundaries decreases the fraction of  S 1\~3 type grain boundaries and the capability of excluding impurity O from the grain boundaries. With decreased  S 1\~3 boundaries and higher O content, the 0.7Mg and 1Mg alloys fracture in a mixed transgranular-intergranular mode at room temperature with lower strength and ductility under tension. However, the 0.1 and 0.3Mg alloys fracture in a transgranular mode with higher strength and ductility. The four alloys exhibit compressive  ﬂ ow stress anomalies at 700    C and the strength obviously decreases when the nominal Mg contents increase to 0.7 e 1 at.% at 600 e 800    C. As for the 0.1Mg and 0.3Mg alloys, the strengths from room temperature to 800    C are higher and not obviously affected by the nominal Mg contents, primarily owing to the two alloys having almost the same and higher  S 1\~3 grain boundary fraction and the lower O concentration in the grain boundary.

# 1. Introduction  

The successive development of superalloys has been motivated by the requirements in promoting the performance and ef ﬁ ciency of gas turbines in aerospace and power-generation industries. With superior corrosion, oxidation and wear resistance, the conventional Co-based superalloys have a microstructure consisting of a continuous Co solid-solution matrix ( g -Co SS ) and various carbides, in which the carbides are the main strengthening phases  [1,2] . However, the conventional Co-based superalloys exhibit lower temperature capability in comparison with Ni-based superalloys, because the Ni-based superalloys are mainly strengthened by the stable intermetallic  g 0 -Ni 3 Al precipitates and the coherent interface of the  g / g 0   microstructure  [3] . Recently, nano-size cuboidal L1 2 - type  g 0   precipitate, i.e., an intermetallic compound Co 3 (Al, W), has been found in the  g  matrix of a Co-Al-W ternary  [4] . This  g 0 -Co 3 (Al,  

In order to further develop this new class of superalloys as promising structural materials, macro-alloying the Co-Al-W ter- naries by Ta, Nb, Mo, Hf and Ti  [9 e 13]  has been achieved to investigate the microstructural evolution including the elemental partitioning in each phase  [9 e 11]  and the  g 0   solvus temperature [12,13] . These element additions, strengthen the grain boundary by borides or intermetallic phases [14] or improve the high- temperature oxidation resistance  [15]  by Boron, alter the volume fraction of the  g 0   phase  [16]  and the mis ﬁ t between the  g  matrix and  g 0   precipitate  [17,18]  or increase the high-temperature stability of the  g 0   phase  [5,16]  and are bene ﬁ cial to the bulk strength of the Co-Al-W based alloys. Ta has been con ﬁ rmed to be the most ef ﬁ - cient strengthening element for Co-Al-W based alloys  [5] . For example, with the addition of 2 at.% Ta, the solvus temperature of the  g 0   phase in the Co-9Al-9W alloy increases from 1000    C up to 1079    C, and the compressive strength at 800    C increases from 360 to 580MPa  [5 e 7] , whereas this value was only 300MPa for the conventional Co-based alloys  [1,6] . Mo partitions strongly to the  g 0 precipitates and just replaces the W atoms when added to the Co- Al-W alloy. It is clear that Mo partitions strongly to the  g 0   pre- cipitates  [19] , which is desirable for tensile elongation of the coherent  g / g 0   microstructure at room temperature  [8] . The  ﬂ ow stress anomalies at 700 e 800    C have also been found in Co-Al-W ternaries with a coherent  g / g 0   microstructure. This stress anomaly has been recognized as a mechanism of the slip of paired 1/2 〈 11 0 〉 superpartial dislocations on the {0 0 1} planes in addition to that on the {1 1 1} planes in the  g 0   precipitates  [5,6] . This mechanism is the same as that in the  g 0 -Ni 3 Al compound  [20,21] . TEM analysis con ﬁ rmed that slip of ordinary a/2 〈 1 0 1 〉 {1 1 1} dislocations through the  g -Co SS  matrix, rafting of the  g 0   precipitates perpen- dicular to the external compressive stress axis  [22]  and glide of the superlattice intrinsic and extrinsic stacking faults in the  g 0   pre- cipitates  [23,24]  are the main creep mechanisms of the Co-Al-W base alloys.  

For the Co-Al-W based system, the composition window for the formation of the two-phase  g / g 0   coherent microstructure is nar- row; therefore, macro-alloying Co-Al-W compositions, which cau- ses a slight shift from the composition window, may result in a third intermetallic phase precipitating along the grain boundaries [6,8,14] , which is harmful to the tensile ductility of  g '-strengthened Co-base superalloys. Micro-alloying, i.e., alloy addition of generally less than 1 at.%, is probability the most effective approach to improve the properties mainly via changing the metallurgical characteristics of the grain boundary  [25] . At present, systemic works regarding the effect of micro-alloying on the microstructure and mechanical behaviours of Co-Al-W based alloys are still seldom. B was found to be the most effective alloying element to improve the ductility of the Co e Al e W ternary. A considerable increment in elongation of 7.6% takes place, i.e. from 4.7% to 12.3%, in Co e 9.2Al e 9.5W alloy when doped with 0.02 at.% B  [7] . In this study, magnesium (Mg) is employed as a micro-alloying element in a novel Co-9Al-4.5W-4.5Mo-2Ta-0.02B composition with a  g / g 0 coherent microstructure, in which 4.5 at.% W is replaced by 4.5 at.% Mo to reduce the density of the Co-9Al-9W based composition. It is expected that Mg will affect the microstructure, especially the grain boundary character distribution (GBCD) and the element distribu- tion at the grain boundaries, which will subsequently alter the deformation and fracture behaviours of the Co-Al-W based alloys at different temperatures. A detailed investigation on the micro- structural evolution and mechanical responses of Co-9Al-4.5W- 4.5Mo-2Ta-0.02B- x Mg alloys (where x  ¼  0.1, 0.3, 0.7 and 1.0 at.%) is performed.  

# 2. Experimental procedures  

Button ingots of the Co-9Al-4.5W-4.5Mo-2Ta-0.02B-(0.1, 0.3, 0.7 and 1.0) Mg alloys (referred to as 0.1Mg, 0.3Mg, 0.7Mg and 1Mg alloy, hereafter), each with a mass of 150 grams, were prepared by arc melting  ﬁ ve or six times using the vacuum arc melting (VAR) method to homogenize the compositions of the ingots. An arc- melting furnace (FD-600H, KYKY Technology Development, She- nyang, China) was used for preparation of ingots of Co-Al-W based alloys. The purity of all of the raw elements was 99.95% by mass or higher. In this work, thin magnesium piece was used as raw ma- terials. Actually, there is a huge difference between the melting and vaporization temperature of Mg and the temperature of the melt, and this may result in obvious vaporization and loss of Mg during melting if no any protection. To reduce the loss of Mg during melting as less as possible, the following routes were employed: 1)  

Enwrapped the thin Mg pieces with the Al foil to form a package, and then put the package on the bottom of the melting crucible; 2) Piled up the other raw material plates from the package to the top according to the order of Al, Co, Mo, Ta and W; 3) At the beginning of arc melting, the W, Ta and Mo plates on the top were  ﬁ rstly melted by arc and then the W, Ta and Mo melt with a temperature of approximately 3500    C (larger than the melting point of W, 3410    C) dropped down to the crucible bottom through gaps of the raw Al and Co plates. At the same time, the melt heated the raw Al and Co plates along with the arc, and  ﬁ nally it fully enwrapped the package of the Al foil and Mg pieces. In this case, the package was insulated from furnace chamber and Mg was melted through heat transmission of the surrounded W, Ta and Mo melt, as well as Co and Al melt, but was not melted by the directly raying of arc. Thus, the obvious vaporization and loss of Mg could be effectively sup- pressed. The ingots were solution-treated at 1350    C for 8 h in a vacuum, followed by furnace cooling. Then, they were sealed in an evacuated quartz capsule back- ﬁ lled with argon and aged at 800    C for 50 h, followed by quenching. X-ray diffraction (XRD) analysis was performed on the bulk samples to identify the phase consti- tution using Cu K a  ( l  ¼  0.15405 nm) radiation at 40 kV and 40 mA (Rigaku D/Max 2500PC). Back-scattered electron (BSE) images were taken to investigate the microstructures of the as-cast and aged samples and the crack paths of the tensile and compressive tested samples using an SEM (JEOL JXA-8100), which was equipped with energy-dispersion X-ray spectroscopy (EDS, Oxford Instruments, UK) to analyse the phase compositions and bulk compositions of the as-cast alloys. Secondary electron images (SEI) of the fracture surfaces were obtained using an FEI Quanta 200F scanning electron microscope. The phase area fraction was statistically determined by quantitative metallographic analysis of the microstructure images taken in BSE mode. The misorientation of the grain boundaries was measured from a crystal orientation map using electron backscat- tering diffraction (EBSD) with scanning step sizes of 1  m m e 3  m m. The distribution of Mg and O across the grain interior and grain boundary was analysed by an Auger electron spectrometer (AES) through line scanning manner. Tensile tests were conducted at room temperature in air on an Instron 8810 machine at a strain rate of 3    10  4   s  1 . Dog-bone plate specimens were used for the room- temperature tensile tests. The dimensions of the parallel parts of the dog-bone plates were 3 mm    2 mm in cross section and 16 mm in length. Compressive tests at room temperature, 600    C, 700    C and 800    C were conducted in atmosphere at a strain rate of 3    10  4   s  1   using an Instron 8810 machine. The compressive cylinders were 3 mm in diameter and 6 mm in length. All speci- mens for the tensile and compressive tests were mechanically polished using SiC paper (1500-grit) with water before testing. To obtain the average strength, three samples of each alloy were taken for measurements of the tensile and compressive stress-strain curves at each testing temperature. Transmission electron micro- scopy (TEM) samples were prepared to investigate the dislocation morphologies and deformation mechanisms of the samples com- pressed at different temperatures using a JEOL JEM-2100F micro- scope. The electron diffraction patterns and energy-dispersive X- ray spectroscopy (EDS) of selected areas were obtained from the TEM samples to con ﬁ rm the phase crystal structure and composi- tion of the aged samples, respectively. The preparation of the TEM samples has been reported elsewhere  [8] .

For all four as-cast alloys, the Mg additions do not cause any consid- erable change in the element distribution in the constituent phases. The EDS analysis indicates that in the as-cast condition, Mg atoms partition to the Co 3 Ta-based phase rather than to the  g  matrix, and the Mg concentration in the Co 3 Ta-based phase exhibits an increasing trend, from 0.67 at.% to 2.15 at.%, as the nominal Mg content increases from 0.1 at.% to 1.0 at.%, respectively. Most of Ta atoms dissolve to the Co 3 Ta-based phase. Mo atoms partition to the Co 3 Ta-based phase rather than to the  g  phase, and the Mo con- centration in the Co 3 Ta-based phase is almost two times that in the g  phase; however, the opposite occurs with Al. The W concentra- tion is found to be slightly higher in the Co 3 Ta-based phase than in the  g  phase.  

After the aging treatment, the cuboidal  g 0 -Co 3 (Al, W) phase precipitates from the supersaturated  g  matrix, while the Co 3 Ta- based phase disappears. This phase transformation results in a re- distribution of the alloying elements in each phase of the  g 0 -Co SS / g 0 -Co 3 (Al, W) coherent microstructure, when compared to that in the as-cast samples, especially in the distribution of Mg. It is clear that Al distributes evenly in both phases. For Mo and W, their concentrations in each of the  g 0   and  g  phases are almost at the same level, but the concentrations in the  g 0   phase are slightly greater than in the  g  phase. The Ta concentrations have a small variation in each phase, but Ta obviously segregates in the  g 0   phase. These elemental distributions are fairly consistent with those of Mg-free Co-9Al-5W-4Mo-2Ta-0.02B alloy aged at 800    C for 100 h  [8] . It should be mentioned that Mg atoms segregate at the grain boundary and are present in the  g 0   phase but are absent in the  g phase. Note that the compositions of the grain boundaries were obtained by mainly analyzing the enlarged dark areas of the grain boundaries (see circles in  Fig. 3 a ’  to d ’ ). A comparison of the Mg concentration in the grain boundary of the 0.7Mg alloy to that of the 0.1 and 0.3Mg alloys reveals that Mg preferentially partitions to the grain boundary rather than to the  g 0   phase when the Mg con- tent is less than 0.7 at.%. When the nominal Mg contents are 0.7 at.% and 1.0 at.%, the Mg concentration in the grain boundaries shows a constant value (see  Table 1 ). This con ﬁ rms that the solubility of Mg in the grain boundary is approximately 0.9 at.%. Comparing to the 0.7Mg alloy, the extra 0.3 at.% Mg in the 1Mg alloy then solidi ﬁ es in the  g 0   phase, in which the Mg concentration is higher than that in the  g 0   phase of the 0.7Mg alloy. To further determine the segrega- tion of Mg at the grain boundary, an elementary comparison of Mg distribution across the grain boundaries carried out via an AES line scan with the pro ﬁ les are inserted in  Fig. 3 a ’ -d ’ , in which the white lines are the scan position and the black lines are the Mg distri- bution. The four alloys exhibit an obvious Mg peak at the grain boundaries (see  Fig. 3 a ’  and b ’ ), and with increase of the nominal Mg content, the Mg peak at the grain boundary becomes higher, which is consistent with the EDX measurements, acknowledging the segregation of Mg at grain boundary. For the 0.7Mg and 1Mg alloys, Mg shows the similar pro ﬁ le and peak value, con ﬁ rming the Mg segregation at the grain boundary is saturated in the two alloys.

To identify the mechanisms allowing the coherent  g / g 0   micro- structure to have such a signi ﬁ cant variation in ductility with the nominal Mg content at room temperature, the deformation and failure behaviours were investigated.  Fig. 5  shows the deformation morphologies of the  g / g 0   microstructure in a position away from the fracture plane 0.3 e 0.5 mm along the tensile axis direction. The four fractured alloys all show two groups of dense slip bands with different orientations, between which the angle is approximately in the range of 90 e 120  , indicating severe plastic deformation with strain of 7.1 e 18.4%, which correspond to the different densities of the slip bands. The enlarged inserts in  Fig. 5 a and 5d reveal that the long and straight slip lines go through the aligned  g  and  g 0   phases simultaneously. Thus, one can understand that at room tempera- ture, both the  g -Co SS  and the  g 0 -Co 3 (Al, W) intermetallic phases can continuously plastically deform with the same slip system in ten- sion, which con ﬁ rms the excellent plastic deformation ability of the intermetallic phase and reveals the reason why the  g / g 0   micro- structure has such a higher tensile elongation  [28] . Even the bulk elongation has a larger difference when the nominal Mg content changes from 0.1 to 1.0 at.%.  

The SEM fractographic analysis shown in  Fig. 6  displays the tensile fracture mechanisms of the coherent  g / g 0   microstructure at room temperature. For the 0.1Mg and 0.3Mg alloys, transgranular fracture is observed, as seen in  Fig. 6 a and 6b. The enlarged SEM images inserted in  Fig. 6 a and 6b demonstrate dimple morphology, revealing the dominant fracture mode to be transgranular ductile. As the nominal Mg content increases to 0.7 and 1.0 at.%, a mixed transgranular-intergranular fracture takes place, as observed in Fig. 6 c and 6d. In the transgranular failure area, as seen inserts in Fig. 6 e and 6f, the  g / g 0   microstructure fractures in a dimple mode, whereas in the intergranular failure area, relatively smooth surfaces reveal an intergranular cleavage mode. Partial intergranular cleavage happening in the 0.7Mg and 1Mg alloys is most likely the reason why their elongations are lower than those of the 0.1Mg and 0.3Mg alloys with fully transgranular ductile failure.  

The compressive stress-strain curves of the four aged alloys at RT, 600    C, 700    C and 800    C are shown in  Fig. 7 . All of the alloys show noticeable plastic deformation and similar compressive stress-strain characteristics. The alloys exhibit a strong strain- hardening stage in the wide range of 20 e 35% plastic strain after yielding, and as the stress reaches the peak value, it drops quickly corresponding to failure of the testing sample. The temperature dependence of the 0.2% yield strength,  s 0.2 , is plotted to determine the effect of Mg on the strength, which is shown in  Fig. 8 . It is clear that the 0.1Mg and 0.3Mg alloys have the same  s 0.2P  level at the various temperatures, and with further increase of the nominal Mg content,  s 0.2P  obviously decreases. The compressive strengths of the Mg free alloy (702 MPa at 600    C, 771 MPa at 700    C and 739 MPa at 800    C  [27] ) are lower than those of the 0.1Mg and 0.3Mg alloys, but higher than those of the 0.7Mg and 1Mg alloys. Under compression, the four alloys more or less show yield strength anomalies ( Fig. 8 ). That is,  s 0.2P  decreases with tempera- ture and reaches a minimum value at 600    C before rising to the peak value as the temperature reaches 700    C; the strength then decreases again with further increase of the temperature to 800    C. Therefore, the temperature at which the strength anomaly occurs for the four alloys is near 700    C.

# 4. Discussion  

The backscattered SEM observations show that the additions of 0.1Mg\~1Mg do not result in visible metallurgical changes in the element distribution in each phase, the phase constitution and fraction and the microstructure of the aged Co-9Al-4.5W-4.5Mo- 2Ta-0.02B alloy; however, the mechanical responses of the aged Co-9Al-4.5W-4.5Mo-2Ta-0.02B alloy at room and high tempera- tures are signi ﬁ cantly affected by the nominal Mg contents, or the Mg concentrations in the grain boundary. The tensile strength and ductility at room temperature and the compressive strength at 600 e 800    C obviously decrease only when 0.7 e 1 at.% Mg are added ( Figs. 4 and 8 ), whereas tiny additions of 0.1 e 0.3 at.% Mg show no effect on these properties. The fractographic analysis shown in Fig. 6  suggest that dependence of the mechanical properties of the coherent  g / g ' microstructure on the Mg content at room temper- atures is relative to the intergranular failure, which may be caused by a decrease of the grain boundary strength when more Mg seg- regates on the grain boundary ( Fig. 3  and  Table 1 ) to some extent in this investigation. Degradation of the grain boundary strength in- creases the tendency of intergranular failure ( Fig. 6 c and 6d) and decreases the bulk ductility of the coherent  g / g ' microstructure when intergranular cleavage occurs ( Fig. 4  and  Table 2 ). The above phenomena con ﬁ rm that the segregation of more Mg may bring about some metallurgical changes of the grain boundary and  

# enhance intergranular cleavage.  

In particular, the transgranular or intergranular fracture of polycrystalline intermetallics is found to usually depend on the grain boundary precipitation, the grain boundary character distri- bution (GBCD)  [33 e 36] , segregation of the impurity in the grain boundary, and sliding and migration of the grain boundary. With the perfect and clear grain boundaries (no precipitates) shown in Fig. 3 , the grain boundary failure of the coherent  g / g ' microstruc- ture caused by the precipitates is excluded. Early works on L1 2 - Ni 3 Al  [37]  found that only low-angle  S 1 and possibly  S 3 boundaries are particularly resistant to intergranular fracture through avoiding the cutting and stress concentration effects at the grain boundaries. Therefore, the fracture strength or the ductility of a microstructure with intergranular failure may be increased only by increasing the fraction of  S 1 and  S 3 boundaries  [32] . The frequency of the  S 1-3 counted is approximately 78% for the 0.1Mg and 0.3Mg alloys ( Fig. 10 ), which reinforce the fracture strength, as well as the ductility of the coherent  g / g ' microstructure ( Table 2 ). In contrast, the fraction of high-angle or high-CSL grain boundaries, which particularly enforce intergranular fracture, increases with increasing Mg concentration in the grain boundary. A high high-CSL grain boundary fraction, especially in the 1Mg alloy, signi ﬁ cantly reduces the grain boundary strength and brings more intergranular fracture fraction ( Fig. 6 d), which subsequently decrease the strength and ductility of the coherent  g / g ' microstructure at room and/or high temperatures ( Table 2  and  Fig. 8 ). Secondly, O is such a well-known element which decreases the binding energy and strength of the grain boundary  [35,38,39] . From the failure modes and mechanical properties versus the Mg content or the Mg con- centration in the grain boundary ( Fig. 3  and  Table 1 ), the phe- nomenon that the higher Mg additions (0.7 e 1.0 at.%) reduce the strength and ductility of the Co-9Al-4.5W-4.5Mo-2Ta-0.02B alloy can be naturally explained also from the concentration of the im- purity O in the grain boundary. The correlation is that a higher Mg content maintains a higher O concentration in the grain boundary, which enhances the intergranular fracture and decreases the strength and ductility of the bulk coherent  g / g ' microstructure. In addition, the moderate segregation of Mg was also found to decrease the grain boundary energy and increase the grain boundary binding force and strength of the Ni superalloys (with  g / g ' microstructure), the  g ' -Ni 3 Al  [40]  and Fe 3 Al alloys  [41]  through formation of covalent bonds and repulsion of Al-Al atom bonds. This strength mechanism may also available in the Co-Al-W based alloys with the  g / g ' microstructure when the moderate Mg segre- gates at the grain boundary, which is still needed to be further identi ﬁ ed for the Co-Al-W based system.  

# 5. Conclusions  

A comprehensive study of the phase constitution and morphology, grain boundary character distribution (GBCD), O segregation at the grain boundary and mechanical behaviours of the Co-9Al-4.5W-4.5Mo-2Ta-0.02B-(0.1, 0.3, 0.7, 1)Mg alloys was conducted. The major conclusions are summarized as follows.  

1) The microstructure of the as-cast 0.1 e 1.0Mg alloys consisted of a g -Co SS  matrix and Co 3 Ta-based chains, exhibiting a dendritic- like morphology. After aging at 800    C for 50 h, the Co 3 Ta- based phase disappeared, and the microstructure was composed of the  g -Co SS  matrix and nano-scale cuboidal  g '- Co 3 (Al, W) precipitates, and the two phases cohered with each other. An increasing Mg content from 0.1 to 1.0 at.% did not obviously change the cuboidal  g '-Co 3 (Al, W) size (150 e 200 nm in the cube edge length) and fraction (80 e 85%). 2) The Mg atoms were found to mainly segregate at the grain boundaries and  g '-Co 3 (Al, W) phase, and the peak value of the Mg concentration of approximately 0.9 at.% at the grain boundaries was reached in the 0.7Mg and 1Mg alloys. The  S 1\~3 boundary fraction and the capability of excluding impurity O from the grain boundaries obviously decreased when the nominal Mg content increased up to 0.7 and 1 at.%. 3) The four alloys exhibited compressive  ﬂ ow stress anomalies at temperatures above 600    C. With less  S 1\~3 boundary fraction and higher O concentration at the grain boundary, the 0.7Mg and 1Mg alloys always showed lower strength than the 0.1Mg and 0.3Mg alloys at room temperature and 600 e 800    C, while the strength of the 0.1Mg and 0.3Mg alloys were almost at the same level. 4) Under tension at room temperature, the 0.1Mg and 0.3Mg alloys showing higher strength and elongation failed in a trans- granular dimple mode owing to their higher  S 1\~3 boundary fraction and lower O concentration at the grain boundary, while the 0.7Mg and 1Mg alloys with lower strength and elongation fractured in a mixed transgranular-intergranular mode. Partial intergranular cleavage was found to be the reason for the lower tensile strength and ductility in the 0.7Mg and 1Mg alloys.
