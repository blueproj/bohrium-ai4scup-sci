The eutectic  c 0   and  b  are well distributed and nucleate in random directions. # 1. Introduction  

Co–Al–W ternary system has attracted much attention recently for the existence of a stable  c 0 -Co 3 (Al, W) phase with an ordered L1 2  structure  [1,2] . The  c 0   phase was reported to be stabilized toward higher temperatures by the addition of alloying elements, such as Ta, Ti and Ni  [1,3,4] . These cobalt-base alloys have a melting temperature higher than that of nickel-base superalloys and potentially offer higher temperature capacity. New  c / c 0 Co–Al–W–Ti alloys with appropriated Ti-alloying level can increase the high temperature capacity without much secondary phase precipitation, meanwhile result in the lower density than that of Co–Al–W and other quaternary alloys, which provide much motivation for their future use in gas turbine engines.  

Liquid metal cooling (LMC) Bridgman directional solidiﬁcation is one of important ways to fabricate the single crystal superalloys as well as one of effective methods to investigate the solidiﬁcation path through quenching performed during the directional solidiﬁ- cation  [9,10] . Solidiﬁcation microstructures formed at different temperatures are quenched and then orderly presented at different locations in quenched mushy zones. The solidiﬁcation process of the interdendritic phase even in small volume fraction can be investigated by orderly checking the quenched solidiﬁcation microstructure along directional solidiﬁcation direction.  

The purpose of this paper is to determine the microstructure formation in Co–Al–W–Ti alloys through quenching performed during LMC Bridgman directional solidiﬁcation. The solidiﬁcation path and the formation of eutectic colonies with emphasis on ter- minal stage of solidiﬁcation are mainly investigated. The possibility of grain defect formation related to the micro segregation and the phase transformation in quenched mushy zones is also discussed.  

The solidiﬁcation microstructure formation can provide useful information, not only for subsequently required heat treatments but also for the casting process itself. In Ni-base superalloys, much of this knowledge has been effectively applied to understand the solidiﬁcation behavior and evaluate the casting properties and solidiﬁcation defects  [5,6] . Many studies were conducted on the solidiﬁcation path, micro segregation and the euctetic phase forma- tion, which may have strong inﬂuence on ﬁnal as-cast and subse- quently heat-treated microstructures  [5,7,8] . In Co-base Co–Al–W alloys, however, there are still no reports on the solidiﬁcation microstructure formation, especially on the details of the last stage  

# 2. Experimental details  

# 2.1. Materials  

Previous studies have shown that the solvus temperature of  c 0   phase at 1029   C can be achieved in Co–9Al–10W (at.%) alloys and the volume fraction of the  c 0   phase reached 70% in the aged alloys  [11] . Based on Co–9Al–10W alloys, 4 at.% Ti-alloying were added in order to further increase the temperature capacity. However, a small quantity of secondary phases, such as  l  and DO 19 , were precipitated after the Co– 9Al–10W–4Ti alloys were long-term aged for 300 h at 900   C.  Fig. 1  shows the microstructures of the long-term aged alloys. In order to obtain an improved microstructure stability, the new alloys were designed according to the mean composition of the base  c / c 0   microstructures, which are shown in an upper-left pic- ture in  Fig. 1 . The composition of the base  c / c 0   microstructures was determined to be Co–6.7Al–8.9W–4.1Ti (at.%) by a JEOL JXA-8800R electron probe micro-analyser (EPMA). Consquently, Co–6.7Al–8.9W–4.1Ti alloys are prepared for the current study.  

# 2.2. Experimental procedure  

Co–6.7Al–8.9W–4.1Ti alloys with the exact chemical composition of Co–

 6.76Al–8.89W–4.29Ti (at.%) were prepared by the vacuum induction melting

 (VIM). The VIM ingot was cut into bars typically with the size of 8 mm in diameter and 100 mm in length. The bars were re-melted and directionally solidiﬁed by using  l m/s, respectively. The temperature gradient ( G ) is about 80–90 K/mm. Quenching was performed immediately after the directional solidiﬁcation to a constant length of 50 mm. The directionally solidiﬁed bars were sectioned longitudinally and transversely and then polished using standard metallographic techniques. The microstructure and phase composition were analyzed by ﬁeld-emission scanning electron micros- copy (FESEM) using a Zeiss SUPRA 55 in back-scattered electron (BSE) imaging mode and equipped with an energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) detector. The composition of eutectic colony with two phases in quenched mushy zones was determined by the FESEM-EDS element mapping in 2  l m    2  l m areas. EPMA was used to quantitatively determine the compositions in the dendrite core and interdendritic region without any eutectic and granular phases in the directionally solidiﬁed alloys. In order to obtain the phase transformation temperatures, the dif- ferential scanning calorimetric (DSC) measurement was performed at heating rate of 10   C/min after the casted alloys were treated by a solid solution of 1300   C/ 24 h and an aging of 900   C/300 h.

# 3.2. Microstructure of the directionally solidiﬁed alloys  

Fig. 4 a, b and c show the transversal BSE microstructures of the directionally solidiﬁed bars at withdraw rates of 20, 56 and 100  l m/s, respectively. Typical interdendritic microstructures are presented in an upper-right picture in  Fig. 4 a. Some eutectic colo- nies, granular  c 0   and  b  phases with few micrometers in size and no secondary phases were observed in the interdendritic region. The statistical results of the primary dendrite spacing and the volume fraction of interdendritic eutectic colonies are summarized in Fig. 5 a. The primary dendrite spacing decreases, but the volume fraction of the eutectic colony and granular phases increases with increasing withdraw rate.  

The segregation coefﬁcient  k  is one of the important parameters for the directionally solidiﬁed microstructures, which is associated with the segregation of the constituent alloying elements. It is deﬁned as the ratio of the concentration of the elements in the dendrite core to that in the interdendritic region  [13] . The process- ing parameter, e.g., the cooling rate ( G  V ), has great inﬂuence on the segregation coefﬁcient.  Fig. 5 b presents the segregation coefﬁcients of alloying elements in the directionally solidiﬁed microstructures at different withdraw rates. It is shown that the segregation coefﬁcients of Co, Al and W are close to 1, while Ti much lower than 1, which suggests Ti is much preferentially segregated to the interdendritic region. Under experimental conditions, the segregation coefﬁcient of the refractory element W ﬁrstly decreases then nearly remains unchanged with increasing withdraw rate.

# 4. Discussion  

The solidiﬁcation path and phase transition, as well as the solidiﬁcation segregation, play critical roles in the solidiﬁcation microstructure. Phase equilibria in the Co-rich Co–Al–W–Ti qua- ternary system has been investigated by Kobayashi et al.  [14] , however, the detailed solidiﬁcation path under non-equilibrium casting conditions especially during directional solidiﬁcation is unclear yet. There are still no direct evidences on eutectic phase formation process during the ﬁnal stage solidiﬁcation.  

As indicated by Warnken et al., in the Ni-base superalloys, the eutectic colonies epitaxially nucleate and grow on the primarily solidiﬁed  c  dendrite , and the coarse eutectic island with fan- like shape is eventually formed in interdendritic bulk liquid  [16] . The growth direction of the fan-like eutectic colony has presumed to start with the neighbouring  c  dendrite and spread into outside of the fan. In the Co–Al–W–Ti alloys, however, eutectic ( b  +  c 0 ) in- stead of ( c  +  c 0 ) were observed to be formed in the interdendritic region in terminal stages of solidiﬁcation. As shown in  Fig. 3 b, the eutectic ( b  +  c 0 ) did not grow on the primary  c  dendrite. The nucleus of eutectic ( b  +  c 0 ) does not act as one of the existing solid- iﬁed phases. Accordingly, the eutectic colonies can randomly nucleate from the interdendritic liquid and shape in blocky mor- phology. The growth direction of the eutectic colony should start with its inside and spread into all around. Compared with the Ni- base superalloys, the eutectic  b  and  c 0   are well distributed and nucleate in random directions.  

The interdendritic solidiﬁcation sequence can be determined by checking the quenched microstructure evolution in the quenching region along the directional solidiﬁcation direction. The granular  c 0 and  b  phases, so-called primary  c 0   and  b , are disparately deposited from the liquid out of the ( b  +  c 0 ) eutectic pool due to the eutectic phase coexist regions during a later stage of solidiﬁcation. As shown in center with bright grey contrast in  Fig. 3 b and c, the pri- mary  c 0   should be deposited during the solidiﬁcation stage later than the formation of eutectic ( b  +  c 0 ). As indicated by Locations 1, 6 in  Fig. 3  and  Table 1 , Co and W are accumulated to eutectic colonies, but Al and Ti diffuse in the reverse directions during the eutectic reaction. As a result,  b  phase with Al enrichment and Co depletion could form around the eutectic colonies. It is

The elemental accumulation or depletion in the ﬁnal solidiﬁed liquid obviously depends on the dendritic  c  micro segregation, however, the segregation coefﬁcient is related not only to the micro segregation but also to the formation of the eutectic and pri- mary phases in the interdendritic region. Increasing cooling rate may result in a signiﬁcant constitutional undercooling of the remaining liquid, then the increasing volume fraction of the eutec- tic colonies, as shown in  Figs. 4 and 5 a. With increasing withdraw rates, the quantity of the refractory element W consumed in eutec- tics and primary phases interdendritic region is possibly alterable, which induces to a relatively varied segregation coefﬁcient of W.  

The solidiﬁcation behavior of the Co–Al–W–Ti alloys is a key element to assess their potential applications for commercial components. One main concern for the directional solidiﬁcation process is the grain defect formation when used in single crystal components  [17,18] . Typically, grain defects, such as freckles and misoriented grains, are caused by thermosolutal convective insta- bilities induced by dendritic segregation and density gradients which develop in the mushy zone. The segregation coefﬁcients of Co, Al and W measured in the present Co–Al–W–Ti alloys are close to 1. Only the element Ti is much preferentially segregated to the interdendritic liquid, which may possibly have a tendency to in- duce convective instabilities. However, the convective instabilities are likely restricted by the growth of W-rich eutectic colonies and primary  c 0 , which nucleates on no limited sites at the cost of increased Ti consumption. The eutectic colonies and primary  c 0 precipitated at earlier stage of the terminal solidiﬁcation restrict the free passage of liquid and isolate the liquid pools, thus decrease the relative movement of the interdendritic liquid. Accordingly, the eutectic colonies should play the same role as the carbides in Ni-base superalloys  [18] . The unique solidiﬁcation characteristics indicate that the Co–Al–W–Ti alloys will be resistant to the grain defect formation and suitable for fabricating large-scale single- crystals.  

# 5. Conclusions  

(1) The solidiﬁcation path of the Co–Al–W–Ti alloys during the directional solidiﬁcation is determined as follows: L  ?  L +  c  ?  L +  c  + ( b  +  c 0 )  ?  L +  c  +  c 0   + ( b  +  c 0 )  ?  c  +  c 0   +  b + ( b  +  c 0 ). The ( b  +  c 0 ) eutectic and the primary  c 0   and  b  are orderly deposited in terminal stages of solidiﬁcation. The eutectic  c 0   and  b  are well distributed and nucleate in random directions. (2) The  c 0   particles are precipitated initially from the interden- dritic Al-rich regions and subsequently from the dendritic region. The sizes of the  c 0   particles close to the primary  c 0 and  b  phases are greater than that of the particles far from them. (3) Due to the growth of eutectic colonies restricted the inter- dendritic liquid convection at earlier stage of the terminal solidiﬁcation, Co–Al–W–Ti alloys are suitable for fabricating single crystal components.
