# Introduction  

The development and exploitation of gas-turbine blades cast from nickel-base superalloys in single crystal form has been one of the most successful industrial and commercial ventures relating to advanced structural materials over the last twenty years or so (1). The benefits of producing gas-turbine blades from single crystal superalloys has resulted from at least two factors. Firstly, the elimination of grain boundaries from the component has increased the creep ductility by removing sites where cavities develop and has allowed the chemistry of superalloys, designed specifically for use in the single crystal form, to be free of grain boundary strengthening elements thus increasing the melting temperature and potential operating temperature. Secondly, by controlling the orientation of the single crystal casting, normally having the low modulus  , 001 .  direction parallel to the centrifugally stressed blade axis, thermal stresses are minimised and the thermal fatigue resistance is greatly enhanced. However, these advantages have been obtained at the expense of dealing with a highly anisotropic material. In order to exploit the full potential of single crystal superalloys, an effective anisotropic design strategy is required.  

# Anisotropic Creep Behaviour of Single Crystal Superalloys  

A successful model of the creep behaviour of single crystal superalloys must be capable of describing a number of characteristic features:  

a) The shape of the creep curves at 850°C and above are generally dominated by a progressively increasing creep rate for both constant stress and constant load conditions, and both in tensile and compressive loading. Primary creep is more apparent in lower temperature testing and in stressing parallel to  , 111 .  rather than  , 001 . . This strongly suggests the development of a “damage” that progressively weakens the material; however, the absence of grain boundaries means that the traditional cavitation damage, which would preferentially affect tensile creep behaviour, can not be a significant factor. A comprehensive study on CMSX-4 by Ohno and Takeuchi (2) also shows that thermal ageing leading to coarsening of the precipitate microstructure has an insignificant effect. The proposed mechanism of strain softening by the accumulation of mobile dislocations with increasing plastic (creep) strain by Dyson and McLean (3) is capable of accounting for both the dominant tertiary creep behaviour and the weak tension/compression asymmetry.  

b) The differences in uniaxial creep behaviour for different crystal orientations are not constant, but vary with stress, temperature and alloy composition. In particular, in a given alloy  , 001 .  crystals can have better creep properties than  , 111 .  in a certain range of stress and temperature, whereas the opposite is true in other conditions. This strongly indicates that the anisotropy cannot be simply expressed in terms of resolved stresses on a single deformation system. If that were true then the anisotropy would be a simple function of the Schmid factor and be totally defined by the crystallo- graphic relationships. A number of crystallographic based models have been developed that have successfully accounted for the anisotropy of both creep and fatigue deformation. However, all of them consider the operation of two or more families of slip systems. The time dependence of the creep deformation is introduced by allowing the deformation to be described by viscous glide on each system with different stress and temperature dependencies. The challenge with respect to these models is to identify the specific deformation modes that operate.  

It is clear from the above summary that it is critical that the appropriate modes of deformation be identified. A number of previous studies have pointed to the operation of a range of slip systems predominantly by examining the dislocation structures by TEM. In general creep tests at stresses higher than 450 MPa and temperatures lower than 850°C exhibit limited activation of partial dislocations of type a/6 , 112 .  within  g 9  precipitates. It has been reported that at temperatures  .  850°C most dislocations have (111) , 011 .  characteristics (4). There has been limited evidence of (100) , 011 . systems being active during creep testing of  , 111 .  single crystal superalloys (5). At these relatively higher temperatures generation of misfit dislocations at  g / g 9  interfaces, which reduce the coherency stress within the system, appear to inhibit the activation of the partial dislocations. However, there is no consensus on either the existence or the importance of all of the systems. Part of the present study aims to clarify this issue and to incorporate the findings into a continuum damage mechanics model of creep anisotropy.

# Results and Discussion  

The orientations of single crystal specimens can be measured with accuracy of  ;  1° even for highly strained ( , 80%) material with the EBSD technique. It also provides crystallographic information on a very fine spatial scale ( ; 1  m m 2 ). Typically the specimens show considerable necking and consequently there are large differences in local creep deformation along the specimen length. Crystal orientations are determined across the cross-section and at various positions along the length of such a failed specimen and the measurements are related to the initial single crystal orientation. In this way the rotations of the crystal as a function of creep strain can be followed. The orientation distribution for three single crystals, determined before creep testing, revealed that the orientation of the tensile axis varies along the gauge length by up to 8°, probably due to the presence of dendrite misorientations. Therefore, throughout this work, nominal orientations, i.e. the low index orientaion close to the tensile axis, rather exact crystal orientations which vary spatially and with strain are used to describe the changes in orientation that occur as a result of creep deformation. The shape change results, presented as transverse strain, are confined to high strain regimes of  , 011 .  and  , 112 .  crystal orientations that show significant deviation from circular cross-section. An earlier report (7) has considered a wider range of crystal orientations and strains. The TEM observations reported in this work are specific to large strain deformation mechanisms, which may be different than small strain mechanisms.

Analysis shows that these two regions have a twin orientation relationship of 60° rotation about  , 111 . . Such mechanical twinning is associated with a (111) , 112 .  displacement. # [111] Tensile Axis  

As with the [001] specimens the range of orientations present increases with increasing strain. However at high strains twinning occurs in isolated regions which have orientations far from the original [111] tensile orientation. Moreover, the initiation of twins is associated with a sharp change in the average orientation, Fig (4a), of the single crystal. By contrast the crystal rotations in [011] and [001] crystals are continuous. SEM inspection shows, Fig. (4b), evidence of such mechanical twinning. Again the extent of twinning and the fact that it occurs relatively late in life can only account for a small fraction of the total strain, as shown for the  , 011 .  crystal. For tensile deformation close to  , 111 .  the rotations are inconsistent with {1 11} , 011 .  deformation. It is difficult by simple analysis of the tensile axis to differentiate between {111} , 112 .  and {100} , 011 .  shear, particularly for orientations close to the  , 011 .  zone boundary where two systems have similar Schmid factors. No significant changes in specimen shape were measured.

The above analysis provides strong evidence that slip can occur on cube planes with a  , 011 .  slip vector. The crystallographic models of creep implicitly ignore the two phase nature of superalloys. Since the  g 9  phase constitutes around 70% by volume of single crystal superalloys at operating temperatures, this is an unjustified assumption. We note that the  g 9  is distributed as quite regular cubes with faces parallel to the crystallographic cubic planes; consequently, the minority  g  phase forms well aligned channels separating these cube faces. In the presence of tensile stresses inclined to the cube faces there are relatively high shear stresses along these unreinforced channels. Macroscopic cube shear can occur by constrained deformation through these channels due to activation of dislocation activity on two or more octahedral planes or alternatively by climb of dislocations along the  g - g 9  interface which is a (001) plane. These observations support the basic assumption of the models developed by Ghosh  et al.  (9) for the simultaneous operation of shear on both octahedral (I) and cube planes.  

# Conclusions  

1. The crystals with initial crystal orientations close to  , 001 .  and  , 011 .  axes deform mainly by glide on the {111} , 011 .  systems; Crystals with orientations near to  , 111 .  deform by {100} , 011 .  systems.

 2. The nucleation of misfit dislocations at high temperatures ( . 850°C) relaxes the internal coherency stress associated with lattice mismatch between  g / g 9  phases, which eliminates activation of a/6 , 112 .  partial dislocations.

 3. The contribution of a/6 , 112 .  partial dislocations to deformation at low temperatures ( , 850°C) at high strains ( . 10%) is related to formation of mechanical twins, which results in {111} , 112 . displacements. However, the maximum strains associated with twins are much smaller than the measured creep strain.
