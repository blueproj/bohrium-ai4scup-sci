# Abstract  

In the context of topical and transdermal delivery, cosol- vents may be expected to enhance solubility, modify drug  partitioning behaviour and alter skin barrier properties. In  the present study, we have investigated systematically the  influence of increasing amounts of the cosolvent ethanol on  the solubility, ionization, and permeability characteristics of  ibuprofen in silicone and in skin. The possibility of ibuprofen  self-association was also examined. As the amount of etha- nol was raised from 0 to 100%, ibuprofen solubility was en- hanced 5,500-fold relative to its aqueous solubility. Up to  50%, the pK  a  of ibuprofen shifted from 4.44 to 5.68. Infrared  spectroscopy confirmed the presence of ibuprofen in both  monomer and dimer forms in vehicles containing 75 and  100% ethanol. In silicone membranes, the flux of ibuprofen  increased 8-fold over the range of ethanol concentrations  studied (0–100%) relative to aqueous solutions, with the  highest flux observed for 100% ethanol. However, in skin,  the flux of ibuprofen was optimal for 50:  50 and 75:  25 etha- nol-water vehicles (  1 10-fold flux enhancement). The lower  ibuprofen flux from pure ethanol may reflect the alcohol’s  ability to dehydrate human skin. In general, the flux data  confirm that increasing ethanol content enhances the solu- bility of ibuprofen in silicone and human skin as well as in the  vehicle itself.  Copyright © 2008 S. # Introduction  

To understand the role of formulation components in  topical drug delivery, it is necessary to separate the vari- ous contributing factors to the permeation process which  may include drug thermodynamic activity, the ionization  state of the permeant and any vehicle effects on mem- brane properties. For ionizable permeants there is also  the possibility that the ionized species will permeate,  even though its partitioning into the stratum corneum  may be orders of magnitude lower than that for the union- ized moiety   [1]  

The steady-state flux of a drug through the stratum  corneum is described by Fick’s first law of diffusion  (equation 1)  

where J represents drug flux, D is the drug diffusion co- efficient, K is the partition coefficient for drug between  the stratum corneum and the vehicle, C  vehicle  is the drug  concentration in the vehicle and h is the diffusional path  length for the drug.  

If a cosolvent does not affect membrane barrier prop- erties, its inclusion in the formulation of a weakly acidic  drug of the form HX is expected to  

(1) increase the unionized fraction (HX) of the drug.  Since the latter has a higher lipophilicity relative to  X  – , the overall skin flux, which is proportional to the  skin-formulation partition coefficient, K (equation 1)  should theoretically be enhanced  

(2)  increase drug solubility in the formulation. If the drug  concentration is kept constant, this effectively de- creases the degree of saturation and hence should lead  to a lower flux. The precise outcome will depend ulti- mately on the nature of the cosolvent, the pH of the  formulation and the pK   of the drug.  

Many non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs are or- ganic acids with pK  s ranging from 3 to 5. At the pH of  the skin surface (   5), such compounds will be ionized.  In the deeper layers of the skin, a less acidic environment  is encountered and the degree of ionization of such mol- ecules will therefore increase with consequent effects on  solubility and permeation. Ibuprofen is one of the most  widely available non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs  for the management of inflammation and is available in  both gel and cream formulations. Although the perme- ation of ibuprofen has been studied from a range of ve- hicles   [2]  , there has been, to our knowledge, no system- atic investigation into the manner in which formulation  components such as cosolvents may influence the ioniza- tion and permeability of ibuprofen into and through the  skin. The aim of the present study therefore was to exam- ine the influence of increasing amounts of the cosolvent  ethanol on the solubility, ionization and permeation  characteristics of ibuprofen through silicone and human  skin. As saturated solutions were used throughout this  work, a further aim was to investigate the possibility of  self-association of ibuprofen in the different cosolvent  mixtures.  

# Materials and Methods  

Materials  Ibuprofen was a generous gift from Wyeth Consumer Health- care, Havant, UK. All other materials were of reagent grade and  were obtained from VWR, UK.  

Solubility of Ibuprofen in Aqueous-Ethanol Mixtures  

Excess drug was added to each solvent or cosolvent mixture  and stirred with a magnetic bar for 48 h in a water bath main- tained at 32  °  C. Solutions were then centrifuged for 15 min at  4,000   g  and the supernatant was diluted and assayed either by  HPLC or UV spectroscopy. For HPLC analysis, the sample was  diluted in isopropanol, followed by appropriate dilution in meth- anol. The stationary phase was an Apex reverse-phase ODS 5-   m  packed column (250   !  4.6 mm) and a mobile phase of 65% ace- tonitrile and 35% phosphate buffer (adjusted to pH 3.2 using or- thophosphoric acid). UV detection was employed at 225 nm and  a flow rate of 1.2 ml  min  –1 . The retention time was approximate- ly 5 min, and calibration curves were constructed on the basis of  peak area measurements using   1 5 standard solutions. Experi- ments were performed in triplicate and mean values with stan- dard deviation and coefficient of variation were calculated. Re- producibility was evaluated prior to injection of the samples and  during analysis, and CV values were   ! 5% in all experiments.  

# Saturated Aqueous Solubility as a Function of pH  

Excess drug was added to 10 ml of phosphate-citrate buffer   [3]  at different pH values and stirred with a magnetic bar for 48 h in  a water bath maintained at 32  °  C. The ionic strength was con- trolled by inclusion of 3   M  NaClO  4  in the buffer solutions. Solu- tions were centrifuged for 30 min at 10,000 rpm. The supernatant  was diluted and assayed by UV spectroscopy. Experiments were  performed in triplicate and mean values with SD and CV were  calculated.  

# pK  a  and log P and log D Determination  

The pK   values of ibuprofen were determined using published  methods   [4, 5]  . Ibuprofen was titrated in mixtures of water and  ethanol using a GlpKa apparatus (Sirius Analytical Instruments,  Forrest Row, UK). The ionic strengths of the solutions were set at 

 0.15   M  with KCl. Yasuda-Shedlovsky plots were used to calculate 

 ‘aqueous’ pK   values   [6, 7]  . Using octanol as the partition solvent,  log P values were also determined by potentiometric titration.  The pK   log P program (version 5.1, ACD Ltd.) was also used to  predict the distribution coefficients (log D) which were subse- quently compared with the experimentally derived values.  

# Permeation Studies in Silicone Membrane and Skin  

The silicone membranes used in this work were from Samco  (Nuneaton, UK) with a measured thickness of 400    m as de- scribed previously   [8]  . Full-thickness human skin from cosmetic  surgery, obtained following informed consent and with appropri- ate institutional ethical approval was used for skin permeation  studies. The epidermis was separated by immersing the skin in  water maintained at 60  °  C for 1 min   [9]  . The tissue was then  pinned to a corkboard and the epidermis was very carefully peeled  away from the dermis. The epidermis was immersed in cold water,  mounted onto filter paper, and stored in a freezer at –20  °  C until  required. Prior to the diffusion experiment the skin was defrosted  and cut to appropriate size.  

Permeation experiments (n   6 4) were conducted in Franz dif- fusion cells with a receptor volume of 2.5 ml, and a diffusion area  of 1 cm  2 . For all permeation experiments the receptor fluid was  pH 7.4 phosphate-buffered saline. The diffusion experiments  were performed under occluded conditions by sealing the donor  compartment with microscope coverslips. Studies were also con- ducted with membranes pretreated with ethanol for 24 h to fur- ther investigate the influence of ethanol on ibuprofen perme- ation.  

Flux values were calculated from the cumulative amount of  drug diffusing into the receptor compartment as a function of  time. # Attenuated Total Reflectance Fourier Transform Infrared   Spectroscopy  

The presence of ibuprofen monomer and dimer species was  evaluated using multibounce attenuated total reflectance Fourier  transform infrared (ATR-FTIR). A Bruker Tensor 27 instrument  fitted with a Specac multiple reflection accessory with a zinc sel- enide crystal was used and data were acquired using OPUS soft- ware, version 5.5 (Bruker, Germany).  

# Results and Discussion  

# Solubility Studies  

Addition of 25% ethanol increased the solubility of  ibuprofen to 343 mg/ml, a 3,800-fold enhancement rela- tive to the drug’s aqueous solubility (  table 1  ). In pure eth- anol, the solubility is 5,500-fold higher than in water. Ibu- profen has a reported pK   of 4.41   [11]  , explaining the con- siderable solubility enhancement (50-fold) over the pH  range 2–6 (  table 2  ). Similar solubility data have been re- ported over a comparable pH range elsewhere   [12]  

# pK  a  Determination  

The pK   of ibuprofen in water, and in 25:  75 and 50:  50  ethanol-water mixtures, was determined by potentiom- etry. From Yasuda-Shedlovsky plots constructed for ibu- profen in ethanol the relative fraction of drug which is  ionized can be determined as a function of pH. Increas- ing the ethanol content from 0 to 50% shifted the pK   of  ibuprofen from 4.44 to 5.68 (  fig. 1  ). In water, approxi- mately 80% of ibuprofen is ionized at pH 5, which is close  to that typically found at the skin surface. However, in 50:  50 (v/v) ethanol-water, the pK   shift reduces the per- centage of the ionized form of ibuprofen to    15% at pH  5. Therefore, a greater amount of unionized ibuprofen is  available with increasing ethanol content.

Log D  

There is a good correlation between the measured and  predicted log D values as shown in   figure 2  ; the results  also agree well with previous measurements   [1]  . The log  D of unionized ibuprofen is 4.0, whereas that of the ion- ized form is 0.08. At the pH of the skin surface (   5), log  D is predicted to be 3.2. With increasing amounts of eth- anol, log D increases further because of the increase in  pK   and the suppression of ibuprofen ionization.  

# ATR-FTIR Spectroscopy  

The possibility of self-association was investigated by  ATR-FTIR as such behaviour is typical of carboxylic ac- ids in organic media   [13]  . The infrared reference spec- trum of ibuprofen exhibits a sharp C = O bond absor- bance peak around 1,710 cm  –1  which can be used to mon- itor the presence of monomer or dimer in solution. In  order to form dimers, the ibuprofen molecules will associ- ate through interactions between the hydroxyl hydrogen  and the carbonyl oxygen of two adjacent molecules. This  interaction will introduce an extra constraint to the vi- bration of the C = O bond, therefore decreasing its stretch- ing frequency. Consequently, the ibuprofen dimer will  absorb at a lower wavenumber relative to the monomer  [14]  . Ad- dition of 25% ethanol induced a 2-fold increase in flux.  Increasing the amount of ethanol to 50% again doubled the  flux. While 75:  25 (v/v) ethanol-water did not cause a fur- ther significant increase in transport, the use of pure etha- nol resulted in the highest flux observed,    1.5 mg/cm  2 /h.  

The slope of the Yasuda-Shedlovsky plot used to calcu- late the aqueous pK   is inversely proportional to the aver- age ionic diameter of the solvated molecule   [7]  . From the  plots constructed in this work, it appears that there is a  change in the solvation structure at around 30:  70 (v/v) eth- anol-water, as there is a change in slope at this cosolvent  mixture (data not shown). It is possible that the enhanced  flux reflects the faster permeation of ethanol relative to  water and that ibuprofen within an ethanol ‘cage’ is taken  across the membrane. Because of the change in pK   with  solvent polarity, and the greater amount of unionized ibu- profen available for permeation, the partitioning of ibu- profen into the membrane should be affected.

Ethanol-Water Vehicles – Membrane Pretreatment  Studies  

The results of the pretreatment studies showed that  the fluxes for the permeation of ibuprofen through sili- cone membrane pretreated with ethanol are not signifi- cantly higher than for the 25:  75 and pure ethanol systems  through untreated membrane (  table 4  ). The 75:  25 etha- nol-water formulation has a slightly lower flux (   700 vs.   900    g/cm  2 /h). As noted for the studies where the  membrane was not pretreated, relatively low amounts of  ethanol can significantly enhance permeation. The parti- tion parameter values for the 100% ethanol vehicle is sig- nificantly higher than for the 25:  75 and 75:  25 vehicles  (  table 3  ), suggesting that the major effects of ethanol are  to change the solubility properties of ibuprofen in the  membrane thus enhancing flux.  

# Permeation Studies in Skin  

Ibuprofen fluxes across skin are shown in   table 5  ; the  values were lower than those through silicone membrane  (  fig. 4  ). Diffusion across skin increased with the amount  of ethanol in the formulation. These effects reflect the  ability of ethanol-water mixtures to modify the barrier  function of the skin by a number of mechanisms which  may include lipid fluidization   [16, 17]  , lipid disordering  [18]  , lipid extraction   [19]  , and modulation of the lipid bar- rier and keratinized protein conformation at low ethanol  concentrations   [20]  

The lower flux values for pure ethanol in human skin  versus silicone reflect a direct effect of ethanol on the skin  barrier. Ethanol and methanol alter the stratum corneum  to a much greater degree than other alcohols   [21] and the  findings of the present study are in line with earlier ob- servations   [22]  . In that work, ethanol flux through skin  was a function of ethanol volume fraction in the donor  solution and dehydration and shrinkage of the stratum  corneum was seen after equilibration with pure ethanol.  The decrease in oestradiol flux through human skin at  ethanol-water ratios higher than 60:  40 (v/v) observed be- fore   [15] has also been ascribed to ethanol dehydration  effects on the stratum corneum.

# Conclusions  

We have previously evaluated the skin penetration of  topical formulations of ibuprofen and we have shown that  the composition of the vehicles can have a significant im- pact on the percutaneous penetration of the drug   [2, 24– 27]  . A typical cream formulation may contain up to 10% of  a cosolvent which can alter the pK   of a weak acid or a weak  base and change thereby its solubility and partitioning be- haviour. As these changes will influence percutaneous  penetration, the present study was designed to explore the  modulation of ibuprofen flux across silicone membrane  and skin. In the lipophilic environment of the stratum cor- neum, an ionized species is expected to permeate more  slowly than a unionized moiety. From the pK   and log D  measurements, increasing amounts of ethanol increased  the pK   and the proportion of unionized ibuprofen, en- hancing the partitioning of the compound. The presence  of ibuprofen dimers at higher ethanol concentrations may  also influence partitioning behaviour. The actual effects of  ethanol on partitioning and diffusion are difficult to de- convolve from conventional permeation studies as can be  seen from the present work. For both skin and silicone,  flux increased with increasing amounts of ethanol; how- ever, pure ethanol solutions did not enhance the skin per- meation of ibuprofen. Both silicone and skin permeation  data suggested that the influence of ethanol is primarily  due to enhanced solubility of ibuprofen in the membrane.  

The addition of the cosolvent clearly has a significant  effect upon the physicochemical properties of ibuprofen  in the systems studied. The vehicles used in the present  study were simple binary ethanol-water systems, whereas  conventional topical formulations will generally contain  a far greater number of excipients. Future studies will ex- amine more complex systems and will also investigate  how the partition and diffusion parameters change with  the specific vehicle.
