# Few-Shot Drug Synergy Prediction with a Prior-Guided Hypernetwork Architecture  

Qingqing Zhang, Shaowu Zhang\*, Yuehua Feng, and Jianyu Shi  

Abstract —Predicting drug synergy is critical to tailoring feasible drug combination treatment regimens for cancer patients. However, most of the existing computational methods only focus on data-rich cell lines, and hardly work on data-poor cell lines. To this end, here we proposed a novel few-shot drug synergy prediction method (called HyperSynergy) for data-poor cell lines by designing a prior-guided Hypernetwork architecture, in which the meta-generative network based on the task embedding of each cell line generates cell line dependent parameters for the drug synergy prediction network. In HyperSynergy model, we designed a deep Bayesian variational inference model to infer the prior distribution over the task embedding to quickly update the task embedding with a few labeled drug synergy samples, and presented a three-stage learning strategy to train HyperSynergy for quickly updating the prior distribution by a few labeled drug synergy samples of each data-poor cell line. Moreover, we proved theoretically that HyperSynergy aims to maximize the lower bound of log-likelihood of the marginal distribution over each data-poor cell line. The experimental results show that our HyperSynergy outperforms other state-of-the-art methods not only on data-poor cell lines with a few samples ( e.g. , 10, 5, 0), but also on data-rich cell lines. Source code and data are at https://github.com/NWPU-903PR/HyperSynergy.  

Index Terms —Drug Synergy Prediction, Cancer Cell Line, Few-Shot Learning, Hypernetwork, Bayesian Variational Inference.  

# 1 I NTRODUCTION  

T HROUGH extensive research on cancers in recent decades, it is found that two main reasons contribute to the increasing cancer mortality [1]. One reason is that there are complex compensatory mechanisms in cancer cells, leading to the hereditary/acquired resistance of tumors to the traditional anti-cancer monotherapy [2], [3], [4]. Another reason is that the response of different patients to the same treatment is significantly different, resulting in the traditional ”one size fits all” anti-cancer treatment often unable to achieve satisfactory effect in clinical trials [5], [6]. Therefore, the treatment methods of tailoring feasible drug combination treatment regimen for cancer patients have attracted more and more attention [7], [8].  

Usually, drug synergy is used to quantify the potential of a drug pair. The higher the synergy score, the more likely it is to become a drug combination with good therapeutic effect and low adverse reactions. Some mathematical mod- els [9], [10] have been developed to assess the drug synergy. For example, Loewe additivity model [9] presented a Loewe score to estimate the drug synergy, and Bliss independence model [10] designed a Bliss score to assess the drug synergy. However, these models require to input full dose–response matrix ( i.e. , the drug response matrix of two drugs un- der different dose combinations). Obviously, with limited resources, it is difficult and even infeasible to screen the potential drug combinations for different diseases/patients through the clinical trial and error methods, and even the high-throughput screening methods [11], because the space of potential drug combinations is astronomical [12]. In view of these facts, predicting drug synergy in cancer cell lines [13] becomes an urgent and critical task for prioritizing combination therapies with therapeutic potential.  

According to the drug screening criteria [14], existing drug synergy prediction methods can be categorized into two main groups: target-based methods and phenotype- based methods. Target-based methods, such as DrugComb- oRanker [15], NLLSS [16], Cheng  et al.  [17], SynGeNet [18] and CPGD [19], aim to design the scoring function of drug synergy according to the biochemical knowledge. Generally, these methods first need to build/identify the disease mod- ules/pathways for a specific disease in the context of human protein-protein interaction network, and then evaluate the drug synergy according to the relationship between drug target and disease modules/pathways, as well as the ability of drugs to reverse disease state. Although target-based methods have a clear screening mechanism for synergistic drug combinations, but they require to explicitly know the targets of drugs, the genes or pathways related to specific diseases, the drug-induced transcriptome data, and so on.  

Phenotype-based methods, such as, DeepSynergy [20], DTF [21] and MatchMaker [22], focus on exploiting a great deal of drug synergy samples to train a machine learning model for predicting drug synergy in cell lines. In these methods, each drug synergy sample is a quadruple that consists of drug A, drug B, a cell line, and drug synergy score ( e.g. , Doxorubicin, Sorafenib, A-375, 0.1238). Usually, drugs are characterized by their biochemical attributes ( e.g. , physicochemical characteristics, simplified molecular input line entry system, molecular fingerprints), and cell lines are characterized by their biomolecular attributes ( e.g. , gene expression level, somatic mutation, copy number variation, gene methylation level), and each sample is characterized by combining the attributes of drug A, drug B, and a cell line. Compared with the target-based methods, phenotype- based methods primarily rely on the biochemical attributes of drugs and biomolecular attributes of cell lines, which are relatively easy to obtain. However, the prediction perfor- mance of existing phenotype-based methods is not always satisfactory due to insufficient drug synergy data or un- derfitting prediction models, especially their performance worsens quickly when working on new cell lines that only have a few drug synergy data.  

Learning from a few samples is possible only with strong inductive biases [23]. Recently, few-shot learning [24] as an emerging method of transfer leaning has been success- fully applied to biomedicine, such as predicting cancer survival outcome [25], inferring the properties and activi- ties of small-molecule compounds [26], predicting the drug response in clinical contexts ( i.e. , patient-derived tumor cell lines/xenografts) [27]. Most few-shot learning methods are based on meta-learning, which aims to learn task-general knowledge ( i.e. , meta-knowledge) that can be quickly and effectively adapt to new tasks from a bound of similar tasks. Compared with the traditional transfer learning based on fine-tuning, few-shot learning may have more potential for the few-shot drug synergy prediction, because it explicitly incorporates adaptation in the training algorithm. However, over-fitting is still an open problem in few-shot learning. To alleviate this issue, the common approaches in biomedical field are to reduce the model complexity, or decompose a complex task into multiple simple tasks. For example, Ma  et al.  [27] decomposed pan-drug ( i.e. , any drug) response task into the single drug ( i.e. , specific drug) response tasks, and utilized the Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning (MAML) [28] to pretrain a small-scale multi-layer perceptron model for predicting the response of one drug in different cell lines. Nevertheless, the simple model is easy under-fitting, and it is inconvenient to re-pretrain a model for each drug. Being aware of these issues, we aim at developing a novel few-shot drug synergy prediction method for not only predicting the pan-drug synergy in date-poor cell lines, but also without sacrificing its non-linear fitting ability.  

To this end, here we proposed a novel few-shot learning method (called HyperSynergy) for few-shot drug synergy prediction. HyperSynergy is composed of three deep net- works: feature embedding network, meta-generative net- work, and synergy prediction network. The meta-generative network and synergy prediction network constitute the prior-guided hypernetwork. Based on the deep message passing neural network [29] and deep convolutional neural network, the feature embedding network extracts the high- level features of drugs and cell lines from drugs molec- ular graphs and cell line transcriptome data. The meta- generative network generates the weights and bias parame- ters of cell line-specific synergy prediction network based on the task embedding vectors of each cell line. Given the functional weights generated with meta-generative net- work and the high-level features learned from the feature embedding network, the synergy prediction network can directly compute the drug synergy. Different from other hypernetwork-based methods that use the arbitrary fixed- length vector representation ( e.g. , one-hot encoding vector of task identity) as the initial task embedding, we design a deep Bayesian variational inference model to infer the prior distribution over the task embedding of each cell line, from which we sample the task embedding as the initial task embedding of the weights generation network, so that the task embedding vectors can be quickly updated with a few samples after a few steps of stochastic gradient descent optimization. In addition, we present a three-stage learning strategy to train HyperSynergy for quickly updating the prior distribution with a few labeled drug synergy samples for each task, thus improving its prediction performance, and enhancing its robustness in the data-poor cell lines. The first stage pretrains the feature embedding network on the instances sampled from data-rich cell lines. The second stage learns the parameters of the meta-generative network on the training tasks driven from data-rich cell lines. The third stage updates the prior distribution over the context embedding of a new data-poor cell line by using a few samples.  

Under the theoretical framework of hierarchical Bayesian variational inference, we prove that given a few samples, HyperSynergy aims to maximize the variational lower bound of marginal distribution log-likelihood over the drug synergy data in each cell line, and the learned “meta-knowledge” is the parameters of meta-generative network, which are shared across all cell lines. The results on the real-world drug synergy datasets show that Hy- perSynergy outperforms other state-of-the-art drug synergy prediction methods both in few-shot (or zero-shot) setting and data-rich setting.  

In sum, the main contributions of this work are as follows:  

1) We introduce few-shot learning into the task of drug synergy prediction, and built a prior-guided hyper- network architecture to predict the drug synergy on data-poor cell lines. 2) Different from previous hypernetwork, we design a deep Bayesian variational inference model to infer the prior distribution over the task embedding of each cell line for sampling the task embedding vec- tors as the inputs of the weight generation network. It can provide a good initial point for parameters optimization, and effectively reduce the free space of parameters optimization. 3) We present a three-stage learning strategy to quickly update the prior distribution with a few samples for each few-shot drug synergy prediction task by trans- ferring the “meta-knowledge” learned from data-rich cell lines, and an “unequal sampling strategy” to boost the performance on the data-poor cell lines. 4) We prove theoretically that HyperSynergy aims to maximize the lower bound of log-likelihood of the marginal distribution over each few-shot drug syn- ergy prediction task. 5) We perform comprehensive experiments on a dataset consisted of multi-studies, and demonstrate the ef- fectiveness of HyperSynergy on few-shot setting, zero-shot setting and data-rich setting.  

# 2 R ELATED  W ORK  

In this section, we first review the related studies about drug synergy prediction, and then discuss some few-shot learning methods that are closely related to our work.  

# 2.1 Drug synergy prediction  

In this part, we only introduce the phenotype-based meth- ods related to our HyperSynergy method, such as DeepSyn- ergy [20], MatchMarker [22], Li  et al.  [30], Kim  et al.  [31], and Celebi  et al.  [32]. DeepSynergy [20] used a feed forward neural network to predict the drug synergy by integrating the chemical characteristics of a drug pair and genomic features of a cell line. The chemical characteristics include the counts of extended connectivity fingerprints, physio- chemical properties and binary toxicophoric features. In- stead of directly concatenating different features of drugs and cell lines, MatchMaker [22] first trained two parallel fully connected networks ( i.e. , one network per drug) to learn the drug specific representation from 11 kinds of drug chemical structure features and the untreated cell line transcriptional profiles, then the concatenated embedding vectors of both drugs were fed into the third fully connected network to predict the Loewe values of drug pairs. Li  et al.  [30] and Celebi  et al.  [32] adopted the machine learn- ing model ( i.e. , random forest, support vector machine, or XGBoost) to predict the drug synergy score by integrating the features of drug pairs and cell lines, such as the chem- ical structure, drug targets, target protein domains, target pathways, interaction networks, gene expression, mutations and copy number variations, and monotherapy informa- tion. Kim  et al.  [31] proposed a drug synergy prediction model (named as TFSynergy), which is composed of a drug encoder, a cell line encoder and a merging layer. The drug encoder learns the embedding representations of each drug from Molecular ACCess System fingerprints, canonical SMILES, and target genes, and the cell line encoder learns the embedding representations of each cell line from cancer type and gene expression data. The merging layer merges the embedding representations of drug pair and cell line to output the drug synergy score. However, these methods only focus on the cell lines that have many drug synergy data, and it is difficult to operate normally on cell lines with few and even without synergy data. Although Kim  et al.  [31] adopted transfer learning to predict drug synergy in data- poor cancer tissues, it still required many training samples to pre-train and fine-tune the model. Besides, most of exiting methods for predicting drug synergy use the handcraft features that rely on human experience. In this work, we directly learn the high-level embedding representation from the molecular graphs of drugs, and the untreated cell line transcriptional profiles to predict the drug synergy in data- poor cell lines.  

# 2.2 Few-shot learning  

The task of few-shot drug synergy prediction is a few- shot learning problem. Few-shot learning is a new machine learning paradigm, which utilizes the previously learned knowledge to learn quickly when only a few training sam- ples are available. There are many works for few-shot learn- ing, such as embedding learning [33], [34], meta learning [28], [35], and generative modeling [36], [37]. Among them, meta-learning is the most common in few-shot learning approaches, which learns from a bound of the labeled tasks rather than the labeled samples, and each task consists of a labeled support sample set and a labeled query sample set. In this work, we define the few-shot task as follows: given the synergy scores of a few drug pairs in a cell line, we will predict the synergy scores of other drug pairs in this cell line. To achieve this few-shot task of predicting the drug synergy in a data-poor cell line, we use a bound of few- shot tasks sampled from data-rich cell lines to meta-train the prior-guided hypernetwork. So that, our HyperSynergy method can learn quickly from a few samples in a data-poor cell line, and then predict the synergy scores of other drug pairs.  

Our HyperSynergy method is also relevant to the stud- ies of hypernetworks ( e.g. , Ha  et al.  [38], Oswald  et al. [39], MeLA [40]) and task recognition/coding ( e.g. , Neural Statistics [41], Variational Homo-Encoder [23]). Ha  et al. [38] presented the hypernetwork architecture to generate the weights for a larger recurrent neural network (RNN) by using a small RNN, where the inputs of the small RNN are trainable. Oswald  et al.  [39] introduced the task- conditioned hypernetworks to generate the weights of a target model based on task embedding, where each task has a set of differentiable deterministic parameters that can be learned. However, Ha  et al.  [38] and Oswald  et al.  [39] only aim to design a small neural network to generate the weights of a target model, so that the optimization is constrained to a smaller weight space, and the initial representation for each task is uninformed ( e.g. , a one- hot encoding of the task identity). Meta-learning Autoen- coder (MeLA) [40] combined three models of task-specific model, meta-recognition model and meta-generative model to realize the few-shot predict task. MeLA augments a pre-existing, single-task neural network ( i.e. , task-specific model) with a meta-recognition model to generate the task embedding vector with a few samples, and the embedding vector is then employed by a meta-generative model to generate the parameters of the task-specific model. Same as the literature [42], MeLA concatenated the features and label of each sample, mapping them into a low dimensional space to generate the model/task embedding vector. However, when the dimension of features is much higher than that of labels, the label information will be submerged. On the other hand, Neural statistics [41] learned to infer the posterior distribution over context for a given dataset by an extension of the variational encoder. Variational Homo-Encoder [23] learned to infer the posterior distribution over context for a given dataset with only a few samples. However, these two works aim to generate new samples ( e.g. , handwritten digits, handwritten characters), which is different from the purpose of our few-shot drug synergy prediction task.  

Inspired by above works, here we proposed a novel prior guided hypernetwork architecture for few-shot drug synergy prediction. Our HyperSynergy is different from above works in the following three key aspects: i) Instead of using the uninformed initial task embedding, we designed a deep Bayesian variational inference model to infer the posterior distribution over context of a cell line by using the features of a few support samples, and took it as the prior distribution over the task embedding to sample a vector as the initial task embedding vector of the cell line. ii) We have proved theoretically that HyperSynergy aims to maximize the lower bound of log-likelihood of the marginal distribu- tion over each data-poor cell lines samples. iii) Instead of generating the distribution directly over labels of the query samples ( i.e. , by connecting the task embedding vectors and the features of query samples), we firstly obtained the prior distribution over task embedding of each cell line with the support samples, from which we sampled one initial task embedding vector, then fine-tuned the task embedding vector with the support samples to generate the weights for synergy prediction network.

Molecular graphs of drugs.  Drugs are characterized by drug molecular graphs. The Isomeric Simplified Molecular Input Line Entry System (SMELIS) string of each drug was obtained from the SDF file, and it is compiled into a molecular graph with the open-source library RDKit, in which the nodes and edges denote chemical atoms and bonds linking two atoms, respectively.  

Transcriptional profiles of cancer cell lines.  Cancer cell lines are characterized by transcriptional profiles without treat- ment by the compounds. According to the manually cali- brated names of 125 cancer cell lines, we obtained the raw expression microarray data of 82 cancer cell lines from Ar- rayExpress (E-MTAB-3610) [45], the RNA-seq gene expres- sion data ( i.e. , Reads Per Kilobase of exon model per Million mapped reads, RPKM) of 30 cancer cell lines from Cancer Cell Line Encyclopedia (CCLE) [46], and the RNA-seq gene expression data of 13 cancer cell lines from SYNERGxDB [43]. For microarray data, we performed the multi-array average normalization and log2-transformed of raw probe intensities, and then aggregated the probe intensities to the level of genes. For RNA-seq data, we converted the RPKM values of cancer call lines into their TPM ( i.e. , Transcripts Per Kilobase of exon model per Million mapped reads) values, and then performed log2-transformed. Finally, we got the expression values of 17,135 overlapping genes in 125 cell lines.

# 3.3.1 Prior-Guided Hypernetwork  

The prior-guided hypernetwork architecture consists of a meta-generative network and a cell line-specific drug synergy prediction network. The deep Bayesian variational inference model in meta-generative network aims to infer prior distribution  p ( z j )  over the task embedding  z j  for cell line  c j  with the feature vector  X s,j  of support samples  D s,j , and the weights generation network in meta-generative network aims to generate the specific weight and bias parameters  Θ j for the cell line-specific drug synergy prediction network under two conditions of the inferred prior distribution  p ( z j )  and support samples  D s,j . The parameters θ of deep Bayesian variational inference model, and the parameters  φ , α  of weights generation network in meta-generative network are jointly learned via maximizing the evidence lower bound  L ( θ ,  φ , α )  of the marginal likelihood  logp ( y q | x q , D s ) .

# 3.4 Three-Stage Training Strategy  

To train our HyperSynergy model, we presented a three- stage learning strategy to implement the few-shot drug synergy prediction (Algorithm 1 in APPENDIX A.2). The three-stage learning strategy consists of the representation learning stage, meta-learning stage and fine-tuning stage. The representation learning stage pretrains the feature embedding network at the sample-level with the base cell lines. The meta-learning stage trains the meta-generative network at the task-level with the base cell lines. The fine-tuning stage fine-tunes the parameters of drug synergy prediction network for each new cell line with few drug synergy data.  

Representation learning stage. # 4 EXPERIMENTS AND RESULTS  

In this section, we first describe the experimental settings and evaluation metrics, then present the experimental re- sults of using different numbers of samples ( e.g. , few sam- ples, zero sample, and more samples) in each cell line, as well as the results of ablation study. Moreover, we also explore the performance of HyperSynergy on different cell lines and the inference of randomly sampled drug pairs on HyperSynergy. Finally, we conduct the case studies to demonstrate the power of HyperSynergy in systematically screening new drug combinations for data-poor cell lines.  

# 4.1 Experimental Settings and Evaluation Metrics  

Experimental Settings.  We evaluated our HyperSynergy in the following three experimental settings: few-shot setting, zero-shot setting, and data-rich setting, in which the few- shot setting was the core. In the few-shot/zero-shot setting experiments, considering that there were only 106 cell lines with more than 30 drug synergy samples, we randomly split the 106 cell lines into meta-training cell lines (85 cell lines) and meta-test cell lines (21 cell lines) according to the ratio of 80% and 20% (Fig. S4). The drug synergy samples in meta- training cell lines were used for building the meta-training set, and the drug synergy samples in meta-test cell lines were used for building the meta-test set. As shown in Fig. S1e, there were 284 drugs in the meta-training set and 1894 drugs in the meta-test set. Meanwhile, 250 drugs consisted in both meta-training set and meta-test set (Fig. S1e).  

At representation learning stage, all drug synergy sam- ples in meta-training set were used to pretrain the fea- ture embedding module for 500 epochs. At meta-learning stage, we randomly sampled 40,000 episodes (every episode contains 50 50-shot tasks) from meta-training set to train HyperSynergy. After training with every 200 episodes, we randomly sampled 1500 50-shot tasks from meta-test set as validation tasks to validate the performance of HyperSyn- ergy. At meta-test stage, we randomly sampled 10000 K- shot tasks (K=5, 10, 30) from meta-test set to evaluate the performance of HyperSynergy.  

For the new task of few-shot drug synergy prediction, we improved some previous methods for data-rich cell lines ( e.g. , TFSynergy [31], MatchMarker [22], DeepSynergy [20]) to get their improved methods (named as TFSyn- ergy\*, MatchMarker\*, and DeepSynergy\*, respectively) to implement the task of few-shot drug synergy prediction, and then compared them with our HyperSynergy model. TFSynergy\* pretrained TFSynergy with drug synergy sam- ples from meta-training set, and then fine-tuned the latter layers of TFSynergy with K samples from each of the meta-test cell lines. DeepSynergy\*/MatchMarker\* trained DeepSynergy/MatchMarker by mixing the drug synergy samples from meta-training set and K samples from each of meta-test cell lines. We also compared our HyperSynergy method with other four traditional machine learning meth- ods, that was, K-nearest neighbor (KNN), support vector machine (SVM), extreme gradient boosting (XGBoost) and random forest (RF). For KNN, SVM, XGBoost and RF, we first extracted multi-omics features of drug pairs and cell lines referring to the work of Celebi et al. [32], and then inputed them into KNN, SVM, XGBoost and RF to predict drug synergy, respectively. The multi-omics features con- sisted of the Molecular Access System (MACCS) chemical fingerprints, the summed bit vectors of drug targets, the structural protein domain features of drug targets, the bit vectors of biological pathways involving the drug targets, three network proximity features for each drug pair in drug synergy network, gene expression features of cell lines, mutations and copy number variations features of cell lines. Besides, the principal component analysis method was used to reduce feature dimensions. The training of KNN, SVM, XGBoost and RF was same as MatchMarker\*. For fair comparison, all the methods used the same meta-training and meta-test data, repeating 20 times.  

Considering that it was difficult to select so many hyper- parameters of HyperSynergy with Grid Search method, we first adopted the trial-and-error method to select hyperpa- rameters, where the range space of hyperparameters and the final selected hyperparameters were listed in TABLE S5 and TABLE S6. Then, we used these selected hyperpa- rameters to implement all experiments. Besides, some key hyperparameters, such as the dimension of task embedding vector  z i , the weights of regularization terms  λ 1  and  λ 2 , the number of tasks per episode  T , the number of query samples  Q , initial inner learning rate  α 0 , learning rate  γ  and inner learning rate  β , will be completely study in section 4.4. All experiments run on Linux OS with 24G×4 NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPU, 40×2.4GHZ Intel Xeon CPU, and 128GB RAM. Our codes are all based on PyTorch 1.7 with CUDA 11.0 and PyTorch Geometric 1.7.  

Evaluation Metrics.  The Mean Squared Error (MSE), Spear- man Correlation Coefficient (SCC) and R-square (R 2 ) were used to evaluate the performance of HyperSynergy. The AUC value ( i.e. , the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve), AUPR value ( i.e. , the area under the precision-recall curve), F1-score and Kappa were also used to evaluate the power of HyperSynergy on identifying the synergistic drug combinations. The definitions and formulas of these metrics are provided in the APPENDIX A.3 of Supplementary Materials. In addition, to assess the power of regression models on identifying the synergistic drug combinations, we binarized the observed drug synergy scores ( y  ∈ R ) according to the selected threshold. If the tested drug synergy score of one drug pair is larger than the threshold, the drug pair is labeled as a synergistic drug pair ( y  = 1 ), otherwise the drug pair is labeled as a non- synergistic drug pair ( y  = 0 ). In this work, the top 10% percentile (-0.7569) of all synergy scores was selected as the cutoff to binarize the data, which was same as the work of Kristina  et al.  [20].

Now, most meta-learning methods use “equal task setting” strategy, that is, the number  K tr   of support samples in meta-training tasks is equal to the number  K te   of support samples in meta-test tasks. Considering that  K te   is not equal to  K tr in most few-shot drug synergy prediction tasks, here we adopt the “unequal task setting” strategy, that is, we first set the number  K te   of support samples in meta-test tasks, then use more labeled support samples in meta-training tasks ( i.e. ,  K te   ≤ K tr   ). In order to investigate the effect of “unequal task setting” strategy used in fine-tuning stage, we test the power of HyperSynergy (Fig. 4). From Fig. 4, we can see that at a fixed  K te , SCC of HyperSynergy raises with the increase of  K tr , indicating that our “unequal task setting” strategy is effective in the few-shot drug synergy prediction task. Especially, when  K te =5,10, setting  K tr =50, HyperSynergy achieves the best results. In addition, when K tr   =  K te , we do not require to retrain HyperSynergy.  

# 4.4 Parameter sensitivity analysis  

In order to completely study the potential impacts of these parameters on HyperSynergy, we took 5-shot setting as an example to implement HyperSynergy with some key parameters, such as the dimension of task embedding vector z i  in range of  { 32, 64, 96, 128, 160 } , the weights of regular- ization terms  λ 1  in range of  { 10 − 1 ,  10 − 2 ,  10 − 3 , 10 − 4 }  and  λ 2 in range of  { 10 − 3 ,  10 − 6 ,  10 − 9 , 10 − 12 } , the number of tasks per episode  T  in range of  { 32, 50, 64 } , the number of query samples  Q  in range of  { 30, 40, 50 } , the initial inner learning rate  α 0  in range of  { 1, 0.1, 0.01 } , the learning rate  γ  in range of  { 0.0005, 0.001, 0.005 } , the inner learning rate  β  in range of  { 0.001, 0.01, 0.1, 1 } . The results are shown in Fig. 5, Fig. S11 and Fig. S12.  

From Fig. 5, we can see that  R 2   increases sharply as the dimension of  z i  increases from 32 to 64, then increases gradually as the dimension of  z i  increases from 64 to 128, and it hardly changes as the dimension of  z i  increases from 128 to 160. As the dimension of  z i  increases from 32 to 96, SCC increases first and then decreases slowly, and it increases sharply as the dimension of  z i  increases from 96 to 128, then gradually decreases as the dimension of  z i increases from 128 to 160. By comprehensively considering the values of  R 2   and SCC, we set dimension of  z i  to 128 in this work. From Fig. S11, we can see that when  λ 1  =  10 − 3 , λ 2  =  10 − 9 ,  Q  = 40,  T  = 50, the values  R 2   and SCC reach the highest. Thus, we set  λ 1  = 10 − 3 ,  λ 2  = 10 − 9 ,  Q = 40 and  T = 50 in this work. In addition, as shown in Fig. S12, the values of R 2   and SCC reach the largest at the learning rate  γ  =0.001, initial inner learning rate  α 0  =0.1 and inner learning rate  β = 0.1. Hence, we set  α 0  = 0.1,  γ  =0.001 and  β  = 0.1.  

# 4.5 Performance of HyperSynergy on different cell lines  

In order to investigate the prediction performance of Hyper- Synergy on different cell lines, we first randomly sampled 1,000 test-tasks from each meta-test cell line to calculate the MSE of each task, and then evaluated the performance of HyperSynergy on different cell lines. # 5 C ONCLUSION  

In this work, we proposed a novel few-shot model of Hyper- Synergy to predict the drug synergy in data-poor cell lines by designing a prior guided hypernetwork architecture. The prior guided hypernetwork can generate the specific weights and bias terms for the drug synergy prediction net- work by using a deep Bayesian variational inference model to infer the prior distribution over the task embedding of each cell line, and sampling the task embedding vectors from the prior distribution as the inputs of the weight gener- ation network. The task embedding vectors can be quickly updated by using a few labeled drug synergy samples. In addition, we also presented a three-stage learning strategy to train HyperSynergy for quickly updating the prior dis- tribution with a few labeled drug synergy samples for each task. Especially, according to the framework of hierarchical Bayesian variational inference, we theoretically proved that the prior guided hypernetwork architecture aims to max- imize the lower bound of log-likelihood of the marginal distribution over each data-poor cell line data. Experimental results demonstrate that HyperSynergy is superior to other state-of-the-art methods not only in predicting the drug synergy on data-poor cell lines, but also on data-rich cell lines. Using a deep Bayesian variational inference model in the meta-learning stag to infer the prior distribution over the task embedding of each cell line can provide a good initial point for parameters optimization, and effectively reduce the free space of parameters optimization. The rep- resentation learning stage in three-stage learning scheme not only speed up the convergence, but also stabilize the optimization process of HyperSynergy. Fine-tuning the task embedding in the fine-tuning stage is more effective than fine-tuning the specific weights with a few labeled support samples. In addition, our “unequal task setting” strategy is effective in the few-shot drug synergy prediction task. The case studies show that HyperSynergy can recommend new synergistic drug combinations for different data-poor cell lines.  

In future work, we should explore the feature em- bedding model with stronger representation ability, and more effective strategy of pretraining the feature embedding model to further improve the performance of our Hyper- Synergy. Besides, due to that the performance of Hyper- Synergy is not better in the cell lines with large variation of drug synergy scores, thus we should consider designing a variational inference model with stronger adaptability to infer the prior distribution over task embedding of the cell lines, which can be as a promising direction for future work. And finally, although some methods have better performance on balanced dataset, it is more important to investigate the methods with fine predictive performance on on the integrated dataset, due to the relatively small size of current balanced datasets and in order to making full use of the available high-throughput drug combination screening data.
