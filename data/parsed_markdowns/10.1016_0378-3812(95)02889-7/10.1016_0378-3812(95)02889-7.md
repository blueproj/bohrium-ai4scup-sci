ABSTRACT  

A simple and accurate apparatus for the measurement of salt solubilities in mixed solvents by an analytical method is presented.Salt solubility datahavebeen measuredfor the systems water/methanol/NaCl water/methanol/KCl at 298.15 and 323.15 K and for the system water/ethanol/NaCl at 298.15, 323.15 and 348.15K.  

To correlate the solubility data two models are proposed.One assumes that the salt in solution is in the molecular form and the original UNIQUAC model with linear temperature dependent parameters is used, the other takes into consideration the salt dissociation and the original UNIQUAC model is combined with a Pitzer-Debye-Huickel expression to take into account the long-range interaction forces. In order to simplify the solubility calculations a new formulation is presented involving the symmetric convention for all species. This makes possible the direct access to the solubility product of the salt in terms of its thermodynamic properties. Both models correlate satisfactorily the solubility data although both temperature and electrostatic effects are very important in this typeof equilibrium calculations.  

# 1. INTRODUCTION  

The study of phase equilibria in electrolyte systems and more specifically the determination of salt solubilities in mixed solvents is extremely important, either from a scientific or an industrial point of view. Besides the many relevant indications that can be given concerning the liquid phase structure and its thermodynamic properties, it is also very useful as a support for the design and simulation of unity operations such as crystallization,extractive or azeotropic distillation and liquid-liquid extraction(Horvath,1985; Galindez andFredenslund,1987;Furter,1992).  

However, a detailed literature search based on the open literature and the compilations by Linke and Seidell (1958,1965) and Stephen and Stephen (1963 a,b),makes it possible to find out an enormous lack of experimental data at temperatures different from 25 oC.Moreover, even at this temperature, the deviations between different published results for the so lub ili ties of the same salt in the same solvent may attain 2 o%and for some systems the exhibition of im misc i bil it y is not registered.  

Concerning modeling work, only a few works have been carried out since solid-liquid equilibrium (SLE) studies in electrolyte systems were, until nowadays, almost totally dedicated to uni or multi-salt aqueous systems (Chen, 1986; Sander et al., 1986a; Nicolaisen et al., 1993). Very little attention has been given to the thermodynamic modeling of this type of equilibria in mixed solvent systems. The great lack of available and accurate data, the complex interaction and the physical and chemical phenomena such as solvation and association,very important in these systems,are the major restrictive factors for the establishment of a model for S LE calculations.  

Important demonstrations of the increasing interest in this area have been put into evidence. At the experimental level the works by Greve and Kula (1991),Rajendran et al.(1991), Chiavone-Filho and Rasmussen(1993)andGomisetal.(1993,1994)enhance the importance of this type of data as well as the evidentneedof concentrating efforts in the experimental work in order to establish areliable and consistent database.  

Concerning the theoretical developments the works by Lorimer (1993) and Chiavone-Filho (1993) should be focused. Lorimer (1993) developed a method based on the unsymmetric convention for the normalization of the activity coefficients,which introduces enormous difficulties in the solubility calculations due to the Gibbs energy of transfer that must be known. Also during 1993, Chiavone-Filho combined the UNIQUAC equation with temperature dependent water/ion interaction parameters, with a Pitzer-Debye-Huickel (Pitzer, 1980) expression to take into account, respectively, the short and long-range interaction forces. However, a systematic study, which is known to be fundamental nowadays, has not been tried in neither of the above- mentionedworks.  

In this work the correlation capabilities of two models are studied based on literature and measured solubility data. The solubilities can be calculated with an average absolute deviation around 4%, and it is possible to conclude that the temperature and the electrostatic effects should, if possible,be considered together

The jacketed glass cell of about 100 cm? is charged with dried salt in a small excess over the estimated solubility and with the solvent mixture prepared by weighting in a 0.1mg precision balance the desired amount of each solvent. Then, the cell is conveniently closed to avoid evaporation and the consequent change of solvent composition.The temperature in the cell is controlled by circulating thermostated water in the jacket and it isconsidered to be accurate within± O.1K.  

In order to avoid the formation of micro crystals, normally the temperature is first set slightly above the equilibrium value. A cooling cycle was used for the temperatures 323.15 and 298.15 K, i. e., the temperature was first set at 325.15 K and then the solubilities were measured at 323.15 and 298.15 K. It should be noticed that when doing a cooling cycle after the first sampling (at 323.15 K) fresh solvent mixture already prepared was added to the cell in order to decrease the amount of the salt in the solid phase as well as the amount of vapor over the liquid phase. The solubilities at 348.15 K were measured separately since it turned out that the composition of the solvent would change significantly because of the high temperature values.  

Foreachdeterminationthemixtureisstirredfor about2hoursandthen allowedtosettlefor1/2hour before sampling with a thermostated syringe, usually 5K above the working temperature, to avoid undesired precipitation.  

The solubility is determined by slow evaporation of the solvent mixture from a previous weighted sampie of saturated solution (3-5 cm3) and by weighting the precipitated salt regularly until a constant value is achieved. Each experimental point is an average of three different measurements with a reproducibility better than 0.1 wt %. If the experimental solubility is less than 10 wt% this criterion will be difficult to attain and in this case an equivalent criterion is that the standard deviation between the different results should be lower than 0.005.
