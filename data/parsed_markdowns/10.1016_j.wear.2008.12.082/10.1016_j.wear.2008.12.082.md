Multi-component metallic alloys have been developed to withstand high-temperature service in corro- sive environments. Some of these applications, like exhaust valve seats in internal combustion engines, must also resist sliding, impact, and abrasion. The conjoint effects of temperature, oxidation, and mechan- ical contact can result in accelerated wear and the formation of complex surface layers whose properties differ from those of the base metal and the oxide scale that forms in the absence of mechanical contact. The authors have investigated the effects of prior surface damage, produced by scratch tests, on the local- ized reformation of oxide layers. Three high-performance commercial alloys, based on iron, nickel, and cobalt, were used as model materials. Thermo grav i metric analysis (TGA) was used to determine their static oxidation rates at elevated temperature (850   ◦ C). A micro-abrasion, ball-cratering technique was used to measure oxide layer thickness and to supplement the TGA results. By using taper-sectioning techniques and energy-dispersive elemental mapping, a comparison was made between oxide composi- tions grown on non-damaged surfaces and oxides that formed on grooves produced by a diamond stylus. Micro indentation and scratch hardness data revealed the effects of high-temperature exposure on both the substrate hardness and the nature of oxide scale disruption. There were signiﬁcant differences in elemental distribution between statically formed oxides and those that formed on scratched regions.

These include the fuel system, the engine block with its pistons and cylinders, the valve train, the transmission, and the exhaust system. The current effort investigates certain tribological characteristics of metallic alloys such as those used in valves and valve seats. Stott, Wood, and their colleagues have investigated the inter- action between sliding wear and high-temperature oxidation in superalloys  [4–6] . They described the role of so-called ‘glazes’ that form on sliding surfaces during frictional contact  [7] . Under certain circumstances, these glazes can protect the surfaces from contact damage, at least until they wear off. In some cases, they can be reformed during continuing operation of the machinery, but as substrate elements (e.g. Cr) oxidize due to the environment, the reaction products that reform on the worn surface can be differ- ent from those that formed from the initial, non-depleted surface species. Hence it is important to understand how mechanical dam- age can inﬂuence the subsequent redistribution of elements at or near the surfaces of high-performance, Co-, Ni-, and Fe-based alloys as they oxidize. The current investigation used single-point, ex situ scratch tests to produce controlled abrasion damage on previously oxidized metal alloys and used surface analysis tools to characterize the effects of further high-temperature exposure on the damaged areas. It extends prior work on the Ni-based alloy that has been reported elsewhere  [8] .

# 2. Materials and experimental details  

In this work, it was of interest to characterize and compare the combined effects of single point abrasion with elevated tem- perature oxidation of representative Fe-, Ni-, and Co-based alloys. Three commercial alloys were selected for this work. Their compo- sitions and Brinell hardness numbers (HB) are presented in  Table 1 . Compositions of the three alloys were determined by a plasma- combustion-based spectroscopy method at a commercial analysis laboratory. Two of the three alloys contained the relatively high levels of Cr that typify high-performance alloys for elevated temper- ature use. Stellite TM   6B is a Co-based, superalloy used for corrosion- and wear-resistance. Pyromet TM   80A (similar to Nimonic TM   80A) is a Ni-based, oxidation-resistant alloy with creep-resisting proper- ties. Custom 465 TM   is a martensitic, Fe-based, age-hardenable alloy that was designed for corrosion resistance at <700   ◦ C.  

Specimens of each alloy were ground and then polished to a 6   m diamond paste ﬁnish to provide a smooth surface. They were then oxidized in air at 850   ◦ C for 2 h and cooled to room tem- perature. Next, a series of parallel scratches was made using a hemispherically tipped diamond stylus (tip radius 0.2 mm), at a nor- mal force of 9.81 N, with a speed of 0.2 mm/s (Revetest TM   scratch tester, CSM Instruments, Switzerland). The scratched specimens were then replaced in the furnace at 850   ◦ C for 4 additional hours, cooled and scratched again at a location adjacent the original set of scratches.  

It was also of interest to characterize the oxidation behavior of non-damaged surfaces. A 25 h thermo grav i metric analysis (TGA) was conducted at the same temperature as was used for the scratch testing experiment (850   ◦ C) but in a dry oxygen environment rather

For the Co- and Ni-based alloys, this measurement was relatively straightforward, but for the Fe alloy the porous, non-uniform scale structure did not clearly reveal the oxide thickness using the ball- cratering method. Thickness of the scale on the Fe-based alloy was therefore estimated from polished cross-sections of the TGA spec- imens.  

Metallographic taper sectioning, a long-established technique in materials tribology (e.g.,  [9–12] ), was used to facilitate exami- nation of the surface regions of scratched specimens in order to study the scales and subsurface damage adjacent to the scratches. The scratched surfaces were afﬁxed to slanted wedges that were embedded into epoxy mounts. Scratches were oriented ‘down hill’, in the direction of tilt. Grinding and polishing of the specimens was done parallel to the base of the mount until the plane of polish intersected the inclined scratch grooves, magnifying their features. The apparent magniﬁcation of these features in the direction of incline is determined from the cosecant of the tilt angle relative to the original surface. In the present case, a 5.75 ◦ taper angle pro- duced an apparent 10 ×  enlargement of features in that direction. To indicate this effect, both photo micrographs and composition maps were given orthogonal scale bars differing in magniﬁcation by a factor of 10. Fortuitous shrinkage of the epoxy mounting materials away from the oxidized surfaces of the specimens during curing exposed a portion of the original free surface that intersected the plane of taper polish. Therefore, as will be described in the Results section, both surface and subsurface features could be observed on the same image.  

Traditional Vickers and Knoop micro indentation methods were used. The Vickers method was used on surfaces before and after exposure, and Knoop method was used to probe various locations on polished taper sections. Scratch hardness numbers (HS) were obtained from scratch width measurements  [13] .  

# 3. Results and discussion  

# 3.1. Simple oxidation results  

Fig. 1  shows the TGA mass gain data for the three alloys at 850   ◦ C in dry O 2 . The mass gain data was plotted versus the square root of time to illustrate the parabolic reaction kinetics  [14]  and the parabolic rate constants are shown in  Fig. 1 . The Ni- and Co-based alloys showed a low mass gain due to the formation of a protec- tive Cr-rich oxide scale, nominally Cr 2 O 3 . The Fe-based alloy had a much higher oxide growth rate, particularly after the ﬁrst hour of exposure. Due to the lower Cr content in this alloy ( Table 1 ), this behavior was not unexpected  [15] . There was not enough Cr in the alloy to sustain the formation of a Cr-rich oxide after 1 h and a much faster-growing Fe-rich oxide formed. Martensitic Fe–Cr alloys tend to form a double-layer oxide with a brittle FeO x  top layer and a (Fe, Cr)O x  inner layer. The thick outer layer tends to spall after cool- ing to room temperature due to the thermal expansion difference between the oxide and substrate. (The spallation is not reﬂected in  Fig. 1  because it occurs after the specimen is removed from the furnace.)  Fig. 2  shows polished cross-sections of the three speci-

# 3.2. Composition mapping  

Energy-dispersive X-ray composition maps of polished taper sections of scratches were obtained using a Hitachi 3400 environ- mental scanning electron microscope. EDX mapping was used to study the redistribution of individual alloying elements and oxygen both on and remote from the scratch grooves. Taper polished EDX elemental maps were obtained for fresh scratches upon surfaces that were exposed for 6 h, and for surfaces that were oxidized for 2 h, scratched, and then re-oxidized for an additional 4 h. Maps were made for O, Fe, Cr, Ni, Al, Si, and Ti, but only those that displayed noteworthy or illustrative features are present here. In  Figs. 3–5 , the ﬁeld of view is 223   m tall  ×  318   m wide and the scale bars shown in  Fig. 3  (accounting for the taper) apply to all composition maps.  

# 3.2.1. Oxygen distribution  

Fig. 3 (a)–(c) indicates the oxygen distribution around scratches on alloy surfaces that were oxidized for a total of 6 h before scratch- ing. Owing to the taper, the features are elongated by a factor of ten in the vertical direction relative to the horizontal direction. In Fig. 3 (a), the brittle oxide on the Fe-based alloy seems to have been scratched nearly completely off, but it seems to have been mixed into the surface in the Ni-alloy and Co-alloy. There are indications of enriched O in the blocky phases in the Stellite 6B (see  Fig. 3 (c)), both on the oxidized surface and somewhat less so in the subsurface carbides.  

Fig. 3 (d)–(f) shows O maps of scratches made at 2 h and then reoxidized for 4 h. From the increased signal intensity on the scratch grooves, it is apparent that the O concentration is greater there than on the statically oxidized surfaces to either side of the scratch, especially in the case of the Fe-based alloy ( Fig. 3 (d)). One might conclude that mechanically induced defects on the scratch bottoms in each case accelerated the oxidation locally. Qualitatively, the Co- based alloy seemed least enriched in O in the scratch groove relative to the non-scratched areas. The Ni-based alloy showed a slight indi- cation of enhanced O content on the alloy grain boundaries on both sides of the scratch (the oxide grain size is much smaller, likely <1   m). That indicates a preference for internal oxidation on the alloy grain boundaries in the Ni-based alloy (see  Fig. 2 (a)). The main oxide-forming element in the alloy is Cr, however, Al, Ti and Si all form more stable oxides that can oxidize beneath the Cr-rich external oxide.

# 3.2.3. Nickel distribution  

Fig. 4 (a) displays the Ni distribution around re-oxidized scratches on the Pyromet 80A alloy. There is less Ni in the groove than in the oxide or bulk areas because of the selective oxidation of Cr. The Custom 465 had a similar appearance, and the Stellite 6B had a relatively low Ni content. Ni maps for these two alloys are not shown. The Fe-based alloy had a bulk Ni content of only about 11 wt% and the scale was barely enriched relative to the under- lying material; however, Pyromet 80A has over 74 wt% Ni, so the bulk alloy shows up brightly. The areas of internal oxidation in the Pyromet 80A substrate appear darker and Ni-poor. This is not unex- pected as the internal oxidation should be predominantly oxides of Ti and/or Al, not NiO.  

# 3.2.4. Light element distribution (Al, Ti, Si)  

The Al and Ti maps near the re-oxidized scratch on the Pyromet 80A specimen are shown in  Fig. 4 (b) and (c), respectively. Both ele- ments form oxides more stable than Cr 2 O 3  and thus can oxidize internally. (When Al is present at higher levels, a continuous Al 2 O 3 oxide can form  [16] ). With the 1.4 wt% Al, the Al map indicates that Al was present in the oxide layer and also concentrated on the alloy grain boundaries beneath the oxide. Based on the O map in  Fig. 3 (e), these are Al 2 O 3  grain boundary phases. The brighter intensity along the walls of the groove may be remnants of the ear- lier oxide scale that was scratched away, and the darker upper layer indicates that the oxide that reformed on the scratch groove was Al poor.  

For the 2.5 wt%Ti in Pyromet 80A, a different distribution was observed ( Fig. 4 (c)). The Ti level in the oxide was lower than Al except for a network of Ti-rich oxide, that appears to be related to the alloy grain structure, suggesting enhanced Ti transport into the oxide above alloy grain boundaries. Ti was uniformly concentrated in the oxide reformed on the scratch and appears to be depleted in the substrate adjacent to the oxide. There are also a few small inclusions with high Ti content visible.  

Like Al, Si also forms a very stable oxide.  Fig. 5  shows the Si map from the Stellite 6B specimen. A SiO 2  subscale has formed at the interface between the external oxide layer and underlying metal, typical of Si additions in chromia-forming alloys  [17] . The Si also lies along the groove wall as if it were simply pressed down during scratching but not abraded away.

Backscattered electron (BSE) images, besides providing atomic number contrast, provided detailed information on the differences in deformation between the three alloys after scratching and re- oxidation.  Fig. 6 (a)–(c) compares BSE images of the groove bottoms. The oxide deposit on the Fe-based alloy looks relatively ductile and smeared. The darker shade implies a lower atomic number, con- sistent with the high O content displayed in  Fig. 3 (d)–(f). Small, isolated islands of oxide decorate the layers a few micrometers below the groove walls and bottom.

The same scratches that were used to study re-oxidation effects could also be used to obtain the scratch hardness numbers (HS) for the oxidized surfaces. These data are not expected to mimic the quasi-static HV and HK data because they reﬂect the resistance to penetration of the scale-covered surfaces by a moving, rounded diamond stylus. HS for of the oxidized surfaces of the alloys after either 2 or 6 h of exposure, and under a load of 1000 g–f (9.81 N), are given in  Table 5 . Unlike the changes in HV of the bulk alloy ( Table 3 ), HS for the oxidized surfaces decreased only slightly for all three alloys from 2 to 6 h of high-temperature exposure. This indi- cates that, unlike the bulk materials which can age after heating, the abrasion resistance of oxidized surfaces changed less dramatically, since it was controlled by small changes in the composition of the oxidation products. The Custom 465 alloy with its highest oxida- tion rate ( Fig. 1 ), suffered the greatest decline in scratch resistance between 2 and 6 h (12.7%), about twice that observed for the Stellite 6B (5.7%).  

Overall, these results indicate striking differences in the ele- mental redistribution and microstructures between Fe-, Ni-, and Co-based alloys exposed to high temperatures after single-point abrasion. These changes can be correlated with their microin- dentation and scratch hardness responses, and they reﬂect the embrittlement or hardening of the near-surface layers, depending on the type, morphology, and degree of penetration into the sub- strate of the oxidation products. The Fe-based alloy was admittedly exposed to elevated temperatures well above those recommended for its use by the supplier, and consequently had the thickest oxide and lowest scratch hardness. Of the two superalloys, the Stellite 6B was harder and retained its high scratch resistance even after prolonged temperature exposure.  

# 4. Conclusions  

Studies of the effects of single-point abrasion on the re-oxidation of Fe-, Ni-, and Co-based alloy surfaces were conducted in air at 850   ◦ C. Results from X-ray mapping and hardness testing on taper-polished cross-sections of the scratched surfaces indicated signiﬁcant differences in the compositions and hardness of re- oxidation products. There was an obvious redistribution of alloying elements into the scales and grain boundaries, both within and dis- tant from the scratch grooves. At the bottom of the grooves, oxide penetration into the regions damaged by scratching changed the hardness locally. There were no obvious gradients in microinden- tation hardness below the scratch groove bottoms. This could be because subsequent heating annealed the material, or because the cutting action of scratching removed material more cleanly than the shearing action of sliding wear.  

The Fe-based alloy (Custom 465) had the highest static oxidation rate and formed a thick, porous scale. While this alloy age-hardened upon high-temperature exposure, it was also the softest of the three alloys in terms of scratch resistance. Stellite 6B and Pyromet 80A had similar and much lower oxidation rates than the Custom 465 steel, but the scale on the Stellite 6B had a higher resistance to penetration at the longer exposure times and that is consistent with its use as a valve seat insert material, as well as other high-temperature wear applications.
