# Microstructure Evolution Characteristics of Inconel 625 Alloy   from Selective Laser Melting to Heat Treatment  

C. Li a , R. White b , X.Y. Fang c\* , M. Weaver b , Y.B. Guo a   

a  Dept. of Mechanical Engineering, The University of Alabama, Tuscaloosa, AL 35487 USA  b  Dept. of Metallurgical and Materials Eng., The University of Alabama, Tuscaloosa, AL 35487 USA  c  Institute for Advanced Manufacturing, Shandong University of Technology, Zibo 255049, China  

# Abstract  

Superalloy Inconel 625 has been widely used in selective laser melting (SLM). Since SLM- induced microstructure with columnar grains, strong texture, porosity, and undesired properties,  heat treatment is often used to tune the microstructure and mechanical properties. However, the  microstructure evolution of IN 625 from SLM to heat treatment is poorly understood. In this  study, IN 625 samples were SLMed and then heat treated at elevated temperatures.  Microstructure evolution characteristics of the processed IN 625 alloy have been characterized  using optical metallography, scanning electron microscopy (SEM), electron backscatter  diffraction (EBSD), transmission electron microscopy (TEM), X-ray diffraction, and micro  indentation. Fine dendrite microstructures with strong texture parallel to layer build-up direction  was observed in the as-SLM samples due to rapid cooling and epitaxial growth. High dislocation  density and high microhardness were found in the    matrix which also contains high Z-contrast  precipitates. After annealing at high temperatures, random grain growth accompanied by  dislocation annihilation and twinning occurs. The decrease in lattice parameter and the  prevalence of large grain boundary misorientation in the    matrix suggests that SLM-induced  residual stress be significantly reduced.   In addition, the uncertainty of mechanical properties due  to the process variations was quantified.  

Keywords : Selective laser melting; heat treatment; microstructure; Inconel superalloy; additive  manufacturing  

# 1.  Introduction  

Nickel-based superalloys have a broad range of applications in aerospace and energy  industries because of their excellent fatigue and corrosion resistance at elevated temperatures [1].  Inconel 625, a solution strengthened nickel-based superalloy, has been widely used to make  engine components, heat exchangers, pressure valves, and etc. In the past decade, laser based  additive manufacturing such as selective laser melting (SLM) [2] has been explored to  manufacture functional metal parts (e.g., gas turbine blades, injection molds, heat exchangers)  with near full density and complex internal structures directly from a CAD model [3,4].  

Unlike any conventional annealing, metal powders during a SLM process experience a  unique thermal cycle ranging from rapid melting with steep temperature gradients, rapid  solidification with high cooling rates, to potential remelting (location dependent) [5,6]. The  combination of rapid melting and solidification in SLM allows the quenching-in of  microstructures that are far from thermodynamic equilibrium [7], which offers the opportunity to  manufacture alloys that are not possible using conventional materials processing techniques such  as casting. The primary variables that determine any alloy’s microstructure and defects are the  thermal gradient, solidification velocity, and cooling rate.  

Nickel-based supperalloys like Inconel 718 and Inconel 625 processed by electron beam  melting (EBM) and SLM have shown columnar microstructures and strong texture in build  direction [8,9]. Similar to the grain growth behavior in most SLMed alloys, SLMed Inconel 625  forms a columnar microstructure with elongated grains in the build direction. The SLMed  Inconel 625 alloy in the as-build state has a typical γ-FCC matrix with strong texture in <100>  direction [10,11]. Intermetallic precipitate γ'' (Ni 3 Nb) locating along the melt pool boundaries  was also observed by Amato  et al.  [8]. The special microstructures induced in SLM are usually  associated with the distinct physical properties such as high tensile residual stress, high hardness,  low toughness, and anisotropic properties. In addition, elemental segregations inside an  individual grain and across the part are expected.  

Dinda  et al.  [12] studied the effects of heat treatment on the microstructure of Inconel 625  fabricated by directed energy deposition. A coarser microstructure than that by SLM was  observed due to the lower cooling rate of the melt pool. However, mechanical properties and  texture analysis were not addressed. Li  et al.  [11] studied the microstructure and hardness change  after heat treatment of the SLMed Inconel 625 alloy and they found that the hardness decreases  as the increasing heat treatment temperature.  

Nickel-based superalloys in the as-SLM condition have demonstrated comparable or  improved mechanical properties in comparison to the conventionally manufactured materials  (casting or forging) in terms of hardness, tensile strength, and ultimate strength, but usually low  ductility [9,13]. The mechanical properties of as-built Inconel 625 alloy typically have high  dependency on the building orientation. A post build heat treatment is necessary to improve the  microstructural homogeneity and mechanical properties, and to increase the ductility at the  expense of lowering tensile strength [10]. SLMed Inconel 718 alloy exhibits less resistance to the  crack growth in the near-threshold region than the conventionally manufactured material [14].   Witkin [15] reported that the poor surface finish of the as-SLMed Inconel 625 part resulted in  very limited fatigue life. The fatigue performance of Inconel 625 was significantly improved by  post machining and hot isostatic pressing (HIP), which is comparable to a wrought one in a fully  reversed tension-compression fatigue testing. Kuo  et al.  [16] studied the creep behavior of  Inconel 718 alloy processed by SLM. Direct aging of the as-SLM Inconel 718 alloy shows an  improved creep behavior than the SLMed sample. Compared to the wrought material, the  SLMed and aged Inconel 718 alloy has much lower creep rupture live due to the concentration of  brittle δ precipitates in the matrix and the existence of high dislocation density which is blocked  by the subgrain boundary. Pröbstle  et al.  [17] claimed that solution heat treatment of the SLMed  Inconel 718 alloy produced superior creep strength to the wrought material due to the high  fraction of γ''/γ' strengthening phases and the nearly completely dissolution of brittle phases (δ  and Laves).  

In summary, the bulk literature has been limited to SLM or directed energy deposition of  Inconel 718. Furthermore, little study has been done to focus on an in-depth crystallography  analysis instead of a single aspect of microstructure in SLM of Inconel 625 alloy. Hence, a  comprehensive understanding of the microstructure evolution from SLM to heat treatment  Inconel 625 alloy is highly needed. Therefore, the twofold research objectives of this study are  to: (1) comprehensively investigate the microstructures in SLM and heat treated Inconel 625  through various material characterization methods; and (2) characterize the microstructural  evolution including grain boundary, dislocation density, and mechanical property uncertainty.

# 2.3 Experiment conditions  

In this study, a laser power of 80 W, scanning speed of 1500 mm/min, layer thickness of 50  µm, and scan spacing of 100 µm were used to fabricate the samples in Fig. 3a. The Inconel 625  material was deposited on a circular steel disk of 6 mm thickness. Eight samples were produced,  each with dimensions of 9 mm × 10 mm × 3 mm (X × Y × Z) (Table 2). Although the sample  size in this study is smaller than a practical part with size up to hundreds of millimeters, a series  of 10 millimeters blocks could be large enough to obtain stale microstructure for a fundamental  investigation of the microstructural evolution of the SLMed samples under different heat  treatment conditions. Two identical samples were assigned to each heat treatment (HT)  conditions. The laser scanning sequences were selected from sample 1 to sample 4 as shown in  Fig. 3b, which is typically favorable for dissipating heat evenly within the substrate. Three  directions were defined as shown in Fig. 3c, in which X, Y, Z  represent the step-over, scanning,  and building directions, respectively. The same scanning strategy was applied to each layer as  shown in Fig. 3d.

# 2.4 Characterization methods  

A length-scale based characterization scheme is shown in Fig. 5 to comprehensively  characterize the microstructure of the SLMed and heat treated samples. Samples #1-#4 were  sectioned, mounted, ground, and mechanical polished following standard metallographic  procedures to a surface finish  of  Ra = 0.05 µm in the XZ and YZ planes, respectively. The  samples for microstructure characterization were electro-etched using the 70 ml phosphoric acid  and 30 ml water under a voltage of 5 V for 5\~40 s. Microstructure was examined on an optical  microscope (VHX series, KEYENCE) and a field emission scanning electron microscope (SEM:  JEOL 7000F). Electron backscatter diffraction (EBSD) analysis was also conducted on the SEM  to characterize grain orientations and misorientation distributions. X-ray diffraction analysis was  performed on the XY planes of the samples to estimate the lattice parameter change and  precipitation behavior using a Philips MPD XRD machine. TEM thin foil specimens were  prepared using a variation of the focused ion beam (FIB) lift out method in an FEI Quanta 200  3D Dualbeam FIB microscope [19]. The specimens were cut out across the melt pool boundary  in the XZ plane and imaged via scanning transmission electron microscopy-high angle annular  darkfield (STEM-HAADF) to observe dislocations and precipitates on an FEI Tecnai G2 F-20  Supertwin TEM. In addition, sample microhardness was measured using a Vickers indenter at  load of 200 g for 15 s.  

Similar to processing samples #1-#4, the samples for measuring surface roughness were  sectioned, mounted, and finishing polished with a 1   µm diamond suspension, and then electro- etched to reveal the microstructure of the deposited material.

# 3.  Results and Discussions  

# 3.1 Surface topography and defects of the as-SLM IN 625 alloy  

Fig. 6a shows surface topography of the as-SLMed Inconel 625 samples. Open pores and  balling spheres, which are responsible for poor surface finish of the SLMed samples, were  observed on the surface. Microcracks also exist on the top surface in Fig. 6b.  When the powder  material is melted by the moving laser, the molten material begins to rapidly cool down and  solidify after the laser moves away. The material shrinks because of thermal contraction and this  shrinkage is further restricted by the previously deposited material due to the temperature  gradient in the build direction [20,21].  

Figs. 6c and 6d show some typical cross-sectional defects identified in the as-SLMed  Inconel 625 samples. A back scattered SEM image of the XZ cross-sections illustrates the lack of  fusion porosity and micro-pores between the interfaces of two adjacent layers as shown in  Fig. 6c. The micro-pores could be caused by trapped gas bubbles.  Fig. 6d is a secondary electron  SEM image titled by 70° showing two typical types of porosity. The first type of pore is  produced by the balling effect, and the other one is produced by the lack of fusion. The balling  effect is considered as a typical drawback of SLM processes. A balling sphere with a diameter of  50 µm trapped in a pore was clearly shown on the top-left corner in Fig. 6d. The formation of  balling is mainly caused by high viscosity and surface tension of the molten material which tends  to minimization of its surface area by forming liquid spheres. Those solidified spheres will result  in poor surface finish. The balling effect also partially contributes to the porosity defect of an  end-use part [22]. A cluster of unmelted powders were trapped in the cavity with a size of  proximately 150 µm in length and 50 µm in width. The existence of unmelt powders could be  caused by the unevenly distributed layer thickness. The unmelted powder cluster was trapped by  the liquid metal in the melt pool and significantly decreased the wettability of the melt pool to  the previously layer. A micro crack with a length of proximately 50 µm was also observed on the  interior wall of the cavity as seen in Fig. 6d. The microcrack could be induced by the high tensile  residual stress generated due to the rapid cooling during SLM. This interior crack would be  harmful for the fatigue performance of the end-use part.  

Rough surface finish is one of the critical issues in SLM parts, in particular for the parts with  over-hangs and curved surfaces. Surface roughness is significantly affected by process  parameters like hatch spacing, laser power, scanning speed, scanning strategy, and part geometry  [23-25]. Fatigue initiation sites were generally found in the near surface of an as-SLM metal part  under the cyclic loading conditions due to the open pores on the surface [26,27]. Post-processes  like machining [28] and laser re-melting [29] for the as-SLM surface were usually applied to  decrease surface roughness.  

![](images/0d41dc2d4229a99848db43b2612291b8d3080a16f927a0745835e05d366b4f87.jpg)  
Fig. 6  Surface topography of as-SLM samples (a & b); Defects in the subsurface (c & d).  

In this study, the overlap ratio of two neighboring melt pools is defined as the ratio of the  hatch spacing to the melt pool width. Six levels of overlap ratios from 86% to 300% were used in  Fig. 7. When the overlap ratio is over 120%, the low-power cases (60 W) tend to generate  slightly rougher surface than the high-power cases (80 W). Fig. 8 gives a comparison of surface  morphology (one-layer deposition) generated by the two levels of laser powers. It can be seen  that an approximately 100 μm overlap for two neighboring melting tracks is consistent with the  experiment pre-set of the hatch spacing (100 μm). In the case of low laser power (Fig. 8a) more  balling spheres were generated compared to the high laser power case (see Fig. 8b), which  indicates that low heat input is susceptible to generate balling effect which leads to a rough  surface [22]. With the decreasing of overlap ratio (when it is lower than 200 %) for a fixed laser  power, the hatch spacing increases and the surface roughness tends to decrease. Fig. 7c gives a  schematic to show the effect of hatch spacing on surface roughness of a face-up surface. The  dimensions of each melt pool are assumed to stay the same with same laser power and scan  speed, although slight differences on the width and depth of the melt pools can be seen in Fig. 7b.  The red-dot lines in Fig. 7c represent the surface profile of the SLMed samples. With the  decreasing hatch spacing, the surface profile tends to become more flat and show less fluctuation  and, thus, leads to a smooth surface.

# 3.2 Microstructure evolution during heat treatment  

# 3.2.1 SEM and optical microscope analysis  

Fig. 9 shows the XZ cross-sections of the melt pools in the as-SLM condition. The typical  “fish scale” morphology of the melt pool is clearly observed in Fig. 9a due to the Gaussian  distribution of the laser. The dimensions of each melt pool were proximately 75-100 µm in width  and 25-40 µm in depth. As the powder layer thickness of 50 µm was used, it could be clearly  seen that every melt pool (previous layer) was remelted due to the melting of the subsequently  added powders. Fig. 9b shows the melt pools at high magnification. A typical columnar dendritic  microstructure was observed in the melt pool. As indicated by the arrows, the dendritic structure  grew perpendicular to the direction of the melt pool boundary, which is also the direction of the  highest temperature gradient. The primary cell spacing of the dendritic structure was  approximately 1 µm. According to a previous study [18], this cell spacing is sensitive to the heat  input and tends to increase with the increase of laser power. Dinda found the primary arm  spacing for Inconel 625 fabricated by directed energy deposition to be tens of microns [12], an  order or magnitude larger than the present study. The reason is that a much smaller laser beam  diameter and thinner layer thickness are usually used in the SLM process, which generates a  much smaller melt pool [5] during SLM than the directed energy deposition process.  Furthermore, the small volume of the melt pool in SLM leads to a much higher cooling rate than  that in the directed energy deposition process and to a much finer microstructure [30].  

![](images/e2f4118280890760c9da2492b2f07b1a861fbd231f2283494d93f4b886db1cbd.jpg)  
Fig. 9  SEM SE microstructure of the melt pools of as-SLM IN 625.  

Microstructure evolution of Inconel 625 from as-SLM to heat treatment is shown in Fig. 10.  The microstructure of the sample heat treated at 870 °C was very similar to the as-SLM  condition. Both have similar “fish scale” morphology of melt pools and showed a fully dendritic  structure, but the melt pool boundary was blurrier compared to the as-SLM condition. This could  be attributed to a limited mass transformation at the holding temperature of 870 °C for 1 hour.  As the holding temperature increased up to 980 °C, the melt pool boundary became invisible.  Some recrystallized structures could be clearly observed as shown in Figs. 10e and 10f. However,  the dendritic structures were still visible along the blurry melt pool boundary. When the as-SLM  sample was annealed at 1150 °C, a fully recrystallized microstructure was observed as shown in  Figs. 10g and 10h.

# 3.2.2 TEM analysis  

Fig. 11 shows the TEM comparison of the as-SLM and heat treated samples at 1150 °C. The  as-SLM sample showed a dense distribution of dislocations throughout the entire thin foil as  shown in Fig. 11a in the STEM HAADF mode. The high density of dislocation in the melt pool  indicates that a high level of tensile residual stress exists in the as-SLM state. The high tensile  residual stress caused by the high cooling rate is considered as one of the major drawbacks of  SLMed parts [31,32]. This high dislocation density was also observed in other SLMed Ni-based  superalloys [8,32,33]. Tomus  et al . [32] thought that the high dislocation density is the key  contributor to the high yield strength of the as-SLM material. A few high Z-contrast precipitates  of \~100 nm in size were also observed in STEM-HAADF as illustrated in Fig. 11b. The EDS  showed these precipitates to be enriched in Nb and Mo, which is evidenced by the corresponding  Kα and Lα peaks in the particle spectrum (Fig. 11c). These findings are also supported by results  from EBM Inconel 625, where Nb-rich particles ranging in size from 50-80 nm were found in  the melt pool [8]. It should be pointed out that the presence of Cu, Fe, and Co EDS peaks can be  attributed to the sample holder, grid, or objective lens. After heating at 1150 °C, the dislocation  density is reduced dramatically due to dislocation motion and annihilation. These particles  present relatively low Z-contrast compared to those in the as-SLM sample, indicating that high  Z-contrast precipitates have dissolved into the matrix during heat treatment as shown in Fig. 11d  [8,32,33]. The low Z-contrast precipitates exhibit two different shapes (nearly round and square  in 2-dimensional view) as can be seen in Fig. 11e. The EDS results in Fig. 11f show that the  round precipitates are enriched in Cr. By comparison, the larger square precipitates could be Cr  oxide due to the presence of high amounts of Cr and oxygen elements. In addition, the round Cr- enriched precipitates are evenly distributed throughout the thin foil, while few Cr oxides were  observed.

# 3.2.3 EBSD analysis  

The microstructural evolution from as-SLM to heat treated was further analyzed using EBSD.  Fig. 12 shows the inverse pole figure (IPF) maps of the YZ cross-sections for the as-SLM and  heat-treated samples. Fig. 12a shows the texture and grain size of the as-SLM sample in the  subsurface along the scanning direction. Approximately 80% of the grains were found to be  orientated parallel to the (111) and (101) planes within a certain variation of orientation. A series  of columnar grains crossing several layers parallel to the build direction were present in the as- SLM sample. This is caused by directional heat transfer during fast solidification along the build  direction. As indicated by the dotted line in Fig. 12a, some smaller equiaxed grains with a size  under 20 µm were also observed in the remelted zone between the adjacent melt pools. This  could be attributed to epitaxial growth of the grains in the melt pool.  Fig. 12b shows the IPF  map of the of the sample heat treated at 870 °C. The grains become a little bigger but the  elongated morphology remains observable. Obvious grain growth in random directions was  found along with nearly equiaxed grain structures in the samples heat treated at 980 °C and  1150 °C (Figs. 12c and 12d). The grain size distributions in all of the samples are given in Fig.  13. It can be seen that the large grains, with average grain size more than 100 µm, emerged and  account for approximately 20% and 25% of the grains in the samples heat treated at 980 °C and  1150 °C, respectively. Few grains larger than 100 µm were observed in the as-SLM sample.  Twinning was observed in the annealed sample   at 1150 °C (Fig. 12d).  The formation of  annealing twins provides an evidence that a high level of residual stress in the as-SLM sample is  partially released during the post-annealing at 1150 °C [12]. In addition, Fig. 14 shows the grain  boundary misorientation maps of all samples under different conditions. The red lines represent  the small angle grain boundaries (2° - 5°), while the blue lines identify the large angle grain  boundaries (GB) with a misorientation angle higher than 15°. There is a clear trend that small  angle GBs, which is actually composed of dislocations, begin to disappear after heat treatment,  especially for the heat treated samples at 1150 °C. It indicates that recrystallization occurs with  the relaxation of residual stress primarily introduced by the dense dislocations during SLM  processing. The subsequent grain growth after recrystallization yields more homogenous  microstructure and weak grain orientation texture as shown in Fig. 12d.

# 3.2.4 X-ray diffraction analysis  

XRD of the pre-alloyed Inconel 625 powder and the XY cross-sections of all samples are  shown in Fig. 15. A typical Ni-Cr γ matrix with FCC lattice was detected for all samples. It has  been reported that fine intermetallic γ'' (Ni 3 Nb) particles were found in the NiCr matrix in the as- SLM state and then dissolved into the matrix in the heat treated state [8]. Other intermetallic  phases like δ (Ni 3 Nb), Laves (NbCr 2 ) will precipitate under different heat treatment conditions.  In this study, TEM analysis shows that Nb-/Mo-rich particles precipitated during SLM have  dissolved upon heat treatment at 1150 °C. Peaks of γ'' Ni 3 Nb overlap with γ-FCC matrix spectra  peaks in (200) and (220) planes for the as-SLM sample as shown in Fig. 15a. No other phases  were observed except the formation of carbides during heat treatments at 980 °C and 1150 °C.  

The lattice parameters for all samples were calculated from the XRD spectrum in (111) plane  are as follows: 3.60167 Å (as-SLM), 3.60041 Å (870 °C), 3.59789 Å (980 °C), 3.58162 Å  (1150 °C), and 3.57664 Å (powder). The decreasing trend for lattice parameter from as-SLM,  heat treated, to powder is further substantiated by another study in SLM Inconel 625 as a result  of stress relief and precipitate formation [11]. Figs. 15b & 15c illustrate the spectrum peaks in 

 (111) and (200) planes for the samples compared to the powder material. The peak shifts from 

 43.48° of the as-SLM sample to 43.8° of the powder. This shift caused by the change in lattice  parameter provided an indirect evidence of the residual stress reduction after heat treatment,  because the powder materials are considered as stress-free. Additionally, since the samples were  only annealed for one hour in this study, the change of lattice parameters for the NiCr matrix  from as-SLM to heat treatment would give an indirect evidence of the existence of precipitates.   Compared to the standard Ni-Cr FCC matrix lattice parameter (3.591 Å), the increase of lattice  parameter (3.60167 Å) in the as-SLM state is caused by Ni and Cr elements with larger atomic  radii of Inconel 625 which is solution strengthened by elements like Nb and Mo. When the  samples were heat treated at 870 °C, the decrease of lattice parameter (3.60041   Å) is possibly  due to the formation of δ (Ni 3 Nb) precipitates by niobium from the matrix [11]. After heat  treatment at 1150 °C, an increase of lattice parameter is expected due to the fact that δ particles  were dissolved back into the matrix again [12]. However, the decrease in lattice parameter in the  current study is consistent with the literature [11] despite that TEM analysis indicates particle  dissolution into the matrix. The decrease could be caused by the precipitation of MC carbides  which were identified by a clear spectrum peak as shown in Fig. 15. Carbides at the grain  boundary were observed in the sample heat treated at 1150 °C as shown in Fig. 16.

# 3.3 Microhardness  

Fig. 17 shows the statistical data of microhardness for all samples. A total of 40 dents were  made for each sample. The data was fitted using a normal distribution function. The average  microhardness values and standard deviations are in Table 3 for the as-SLM samples and the  heat treated samples at 870 °C, 980 °C, and 1150 °C. The average microhardness of the as-SLM  sample is comparable to the literature data summarized in Table 4. The heat treated samples  show lower microhardness values compared to the as-SLM samples. As illustrated in Fig. 17,  more than 80% of the hardness data points for the as-SLMed sample are higher than 3.4 GPa  with the exception of low limit of 2.65 GPa which could be caused by porosity. The large  standard deviation of the SLM sample hardness indicates that the sample has less homogeneous  mechanical properties than those of the heat treated ones. The microhardness values of the heat  treated samples decrease with an increase of the annealing temperature. The increased grain size  (Fig. 13) with the annealing temperature was responsible for the decreased hardness and stress  relaxation evidenced by the lattice parameter change. As mentioned by Li  et al.  [11] that the  decrease of the lattice parameters is also responsible for hardness reduction due to the dissolution  of δ phase into the matrix. In addition, as-SLM samples have larger microhardness values than  those of the EBMed ones, which is caused by the larger temperature gradients in SLM than those  in EBM since the powder bed in EBM is usually preheated up to 1000 °C.

# 4.  Conclusions  

Nearly full-dense Inconel 625 samples were selective laser melted and then heat treated at the  elevated temperatures to tune the microstructure and properties. The microstructure evolution  from SLM to heat treatment has been comprehensively characterized via optical microscopy,  SEM, EBSD, TEM, XRD, and micro-indentation methods. Key findings are summarized as  follows:  

   Scanning strategy has significant effects not only on surface topography but also on  subsurface defects including porosity and inclusions as a result of different thermal  loading histories.       Fine columnar γ dendrite microstructure with strong texture parallel to layer build-up  direction is the key metallurgical characteristic in the as-SLM samples. High dislocation  density, small angle grain boundaries, Nb-rich precipitates, and high tensile residual  stress are also inherited with the as-SLM melt pool.     MC precipitates peaks exist at high annealing temperatures, which is partially responsible  for the decrease of lattice parameter from as-SLM to annealing. Twinning may also occur  at high annealing temperatures.     Heat treatment recrystallizes microstructures, increases grain size, reduces dislocation  density, decreases grain boundary misorientation, dissolve high Z-contrast precipitates  and reduces the level of tensile residual stress.     The uncertainty of mechanical properties due to process variation is quantified by the  higher average microhardness and larger standard deviation of the as-SLM samples than  those of the heat treated ones.
