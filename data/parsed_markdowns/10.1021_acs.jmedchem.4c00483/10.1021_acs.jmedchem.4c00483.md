ABSTRACT:  Targeting dual-specificity tyrosine phosphorylation- regulated kinase 1A (DYRK1A) has been verified to regulate the progression of tau pathology as a promising treatment for Alzheimer’s disease (AD), while the research progress on DYRK1A inhibitors seemed to be in a bottleneck period. In this work, we identified  32  ( ZJCK-6-46 ) as the most potential DYRK1A inhibitor (IC 50  = 0.68 nM) through rational design, systematic structural optimization, and comprehensive evaluation. Compound  32  exhibited acceptable  in vitro  absorption, distribu- tion, metabolism, and excretion (ADME) properties and significantly reduced the expression of p-Tau Thr212 in Tau (P301L) 293T cells and SH-SY5Y cells. # INTRODUCTION  

Dual-specificity tyrosine phosphorylation-regulated kinase 1A (DYRK1A), a serine/threonine kinase, is a DYRK subfamily that belongs to the cyclin-dependent, mitogen-activated, glycogen synthase, and CDC-like (CMGC) protein kinase family.   The gene expressing DYRK1A is located on human chromosome 21 (HSA21) and is closely related to the development of various important physiological processes in the body, including regulating the expression of microtubule- associated protein tau and amyloid  β -protein (A β ) in the central nervous system (CNS), − 5   regulating neurogenesis and neuronal differentiation during embryonic development, , affecting regenerative pathways relevant to human insulin- producing  β -cells in the pancreas, ,   promoting DNA damage repair, controlling the cell cycle, ,   etc. Consequently, the abnormal expression or overactivation of DYRK1A leads to the occurrence of various diseases, such as Alzheimer’s disease (AD), Parkinson’s disease (PD), Down’s syndrome (DS), diabetes, and cancer. ,   The occurrence of AD has been associated with the accumulation of A β  amyloid plaques and neurofibrillary tangles (NFTs) in the brain. It has been found that DYRK1A can phosphorylate at least 11 serine/threonine sites in the tau protein, resulting in tau self-aggregation and fibrosis. In addition, it can phosphorylate amyloid precursor protein (APP) at the Thr668 residue to accelerate  β / γ - secretase-mediated APP cleavage, resulting in the production of toxic A β  peptides.   The ability of DYRK1A to regulate the expression of phosphorylated tau protein and obstruct the progression of tau pathology has aroused interest in DYRK1A inhibitors as a promising therapeutic for AD. ,  

Recently, some promising inhibitors targeting DYRK1A have emerged, but they still cannot meet the urgent medical needs for treating associated diseases. Despite these efforts, research on  in vivo  disease models has not made significant progress, largely due to limited p harm a co kinetics (PK) characteristics, low ligand utilization rate, or poor selectivity of reported small molecule inhibitors for DYRK1A. So far, several small molecules have been studied in clinical trials: E pig allocate chin gallate (EGCG) 17   and lorecivivint 18   are currently in phase III trials ( Figure 1 ), while others such as SM07883 19   and BBI02 20   (structures of SM07883 and BBI02 not disclosed) are in phase I trials. The difference is that the other three small molecules are ATP-competitive inhibitors, while EGCG is an ATP-noncompetitive inhibitor. Among

Previously, a series of potent diaryl-azaindole inhibitors of DYRK1A (DANDYs) were reported by Gourdain et al.,   and among them the 4-(1 H -pyrrolo[2,3- b ]pyridin-3-yl)phenol scaffold was the most prominent characteristic for compounds with the best enzyme inhibitory activity. However, the presence of phenolic hydroxyl groups in the structures might greatly limit their  in vivo  application. Afterward, Chaikuad et al. reported the methyl 9-anilinothiazolo[5,4- f  ]quinazoline-2- carbimidate (EHT) series with picomolar DYRK1A binding affinity.   We speculated that this excellent affinity was largely attributed to the good matching between the quinazoline moiety in their structures and the catalytic machinery in the binding pocket. DYRK1A possesses the conventional folding conformation of a protein kinase, its ATP-binding pocket is a cleft that consists of a large, predominantly  α -helical subdomain connected to a predominantly  β -sheet subdo- main.   As described in  Figure 2 , we fused the 1 H -pyrrolo[2,3- b ]pyridine and the quinazoline fragments to obtain the 6-(1 H - pyrrolo[2,3- b ]pyridin-3-yl)quinazoline hit scaffold based on the above research. The 1 H -pyrrolo[2,3- b ]pyridine moiety as the donor − acceptor − donor motif binds to the so-called “hinge” region and the 1-position N atom of the quinazoline moiety binds to the catalytic lysine. Once again, from the perspective of electrostatic potential (ESP), the quinazoline fragment of the scaffold exhibits a good match with the catalytic machinery in the pocket, especially the 2-position C − H (so-called a polarized proton) exhibits a strong positive ESP, which is due to the superposition of the electron-withdrawing effects of the two adjacent N atoms, leading to the formation of extremely favorable electrostatic complementarity between the 2-position C − H and the oxygen atom on the carbonyl group of Asp307. Additionally, further structural modification at the 4- position of the quinazoline ring to further occupy the catalytic machinery in the pocket seems to have a significant impact on the activity and even selectivity for DYRK1A. Meanwhile, the 4-position of the quinazoline ring exhibiting high reactivity has been widely studied and reported. − 26   Based on such a binding mode, with respect to synthetic tractability and freedom to operate, we have mainly focused our research on the catalytic machinery that has not been fully bound by the scaffold, resulting in the generation of 4-position substituted derivatives with completely different structures on the quinazoline moiety. Through fragment fusion and target- based drug design (TBDD) strategies, we reported the structure-guided development of the 6-(1 H -pyrrolo[2,3- b ]- pyridin-3-yl)quinazoline-4-substituted series as DYRK1A in- hibitors, which showed highly potent inhibitory activity against DYRK1A. Ultimately,  32  ( ZJCK-6-46 ) was identified as the most promising candidate compound, achieving balanced optimization between DYRK1A inhibitory potency, kinase selectivity, as well as  in vitro  and  in vivo  absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion (ADME) properties. Nevertheless, on the one hand, due to the presence of a polar ionizable carboxyl group, these carboxylic acid analogues possessed relatively low cLog  P  and favorable LLE while their BBB permeability might be greatly limited. On the other hand, introducing isonipecotic acid and 3-amino-2,2-dimethylpropa- noic acid fragments might not be conducive to the kinase selectivity of compounds. So, we abandoned the carboxyl group that could serve as a potential hydrogen bond acceptor and retained the characteristic of possessing an exposed NH proton at the 4-position of the quinazoline for the next investigation. Kinase Selectivity Improvement and Binding Mode Analysis.  For these synthetic novel derivatives with the 6- (1 H -pyrrolo[2,3- b ]pyridin-3-yl)quinazoline scaffold, through a comprehensive assessment based on the DYRK1A inhibitory potency, lipophilicity, and LLE, we ultimately identified three potential candidates  12 ,  26 , and  29 , which were substituted by completely different amine fragments. Especially, GSK-3 β  is another recognized kinase closely related to tau pathology, and studies have shown inhibition of its activity can also reduce the expression of phosphorylated tau and regulate the progression of tau pathology. ,   To further explore the actual potential of the DYRK1A inhibitors in ameliorating tau pathology, it seemed to be necessary to weaken the inhibitory potency of the compounds against GSK-3 β . # CONCLUSIONS  

In this study, we have carried out a classical medicinal chemistry program and successfully identified that compound 32  ( ZJCK-6-46 ) was a highly potent DYRK1A inhibitor with an IC 50  value of 0.68 nM, excellent kinase selectivity, moderate metabolic stability, superior passive diffusion property to penetrate the BBB, and acceptable cellular permeability. In the Tau (P301L) 293T cells and SH-SY5Y cells, compound  32 showed strong efficacy in reducing tau phosphorylation with low nanomolar EC 50  values. Further PK profile studies indicated that compound  32  had a moderate half-life, favorable oral bioavailability, and acceptable BBB permeability  in vivo . Additionally, compound  32  also showed the potential of ameliorating cognitive impairment in OA-induced mice via significantly decreasing tau phosphorylation and neuronal cell death, with low acute toxicity and a low risk of acute liver and kidney injury. Altogether, compound  32  is deserved as a valuable molecular tool to reveal the key roles of DYRK1A in the pathogenesis of AD for further development of anti-AD drug. Meanwhile, compound  32  has currently exposed a few urgent problems to be solved, such as the inevitable inhibitory effects on the closely related CLKs, especially CLK1 and CLK4, and the potential human P-pg efflux transport as a CNS drug. Further research is also necessary to investigate the impacts of the potential inhibition of compound  32  against CLK1 and CLK4 for the treatment of AD and to reduce the efflux transport by human P-pg. Furthermore, it is worth further exploring whether compound  32  has other biological functions in the treatment of AD.

The receiver solution for the B-to-A direction was prepared with 3 mL of HBSS buffer with DMSO (0.4%) and LY (5 mM). The cell culture plate seeded with MDCK-MDR1 cells was taken out of the incubator and the cell monolayers was washed with HBSS buffer. The transepithelial electrical resistance (TEER) value of cell monolayers from randomly selected wells was found to be 450  ± 20  Ω· cm 2   (cell monolayer would be used if TEER value >100  Ω· cm 2 ). The solution based on the volumes was added into the corresponding chamber as follows: for the A-to-B transport direction, 600  μ L of dosing solution as the apical buffer was added into the donor chamber (100  μ L for LY measurement and 100  μ L for  T 0  as the Backup) and 800  μ L of receiver solution as the basolateral buffer was added into the corresponding receiver chamber; for the B-to-A transport direction, 900  μ L of dosing solution as the basolateral buffer was added into the donor chamber (100  μ L for Backup) and 500  μ L of receiver solution as the apical buffer was added into the receiver chamber (100  μ L for LY measurement). Then 100  μ L of sample from apical chambers was taken into an opaque plate to determine LY concentration for LY T0 . The apical and basolateral plates were prewarm for about 5 min at 37  ° C, followed by beginning transport by placing the apical plate onto the basolateral plate. After incubating for 90 min at 37  ° C, the apical plate was separated from the basolateral, and 100  μ L of samples from the basolateral plate were taken to an opaque plate as LY T90 . The LY concentrations for LY T0  and LYT 90 were measured by Fluorometer (at excitation of 485 nm/emission of 535 nm), and the  P app  value of LY must meet less than 5.0  ×  10 − 6   cm/ s in order to conduct subsequent experiments (values higher than 5.0 ×  10 − 6   cm/s might indicate leaky monolayers). Finally, 60  μ L of diluted or undiluted donor or receiver samples was added into the mixture solution containing 60  μ L of ACN with IS (osalmid or imipramine) and 50  μ L of H 2 O for LC-MS/MS analysis. Metoprolol, atenolol, and digoxin were used as the reference compounds.

Behavior Tests.  MWM test was carried out as previously described.   Briefly, the MWM test was performed to evaluate the effect of compound  32  on ameliorating cognitive dysfunction in 100 ng OA (S1786, Beyotime Biotechnology Co., Ltd., Shanghai, China)- induced C57BL/6 mice. After 2 weeks of oral administration, the visible platform pretraining was carried out for 3 days, excluding the factors that affect the normal experiment of mice, such as the inability of mice to swim, the presence of visual impairment, and the adaptation to the new environment. According to the results of the visible platform test, the mice with visual impairment or unable to swim were excluded from the hidden platform test for 6 days. The platform should be hidden under the water surface. The six groups of mice were trained in the order of WT group, MOD group, compound 32  (10 mg/kg), compound  32  (20 mg/kg), compound  32  (30 mg/ kg), and donepezil (1.3 mg/kg) groups. If the escape zone was found within 60 s, the mouse was allowed to stay in the escape zone for 5 s and then immediately moved to the breeding cage. If not found, it was placed in the shelter for 20 s and then immediately moved to the breeding cage. On day 7 of the hidden platform test, a prospective randomized open-blinded endpoint (probe) trial was performed to record the times of passing through the platform and the length of escape latency of each group.

The levels of p-Tau Thr212 and total Tau in brain tissues were obtained using mouse ELISA kits (LV31131 and LV30887) from Animal Union Biotechnology Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). All processes were carried out in accordance with the instructions.  

Nissl Staining.  In this study, Nissl staining solution (G1036-100 ML, Service Biotechnology Co., Ltd., Wuhan, China) was used to stain the sections. Paraffin sections were dewaxed using xylene for 5 − 10 min, sliced, and added into anhydrous ethanol for 5 min, and treated with 75% ethanol for 5 min, then washed with water for 2 min. These treated samples were stained with Nissl staining solution for 2 − 5 min (the time could be adjusted according to the dyeing requirements). Then, the brain tissues were differentiated with 0.1% glacial acetic acid until the Nissl bodies were dark blue and the background was light blue or colorless. The slices were dried in an oven at 65  ° C for 4 h or more. After being fully baked and dried, the glass slides were immersed in xylene, and cooled for 10 min, and then sealed with neutral gum.  

Immuno his to chemistry.  The mouse hemisphere sections were submerged into ethylenediamine-tetraacetic acid (EDTA) antigenic retrieval buffer (pH 8.0) or citrate buffer (0.01 M, pH 6.0). They were treated with 3% H 2 O 2  for 10 min at 37  ° C to eliminate the activity of endogenous peroxidase. Molecular Docking.  Molecular docking studies of the selected compounds to DYRK1A protein were carried out using software Schro dinger maestro 11.9. The DYRK1A crystal structure (PDB ID: 6S17) 57   was obtained from the RCSB protein data bank (PDB) database ( https://www.rcsb.org/ ) and preprocessed, optimized, and minimized by default with Protein Preparation Wizard module (constrained minimization using OPLS3e force field). The ligands were processed by default using the LigPrep module. When screening in the Glide module, the appropriate location was specified in the generation of the receptor grid, and the reference ligand binding site of the protein was selected as the centroid of the 12 Å box. Finally, the docking studies were performed based on the induced-fit method to obtain the results. − 60  

Molecular Dynamics Simulation.  Gromacs 2020 software package was used to perform molecular dynamics simulations to study the binding mode given by molecular docking. − 65   The protein was processed using AMBER99SB-ILDN force field parameters, while the small molecule ligands were processed using the GAFF general force field parameters. The Sobtop program was used to construct the small molecule topology, and RESP was used for charge fitting. The TIP3P explicit water model was selected, where the minimum distance between the atom and the edge of the water box was set to 1.0 nm, and the system charge was neutralized using sodium or chloride ions based on the docking results. The workflow of molecular dynamics simulation included four steps: energy minimization, heating, equilibration, and production kinetics simulation. First, the heavy atoms of the protein (and small molecule ligands) were constrained, and the water molecules were processed using 10 000 steps of energy minimization (including 5000 steps of steepest descent and 5000 steps of conjugate gradient). Subsequently, the constraints were released, and the entire system was subjected to 10 000 steps of energy minimization (including 5000 steps of steepest descent and 5000 steps of conjugate gradient). Energy optimization involved slowly heating the system to 300 K within 50 ps, and the system was equilibrated for 50 ps in the NPT ensemble after completing the heating work. Finally, the system was subjected to a 200 ns molecular dynamics simulation in the NPT ensemble. Trajectory data were saved every 20 ps and the trjconv module was used for relevant analysis. The binding free energy of the ligand and protein was calculated using the g_MMPBSA method.  

Statistical Analysis.  All experiments were independently performed in triplicate. Statistical analysis was performed by GraphPad Prism 9.0, and  p  < 0.05 was considered statistically significant.
