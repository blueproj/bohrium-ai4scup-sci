# a b s t r a c t  

The precipitation behavior of a novel cobalt-based superalloy subjected to prior plastic deformations was inves- tigated in details by using  ﬁ eld emission scanning electron microscope (FE-SEM), transmission electron micro- scope (TEM), etc. The precipitation of a  μ -phase, which appeared at temperatures of 1073 – 1373 K, was con ﬁ rmed, while MC-type carbide was observed at the temperatures  N 1173 K. The prior plastic deformations promoted the precipitation of the  μ -phase during subsequent aging. Further, the precipitation of the  μ -phase at temperatures lower than 1173 K was con ﬁ rmed to occur predominantly along the {111} slip bands; this was the dominant deformation mechanism of this alloy during plastic deformation. In contrast, there was no evidence that the precipitation of the MC-type carbide was affected by the prior plastic deformations in the case of the specimens exposed at 1273 and 1373 K.

# 1. Introduction  

Ni-based and Co-Ni-based superalloys are used widely as turbine disk materials, owing to their superior strength and creep properties at elevated temperatures  [1,2] . Recently, we reported a novel Co – Ni- based superalloy, Co – 35Ni – 17.5Cr – 8Mo – 3Nb – 2Al (wt%), which exhibited superior mechanical properties at temperatures ranging from room temperature to 1073 K. The excellent high-temperature mechan- ical properties of this alloy were ascribable to its high Mo (8 wt%) and Nb (3 wt%) contents  [3] , which, in turn, were attributable to solid-solu- tion strengthening and/or the Suzuki segregation  [4 – 6] , while the Al (2 wt%) leads to the formation of  γ ′  phase (Ni 3 Al)  [7,8] . In addition, it has been shown that combination of these elements (such as Mo and Nb) has a high tendency to form topologically closed-packed (TCP) phases such as  σ -,  μ -, and laves-phases during prolonged exposure to at elevated temperatures  [9 – 12] . It has been suggested that these TCP phases have a deleterious effect on the mechanical properties in the case of some polycrystalline superalloys  [13] . The passive effects of the TCP phases on the mechanical properties of materials have been dem- onstrated through three aspects  [14] . To begin with, the brittle nature of the TCP phases generally leads to an early failure, owing to the ready formation of cracks between the TCP phase and alloy matrix [15] . In addition, the depletion of solid-solution strengthening elements such as W, Mo, Cr, and Re from the matrix because of the formation of the TCP phases, results in the softening of the  γ -matrix  [15] . Finally, the precipitation of the  μ -phase has been shown to be detrimental to the corrosion resistance of a Ni-16Cr-15Mo alloy to HF  [16 – 18] .  

The formation of a  μ -phase was con ﬁ rmed in a few binary alloys such as Co-W  [19] , Co-Mo  [20] , Fe-W  [20] and Fe-Mo  [21]  alloys, as well as in some Ni-based B 7 A 6 -type superalloys  [15,22,23]  such as Co 7 W 6 Co 7 Mo 6 , and Fe 7 W 6 . The structure of the  μ -phase belongs to the  D 5 3 d − R 3 m  space group, with  a  = 0.476 nm and  c  = 2.56 nm  [22] . In some su- peralloys, the precipitation of the  μ -phase is unavoidable because of their high W or/and Mo content (W or/and Mo  N  3.5 at.%)  [24] . Normal- ly, two types of  μ -phases are observed: a primary  μ -phase and a second- ary  μ -phase. The primary  μ -phase precipitates at grain boundaries during solidi ﬁ cation, while the secondary  μ -phase forms during aging or at elevated temperatures  [24] . Although it has been suggested that the precipitation of  μ -phase has a detrimental effect on the mechanical properties at room temperature  [23] , Simonetti et al. found no evidence of a needle-type  μ -phase with a moderate volume fraction having a del- eterious effect on the tensile properties of an MC-type single-crystal su- peralloy at room temperature  [15] .  

It has been shown that prior plastic deformations and aging result in a signi ﬁ cant increase in the strength of the present alloy  [3] . However, it is not clear how the microstructure of this novel cobalt-based superalloy evolves in response to plastic deformations and subsequent aging at var- ious temperatures. With this in mind, the purpose of this study was to in- vestigate the effects of prior plastic deformations on the microstructural evolution of the investigated alloy when exposed to various temperatures.

# 3. Results  

3.1. Microstructure after solution treatment and prior plastic deformations  

After solution treatment at 1473 K, almost a single  γ -phase was obtained with a few of undissolved blocky MC-type carbides, as can be seen from the SEM image in  Fig. 1 . A distinct phase transformation did not occur after the various prior plastic deformations (S20, S40, and S66), as can be seen from the results of the XRD analysis ( Fig. 2 ). However, the intensity of the (111) and (222) peaks increased signi ﬁ - cantly after the plastic deformations, as shown in  Fig. 2 , while (220) peak vanished gradually. It was con ﬁ rmed that this tendency was strongly dependent on the degree of prior plastic deformation (S66  N  S40  N  S20). The increase in the intensity of (111) peak suggested that a deformation texture was induced in the alloy by the prior plastic deformations.

# 4. Discussion  

# 4.1. Effects of refractory elements  

The TCP phases are usually composed of large refractory elements such as W, Mo, and Re as well as smaller elements such as Ni and Cr. As a consequence, the composition of the TCP phases can vary widely [25] . Of the various TCP phases, the  μ -phase, which is an A 7 B 6 -type pre- cipitate, is found frequently in superalloys, especially in those that have high amounts of refractory elements such as Mo and W  [15] . In this study, two types of  μ -phases were observed; they mainly consisted of Mo, Co, Cr, and Ni, as determined by EDX analysis. It has been reported that, of these elements, Mo has a stronger tendency to form the  μ -phase than do the others such as W, Cr, Nb, and Co and that the  μ -phase be- comes the main TCP precipitate if the Mo and W contents are  N 3.5 (at%)  [24] . This was also found to be the case in the present study.

4.3. Effects of prior plastic deformations and temperature on precipitation of μ -phase  

On the basis of the results of the microstructural analysis and the TTT curves, it is clear that the plastic deformations performed before the specimens were exposed to high temperatures promoted the precipita- tion of the  μ -phase. The higher degree of these plastic deformations, the greater was the likelihood of the precipitation of the  μ -phase. One rea- son for this is that dislocations are induced in a greater density by these plastic deformations. This result is similar to that reported by Asgari, who found that the formation of an hcp phase in the alloy MP35N depends on the dislocation density  [29] .  

The formation of stacking faults along the {111} planes with solute atoms (Mo, Cr, and Co) segregated at the stacking fault ribbons has been described previously using the weak-beam dark- ﬁ eld technique and atom probe tomography  [3] . In the case of the investigated alloy, the  〈 111 〉 ﬁ ber deformation texture was induced by the prior plastic de- formations, as indicated by the results of the XRD analysis, shown in  Fig. 2 . In other words, a large number of stacking faults were found after aging via dissociation of perfect dislocation into two Shockley partial dislocations moving on the {111} planes during the prior plastic defor- mations.  Fig. 13  shows the microstructure of S20 as determined using TEM. The bright- ﬁ eld image ( Fig. 13 (a)) and weak-beam dark- ﬁ eld image ( Fig. 13 (b)) of the specimen suggested the presence of a large number of dislocations and the formation of a slip band after the plastic deformations. Most of them were located in the {111} planes and trans- formed into stacking faults during aging. In the case of S40 and S66, a greater number of dislocations and slip bands formed. However, it was dif ﬁ cult to observe the morphologies of the dislocations and slip bands, owing to their high density.  

After the exposure of ST to elevated temperatures, the  μ -phase pre- cipitated randomly in it ( Figs. 5 (c) and (d)). However, the  μ -phase (es- pecially the needle-type  μ -phase) mostly precipitated along certain planes in the specimens subjected to prior plastic deformations (S20, S40, and S66). As indicated by the dashed lines in  Fig. 5 (g) and (k), which show SEM images, these planes had an intersection angle of ap- proximately 70°.  Fig. 14  shows the microstructure of S20, as determined using TEM. After exposure at 1073 K, the  μ -phase was observed to pre- cipitate along certain planes, as indicated by the dashed line and the el- lipse in the bright- ﬁ eld image ( Fig. 14 (a)). On the basis of the selected area diffraction patterns, these planes were found to be approximately parallel to {111} planes. # 5. Conclusions  

1. A deformation texture with a mass of dislocations and slip bands formed in the investigated alloy during aging after prior plastic deformations.  

2. The  μ -phase was the main precipitate in all the specimens after expo- sure at the temperatures lower than 1173 K, while only MC-type car- bide precipitated after exposure at temperatures  N 1273 K. The  μ - phase was con ﬁ rmed as being a Mo-rich precipitate. Further, two types of  μ -phases, a needle-type phase and a blocky phase, were ob- served in all the specimens.  

3. The prior plastic deformations promoted the precipitation of the  μ - phase via the Suzuki segregation, which occurred during aging after the plastic deformations. The solutes Mo, Co, and Cr, which segregat- ed at the stacking fault ribbons, acted as nucleation sites for the  μ - phase. The number of nucleation sites increased with time at temper- atures lower than 1173 K. However, we found no evidence indicating that the precipitation of the MC-type carbide was affected by the prior plastic deformations in the case of the specimens exposed at 1273 and 1373 K.
