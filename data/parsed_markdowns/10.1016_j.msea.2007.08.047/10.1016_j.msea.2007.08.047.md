# The evolution of    phase in Ni–Co base superalloys  

C.Y. Cui   ∗ , Y.F. Gu, D.H. Ping, H. Harada, T. Fukuda  

High Temperature Materials Center, National Institute for Materials Science, 1-2-1 Sengen, Ibaraki 305-0047, Japan  

Received 11 April 2007; received in revised form 13 August 2007; accepted 23 August 2007  

# Abstract  

The evolution of a    phase (Ni 3 Ti) in Ni–Co base superalloys with different Co and Ti content has been investigated. The results showed that the    phase existed in the samples with 23.1 wt.%Co and 5.4 wt.%Ti (TMW-3) and also the samples with 28.6 wt.% Co and 7.4 wt.%Ti (TMW-5) heat-treated at the temperature ranges of 1100–1140   ◦ C, but not in the samples with 26.3 wt.%Co and 6.0 wt.%Ti (TMW-4). Even in TMW-3 and TMW-5, higher solution temperature (1180   ◦ C) can fully reduce the formation of the    phase. The evolution of the    phase is complicated and may be related to the Ti content, Ti/Al ratio and alloy’s composition.

# 1. Introduction  

Commercial Ni-base superalloys mainly consist of    matrix and   ′   (Ni 3 Al) two-phase structure, which is important for them to keep excellent high temperature properties. Al and Ti are the two of major   ′   forming elements. The fraction of   ′   increases with the increasing of the total Al + Ti contents. An increase in Ti/Al ratio and   ′   fraction is beneﬁcial to strength increase  [1] . Substitution of Ti atoms for Al atoms in the   ′   lattice increases the lattice parameter, thereby increasing the lattice misﬁt and anti-phase boundary energy of   ′   [2,3] , both of these effects result in an increased   ′   strengthening contribution. On the other hand, the   ′   phase with sufﬁciently high Al is stable, but when the content of Ti is high, Ni 3 Ti (  ) phase can be formed  [4] .   phase has a hexagonal close packed (hcp) structure that is non-coherent with    matrix. Generally speaking, there are two morphologies of    that may occur, intragranular platelet mor- phology and cellular morphology. The effects of    phase on mechanical property are dependent on its morphology and posi- tion  [5] . The platelet    phase is associated with loss of room temperature ductility, while the cellular    phase can lower the notched stress rupture strength  [5] .  

Recently, a new concept was proposed to design Ni–Co base superalloys for turbine disk application in the temperature regions of 650–750   ◦ C, as shown in  Fig. 1 [6,7] . This concept is based on combining the characters of two kinds of   /  ′   two- phase alloys, i.e., a Ni-base superalloy (U720LI) and a Co-base alloy, Co–16.9 wt.%Ti. Thus, the contents of Co and Ti in new TMW alloys are higher than those in normal turbine Ni-base disk superalloys, as listed in  Table 1 . Previous results indicated that some Ni–Co base alloys with a   /  ′   two-phase structure have improved microstructural stability, superior tensile strength and higher creep resistance at temperatures below 750   ◦ C than cur- rent U720LI  [7,8] . On the other hand, it was also found that the   phase forms in some TMW alloys due to high Ti content  [6,8] .  

It is known that mechanical property of disk superalloys is dependent critically on the microstructure, which is determined by the compositions and heat treatment  [9] . For some alloys, controlling grain size, distribution of   ′   particles and remov- ing unexpected phase can be achieved by selecting suitable heat treatment. For example, in conventional U720LI  [10] , ﬁne grain sizes can be achieved by subsolvus heat treatment, which pro- duce the high tensile yield strength and fatigue crack initiation resistance; while coarse grain size can be obtained by super- solvus heat treatment, which produce the high creep resistance and dwell fatigue crack growth resistance. The distribution of  ′   is rather complex in U720LI. In addition to the primary   ′ , intragranular secondary and tertiary   ′   particles are also present within the grain. Higher solution temperature can reduce the proportion of primary   ′ , thus more secondary and tertiary   ′ phases are formed within the grain, which is beneﬁcial to the strength increase. # 2. Thermodynamic prediction  

As shown in  Fig. 1 , three new alloys, TMW-3, TMW-4 and TMW-5 with different Co–Ti additions were selected in order to clarify the effects of Co–Ti contents on the evolution of    phase. Table 1  lists the chemical compositions of U720LI (base alloy), TMW-3, TMW-4 and TMW-5. The Ti contents are 5.0 wt.% in U720LI, 5.4 wt.% in TMW-3, 6.0 wt.% in TMW-4 and 7.4 wt.% inTMW-5,respectively.The Co contents are 15 wt.%inU720LI, 23.1 wt.% in TMW-3, 26.3 wt.% in TMW-4 and 28.6 wt.% in TMW-5, respectively.  

Before commencing a detailed study, considerations were given to the calculated phase equilibrium using a commercial Thermo-Calc program with the NI-RUMN database (Thermo- Calc software, Sweden).  Fig. 2  shows the predicted phase diagrams. The base alloy, U720LI, consist of   ,   ′   and some minor phases (carbides, borides, etc.). The solvus temperature and volume fraction of   ′   are predicted to 1153   ◦ C and 44% (at 700   ◦ C), respectively. The predicted results are consistent with the experiment results  [12] . For new alloys, the follow- ing features can be obtained from the predicted phase diagram: (1) besides    and   ′   phases,    phase is predicted to exist in all tested alloys; (2) the content and temperature range of    phase increase with the increasing of Ti content; (3) the volume frac- tion of    phase peaks at the temperature which is close to the  ′   solvus temperature at 1140   ◦ C; (4) the solvus temperature of    phase increases with the increasing of the Ti content. As listed in  Table 1 , the solvus temperature of    phase is predicted to 1178   ◦ C in TMW3, which is lower than that of TMW4 (1211   ◦ C) and TMW5 (1271   ◦ C).  

It is noted that the   ′   solvus temperature of new alloys is slightly lower than that of U720LI (1153   ◦ C). As mentioned in Section  1 , the main differences between new alloys and U720LI are the contents of Co and Ti. Increasing content of Co may lower the   ′   solvus temperature, while increasing content of Ti may raise the   ′   solvus temperature. The similar   ′   solvus temperature in the new alloys may be achieved by mixing effects of Co and Ti (Co-16.9 wt.%, as shown in  Fig. 1 ).  

Based on the predications, three solution temperatures, 1100   ◦ C,1140   ◦ Cand1180   ◦ C,whicharebelow,closeandabove the   ′   solvus temperature and the temperature of    phase peak fraction, were selected to examine the temperature dependence of the evolution of the    phase in new Ni–Co base alloys.  

# 3. Experimental procedure  

Fifty kilograms of ingots were cast by a vacuum induction melt and then hot-rolled at the Special Metal Corporation. The plates were hot-rolled at 1120   ◦ C. The ﬁnal thickness of hot- rolled plates with a reduction of 75% was about 13 mm. All the samples were taken from similar locations within the rolled plates and then heat-treated at 1100   ◦ C, 1140   ◦ C and 1180   ◦ C for 4 h followed by water quenching. In addition, in order to know the total   ′   phase fractions in new alloys, the samples were heat-treated at the supersolvus temperature (1220   ◦ C/1 h), and then cooled to 760   ◦ C in 2 weeks. This slow cooling rate may allow all   ′   phase to precipitate and grow at near equilibrium state  [9] . Samples cut from the hot-rolled and heat- treated plates were ground, mechanically polished and then etched in a Kalling solution (100 mL HCl, 100 mL methanol and 50 g CuCl 2 ). The microstructures were examined by an optical microscopy and a scanning electron microscopy (SEM) equipped with an energy-dispersive spectrometer (EDS). The foils for transmission electron microscopy (TEM) observation were prepared by a twin-jet electron polisher in a chemical

# 5. Discussion  

Table 2  summarizes the evolution of    phase in TMW alloys under the present heat treatment conditions. The results indicate that the    phase formed only in TMW-3 and TMW-5, not in TMW-4, though all the alloys contain high contents of Co and Ti. This means that we can obtain   /  ′   two-phase structure in some of TMW alloys with high contents of Co and Ti. This   /  ′ structure is very important for keeping the excellent high tem- perature properties for industrial application. Another important result is that the initial    phase can be reduced by selecting suit- able heat treatment ( Figs. 5–7 ). In fact, keeping some amounts of   phase during processing is a very useful way to control grain size. For example, ﬁne grain size can be achieved even at higher solution temperature in the TMW-5 due to the    with higher solvus temperature and its pinning effect. Also in the TMW-3, the grain size in the region with    phase is smaller than that without    phase. For many years, A-286 alloy has been pro- cessed to ﬁne grain size by forging in the vicinity of    solvus [14] .

It is known that the formation of    phase is strongly related to the Ti content, Ti/Al ratio and alloy compositions  [8] . The main differences among the tested alloys were the con- tents of Co and Ti. TMW-5 with the highest Ti and Ti/Al ratio (Ti:7.4 wt.%, Ti/Al = 3.7) contained higher volume frac- tion of the    phase than TMW-3 (Ti:5.4 wt.%, Ti/Al = 2.8) and TMW-4 (Ti:6.0 wt.%, Ti/Al = 3.1) heat-treated at temperatures of 1100   ◦ C and 1140   ◦ C, indicating that Ti content and Ti/Al ratio are very important for the    phase formation. Moreover, the alloy composition also shows some effects on the existence of    phase. Compared to the TMW3 and TMW4 with similar Ti/Al ratio, the    phase does exist in TMW3 with high Ti and low Co, but not in TMW-4 with higher contents of Co and Ti, implying that high Co is useful in preventing the    phase for- mation. Probably, a partial replacement of Ni by Co may lead to   ′   stability, since the   ′   in TMW alloys has a composition of (Ni, Co) 3  (Al, Ti)  [7] . However, more Ni replaced by Co forms a (Ni, Co, Cr) 3  (Ti, Al) phase with a hexagonal structure [15] .  

As to the effect of    phase on mechanical properties of TMW alloys, previous results showed that formation of the platelet   phase has a slight effect on alloy’s strength and ductility  [16] . The exact effects of    phase on mechanical properties are still under study.  

# 6. Conclusions  

The evolution of    phase was examined in the present study and the following conclusions could be drawn:  

(1) The    phase was only found in TMW-3 and TMW-5 heat- treated at temperature range of 1100–1140   ◦ C, not in TMW- 4 at all the heat treatment condition.  

(2) The evolution of    phase was complicated and may be related to Ti content, Ti/Al ration and alloy composition.

 (3) The initial volume fraction of    phase could be reduced by selecting suitable heat treatment.
