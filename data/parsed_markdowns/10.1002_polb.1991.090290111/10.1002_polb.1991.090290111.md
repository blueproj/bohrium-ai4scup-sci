# INTRODUCTION  

For microelectronic applications requiring planarity, insulating ability, and thermal stability, the mate- rials of choice are generally polyimides.1 Because sorption of ambient water vapor can substantially change the dielectric constant of a polyimide," it is important to know how much water vapor a polyim- ide film can accommodate, particularly at 100% rel- ativehumidity(RH).The maximum water uptake provides an upper limit on the dielectric constant of the material, and therefore a minimum perfor- mancelevelfor thepolymer filmas aninsulator.  

There have been several investigations of water solubility in polyimides. Hubbell et al.? measured the solubility of water in Kapton H\* by both time- lag permeation experiments and direct observation of sorption using an electrobalance sensitive to 2 μg. This pyromellitic dianhydride oxydianiline (PMDA- ODA)materialshowedasolubilityof52cm? (STP)/cm? at 30°C, which corresponds to an up- take of 2.9 wt %. The material obeyed Henry's law; the amount absorbed was proportional to pressure, although the proportionality factor (the solubility coefficient S) decreased with temperature. Sacher and Susko? reinvestigated Kapton, using a modified commercial permeation analyzer, and confirmed the earlier work and extended the investigation to thin- ner samples and higher temperatures.It wasfound that the concentration of water absorbed in the polyimide, when expressed as a function of relative humidity, was temperature independent with a maximum of 4.4 wt% at 100%RH.Yang et al.6took sorption isotherms of 7.6 mK apt on samples at three temperatures using a spring balance. At 30°C, they measureda maximumuptake of49 cm\*(STP)/ cm°, which corresponds to 2.8 wt %, and agrees very well with the measurement of Hubbell et al. The same authors later showed that the diffusion is Fickian?and that there were aging and thickness effects on the diffusion rate.  

Denton and co-workers\* took advantage of the dielectric change induced by water,andmadeade- termination of water uptake in PMDA-ODA based on sensitive electrical measurements on capacitors made from aluminum and polyimide. They found that the capacitancewas alinearfunction ofrelative humidity,independent of temperature.Capacitance was also linear in water uptake as measured with a Mettlerbalance,therefore the relative humidity data were extrapolated to 1 oo%and combined with the weight correlation to yield a maximum uptake of 6 Wt%.  

It would be desirable to measure water sorption by a direct method that can be conveniently em ployed inside a vacuum system containing controlled amounts of water vapor.It would also be of interest to determine the maximum so lub ili ties of several different poly imi des to see if their structures and so lub ili ties can be correlated,and todetermine whether the experimental method is sensitive enough not only to measure small amounts of sorp- tion,butalsotomeasuretheevensmallerdifferences in sorption between similar materials.Accordingly, we have constructed an apparatus that uses aquartz crystal microbalance tomeasure small mass changes directly, and incorporated the sensor into a vacuum system mounted inside a constant temperature bath. Our initial results on a series of four poly imi des are describedbelow.

# DISCUSSION  

The solubility of water in a polymer is a function of several parameters, including pressure of water va- por over the sample,temperature,and characteris- tics of the sample itself, including thickness, chem- ical structure, morphology, and perhaps even method of preparation. We have been able to vary chemical structure and morphology while keeping pressure, temperature, thickness, and method of preparation virtually constant. It must be pointed out,though,that comparison of these results with

In particular, the absolute solubilities for polyimide films whose thicknesses are on the order of 2 microns are not expected to match those of thicker films(see below). The thickness used in these experiments waschosen to fit the constraint onsamplemass that exists for the micro balance to have a linear f re- quencyresponse.  

The differences we observe in water solubility at 100%RH could be due to differences in chemical affinity for water molecules, or differences in the amount of space available for permeation by mole- cules as big as water, or some combination of both. Some light can be shed on the issue by consideration of empirical solubility parameters.l1 Molecules with similar values of the parameter are more mutually soluble than molecules with disparate  values. Hy- drogen bonding affects the solubility as well, because molecules dissolve preferentially in hosts with sim- ilar degrees of hydrogen bonding. In units of (cal/ cm3)1/2, water has a solubility parameter of 23.4. The corresponding values for diphenyl ether and benzene are10.1 and9.2,respectively.Furthermore, water is a “strong" hydrogen bonder, diphenyl ether is “moderate,” and benzene is"poor." Since the PDA moiety in PMDA-PDA is simply benzene, and the ODAmoietyinthetwoPMDA-ODApolyimidesis diphenyl ether, one would predict that on the basis of chemical affinity, water should be more soluble inPMDA-ODAthaninPMDA-PDA.Butweob- serve the reverse. Therefore, it appears that relative water solubility in these polymers is controlled by chain morphology rather thanby differencesin chemical affinity.  

Low water solubility implies effective space-filling (packing) by the polymer chains. Highly linear chains have the potential to pack very well (as in liquid crystals) as long as the glass transition tem- perature (Tg) is low enough to permit some reori- entation during annealing (or, in our case, imidiza- tion). For polymers with high T, values (such as polyimides), however, linearity results in poor packing because the stiff chains are not able to orient themselves cooperatively.As a consequence,PMDA- PDA exhibited the highest solubility of the four polyimides studied here. Chains formed by the other three poly imi des were kinked to some degree. PMDA-3,4'-ODA has two segments per repeat unit, one of 15.4 A and the other of 2.8A,if we model the polymer as a series of rigid line segments. (The di- men sion perpendicular to theline segments would manifest itself primarily as a hindrance to rotation about the segment axes.)BPDA-PDAresembles PMDA-3,4'-ODA in that it has two segments per repeat unit, one being several times as long as the other(12.6and4.3A).PMDA-4,4'-ODAhas0nly one bend per repeat unit and a segment length of 18.2 A in the simple model.  

PMDA-4,4'-ODA was examined several years ago by wide-angle x-ray diffraction, 12 and displayed no long-range crystalline order. Therefore, PMDA-4,4' ODA packs in a more irregular (and denser) fashion, aided by a lower Tg than for PMDA-PDA. It is therefore reasonable that we observe less water up- take in PMDA-4,4'-ODA than we do in PMDA- PDA. For PMDA-3,4'-ODA, the smaller segment is short enough so that rotation about the C-O bond is severely hindered and the chains are more linear than they would be if the segmentswereof equal length ( with that length being great enough to allow rotation of all the ring structures around their axes). Based on these simple geometric considerations, therefore, one would expect its solubility to lie be- tweenthoseofPMDA-PDAandPMDA-4,4'-ODA, as observed. But x-ray diffraction data13 suggest that PMDA-3,4'-ODA actually packsmore effectively than PMDA-4,4'-ODA and should thus have a lower solubility. The difference between the two conclu sions isprobably due to the difference between the two experimental samples.Thex-raydiffraction samples were substantially thicker than the films coated on our quartz crystal microbalances. It has been shown for several polymers, most notably PMDA-4,4'-ODA,14 that the morphology of thinner films exhibits substantially more in-plane orienta- tion than that of thicker films. As a consequence, the thinner films have been shown to absorb N- methyl pyr ro li done (NMP) and dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) faster than thicker flms.15 So the thinner thefilm,themorethemorphologyalongthez-axis differs from what would be found in a truly three- dimensional sample.With packing disrupted in the vertical direction,it is reasonable to expect that a normally tightly packed material would show greater solubility than it would at greater thicknesses.

# CONCLUSIONS  

For films of thickness on the order of 2 μm, we find that water uptake is greatest in PMDA-PDA, fol- lowed by PMDA-3,4'-ODA, PMDA-4,4'-ODA, and BPDA-PDA, in that order. The different solubilities measured here appear to be attributable primarily to morphological differences between the different polyimide films, although the rates of diffusion ap- pear to be infuenced more by the chemical com- positions of the materials. The fact that they are indeed films means that the numbers measured here are not the intrinsic so lub ili ties that would be ex- hibited by thick, three-dimensional blocks of poly- mer unconstrained by bonding to a surface. Given that these materials are usually used in film form, however,our results should have some relevance to applications where water vapor solubility is imp or- tant.
