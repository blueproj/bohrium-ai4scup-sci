# Laser-assisted friction stir processing of IN738LC nickel-based superalloy: stir zone characteristics  

S. M. Mousavizade 1 , M. Pouranvari ∗ 2 , F. M. Ghaini 3 , H. Fujii 4   and Y. D. Chung 4  

Friction stir processing (FSP) of high softening-temperature materials such as nickel-based superalloys is considered to be dif ﬁ cult. Laser heating of a localised area ahead of the FSP tool was used to provide suf ﬁ cient plasticity during the FSP of IN738LC nickel-based superalloy. The stir zone (SZ) microstructure of the friction stir processed and laser-assisted friction stir processed were characterised. Laser-assisted friction stir processing (LAFSP) produced a defect-free pass, but FSP resulted in generation of a discontinuity in the SZ. Both lower volume fraction of partially dissolved  γ ′   precipitates and coarser grain structure of SZ in LAFSP led to more ductility of the SZ material and elimination of the defects.  

Keywords:  Friction stir processing, Nickel-based superalloy, IN738LC, Stir zone, Laser heating  

# Introduction  

Friction stir processing (FSP) is a solid-state metal work- ing technique based on the principles of friction stir weld- ing (FSW).   Friction between the tool and workpiece generates elevated temperatures, which lowers the  ﬂ ow stress of the material and produces a signi ﬁ cant degree of plasticity in the vicinity of the rotating tool. This, in turn, imparts substantial strain to the workpiece resulting in re ﬁ nement and modi ﬁ cation of the microstructure. – 7  

As reviewed by Mishra and Ma 2   and Ma,   FSP has dis- tinct advantages and has been used for fabrication of sur- face composite, homogenisation of powder metallurgy alloys, microstructural modi ﬁ cation of metal matrix com- posites, obtaining high-strain-rate superplasticity and speci ﬁ c property enhancement in cast alloys. Friction stir processing of the cast alloys results in extensive grain re ﬁ nement, breakup and dissolution of the coarse second-phase particles and closure of the porosities. In other words, it may result in conversion of the as-cast microstructure to a wrought condition in the absence of a macroscopic shape change and therefore, it has contin- ued to attract research interest. , – 10  

In the present study, as-cast IN738LC, a precipitation hardened nickel-based superalloy and one of most dif ﬁ - cult to weld superalloys, is subjected to FSP for localised microstructural modi ﬁ cation aimed at a speci ﬁ c property enhancement. This speci ﬁ c property is the weldability and the details of the weldability improvement by application of FSP as a preweld treatment have been previously dis- cussed by the present authors.   It was shown that the combination of very large deformations and heating associated with FSP of the as-cast IN738LC superalloy can change its grain boundary liquation crack susceptible microstructure to a crack-resistant microstructure to that extent required for the complete suppression of liquation cracking in the laser surface melting. Although the weld- ability of this alloy is improved by using FSP,   the rela- tively high elevated temperature  ﬂ ow stress of this alloy makes it dif ﬁ cult to apply FSP to it. The application of FSP or FSW to high softening-temperature materials is mainly limited due to the severe conditions encountered by the FSP tool.   During the FSP of high softening-temp- erature materials, the tool must have a greater resistance to wear, possess higher necessary mechanical strength at elevated temperatures and effectively dissipate the extra heat carried to the tool.   While FSW and FSP of low softening-temperature materials has been studied exten- sively,   limited researches has been published to the FSWor FSP of precipitated-hardened nickel-based super- alloys. In a study by Rule  et al .,   FSP was utilised as a pretreatment technique to modify the base metal micro- structure of nickel-based alloys, including IN600, IN625, Hastelloy X and IN718. Ye  et al .   studied the microstructure and mechanical properties of friction stir welded of IN600 alloy. All of these studies are related to the respectively soft solid solution strengthened superal- loys except IN718 superalloys. In order to overcome the problems associated with the FSP of gamma prime pre- cipitation hardened IN738LC superalloy, particularly the wear and deformation of the tool, laser heating of a localised area ahead of the FSP rotating tool is used in the present study to provide suf ﬁ cient plasticity and per- mit the material  ﬂ ow to enable a FSP pass to be  

fabricated. This article also describes how the laser assists the FSP of this high temperature metal especially from a microstructural viewpoint.

# Overall macrostructure of FSP zone  

# Experimental procedure  

The alloy used in this study was an as-cast IN738LC nickel-based superalloy with the following composition (wt-%): 0.10 C, 15.50 Cr, 9.8 Co, 3.04 W, 2.27 Mo, 0.70 Nb, 0.09 Fe, 4.36 Al, 3.15 Ti, 1.81 Ta, 0.04 Zr, 0.01 B and balance nickel, as determined by using a standard spark emission spectrometer (quantometer).  

The FSP and LAFSP were performed on 5.5 mm thick plates using a load-controlled FSW machine ( Fig. 1 ). Tungsten carbide-based alloy tools with shoulder diam- eter of 15 and 6 mm probe diameter were used in all the experiments. The probe length was 1.2 mm and the tool axis was inclined 3° opposite to the direction of the tool advance. The tool rotation speed of 600 rpm, travelling speed of 50 mm min − 1   and constant force of 30 kN were used for both the FSP and LAFSP. A continuous wave Nd:YAG laser with maximum power of 2 kW was employed for the LAFSP. The laser power source was connected to a  ﬁ bre optic cable in order to pass the beam to a processing head including the optical system. The laser processing head was inclined 45° from the nor- mal position and connected to the head of the FSP machine for simultaneously travelling with the FSP tool. A 1 kW laser power was used for the LAFSP and the laser-heated area was located 10 mm ahead of the FSP tool. The laser spot diameter was about 5.5 mm at the location of the specimen. The start of laser heating was set up in such a way that permits the materials beneath the FSP tool to be heated before plunging of the tool. A protective atmosphere of Ar gas  ﬂ owed around the FSP tool and the laser-heated area.  

The specimens were sectioned transverse to the FSP direction and the microstructures of different zones were examined by optical microscopy (OM), scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and orientation image microscopy (OIM). Polished samples for the OM and SEM examin- ations were electrolytic ally etched using a solution of 12 mL H 3 PO 4  + 40 mL HNO 3  + 48 mL H 2 SO 4  at 6 V for 5 s for the examination of the  γ ′   phase. Chemical etching with marble ’ s reagent (10 g CuSO 4  + 50 mL H 2 O + 50 mL HCl) was used for revealing the grain boundaries. The electron backscatter diffraction (EBSD) samples were mechanically polished (through to a 1-µm  ﬁ nish) and then electropolished in a 10% perchloric-methanol solution maintained at approximately  − 40°C using a 40 V dc potential. The EBSD measurements were carried out using a program developed by TSL (OIM Analysis 5.31) in a JSM7001FA JEOL  ﬁ eld emission SEM Figure 2  shows optical macrographs of transverse section of the LAFSP and FSP samples. Three zones can be identi ﬁ ed in the area subjected to the FSP; i.e. the SZ, the thermo mechanically affected zone and the heat affected zone. The SZ exhibits a basin-like shape signi ﬁ - cantly widening towards the upper surface; this may be associated with the strong in ﬂ uence of the tool shoulder on the material  ﬂ ow during the processing.   The inter- face between the SZ of both samples and the base material is relatively sharp. It is apparent that the SZ of the LAFSP sample exhibits a high degree of continuity and no defects, but a discontinuity is observed on the advancing side of the FSP sample. This defect was not vis- ible on the surface of the processed materials right after processing, but became apparent on the surface after cool- ing, possibly due to development of the residual stresses. Therefore, the discontinuity path shows cracking features near the surface which is produced after processing and groove-like features interior of the SZ which are produced during the processing.  

This defect is produced due to improper  ﬂ ow of metal and insuf ﬁ cient consolidation of metal in the FSP region. – 22   It has been reported that a defect-free FSP or FSW forms when the shoulder-driven material merges adequately with both the pin-driven material and base material. In addition to the elimination of macroscopic defects during the LAFSP, it was found that the tool wear and deformation are signi ﬁ cantly decreased during the proces- sing, and therefore it is reasonable to expect that the laser preheating can expand the FSP window. Accordingly, it can be expected that the laser preheating enables the FSP of high softening-temperature materials with less force and higher speeds without causing excessive wear and without strong clamping  ﬁ xtures.  

It should be noted that the high laser power during the LAFSW produces a shallow fusion zone ahead of the tool. Hence, the hot solidi ﬁ ed material is then affected by the FSP tool. If the laser only preheats the material without any melting, the temperature of the material will not be suf ﬁ ciently high when it is touched by the tool. This is because of a signi ﬁ cant distance between the laser-heated area and the FSP tool which must be con- sidered in the LAFSP set-up as an instrumental limit- ation. Also, the absorption of laser beam energy increases when the laser-heated zone is melted.  

# Microstructure characteristics of SZ  

Low-magni ﬁ cation examination of the as-cast base metal (not shown here) indicated that the grain size of the as- cast alloy varied from 35 to 420 µm with an average of 190 µm.  Figure 3 a – c  shows the microstructure of the as- cast IN738LC alloy indicating that the base metal exhib- ited the following microstructural features:  

(i) Extensive precipitation of  γ ′   phase (about 40 pct) within the dendrite core and in the interdendritic region.  

(ii) The  γ ′   particles were  ‘ ogdoadically ’ diced cube shape (clusters of cube particles) in the interdendritic regions with the average edge length of 570 nm ( Fig. 3 b ). The morphology of the  γ ′   particles in the dendrite core changed to spheroidal or rounded cuboidal with the average diameter of 250 nm ( Fig. 3 c ). The large vari- ation in the size and morphology of the  γ ′   particles from the dendrite core to interdendritic regions is related to segregation of Ti during solidi ﬁ cation of the ingot.  

(iii) Several secondary solidi ﬁ cation constituents in the form of MC-type carbides, M 2 SC sulfocarbide and  γ – γ ′ eutectic were formed during the solidi ﬁ cation and are observed within the interdendritic regions ( Fig. 3 a ). The similar secondary phases in IN738LC are also reported by Ojo et al.

# Grain structure and carbide evolution  

Results showed that the FSP is very effective in re ﬁ ning the cast microstructure.  Figure 4 a  and  b  demonstrated the microstructures at the centre of the SZ of FSP and LAFSP samples showing  ﬁ ne equiaxed grains due to dynamic re crystal lisa tion.  

Figure 5  shows EBSD colour maps of different regions in the SZs. The minimum average con ﬁ dence index for these EBSD maps was 0.75. In order to eliminate spurious boundaries caused by orientation noise, a lower limit boundary misorientation cut-off of 2° was used. The top region of the SZ with depth of about 0.5 mm from the sur- face, which can be considered the shoulder-driven layer, shows a different grain size from the centre of the SZ in both samples. The average grain sizes at the centre of the SZ of the LAFSP and FSP samples are about 17.3 and 4.9 µm, respectively ( Fig. 5 ). The grain size at the centre of the FSP sample is much smaller than those of the LAFSP sample. It has been suggested that the nucleated grains due to the dynamic re crystal lisa tion experience static grain growth during the cooling of the thermal cycle. ,   The additional heat due to the laser increases the heat input during the LAFSP and hence decreases the cooling rate. The higher heat input and lower cooling rate of the LAFSP sample result in a higher temperature of the SZ relative to the FSP sample and also allow more time at high temperature for grain growth to occur.  

According to  Fig. 6 a  and  b , the stirring action results in the signi ﬁ cant breakup of the MC carbides. In  Fig. 4 a  and b , the MC carbides are shown to be not only re ﬁ ned, but also more evenly distributed than in the cast structure. These carbides are mostly associated with the grain boundaries.

# Correlation of microstructure and defect formation in SZ  

As mentioned earlier, a discontinuity is formed in the SZ of the FSP sample due to the lack of bonding between the shoulder-driven material (top region of SZ) and pin-dri- ven material (centre and bottom regions). This can be related to the microstructural differences between these two regions. The temperature rise is not high enough for signi ﬁ cant dissolution of the  γ ′   particles at the centre,

The  ﬂ ow stress of the nickel-based superalloys is increased with the increasing volume fraction of the  γ ′   particles. Thus, the ﬂ ow stress of the materials at the centre of the FSP sample was higher than that of the top due to the presence of a high volume fraction of partially dissolved  γ ′   particles in this region. Therefore, the top layer can  ﬂ ow more easily than the centre. As a consequence, these layers could not appropriately  ﬂ ow together and  ﬁ nally bond to each other. On the contrary, the in LAFSP, laser preheating caused more dissolution of the  γ ′   particles at the centre of the SZ and therefore, a more uniform microstructure with approximately similar ductility is formed during the processing which can prevent the formation of the dis- continuity. The higher room temperature hardness values of the SZ in FSP sample can be an implication of its lower ductility compared to LAFSP sample. To provide a better indication of materials  ﬂ ow during processing at high temperature, the torque variations during FSP and LAFSP were recorded. According to  Fig. 8 , the torque imposed on the tool during LAFSP is lower than that of in FSP indicating higher ductility of the materials during processing which in turns provides suf ﬁ cient materials  ﬂ ow to produce defect-free SZ.  

# Conclusions  

Laser heating was used as a means for improving the FSP of a nickel-based superalloy (IN738LC). It was shown that the LAFSP was successful in producing defect-free SZ with remarkable microstructural modi ﬁ cation. The following conclusions can be drawn from this study:  

(i) The stirring action results in signi ﬁ cant grain re ﬁ ne- ment, precipitation of  ﬁ ne gamma prime and breakup of MC carbides of the base metal. Compared with FSP sample, LAFSP sample exhibited coarser grain structure with lower volume fraction of partially dis- solved  γ ′   precipitates in the centre and bottom of the SZ.  

(ii) A defect-free pass is successfully produced by the LAFSP whereas a discontinuity is formed in the SZ of the FSP sample, mainly due to lower heat input and plasticity associated with the FSP relative to the LAFSP.  

(iii) The volume fraction of the partially dissolved  γ ′ particles is signi ﬁ cantly decreased due to the laser heat- ing which leads to more ductility of the SZ materials and consequent elimination of the discontinuity defect in the SZ. The lower torque imposed on the tool during LAFSP con ﬁ rms improving materials  ﬂ ow due to pre- heating effect of laser melting of a localised area ahead of the FSP rotating tool.
